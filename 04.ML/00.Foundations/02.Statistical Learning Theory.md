## 1. Why a Theory of Learning?

The previous note established the informal goal: find a hypothesis $h \in \mathcal{H}$ that approximates the unknown target function $f^*$ well on _unseen_ data. But several questions remain unanswered:

- How much training data is enough to guarantee good generalisation?
- How do we quantify the complexity of a hypothesis class?
- What is the precise relationship between training error and test error?
- Can we prove that a learning algorithm will work, before seeing test data?

Statistical learning theory answers these questions formally. The framework developed here — risk minimisation, PAC learning, VC theory, and the bias-variance decomposition — underlies every algorithm in this vault, even when it operates silently in the background.

---

## 2. The Statistical Framework

### 2.1 Setup

Let $\mathcal{X}$ be the input space and $\mathcal{Y}$ the output space. Assume all data is drawn i.i.d. from a fixed but unknown joint distribution:

$$P_{XY} \text{ over } \mathcal{X} \times \mathcal{Y}$$

The i.i.d. assumption is strong. It says:

1. Every example is drawn from the same distribution (identically distributed).
2. Examples are drawn independently — the $i$-th example gives no information about the $(i+1)$-th.

In practice, this assumption is violated constantly — sequential data, selection bias, distribution shift over time. Knowing the assumption allows you to reason about when violations matter.

### 2.2 The Loss Function

A **loss function** $\ell : \mathcal{Y} \times \mathcal{Y} \to \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0}$ quantifies the cost of predicting $\hat{y}$ when the true label is $y$.

$$\ell(\hat{y}, y) \geq 0, \quad \ell(\hat{y}, y) = 0 \text{ if and only if } \hat{y} = y \text{ (for most losses)}$$

Common examples:

|Loss|Formula|Used for|
|---|---|---|
|Squared loss|$\ell(\hat{y}, y) = (\hat{y} - y)^2$|Regression|
|Absolute loss|$\ell(\hat{y}, y) =|\hat{y} - y|
|0-1 loss|$\ell(\hat{y}, y) = \mathbf{1}[\hat{y} \neq y]$|Classification|
|Log loss|$\ell(\hat{p}, y) = -y \log \hat{p} - (1-y)\log(1-\hat{p})$|Probabilistic classifiers|
|Hinge loss|$\ell(\hat{y}, y) = \max(0, 1 - y\hat{y})$|SVM|

The choice of loss function is a modelling decision. It encodes what kinds of errors you care about and how much.

### 2.3 Population Risk

The **population risk** (or expected risk, or true risk) of a hypothesis $h$ is:

$$R(h) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim P_{XY}}\left[\ell(h(x), y)\right]$$

This is the average loss over the entire data-generating distribution. It is what we actually want to minimise. It is also what we can never compute directly, because $P_{XY}$ is unknown.

The **Bayes-optimal predictor** is:

$$h^* = \underset{h}{\arg\min} \ R(h)$$

This is the best any model can do. For squared loss, the Bayes-optimal predictor is $h^_(x) = \mathbb{E}[y \mid x]$ — the conditional expectation. The Bayes risk $R(h^_)$ is the irreducible noise floor, determined entirely by the randomness in $P_{XY}$.

### 2.4 Empirical Risk

Since $P_{XY}$ is unknown, we cannot compute $R(h)$. Instead, we approximate it using the training set $\mathcal{D} = {(x_i, y_i)}_{i=1}^n$:

$$\hat{R}(h) = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \ell(h(x_i), y_i)$$

This is the **empirical risk** — the average loss on training data. It is computable. It is also a biased estimate of $R(h)$ when $h$ is chosen using the same data.

The quantity we truly care about is $R(h)$. The quantity we can measure is $\hat{R}(h)$. The gap between them is the central object of study in learning theory.

## 3. Empirical Risk Minimisation

**Empirical Risk Minimisation (ERM)** is the learning principle: choose the hypothesis in $\mathcal{H}$ that minimises empirical risk:

$$\hat{h} = \underset{h \in \mathcal{H}}{\arg\min} \ \hat{R}(h)$$

Almost every supervised learning algorithm is ERM in some form:

- Linear regression: ERM with squared loss and linear $\mathcal{H}$
- Logistic regression: ERM with log loss and sigmoid-linear $\mathcal{H}$
- SVM: regularised ERM with hinge loss
- Decision tree: approximate ERM with 0-1 loss (approximate because exact minimisation is NP-hard)

ERM is a reasonable principle. But it has a critical failure mode: if $\mathcal{H}$ is too large, ERM can find a hypothesis that achieves zero training error while having arbitrarily bad test error. This is overfitting. The theory below characterises when ERM is safe.

---

## 4. The Generalisation Gap

Define the **generalisation gap** as:

$$\text{gen gap} = R(\hat{h}) - \hat{R}(\hat{h})$$

This is the difference between true error and training error for the learned hypothesis. It is always non-negative in expectation — training error is optimistically biased because $\hat{h}$ was chosen to minimise it on the training data.

The fundamental question of learning theory: how large can the generalisation gap be, and what controls it?

The answer depends on two things:

1. The **complexity of $\mathcal{H}$** — richer hypothesis classes can fit training data better, but they also have more room to overfit.
2. The **number of training examples $n$** — more data shrinks the gap.

The theory below makes this precise.

---

## 5. PAC Learning

**Probably Approximately Correct (PAC) learning** (Valiant, 1984) is the foundational framework for asking: when does learning _provably_ work?

### 5.1 The Definition

A hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ is **PAC-learnable** if there exists an algorithm $\mathcal{A}$ and a function $n_{\mathcal{H}}(\epsilon, \delta)$ such that: for any $\epsilon > 0$ (accuracy parameter), any $\delta > 0$ (confidence parameter), and any distribution $P_{XY}$, when $\mathcal{A}$ is run on $n \geq n_{\mathcal{H}}(\epsilon, \delta)$ i.i.d. examples, it outputs $\hat{h}$ satisfying:

$$P\left(R(\hat{h}) - R(h^*) \leq \epsilon\right) \geq 1 - \delta$$

Unpacking: with probability at least $1 - \delta$ over the random draw of training data, the algorithm produces a hypothesis whose true risk is within $\epsilon$ of the best possible.

The word "probably" refers to $1 - \delta$. The word "approximately" refers to $\epsilon$. Correct means we are close to the best hypothesis in $\mathcal{H}$.

### 5.2 Sample Complexity

The function $n_{\mathcal{H}}(\epsilon, \delta)$ is the **sample complexity**: the minimum number of training examples required to guarantee PAC learning.

For a finite hypothesis class $|\mathcal{H}| < \infty$, a clean bound holds. With probability at least $1 - \delta$, for all $h \in \mathcal{H}$ simultaneously:

$$R(h) \leq \hat{R}(h) + \sqrt{\frac{\log|\mathcal{H}| + \log(1/\delta)}{2n}}$$

This is a **uniform convergence bound** — it holds for all $h$ at once, not just for the particular $\hat{h}$ chosen by ERM. The bound tells us:

- More hypotheses ($|\mathcal{H}|$ larger) → larger gap. Richer classes require more data.
- More data ($n$ larger) → smaller gap. At rate $O(1/\sqrt{n})$.
- Higher confidence required ($\delta$ smaller) → slightly larger gap, only logarithmically.

The sample complexity for finite $\mathcal{H}$ is:

$$n_{\mathcal{H}}(\epsilon, \delta) = O!\left(\frac{\log|\mathcal{H}| + \log(1/\delta)}{\epsilon^2}\right)$$

### 5.3 Proof Sketch (Finite $\mathcal{H}$)

The proof uses two standard tools: Hoeffding's inequality and the union bound.

**Hoeffding's inequality.** If $Z_1, \ldots, Z_n$ are i.i.d., bounded in $[a, b]$, with mean $\mu$, then for any $t > 0$:

$$P!\left(\bar{Z} - \mu \geq t\right) \leq \exp!\left(-\frac{2n^2 t^2}{\sum_i (b_i - a_i)^2}\right)$$

For a single hypothesis $h$, each $\ell(h(x_i), y_i)$ is a bounded random variable with mean $R(h)$. Hoeffding gives:

$$P!\left(R(h) - \hat{R}(h) \geq \epsilon\right) \leq e^{-2n\epsilon^2}$$

**Union bound.** For $|\mathcal{H}|$ hypotheses, taking a union over all of them:

$$P!\left(\exists h \in \mathcal{H}: R(h) - \hat{R}(h) \geq \epsilon\right) \leq |\mathcal{H}| \cdot e^{-2n\epsilon^2}$$

Setting the right side equal to $\delta$ and solving for $\epsilon$ gives the bound in Section 5.2. $\square$

### 5.4 The Problem with Infinite $\mathcal{H}$

The bound in Section 5.2 requires $|\mathcal{H}| < \infty$. But most useful hypothesis classes — linear classifiers, neural networks — are infinite. We need a different notion of complexity.

## 6. VC Dimension

The **Vapnik-Chervonenkis (VC) dimension** (Vapnik & Chervonenkis, 1971) measures the complexity of an infinite hypothesis class by how many points it can classify in all possible ways.

### 6.1 Shattering

A hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ **shatters** a set ${x_1, \ldots, x_m} \subset \mathcal{X}$ if for every possible binary labelling $y \in {0,1}^m$ of those points, there exists $h \in \mathcal{H}$ that achieves it:

$$\forall (y_1, \ldots, y_m) \in {0,1}^m, \exists h \in \mathcal{H}: h(x_i) = y_i \text{ for all } i$$

Shattering means $\mathcal{H}$ is expressive enough to represent every possible dichotomy on that set.

### 6.2 VC Dimension Defined

$$\text{VC-dim}(\mathcal{H}) = \max{m : \exists {x_1,\ldots,x_m} \subseteq \mathcal{X} \text{ that } \mathcal{H} \text{ shatters}}$$

The VC dimension is the size of the largest set that $\mathcal{H}$ can shatter.

### 6.3 Examples

**Linear classifiers in $\mathbb{R}^d$** (hyperplanes, i.e., $h(x) = \text{sign}(w^Tx + b)$):

$\text{VC-dim} = d + 1$.

To see why: in $\mathbb{R}^2$, any 3 non-collinear points can be shattered by lines — for any binary labelling of 3 points, a line exists separating the two classes. But no set of 4 points can always be shattered — the XOR labelling (alternating corners of a square) cannot be achieved by a single line.

In $\mathbb{R}^d$: any $d+1$ points in general position can be shattered, but no set of $d+2$ points can always be shattered.

**Intuition.** A linear classifier in $\mathbb{R}^d$ has $d+1$ free parameters ($d$ weights + 1 bias). The VC dimension matches the number of parameters for linear models — a useful rule of thumb, though not a theorem.

**Intervals on $\mathbb{R}$** (classify $x$ as positive iff $x \in [a, b]$):

$\text{VC-dim} = 2$.

Any two points $x_1 < x_2$ can be shattered: choose $[x_1 - \epsilon, x_1 + \epsilon]$ for ${+, -}$, $[x_2 - \epsilon, x_2 + \epsilon]$ for ${-, +}$, $[x_1 - \epsilon, x_2 + \epsilon]$ for ${+, +}$, and $(-\infty, x_1 - \epsilon)$ for ${-, -}$. But 3 points $x_1 < x_2 < x_3$ cannot be shattered — the labelling ${+, -, +}$ requires a union of two intervals, which a single interval cannot achieve.

**Convex polygons in $\mathbb{R}^2$:**

$\text{VC-dim} = \infty$.

For any $n$, place $n$ points on a circle. For any subset $S$, the convex hull of $S$ correctly classifies all points: those in $S$ are inside (positive), those outside $S$ are outside (negative). Since this works for any $n$, the VC dimension is infinite.

**Takeaway.** Infinite VC dimension does not mean the class is unlearnable — it means PAC learning in the realizable setting requires additional assumptions. In practice, infinite VC dimension is a signal that the class can overfit badly with finite data.

### 6.4 The Fundamental Theorem of Statistical Learning

For binary classification with 0-1 loss, the following are equivalent:

1. $\mathcal{H}$ is PAC-learnable.
2. $\text{VC-dim}(\mathcal{H}) < \infty$.
3. $\mathcal{H}$ has the uniform convergence property.

This is the fundamental theorem. VC dimension fully characterises learnability for binary classification.

### 6.5 VC Generalisation Bound

For any $\mathcal{H}$ with $\text{VC-dim}(\mathcal{H}) = d < \infty$, with probability at least $1 - \delta$:

$$R(\hat{h}) \leq \hat{R}(\hat{h}) + O!\left(\sqrt{\frac{d \log(n/d) + \log(1/\delta)}{n}}\right)$$

The sample complexity becomes:

$$n_{\mathcal{H}}(\epsilon, \delta) = O!\left(\frac{d \log(d/\epsilon) + \log(1/\delta)}{\epsilon^2}\right)$$

Key observations:

- The rate is still $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ in samples.
- Complexity is controlled by $d = \text{VC-dim}(\mathcal{H})$, not by $|\mathcal{H}|$.
- For linear classifiers in $\mathbb{R}^d$: $d+1$ dimensions of VC complexity → sample complexity scales linearly with the number of features.

## 7. The Bias-Variance Decomposition

The VC bound is a worst-case result. It tells us when learning is _possible_. The bias-variance decomposition tells us _what kind_ of error we are making and why.

### 7.1 Setup

Fix a point $x \in \mathcal{X}$. Suppose we draw many training sets $\mathcal{D}$ of size $n$ from $P_{XY}$, train a model $\hat{h}_{\mathcal{D}}$ on each, and observe the resulting predictions $\hat{h}_{\mathcal{D}}(x)$.

Define:

- The **expected prediction** at $x$: $\bar{h}(x) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}[\hat{h}_{\mathcal{D}}(x)]$
- The true label generating process: $y = f^*(x) + \epsilon$, where $\epsilon \sim (0, \sigma^2)$ is noise with mean zero and variance $\sigma^2$, independent of $x$

### 7.2 The Decomposition

For squared loss, the expected prediction error at a point $x$ decomposes as:

$$\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}, \epsilon}\left[(\hat{h}_{\mathcal{D}}(x) - y)^2\right] = \underbrace{\left(\bar{h}(x) - f^*(x)\right)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}\left[(\hat{h}_{\mathcal{D}}(x) - \bar{h}(x))^2\right]}_{\text{Variance}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{Irreducible noise}}$$

### 7.3 Full Derivation

Let $\hat{h} = \hat{h}_{\mathcal{D}}(x)$, $\bar{h} = \bar{h}(x)$, $f = f^*(x)$, $y = f + \epsilon$.

$$\mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - y)^2] = \mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - f - \epsilon)^2]$$

$$= \mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - f)^2] - 2\mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - f)\epsilon] + \mathbb{E}[\epsilon^2]$$

Since $\epsilon$ is independent of $\hat{h}$ (which depends only on training data, not on the noise at test time), $\mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - f)\epsilon] = 0$. So:

$$= \mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - f)^2] + \sigma^2$$

Now expand the first term by adding and subtracting $\bar{h}$:

$$\mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - f)^2] = \mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - \bar{h} + \bar{h} - f)^2]$$

$$= \mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - \bar{h})^2] + 2\mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - \bar{h})](\bar{h} - f) + (\bar{h} - f)^2$$

The cross-term vanishes: $\mathbb{E}[\hat{h} - \bar{h}] = \mathbb{E}[\hat{h}] - \bar{h} = 0$ by definition of $\bar{h}$.

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - y)^2] = \underbrace{(\bar{h} - f)^2}_{\text{Bias}^2} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[(\hat{h} - \bar{h})^2]}_{\text{Variance}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{Irreducible}}}$$

$\square$

### 7.4 Interpreting Each Term

**Bias$^2$.** The squared difference between the average prediction of the model (across all possible training sets) and the true function value. Bias measures _systematic_ error — the error that persists even with infinite data. A linear model fitting a cubic function has high bias: no matter how much data you give it, it will never recover the cubic.

$$\text{Bias}^2 = (\bar{h}(x) - f^*(x))^2$$

High bias is caused by: too simple a hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$, wrong inductive bias, too much regularisation.

**Variance.** The expected squared deviation of the model's prediction from its own average across different training sets. Variance measures _instability_ — sensitivity to the particular training set drawn. A depth-100 decision tree with 100 training points has high variance: swap a few training examples and the tree changes completely.

$$\text{Variance} = \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}[(\hat{h}_{\mathcal{D}}(x) - \bar{h}(x))^2]$$

High variance is caused by: too complex a hypothesis class, too little data, too little regularisation.

**Irreducible noise $\sigma^2$.** The variance of the label noise. This cannot be reduced by any model, regardless of its complexity or the amount of data. It reflects genuine randomness in $P_{XY}$. The Bayes risk is exactly $\sigma^2$.

### 7.5 The Bias-Variance Tradeoff

The total expected error is $\text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \sigma^2$. Since $\sigma^2$ is fixed, we want to minimise $\text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance}$.

These two terms pull in opposite directions as model complexity increases:

```
Error
  |
  |  \          Bias^2 + Variance
  |   \       /
  |    \     /
  |     \   /   Total error
  |      \ /
  |       X  <- optimal complexity
  |      / \
  |     /   \  Variance
  |    /     ---
  |---        Bias^2
  |_________________________ Model complexity
```

As complexity increases:

- Bias decreases: richer $\mathcal{H}$ can approximate $f^*$ more closely.
- Variance increases: richer $\mathcal{H}$ is more sensitive to the particular training set.

The optimal model complexity sits at the bottom of the total error U-curve.

**Important caveat — double descent.** Modern deep learning has revealed that the classical U-curve is incomplete. For very large models (neural networks with millions of parameters trained on limited data), test error can _decrease again_ after the classical overfitting peak. This "double descent" phenomenon occurs near the interpolation threshold where the model just fits the training data, and then continues to decrease as the model is made even larger. The bias-variance framework is still valid, but the relationship between model complexity and variance is more subtle than the classical picture suggests.

### 7.6 Implications for Regularisation

The bias-variance decomposition directly motivates regularisation:

- **L2 regularisation (Ridge):** adds a penalty $\lambda |w|_2^2$ to the loss. This constrains the hypothesis class, reducing variance at the cost of slightly increased bias. Equivalent to placing a Gaussian prior on weights.
- **L1 regularisation (Lasso):** adds $\lambda |w|_1$. Induces sparsity — drives some weights to exactly zero, effectively shrinking $\mathcal{H}$ by removing features.
- **Early stopping:** terminating gradient descent before convergence prevents the model from fully memorising the training set. Equivalent to L2 regularisation for gradient descent on convex losses.
- **Dropout:** randomly zeroing activations during training. Equivalent to training an ensemble of thinned networks — reduces variance by averaging.

All regularisation is variance reduction at the cost of some bias. The hyperparameter ($\lambda$, $k$ in dropout, number of epochs) controls where on the bias-variance curve you sit.

## 8. Rademacher Complexity

VC dimension is combinatorial and only applies to binary classification. A more general complexity measure is **Rademacher complexity**, which applies to any real-valued function class and any loss.

### 8.1 Definition

Let $\mathcal{F}$ be a class of functions $f : \mathcal{X} \to \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathcal{D} = {x_1, \ldots, x_n}$ a sample. The **empirical Rademacher complexity** is:

$$\hat{\mathcal{R}}_n(\mathcal{F}) = \mathbb{E}_\sigma!\left[\sup_{f \in \mathcal{F}} \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i f(x_i)\right]$$

where $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \text{Uniform}({-1, +1})$ are **Rademacher random variables**.

The **Rademacher complexity** is $\mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{F}) = \mathbb{E}_{\mathcal{D}}[\hat{\mathcal{R}}_n(\mathcal{F})]$.

### 8.2 Intuition

The Rademacher variables $\sigma_i$ are random $\pm 1$ labels. The empirical Rademacher complexity asks: how well can functions in $\mathcal{F}$ correlate with _random noise_?

A very rich function class can always find some $f$ that correlates well with any random labelling. A simple class cannot. So $\hat{\mathcal{R}}_n(\mathcal{F})$ measures the expressive power of $\mathcal{F}$ — how much it can overfit noise.

### 8.3 Rademacher Generalisation Bound

With probability at least $1 - \delta$ over a sample of size $n$:

$$R(\hat{h}) \leq \hat{R}(\hat{h}) + 2\mathcal{R}_n(\mathcal{H}_\ell) + \sqrt{\frac{\log(1/\delta)}{2n}}$$

where $\mathcal{H}_\ell = {\ell(h(\cdot), \cdot) : h \in \mathcal{H}}$ is the loss class induced by $\mathcal{H}$.

This bound is tighter than the VC bound in many cases and applies beyond binary classification to regression, multi-class, and structured prediction.

## 9. The Decomposition of Excess Risk

The total excess risk of a learned hypothesis $\hat{h}$ over the Bayes-optimal $h^*$ can be decomposed into two interpretable terms:

$$\underbrace{R(\hat{h}) - R(h^_)}_{\text{Excess risk}} = \underbrace{R(h^__{\mathcal{H}}) - R(h^*)}_{\text{Approximation error}} + \underbrace{R(\hat{h}) - R(h^*_{\mathcal{H}})}_{\text{Estimation error}}$$

where $h^*_{\mathcal{H}} = \arg\min_{h \in \mathcal{H}} R(h)$ is the best hypothesis in the class.

**Approximation error** (bias term). How much worse is the best hypothesis in $\mathcal{H}$ compared to the true Bayes-optimal? This is determined by the richness of $\mathcal{H}$ and cannot be reduced by more data — only by a richer class.

**Estimation error** (variance term). How much worse is the learned $\hat{h}$ compared to the best possible $h^*_{\mathcal{H}}$? This is due to finite samples and decreases as $n \to \infty$.

The bias-variance tradeoff, VC theory, and Rademacher complexity all formalise different aspects of the same underlying tension: approximation error decreases as $\mathcal{H}$ grows, estimation error increases as $\mathcal{H}$ grows.

## 10. Occam's Razor as a Theorem

Occam's Razor — prefer simpler hypotheses — is not just philosophical wisdom. It follows from the PAC learning bound.

Recall from Section 5.2: with probability at least $1 - \delta$, for all $h \in \mathcal{H}$:

$$R(h) \leq \hat{R}(h) + \sqrt{\frac{\log|\mathcal{H}| + \log(1/\delta)}{2n}}$$

Suppose we can assign a prior complexity $c(h) > 0$ to each hypothesis such that $\sum_{h \in \mathcal{H}} e^{-c(h)} \leq 1$ (a valid probability distribution over hypotheses). Then a tighter bound holds:

$$R(h) \leq \hat{R}(h) + \sqrt{\frac{c(h) + \log(1/\delta)}{2n}}$$

A hypothesis with lower complexity $c(h)$ gets a smaller generalisation penalty. The theorem says: if two hypotheses achieve the same training error, the simpler one (smaller $c(h)$) has a tighter bound on its true error.

This is Occam's Razor as a theorem. Simplicity is not an aesthetic preference — it is a statistical guarantee.

---

## 11. Structural Risk Minimisation

**Structural Risk Minimisation (SRM)** (Vapnik, 1982) uses the theory above to choose both a hypothesis _and_ a hypothesis class simultaneously.

Suppose we have a nested sequence of hypothesis classes:

$$\mathcal{H}_1 \subset \mathcal{H}_2 \subset \cdots \subset \mathcal{H}_k$$

with increasing complexity $d_1 \leq d_2 \leq \cdots \leq d_k$ (e.g., linear classifiers of increasing VC dimension, or polynomial regressors of increasing degree).

SRM selects:

$$\hat{k}, \hat{h} = \underset{k, h \in \mathcal{H}_k}{\arg\min} \left[\hat{R}(h) + \text{complexity penalty}(n, d_k, \delta)\right]$$

where the complexity penalty comes from the VC bound. SRM trades off training fit and model complexity in a principled way.

In practice, SRM motivates:

- Cross-validation for model selection (empirically approximating the bound)
- Regularisation paths in Lasso and Ridge (tracing $\hat{h}$ as $\lambda$ varies)
- Bayesian model selection via marginal likelihood (integrating out parameters)

## 12. What This Theory Does and Does Not Say

### What it says

- PAC learning gives sufficient conditions for generalisation: if $\text{VC-dim}(\mathcal{H}) < \infty$ and $n$ is large enough, ERM generalises.
- The generalisation gap shrinks at rate $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ in samples.
- Model complexity must be matched to sample size.
- Simpler models that fit the data well are preferable to complex ones that fit equally well.

### What it does not say

- The bounds are often very loose. A VC bound may say you need $10^6$ samples; in practice $10^3$ suffice. The bounds are informative about _rates_ and _dependencies_, not about _constants_.
- The theory assumes i.i.d. data. Distribution shift, covariate shift, and temporal dependence are not covered.
- The theory does not explain the empirical success of modern deep learning, where models interpolate (zero training error) yet generalise. This is an active research area. The double descent phenomenon and implicit regularisation of SGD are partial explanations.
- For neural networks, VC dimension is enormous (proportional to the number of parameters times the number of layers) — yet practical generalisation is far better than the bound predicts. The theory is necessary but not sufficient for understanding deep learning.
