
## ✦ The Problem

A dataset $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$ has $n$ observations and $d$ features. In practice:

- $d$ may be in the hundreds or thousands (gene expression, images, text embeddings).
- Most of the variance lives in far fewer than $d$ directions.
- Algorithms degrade in high dimensions (cf. [[07.k-Nearest Neighbours]] — the curse of dimensionality).
- Humans can only visualise $\mathbb{R}^2$ or $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Dimensionality reduction maps $X \in \mathbb{R}^d \to Z \in \mathbb{R}^k$ with $k \ll d$, preserving whatever structure matters for the downstream task.

The word "preserving" hides the entire difficulty. Different methods preserve different things:

| Method            | What it preserves                    | What it sacrifices                           |
| ----------------- | ------------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------- |
| [[01.Principal Component Analysis(PCA)]]        | Global variance, linear structure    | Nonlinear geometry                           |
| [[02.Kernel PCA]] | Nonlinear structure (kernel-defined) | Interpretability, out-of-sample cost         |
| [[03.t-SNE]]      | Local neighbourhood structure        | Global distances, inter-cluster meaning      |
| [[04.Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP)]]       | Local and some global structure      | Mathematical guarantees (optimisation-based) |
| [[05.Autoencoders]]  | Task-relevant features (learned)     | Interpretability                             |

There is no free lunch here either — no method preserves everything.

## ✦ Why High Dimensions Are Dangerous

The **curse of dimensionality** (Bellman, 1957) is not a vague warning — it has precise geometric content.

### Volume concentration

The volume of a unit $d$-ball is: $$V_d = \frac{\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2 + 1)}$$

This peaks around $d = 5$ and then **decreases to zero** as $d \to \infty$. Almost all the volume of a high-dimensional sphere sits in a thin shell near the surface.

Formally: for a uniform distribution on the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^d$, the fraction of volume within distance $\varepsilon$ of the surface is: $$1 - (1-\varepsilon)^d \to 1 \quad \text{as } d \to \infty$$

For $\varepsilon = 0.05$ and $d = 100$: $1 - 0.95^{100} \approx 0.994$. Nearly all data lives near the boundary.

### Distance concentration

For i.i.d. points drawn from a distribution in $\mathbb{R}^d$, as $d \to \infty$: $$\frac{d_{\max} - d_{\min}}{d_{\min}} \to 0$$

All pairwise distances become approximately equal. This destroys the signal for any distance-based algorithm (KNN, DBSCAN, kernel methods).

### Implication for ML

To maintain a fixed data density (points per unit volume), the required sample size grows **exponentially** with $d$. A grid with 10 points per axis requires $10^d$ total points.

Every DR method is, at its core, a strategy for escaping these pathologies.

## ✦ Two Philosophies

### Linear Methods

Assume the low-dimensional structure is a **linear subspace** of $\mathbb{R}^d$: $$z = Wx + b, \quad W \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$$

Advantages: closed-form solutions, interpretable components, fast, out-of-sample projection is trivial ($z_{\text{new}} = Wx_{\text{new}}$).

Limitations: cannot capture curved or folded manifolds.

**Methods:** PCA ([[01.Principal Component Analysis(PCA)]]), Factor Analysis, ICA, Random Projections, Linear Discriminant Analysis.

### Nonlinear Methods (Manifold Learning)

Assume the data lies on a **nonlinear manifold** $\mathcal{M}$ embedded in $\mathbb{R}^d$. The intrinsic dimensionality of $\mathcal{M}$ is $k \ll d$.

Advantages: can unfold complex geometry (Swiss roll, toroidal manifolds, etc.).  
Limitations: no closed-form, expensive, out-of-sample extension is non-trivial or impossible.

**Methods:** Kernel PCA ([[02.Kernel PCA]]), t-SNE ([[03.t-SNE]]), UMAP ([[04.Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP)]]), Isomap, LLE, Diffusion Maps.

## ✦ The Manifold Hypothesis

The central assumption underlying modern DR (and much of deep learning):

> **Real high-dimensional data concentrates near a low-dimensional manifold embedded in the ambient space.**

Natural images do not fill $\mathbb{R}^{256 \times 256}$ uniformly. They live near a manifold of plausible images — a tiny, structured subset of all possible pixel arrays. The same holds for text, audio, molecular structures, and financial time series.

Evidence:

- Intrinsic dimensionality estimates (e.g., via two-NN estimator) of real datasets are often 10–100× smaller than the ambient dimension.
- DR methods that exploit manifold structure dramatically outperform linear methods on real data visualisation tasks.
- Deep networks learn increasingly compact representations in deeper layers — implicit manifold learning.

The manifold hypothesis does not say _which_ manifold, or that the manifold is smooth, or that it is connected. It is an empirical observation, not a theorem.

## ✦ The Intrinsic Dimensionality Problem

Before choosing $k$, one should ask: what is the intrinsic dimensionality of the data?

### Explained Variance (PCA-based)

Run PCA, plot the cumulative explained variance ratio vs. number of components. Choose $k$ such that, e.g., 95% of variance is explained. This is only valid if the data structure is well-captured by PCA.

### Two-NN Estimator (Facco et al., 2017)

For each point, let $r_1$ and $r_2$ be the distances to its 1st and 2nd nearest neighbours. Under the assumption that the data is locally uniform on a $k$-dimensional manifold:

$$\mu_i = \frac{r_2}{r_1}$$

The empirical CDF of $\mu$ follows $1 - \mu^{-k}$. Regress to estimate $k$. This is model-free and requires no DR to be performed first.

### Correlation Dimension

Count the number of pairs of points within distance $r$: $C(r) \propto r^k$. Plot $\log C(r)$ vs. $\log r$; the slope is $k$.

## ✦ Reconstruction vs. Visualisation vs. Preprocessing

DR is used for three distinct purposes, which call for different methods and different quality criteria:

### 1. Preprocessing for downstream ML

Goal: reduce noise, remove redundancy, speed up training, mitigate curse of dimensionality.

Criterion: downstream task performance (not DR quality per se). Use CV to select $k$.

Best methods: PCA (fast, deterministic, interpretable). Random projections (Johnson-Lindenstrauss) for very large $d$.

**Warning:** Fit the DR transform on the training set only. Apply the same transform to test/validation sets. DR is part of the pipeline — it can leak if misapplied (cf. [[02.Pipelines and Experiments]]).

### 2. Visualisation

Goal: produce a 2D or 3D plot that reveals structure in the data.

Criterion: visual separation of known or hypothesised groups; local neighbourhood preservation.

Best methods: t-SNE, UMAP. PCA as a sanity check.

**Critical warning:** t-SNE and UMAP visualisations are **not quantitatively interpretable**. Cluster sizes, inter-cluster distances, and global layout are artefacts of the optimisation, not properties of the data. See [[03.t-SNE]] for the full treatment.

### 3. Representation learning / feature extraction

Goal: learn a compact representation that captures the semantically meaningful variation in the data, for use in a downstream task.

Criterion: quality of the learned representation for the target task.

Best methods: Autoencoders ([[04.Representation Learning]]), Kernel PCA for small datasets with known kernel structure.

## ✦ The Geometry of Linear Projections

Any linear DR method computes a projection $Z = XW^T$ where $W \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times d}$ has orthonormal rows ($WW^T = I_k$). The projection is **onto the column space of $W^T$**.

The reconstruction of $X$ from $Z$ is: $$\hat{X} = ZW = XW^TW$$

The reconstruction error is: $$|X - \hat{X}|_F^2 = |X - XW^TW|_F^2$$

PCA chooses $W$ to minimise this. Every other linear method chooses a different objective — Fisher's LDA maximises class separation, ICA maximises statistical independence — but the projection structure is the same.

The **Johnson-Lindenstrauss lemma** tells us that a random projection to $k = O(\varepsilon^{-2} \log n)$ dimensions preserves all pairwise distances to within a factor of $(1 \pm \varepsilon)$ with high probability. This is a remarkable fact: you do not need to find a good projection — a random one suffices for distance preservation.

## ✦ Connection to AI, ML, and Deep Learning

Dimensionality reduction is not a peripheral technique — it is structurally central to modern ML in ways that are often invisible.

### The Encoder-Decoder Architecture

Every autoencoder, VAE, and the encoder half of a transformer is performing learned DR. The bottleneck layer in a standard autoencoder is literally a low-dimensional projection learned end-to-end. The difference from PCA: the projection is nonlinear (via activation functions) and task-conditioned (the loss may include a downstream objective). See [[04.Representation Learning]].

### Attention as Soft DR

The query-key-value mechanism in transformers projects the full token sequence into a lower-dimensional "attention space" and back. Multi-head attention runs $h$ parallel low-rank projections and concatenates. The $W_Q, W_K, W_V$ matrices are exactly the DR matrices of linear projections — learned jointly with the task.

### Embeddings Are DR

Word2Vec, GloVe, BERT embeddings, image embeddings from ResNets — all of these are learned DR maps from a high-dimensional space (vocabulary $\sim 10^5$, image pixels $\sim 10^6$) to a dense low-dimensional vector ($\sim 10^2$–$10^3$). The "embedding layer" in any neural network is a DR operation.

### Dimensionality Reduction for Interpretability

Principal Components of model activations are used to identify "concept directions" in neural networks (TCAV, linear probing). The geometry of the embedding space — which directions correspond to which concepts — is a DR question.

### Compression and Efficiency

Knowledge distillation (training a small network to mimic a large one) is, in part, a DR problem: find a low-dimensional representation of the large network's behaviour. LoRA (Low-Rank Adaptation), the dominant fine-tuning method for LLMs as of 2024, explicitly constrains weight updates to be low-rank — a DR constraint on the parameter space.
