
> _The central bet of modern machine learning: that the right features are more valuable than the right model. A linear classifier on a good representation beats a deep network on raw pixels. The history of ML is largely the history of finding better representations — and the history of deep learning is largely the history of learning them automatically._

## ✦ What Is a Representation?

A **representation** of $x$ is any function $z = f(x)$ where $z$ is more useful for downstream tasks than $x$ itself.

More formally: let $\mathcal{X}$ be the raw input space (pixels, token counts, sensor readings) and $\mathcal{Z}$ be a latent space. A representation is a map $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Z}$. The claim is that some $\mathcal{Z}$ makes the structure of $p^*(x,y)$ simpler — typically that $p(y | z)$ is easier to estimate than $p(y | x)$.

**Three reasons this is necessary:**

**1. High dimensionality with low intrinsic dimension.** A 224×224 RGB image lives in $\mathbb{R}^{150528}$, but the manifold of natural images has intrinsic dimension estimated at $\sim 40$–$100$. Raw pixels are a vastly redundant encoding of the true degrees of freedom.

**2. Statistical inefficiency of raw features.** A logistic regression on raw pixels must learn $O(d)$ weights; a logistic regression on a 128-dim representation learned from millions of images needs far fewer labelled examples. Representation learning transfers statistical strength from unlabelled data to labelled data.

**3. The disentanglement hypothesis.** In well-learned representations, semantically meaningful factors of variation (identity, pose, lighting) correspond to separate, independent dimensions of $\mathcal{Z}$. This is what makes the embedding space arithmetic of word2vec possible: $\vec{\text{king}} - \vec{\text{man}} + \vec{\text{woman}} \approx \vec{\text{queen}}$.

## ✦ The Spectrum of Supervision

Representation learning methods lie on a continuum from fully supervised to fully unsupervised:

```
Fully                                                       Fully
Supervised ◄────────────────────────────────────────► Unsupervised
     │                    │                    │              │
     │             Semi-supervised        Self-supervised     │
     │                    │                    │              │
  Labels          Labels + unlabelled     Labels from      No labels
  for all         data; propagate         data structure   at all
  data            structure               itself
     │                    │                    │              │
  Standard           Label              BERT, SimCLR,     PCA, AE,
  supervised         propagation,       MAE, GPT          KDE, GMM
  learning           MixMatch
```

The key insight of the last decade: **self-supervised learning** — where the supervision signal is derived from the data itself — can learn representations competitive with supervised pretraining, using orders of magnitude more data (all unlabelled).

## ✦ Classical Representations

Before deep learning, representations were engineered by hand or learned by shallow methods.

### Linear: PCA

PCA finds the $k$-dimensional linear subspace that maximises preserved variance. The representation is $z = V_k^T (x - \bar{x})$, where $V_k$ contains the top-$k$ eigenvectors of the sample covariance.

Properties: uncorrelated dimensions (by construction), ordered by variance, interpretable as directions of maximum spread. Fails when the data manifold is curved — the Swiss roll example (see [[03.t-SNE]]).

### Sparse Coding

Olshausen & Field (1996) proposed learning a dictionary $D \in \mathbb{R}^{d \times m}$ with $m > d$ (overcomplete) such that each $x_i \approx D\alpha_i$ with $\alpha_i$ sparse:

$$\min_{D, {\alpha_i}} \sum_i |x_i - D\alpha_i|^2 + \lambda|\alpha_i|_1, \quad |D_j|_2 = 1 \ \forall j$$

The representation $z = \alpha$ (the sparse codes). This was the first evidence that the brain might use sparse distributed representations — the learned dictionary on natural image patches recovers oriented edge detectors resembling V1 simple cells.

**Connection to deep learning:** the first layer of a CNN trained on images learns filters that look exactly like sparse coding dictionaries. The architecture builds in what sparse coding learns.

### Word Embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe)

A word in a vocabulary of size $V$ is a one-hot vector in $\mathbb{R}^V$ — a terrible representation (high-dimensional, no notion of similarity, all words equidistant). The representation learning objective: find $z_w \in \mathbb{R}^d$ ($d \ll V$) such that similar words have similar vectors.

**Word2Vec — Skip-gram with Negative Sampling:**

Given a word $w$ in context window position $t$, predict surrounding words $w_{t+k}$ for $|k| \leq C$. Objective:

$$\mathcal{L} = \sum_{(w, c) \in \mathcal{D}} \log \sigma(z_w^T z_c) + \sum_{(w, c') \notin \mathcal{D}} \log \sigma(-z_w^T z_{c'})$$

No labels — the supervision is co-occurrence in text. The representation that emerges captures semantic and syntactic regularities as linear structure in $\mathcal{Z}$.

**Why does the arithmetic work?** If the embedding captures conditional distributions of the form $P(\text{context} | w)$, and two words have the same conditional distribution up to a shift in some directions (e.g., "king" and "queen" differ only in the gender direction), the linear algebra of the objective forces their embeddings to differ by approximately the same vector as "man"/"woman".

## ✦ Autoencoders as Representation Learners

Covered in full in [[05.Autoencoders]]. Key summary here:

|Variant|Regularisation|What the Representation Captures|
|---|---|---|
|Undercomplete AE|Bottleneck dimension|PCA subspace (linear) / curved manifold (nonlinear)|
|Sparse AE|L1 on $z$|Dictionary of reusable parts|
|Denoising AE|Corrupted input|Score function $\nabla_x \log p^*(x)$; data manifold|
|Contractive AE|Jacobian Frobenius norm|Locally invariant features|
|VAE|KL to $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$|Continuous, regularised, sampleable latent space|

The common thread: every variant imposes a different **prior over representations** — a different inductive bias about what structure is worth encoding.

## ✦ Self-Supervised Learning

Self-supervised learning (SSL) is the paradigm that currently dominates representation learning in vision, NLP, audio, and biology. The pattern:

1. **Define a pretext task** whose supervision signal is derived from the data itself.
2. **Train a large model** on this task using all available unlabelled data.
3. **Transfer the learned encoder** to downstream tasks by fine-tuning or linear probing.

The pretext task is a means, not an end — the representation it induces is the goal.

### Generative Pretext Tasks

**Masked Language Modelling (BERT):** Randomly mask 15% of tokens in a sentence; predict the masked tokens from the unmasked context.

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{MLM}} = -\sum_{i \in \mathcal{M}} \log p_\theta(w_i \mid w_{\setminus \mathcal{M}})$$

The model must represent each token in terms of its semantic relationship to context — forcing rich contextual embeddings. BERT-large: 340M parameters, trained on BookCorpus + Wikipedia.

**Causal Language Modelling (GPT):** Predict the next token given all preceding tokens.

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{CLM}} = -\sum_t \log p_\theta(w_t \mid w_1, \ldots, w_{t-1})$$

Left-to-right factorisation of $p(\text{sequence})$. The representation must capture enough to predict continuations — which requires understanding syntax, semantics, world knowledge.

**Masked Autoencoders (MAE):** Mask 75% of image patches; reconstruct pixel values of masked patches from visible patches (see [[05.Autoencoders]]).

### Contrastive Pretext Tasks

Contrastive learning is the other major SSL paradigm, dominant in vision before MAE. The key idea: define positive pairs (two views of the same instance should be similar) and negative pairs (different instances should be dissimilar), and train an encoder to embed positives close and negatives far.

#### SimCLR (Chen et al., 2020)

For each image $x_i$, generate two views $\tilde{x}_i^{(1)}$ and $\tilde{x}_i^{(2)}$ via random augmentation (crop, colour jitter, blur). Within a mini-batch of $N$ images, there are $2N$ augmented views. The $2(N-1)$ other views are treated as negatives.

The **NT-Xent loss** (normalised temperature-scaled cross-entropy):

$$\mathcal{L}_{i,j} = -\log \frac{\exp(\text{sim}(z_i, z_j)/\tau)}{\sum_{k=1}^{2N} \mathbf{1}_{[k \neq i]} \exp(\text{sim}(z_i, z_k)/\tau)}$$

where $\text{sim}(u,v) = u^T v / (|u| |v|)$ (cosine similarity) and $\tau$ is a temperature hyperparameter.

The loss pushes $z_i$ and $z_j$ (two views of the same image) to have high cosine similarity, and pushes $z_i$ away from all $2(N-1)$ negatives.

**Projection head:** after the backbone encoder $f_\phi$, a small MLP $g_\theta$ maps to the contrastive space: $z = g_\theta(f_\phi(x))$. Critically, the representation used for downstream tasks is $h = f_\phi(x)$, not $z$. The projection head is discarded after pretraining. This is empirically important — the projection head absorbs information specific to the pretext task; $h$ retains more general structure.

```
x ──► [ResNet f_φ] ──► h (representation, kept)
                            │
                            └──► [MLP g_θ] ──► z (contrastive space, discarded)
```

#### MoCo (Momentum Contrast, He et al., 2020)

SimCLR requires large batches ($N \sim 4096$) because it needs many negatives. MoCo decouples the number of negatives from batch size using a **momentum encoder** and a **queue**:

- **Online encoder** $f_\phi$: updated by gradient descent.
- **Momentum encoder** $f_{\phi'}$: updated by exponential moving average:

$$\phi' \leftarrow m \phi' + (1-m)\phi, \qquad m \approx 0.999$$

The queue stores encoded keys from recent mini-batches — a large pool of negatives ($\sim 65536$) that doesn't fit in a single batch.

The momentum encoder ensures that the queue representations don't change too rapidly (which would destabilise training).

#### BYOL (Bootstrap Your Own Latent, Grill et al., 2020)

BYOL eliminates negatives entirely — the most surprising result in recent SSL. An online network predicts the representation of the momentum network on a different augmented view. No negative pairs, no collapse (empirically), because the asymmetry between online and momentum networks acts as an implicit regulariser.

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{BYOL}} = 2 - 2 \cdot \frac{q_\theta(z_\theta)^T z_{\theta'}}{|q_\theta(z_\theta)|, |z_{\theta'}|}$$

Why doesn't BYOL collapse to a constant representation? Theoretical explanation remains partially open — batch normalisation and the stop-gradient on the momentum network are both implicated.

#### DINO (Self-Distillation with No Labels, Caron et al., 2021)

Applies the BYOL-style momentum distillation to Vision Transformers. The student (online) predicts the teacher (momentum) output distribution. A **centering** operation prevents collapse. The representations learned by DINO exhibit emergent attention maps that segment objects without any segmentation supervision.

## ✦ The Uniformity–Alignment Framework

Wang & Isola (2020) provide a useful decomposition of what makes a good contrastive representation. Define:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{align}} = \mathbb{E}_{(x, x^+) \sim p_{\text{pos}}} |f(x) - f(x^+)|^2$$

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{uniform}} = \log, \mathbb{E}_{x, y \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} p_{\text{data}}} e^{-2|f(x)-f(y)|^2}$$

**Alignment:** positive pairs should map to nearby points. Measures how well $f$ preserves augmentation-invariant structure.

**Uniformity:** the representation should be spread uniformly on the hypersphere. Measures how much information is preserved (a collapsed representation fails uniformity).

Good contrastive representations optimise both: tight clusters (alignment) that are well-spread across the sphere (uniformity). NT-Xent and related losses are doing exactly this, implicitly.

## ✦ Disentangled Representations

A representation is **disentangled** if each dimension $z_j$ corresponds to a single independent generative factor of variation, and each factor is captured by a single dimension.

**Formal definition (Higgins et al., 2018):** $z$ is disentangled with respect to factors $v = (v_1, \ldots, v_K)$ if:

1. The factors are independent: $p(v) = \prod_k p(v_k)$.
2. There is a bijection between $z$ and $v$.
3. The bijection is group-equivariant: changing $v_k$ changes only $z_k$, leaving all other $z_j$ unchanged.

**$\beta$-VAE** (Higgins et al., 2017): scale the KL term in the VAE ELBO:

$$\mathcal{L}_\beta = \mathbb{E}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - \beta \cdot D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) | p(z)), \qquad \beta > 1$$

Larger $\beta$ → stronger pressure toward $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$ → each dimension must be independent → disentanglement emerges. But higher $\beta$ → worse reconstruction (explicit tradeoff).

**Locatello et al. (2019) — the impossibility result:** without inductive biases, disentanglement cannot be achieved from observational data alone, regardless of the model. Unsupervised disentanglement is fundamentally impossible without priors. This is a published negative result that should temper claims about "disentangled VAEs" learned purely from data.

## ✦ Transfer Learning and Fine-Tuning

The purpose of representation learning in practice is **transfer**: learn features from abundant data (unlabelled, or labelled in a related domain), then transfer to a task with scarce labels.

### The Linear Probe Protocol

Freeze the encoder $f_\phi$; train a linear classifier on top of $f_\phi(x)$:

$$\min_{W} \sum_i \ell(W f_\phi(x_i), y_i)$$

If the linear probe achieves high accuracy, the representation is **linearly separable** — the encoder has done the hard work of separating classes. This is the standard benchmark for SSL representation quality (e.g., ImageNet linear probing accuracy).

### Fine-Tuning

Initialise the encoder with pretrained weights; continue training the full model (encoder + task head) on labelled data with a small learning rate. Better performance than linear probing because the encoder can adapt, but requires more labelled data.

**Full fine-tuning vs. parameter-efficient fine-tuning (PEFT):** as models grow to billions of parameters, fine-tuning everything is expensive. PEFT methods — LoRA, prefix tuning, adapters — fine-tune a small number of added parameters while freezing most of the pretrained weights. The representation is mostly fixed; only small adapter layers adapt.

### The Feature Hierarchy

In deep networks, different layers capture different levels of abstraction. Classic analysis (Zeiler & Fergus, 2014 on CNNs):

- **Layer 1:** edges, orientations, colours.
- **Layer 2:** textures, simple patterns.
- **Layer 3:** object parts.
- **Layer 4–5:** object-level features, class-specific.

For transfer to a visually similar domain (e.g., medical imaging): fine-tune deep layers, freeze early layers. For transfer to a distant domain: fine-tune more, or even retrain from scratch.

## ✦ Information-Theoretic View

### The Information Bottleneck (Tishby et al., 2000)

Given input $X$ and label $Y$, find a representation $Z = f(X)$ that:

- **Compresses** $X$: minimise $I(X; Z)$ (mutual information — how much of $X$ is retained).
- **Preserves relevance**: maximise $I(Z; Y)$ (how much of $Y$ can be predicted from $Z$).

The information bottleneck Lagrangian:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{IB}} = -I(Z; Y) + \beta, I(X; Z)$$

$\beta$ controls the compression-relevance tradeoff. The optimal $Z$ lies on the **information plane** $(I(X;Z),, I(Z;Y))$ at the Pareto frontier — the information bottleneck curve.

**Connection to DNNs:** Tishby & Schwartz-Ziv (2017) claimed that SGD training in deep networks undergoes two phases — an initial fitting phase (increasing $I(Z;Y)$) followed by a compression phase (decreasing $I(X;Z)$). This claim is contested but has generated substantial research on understanding what DNNs learn.

**Connection to $\beta$-VAE:** the ELBO of the $\beta$-VAE is exactly:

$$\mathcal{L}_\beta = \mathbb{E}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - \beta, D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) | p(z))$$

Under certain conditions, $D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) | p(z))$ is an upper bound on $I(X;Z)$ — so the $\beta$-VAE is approximately optimising the information bottleneck with $Y = X$ (reconstruction as the relevance task).

### Mutual Information Maximisation (MI-Max)

Deep InfoMax (Hjelm et al., 2019): maximise mutual information $I(X; Z)$ between input and representation. Since MI is intractable, use a neural lower bound (Jensen-Shannon divergence or MINE estimator). The representation is learned to capture as much of the input as possible in a compact form.

CPC (Contrastive Predictive Coding, van den Oord et al., 2018): maximise MI between a context vector and future observations. The key insight: the InfoNCE loss (used in CPC and related methods) is a lower bound on MI:

$$I(z_t; z_{t+k}) \geq \log N - \mathcal{L}_{\text{InfoNCE}}$$

Contrastive learning is MI maximisation. SimCLR, MoCo, and CPC are all optimising lower bounds on mutual information between different views of the same data.

## ✦ Representation Quality: Diagnostics

### Linear Probing Accuracy

Train a linear classifier on frozen representations; evaluate on held-out labels. The gold standard for SSL benchmarking (ImageNet linear probe top-1 accuracy).

### $k$-NN Accuracy

Find $k$ nearest neighbours in representation space; classify by majority vote. No training required — directly measures geometric quality of the space.

### Centred Kernel Alignment (CKA)

Measures similarity between two representation matrices $K_1 = f_1(X) f_1(X)^T$ and $K_2 = f_2(X) f_2(X)^T$:

$$\text{CKA}(K_1, K_2) = \frac{\text{HSIC}(K_1, K_2)}{\sqrt{\text{HSIC}(K_1, K_1), \text{HSIC}(K_2, K_2)}}$$

HSIC is the Hilbert-Schmidt independence criterion. CKA measures how similarly two models represent the same data — used to compare representations across layers, architectures, and training regimes.

### t-SNE / UMAP Visualisation

Projects representations to 2D (see [[03.t-SNE]], [[04.UMAP]]). Qualitative: are semantically similar examples close? Are classes separated? Remember: inter-cluster distances in t-SNE are not interpretable.

### Reconstruction Error (AE setting)

How well can the decoder reconstruct $x$ from $z$? Low reconstruction error is necessary but not sufficient for a useful representation.

## ✦ Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

### Foundation Models

GPT, BERT, CLIP, Stable Diffusion, AlphaFold — all are large-scale representation learners. Their central innovation is not the downstream task; it is the pretraining objective that produces a rich general-purpose representation. The downstream tasks are fine-tuned on top of this base.

CLIP (Contrastive Language-Image Pretraining, Radford et al., 2021): contrastive learning on (image, caption) pairs. Aligns the image representation space and text representation space. The alignment enables zero-shot classification: compute the similarity between an image embedding and text embeddings of class names; classify by argmax.

### Emergent Properties in Large Models

As model scale increases, qualitatively new capabilities emerge that are absent at small scale. These are believed to arise from the representations becoming richer — capturing higher-order structure that small models cannot. The representation quality scales as a power law with compute, dataset size, and model size (Hoffmann et al., 2022 — Chinchilla scaling laws).

### Representation Engineering and Interpretability

Probing classifiers: train a linear probe to predict a specific property (e.g., part of speech, sentiment, presence of a concept) from intermediate layer representations. High probe accuracy → that property is linearly encoded at that layer.

Sparse autoencoders applied to LLM activations (see [[05.Autoencoders]] — mechanistic interpretability section): decompose superposed representations into sparse, interpretable features. This is representation learning _about_ representations — using the tools developed for learning to understand what has been learned.

### Self-Supervised Pretraining as the New Default

The dominant paradigm in NLP since BERT (2018) and increasingly in vision since MAE/DINO (2021): pretrain a large model self-supervisedly on massive unlabelled data, then fine-tune on small labelled datasets. Labelled data is the scarce resource; representations multiply its value.
