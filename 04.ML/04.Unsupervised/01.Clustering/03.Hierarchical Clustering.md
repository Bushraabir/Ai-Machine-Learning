## ✦ The Core Problem with Flat Clustering

Both K-Means and GMM require you to commit to $k$ before you look at the data. That is a strong assumption — and often the wrong one.

Hierarchical clustering asks a different question: _at every possible level of granularity, which points are most similar to each other?_ The answer is a tree, not a partition. You get a partition as a free byproduct by cutting the tree at any height.

There are two strategies:

|Strategy|Direction|Start|End|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Agglomerative**|Bottom-up|$n$ singletons|1 cluster|
|**Divisive**|Top-down|1 cluster|$n$ singletons|

Almost all practical use is agglomerative. Divisive methods are $O(2^n)$ to do optimally and rarely appear outside of theory.

---

## ✦ Agglomerative Clustering: The Algorithm

### Procedure

```
Input: data X = {x₁, ..., xₙ}, linkage criterion L, distance metric d
Output: a binary merge tree (the dendrogram)

1. Initialise: each point is its own cluster Cᵢ = {xᵢ}
2. Compute the n×n distance matrix D where D[i,j] = d(xᵢ, xⱼ)
3. Repeat until one cluster remains:
   a. Find the pair (Cᵢ, Cⱼ) with minimum inter-cluster distance under L
   b. Merge Cᵢ and Cⱼ into a new cluster Cₖ = Cᵢ ∪ Cⱼ
   c. Record the merge height h = L(Cᵢ, Cⱼ) in the dendrogram
   d. Update the distance matrix: remove rows/cols for Cᵢ, Cⱼ; add row/col for Cₖ
4. Return the dendrogram
```

The algorithm is exact — no random initialisation, no local minima in the usual sense. The merge decisions are irrevocable (greedy), but the tree is globally determined.

### Complexity

Naively: $O(n^3)$ — at each of the $n-1$ merge steps, scan the full $(n-k) \times (n-k)$ distance matrix.

With a priority queue (min-heap over cluster pairs): $O(n^2 \log n)$.

With specific linkages (single, complete, average) and the **Lance-Williams update formula**, the distance matrix can be updated in $O(1)$ per merge rather than recomputed — bringing the best implementations to $O(n^2)$ space and $O(n^2 \log n)$ time.

> **The bottom line:** Hierarchical clustering does not scale. For $n \gg 10^4$, consider approximate variants or switch to HDBSCAN.

## ✦ Linkage Criteria: The Heart of the Method

The linkage criterion defines what "distance between two clusters" means. This single choice determines the shapes of clusters the algorithm can discover.

Let $C_A$ and $C_B$ be two clusters. Let $d(x, y)$ be a point-to-point distance (Euclidean by default).

### Single Linkage (Minimum)

$$d_{\text{single}}(C_A, C_B) = \min_{x \in C_A,, y \in C_B} d(x, y)$$

Uses the **closest pair** of points across the two clusters.

**Behaviour:** Chains. If there is any path of close points connecting two groups, single linkage will merge them early. Produces elongated, non-convex clusters. Extremely sensitive to noise and bridges.

```
•  •  •  •  •        •  •  •  •
     ↑ one bridging point merges everything
```

**Use when:** The true clusters are filamentary or non-convex (e.g., genomic sequences, manifold data). Single linkage is the basis for the **minimum spanning tree** — the dendrogram from single linkage encodes the MST of the data.

### Complete Linkage (Maximum)

$$d_{\text{complete}}(C_A, C_B) = \max_{x \in C_A,, y \in C_B} d(x, y)$$

Uses the **farthest pair** of points across the two clusters.

**Behaviour:** Compactness. Merges are only made when the diameter of the resulting cluster is controlled. Tends to produce tight, roughly equal-sized clusters. Sensitive to outliers (a single far point inflates the inter-cluster distance).

**Use when:** You expect compact, roughly spherical clusters and want to avoid chaining.

### Average Linkage (UPGMA)

$$d_{\text{avg}}(C_A, C_B) = \frac{1}{|C_A||C_B|} \sum_{x \in C_A} \sum_{y \in C_B} d(x, y)$$

Uses the **mean pairwise distance** between all cross-cluster point pairs.

**Behaviour:** A compromise. Less sensitive to outliers than complete linkage, less prone to chaining than single linkage. The workhorse choice for most applications. Used extensively in phylogenetics.

> **UPGMA** = Unweighted Pair Group Method with Arithmetic mean. There is also **WPGMA** (weighted), which accounts for unequal cluster sizes differently.

### Ward's Linkage

Ward's criterion is fundamentally different — it is not based on pairwise point distances at all.

$$d_{\text{Ward}}(C_A, C_B) = \frac{|C_A||C_B|}{|C_A| + |C_B|} |\mu_A - \mu_B|^2$$

where $\mu_A, \mu_B$ are the centroids of $C_A, C_B$.

**Interpretation:** At each step, Ward merges the pair of clusters whose union results in the **minimum increase in total within-cluster variance** (sum of squared deviations from centroid):

$$\Delta W = W(C_A \cup C_B) - W(C_A) - W(C_B)$$

where $W(C) = \sum_{x \in C} |x - \mu_C|^2$.

You can verify the equivalence algebraically — the formula above for $d_{\text{Ward}}$ is exactly this incremental variance.

**Behaviour:** Strongly prefers merging small, compact clusters. Tends to produce roughly equal-sized clusters of similar variance. The most commonly recommended default for exploratory work on Euclidean data.

**Requires:** Euclidean distance specifically. Ward's criterion is not well-defined with non-Euclidean metrics (you need a notion of centroid).

### Centroid Linkage

$$d_{\text{centroid}}(C_A, C_B) = |\mu_A - \mu_B|^2$$

Distance between cluster centroids.

**Pathology:** Can exhibit **inversions** — a merge at a later step has a _lower_ height than an earlier merge, making the dendrogram non-monotone. Generally avoided.

### Summary Table

|Linkage|Based on|Cluster Shape|Sensitivity|Requires|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Single|Min pairwise distance|Chains, non-convex|Bridges, noise|Any metric|
|Complete|Max pairwise distance|Compact, equal|Outliers|Any metric|
|Average|Mean pairwise distance|Intermediate|Moderate|Any metric|
|Ward|Variance increase|Compact, equal size|Outliers (mild)|Euclidean|
|Centroid|Centroid distance|Varied|Inversions possible|Euclidean|

## ✦ The Dendrogram

The dendrogram is the primary output. It is a binary tree where:

- Leaves are the individual data points $x_1, \ldots, x_n$.
- Each internal node represents a merge event.
- The **height** of an internal node = the inter-cluster distance at which that merge occurred (under the chosen linkage).
- The **horizontal position** of leaves is arbitrary (reordered for visual clarity).

### Reading a Dendrogram

```
Height
  │
  │    ┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
4 │    │                                     │
  │  ┌─┴──────┐                        ┌────┴────┐
2 │  │        │                        │         │
  │ ┌┴┐    ┌──┴──┐                  ┌──┴──┐   ┌──┴──┐
1 │ │ │    │     │                  │     │   │     │
  │ A B    C     D                  E     F   G     H
```

- Cut at height $h = 2$: three clusters ${A,B}$, ${C,D}$, ${E,F,G,H}$
- Cut at height $h = 4$: two clusters ${A,B,C,D}$, ${E,F,G,H}$
- The **cophenetic distance** between points $A$ and $C$ is 2 (the height of their LCA).

### The Cophenetic Correlation Coefficient

For a given dendrogram and pairwise distance matrix $D$, define the **cophenetic matrix** $C$ where $C[i,j]$ = height at which $x_i$ and $x_j$ first merged.

$$r_{\text{cophenetic}} = \text{corr}(D, C)$$

High cophenetic correlation ($> 0.8$) means the dendrogram faithfully represents the pairwise distances. Use this to compare linkages on a given dataset.

## ✦ Choosing the Number of Clusters

Since the dendrogram gives you all possible $k$ simultaneously, the question is where to cut.

### Method 1: Visual inspection

Cut where there is a **large gap in merge heights** — a long vertical line in the dendrogram before the next merge. This gap signals that the next merge would join genuinely dissimilar clusters.

### Method 2: Silhouette Score

For a cut producing $k$ clusters with assignment $C(x)$:

$$s(x) = \frac{b(x) - a(x)}{\max(a(x), b(x))}$$

where:

- $a(x)$ = mean distance from $x$ to all points in its own cluster (cohesion)
- $b(x)$ = mean distance from $x$ to all points in the nearest other cluster (separation)

$s(x) \in [-1, 1]$. Mean silhouette score near 1 → well-separated clusters. Choose $k$ that maximises the mean silhouette.

### Method 3: Inconsistency Criterion

For each merge at height $h$, compare $h$ to the mean and standard deviation of the heights of all merges below it (within a depth window). If $h$ is many standard deviations above the local mean, it is "inconsistent" — a natural cut.

## ✦ The Lance-Williams Update Formula

Rather than recomputing all inter-cluster distances from scratch after each merge, most implementations use the **Lance-Williams recurrence**: after merging $C_i$ and $C_j$ into $C_k$, the distance from $C_k$ to any other cluster $C_q$ is:

$$d(C_k, C_q) = \alpha_i \cdot d(C_i, C_q) + \alpha_j \cdot d(C_j, C_q) + \beta \cdot d(C_i, C_j) + \gamma \cdot |d(C_i, C_q) - d(C_j, C_q)|$$

The coefficients $(\alpha_i, \alpha_j, \beta, \gamma)$ depend on the linkage:

|Linkage|$\alpha_i$|$\alpha_j$|$\beta$|$\gamma$|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Single|$1/2$|$1/2$|$0$|$-1/2$|
|Complete|$1/2$|$1/2$|$0$|$+1/2$|
|Average (UPGMA)|$\frac{n_i}{n_i+n_j}$|$\frac{n_j}{n_i+n_j}$|$0$|$0$|
|Ward|$\frac{n_i+n_q}{n_i+n_j+n_q}$|$\frac{n_j+n_q}{n_i+n_j+n_q}$|$\frac{-n_q}{n_i+n_j+n_q}$|$0$|

This turns an $O(n^2)$ recomputation into $O(n)$ per merge — a crucial speedup.

## ✦ Worked Example (by hand)

**Data:** 5 points with the following distance matrix:

$$D = \begin{pmatrix}

- & 1 & 5 & 7 & 8 \ 1 & - & 4 & 6 & 7 \ 5 & 4 & - & 2 & 3 \ 7 & 6 & 2 & - & 1 \ 8 & 7 & 3 & 1 & - \end{pmatrix}$$

Points: $A, B, C, D, E$ (rows/columns in order).

**Single linkage step-by-step:**

1. Minimum distance: $D[A,B] = 1$ and $D[D,E] = 1$. Say we merge $D$ and $E$ first → cluster $DE$ at height $1$.
    
2. Update distances to $DE$ (single linkage = min):
    
    - $d(A, DE) = \min(7,8) = 7$
    - $d(B, DE) = \min(6,7) = 6$
    - $d(C, DE) = \min(2,3) = 2$
    
    New matrix ($A, B, C, DE$): $$\begin{pmatrix} - & 1 & 5 & 7 \ 1 & - & 4 & 6 \ 5 & 4 & - & 2 \ 7 & 6 & 2 & - \end{pmatrix}$$
    
3. Minimum: $D[A,B] = 1$ → merge $AB$ at height $1$.
    
4. Update ($AB, C, DE$):
    
    - $d(AB, C) = \min(5,4) = 4$
    - $d(AB, DE) = \min(7,6) = 6$
    - $d(C, DE) = 2$
5. Minimum: $D[C, DE] = 2$ → merge $CDE$ at height $2$.
    
6. Final merge: $AB$ and $CDE$ at height $4$.
    

**Resulting dendrogram heights:** $1, 1, 2, 4$.

Cut at height $h < 2$: four clusters ${A,B}, {C}, {D}, {E}$.  
Cut at $2 \leq h < 4$: two clusters ${A,B}$, ${C,D,E}$.

## ✦ Divisive Clustering (DIANA)

DIANA (DIvisive ANAlysis) works top-down:

1. Start with all $n$ points in one cluster.
2. At each step, select the cluster with the **highest average dissimilarity** (the most "spread out").
3. Find the point within that cluster that is most dissimilar to all others. It becomes a "splinter group."
4. Assign each remaining point in that cluster to either the original group or the splinter group based on which has smaller average distance.
5. Repeat.

**Complexity:** $O(n^2)$ per split (computing average dissimilarities), $O(n^3)$ total in the naive form.

DIANA is implemented in R's `cluster` package. Rarely used in Python/sklearn contexts. The primary advantage: it may discover the largest split more accurately (the first split of the whole dataset is globally informed, whereas agglomerative methods build up from many local decisions).

## ✦ Connection to Minimum Spanning Tree

**Theorem:** The dendrogram produced by **single linkage** agglomerative clustering is equivalent to the **Minimum Spanning Tree (MST)** of the complete graph on $n$ points (where edge weights are pairwise distances).

_Proof sketch._ The MST merges nodes by the smallest edge weight not forming a cycle — exactly the greedy nearest-pair rule of single linkage. The merge order in single linkage is the edge order of Kruskal's algorithm.

**Consequence:** Single linkage runs in $O(n^2)$ (computing pairwise distances dominates) — but can be accelerated to $O(n \log n)$ for low-dimensional data via Prim's or Kruskal's algorithm on a spatial graph.

## ✦ Generalisation: HDBSCAN

Standard hierarchical clustering requires all points to belong to a cluster. HDBSCAN (Hierarchical DBSCAN) builds a hierarchy of density-based clusters and automatically identifies noise:

1. Compute **core distances**: $d_{\text{core},k}(x)$ = distance to $k$-th nearest neighbour.
2. Compute **mutual reachability distance**: $d_{\text{mreach}}(x, y) = \max(d_{\text{core}}(x), d_{\text{core}}(y), d(x,y))$ — smooths out low-density regions.
3. Build the MST of the mutual reachability graph.
4. Extract a **condensed cluster tree** by collapsing small clusters below a `min_cluster_size` threshold.
5. Select persistent clusters from the condensed tree (those with high "stability").

**Advantages over vanilla hierarchical clustering:**

- Automatically determines the number of clusters.
- Handles varying-density clusters.
- Identifies noise points explicitly.
- Scalable: $O(n \log n)$ in practice with approximate NN graphs.

HDBSCAN should be considered the modern default when DBSCAN or hierarchical clustering is appropriate. Available as `hdbscan` (pip) or `sklearn.cluster.HDBSCAN` (sklearn ≥ 1.3).

## ✦ Connection to AI, ML, and Deep Learning

Hierarchical clustering is not merely a standalone tool — it appears throughout the ML ecosystem in both practical and theoretical roles.

### Taxonomic Embeddings and Word Hierarchies

Hierarchical structure is latent in many ML problems. In NLP, **WordNet** organises concepts hierarchically (hypernym/hyponym relations). Dendrograms built on word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) often recover semantic groupings — verbs cluster by type, nouns by domain — without labels.

### Transfer Learning and Feature Hierarchy

Deep neural networks learn **hierarchical representations**: edges → textures → parts → objects (cf. [[04.Representation Learning]]). Applying agglomerative clustering to neuron activations at intermediate layers is a diagnostic tool for understanding what a network has learned. SVCCA and representational similarity analysis use hierarchical clustering to compare layer representations across networks.

### Hierarchical Softmax

In language models with large vocabularies ($\sim 10^5$ words), the softmax over all classes is expensive: $O(V)$ per token. **Hierarchical softmax** organises vocabulary into a binary tree (often built by agglomerative clustering of word frequencies or embeddings). Prediction traverses the tree: $O(\log V)$ per token. Used in early Word2Vec training.

### HAC for Coreference Resolution

Coreference resolution (deciding which mentions in text refer to the same entity) is naturally hierarchical. Agglomerative clustering with a learned similarity model (e.g., a trained neural scorer) is a standard approach in NLP pipelines.

### Neural Clustering and Differentiable Hierarchies

Recent work makes the dendrogram itself differentiable. **DIFFRAC** (Wilder et al., 2019) and **SoftTree** methods relax the hard merge decisions to probability distributions, enabling end-to-end gradient-based learning of cluster structure — pushing hierarchical clustering into the deep learning paradigm.

### Hyperparameter Tuning Visualisation

When performing a grid search over hyperparameters, agglomerative clustering on the resulting performance matrix (with Ward's linkage) reveals which hyperparameter combinations behave similarly — useful for pruning search spaces.

### Federated Learning

In federated learning, client models can be hierarchically clustered by their gradient similarity. This reveals data heterogeneity: clients with similar gradient updates probably have similar data distributions. The cluster structure informs personalisation strategies.

## ✦ Implementation (NumPy, from scratch)

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.spatial.distance import pdist, squareform

def hierarchical_cluster(X, linkage='single'):
    """
    Agglomerative clustering returning the merge sequence.
    Returns: list of (cluster_i, cluster_j, height, new_size) — the linkage matrix.
    """
    n = len(X)
    D = squareform(pdist(X, metric='euclidean'))  # (n x n) distance matrix
    np.fill_diagonal(D, np.inf)

    # Track which original indices belong to each "active" cluster
    clusters = {i: [i] for i in range(n)}
    active = list(range(n))
    linkage_matrix = []

    for step in range(n - 1):
        # Find the minimum inter-cluster distance
        min_dist = np.inf
        merge_i, merge_j = -1, -1
        for ii, i in enumerate(active):
            for jj, j in enumerate(active):
                if ii >= jj:
                    continue
                if D[i, j] < min_dist:
                    min_dist = D[i, j]
                    merge_i, merge_j = i, j

        new_id = n + step
        size_new = len(clusters[merge_i]) + len(clusters[merge_j])
        linkage_matrix.append((merge_i, merge_j, min_dist, size_new))

        # Compute distances from new cluster to remaining clusters
        new_dists = {}
        for k in active:
            if k == merge_i or k == merge_j:
                continue
            if linkage == 'single':
                new_dists[k] = min(D[merge_i, k], D[merge_j, k])
            elif linkage == 'complete':
                new_dists[k] = max(D[merge_i, k], D[merge_j, k])
            elif linkage == 'average':
                ni, nj = len(clusters[merge_i]), len(clusters[merge_j])
                new_dists[k] = (ni * D[merge_i, k] + nj * D[merge_j, k]) / (ni + nj)
            elif linkage == 'ward':
                ni = len(clusters[merge_i])
                nj = len(clusters[merge_j])
                nk = len(clusters[k])
                new_dists[k] = (
                    (ni + nk) / (ni + nj + nk) * D[merge_i, k] +
                    (nj + nk) / (ni + nj + nk) * D[merge_j, k] -
                    nk      / (ni + nj + nk) * D[merge_i, merge_j]
                )

        # Update data structures
        clusters[new_id] = clusters[merge_i] + clusters[merge_j]
        active.remove(merge_i)
        active.remove(merge_j)
        active.append(new_id)

        # Expand D with new row/col
        D_new = np.full((len(D) + 1, len(D) + 1), np.inf)
        D_new[:len(D), :len(D)] = D
        for k, dist in new_dists.items():
            D_new[new_id, k] = dist
            D_new[k, new_id] = dist

        D = D_new

    return linkage_matrix


def cut_tree(linkage_matrix, n_clusters):
    """Cut the dendrogram to produce a flat clustering."""
    n = len(linkage_matrix) + 1  # number of original points
    # Each step in the linkage_matrix corresponds to a merge
    # Stop after (n - n_clusters) merges
    cuts = n_clusters
    # Use a union-find structure
    parent = list(range(2 * n - 1))
    for step, (i, j, h, size) in enumerate(linkage_matrix):
        if n - 1 - step < cuts:
            break
        new_id = n + step
        parent[int(i)] = new_id
        parent[int(j)] = new_id

    def find(x):
        while parent[x] != x:
            parent[x] = parent[parent[x]]
            x = parent[x]
        return x

    return [find(i) for i in range(n)]
```

## ✦ Compared to K-Means and GMM

|Property|K-Means|GMM|Hierarchical|
|---|---|---|---|
|Requires $k$ upfront|Yes|Yes|No (choose at cut time)|
|Cluster shape|Spherical|Ellipsoidal|Depends on linkage|
|Soft assignments|No|Yes|No|
|Probabilistic model|No|Yes|No|
|Deterministic|No (init)|No (init)|Yes (given $d$, linkage)|

|Hierarchical output|No|No|Yes|
|Handles noise|No|No|No (HDBSCAN does)|

The key unique value proposition of hierarchical clustering: **it reveals all scales of structure without committing to $k$**, and the result is interpretable as a tree.

## ✦ Practical Guidance

**When to use hierarchical clustering:**

- You do not know $k$ and want to explore cluster structure visually.
- You need a tree representation (phylogenetics, taxonomy, customer hierarchy).
- $n$ is modest ($n \lesssim 5000$) and interpretability matters.
- The ground truth clusters are non-convex and you suspect single linkage will capture them.

**When not to use it:**

- $n > 10^4$: use HDBSCAN, K-Means, or mini-batch variants.
- You need cluster probabilities: use GMM.
- You need online/streaming clustering: use MiniBatchKMeans or BIRCH.

**Feature scaling:** Required. Like all distance-based methods, hierarchical clustering is not scale-invariant. Always standardise features before computing the distance matrix (unless using a domain-specific metric that already accounts for scale).

**Linkage choice heuristic:**

- Default: Ward's (compact, equal-size clusters, Euclidean data).
- Non-Euclidean or precomputed distance matrix: average or complete.
- Known non-convex structure: single.
- High noise: avoid single; use complete or Ward's.
