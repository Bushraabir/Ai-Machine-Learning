
> _"Supervised learning is told what to find. Clustering finds structure in data that no one labelled — and must decide, from first principles, what 'structure' even means."_

---

## ✦ What Is Clustering?

Clustering is the task of partitioning a dataset $\mathcal{D} = {x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n}$, $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$, into groups such that points within a group are more **similar** to each other than to points in other groups.

Unlike supervised learning, there is no target $y_i$. The algorithm must discover structure using only the geometry of the data. This raises an immediate philosophical question: what does "similar" mean? The answer is not universal — it depends on the geometry of the problem and the intended use. Different definitions of similarity lead to fundamentally different algorithms, each capable of finding qualitatively different structures.

Formally, clustering seeks a mapping:

$$c: {1, \ldots, n} \to {1, \ldots, K}$$

where $c(i)$ is the cluster assignment of point $x_i$, and $K$ is the number of clusters (sometimes given, sometimes inferred from data).

---

## ✦ The Three Questions Every Clustering Algorithm Answers

Before choosing an algorithm, every clustering problem requires three explicit design decisions:

**1. What is the similarity (or distance) measure?**

- Euclidean distance: $|x - x'|_2$ — appropriate for isotropic, real-valued data
- Cosine similarity: $\frac{x \cdot x'}{|x||x'|}$ — appropriate for text, high-dimensional sparse vectors
- Correlation distance: $1 - \text{corr}(x, x')$ — appropriate for time-series, when magnitude is irrelevant
- Mahalanobis distance: $\sqrt{(x-x')^T \Sigma^{-1}(x-x')}$ — accounts for feature covariance
- Custom domain kernels: for graphs, sequences, trees

The choice of distance metric is **a stronger prior than the choice of algorithm**. Two algorithms with the same distance metric on the same data will often agree. The same algorithm with different distance metrics can give completely different clusters.

**2. What is the cluster shape assumption?**

|Algorithm|Assumed Shape|Can Find|
|---|---|---|
|K-Means|Spherical, equal variance|Convex globular clusters|
|GMM|Ellipsoidal (general covariance)|Any ellipsoidal shape|
|Hierarchical (Ward)|Roughly spherical|Compact clusters|
|DBSCAN|Arbitrary|Any shape connected by density|
|Spectral Clustering|Connected regions (graph-based)|Non-convex, manifold-shaped|

**3. What is the criterion for a "good" clustering?**

- Minimise within-cluster variance: K-Means objective
- Maximise likelihood of a generative model: GMM / EM
- Minimise a linkage criterion: hierarchical clustering
- Maximise density connectivity: DBSCAN
- Each criterion formalises a different notion of what "structure" means

## ✦ The Cluster Validity Problem

There is no universal ground truth for clustering. You cannot ask "is this clustering correct?" in the same way you ask "does this classifier have 95% accuracy?" You can only ask: does this clustering satisfy some criterion of quality?

### Internal Validity Indices (no ground truth needed)

**Silhouette Score** — the canonical measure of cluster quality. For point $x_i$ assigned to cluster $C_k$:

$$a(i) = \frac{1}{|C_k| - 1}\sum_{j \in C_k, j \neq i} d(x_i, x_j) \quad \text{(mean intra-cluster distance)}$$

$$b(i) = \min_{C_{k'} \neq C_k} \frac{1}{|C_{k'}|}\sum_{j \in C_{k'}} d(x_i, x_j) \quad \text{(mean distance to nearest other cluster)}$$

$$s(i) = \frac{b(i) - a(i)}{\max(a(i), b(i))} \in [-1, 1]$$

$s(i) \approx 1$: well inside its cluster. $s(i) \approx 0$: on the boundary between clusters. $s(i) < 0$: probably in the wrong cluster.

The mean silhouette score over all points measures overall clustering quality. Maximise this over $K$ to choose the number of clusters.

**Davies-Bouldin Index** — ratio of within-cluster scatter to between-cluster separation. Lower is better.

$$DB = \frac{1}{K}\sum_{k=1}^K \max_{k' \neq k} \frac{s_k + s_{k'}}{d(c_k, c_{k'})}$$

where $s_k$ is the mean distance of cluster $k$'s points to its centroid, and $d(c_k, c_{k'})$ is the distance between centroids.

**Calinski-Harabasz Index** (Variance Ratio Criterion):

$$CH = \frac{\text{tr}(B_K)}{\text{tr}(W_K)} \cdot \frac{n - K}{K - 1}$$

where $B_K$ is the between-cluster scatter matrix and $W_K$ is the within-cluster scatter matrix. Higher is better.

### External Validity Indices (requires ground truth labels)

When ground truth cluster labels are available (e.g., for algorithm evaluation), use:

**Adjusted Rand Index (ARI):**

$$\text{ARI} = \frac{\text{RI} - \mathbb{E}[\text{RI}]}{\max(\text{RI}) - \mathbb{E}[\text{RI}]} \in [-1, 1]$$

The Rand Index counts agreeing pairs (both same cluster or both different) divided by total pairs. ARI adjusts for chance. ARI = 1 is perfect; ARI = 0 is random; ARI < 0 is worse than random.

**Normalised Mutual Information (NMI):**

$$\text{NMI}(C, C^_) = \frac{2 \cdot I(C; C^_)}{H(C) + H(C^*)} \in [0, 1]$$

where $C$ is the predicted clustering and $C^*$ is the ground truth.

**Fowlkes-Mallows Index:**

$$\text{FM} = \sqrt{\frac{TP}{TP + FP} \cdot \frac{TP}{TP + FN}}$$

where TP/FP/FN are defined over pairs of points (do they agree on same-cluster vs. different-cluster?).

```python
from sklearn.metrics import (
    silhouette_score, davies_bouldin_score, calinski_harabasz_score,
    adjusted_rand_score, normalized_mutual_info_score
)

# Internal (no ground truth)
sil = silhouette_score(X, labels)
db  = davies_bouldin_score(X, labels)
ch  = calinski_harabasz_score(X, labels)

print(f"Silhouette:      {sil:.4f}  (higher is better, max 1)")
print(f"Davies-Bouldin:  {db:.4f}  (lower is better)")
print(f"Calinski-Harabasz: {ch:.1f}  (higher is better)")

# External (requires true_labels from a benchmark)
ari = adjusted_rand_score(true_labels, labels)
nmi = normalized_mutual_info_score(true_labels, labels)
print(f"ARI: {ari:.4f},  NMI: {nmi:.4f}")
```

## ✦ Choosing $K$: The Number of Clusters

One of the most practical problems in clustering is choosing $K$. There is no universally correct answer, but several principled methods exist.

### The Elbow Method

Plot the within-cluster sum of squares (WCSS / inertia) as a function of $K$:

$$\text{WCSS}(K) = \sum_{k=1}^K \sum_{x_i \in C_k} |x_i - \mu_k|^2$$

WCSS always decreases as $K$ increases (more clusters = smaller clusters = tighter fit). Look for the "elbow" — the point where marginal gain from adding one more cluster drops sharply. The elbow is often ambiguous.

### Silhouette Analysis

Compute the mean silhouette score for each $K$. Choose $K$ that maximises it. More reliable than the elbow method, but slower ($O(n^2)$ per $K$).

### Gap Statistic (Tibshirani et al., 2001)

Compare the WCSS of the clustering to its expected value under a null reference distribution (uniform data):

$$\text{Gap}(K) = \mathbb{E}_{\text{null}}[\log \text{WCSS}(K)] - \log \text{WCSS}(K)$$

The optimal $K$ is the smallest $K$ such that $\text{Gap}(K) \geq \text{Gap}(K+1) - s_{K+1}$, where $s_{K+1}$ is the simulation standard error. Statistically grounded but expensive (requires running clustering on many null reference datasets).

### BIC / AIC for Probabilistic Models

For GMM, use BIC (Bayesian Information Criterion) or AIC:

$$\text{BIC} = -2\log \hat{L} + p \log n$$ $$\text{AIC} = -2\log \hat{L} + 2p$$

where $\hat{L}$ is the maximised log-likelihood and $p$ is the number of free parameters. Choose $K$ that minimises BIC (preferred — penalises complexity more strongly than AIC for large $n$). This is principled model selection, not a heuristic.

```python
from sklearn.mixture import GaussianMixture
import numpy as np

K_range = range(1, 15)
bic_scores = []

for K in K_range:
    gmm = GaussianMixture(n_components=K, covariance_type='full',
                          random_state=42)
    gmm.fit(X)
    bic_scores.append(gmm.bic(X))

best_K = K_range[np.argmin(bic_scores)]
print(f"BIC-optimal K: {best_K}")
```

## ✦ The Curse of Dimensionality in Clustering

Clustering degrades gracefully in low dimensions and catastrophically in high dimensions. The reason is the same as for KNN (see [[07.k-Nearest Neighbours|k-Nearest Neighbours]]): in high dimensions, all points become approximately equidistant.

**Formal statement:** For $n$ points drawn uniformly from the $d$-dimensional unit hypercube, the ratio of the maximum to minimum pairwise distance converges to 1 as $d \to \infty$:

$$\frac{\max_{i,j} |x_i - x_j| - \min_{i,j} |x_i - x_j|}{\min_{i,j} |x_i - x_j|} \to 0 \quad \text{as } d \to \infty$$

When all pairwise distances are approximately equal, the notion of "nearest neighbour" is meaningless — and clustering by proximity is meaningless.

**Practical consequences:**

- K-Means WCSS improves only marginally from 1 cluster to $K$ clusters in high dimensions
- Silhouette scores are unreliable above ~50 dimensions
- Euclidean distance loses discriminative power; cosine similarity often works better

**Remedies:**

- Dimensionality reduction before clustering: PCA, UMAP (see [[04.Uniform Manifold Approximation and Projection (UMAP)|UMAP]])
- Use a distance metric appropriate for the domain (cosine for text)
- Subspace clustering: cluster in relevant low-dimensional subspaces

The common pipeline for high-dimensional clustering:

```
Raw data (high-dim) → PCA / UMAP (reduce to 10–50 dims) → Cluster
```

## ✦ Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

Clustering is not a peripheral topic — it is foundational to how modern AI systems discover and represent structure.

**Self-supervised learning.** Contrastive methods (SimCLR, MoCo) learn representations by pulling augmented views of the same image together and pushing different images apart — this is clustering in the representation space, done implicitly. SWAV explicitly assigns images to cluster prototypes as part of its training objective.

**Vector quantisation and codebooks.** VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) replaces the continuous latent space of a VAE with a discrete codebook — a set of $K$ learnable cluster centroids. The encoder maps inputs to their nearest centroid. The codebook is updated by K-Means-like updates. This is clustering inside a deep generative model, enabling discrete representations for audio (WaveNet) and images (DALL-E v1).

**K-Means as a neural network initialisation.** The weights of the first layer of a neural network can be initialised by running K-Means on the training data and using the resulting centroids. This was important for early deep learning (RBMs, autoencoders). Less critical now but conceptually illuminating: neurons in deep networks often behave as soft cluster detectors.

**Topic modelling as probabilistic clustering.** Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) is a hierarchical probabilistic model that clusters documents into topics and words into topics simultaneously. It extends GMM to the discrete, bag-of-words setting. Modern neural topic models (ProdLDA, CTM) replace LDA's mean-field inference with amortised variational inference using neural networks.

**Clustering in reinforcement learning.** In model-based RL, clustering is used to discretise continuous state spaces into abstract states — the basis for options frameworks and hierarchical RL. In reward shaping, density-based clustering identifies unexplored regions of state space for curiosity-driven exploration.
