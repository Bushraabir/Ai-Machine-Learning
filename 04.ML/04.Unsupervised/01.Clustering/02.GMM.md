## §1 — Motivation: What K-Means Cannot Do

K-Means makes three hard assumptions baked into its objective function:

1. **Hard assignments** — every point belongs to exactly one cluster with certainty.
2. **Spherical geometry** — clusters are balls ($\Sigma_k = \sigma^2 I$, isotropic covariance).
3. **Equal weight** — all clusters are implicitly treated as equally probable.

Real data violates all three. GMM relaxes each explicitly:

|Property|K-Means|GMM|
|---|---|---|
|Assignments|Hard: $r_{ik} \in {0,1}$|Soft: $r_{ik} \in [0,1]$, $\sum_k r_{ik} = 1$|
|Cluster shape|Spherical (isotropic)|Ellipsoidal (full $\Sigma_k$)|
|Cluster weight|Implicit equal weight|Explicit mixing weights $\pi_k$|
|Objective|Geometric: minimise WCSS|Probabilistic: maximise log-likelihood|

The price of this generality: we cannot solve the learning problem in closed form. We need the **Expectation-Maximisation (EM)** algorithm.

> _Professor's marginal note: K-Means is EM in disguise. It is exactly the limit of GMM-EM as $\sigma^2 \to 0$. We will make this precise in §10. Always derive the general case first, recover the special case second._

## §2 — The Generative Model

A GMM posits that each data point $x_i \in \mathbb{R}^d$ was generated by the following **stochastic process**:

**Step 1.** Draw a discrete latent variable $z_i \in {1, \ldots, K}$ from a categorical distribution: $$P(z_i = k) = \pi_k, \quad \pi_k \geq 0, \quad \sum_{k=1}^K \pi_k = 1$$

The $\pi_k$ are called **mixing weights**.

**Step 2.** Draw the observed point from the selected Gaussian component: $$x_i \mid z_i = k ;\sim; \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \Sigma_k)$$

The **joint distribution** is: $$P(x_i, z_i = k) = \pi_k ,\mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)$$

**Marginalising** over the unobserved $z_i$:

$$\boxed{p(x_i) = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k ,\mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}$$

where the $d$-dimensional Gaussian density is: $$\mathcal{N}(x \mid \mu, \Sigma) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(x-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)\right)$$

The **model parameters** are $\theta = {\pi_k, \mu_k, \Sigma_k}_{k=1}^K$, totalling $(K-1) + Kd + K \cdot d(d+1)/2$ free parameters.

**Why $z_i$ is latent:** We observe $x_i$ but not $z_i$. If we observed $z_i$, the MLE would be trivial: fit each component independently to its assigned points. The whole difficulty of GMM is that $z_i$ is hidden.

## §3 — Why Direct MLE Fails: The Log-Sum Problem

Given $n$ i.i.d. observations, the **log-likelihood** is:

$$\ell(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log p(x_i) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \log \left( \sum_{k=1}^{K} \pi_k ,\mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k) \right)$$

Setting $\partial \ell / \partial \mu_k = 0$:

$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mu_k} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \underbrace{\frac{\pi_k ,\mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}{\sum_j \pi_j \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_j, \Sigma_j)}}_{=:; r_{ik}} \cdot \Sigma_k^{-1}(x_i - \mu_k) = 0$$

This gives $\mu_k = \frac{\sum_i r_{ik} x_i}{\sum_i r_{ik}}$, but $r_{ik}$ itself depends on $\mu_k$ through the denominator. **The equations are coupled and self-referential.** No closed-form solution exists.

The obstacle is structural: the log sits _outside_ the sum over $k$. This is the defining signature of a **latent variable model** — the component label $z_i$ is unobserved, so we cannot separate the likelihood into $K$ independent parts.

## §4 — The EM Algorithm: Derivation from First Principles

### 4.1 Constructing a Tractable Lower Bound

Introduce an arbitrary distribution $q_i(z_i)$ over the latent variable for each observation. Using the identity $\log x = \log(x \cdot \frac{q_i(k)}{q_i(k)})$:

$$\ell(\theta) = \sum_i \log \sum_k q_i(k) \cdot \frac{\pi_k \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}{q_i(k)}$$

Apply **Jensen's inequality** (log is concave, so $\log \mathbb{E}[X] \geq \mathbb{E}[\log X]$):

$$\ell(\theta) \geq \sum_i \sum_k q_i(k) \log \frac{\pi_k \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}{q_i(k)} ;\equiv; \mathcal{L}(q, \theta)$$

This lower bound is the **Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO)**. Expanding:

$$\mathcal{L}(q, \theta) = \underbrace{\sum_i \sum_k q_i(k) \log P(x_i, z_i=k;\theta)}_{\text{expected complete-data log-likelihood}} ;+; \underbrace{\sum_i H(q_i)}_{\text{entropy of } q}$$

The **gap** between $\ell$ and $\mathcal{L}$ is:

$$\ell(\theta) - \mathcal{L}(q, \theta) = \sum_i D_{\mathrm{KL}}\bigl(q_i ,|, P(z_i \mid x_i, \theta)\bigr) \geq 0$$

This gap vanishes — the bound is **tight** — when $q_i(k) = P(z_i = k \mid x_i, \theta)$, the true posterior over latent variables.

### 4.2 Coordinate Ascent on the ELBO

**E-step (Expectation):** Fix $\theta$. Maximise $\mathcal{L}(q, \theta)$ over $q$ by zeroing the KL gap: $$q_i(k) ;\leftarrow; P(z_i = k \mid x_i, \theta)$$ After this step, $\mathcal{L}(q^{\text{new}}, \theta) = \ell(\theta)$ — the bound is tight.

**M-step (Maximisation):** Fix $q$. Maximise $\mathcal{L}(q, \theta)$ over $\theta$. The entropy $H(q_i)$ does not depend on $\theta$, so: $$\theta ;\leftarrow; \arg\max_\theta \sum_i \sum_k q_i(k) \log P(x_i, z_i=k;\theta)$$ This is the **expected complete-data log-likelihood** and has a closed-form maximiser for exponential family models (including Gaussians).

### 4.3 Monotonic Convergence Proof

$$\ell(\theta^{t+1}) ;\geq; \mathcal{L}(q^{t+1}, \theta^{t+1}) ;\geq; \mathcal{L}(q^{t+1}, \theta^t) ;=; \ell(\theta^t)$$

- **First $\geq$:** $\mathcal{L}$ is always a lower bound on $\ell$ (KL $\geq 0$).
- **Second $\geq$:** The M-step maximises $\mathcal{L}$ over $\theta$ at fixed $q^{t+1}$.
- **Equality:** After the E-step, $q^{t+1}$ makes the bound tight at $\theta^t$.

Therefore $\ell(\theta)$ is **monotonically non-decreasing** at every EM iteration. Since it is bounded above (likelihoods $\leq 1$ for proper densities — with the caveat of singularities), it converges. **But only to a local maximum or saddle point**, not the global maximum in general.

## §5 — EM for GMM: Explicit Update Equations

### 5.1 E-Step: Compute Responsibilities

The posterior over latent variables is obtained by Bayes' theorem:

$$\boxed{r_{ik} = P(z_i = k \mid x_i, \theta) = \frac{\pi_k ,\mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)}{\displaystyle\sum_{j=1}^{K} \pi_j ,\mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_j, \Sigma_j)}}$$

Numerator: prior weight times likelihood for component $k$. Denominator: marginal likelihood (normalisation). Each $r_{ik} \in [0,1]$ and $\sum_k r_{ik} = 1$ for every $i$.

**Interpretation:** $r_{ik}$ is the probability that point $x_i$ was generated by component $k$, given the current parameter estimates.

### 5.2 M-Step: Update Parameters

Define the **effective cluster size**: $N_k = \sum_{i=1}^{n} r_{ik}$.

**Mixing weights** (Lagrange multiplier for $\sum_k \pi_k = 1$):

$$\boxed{\pi_k = \frac{N_k}{n}}$$

_Full derivation:_ The relevant terms in $\mathcal{L}$ are $\sum_k N_k \log \pi_k$. Form the Lagrangian $\mathcal{L}' = \sum_k N_k \log \pi_k - \lambda(\sum_k \pi_k - 1)$. Setting $\partial \mathcal{L}' / \partial \pi_k = N_k/\pi_k - \lambda = 0$ gives $\pi_k = N_k/\lambda$. The constraint $\sum_k \pi_k = 1$ gives $\lambda = \sum_k N_k = n$.

**Means:**

$$\boxed{\mu_k = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} r_{ik} , x_i}{N_k}}$$

_Full derivation:_ The relevant terms are $-\frac{1}{2}\sum_i r_{ik}(x_i - \mu_k)^T\Sigma_k^{-1}(x_i-\mu_k)$. Differentiating with respect to $\mu_k$: $\Sigma_k^{-1}\sum_i r_{ik}(x_i - \mu_k) = 0$. Since $\Sigma_k$ is positive definite (invertible), the factor in parentheses must vanish: $\sum_i r_{ik} x_i = N_k \mu_k$.

**Covariances:**

$$\boxed{\Sigma_k = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^{n} r_{ik} ,(x_i - \mu_k)(x_i - \mu_k)^T}{N_k}}$$

_Full derivation:_ Differentiate with respect to the precision matrix $\Lambda_k = \Sigma_k^{-1}$, using the matrix identity $\partial \log|\Lambda_k|/\partial \Lambda_k = \Lambda_k^{-1} = \Sigma_k$ and $\partial, \mathrm{tr}(\Lambda_k A)/\partial \Lambda_k = A^T$. Setting the gradient to zero and solving gives the weighted sample covariance above.

**Universal pattern:** All three M-step updates are **responsibility-weighted MLE estimates**. Hard counts $n_k = |{i: z_i = k}|$ are replaced by soft counts $N_k$; sums over hard-assigned points are replaced by responsibility-weighted sums over all points. This pattern holds for any exponential family component in an EM derivation.

### 5.3 Complete Algorithm

```
Input:  data X ∈ ℝ^{n×d}, components K, tolerance ε
Output: parameters {π_k, μ_k, Σ_k}

Initialise:
  μ_k ← K-Means centroids (recommended)
  Σ_k ← I_d  for all k
  π_k ← 1/K  for all k

Repeat until |ℓ^{t+1} − ℓ^t| < ε:

  ── E-STEP ──
  For i = 1..n, k = 1..K:
    log_num_{ik} = log π_k + log N(x_i | μ_k, Σ_k)
  For i = 1..n:
    log_denom_i  = logsumexp_k [log_num_{ik}]
    r_{ik}       = exp(log_num_{ik} − log_denom_i)

  ── M-STEP ──
  For k = 1..K:
    N_k   = Σ_i r_{ik}
    π_k   = N_k / n
    μ_k   = (Σ_i r_{ik} x_i) / N_k
    Σ_k   = (Σ_i r_{ik} (x_i − μ_k)(x_i − μ_k)^T) / N_k
    Σ_k  += ε_reg · I               ← numerical floor

  ── LOG-LIKELIHOOD ──
  ℓ = Σ_i log_denom_i

Return {π_k, μ_k, Σ_k}
```

## §6 — Numerical Issues

### 6.1 Log-Sum-Exp Trick (Non-Negotiable)

Computing $\sum_k \pi_k \mathcal{N}(x_i \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k)$ naively underflows to zero for $d \gtrsim 20$, because $\mathcal{N}$ can evaluate to numbers like $10^{-500}$.

**Solution:** Work in log space, using the log-sum-exp identity:

$$\log \sum_k e^{a_k} = a^* + \log \sum_k e^{a_k - a^_}, \quad a^_ = \max_k a_k$$

All subtracted exponentials are $\leq 1$ — no overflow. Adding $a^*$ back via the outer log is exact.

```python
def logsumexp(log_vals):            # naive implementation
    a_star = np.max(log_vals)
    return a_star + np.log(np.sum(np.exp(log_vals - a_star)))
```

Use `scipy.special.logsumexp` in production.

### 6.2 Covariance Collapse (Singularities)

A component can **degenerate**: $\mu_k \to x_j$ for some $j$, $r_{jk} \to 1$, $\Sigma_k \to 0$, and $\log \mathcal{N}(x_j \mid \mu_k, \Sigma_k) \to +\infty$. The log-likelihood has genuine singularities at these degenerate solutions — $\ell \to \infty$ trivially but meaninglessly.

**Detection:** $N_k < 1$ signals collapse (a component has claimed less than one effective point).

**Fixes:**

- **Covariance flooring:** Add $\epsilon_{\text{reg}} I$ (e.g., $\epsilon = 10^{-6}$) after each M-step.
- **Component reinitialisation:** If $N_k < 1$, resample $\mu_k$ from a high-residual data point.
- **MAP / Bayesian GMM:** Place an inverse-Wishart prior on $\Sigma_k$. The MAP M-step adds a prior pseudo-count, bounding $\Sigma_k$ away from zero.

### 6.3 Identifiability and Label Switching

Permuting component labels leaves $\ell(\theta)$ unchanged — the likelihood is invariant under the group of $K!$ permutations. This creates $K!$ equivalent global maxima. It does not affect clustering quality but complicates parameter interpretation across runs and is a serious issue for Bayesian inference (MCMC chains switch labels mid-run).

## §7 — Worked Example: Two Gaussians in 1D

Let $K=2$, $n=5$ points: $X = {1.0,; 1.5,; 5.0,; 5.5,; 6.0}$.

**Initialise:** $\mu_1 = 2.0$, $\mu_2 = 5.0$, $\sigma_1^2 = \sigma_2^2 = 1.0$, $\pi_1 = \pi_2 = 0.5$.

**E-step, $x = 1.0$:**

$$\mathcal{N}(1.0 \mid 2.0,, 1) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-0.5} \approx 0.2420, \qquad \mathcal{N}(1.0 \mid 5.0,, 1) = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-8} \approx 3.35 \times 10^{-4}$$

$$r_{1,1} = \frac{0.5 \times 0.2420}{0.5 \times 0.2420 + 0.5 \times 3.35 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 0.9986$$

Point $x = 1.0$ is $99.86%$ assigned to component 1. By symmetry, $x = 5.5$ and $x = 6.0$ will be $>99%$ assigned to component 2.

**M-step (approximate, after computing all responsibilities):**

$$N_1 \approx 2.0, \quad N_2 \approx 3.0, \quad \pi_1 \approx 0.4, \quad \pi_2 \approx 0.6$$ $$\mu_1 \approx \frac{1.0 + 1.5}{2} = 1.25, \qquad \mu_2 \approx \frac{5.0 + 5.5 + 6.0}{3} = 5.5$$

After a few iterations: $\mu_1 \approx 1.25$, $\mu_2 \approx 5.5$, $\sigma_1^2 \approx 0.06$, $\sigma_2^2 \approx 0.17$, $\pi_1 = 0.4$, $\pi_2 = 0.6$.

**Lesson:** The log-likelihood at the true clusters far exceeds the initialisation value. For well-separated components, EM converges in very few iterations because responsibilities quickly polarise.

## §8 — Covariance Structures

The GMM family is parameterised by the constraint placed on $\Sigma_k$. sklearn exposes this via `covariance_type`:

|Type|$\Sigma_k$ structure|Free params/component|Cluster geometry|
|---|---|---|---|
|`'spherical'`|$\sigma_k^2 I$|1 scalar|Sphere, varying radius|
|`'diag'`|$\mathrm{diag}(\sigma_{k1}^2, \ldots, \sigma_{kd}^2)$|$d$ scalars|Axis-aligned ellipsoid|
|`'tied'`|$\Sigma$ (shared across components)|$d(d+1)/2$ total|Same shape, different centres|
|`'full'`|$\Sigma_k$ unrestricted SPD|$d(d+1)/2$ each|Arbitrary ellipsoid|

`'spherical'` is K-Means in the soft-assignment limit (see §10). `'full'` is the most expressive but most prone to overfitting and covariance collapse. Choosing `covariance_type` is a **regularisation decision**: more constrained = fewer parameters = more bias, less variance.

## §9 — Model Selection: How Many Components?

Unlike K-Means where the elbow method is heuristic, GMM admits **principled information-theoretic model selection**.

### 9.1 Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC)

$$\mathrm{BIC}(K) = \ln(n) \cdot |\theta_K| - 2\ell(\hat{\theta}_K)$$

where $|\theta_K|$ is the number of free parameters for $K$ components. Choose $K$ minimising BIC.

**Why $\ln(n)$?** BIC approximates $-2\log P(\mathcal{D})$ (the marginal likelihood) under a broad prior via Laplace approximation. The $\ln(n)$ penalty grows with the dataset size, making BIC a consistent model selector: as $n \to \infty$, it selects the true $K$ (if the model is well-specified).

### 9.2 Akaike Information Criterion (AIC)

$$\mathrm{AIC}(K) = 2|\theta_K| - 2\ell(\hat{\theta}_K)$$

AIC uses a fixed penalty of 2 per parameter, derived as an estimate of out-of-sample prediction error (Kullback-Leibler divergence to the true model). AIC is **not consistent** for model selection — it tends to overestimate $K$. Prefer BIC for structure discovery; AIC if you care about predictive accuracy.

### 9.3 Bayesian / Variational GMM

A principled alternative: place a Dirichlet prior on $\pi$ (and conjugate priors on $\mu_k$, $\Sigma_k$) and train via **Variational Bayes**. Components with low effective count will have their $\pi_k \to 0$, automatically pruning excess components. Start with $K_{\max}$ and let the model kill off unneeded ones.

Available in sklearn as `BayesianGaussianMixture(n_components=K_max)`.

## §10 — K-Means as a Special Case of GMM-EM

**Setup:** Consider GMM-EM with equal mixing weights ($\pi_k = 1/K$) and isotropic, equal covariances ($\Sigma_k = \sigma^2 I$).

**E-step responsibilities** become:

$$r_{ik} = \frac{\exp!\left(-\dfrac{|x_i - \mu_k|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)}{\displaystyle\sum_j \exp!\left(-\dfrac{|x_i - \mu_j|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)}$$

This is a **softmax over negative squared distances** with temperature $\sigma^2$.

**Take the limit $\sigma^2 \to 0$:**

For any point $x_i$, let $k^* = \arg\min_k |x_i - \mu_k|^2$. For any $k \neq k^*$:

$$\frac{e^{-|x_i - \mu_k|^2 / 2\sigma^2}}{e^{-|x_i - \mu_{k^_}|^2 / 2\sigma^2}} = e^{-(|x_i - \mu_k|^2 - |x_i - \mu_{k^_}|^2)/2\sigma^2} \xrightarrow{\sigma^2 \to 0} 0$$

since the exponent $\to -\infty$. Therefore:

$$r_{ik} ;\xrightarrow{\sigma^2 \to 0}; \begin{cases} 1 & k = \arg\min_j |x_i - \mu_j|^2 \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

This is precisely the K-Means **ASSIGN step**. The **M-step** $\mu_k = \frac{\sum_i r_{ik} x_i}{N_k}$ remains the mean of hard-assigned points.

**Consequence:** The K-Means objective WCSS = $\sum_i \sum_k r_{ik} |x_i - \mu_k|^2$ is (up to constants) the negative expected complete-data log-likelihood of this degenerate GMM. **K-Means minimises a geometric surrogate; GMM maximises the true log-likelihood.**

## §11 — GMM as a Density Estimator

GMM is not just a clustering algorithm — it is a **universal density approximator**:

> _Any continuous density on a compact domain can be approximated arbitrarily closely by a GMM with sufficiently many components_ (density approximation theorem for mixtures).

As a density estimator, GMM competes with Kernel Density Estimation (KDE):

||KDE|GMM|
|---|---|---|
|Number of parameters|$n$ (one kernel per data point)|$O(Kd^2)$ (fixed $K$)|
|Evaluation cost per query|$O(nd)$|$O(Kd^2)$|
|Out-of-sample|Direct|Direct|
|Interpretability|None|Components have semantic meaning|
|Model selection|Bandwidth $h$|Number of components $K$|

For large $n$, GMM is far more efficient to evaluate. For small datasets or complex density shapes, KDE with a well-chosen bandwidth can be more accurate.

## §12 — Connection to AI / Deep Learning

### 12.1 The ELBO in Variational Autoencoders

The VAE training objective is:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{VAE}}(\phi, \theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi(z|x) ,|, p(z))$$

This is identical in structure to the GMM ELBO: $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{GMM}}(q, \theta) = \sum_i \mathbb{E}_{q_i}[\log P(x_i, z_i)] + H(q_i)$$

The difference: in GMM, the E-step has a closed-form posterior (Bayes' theorem). In a VAE, the posterior $p_\theta(z|x)$ is intractable for a nonlinear decoder, so $q_\phi(z|x)$ is a learned Gaussian approximation (the encoder network). **The VAE is exactly EM with an amortised, approximate E-step.**

**GMM-VAE:** The standard VAE uses a single isotropic Gaussian prior $p(z) = \mathcal{N}(0, I)$. Replacing this with a GMM prior $p(z) = \sum_k \pi_k \mathcal{N}(\mu_k, \Sigma_k)$ gives a structured latent space with cluster geometry, enabling conditional generation and disentanglement.

### 12.2 EM as a Universal Pattern in Deep Learning

The EM structure — alternate between inferring latent variables and updating parameters — appears throughout:

|Algorithm|E-step analogue|M-step analogue|
|---|---|---|
|GMM-EM|Compute responsibilities $r_{ik}$|Update ${\pi_k, \mu_k, \Sigma_k}$|
|K-Means|Hard-assign cluster labels|Update centroids|
|HMM Baum-Welch|Forward-backward algorithm|Update transition/emission params|
|DeepCluster (Caron 2018)|CNN forward pass + K-Means on features|Backprop with pseudo-labels|
|EM for RL (REPS)|Weight trajectories by advantage|Update policy distribution|
|MoE (Mixture of Experts)|Gate network output|Expert network update|

### 12.3 Mixture of Experts in Large Language Models

Modern LLMs (e.g., Mixtral, Switch Transformer) use a **Mixture of Experts** layer: a gate network routes each token to one or more "expert" FFN layers. The gating mechanism is a soft (top-$k$) assignment — analogous to the E-step responsibilities. Training balances load across experts, echoing the equal-weight challenge in GMM initialisation.

### 12.4 VQ-VAE and Discrete Latents

VQ-VAE (van den Oord et al., 2017) replaces the continuous latent space of a VAE with a **discrete codebook** learned by K-Means (which, as shown in §10, is a hard-assignment GMM). The encoder output is quantised to the nearest codebook vector via straight-through gradient estimation. This connects the GMM density model directly to modern generative modelling.

### 12.5 Speaker Verification (GMM-UBM)

Before neural embeddings, speaker verification used GMM-UBM: a **Universal Background Model** (large GMM trained on many speakers) adapted per-speaker via MAP. The MAP adaptation update is a convex combination of the UBM parameters and the speaker's MLE parameters — a direct application of the GMM MAP M-step. Many design principles (adaptation, scoring, normalisation) from GMM-UBM persist in modern x-vector and ECAPA-TDNN systems.

## §13 — Implementation from Scratch

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy.stats import multivariate_normal
from scipy.special import logsumexp


def gmm_em(
    X: np.ndarray,
    K: int,
    max_iter: int = 200,
    tol: float = 1e-6,
    reg_covar: float = 1e-6,
    random_state: int = 42,
) -> dict:
    """
    Full-covariance GMM via EM with K-Means warm start.

    Returns dict: pi, mu, Sigma, responsibilities, log_likelihood_history
    """
    rng = np.random.default_rng(random_state)
    n, d = X.shape

    # ── Initialisation: K-Means warm start ──────────────────────────────────
    from sklearn.cluster import KMeans
    km = KMeans(n_clusters=K, n_init=5, random_state=random_state).fit(X)
    mu    = km.cluster_centers_.copy()                            # (K, d)
    pi    = np.array([(km.labels_ == k).mean() for k in range(K)])  # (K,)
    Sigma = np.array([
        np.cov(X[km.labels_ == k].T) + reg_covar * np.eye(d)
        for k in range(K)
    ])                                                             # (K, d, d)

    log_likelihoods = []

    for iteration in range(max_iter):

        # ── E-STEP ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
        # log π_k + log N(x_i | μ_k, Σ_k), shape (n, K)
        log_prob = np.column_stack([
            np.log(pi[k] + 1e-300) +
            multivariate_normal.logpdf(X, mean=mu[k], cov=Sigma[k])
            for k in range(K)
        ])

        # log-sum-exp per row → log-responsibilities
        log_norm = logsumexp(log_prob, axis=1, keepdims=True)    # (n, 1)
        log_r    = log_prob - log_norm                           # (n, K)
        r        = np.exp(log_r)                                 # responsibilities

        ll = log_norm.sum()
        log_likelihoods.append(float(ll))
        if iteration > 0 and abs(log_likelihoods[-1] - log_likelihoods[-2]) < tol:
            break

        # ── M-STEP ──────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
        N = r.sum(axis=0)                                        # (K,) effective sizes
        N = np.maximum(N, 1e-10)                                 # guard against collapse

        pi = N / n

        for k in range(K):
            rk   = r[:, k]                                       # (n,)
            mu[k] = (rk[:, None] * X).sum(axis=0) / N[k]

            diff      = X - mu[k]                                # (n, d)
            Sigma[k]  = (rk[:, None] * diff).T @ diff / N[k]
            Sigma[k] += reg_covar * np.eye(d)                    # numerical floor

    return {
        "pi": pi, "mu": mu, "Sigma": Sigma,
        "responsibilities": r,
        "log_likelihood_history": log_likelihoods,
    }


def gmm_predict(X: np.ndarray, params: dict) -> np.ndarray:
    """Hard cluster assignments: argmax over responsibilities."""
    K = len(params["pi"])
    log_prob = np.column_stack([
        np.log(params["pi"][k] + 1e-300) +
        multivariate_normal.logpdf(X, mean=params["mu"][k], cov=params["Sigma"][k])
        for k in range(K)
    ])
    return np.argmax(log_prob, axis=1)
```

**Model selection with BIC:**

```python
bics = []

for k in K_range:
    gm = GaussianMixture(n_components=k, covariance_type='full', n_init=5).fit(X)
    bics.append(gm.bic(X))

print(f"Best K by BIC: {best_k}")   # should recover 3
```

## §14 — Complexity Summary

|Operation|Time|Space|
|---|---|---|
|E-step (full covariance)|$O(nKd^2)$|$O(nK)$|
|M-step: $\Sigma_k$ update|$O(nKd^2)$|$O(Kd^2)$|
|Per full EM iteration|$O(nKd^2)$|$O(nK + Kd^2)$|
|Diagonal covariance|$O(nKd)$|$O(nK + Kd)$|
|BIC over $K_{\max}$ models|$O(K_{\max} \cdot T \cdot nKd^2)$|—|

For $n = 10^4$, $K = 10$, $d = 50$: full-covariance EM is feasible in seconds. For $d = 1000$: covariance matrices are $10^6$ entries each — use `'diag'` or apply PCA first.

## §15 — GMM vs. K-Means vs. DBSCAN

|Property|K-Means|GMM|DBSCAN|
|---|---|---|---|
|Assignments|Hard|Soft (probabilistic)|Hard + noise class|
|Cluster shape|Spherical|Ellipsoidal (full $\Sigma_k$)|Arbitrary|
|Model|Geometric|Probabilistic|Density-based|
|Chooses $K$?|No|No (BIC/Variational assists)|No ($\epsilon$, MinPts)|
|Outliers|Assigned to nearest centroid|Low responsibility|Explicitly labelled "noise"|
|Scalability|$O(nKd)$ per iter|$O(nKd^2)$ per iter|$O(n \log n)$ with index|
|Uncertainty|None|Full posterior $r_{ik}$|None|

**Choosing rule:** know $K$ and trust spherical clusters → K-Means; want uncertainty and ellipsoidal clusters, use BIC → GMM; arbitrary shapes or outliers, unknown $K$ → DBSCAN / HDBSCAN.

## §16 — Common Mistakes and Gotchas

1. **Not standardising features.** A feature with scale $10^4$ will dominate the Mahalanobis distance computation, forcing all variance onto one dimension even with full covariance.
    
2. **Not adding covariance regularisation.** Covariance collapse is a real failure mode. `reg_covar=1e-6` is not a hack — it is numerically necessary for high-dimensional or low-$n$ settings.
    
3. **Using a single EM run.** EM converges to a local maximum. Always use `n_init ≥ 5`. sklearn's `GaussianMixture` default is `n_init=1` — override it.
    
4. **Using AIC instead of BIC for component selection.** AIC overestimates $K$ in finite samples. BIC is consistent.
    
5. **Interpreting component parameters across runs as comparable.** Label-switching: component 0 in run A may correspond to component 2 in run B. Only within-run parameters are meaningful.
    
6. **Assuming convergence means global optimality.** EM monotonically increases $\ell$ but only to a local maximum. Multiple random restarts and the best $\ell$ across them is the standard mitigation.
    
7. **Using GMM on non-Gaussian clusters expecting it to handle them.** Full-covariance GMM fits ellipsoidal Gaussians. For crescent-shaped or toroidal clusters, GMM will fail exactly as K-Means does, just with slightly more flexibility. GMM is not a universal shape detector.
