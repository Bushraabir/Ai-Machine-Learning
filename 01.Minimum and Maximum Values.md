## ┬¦1. The Absolute Extrema ŌĆö Definitions

Let $f$ be a function defined on a domain $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}$.

> **Definition 1.1 (Absolute Maximum).** A value $f(c)$ is the **absolute maximum** of $f$ on $D$ if $$f(c) \geq f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in D.$$

> **Definition 1.2 (Absolute Minimum).** A value $f(c)$ is the **absolute minimum** of $f$ on $D$ if $$f(c) \leq f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in D.$$

The absolute maximum and minimum are collectively called **extreme values** or **absolute extrema**.

**Remark.** Existence is _not_ guaranteed in general. Consider:

- $f(x) = x$ on $(0,1)$: no maximum, no minimum ŌĆö the interval is open.
- $f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$ on $(0, \infty)$: no minimum.
- $f(x) = \sin(x)$ on $\mathbb{R}$: maximum value $1$ achieved at infinitely many points, but the _set_ of maxima is well-defined.

This motivates the fundamental existence theorem.

## ┬¦2. The Extreme Value Theorem

> **Theorem 2.1 (Extreme Value Theorem ŌĆö EVT).** If $f$ is **continuous** on a **closed, bounded** interval $[a, b]$, then $f$ attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on $[a, b]$.
> 
> That is, there exist $c, d \in [a, b]$ such that $$f(c) \leq f(x) \leq f(d) \quad \text{for all } x \in [a, b].$$

**Why both conditions are necessary:**

|Condition dropped|Counterexample|What fails|
|---|---|---|
|Continuity|$f(x) = \begin{cases} x & x \in (0,1] \ 2 & x=0 \end{cases}$ on $[0,1]$|Min not attained|
|Closed interval|$f(x) = x$ on $(0,1)$|Neither attained|
|Bounded interval|$f(x) = x$ on $[0, \infty)$|No max|

**Proof sketch.** The proof uses two deep facts from real analysis:

1. A continuous image of a compact set is compact (HeineŌĆōCantor).
2. A compact subset of $\mathbb{R}$ is closed and bounded, hence contains its supremum and infimum.

Since $[a,b]$ is compact and $f$ is continuous, $f([a,b])$ is compact, so $\sup f([a,b])$ and $\inf f([a,b])$ are attained. $\blacksquare$

## ┬¦3. Local Extrema

Global search is hard. We begin locally.

> **Definition 3.1 (Local Maximum).** $f$ has a **local maximum** at $c$ if there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $$f(c) \geq f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in (c-\delta, c+\delta) \cap D.$$

> **Definition 3.2 (Local Minimum).** $f$ has a **local minimum** at $c$ if there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $$f(c) \leq f(x) \quad \text{for all } x \in (c-\delta, c+\delta) \cap D.$$

**Intuition.** A local max is the highest point in its _neighborhood_ ŌĆö it doesn't need to beat everyone, just its neighbors. This is critical in optimization: most algorithms find local extrema, and global optimization is generally NP-hard.

---

## ┬¦4. Fermat's Theorem ŌĆö The Critical Point Condition

> **Theorem 4.1 (Fermat's Theorem).** If $f$ has a local extremum at $c$ and $f'(c)$ exists, then $$f'(c) = 0.$$

**Proof.** Suppose $f$ has a local maximum at $c$. Then for all $x$ sufficiently close to $c$: $$f(x) - f(c) \leq 0.$$

**Case 1:** $x > c$. Then $\dfrac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} \leq 0$, so taking $x \to c^+$: $$f'(c) = \lim_{x \to c^+} \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} \leq 0.$$

**Case 2:** $x < c$. Then $\dfrac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} \geq 0$, so taking $x \to c^-$: $$f'(c) = \lim_{x \to c^-} \frac{f(x)-f(c)}{x-c} \geq 0.$$

Since $f'(c)$ exists and satisfies both $f'(c) \leq 0$ and $f'(c) \geq 0$, we conclude $f'(c) = 0$. The local minimum case is symmetric. $\blacksquare$

> **Warning (Converse is FALSE).** $f'(c) = 0$ does **not** imply $c$ is an extremum.
> 
> _Example:_ $f(x) = x^3$. Then $f'(0) = 0$, but $x=0$ is an **inflection point**, not an extremum. The function changes sign of its derivative here ŌĆö it's increasing on both sides of $0$.

## ┬¦5. Critical Numbers

> **Definition 5.1.** A number $c$ in the domain of $f$ is a **critical number** (or **critical point**) if either:
> 
> 1. $f'(c) = 0$, or
> 2. $f'(c)$ **does not exist**.

**Why include non-differentiable points?** Fermat's theorem requires $f'(c)$ to exist. But extrema can occur where the derivative fails to exist.

_Example:_ $f(x) = |x|$ on $[-1, 1]$. The minimum is at $x = 0$, but $f'(0)$ doesn't exist (corner point). So $0$ is a critical number.

_Example:_ $f(x) = x^{1/3}$. Then $f'(x) = \frac{1}{3}x^{-2/3}$, which doesn't exist at $x = 0$. The point $x=0$ is a critical number (it's an inflection point here, but could be an extremum in other examples).

> **Corollary 5.1.** If $f$ has a local extremum at $c$, then $c$ is a critical number of $f$.

_This is the contrapositive of Fermat's theorem combined with the observation about non-differentiable points._

---

## ┬¦6. The Closed Interval Method

The EVT guarantees extrema exist on $[a,b]$. The **Closed Interval Method** finds them:

> **Algorithm 6.1 (Closed Interval Method).** To find the absolute extrema of a continuous $f$ on $[a,b]$:
> 
> 1. Find all critical numbers of $f$ in $(a, b)$.
> 2. Evaluate $f$ at each critical number.
> 3. Evaluate $f$ at the endpoints: $f(a)$ and $f(b)$.
> 4. The **largest** value is the absolute maximum; the **smallest** is the absolute minimum.

**Why does this work?** By EVT, the absolute extrema exist. By Corollary 5.1, any interior extremum must be a critical point. The endpoints are candidates by default (boundary of the domain). Exhausting all candidates and comparing gives the answer.

## ┬¦7. Worked Examples

### Example 7.1 ŌĆö Polynomial

Find the absolute extrema of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 1$ on $[-\frac{1}{2}, 4]$.

**Step 1.** $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x = 3x(x-2)$.

Critical numbers in $(-\frac{1}{2}, 4)$: $x = 0$ and $x = 2$.

**Step 2ŌĆō3.** Evaluate:

|$x$|$f(x)$|
|---|---|
|$-\frac{1}{2}$|$(-\frac{1}{2})^3 - 3(\frac{1}{4}) + 1 = -\frac{1}{8} - \frac{3}{4} + 1 = \frac{1}{8}$|
|$0$|$0 - 0 + 1 = 1$|
|$2$|$8 - 12 + 1 = -3$|
|$4$|$64 - 48 + 1 = 17$|

**Absolute maximum:** $f(4) = 17$. **Absolute minimum:** $f(2) = -3$.

### Example 7.2 ŌĆö With Non-differentiable Point

Find the absolute extrema of $f(x) = |x^2 - 1|$ on $[-2, 2]$.

$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 - 1 & |x| \geq 1 \ 1 - x^2 & |x| < 1 \end{cases}$

$f'(x) = \begin{cases} 2x & |x| > 1 \ -2x & |x| < 1 \ \text{DNE} & x = \pm 1 \end{cases}$

Critical numbers: $x = 0$ (where $f' = 0$), $x = \pm 1$ (where $f'$ DNE).

|$x$|$f(x)$|
|---|---|
|$-2$|$3$|
|$-1$|$0$|
|$0$|$1$|
|$1$|$0$|
|$2$|$3$|

**Absolute maximum:** $f(\pm 2) = 3$. **Absolute minimum:** $f(\pm 1) = 0$.

## ┬¦9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _This is not a digression. This entire chapter IS the theoretical foundation of machine learning._

### 9.1 Loss Minimization as the Core Problem

In supervised learning, we train a model with parameters $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^n$ by minimizing a **loss function**: $$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{m} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \ell(f_\theta(x^{(i)}), y^{(i)})$$

where $\ell$ is a pointwise loss (e.g., squared error, cross-entropy). **Finding optimal $\theta^*$ is exactly an absolute minimum problem.**

The critical point condition becomes: $$\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta^*) = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{(gradient is the zero vector)}$$

This is Fermat's theorem in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

### 9.2 Gradient Descent ŌĆö Iterative Fermat

Since $\mathcal{L}$ is typically non-convex and high-dimensional (GPT-4 has ~$10^{12}$ parameters), we can't solve $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 0$ analytically. Instead:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the **learning rate**. This is a discrete approximation to following the steepest descent path toward a local minimum. The fixed points of this update ($\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t$) are exactly the critical points.

**Variants:**

- **SGD** (Stochastic Gradient Descent): Uses a minibatch to approximate $\nabla \mathcal{L}$
- **Adam**: Adapts the learning rate per parameter using first and second moment estimates
- **RMSProp**: Divides by running average of squared gradients

All seek the same thing: a point where $\nabla \mathcal{L} \approx \mathbf{0}$.

### 9.3 The Local vs Global Minimum Problem

A deep neural network's loss landscape is non-convex, so gradient descent finds **local minima**, not necessarily global ones. This is the direct multi-dimensional generalization of our observation that $f'(c) = 0$ does not guarantee a global minimum.

**Surprising empirical finding (2014ŌĆōpresent):** In practice, local minima of large neural networks tend to have _similar_ loss values to the global minimum. The more dangerous critical points are **saddle points** ŌĆö where $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 0$ but the Hessian $H$ is indefinite (some eigenvalues positive, some negative). These are the analog of $f(x) = x^3$ at $x=0$.

**Theorem (informal, Dauphin et al., 2014).** In high dimensions, most critical points with high loss are saddle points, not local minima. True local minima with high loss are exponentially rare.

### 9.4 Second-Order Methods and the Hessian

The **second derivative test** in 1D:

- $f''(c) > 0$ at a critical point $\Rightarrow$ local min
- $f''(c) < 0 \Rightarrow$ local max
- $f''(c) = 0 \Rightarrow$ inconclusive

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the **Hessian matrix** $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$ plays this role:

- $H$ positive definite $\Rightarrow$ local min
- $H$ negative definite $\Rightarrow$ local max
- $H$ indefinite $\Rightarrow$ saddle point

**Newton's method** for optimization uses the full Hessian: $$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - H^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

This converges quadratically near a minimum but costs $O(n^3)$ to invert $H$ ŌĆö infeasible for large networks. **Quasi-Newton methods** (L-BFGS) approximate $H^{-1}$ cheaply.

### 9.5 Convex Optimization ŌĆö When Local = Global

If $\mathcal{L}$ is **convex** (e.g., linear regression with MSE loss, logistic regression), then: $$\text{every local minimum is a global minimum}$$

This is the analog of: a function with $f'' > 0$ everywhere on an interval has at most one local (= global) minimum. Convexity is defined by: $$\mathcal{L}(\lambda \theta_1 + (1-\lambda)\theta_2) \leq \lambda \mathcal{L}(\theta_1) + (1-\lambda)\mathcal{L}(\theta_2), \quad \lambda \in [0,1]$$

**Support Vector Machines** and **linear models** exploit convexity to guarantee finding the global minimum. This is why they have convergence guarantees that deep networks lack.

### 9.6 Regularization as a Constraint

**L2 regularization** (weight decay) solves: $$\min_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta) + \frac{\lambda}{2}|\theta|^2$$

This is equivalent to finding the minimum of the unconstrained loss subject to $|\theta| \leq R$ for some $R$ ŌĆö a constrained optimization problem. The method of **Lagrange multipliers** (multivariable calculus) formalizes this connection, and the critical point condition becomes the **KKT conditions** in convex optimization.

## ┬¦10. The Second Derivative Test (Preview)

_Full treatment in the next section, but the setup:_

If $f'(c) = 0$, how do we classify $c$?

> **Theorem 10.1 (Second Derivative Test).** Suppose $f''$ is continuous near $c$ and $f'(c) = 0$:
> 
> - If $f''(c) > 0$: $c$ is a **local minimum** ($f$ is concave up)
> - If $f''(c) < 0$: $c$ is a **local maximum** ($f$ is concave down)
> - If $f''(c) = 0$: **inconclusive** (need higher derivatives or sign analysis)

**Intuition.** Near a local min, the graph looks like a upward parabola ŌĆö $f'' > 0$ means concave up, i.e., the function curves away from the tangent line _upward_. This is exactly why gradient descent converges: near a true minimum, the loss surface curves upward in all directions.

## ┬¦12. Common Mistakes and Traps

> **Trap 1.** Assuming $f'(c) = 0$ implies $c$ is an extremum. _Counterexample:_ $f(x) = x^3$ at $x=0$. Always check sign change of $f'$.

> **Trap 2.** Forgetting to check endpoints on closed intervals. _The absolute max/min can occur at $a$ or $b$._

> **Trap 3.** Applying EVT to open intervals or discontinuous functions. _The theorem's conclusion is false without both hypotheses._

> **Trap 4.** In ML: confusing "gradient is zero" with "global minimum found." _In non-convex problems, $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 0$ only guarantees a critical point._

> **Trap 5.** Using the second derivative test when $f''(c) = 0$. _The test is inconclusive. Use first derivative sign analysis instead._
