## 0. Preliminary Remarks

The predator–prey system is one of the great examples of applied mathematics: simple equations, profound behavior, no closed-form solution — yet completely tractable by qualitative analysis. It sits at the intersection of differential equations, dynamical systems, linear algebra (linearization), and nonlinear analysis.

This note builds the theory from scratch: model derivation → equilibria → linearization → phase plane → full qualitative picture → extensions → connections to ML.

The mathematics here lives in **ODE theory** and **dynamical systems**, but the tools are exactly those of [[Linear Equations]] — applied locally near equilibria via the Jacobian.

---

## 1. Model Derivation — From Biology to Equations

### 1.1 The Two Species

Let:

- $x(t) \geq 0$ = population of **prey** (e.g., rabbits) at time $t$
- $y(t) \geq 0$ = population of **predators** (e.g., foxes) at time $t$

### 1.2 Modelling Assumptions

|Assumption|Mathematical consequence|
|---|---|
|Prey grow exponentially without predators|$\dot{x} = ax$ for some $a > 0$|
|Each predator-prey encounter reduces prey at rate proportional to both populations|$-bxy$ term in $\dot{x}$|
|Predators die exponentially without prey|$-cy$ term in $\dot{y}$|
|Each encounter increases predator population|$+dxy$ term in $\dot{y}$|

The term $xy$ is the **law of mass action**: encounters occur at rate proportional to the product of both populations (like molecules colliding in a gas).

### 1.3 The Lotka–Volterra Equations

> **Definition 1.1 (Lotka–Volterra System).** The _predator–prey system_ with parameters $a, b, c, d > 0$ is: $$\boxed{\dot{x} = ax - bxy, \qquad \dot{y} = -cy + dxy.}$$ Equivalently, with $\mathbf{u} = (x, y)^T$: $$\dot{\mathbf{u}} = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{u}) = \begin{pmatrix} ax - bxy \ -cy + dxy \end{pmatrix}.$$

**Historical note.** Vito Volterra derived these equations in 1926 to explain oscillations in Adriatic fish populations following World War I. Alfred Lotka independently derived them in 1910 in a chemical context. The mathematics predates the biology.

**Factored form** (useful for finding equilibria): $$\dot{x} = x(a - by), \qquad \dot{y} = y(-c + dx).$$

## 2. Equilibria

> **Definition 2.1.** A point $\mathbf{u}^* = (x^_, y^_)$ is an _equilibrium_ (fixed point) of the system if $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{u}^*) = \mathbf{0}$, i.e., the population does not change.

Setting $\dot{x} = 0$ and $\dot{y} = 0$:

$$x(a - by) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad y(-c + dx) = 0.$$

**Case 1:** $x = 0$ and $y = 0$. Equilibrium at the **origin** $E_0 = (0, 0)$.

**Case 2:** $x \neq 0$ and $y \neq 0$. Then $a - by = 0 \Rightarrow y^* = a/b$, and $-c + dx = 0 \Rightarrow x^* = c/d$.

Equilibrium at the **coexistence point**: $$\boxed{E^* = \left(\frac{c}{d},\ \frac{a}{b}\right).}$$

> **Remark.** There are exactly two equilibria in the first quadrant (the biologically relevant region $x \geq 0, y \geq 0$). The entire qualitative theory will determine what happens near each.

## 3. Linearization — The Jacobian

The key tool for understanding behavior near equilibria is **linearization**: approximate the nonlinear system by its best linear approximation at the equilibrium point. This is exactly the multivariable Taylor theorem.

> **Definition 3.1 (Jacobian).** The Jacobian matrix of $\mathbf{F} = (F_1, F_2)$ at a point $(x_0, y_0)$ is: $$J(x_0, y_0) = \begin{pmatrix} \partial F_1/\partial x & \partial F_1/\partial y \ \partial F_2/\partial x & \partial F_2/\partial y \end{pmatrix}.$$

For the Lotka–Volterra system: $$J(x, y) = \begin{pmatrix} a - by & -bx \ dy & -c + dx \end{pmatrix}.$$

> **Theorem 3.2 (Hartman–Grobman).** Near a **hyperbolic** equilibrium $\mathbf{u}^_$ (one where $J(\mathbf{u}^_)$ has no eigenvalues with zero real part), the nonlinear system is topologically conjugate to its linearization $\dot{\boldsymbol{\xi}} = J(\mathbf{u}^*)\boldsymbol{\xi}$.

In plain language: the eigenvalues of $J$ at an equilibrium determine the local qualitative behavior of the full nonlinear system.

### 3.1 Linearization at $E_0 = (0, 0)$

$$J(0,0) = \begin{pmatrix} a & 0 \ 0 & -c \end{pmatrix}.$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = a > 0$, $\lambda_2 = -c < 0$.

Since eigenvalues have opposite signs, $E_0$ is a **saddle point** — unstable. Solutions starting near the origin move away (except along the $y$-axis, which is the stable manifold).

**Biological meaning:** If both populations are near zero, prey grow (positive eigenvalue in $x$-direction) and predators die (negative eigenvalue in $y$-direction). The extinction state is unstable: any small prey population recovers.

### 3.2 Linearization at $E^* = (c/d, a/b)$

$$J!\left(\frac{c}{d}, \frac{a}{b}\right) = \begin{pmatrix} a - b\cdot\frac{a}{b} & -b\cdot\frac{c}{d} \ d\cdot\frac{a}{b} & -c + d\cdot\frac{c}{d} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -\frac{bc}{d} \ \frac{ad}{b} & 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$

**Characteristic equation:** $$\det(J - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 + \frac{bc}{d} \cdot \frac{ad}{b} = \lambda^2 + ac = 0.$$

Eigenvalues: $$\lambda = \pm i\sqrt{ac}.$$

Pure imaginary eigenvalues! The linearization gives a **center**.

> **Warning.** Here Hartman–Grobman does NOT apply — $E^*$ is not hyperbolic (eigenvalues have zero real part). The linearization says "center," but the nonlinear system could be a center, a stable spiral, or an unstable spiral. We need a different tool: a conserved quantity.

## 4. The Conserved Quantity — Proof of Periodic Orbits

> **Theorem 4.1.** The function $$H(x, y) = d,x - c\ln x + b,y - a\ln y$$ is a **first integral** of the Lotka–Volterra system: $\frac{d}{dt}H(x(t), y(t)) = 0$ along every solution with $x, y > 0$.

**Proof.** Compute $\dot{H}$ along solutions: $$\frac{dH}{dt} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial x}\dot{x} + \frac{\partial H}{\partial y}\dot{y} = \left(d - \frac{c}{x}\right)(ax - bxy) + \left(b - \frac{a}{y}\right)(-cy + dxy).$$

Expand each term: $$\left(d - \frac{c}{x}\right)(ax - bxy) = adx - bdxy - ac + bcy,$$ $$\left(b - \frac{a}{y}\right)(-cy + dxy) = -bcy + bdxy + ac - adx.$$

Sum: $$\dot{H} = (adx - bdxy - ac + bcy) + (-bcy + bdxy + ac - adx) = 0. \quad \blacksquare$$

> **Corollary 4.2.** Every solution with $x(0), y(0) > 0$ is a **closed orbit** (periodic solution). The period and amplitude depend on the initial condition.

**Proof.** $H$ is constant along each trajectory. The level sets ${H(x,y) = k}$ are closed curves in the first quadrant (one verifies this by analyzing $H$'s critical points — its unique minimum in the first quadrant is at $E^*$). Since trajectories follow level sets, they are closed. $\blacksquare$

## 5. Phase Plane Analysis

### 5.1 Nullclines

> **Definition 5.1.** The _$x$-nullcline_ is ${(x,y) : \dot{x} = 0}$. The _$y$-nullcline_ is ${(x,y) : \dot{y} = 0}$.

For Lotka–Volterra:

- **$x$-nullclines:** $x = 0$ (the $y$-axis) and $y = a/b$ (horizontal line)
- **$y$-nullclines:** $y = 0$ (the $x$-axis) and $x = c/d$ (vertical line)

The equilibria are exactly the intersections of $x$- and $y$-nullclines.

### 5.2 Direction Field — Four Quadrant Analysis

The nullclines divide the first quadrant into four regions. In each, determine signs of $\dot{x}$ and $\dot{y}$:

```
         y
         │
    a/b ─┼────────────────────────────────
         │  II           │   I
         │  ẋ < 0        │   ẋ > 0
         │  ẏ > 0        │   ẏ > 0
         │  (prey ↓,     │   (prey ↑,
         │  pred ↑)      │   pred ↑)
         │               │
         │               E* = (c/d, a/b)
         │               │
         │  III          │   IV
         │  ẋ < 0        │   ẋ > 0
         │  ẏ < 0        │   ẏ < 0
         │  (prey ↓,     │   (prey ↑,
         │  pred ↓)      │   pred ↓)
         │               │
    ─────┼───────────────┼──────────────── x
         0              c/d
```

Reading the orbit direction **counterclockwise**: I → II → III → IV → I.

> **Mnemonic.** When prey are plentiful and predators few (Quadrant IV), predators thrive and prey decline → Quadrant I. When predators are many and prey scarce (Quadrant II), predators starve → Quadrant III. The system perpetually chases its tail.

### 5.3 Time Series — Oscillations with Phase Lag

The populations oscillate periodically. The predator population **lags** the prey population by approximately a quarter period:

```
Population
    │
    │    ╭─── Prey x(t)
    │   ╱ ╲              ╭──
    │  ╱   ╲            ╱
    │ ╱     ╲    ╭─── Predator y(t)
    │╱       ╲  ╱  ╲
    │         ╲╱    ╲
    │               ╲   ╭──
    │                ╲ ╱
    ├─────────────────────────── t
         T/4   T/2  3T/4   T

Phase lag: y peaks after x peaks.
```

**Why the lag?** Prey grow → more food for predators → predators grow → prey decline → predators decline → prey recover. Each step takes time. The quarter-period lag is a signature of the $\pm i\sqrt{ac}$ eigenvalues (phase angle = $\pi/2$).

## 6. The Conservation Law — Deeper Analysis

### 6.1 Shape of Level Sets of $H$

At the coexistence equilibrium $E^* = (c/d, a/b)$, compute $H$: $$H^* = H(E^*) = d\cdot\frac{c}{d} - c\ln\frac{c}{d} + b\cdot\frac{a}{b} - a\ln\frac{a}{b} = c + a - c\ln\frac{c}{d} - a\ln\frac{a}{b}.$$

For any other initial condition $(x_0, y_0)$, $H(x_0, y_0) > H^_$ (since $E^_$ is the global minimum of $H$ in the first quadrant — proven below), and the orbit is a closed curve enclosing $E^*$.

> **Lemma 6.1.** $E^*$ is the unique global minimum of $H$ in $\mathbb{R}_{>0}^2$.

**Proof.** $\partial H/\partial x = d - c/x = 0 \Rightarrow x = c/d$, $\partial H/\partial y = b - a/y = 0 \Rightarrow y = a/b$. So $E^_$ is the unique critical point. The Hessian: $$\nabla^2 H = \begin{pmatrix} c/x^2 & 0 \ 0 & a/y^2 \end{pmatrix}\Bigg|_{E^_} = \begin{pmatrix} d^2/c & 0 \ 0 & b^2/a \end{pmatrix},$$ which is positive definite (diagonal with positive entries). So $E^*$ is a strict local minimum. Since $H \to +\infty$ as $x \to 0^+$, $x \to +\infty$, $y \to 0^+$, or $y \to +\infty$, it is the global minimum. $\blacksquare$

### 6.2 Average Populations Over One Period

> **Theorem 6.2.** The time-average of $x(t)$ over one period $T$ is $\bar{x} = c/d$. The time-average of $y(t)$ over one period is $\bar{y} = a/b$.

**Proof.** Integrate $\dot{x}/x = a - by$ over one period: $$\int_0^T \frac{\dot{x}}{x},dt = \int_0^T (a - by),dt = aT - b\int_0^T y,dt.$$

The left side is $[\ln x]_0^T = 0$ (periodic). So $b\int_0^T y,dt = aT$, giving $\bar{y} = a/b$. Similarly for $\bar{x} = c/d$. $\blacksquare$

> **Corollary (Volterra's Principle).** The time-averaged populations equal the equilibrium values regardless of amplitude. Uniform harvesting of prey shifts the equilibrium and thus the average predator population — a counterintuitive consequence.

## 7. Stability Analysis — Summary

|Equilibrium|Jacobian eigenvalues|Type|Stability|
|---|---|---|---|
|$E_0 = (0,0)$|$\lambda = a > 0,\ \lambda = -c < 0$|Saddle|Unstable|
|$E^* = (c/d, a/b)$|$\lambda = \pm i\sqrt{ac}$|Center (nonlinear)|Neutrally stable (Lyapunov stable, not asymptotically stable)|

> **Definition 7.1.** An equilibrium is **Lyapunov stable** if solutions starting close remain close for all time, but do not necessarily converge to it. It is **asymptotically stable** if solutions also converge to it as $t \to \infty$.

$E^*$ is Lyapunov stable (orbits stay on their level curve of $H$) but **not** asymptotically stable (no convergence — the orbit is periodic).

## 8. Extensions and Modifications

### 8.1 Logistic Prey (More Realistic)

Replace exponential prey growth with logistic growth: $$\dot{x} = ax\left(1 - \frac{x}{K}\right) - bxy, \qquad \dot{y} = -cy + dxy.$$

The carrying capacity $K$ limits prey growth. The coexistence equilibrium becomes: $$x^* = \frac{c}{d}, \quad y^* = \frac{a}{b}\left(1 - \frac{c}{dK}\right).$$

(Exists only if $c/d < K$, i.e., predators can survive before prey hit carrying capacity.)

The Jacobian at $E^*$ now has eigenvalues with **negative real part** — the equilibrium becomes asymptotically stable. Orbits spiral inward: populations don't oscillate forever, they settle to coexistence. This is the **Poincaré–Bendixson** picture.

### 8.2 Holling Type II Functional Response

Real predators don't hunt indefinitely faster as prey increase — they saturate (handling time). Replace $bxy$ with: $$\frac{bxy}{1 + bh,x},$$ where $h$ is handling time per prey. This is **Holling Type II** and introduces a new equilibrium branch and can generate **limit cycles** (Hopf bifurcation).

### 8.3 The General Form

$$\dot{x} = x,f(x,y), \qquad \dot{y} = y,g(x,y).$$

Both $f$ and $g$ are general functions. Lotka–Volterra is the case $f = a - by$, $g = -c + dx$ — both **linear** in $(x,y)$. The entire rich theory of predator–prey models generalizes from this observation.

## 9. Numerical Example — Concrete Computation

Parameters: $a = 1,\ b = 0.1,\ c = 1.5,\ d = 0.075$.

**Equilibrium:** $$E^* = \left(\frac{1.5}{0.075},\ \frac{1}{0.1}\right) = (20,\ 10).$$

**Jacobian at $E^*$:** $$J(20,10) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -0.1 \cdot 20 \ 0.075 \cdot 10 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -2 \ 0.75 & 0 \end{pmatrix}.$$

**Eigenvalues:** $$\lambda^2 + 2 \cdot 0.75 = 0 \implies \lambda^2 = -1.5 \implies \lambda = \pm i\sqrt{1.5} \approx \pm 1.225i.$$

**Period of linearized oscillation:** $$T \approx \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{ac}} = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{1 \cdot 1.5}} \approx 5.13\ \text{time units}.$$

**Initial condition:** $(x_0, y_0) = (25, 10)$. Prey start above equilibrium, predators at equilibrium.

Conservation value: $$H(25, 10) = 0.075 \cdot 25 - 1.5\ln 25 + 0.1 \cdot 10 - 1\ln 10 \approx 1.875 - 4.832 + 1 - 2.303 = -4.26.$$

Compare with $H(E^*) = 0.075 \cdot 20 - 1.5\ln 20 + 0.1 \cdot 10 - 1\ln 10 \approx 1.5 - 4.497 + 1 - 2.303 = -4.30$.

Since $H(x_0, y_0) > H(E^*)$ (less negative), the orbit is outside the equilibrium — a closed curve slightly larger.

## 11. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 11.1 Lotka–Volterra as a Dynamical System — Neural ODE

The Lotka–Volterra equations are an **autonomous ODE**: $\dot{\mathbf{u}} = \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{u})$. This is exactly the structure of a **Neural ODE** (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2018):

> **Definition.** A _Neural ODE_ parameterizes the vector field $\mathbf{F}$ by a neural network $f_\theta$: $$\dot{\mathbf{h}}(t) = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}(t), t), \qquad \mathbf{h}(t_1) = \text{ODESolve}(f_\theta, \mathbf{h}(t_0), t_0, t_1).$$

One trains $\theta$ to fit observed trajectories. If the true dynamics are Lotka–Volterra, a Neural ODE should discover the vector field $\mathbf{F}(x,y) = (x(a-by),\ y(-c+dx))$.

The conservation law $H$ is a **first integral** — a Neural ODE with a Hamiltonian structure (Hamiltonian Neural Network, Greydanus et al., 2019) can be constrained to discover and preserve $H$ exactly.

### 11.2 Predator–Prey Dynamics in GANs

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) pit a **generator** $G$ against a **discriminator** $D$ in a minimax game: $$\min_G \max_D\ \mathbb{E}[\log D(x)] + \mathbb{E}[\log(1 - D(G(z)))].$$

The training dynamics of GANs are notoriously **oscillatory** and unstable — reminiscent of predator–prey orbits around the Nash equilibrium $(G^_, D^_)$. This is not a coincidence:

> **Theorem (informal).** Near a Nash equilibrium, simultaneous gradient descent on a two-player zero-sum game has eigenvalues $\pm i\omega$ — exactly the pure imaginary eigenvalues of the Lotka–Volterra linearization. Training oscillates; it does not converge.

This is why GAN training is hard and why techniques like **gradient penalty**, **spectral normalization**, and **unrolled optimization** are needed — they add damping (negative real part to eigenvalues) converting the center to a stable spiral.

### 11.3 Competitive Dynamics in Resource Allocation (RL)

In multi-agent reinforcement learning, agents compete for resources. Their populations evolve under replicator dynamics: $$\dot{x}_i = x_i\left(f_i(\mathbf{x}) - \bar{f}(\mathbf{x})\right),$$

where $f_i$ is agent $i$'s fitness and $\bar{f}$ is the population average. This is a **generalization of Lotka–Volterra** to $n$ competing strategies. The game-theoretic equilibria correspond to Nash equilibria; their stability is analyzed via the Jacobian — exactly as in §3.

### 11.4 Ecological Modeling with ML

Modern approaches use ML to **learn** predator–prey dynamics from data:

1. **SINDy (Sparse Identification of Nonlinear Dynamics):** Given time series $(x(t), y(t))$, compute $\dot{x}$ and $\dot{y}$ numerically, then regress $\dot{\mathbf{u}}$ against a library of candidate terms ${x, y, xy, x^2, \ldots}$ using sparse linear regression ($L^1$ regularization). If the true model is Lotka–Volterra, SINDy recovers $\dot{x} = ax - bxy$ exactly.
    
2. **Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs):** Train a neural network $\hat{x}(t), \hat{y}(t)$ to satisfy the Lotka–Volterra ODEs as a penalty in the loss: $$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{|\hat{x}(t_i) - x_i|^2}_{\text{data fit}} + \lambda \underbrace{|\dot{\hat{x}} - \hat{x}(a - b\hat{y})|^2}_{\text{ODE residual}}.$$
    
3. **Conservation-aware learning:** Enforce $H(x(t), y(t)) = \text{const}$ as a hard constraint. This dramatically reduces the hypothesis space and improves generalization.

## 12. Key Theorems — Summary

|Theorem|Statement|
|---|---|
|**Equilibria**|$E_0 = (0,0)$ and $E^* = (c/d, a/b)$|
|**Saddle at origin**|$J(0,0)$ has eigenvalues $+a, -c$: saddle point, unstable|
|**Center at $E^*$**|$J(E^*)$ has eigenvalues $\pm i\sqrt{ac}$: linearized center|
|**Conservation (Thm 4.1)**|$H = dx - c\ln x + by - a\ln y$ is constant along orbits|
|**Periodic orbits (Cor 4.2)**|All orbits in $\mathbb{R}_{>0}^2$ are closed (periodic)|
|**$E^*$ is global min of $H$ (Lemma 6.1)**|Hessian of $H$ at $E^*$ is positive definite|

|**GAN analogy**|Simultaneous gradient descent near Nash $\Leftrightarrow$ LV near $E^*$: eigenvalues $\pm i\omega$|

## 13. Things to Prove Yourself

1. **Verify** by direct computation that $\dot{H} = 0$ along solutions of the Lotka–Volterra system (fill in all algebra from Theorem 4.1).
2. **Prove** Volterra's Principle (Theorem 6.2) for $\bar{x} = c/d$ by integrating $\dot{y}/y$ over one period.
3. **For the logistic-prey system** ($\dot{x} = ax(1 - x/K) - bxy$, $\dot{y} = -cy + dxy$): find the coexistence equilibrium and compute its Jacobian. Show the eigenvalues have negative real part when $c/d < K$, confirming asymptotic stability.
4. **Show** that the linearization $\dot{\boldsymbol{\xi}} = J(E^_)\boldsymbol{\xi}$ with $J(E^_) = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -bc/d \ ad/b & 0\end{pmatrix}$ gives solutions of the form $\boldsymbol{\xi}(t) = e^{i\sqrt{ac},t}\mathbf{v}$ and write out $\mathbf{v}$ explicitly.
5. **Prove** that the function $g(s) = s - 1 - \ln s$ satisfies $g(s) \geq 0$ for all $s > 0$ with equality iff $s = 1$. Use this to show $H(x,y) \geq H(E^_)$ for all $x, y > 0$. (This gives a clean proof that $E^_$ is the global minimum.)
