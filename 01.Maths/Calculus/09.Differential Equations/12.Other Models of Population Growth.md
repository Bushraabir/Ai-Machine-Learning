## 1. The Zoo of Growth Models вЂ” Overview

All population models specify the **per-capita growth rate** $\frac{1}{P}\frac{dP}{dt}$. The models differ in how this rate depends on $P$:

|Model|Per-capita rate $\frac{1}{P}\dot{P}$|ODE|
|---|---|---|
|Malthus (exponential)|$r$ (constant)|$\dot{P} = rP$|
|Logistic (Verhulst)|$r(1 - P/K)$ (linear decay)|$\dot{P} = rP(1-P/K)$|
|Gompertz|$r\ln(K/P)$ (log decay)|$\dot{P} = rP\ln(K/P)$|
|Allee effect|$r(1-P/K)(P/A - 1)$|$\dot{P} = rP(1-P/K)(P/A-1)$|
|Theta-logistic|$r(1-(P/K)^\theta)$|$\dot{P} = rP(1-(P/K)^\theta)$|
|Doomsday|$rP^{1+\epsilon}$|$\dot{P} = rP^{1+\epsilon}$|
|Harvesting|$r(1-P/K) - H/P$|$\dot{P} = rP(1-P/K) - H$|

Each model is a **separable first-order ODE** (or reducible to one). The solution techniques are the same; the qualitative behavior is radically different.

## 2. The Gompertz Model

### 2.1 Setup

Proposed by Benjamin Gompertz (1825), originally for mortality curves. The per-capita growth rate decays **logarithmically** rather than linearly:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\ln\frac{K}{P} \tag{1}$$

Note: $\ln(K/P) = \ln K - \ln P > 0$ when $P < K$, and $< 0$ when $P > K$. So the qualitative behavior is similar to the logistic, but the approach to $K$ is **asymmetric** вЂ” slower from below, faster from above.

### 2.2 Solving the Gompertz ODE

Rewrite: $\ln(K/P) = \ln K - \ln P$.

**Separate:**

$$\frac{dP}{P\ln(K/P)} = r, dt$$

**Substitution:** Let $u = \ln(K/P)$. Then:

$$\frac{du}{dP} = -\frac{1}{P} \implies du = -\frac{dP}{P}$$

So $\frac{dP}{P\ln(K/P)} = \frac{-du}{u}$. The ODE becomes:

$$-\frac{du}{u} = r, dt \implies \frac{du}{u} = -r, dt$$

$$\ln|u| = -rt + C_1 \implies u = Ae^{-rt}$$

Back-substitute $u = \ln(K/P)$:

$$\ln\frac{K}{P} = Ae^{-rt}$$

$$\frac{K}{P} = e^{Ae^{-rt}}$$

$$\boxed{P(t) = K, e^{-Ae^{-rt}}} \tag{2}$$

**Apply $P(0) = P_0$:**

$$P_0 = Ke^{-A} \implies A = \ln\frac{K}{P_0}$$

$$P(t) = K\exp!\left(-\ln!\tfrac{K}{P_0}\cdot e^{-rt}\right) = K\left(\frac{P_0}{K}\right)^{e^{-rt}} \tag{3}$$

### 2.3 Key Properties

- **Equilibria:** $P^* = 0$ (unstable), $P^* = K$ (stable) вЂ” same as logistic.
- **Inflection point:** Set $d^2P/dt^2 = 0$.

From $\dot{P} = rP\ln(K/P)$:

$$\ddot{P} = r\dot{P}\ln\frac{K}{P} + rP \cdot \frac{-\dot{P}}{P} = r\dot{P}\left(\ln\frac{K}{P} - 1\right)$$

Setting $\ddot{P} = 0$ (with $\dot{P} \neq 0$):

$$\ln\frac{K}{P} = 1 \implies P^* = \frac{K}{e} \approx 0.368K$$

The inflection is at $P = K/e$ вЂ” **lower** than the logistic's $K/2$. The Gompertz curve is skewed: it rises steeply early and tails off slowly into $K$.

### 2.4 Applications

- **Tumor growth:** Gompertz fits the growth of solid tumors better than logistic вЂ” they slow down more gradually as they approach their maximum size.
- **Mortality tables:** Gompertz's original use вЂ” human mortality rate increases exponentially with age.
- **Technology adoption:** Product adoption curves often fit Gompertz better than logistic (asymmetric S-curve).

## 3. The Allee Effect

### 3.1 Motivation

The logistic model assumes: more individuals в†’ slower growth (crowding). The **Allee effect** (Warder Clyde Allee, 1931) adds: **too few individuals в†’ slower growth too** (difficulty finding mates, reduced cooperative defense, inbreeding).

This creates a **minimum viable population** $A$ below which the population declines to extinction.

### 3.2 The ODE

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)\left(\frac{P}{A} - 1\right), \quad 0 < A < K \tag{4}$$

**Equilibria:** $P^* = 0$, $P^* = A$, $P^* = K$.

**Stability analysis** вЂ” let $f(P) = rP(1-P/K)(P/A - 1)$:

- $f'(0)$: near $P=0$, $f(P) \approx rP(1)(0/A - 1) = -rP < 0$ в†’ __$P^_ = 0$ is stable_* (extinction attractor).
- $P^* = A$: **unstable** (the tipping point).
- $P^* = K$: **stable** (the survival attractor).

**The Allee threshold $A$ is a separatrix:** populations above $A$ survive; below $A$ they go extinct. This is called the **strong Allee effect**.

### 3.4 Solving the Allee ODE

The ODE (4) is separable. Partial fractions on $\frac{1}{P(1-P/K)(P/A - 1)}$ are messy but tractable. The solution is implicitly defined, and the key qualitative feature is the existence of the threshold $A$ вЂ” the exact formula is less important than the phase line.

### 3.5 Applications

- **Conservation biology:** Minimum viable population estimates for endangered species.
- **Epidemiology:** Below a critical infectious population, an epidemic dies out (Allee-like threshold = herd immunity complement).
- **Invasive species:** A newly introduced population must exceed $A$ to establish.

## 4. The Theta-Logistic (Generalized Logistic)

### 4.1 The ODE

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left[1 - \left(\frac{P}{K}\right)^\theta\right], \quad \theta > 0 \tag{5}$$

The parameter $\theta$ controls the **shape of density dependence**:

- $\theta = 1$: standard logistic.
- $\theta < 1$: density dependence kicks in **early** (when $P$ is still small). Concave per-capita rate.
- $\theta > 1$: density dependence kicks in **late** (population grows almost freely until near $K$). Convex per-capita rate.

### 4.2 Inflection Point

Setting $\ddot{P} = 0$ (similar calculation as before):

$$P^* = K\left(\frac{1}{1+\theta}\right)^{1/\theta}$$

- $\theta = 1$: $P^* = K/2$ вњ“ (logistic)
- $\theta \to 0^+$: $P^* \to K/e$ (approaches Gompertz inflection)
- $\theta \to \infty$: $P^* \to K$ (inflection pushed to the top вЂ” nearly J-shaped)

The theta-logistic is a **one-parameter family interpolating between all major growth curves**.

### 4.3 Solving

The ODE is separable:

$$\frac{dP}{P[1-(P/K)^\theta]} = r, dt$$

Substitute $u = (P/K)^\theta$:

$$du = \theta(P/K)^{\theta-1} \cdot \frac{1}{K}, dP = \frac{\theta}{P}(P/K)^\theta, dP = \frac{\theta u}{P}, dP$$

So $dP/P = du/(\theta u)$:

$$\int \frac{du/(\theta u)}{1 - u} = rt + C$$

$$\frac{1}{\theta}\int \frac{du}{u(1-u)} = rt + C$$

$$\frac{1}{\theta}\left[\ln u - \ln(1-u)\right] = rt + C$$

$$\frac{1}{\theta}\ln\frac{(P/K)^\theta}{1-(P/K)^\theta} = rt + C$$

This defines $P(t)$ implicitly. For $\theta = 1$ it reduces exactly to the logistic solution.

## 5. The Doomsday Model

### 5.1 The ODE

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = kP^{1+\epsilon}, \quad k > 0,, \epsilon > 0 \tag{6}$$

The per-capita rate $kP^\epsilon$ **increases** with $P$ вЂ” no carrying capacity, the population accelerates itself.

### 5.2 Solving (Separable)

$$P^{-(1+\epsilon)}, dP = k, dt$$

$$\int P^{-(1+\epsilon)}, dP = kt + C$$

$$\frac{P^{-\epsilon}}{-\epsilon} = kt + C$$

$$P^{-\epsilon} = -\epsilon kt + C_0$$

Apply $P(0) = P_0$: $C_0 = P_0^{-\epsilon}$:

$$P(t) = \left(P_0^{-\epsilon} - \epsilon k t\right)^{-1/\epsilon} \tag{7}$$

### 5.3 Finite-Time Blow-Up

The solution $P(t) \to \infty$ in **finite time** $t^* = \frac{P_0^{-\epsilon}}{\epsilon k} = \frac{1}{\epsilon k P_0^\epsilon}$.

This is called a **doomsday singularity** вЂ” the population grows so fast it becomes infinite in finite time. Obviously unphysical past some point, but the model fits certain superexponential growth phases (e.g., global human population from 1 AD to 1970, which von Foerster et al. famously fit with $\epsilon \approx 1$ and $t^* \approx 2026$... an eerie coincidence with the present date).

## 6. The Harvesting Model

### 6.1 Constant Harvesting

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) - H, \quad H > 0 \tag{8}$$

**Equilibria:** Set $\dot{P} = 0$:

$$rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) = H \implies \frac{r}{K}P^2 - rP + H = 0$$

$$P^* = \frac{rK \pm \sqrt{r^2K^2 - 4rKH}}{2r} = \frac{K}{2}\left(1 \pm \sqrt{1 - \frac{4H}{rK}}\right)$$

Three regimes:

|Condition|Equilibria|Behavior|
|---|---|---|
|$H < \frac{rK}{4}$|Two real equilibria $P_-^* < P_+^*$|$P_+^_$ stable, $P_-^_$ unstable|
|$H = \frac{rK}{4}$|One equilibrium $P^* = K/2$|Saddle-node bifurcation|
|$H > \frac{rK}{4}$|No real equilibria|$\dot{P} < 0$ always вЂ” extinction|

**Maximum sustainable yield (MSY):** $H^* = rK/4$, achieved at $P = K/2$.

### 6.2 Proportional Harvesting (Threshold Policy)

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) - EP, \quad E > 0$$

$$= P\left[r\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) - E\right] = P\left[(r-E) - \frac{r}{K}P\right]$$

This is still a logistic equation with **modified growth rate** $\tilde{r} = r - E$ and **modified carrying capacity** $\tilde{K} = K(1 - E/r)$ (for $E < r$). Proportional harvesting preserves the logistic structure; constant harvesting does not.

## 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

---

### 8.1 Gompertz Activation and Loss Curves

The Gompertz curve appears in ML as a model for **training loss dynamics**. Loss curves are almost never symmetric S-curves вЂ” they drop rapidly early, then tail off extremely slowly. The Gompertz ODE's inflection at $K/e \approx 0.37K$ (lower than logistic's $K/2$) means the fastest learning happens **early**, matching observed neural network training.

The **Gompertz activation function**:

$$G(x) = e^{-e^{-x}}$$

has been proposed as an alternative to sigmoid. It is the Gompertz solution evaluated at $t = x$ with $K = 1$, $A = 1$. Unlike sigmoid, it is **asymmetric**: slower to saturate at 1 than at 0, which can be beneficial for modeling asymmetric distributions.

---

### 8.2 Allee Effect and Rare Event Learning

The Allee threshold has a direct analogue in **few-shot and zero-shot learning**: a model trained on fewer than $A$ examples of a class fails to learn it (collapses to "extinction" вЂ” random guessing); above $A$ examples it generalizes successfully.

More precisely, in **memorization vs. generalization** (grokking): the model first memorizes (population at $P_0 \ll A$), then вЂ” after enough weight norm regularization acts like environment pressure вЂ” crosses the Allee threshold and "generalizes" (escapes to the $K$-attractor). The Allee model is a minimal ODE that exhibits this **threshold-crossing** behavior.

---

### 8.3 Doomsday and Superexponential Scaling Laws

The **neural scaling laws** (Kaplan et al., 2020) show that language model performance scales as a power law with compute:

$$\mathcal{L}(C) \sim C^{-\alpha}, \quad \alpha \approx 0.05$$

Equivalently, capability $P \sim C^{\alpha}$ grows as a power law in compute $C$. If compute itself grows superexponentially (as it has historically), $C(t) \sim e^{\beta t^\gamma}$, then $P(t)$ grows superexponentially too вЂ” a **doomsday-type trajectory** in capability space.

The finite-time blow-up in the doomsday model is a mathematical caricature of the **intelligence explosion** hypothesis: if capability growth rate $\sim P^\epsilon$ (smarter systems build better systems faster), the system reaches infinite capability in finite time $t^*$.

---

### 8.4 Theta-Logistic and Learning Rate Schedules

The theta-logistic's parameter $\theta$ controls when density dependence (saturation) kicks in. In deep learning:

- **$\theta < 1$ regime** (early saturation): analogous to **aggressive learning rate decay** вЂ” the model slows down early, converging cautiously but potentially to a sharper minimum.
- **$\theta > 1$ regime** (late saturation): analogous to **warmup schedules** вЂ” the model grows nearly freely until close to convergence, then slows.

The **cosine annealing** schedule (Loshchilov & Hutter, 2017) has a theta-logistic-like shape with $\theta > 1$: it keeps the learning rate high for most of training, then decays sharply at the end.

---

### 8.5 Harvesting and Dropout / Regularization

**Dropout** removes a fraction $H$ of activations at each forward pass. The effect on a single layer's output is analogous to constant harvesting:

$$\text{effective output} = f(\mathbf{x}) - H$$

For small $H$ (low dropout rate), two stable regimes exist: high activation (analogous to $P_+^*$, the learned representation) and zero activation (analogous to $P = 0$, dead neurons). For $H$ above the MSY threshold, all activations collapse to zero вЂ” **neuron death**, the analogue of population collapse past the harvesting threshold.

This geometric analogy explains why too-high dropout rates cause training failure: they push the system past the bifurcation point where no stable "alive" equilibrium exists.

---

### 8.6 Compartmental Population Models and Graph Neural Networks

Multi-species population models (Lotka-Volterra predator-prey, SIR epidemics) are **compartmental systems** of ODEs:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{P}}{dt} = F(\mathbf{P}), \quad \mathbf{P} \in \mathbb{R}^n$$

These are the continuous-time substrate of **message-passing in Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)**. The GNN update:

$$\mathbf{h}_v^{(k+1)} = \phi\left(\mathbf{h}_v^{(k)},\ \bigoplus_{u \in \mathcal{N}(v)} \psi(\mathbf{h}_u^{(k)})\right)$$

is an Euler step of a compartmental ODE on the graph, where each node $v$ is a "species" exchanging "population" (features) with neighbors. The stability theory of population ODEs вЂ” equilibria, Jacobian eigenvalues, limit cycles вЂ” directly informs the **over-smoothing problem** in deep GNNs (features converging to a uniform fixed point, like all populations reaching the same carrying capacity).

## 10. Common Pitfalls

1. **Treating all models as separable without checking.** All models in this note are separable (or reducible to separable via substitution). But multi-species models (Lotka-Volterra) are generally **not** separable вЂ” they require phase plane or linearization methods.
2. **Confusing Allee effect stability.** In the strong Allee effect, $P^* = 0$ is **stable** (extinction is an attractor). Students often expect $0$ to be unstable as in the logistic. It is not вЂ” small populations go extinct.
3. **Forgetting $\theta$'s effect on units.** In the theta-logistic, $(P/K)^\theta$ is dimensionless for any $\theta$, so $r$ always has units $1/\text{time}$ regardless of $\theta$.
4. **Missing the MSY bifurcation.** In the harvesting model, the transition at $H = rK/4$ is a **saddle-node bifurcation** вЂ” two equilibria collide and annihilate. This is not a smooth transition; it is a sudden collapse. Fishing industries that ignore this threshold have historically caused stock collapses.
5. **Applying the Doomsday model globally.** The doomsday ODE fits data over a finite interval. Extrapolation to $t \to t^*$ is physically meaningless вЂ” the model breaks down long before blow-up (as Malthus himself understood about food limits).
