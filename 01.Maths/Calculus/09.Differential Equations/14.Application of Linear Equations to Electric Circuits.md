## 0. Preliminary Remarks

Electric circuits are one of the most natural and historically important applications of linear systems. The key insight — due to Kirchhoff in 1845 — is that the physical laws governing current and voltage are **linear constraints**. Every node and every loop contributes one linear equation. The circuit becomes a matrix. Solving it is Gaussian elimination.

This note builds from physical laws → linear equations → full matrix system → worked examples → connection to ML (circuit simulation in neural networks and analog computing).

---

## 1. The Physical Laws — Stated Precisely

### 1.1 Ohm's Law

> **Law 1.1 (Ohm).** For a resistor with resistance $R > 0$ (in ohms, $\Omega$), if current $I$ (amperes) flows through it, the voltage drop across it is: $$V = IR.$$

This is the fundamental **linear** relation between voltage and current. It is a single linear equation in two unknowns — one constraint.

> **Remark.** Ohm's Law is an empirical approximation valid for resistors at constant temperature. It fails for diodes, transistors, and nonlinear elements — which is precisely where circuit analysis becomes nonlinear and requires numerical methods.

### 1.2 Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL)

> **Law 1.2 (KCL).** At every node (junction) of a circuit, the algebraic sum of currents is zero: $$\sum_{k} I_k = 0,$$ where currents entering the node are positive and currents leaving are negative (or vice versa — pick a convention and be consistent).

**Physical meaning:** Charge is conserved. No charge accumulates at a node.

**Mathematical character:** KCL gives one **linear equation per node** in the unknown currents.

### 1.3 Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL)

> **Law 1.3 (KVL).** Around every closed loop in a circuit, the algebraic sum of voltage drops is zero: $$\sum_{k} V_k = 0.$$ Voltage rises (across sources) are counted positive; voltage drops (across resistors) are counted negative.

**Physical meaning:** The electric potential is a well-defined function on the circuit graph. Its line integral around any closed path vanishes — this is the statement that the electric field is conservative ($\nabla \times \mathbf{E} = 0$ in the static case).

**Mathematical character:** KVL gives one **linear equation per independent loop**.

> **Key observation.** Substituting Ohm's Law ($V_k = I_k R_k$) into KVL converts voltage equations into current equations. Every equation is **linear** in the unknown currents. The entire circuit is a **linear system**.

## 2. Setting Up the Linear System

### 2.1 Circuit Vocabulary

|Term|Meaning|
|---|---|
|**Node**|Junction where two or more wires meet|
|**Branch**|A single element (resistor, source) between two nodes|
|**Loop**|Any closed path through the circuit|
|**Mesh**|A loop that encloses no other loop (minimal loop)|
|**Reference node**|One node chosen as "ground" ($V = 0$)|

### 2.2 Two Systematic Methods

|Method|Unknowns|Equations from|Best when|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Node voltage**|Node voltages $V_i$|KCL at each node|Many nodes, few meshes|
|**Mesh current**|Mesh currents $I_m$|KVL around each mesh|Planar circuits, many meshes|

Both methods produce a square linear system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.

## 3. Simple Series Circuit — One Loop

### Circuit Description

A voltage source $\mathcal{E}$ in series with two resistors $R_1$ and $R_2$. One loop, one unknown current $I$.

```
     ┌────R₁────┬────R₂────┐
     │          │          │
    [ε]         │          │
     │          │          │
     └──────────┴──────────┘
          (ground)
```

### Applying KVL

Traverse the loop clockwise. Voltage rises across the source, drops across resistors: $$\mathcal{E} - IR_1 - IR_2 = 0.$$

Rearranging: $$I(R_1 + R_2) = \mathcal{E}.$$

This is a **linear equation in one unknown** $I$.

**Solution:** $$I = \frac{\mathcal{E}}{R_1 + R_2}.$$

Existence and uniqueness follow from Definition 1.1 of [[Linear Equations]], since $R_1 + R_2 > 0$.

**Concrete example.** $\mathcal{E} = 12,\text{V}$, $R_1 = 2,\Omega$, $R_2 = 4,\Omega$: $$I = \frac{12}{2+4} = 2,\text{A}, \quad V_{R_1} = 2 \cdot 2 = 4,\text{V}, \quad V_{R_2} = 2 \cdot 4 = 8,\text{V}.$$

Check KVL: $12 - 4 - 8 = 0$. $\checkmark$

## 4. Two-Loop Circuit — A $2 \times 2$ System

### Circuit Description

```
        R₁          R₂
   ┌────/\/\/────┬────/\/\/────┐
   │             │             │
  [ε₁]          R₃           [ε₂]
   │             │             │
   └─────────────┴─────────────┘
         (ground node)
```

Two meshes. Assign mesh currents $I_1$ (left loop, clockwise) and $I_2$ (right loop, clockwise).

### Applying KVL to Each Mesh

**Mesh 1 (left loop):**

Voltage rise across $\mathcal{E}_1$, drop across $R_1$ (carries $I_1$), drop across $R_3$ (carries $I_1 - I_2$, since $I_2$ flows opposite through $R_3$): $$\mathcal{E}_1 - I_1 R_1 - (I_1 - I_2)R_3 = 0$$ $$\mathcal{E}_1 = I_1(R_1 + R_3) - I_2 R_3. \tag{1}$$

**Mesh 2 (right loop):**

Voltage rise across $\mathcal{E}_2$, drop across $R_2$ (carries $I_2$), drop across $R_3$ (carries $I_2 - I_1$): $$\mathcal{E}_2 - I_2 R_2 - (I_2 - I_1)R_3 = 0$$ $$\mathcal{E}_2 = -I_1 R_3 + I_2(R_2 + R_3). \tag{2}$$

### Matrix Form

$$\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} R_1 + R_3 & -R_3 \ -R_3 & R_2 + R_3 \end{pmatrix}}_{A} \begin{pmatrix} I_1 \ I_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathcal{E}_1 \ \mathcal{E}_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

> **Observation.** The matrix $A$ is **symmetric** and has a special structure: diagonal entries are sums of resistances in each mesh; off-diagonal entries are the negative of shared resistances. This is the **mesh resistance matrix** — it arises from the topology of the circuit, not from the specific values.

### Solvability

$\det(A) = (R_1+R_3)(R_2+R_3) - R_3^2 = R_1 R_2 + R_1 R_3 + R_2 R_3 > 0$ (since all $R_i > 0$).

Therefore $A$ is invertible and the solution is **unique**: $$\begin{pmatrix} I_1 \ I_2 \end{pmatrix} = A^{-1} \begin{pmatrix} \mathcal{E}_1 \ \mathcal{E}_2 \end{pmatrix}.$$

> **Theorem 4.1.** For any circuit of $n$ independent meshes with positive resistances, the mesh resistance matrix $A$ is symmetric positive definite (SPD), hence always invertible. The linear system $A\mathbf{I} = \boldsymbol{\mathcal{E}}$ always has a unique solution.

**Proof sketch.** $A$ is symmetric by construction (shared resistances appear symmetrically). For any nonzero $\mathbf{x}$: $$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \sum_{\text{branches}} R_b \left(\sum_{\text{meshes}\ni b} \pm x_m\right)^2 > 0,$$ since each term is a squared current times a positive resistance, and at least one term is nonzero. Hence $A$ is SPD $\Rightarrow$ $\det(A) > 0$ $\Rightarrow$ unique solution. $\blacksquare$

### Concrete Numerical Example

$R_1 = 1,\Omega,\ R_2 = 2,\Omega,\ R_3 = 3,\Omega,\ \mathcal{E}_1 = 10,\text{V},\ \mathcal{E}_2 = 5,\text{V}$:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & -3 \ -3 & 5 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 \ 5 \end{pmatrix}.$$

$\det(A) = 20 - 9 = 11$.

By Cramer's rule: $$I_1 = \frac{\det\begin{pmatrix}10&-3\5&5\end{pmatrix}}{11} = \frac{50+15}{11} = \frac{65}{11} \approx 5.91,\text{A}.$$ $$I_2 = \frac{\det\begin{pmatrix}4&10\-3&5\end{pmatrix}}{11} = \frac{20+30}{11} = \frac{50}{11} \approx 4.55,\text{A}.$$

Current through $R_3$: $I_1 - I_2 = 15/11 \approx 1.36,\text{A}$.

## 5. General Circuit — The Node Voltage Method

For a circuit with $n+1$ nodes (label one as ground, $V_0 = 0$), the $n$ remaining node voltages $V_1, \ldots, V_n$ are the unknowns.

### KCL at Node $i$

By KCL, the sum of currents leaving node $i$ equals zero. The current from node $i$ to node $j$ through resistance $R_{ij}$ is: $$I_{ij} = \frac{V_i - V_j}{R_{ij}}.$$

Summing over all neighbors $j$ of node $i$: $$\sum_{j \sim i} \frac{V_i - V_j}{R_{ij}} = I_{\text{ext},i},$$

where $I_{\text{ext},i}$ is external current injected at node $i$ by current sources.

Rearranging: $$\left(\sum_{j \sim i} \frac{1}{R_{ij}}\right) V_i - \sum_{j \sim i} \frac{1}{R_{ij}} V_j = I_{\text{ext},i}.$$

### The Conductance Matrix (Laplacian)

Define conductance $G_{ij} = 1/R_{ij}$. The system in matrix form is:

$$\underbrace{L}_{n \times n} \mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I}_{\text{ext}},$$

where $L$ is the **graph Laplacian** of the circuit: $$L_{ii} = \sum_{j \sim i} G_{ij}, \qquad L_{ij} = -G_{ij}\ (i \neq j).$$

> **Theorem 5.1 (Laplacian Structure).** The conductance matrix $L$ satisfies:
> 
> 1. $L$ is symmetric: $L_{ij} = L_{ji}$.
> 2. All diagonal entries are positive.
> 3. All off-diagonal entries are $\leq 0$.
> 4. Each row sums to zero: $\sum_j L_{ij} = 0$.
> 5. $L$ is positive **semi**definite. After fixing ground ($V_0 = 0$), the reduced matrix $\tilde{L}$ is SPD, hence invertible.

**Proof of row-sum zero:** $\sum_j L_{ij} = L_{ii} + \sum_{j \neq i} L_{ij} = \sum_{j\sim i} G_{ij} - \sum_{j\sim i} G_{ij} = 0$. $\blacksquare$

This means $L$ itself has a null space spanned by $(1, 1, \ldots, 1)^T$ (constant potentials correspond to zero current — you can shift all potentials by the same constant). Grounding one node removes this degeneracy and makes the system uniquely solvable.

## 6. Three-Node Example — Full Walkthrough

### Circuit

```
     Node 1 ──── R₁₂ = 2Ω ──── Node 2
        │                          │
       R₁₃ = 4Ω               R₂₃ = 1Ω
        │                          │
     Node 3 ──────────────────── (ground)

External: current source pushes I_s = 3A into Node 1.
          current source pulls I_s = 3A out of Node 2.
```

Unknowns: $V_1, V_2$ (Node 3 is ground: $V_3 = 0$).

### Conductances

$G_{12} = 1/2,\quad G_{13} = 1/4,\quad G_{23} = 1$.

### KCL at Node 1

$$G_{12}(V_1 - V_2) + G_{13}(V_1 - 0) = 3$$ $$\left(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4}\right)V_1 - \frac{1}{2}V_2 = 3$$ $$\frac{3}{4}V_1 - \frac{1}{2}V_2 = 3. \tag{i}$$

### KCL at Node 2

$$G_{12}(V_2 - V_1) + G_{23}(V_2 - 0) = -3$$ $$-\frac{1}{2}V_1 + \left(\frac{1}{2} + 1\right)V_2 = -3$$ $$-\frac{1}{2}V_1 + \frac{3}{2}V_2 = -3. \tag{ii}$$

### Matrix System

$$\begin{pmatrix} 3/4 & -1/2 \ -1/2 & 3/2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} V_1 \ V_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \ -3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\det = \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{3}{2} - \left(-\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{9}{8} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{7}{8}$.

$$V_1 = \frac{1}{\det}\det\begin{pmatrix}3&-1/2\-3&3/2\end{pmatrix} = \frac{8}{7}\left(\frac{9}{2} - \frac{3}{2}\right) = \frac{8}{7} \cdot 3 = \frac{24}{7} \approx 3.43,\text{V}.$$

$$V_2 = \frac{1}{\det}\det\begin{pmatrix}3/4&3\-1/2&-3\end{pmatrix} = \frac{8}{7}\left(-\frac{9}{4} + \frac{3}{2}\right) = \frac{8}{7} \cdot \left(-\frac{3}{4}\right) = -\frac{6}{7} \approx -0.86,\text{V}.$$

**Currents:** $$I_{12} = G_{12}(V_1 - V_2) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{30}{7} = \frac{15}{7},\text{A}.$$ $$I_{13} = G_{13} \cdot V_1 = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{24}{7} = \frac{6}{7},\text{A}.$$ $$I_{23} = G_{23} \cdot V_2 = 1 \cdot \left(-\frac{6}{7}\right) = -\frac{6}{7},\text{A}\ \text{(flows from ground to Node 2)}.$$

## 7. Superposition — The Linearity Principle

> **Theorem 7.1 (Superposition).** In a linear circuit, the response (current or voltage anywhere) due to multiple independent sources equals the **sum** of responses due to each source acting alone (all others replaced by their internal resistances: voltage sources $\to$ short circuit, current sources $\to$ open circuit).

**Proof.** The node voltage system is $L\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I}_{\text{ext}}$. If $\mathbf{I}_{\text{ext}} = \mathbf{I}^{(1)} + \mathbf{I}^{(2)}$ (two sources), then: $$L\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I}^{(1)} + \mathbf{I}^{(2)} \implies \mathbf{V} = L^{-1}\mathbf{I}^{(1)} + L^{-1}\mathbf{I}^{(2)} = \mathbf{V}^{(1)} + \mathbf{V}^{(2)}.$$

This is exactly **linearity of the map** $\mathbf{I}_{\text{ext}} \mapsto \mathbf{V} = L^{-1}\mathbf{I}_{\text{ext}}$. $\blacksquare$

> **Remark.** Superposition fails for power (since $P = V^2/R$ is quadratic, not linear). This is a critical practical note — never superpose power directly.

## 8. Thévenin's Theorem — Existence of a Canonical Form

> **Theorem 8.1 (Thévenin).** Any linear circuit with two output terminals can be replaced by a single voltage source $V_{\text{th}}$ in series with a single resistance $R_{\text{th}}$.

**Proof sketch.** The terminal voltage $V$ and current $I$ satisfy a relation of the form $V = V_{\text{th}} - I R_{\text{th}}$ (a linear equation!) — this follows from the fact that the open-circuit voltage is $V_{\text{th}} = V|_{I=0}$ and the short-circuit current is $I_{sc} = V_{\text{th}}/R_{\text{th}}$. The linear relationship between $V$ and $I$ is guaranteed by the linearity of the underlying system. $\blacksquare$

**How to find $V_{\text{th}}$ and $R_{\text{th}}$:**

1. $V_{\text{th}}$ = open-circuit voltage at terminals (solve the linear system with $I = 0$).
2. $R_{\text{th}}$ = resistance seen from terminals with all independent sources killed.

**Example.** Circuit: $\mathcal{E} = 10,\text{V}$, $R_1 = 6,\Omega$ in series, $R_2 = 3,\Omega$ in parallel with terminals.

$$V_{\text{th}} = \mathcal{E} \cdot \frac{R_2}{R_1 + R_2} = 10 \cdot \frac{3}{9} = \frac{10}{3},\text{V}.$$ $$R_{\text{th}} = R_1 \parallel R_2 = \frac{6 \cdot 3}{6 + 3} = 2,\Omega.$$

This two-parameter description encodes the entire linear circuit for any load.

## 10. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 10.1 Circuit Simulation as Linear Algebra

SPICE (the industry-standard circuit simulator) solves the node voltage system $L\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I}$ at its core. The matrix $L$ is **sparse** (most entries zero — each node connects to few others). Efficient sparse linear solvers (LU factorization with fill-reducing orderings) are the computational heart of every circuit simulator.

The same sparse linear algebra appears in:

- **Graph neural networks (GNNs):** message passing on a graph is exactly the Laplacian operator $L$ applied to node features.
- **Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs):** enforce circuit equations as loss terms.

### 10.2 Resistor Networks as Neural Networks

A resistor network is mathematically equivalent to a **linear neural network**:

- Node voltages $\leftrightarrow$ neuron activations
- Conductances $G_{ij}$ $\leftrightarrow$ weights $w_{ij}$
- KCL $\leftrightarrow$ the linear combination at each neuron: $V_i = \frac{\sum_j G_{ij} V_j}{\sum_j G_{ij}}$

This is not a metaphor. Hopfield (1984) showed that analog circuits with resistors and capacitors perform **associative memory** — a precursor to modern attention mechanisms.

### 10.3 Equilibrium Propagation (Modern ML)

The **Equilibrium Propagation** algorithm (Scellier & Bengio, 2017) trains neural networks by nudging the circuit toward a target state and measuring how the equilibrium $L\mathbf{V} = \mathbf{I}$ changes. The gradient of the loss is encoded in the difference between two linear system solutions. This is a biologically plausible alternative to backpropagation.

### 10.4 Power Systems and Grid ML

Power grids are large-scale circuit systems. The **DC power flow equations** are a linear approximation: $$B\boldsymbol{\theta} = \mathbf{P},$$

where $B$ is the susceptance matrix (a Laplacian), $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ are bus voltage angles, and $\mathbf{P}$ are power injections. Machine learning models for grid optimization (e.g., optimal power flow) are trained on solutions to this linear system.

## 11. Key Theorems — Summary

|Theorem|Statement|
|---|---|
|**Ohm's Law**|$V = IR$ — linear relation, one equation per element|
|**KCL**|$\sum I_k = 0$ at each node — one linear equation per node|
|**KVL**|$\sum V_k = 0$ per loop — one linear equation per loop|
|**Mesh Matrix SPD**|Mesh resistance matrix is always SPD → unique solution|
|**Laplacian Structure**|Conductance matrix is symmetric, row sums zero, SPD after grounding|
|**Superposition**|Response is linear in sources — follows from $L^{-1}$ being linear|
|**Thévenin**|Any linear circuit seen from two terminals = $V_{th}$ + $R_{th}$ in series|

## 12. Things to Prove Yourself

1. **Prove** that the mesh resistance matrix $A$ for any planar resistive circuit with $n$ meshes is symmetric positive definite. (Hint: express $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ as a sum over branches.)
2. **Derive** the $\pi$-to-Y (delta-to-star) transformation for three resistors. Show it reduces to solving a $3 \times 3$ linear system.
3. **Prove** Superposition rigorously from the linearity of $L^{-1}$.
4. **Show** that if all resistances in a circuit are scaled by a constant $\lambda > 0$, then all currents scale by $1/\lambda$ and all voltages remain unchanged (when driven by voltage sources). What does this say about the condition number of $L$?
