## 1. The Physical Setup

A tank contains a solution (fluid + dissolved substance). Two processes happen simultaneously:

- **Inflow:** Solution enters at rate $r_{in}$ (volume/time) with concentration $c_{in}$ (substance/volume).
- **Outflow:** Well-mixed solution leaves at rate $r_{out}$ (volume/time).

We want to track $Q(t)$ = **amount of substance in the tank at time $t$**.

The governing principle is a **balance law** (conservation):

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = \text{Rate in} - \text{Rate out}$$

$$\boxed{\frac{dQ}{dt} = r_{in} \cdot c_{in} - r_{out} \cdot \frac{Q(t)}{V(t)}}$$

where $V(t)$ is the **volume of solution in the tank at time $t$**.

> The term $\frac{Q(t)}{V(t)}$ is the **concentration** in the tank at time $t$ — the "well-mixed" assumption means the outflow carries whatever concentration is currently in the tank.

---

## 2. The Volume Function $V(t)$

$$V(t) = V_0 + (r_{in} - r_{out}),t$$

where $V_0 = V(0)$ is the initial volume.

Three cases:

|Condition|Effect|ODE type|
|---|---|---|
|$r_{in} = r_{out}$|$V(t) = V_0$ (constant)|Linear, constant coefficients|
|$r_{in} > r_{out}$|Tank fills|Linear, variable coefficients|
|$r_{in} < r_{out}$|Tank drains|Linear, variable coefficients (finite time blow-up possible)|

---

## 3. The ODE — First-Order Linear

Substituting $V(t) = V_0 + (r_{in} - r_{out})t$:

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} + \underbrace{\frac{r_{out}}{V_0 + (r_{in} - r_{out})t}}_{P(t)} Q = \underbrace{r_{in} \cdot c_{in}}_{f(t)} \tag{1}$$

This is a **first-order linear ODE** in standard form $Q' + P(t)Q = f(t)$.

### Integrating Factor Method

Multiply both sides by the **integrating factor**

$$\mu(t) = e^{\int P(t), dt},$$

so that the left side becomes an exact derivative:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\left[\mu(t), Q(t)\right] = \mu(t), f(t).$$

Integrate both sides:

$$\mu(t), Q(t) = \int \mu(t), f(t), dt + C.$$

Solve for $Q(t)$ and apply the initial condition $Q(0) = Q_0$.

---

## 4. Rigorous Derivation of the Integrating Factor

**Claim:** If $\mu(t) = e^{\int P(t),dt}$, then $\mu(t)(Q' + P(t)Q) = \frac{d}{dt}(\mu Q)$.

**Proof:**

$$\frac{d}{dt}(\mu Q) = \mu' Q + \mu Q'.$$

Since $\mu = e^{\int P,dt}$, we have $\mu' = P(t),\mu$. Therefore:

$$\frac{d}{dt}(\mu Q) = P\mu Q + \mu Q' = \mu(Q' + PQ). \quad \blacksquare$$

So multiplying the ODE by $\mu$ converts the left side to a perfect derivative, which we can integrate directly.

## 5. Examples

---

### Example 1 — Constant Volume (Equal Flow Rates)

**Setup:** A 500 L tank initially contains 10 kg of salt dissolved in water. Brine containing 0.05 kg/L flows in at 10 L/min. Well-mixed solution drains at 10 L/min.

**Parameters:**

- $V_0 = 500$ L, $r_{in} = r_{out} = 10$ L/min (so $V(t) = 500$)
- $c_{in} = 0.05$ kg/L, $Q_0 = 10$ kg

**ODE:**

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = 10 \cdot 0.05 - 10 \cdot \frac{Q}{500} = 0.5 - \frac{Q}{50}$$

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} + \frac{1}{50}Q = 0.5 \tag{2}$$

**Integrating factor:** $\mu = e^{t/50}$.

$$\frac{d}{dt}\left(e^{t/50} Q\right) = 0.5, e^{t/50}$$

**Integrate:**

$$e^{t/50} Q = 0.5 \cdot 50, e^{t/50} + C = 25, e^{t/50} + C$$

$$Q(t) = 25 + Ce^{-t/50}$$

**Apply $Q(0) = 10$:** $10 = 25 + C \implies C = -15$.

$$\boxed{Q(t) = 25 - 15e^{-t/50} \text{ kg}}$$

**Interpretation:**

- As $t \to \infty$: $Q(t) \to 25$ kg. The **equilibrium concentration** is $c_{in} = 0.05$ kg/L applied to 500 L, giving $25$ kg. ✓
- The system forgets its initial condition exponentially, with **time constant** $\tau = 50$ min.
- At $t = 50$ min (one time constant): $Q(50) = 25 - 15e^{-1} \approx 19.48$ kg.

```
Q(t)
25 ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ────────────────  ← equilibrium
                      ____/
                  ___/
              ___/
          ___/
      ___/
10 ──/
     |
     0        50       100      150   → t (min)
              ↑
         one time constant τ = 50
```

### Example 2 — Draining Tank (Variable Volume)

**Setup:** A 200 L tank initially contains 50 kg of salt. Pure water ($c_{in} = 0$) flows in at 4 L/min. Mixed solution drains at 6 L/min.

**Parameters:**

- $V(t) = 200 + (4-6)t = 200 - 2t$ (tank empties at $t = 100$ min)
- $Q_0 = 50$ kg, $c_{in} = 0$

**ODE:**

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = 0 - 6 \cdot \frac{Q}{200 - 2t} = -\frac{6Q}{200 - 2t} = -\frac{3Q}{100 - t}$$

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} + \frac{3}{100 - t} Q = 0 \tag{3}$$

This is actually **separable** (it's homogeneous linear):

$$\frac{dQ}{Q} = -\frac{3, dt}{100 - t}$$

$$\ln|Q| = 3\ln|100 - t| + C_1$$

$$Q(t) = K(100 - t)^3$$

**Apply $Q(0) = 50$:** $50 = K \cdot 100^3 \implies K = 50/10^6 = 5 \times 10^{-5}$.

$$\boxed{Q(t) = 50\left(\frac{100 - t}{100}\right)^3 = 50\left(1 - \frac{t}{100}\right)^3 \text{ kg}}$$

**Check:** $Q(100) = 0$ — tank is empty and contains no salt. ✓

The concentration in the tank:

$$c(t) = \frac{Q(t)}{V(t)} = \frac{50(1 - t/100)^3}{200 - 2t} = \frac{50(1-t/100)^3}{200(1 - t/100)} = \frac{50}{200}(1-t/100)^2 = \frac{1}{4}\left(1-\frac{t}{100}\right)^2$$

At $t = 0$: $c(0) = 1/4$ kg/L. At $t = 100$: $c(100) = 0$ kg/L. The tank dilutes to zero as it drains.

### Example 3 — Two-Tank Cascade (System of ODEs)

**Setup:** Tank 1 (100 L) and Tank 2 (200 L) are connected. Brine at 0.1 kg/L flows into Tank 1 at 5 L/min. The outflow of Tank 1 (5 L/min) feeds into Tank 2. Tank 2 drains at 5 L/min. Initially both tanks contain pure water.

Let $Q_1(t)$, $Q_2(t)$ = salt in tanks 1 and 2.

**System:**

$$\frac{dQ_1}{dt} = 5 \cdot 0.1 - 5 \cdot \frac{Q_1}{100} = 0.5 - \frac{Q_1}{20}$$

$$\frac{dQ_2}{dt} = 5 \cdot \frac{Q_1}{100} - 5 \cdot \frac{Q_2}{200} = \frac{Q_1}{20} - \frac{Q_2}{40}$$

**Solve Tank 1 first** (same structure as Example 1):

$$Q_1(t) = 10(1 - e^{-t/20})$$

**Substitute into Tank 2's ODE:**

$$\frac{dQ_2}{dt} + \frac{Q_2}{40} = \frac{Q_1}{20} = \frac{10(1-e^{-t/20})}{20} = \frac{1}{2}(1-e^{-t/20})$$

Integrating factor $\mu = e^{t/40}$:

$$\frac{d}{dt}(e^{t/40} Q_2) = \frac{1}{2}e^{t/40}(1 - e^{-t/20}) = \frac{1}{2}e^{t/40} - \frac{1}{2}e^{-t/40}$$

Integrate:

$$e^{t/40} Q_2 = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 40, e^{t/40} - \frac{1}{2} \cdot (-40), e^{-t/40} + C = 20e^{t/40} + 20e^{-t/40} + C$$

$$Q_2(t) = 20 + 20e^{-t/20} + Ce^{-t/40}$$

Apply $Q_2(0) = 0$: $0 = 20 + 20 + C \implies C = -40$.

$$\boxed{Q_2(t) = 20 - 40e^{-t/40} + 20e^{-t/20}}$$

As $t \to \infty$: $Q_2 \to 20$ kg = $0.1$ kg/L $\times$ 200 L. ✓

> **Key structure:** The two-tank cascade is a **compartmental model** — the output of one ODE drives the next. This is the architecture of recurrent and feedforward systems in both biology and machine learning.

## 7. The Equilibrium and Time Constant

For the constant-volume case $Q' + \frac{r}{V}Q = r \cdot c_{in}$, the solution always takes the form:

$$Q(t) = Q_{eq} + (Q_0 - Q_{eq}),e^{-t/\tau}$$

where:

- $Q_{eq} = c_{in} \cdot V$ — the **equilibrium amount** (what the tank converges to)
- $\tau = V/r$ — the **time constant** (how fast it gets there)

**At $t = \tau$:** the gap $|Q(t) - Q_{eq}|$ has decayed to $e^{-1} \approx 36.8%$ of its initial value.

**At $t = 5\tau$:** gap is $e^{-5} \approx 0.67%$ — effectively at equilibrium.

The time constant $\tau$ has a clean physical meaning: it is the time it takes to flush one full tank volume through the system ($\tau = V/r$).

## 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

The mixing problem is not merely a physics exercise. Its mathematical structure — a first-order linear ODE with a source term and a decay term — is the **fundamental equation of memory, forgetting, and information blending** in machine learning.

---

### 8.1 Exponential Moving Average — The Discrete Mixing Problem

In optimization and statistics, the **exponential moving average (EMA)** of a quantity $x_t$ is:

$$S_t = \beta S_{t-1} + (1-\beta) x_t, \quad \beta \in (0,1).$$

This is the **discrete-time version of the mixing ODE**. To see why, write it as:

$$S_t - S_{t-1} = -(1-\beta)(S_{t-1} - x_t)$$

$$\Delta S = -(1-\beta)(S - x_t)$$

In continuous time with step $\Delta t \to 0$, setting $\beta = 1 - r\Delta t / V$:

$$\frac{dS}{dt} = -\frac{r}{V}(S - c_{in}) = \frac{r}{V}c_{in} - \frac{r}{V}S$$

This is **exactly the constant-volume mixing ODE**. The EMA is the mixing problem in disguise, with:

- $\beta$ controlling the **time constant** (memory length)
- $x_t$ playing the role of **inflow concentration**
- $S_t$ being the **amount in the tank**

---

### 8.2 Momentum in Gradient Descent (Heavy Ball / Adam)

The **momentum update** in SGD is:

$$v_t = \beta v_{t-1} + (1-\beta)\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta v_t$$

The velocity $v_t$ obeys a mixing ODE: it is a running average of past gradients, with old gradients "rinsing out" exponentially. The **time constant** is $\tau = 1/(1-\beta)$ steps.

- $\beta = 0.9$: memory of $\tau = 10$ steps.
- $\beta = 0.99$: memory of $\tau = 100$ steps.

**Adam** uses two mixing ODEs simultaneously:

$$m_t = \beta_1 m_{t-1} + (1-\beta_1)\nabla \mathcal{L}$$ (first moment — gradient EMA) $$v_t = \beta_2 v_{t-1} + (1-\beta_2)(\nabla \mathcal{L})^2$$ (second moment — squared gradient EMA)

Both are mixing problems running in parallel, one tracking the "mean tank" and one tracking the "variance tank."

---

### 8.3 Batch Normalization — Mixing Running Statistics

During inference, batch normalization uses **running estimates** of mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$:

$$\mu_{\text{run}} \leftarrow (1-\alpha),\mu_{\text{run}} + \alpha,\mu_{\text{batch}}$$

This is a mixing ODE with time constant $\tau = 1/\alpha$. The running mean is the "amount of substance" in a tank being continuously flushed with the batch statistics.

Choosing $\alpha$ too large = the tank is too reactive (noisy estimates). Too small = too sluggish (doesn't track distribution shift). This is exactly the tradeoff between $\tau$ being too short or too long in Example 1.

---

### 8.4 Leaky Integrator Neurons — The Neural Mixing Tank

The standard model of a biological neuron's membrane potential $V(t)$ is:

$$\tau_m \frac{dV}{dt} = -V(t) + R_m I(t)$$

$$\frac{dV}{dt} + \frac{V}{\tau_m} = \frac{R_m}{\tau_m} I(t)$$

This is **precisely the mixing ODE** with:

- $Q \leftrightarrow V$ (membrane potential ↔ amount of substance)
- $r/V \leftrightarrow 1/\tau_m$ (leak rate)
- $r \cdot c_{in} \leftrightarrow R_m I(t)/\tau_m$ (input current ↔ inflow)

The neuron integrates its input and leaks — it is a mixing tank. The **Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF)** neuron used in spiking neural networks is built directly on this ODE.

In deep learning, **Liquid State Machines** and **Echo State Networks** (reservoir computing) generalize this to high-dimensional networks of leaky integrators — tanks coupled into a cascade, exactly like Example 3.

---

### 8.5 LSTM Forget Gate — Controlled Mixing

The LSTM cell state update:

$$C_t = f_t \odot C_{t-1} + i_t \odot \tilde{C}_t$$

where $f_t \in (0,1)$ is the **forget gate** and $i_t$ is the **input gate**.

Compare with the mixing ODE solution:

$$Q(t + \Delta t) = e^{-\Delta t/\tau} Q(t) + (1 - e^{-\Delta t/\tau}) Q_{eq}$$

The forget gate $f_t$ plays the role of $e^{-\Delta t/\tau}$ — the "fraction remaining in the tank." The input gate $i_t \odot \tilde{C}_t$ is the new inflow. The LSTM **learns** to set its own time constant adaptively, solving a parameterized family of mixing problems at each timestep.

> The entire architecture of LSTMs is a learnable, input-dependent version of the mixing ODE. Grokking one gives you the geometric intuition for the other.

---

### 8.6 Compartmental Models in Epidemiology and Federated Learning

The two-tank cascade (Example 3) generalizes to **compartmental models**: systems of ODEs where substance flows between compartments. In ML:

- **SIR/SEIR epidemic models** (used in federated learning to model data infection/spread across nodes) are compartmental mixing systems.
- **Graph Neural Networks** can be understood as compartmental mixing problems on graphs, where "substance" (feature information) diffuses between nodes according to the graph Laplacian — a high-dimensional mixing ODE.

## 10. Common Pitfalls

1. **Using $Q/V_0$ instead of $Q/V(t)$ in variable-volume problems.** The concentration uses the _current_ volume — a common and fatal error.
2. **Forgetting the units.** Rate in is (L/min) × (kg/L) = kg/min. Rate out is (L/min) × (kg/L) = kg/min. $dQ/dt$ must have units kg/min.
3. **Not checking the domain.** If $r_{out} > r_{in}$, $V(t)$ reaches zero in finite time $t^* = V_0/(r_{out} - r_{in})$. The solution is only valid on $[0, t^*)$.
4. **Confusing $Q(t)$ with $c(t)$.** $Q$ is amount (kg); $c = Q/V$ is concentration (kg/L). The problem statement specifies which one is given and which is sought.
5. **Wrong equilibrium.** The equilibrium is $Q_{eq} = c_{in} \cdot V$ (concentration × volume), **not** $c_{in}$ alone.
