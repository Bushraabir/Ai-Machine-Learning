## 1. The Governing Principle

The **Law of Natural Growth (or Decay)** asserts:

> _The rate of change of a quantity is proportional to the quantity itself._

Mathematically:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = kP, \quad k \in \mathbb{R} \tag{1}$$

where:

- $P(t)$ is the quantity at time $t$
- $k$ is the **proportionality constant** (the growth/decay rate)
- $k > 0$: growth; $\quad k < 0$: decay

This is the simplest separable ODE, and it has a unique, explicit, beautiful solution.

---

## 2. Derivation — Full Rigorous Solution

### 2.1 Separation of Variables

Assuming $P(t) \neq 0$, separate:

$$\frac{dP}{P} = k, dt$$

Integrate both sides:

$$\int \frac{dP}{P} = \int k, dt$$

$$\ln|P| = kt + C_1, \quad C_1 \in \mathbb{R}$$

Exponentiate:

$$|P(t)| = e^{C_1} e^{kt}$$

$$P(t) = \pm e^{C_1} e^{kt} = A e^{kt}, \quad A \in \mathbb{R} \setminus {0}.$$

**Check $P = 0$:** Substituting into (1): $0 = k \cdot 0$ ✓. Recovered by $A = 0$.

**General solution:**

$$\boxed{P(t) = A e^{kt}, \quad A \in \mathbb{R}} \tag{2}$$

### 2.2 Applying the Initial Condition

Given $P(0) = P_0$:

$$P_0 = A e^{0} = A \implies A = P_0$$

$$\boxed{P(t) = P_0, e^{kt}} \tag{3}$$

### 2.3 Uniqueness

By **Picard–Lindelöf**: $f(t, P) = kP$ is Lipschitz in $P$ (with constant $|k|$) on any rectangle, so there exists a **unique** solution through any initial condition $(t_0, P_0)$. The family (2) is therefore the _complete_ solution — no singular solutions exist.

---

## 3. The Constant $k$ — What It Means

Rewrite (3) as:

$$\frac{P(t)}{P_0} = e^{kt}$$

Taking logarithm:

$$k = \frac{1}{t} \ln\frac{P(t)}{P_0}$$

So $k$ is the **instantaneous fractional growth rate** per unit time. Concretely:

- In time $\Delta t$, the quantity changes by the factor $e^{k\Delta t}$.
- For small $k\Delta t$: $e^{k\Delta t} \approx 1 + k\Delta t$ — a growth of approximately $k \cdot 100%$ per unit time.

---

## 4. Half-Life and Doubling Time

### Doubling Time ($k > 0$)

The time $T_2$ for $P$ to double:

$$2P_0 = P_0 e^{kT_2} \implies T_2 = \frac{\ln 2}{k} \approx \frac{0.693}{k}$$

The famous **Rule of 70**: for a growth rate of $k \times 100%$ per year, doubling time $\approx 70/(\text{percent rate})$ years.

### Half-Life ($k < 0$, write $k = -\lambda$, $\lambda > 0$)

The time $t_{1/2}$ for $P$ to halve:

$$\frac{P_0}{2} = P_0 e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}} \implies t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda}$$

**Key fact:** Half-life is **independent of $P_0$**. No matter how much substance you start with, it always takes the same time to halve.

## 5. Examples

---

### Example 1 — Radioactive Decay: Carbon-14

Carbon-14 decays with half-life $t_{1/2} = 5730$ years.

**Find $k$:**

$$k = -\frac{\ln 2}{5730} \approx -1.2097 \times 10^{-4} \text{ yr}^{-1}$$

**Model:** $P(t) = P_0, e^{-1.2097 \times 10^{-4}, t}$

**Radiocarbon dating:** A bone fragment has $72%$ of original C-14. How old is it?

$$0.72 P_0 = P_0, e^{kt} \implies t = \frac{\ln 0.72}{k} = \frac{\ln 0.72}{-1.2097 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 2694 \text{ years}$$

```
P/P₀
1.0 ●
    │\
0.5 │ \●─────────── ← half-life at t = 5730
    │   \
0.25│    \●────────── ← t = 11460 (two half-lives)
    │     \
0.125      \●──────── ← t = 17190
    └──────────────────→ t (years)
       5730 11460 17190
```

### Example 2 — Population Growth: Malthusian Model

A bacterial population of 500 doubles every 3 hours.

**Find $k$:**

$$T_2 = 3 \implies k = \frac{\ln 2}{3} \approx 0.2310 \text{ hr}^{-1}$$

**Model:** $P(t) = 500, e^{0.2310, t}$

**Question:** How many bacteria after 24 hours?

$$P(24) = 500, e^{0.2310 \times 24} = 500, e^{5.545} \approx 500 \times 256 = 128{,}000$$

(Exactly $500 \times 2^8$ — 8 doublings in 24 hours. $\checkmark$)

**Question:** When does the population reach 1 million?

$$10^6 = 500, e^{0.2310, t} \implies t = \frac{\ln(2000)}{0.2310} \approx \frac{7.601}{0.2310} \approx 32.9 \text{ hours}$$

---

### Example 3 — Newton's Law of Cooling (Shifted Natural Decay)

Newton's Law of Cooling states that the rate of change of temperature $T(t)$ of an object is proportional to the difference between $T$ and the ambient temperature $T_a$:

$$\frac{dT}{dt} = k(T - T_a), \quad k < 0$$

**Substitution:** Let $u(t) = T(t) - T_a$. Then $u' = T'$, so:

$$\frac{du}{dt} = ku$$

This is the natural decay equation! Solution: $u(t) = u_0, e^{kt}$, i.e.:

$$\boxed{T(t) = T_a + (T_0 - T_a), e^{kt}}$$

**Example:** Coffee at $90°C$ in a $20°C$ room. After 10 min, coffee is $70°C$. Find $T(30)$.

**Find $k$:**

$$70 = 20 + (90 - 20)e^{10k} \implies 50 = 70 e^{10k} \implies k = \frac{1}{10}\ln\frac{5}{7} \approx -0.03365 \text{ min}^{-1}$$

**At $t = 30$:**

$$T(30) = 20 + 70, e^{-0.03365 \times 30} = 20 + 70, e^{-1.0094} \approx 20 + 70(0.3644) \approx 45.5°C$$

```
T(t) °C
 90 ●
    │\
 70 │ ●
    │  \
 45 │   \  ●
    │    \   \
 20 ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─  ← T_a = 20°C (asymptote)
    └──────────────────────────→ t
     0    10    20    30  (min)
```

---

### Example 4 — Compound Interest

A bank offers continuous compounding at annual rate $r$. Principal $P_0$ dollars.

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP \implies P(t) = P_0, e^{rt}$$

**Comparison with discrete compounding $n$ times/year:**

$$P_n(t) = P_0\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$$

As $n \to \infty$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P_0\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt} = P_0, e^{rt}$$

This is one of the most important limit definitions of $e$ — the natural growth ODE is the continuous limit of compound interest.

---

### Example 5 — Two Measurements, No Initial Condition Given

**Setup:** A radioactive substance measures 8 g at $t = 0$ and 5 g at $t = 1$ year. When will 1 g remain?

**Find $k$** (no need for half-life):

$$5 = 8, e^{k \cdot 1} \implies k = \ln\frac{5}{8} \approx -0.4700 \text{ yr}^{-1}$$

**Model:** $P(t) = 8, e^{-0.4700, t}$

**When is $P = 1$?**

$$1 = 8, e^{-0.4700, t} \implies t = \frac{\ln(1/8)}{-0.4700} = \frac{3\ln 2}{0.4700} \approx 4.42 \text{ years}$$

### Phase Line

For $P' = kP$:

```
k > 0:     ←──── 0 ────→          (0 is unstable equilibrium)
            decay  growth

k < 0:     ────→ 0 ←────          (0 is stable equilibrium)
```

The only equilibrium is $P^* = 0$. Its stability depends entirely on the sign of $k$.

## 7. The Exponential Function IS the Solution

The natural growth ODE $y' = ky$ can be taken as the **defining property** of the exponential function:

> $e^{kt}$ is the unique function equal to $k$ times its own derivative, with value 1 at $t = 0$.

This is why $e$ appears everywhere in calculus — it is the base that makes the derivative of $e^x$ exactly $e^x$ (i.e., $k = 1$). Every other base $a^x = e^{x \ln a}$ satisfies $\frac{d}{dx}a^x = (\ln a), a^x$ — scaling the rate by $\ln a$.

## 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

The natural growth/decay law is not a background fact in ML — it is the **structural skeleton** of training dynamics, weight behavior, and information propagation.

---

### 8.1 Gradient Flow — Parameters Decay to Minimum

For a quadratic loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{\lambda}{2}\theta^2$ (L2-regularized), gradient flow gives:

$$\frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} = -\lambda\theta$$

This is natural decay with rate $k = -\lambda$:

$$\theta(t) = \theta_0, e^{-\lambda t}$$

The parameter **decays exponentially toward zero** at rate $\lambda$ — the regularization strength is literally the decay constant. This is why L2 regularization is also called **weight decay**.

More generally, near a local minimum $\theta^_$, the Hessian $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}(\theta^_)$ governs the linearized dynamics. Each eigenmode $v_i$ of $H$ (with eigenvalue $\lambda_i > 0$) decays independently as $e^{-\lambda_i t}$. **Directions with large curvature ($\lambda_i$ large) decay fast; flat directions ($\lambda_i$ small) decay slowly.**

---

### 8.2 Vanishing and Exploding Gradients — Natural Growth in Backpropagation

In a deep network with $L$ layers, the gradient of the loss with respect to layer $\ell$ involves a product of Jacobians:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_\ell} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_L} \cdot \prod_{i=\ell}^{L-1} \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{i+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_i}$$

If each Jacobian has spectral norm $\rho$ (dominant eigenvalue), this product behaves as $\rho^{L - \ell}$:

$$\left|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_\ell}\right| \approx \left|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_L}\right| \cdot \rho^{L - \ell}$$

This is discrete natural growth/decay in depth $L - \ell$ (playing the role of time):

- $\rho < 1$: **vanishing gradients** — exponential decay in depth.
- $\rho > 1$: **exploding gradients** — exponential growth in depth.
- $\rho = 1$: neutral propagation — why **residual connections** (ResNets) and careful weight initialization (Xavier, He) aim for $\rho \approx 1$.

The law of natural growth is precisely why deep networks are hard to train without architectural care.

---

### 8.3 Learning Rate Decay Schedules

The most common learning rate schedule is **exponential decay**:

$$\eta(t) = \eta_0, e^{-\lambda t}$$

This solves $\frac{d\eta}{dt} = -\lambda \eta$ — natural decay applied to the learning rate itself. The intuition:

- Early training ($t$ small): large $\eta$ for fast progress.
- Late training ($t$ large): small $\eta$ for fine-grained convergence.

The decay constant $\lambda$ controls the tradeoff. In practice, the discrete version is:

$$\eta_t = \eta_0 \cdot \gamma^{t/T}, \quad \gamma \in (0,1)$$

which is $e^{-(t/T)\ln(1/\gamma)}$ — exactly $P_0 e^{kt}$ with $k = -\frac{\ln(1/\gamma)}{T}$.

---

### 8.4 Weight Decay IS Natural Decay

The L2-regularized SGD update:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) - \eta \lambda \theta_t = (1 - \eta\lambda)\theta_t - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

The factor $(1 - \eta\lambda)$ multiplied at each step is the discrete natural decay: after $T$ steps with no gradient signal,

$$\theta_T = (1 - \eta\lambda)^T \theta_0 \approx e^{-\eta\lambda T} \theta_0$$

(using $(1-x)^n \approx e^{-nx}$ for small $x$). Weight decay is natural decay in discrete time — parameter values shrink exponentially toward zero unless gradients push back.

---

### 8.5 Dropout as Stochastic Decay

At each forward pass, dropout retains each neuron with probability $p$ and zeroes it with probability $1-p$. Over $T$ training steps, the expected magnitude of a weight that receives no useful gradient signal decays as:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{effective weight}] \sim p^T \cdot w_0 = e^{T \ln p} \cdot w_0$$

Since $\ln p < 0$ for $p \in (0,1)$, this is natural decay in $T$ — dropout induces an effective exponential shrinkage of "useless" weights.

---

### 8.6 Attention Score Softmax — Natural Growth Normalizing

The softmax function in attention:

$$\text{softmax}(z_i) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}$$

The numerator $e^{z_i}$ is the natural growth function evaluated at the attention logit. The softmax is not merely a convenient normalization — it is the **Boltzmann distribution** arising from entropy maximization, and the exponential is there because the entropy-maximizing distribution over energies $z_i$ takes the form $e^{z_i / T}$ (here temperature $T = 1$). The law of natural growth is the generating mechanism behind every attention score in every transformer.

---

### 8.7 Information-Theoretic Interpretation — KL Divergence and Exponential Families

The natural growth solution $P(t) = P_0 e^{kt}$ belongs to the **exponential family** of distributions — the most important parametric family in statistics and machine learning:

$$p(x; \eta) = h(x)\exp(\eta \cdot T(x) - A(\eta))$$

Every distribution used in deep learning (Gaussian, Bernoulli, Categorical, Poisson) is an exponential family member. Their canonical form is precisely the natural growth function in the sufficient statistic $T(x)$ — which is why the cross-entropy loss (used to train virtually every classifier) takes logarithmic form: $\log p = \eta \cdot T(x) - A(\eta) + \log h(x)$, recovering $kt$ from $e^{kt}$.

## 10. Common Pitfalls

1. **Confusing $k$ and $t_{1/2}$ or $T_2$.** The growth rate is $k = \ln 2 / T_2 = -\ln 2 / t_{1/2}$. Always derive $k$ explicitly before writing the solution.
2. **Using base 2 or base 10 instead of $e$.** The ODE $P' = kP$ forces base $e$. If the problem states doubling time $T_2$, write $P(t) = P_0 \cdot 2^{t/T_2} = P_0 e^{(t/T_2)\ln 2}$ — both are equivalent, but the ODE derivation always produces the $e$-form.
3. **Forgetting to check $P = 0$.** The separation step assumes $P \neq 0$; verify that $P = 0$ is also a solution (it is, trivially).
4. **Newton's cooling — not applying the shift.** Set $u = T - T_a$ first; then it becomes natural decay. Working directly on $T' = k(T - T_a)$ without substitution is error-prone.
5. **Assuming $k$ is always negative for "decay."** $k$ is negative for decay, positive for growth. The sign is determined by the physics/problem statement, not by convention.
