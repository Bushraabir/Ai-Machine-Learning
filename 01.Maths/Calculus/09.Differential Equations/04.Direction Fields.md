## 0. The Central Idea

Most ODEs cannot be solved explicitly. Yet we can _see_ the solution without solving anything.

The direction field (also called a **slope field**) is a picture of the ODE itself ‚Äî before any solving. At every point $(x, y)$ in the plane, the ODE $y' = f(x, y)$ tells us the slope a solution curve must have if it passes through that point. Draw a tiny line segment with that slope at each point, and the plane fills with arrows that _guide_ solution curves like a river guides a boat.

> **Epistemological point worth internalizing:** A direction field is not an approximation. It is an _exact_ graphical representation of the ODE. The solution curves are the unique curves that are everywhere tangent to the field.

---

## 1. Definition

Let $y' = f(x, y)$ be a first-order ODE. The **direction field** (slope field) is the assignment:

$$ (x, y) ;\longmapsto; \text{line segment through } (x,y) \text{ with slope } f(x,y) $$

defined at every point in the domain of $f$.

A **solution curve** (integral curve) is a curve $y = \phi(x)$ such that at every point on the curve, the tangent line coincides exactly with the direction field segment at that point:

$$ \phi'(x) = f(x, \phi(x)) \quad \text{for all } x \text{ in the domain.} $$

## 2. How to Sketch a Direction Field by Hand

### 2.1 The Isocline Method

An **isocline** is the set of points where the slope takes a fixed value $c$:

$$ \mathcal{I}_c = {(x, y) : f(x, y) = c} $$

Along each isocline, all direction-field segments have the _same_ slope $c$. This makes sketching systematic:

**Procedure:**

1. Choose several values $c \in {-2, -1, 0, 1, 2, \ldots}$.
2. Plot the curve $f(x,y) = c$ (the isocline) for each $c$.
3. Along each isocline, draw parallel line segments of slope $c$.
4. Fill in between by interpolation.

### 2.2 Special Case: The Nullcline

The isocline $c = 0$ is called the **nullcline**: $f(x,y) = 0$.

Along the nullcline, all segments are **horizontal** ($y' = 0$). Solution curves have local extrema exactly where they cross the nullcline. This is often the most informative single curve to draw first.

## 3. Autonomous ODEs and Phase Lines

### 3.1 Definition

An ODE $y' = f(y)$ is **autonomous** if $f$ does not explicitly depend on $x$. The direction field has a special structure: **all segments on a given horizontal line $y = c$ have the same slope** $f(c)$.

This means the direction field is translation-invariant in $x$ ‚Äî horizontally shifting a solution gives another solution.

### 3.2 The Phase Line

For autonomous $y' = f(y)$, the full qualitative story is captured on a **1D phase line** ‚Äî just the $y$-axis:

**Procedure:**

1. Find equilibria: $f(y^*) = 0$.
2. Determine the sign of $f(y)$ in each interval between equilibria.
3. Draw arrows: $\uparrow$ where $f > 0$ (solutions increase), $\downarrow$ where $f < 0$ (solutions decrease).
4. Classify each equilibrium:
    - **Stable:** arrows point _toward_ $y^*$ from both sides.
    - **Unstable:** arrows point _away_ from $y^*$ on both sides.
    - **Semi-stable:** arrows on one side point toward, the other away.

**Example: $y' = y(1-y)(2-y)$**

Equilibria: $y^* = 0, 1, 2$.

Sign of $f$:

- $y < 0$: $f = (-)((+))(+) < 0$ ‚Üì
- $0 < y < 1$: $f = (+)((+))(+) > 0$ ‚Üë ... wait, $(1-y) > 0$, $(2-y) > 0$, $y > 0$: $f > 0$ ‚Üë
- $1 < y < 2$: $f = (+)((-))( +) < 0$ ‚Üì
- $y > 2$: $f = (+)((-))(-) > 0$ ‚Üë

Phase line:

```
y
‚Üë  ‚Üë  ‚Üë
2  ‚óè  ‚Üê‚îÄ‚îÄ unstable (arrows away below, away above... wait)
‚Üì  ‚Üì  ‚Üì     actually: ‚Üì below, ‚Üë above ‚Üí unstable
1  ‚óè  ‚Üê‚îÄ‚îÄ stable (‚Üë below, ‚Üì above)
‚Üë  ‚Üë  ‚Üë
0  ‚óè  ‚Üê‚îÄ‚îÄ unstable (‚Üì below, ‚Üë above)
‚Üì  ‚Üì  ‚Üì
```

Wait ‚Äî re-examine at $y=2$: below $y=2$ (i.e., $1<y<2$): $f < 0$ ‚Üì; above $y=2$ (i.e., $y>2$): $f > 0$ ‚Üë. So arrows diverge from $y=2$: **unstable**. At $y=1$: below $‚Üë$, above $‚Üì$: **stable**. At $y=0$: below $‚Üì$, above $‚Üë$: **unstable**.

```
     ‚Üë (y > 2: rising)
 2 ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚óè‚îÄ‚îÄ unstable
     ‚Üì (1 < y < 2: falling)
 1 ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚óè‚îÄ‚îÄ stable  ‚Üê global attractor for 0 < y < 2
     ‚Üë (0 < y < 1: rising)
 0 ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚óè‚îÄ‚îÄ unstable
     ‚Üì (y < 0: falling)
```

The direction field in the $(x,y)$-plane is the "extrusion" of this phase line horizontally ‚Äî the same arrow pattern repeated at every $x$.

## 4. Reading Qualitative Information from Direction Fields

A direction field lets you answer the following questions _without solving_:

|Question|How to read from the field|
|---|---|
|Where are equilibria?|Horizontal rows of horizontal segments (autonomous)|
|Is an equilibrium stable?|Do nearby segments point toward or away from it?|
|Do solutions blow up?|Do slopes steepen and curve sharply upward/downward?|
|Are solutions bounded?|Do all arrows funnel into a bounded band?|
|What is the long-run behavior?|Follow arrows from any initial condition forward|
|Do solutions have inflection points?|Where the slope $f(x,y)$ itself is neither increasing nor decreasing along trajectories|

### 4.1 Funnel and Spout

- **Funnel:** A region in the direction field where all solutions entering it converge to a single curve. Indicates a stable attractor.
- **Spout (anti-funnel):** A region where solutions diverge. Indicates an unstable trajectory.

For $y' = x - y$ above: the line $y = x - 1$ is a **funnel** ‚Äî all solutions flow toward it as $x \to \infty$.

## 5. From Direction Field to Euler's Method

Direction fields are not just pictures ‚Äî they are the geometric soul of **Euler's method**.

Recall Euler's method for $y' = f(x,y)$, $y(x_0) = y_0$, step $h$:

$$ y_{n+1} = y_n + h \cdot f(x_n, y_n) $$

**Geometric interpretation:** Start at $(x_0, y_0)$. The direction field gives slope $f(x_0, y_0)$. Walk along that segment for horizontal distance $h$ to reach $(x_1, y_1)$. Repeat.

```
y
 |        ___true solution curve
 |      _/¬∑
 |    _/ ¬∑¬∑   ‚Üê Euler polygonal path
 |  _/  ¬∑
 |_/  ¬∑
 | ‚Üó¬∑
 |/¬∑
 +‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚Üí x
  x‚ÇÄ  x‚ÇÅ  x‚ÇÇ  x‚ÇÉ

Each arrow = one direction-field segment walked for distance h.
```

Euler's method is literally _following the direction field_ one step at a time. The error at each step arises because the field changes direction before we correct our heading ‚Äî the smaller $h$, the more frequently we re-consult the field, the more accurate the path.

This geometric picture explains:

- Why Euler's method underestimates when the solution is concave-up (the slope is rising, but we're using the old slope too long).
- Why RK4 is better: it samples the field at multiple points within each step, computing a weighted average slope ‚Äî a more faithful reading of the field.

## 6. Existence, Uniqueness, and the Direction Field

Picard‚ÄìLindel√∂f guarantees (under continuity + Lipschitz) that **exactly one solution curve passes through each point**. This has a direct visual consequence:

> **Theorem (Non-Crossing).** If the conditions of Picard‚ÄìLindel√∂f hold, then distinct solution curves of $y' = f(x,y)$ **cannot intersect**.

**Proof.** Suppose two distinct solutions $\phi_1$ and $\phi_2$ both pass through $(x_0, y_0)$: $\phi_1(x_0) = \phi_2(x_0) = y_0$. By uniqueness, $\phi_1 \equiv \phi_2$ on the interval ‚Äî contradiction. $\blacksquare$

**Visual corollary:** In a well-posed direction field, the solution curves form **non-intersecting, non-crossing families** that foliate the plane. Like streamlines of a fluid ‚Äî they never cross.

**Where uniqueness fails:** At points where $f$ or $\partial f/\partial y$ is discontinuous, the non-crossing guarantee breaks, and multiple solution curves can pass through a single point (as in the $y' = y^{1/3}$ example).

### 7.2 Example: $y' = \sin(y)$ (Autonomous)

**Equilibria:** $\sin(y^_) = 0 \Rightarrow y^_ = n\pi$, $n \in \mathbb{Z}$.

**Sign pattern:** $\sin(y) > 0$ for $y \in (0, \pi)$; $\sin(y) < 0$ for $y \in (\pi, 2\pi)$; etc.

**Stability:**

- $y^* = 2k\pi$: $\frac{d}{dy}\sin(y)\big|_{y^*} = \cos(2k\pi) = 1 > 0$ ‚Üí **unstable** (derivative test)
- $y^* = (2k+1)\pi$: $\cos((2k+1)\pi) = -1 < 0$ ‚Üí **stable**

**Linearization stability test:** For autonomous $y' = f(y)$, equilibrium $y^*$ is:

- **Stable** if $f'(y^*) < 0$ (slope of $f$ is negative at the fixed point)
- **Unstable** if $f'(y^*) > 0$

_This is the 1D version of the eigenvalue stability criterion from [[General Differential Equations]] ¬ß6._

**Phase line for $y' = \sin y$:**

```

```

The direction field is a horizontal striped pattern: bands of rising and falling, alternating at each multiple of $\pi$.

## 8. The Geometry of $y' = f(x, y)$: Deeper Structure

### 8.1 Level Sets of $f$ Are Isoclines

The isoclines $f(x,y) = c$ are the **level curves** of $f$ viewed as a function of two variables. Sketching isoclines is exactly the same skill as sketching level curves from MIT 18.02 ‚Äî the direction field connects ODE theory to multivariable calculus.

### 8.2 The Direction Field as a Line Field

Formally, a direction field is a **line field** on the plane: at each point $(x,y)$, we assign a 1-dimensional linear subspace of $\mathbb{R}^2$ ‚Äî the tangent line of slope $f(x,y)$. This is a section of the **projectivized tangent bundle** $PT\mathbb{R}^2$.

A solution curve is a **horizontal section** ‚Äî a curve whose tangent direction is everywhere in the assigned line. This is the beginning of the geometric theory of ODEs (Lie, Cartan, Frobenius), which generalizes to PDEs and differential geometry.

### 8.3 Integral Curves as Trajectories

The direction field of $y' = f(x,y)$ can be written as the vector field:

$$ \mathbf{v}(x,y) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ f(x,y) \end{pmatrix} $$

(We always move at speed 1 in the $x$-direction.) The integral curves of $\mathbf{v}$ ‚Äî curves everywhere tangent to $\mathbf{v}$ ‚Äî are exactly the solution curves. This is the MIT 18.02 viewpoint: **solving an ODE = finding integral curves of a vector field**.

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 9.1 Loss Landscape as a Direction Field

In optimization, the gradient flow $\dot{\theta} = -\nabla L(\theta)$ defines a vector field (direction field) on the parameter space. The direction field of this ODE is the **negative gradient field** ‚Äî the arrows point in the direction of steepest descent.

Gradient descent is **Euler's method** on this direction field. Every insight from ¬ß5 applies directly:

- Large learning rate $h$: coarse reading of the field ‚Üí possible overshoot.
- Small learning rate: fine reading ‚Üí slow but accurate.
- The loss landscape's basins, ridges, and saddles are the direction field's funnels, spouts, and saddle trajectories.

Visualizing the direction field of $\dot{\theta} = -\nabla L(\theta)$ for a simple model (say, 2D parameter space) is literally the same exercise as drawing the direction field of a first-order ODE.

### 9.2 Neural ODE Vector Fields

In a Neural ODE, the network $f_\theta(h, t)$ _defines_ a direction field in the hidden-state space $\mathbb{R}^d$. Training the network is training the direction field itself ‚Äî adjusting $\theta$ to make the integral curves of the field match the desired input-output mapping.

The direction field picture makes certain properties immediately visible:

- If the field is convergent (funneling), the network is robust to perturbations.
- If the field has spirals, the hidden state oscillates ‚Äî potentially useful for time-series.
- If the field has saddles, nearby trajectories diverge exponentially ‚Äî dangerous for stability.

### 9.3 Score Functions in Diffusion Models

The **score function** $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ in a diffusion model is exactly the direction field for the reverse-time SDE. At each point $x$ in data space at time $t$, the score tells you the direction (slope) in which $p_t$ increases most rapidly. The reverse diffusion process follows this direction field from noise back to data ‚Äî it is an integral curve problem.

Learning the score is learning the direction field of the reverse process.

### 9.4 Continuous Normalizing Flows

A **normalizing flow** transforms a simple distribution (e.g., Gaussian) into a complex one via a learned diffeomorphism. The continuous version (CNF) defines this transformation as the flow of an ODE:

$$ \frac{dz}{dt} = f_\theta(z, t), \qquad z(0) \sim p_0 $$

The direction field of $f_\theta$ determines the transformation. The Jacobian of the flow ‚Äî needed to compute the change-of-variables formula for the log-likelihood ‚Äî is computed via the **instantaneous change of variables** formula:

$$ \frac{d\log p(z(t))}{dt} = -\text{tr}!\left(\frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial z}\right) $$

This is a consequence of **Liouville's theorem** from classical mechanics: the divergence of the direction field governs the expansion/compression of probability mass.

### 9.5 Summary

|Direction Field Concept|ML/DL Analogue|
|---|---|
|Direction field / slope field|Gradient field $-\nabla L(\theta)$ in parameter space|
|Integral curve / solution curve|Gradient descent trajectory|
|Euler's method on field|SGD with fixed learning rate|

|Score function $\nabla\log p_t$|Direction field of reverse diffusion SDE|
|Liouville / flow divergence|Log-likelihood in continuous normalizing flows|

## 10. Formal Summary: What a Direction Field Encodes

Given $y' = f(x,y)$:

$$ \text{Direction field} ;\longleftrightarrow; \begin{cases} \text{Geometry of all solutions simultaneously} \ \text{Location and stability of equilibria (autonomous)} \ \text{Long-run behavior without solving} \ \text{Initial value dependence (non-crossing foliation)} \ \text{Numerical method geometry (Euler, RK4)} \end{cases} $$

The direction field is the **qualitative theory** of the ODE made visual.

## 11. Quick-Reference

**Isocline:** $f(x,y) = c$ ‚Äî curve along which all segments have slope $c$.

**Nullcline:** $f(x,y) = 0$ ‚Äî horizontal segments, location of extrema.

**Autonomous ODE $y' = f(y)$:**

- Equilibria at $f(y^*) = 0$
- Stable if $f'(y^_) < 0$; unstable if $f'(y^_) > 0$
- Phase line = 1D direction field on $y$-axis

**Non-crossing theorem:** Under Picard‚ÄìLindel√∂f, distinct solution curves never intersect.

**Euler's method = walking along direction field** with step $h$.

**Stability test (linearization):** Eigenvalue of $f'(y^_)$ determines stability in 1D; eigenvalues of Jacobian $Df(\mathbf{x}^_)$ in 2D.

5. **Stability via linearization.** For $y' = \sin(\pi y)$: find all equilibria in $[-2, 2]$, compute $f'(y^*)$ at each, classify as stable or unstable, and draw the phase line.
    
6. **Funnel.** For $y' = -y + \sin x$: the particular solution is $y_p = \frac{1}{2}(\sin x - \cos x)$. Using the direction field, argue (without solving) that all solutions converge to $y_p$ as $x \to \infty$. What does this say about the direction field far from $y_p$?
    
7. **Neural ODE connection.** Consider the direction field $\dot{h} = -h + \tanh(wh)$ for $h \in \mathbb{R}$, $w > 0$. Find all equilibria (as a function of $w$). Show that for $w \leq 1$ there is only one equilibrium, but for $w > 1$ there are three ‚Äî a **bifurcation**. Sketch the phase line for each case and relate this to the expressivity of a Neural ODE unit.
