## 0. Orientation

A differential equation is a _constraint on a function expressed through its own rates of change._ Nature tends to know rates — not states. It knows how fast a population grows, how fast heat diffuses, how fast a voltage changes. The differential equation is the mathematical language in which nature speaks; our job is to decode it into explicit functions.

This note builds the full architecture: classification → existence/uniqueness → first-order methods → second-order theory → systems → qualitative analysis → the ML thread running through all of it.

---

## 1. What Is a Differential Equation?

A **differential equation (DE)** is an equation involving an unknown function and one or more of its derivatives.

**General implicit form:**

$$ F!\left(x,, y,, y',, y'',, \ldots,, y^{(n)}\right) = 0 $$

where $y = y(x)$ is the unknown, $x$ is the independent variable.

**A solution** is any function $y = \phi(x)$ defined on some interval $I$ such that substituting $\phi$ and its derivatives into the equation produces a true identity for all $x \in I$.

---

## 2. Classification

Every DE should be classified along four axes before attempting a solution. Classification determines which tools apply.

### 2.1 Ordinary vs. Partial

|Type|Unknown depends on|Derivatives|Notation|
|---|---|---|---|
|**ODE**|one variable $x$|ordinary $\frac{dy}{dx}$|$y' = f(x,y)$|
|**PDE**|multiple variables $x,t,\ldots$|partial $\frac{\partial u}{\partial x}$|$u_t = \alpha u_{xx}$|

_All of §3–§7 concern ODEs. PDEs appear briefly in §8._

### 2.2 Order

The **order** is the highest derivative present.

$$ y'' + y = 0 \quad \text{(order 2)}, \qquad \frac{dy}{dx} = y^2 \quad \text{(order 1)}, \qquad y''' = e^x \quad \text{(order 3)} $$

### 2.3 Linearity

An ODE of order $n$ is **linear** if it has the form:

$$ a_n(x),y^{(n)} + a_{n-1}(x),y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + a_1(x),y' + a_0(x),y = g(x) $$

Key requirements:

- The unknown $y$ and each derivative $y^{(k)}$ appear **to the first power**.
- No products like $y \cdot y'$ or functions like $\sin(y)$, $e^y$.

If these fail, the ODE is **nonlinear**.

|ODE|Linear?|Reason|
|---|---|---|
|$y'' - 3y' + 2y = e^x$|✓ Yes|Coefficients depend only on $x$|
|$y' = y^2$|✗ No|$y^2$ is nonlinear|
|$y' + (\sin x)y = 0$|✓ Yes|$\sin x$ is a coefficient, not involving $y$|
|$yy'' = 1$|✗ No|Product $yy''$|
|$y'' + \sin(y) = 0$|✗ No|Pendulum equation — $\sin(y)$ nonlinear|

### 2.4 Homogeneity (for Linear ODEs)

A linear ODE is:

- **Homogeneous** if $g(x) = 0$: $\quad a_n y^{(n)} + \cdots + a_0 y = 0$
- **Non-homogeneous (inhomogeneous)** if $g(x) \neq 0$

_Warning:_ "Homogeneous" has a different (unrelated) meaning for first-order nonlinear ODEs — see §4.4.

## 3. Existence and Uniqueness

Before solving, we ask: _does a solution exist, and is it unique?_ These are not pedantic questions — they tell you whether your model is well-posed.

### 3.1 First-Order IVP (Picard–Lindelöf Theorem)

**Theorem (Picard–Lindelöf).** Consider the initial value problem:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y), \qquad y(x_0) = y_0 $$

If $f$ is **continuous** in a rectangle $R = {|x - x_0| \leq a,; |y - y_0| \leq b}$ and $\partial f / \partial y$ is **continuous** (equivalently, $f$ is Lipschitz in $y$) on $R$, then:

1. A solution **exists** on some interval $|x - x_0| < h$.
2. The solution is **unique** on that interval.

**Lipschitz condition (precise):** $\exists, L > 0$ such that $|f(x,y_1) - f(x,y_2)| \leq L|y_1 - y_2|$ for all $(x,y_1),(x,y_2) \in R$.

_The Lipschitz constant $L$ controls how fast nearby solutions can diverge — it is the local "sensitivity" of the ODE._

### 3.2 Failure of Uniqueness: A Classic Example

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = y^{1/3}, \qquad y(0) = 0 $$

Here $\partial f/\partial y = \frac{1}{3}y^{-2/3}$ blows up at $y = 0$ — Lipschitz fails. Two distinct solutions pass through $(0,0)$:

$$ y_1(x) = 0 \qquad \text{and} \qquad y_2(x) = \left(\frac{2x}{3}\right)^{3/2} $$

Both satisfy the ODE and initial condition. Uniqueness fails exactly where Lipschitz fails. $\blacksquare$

### 3.3 Second-Order IVP

**Theorem.** For $y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = G(x)$ with $P, Q, G$ continuous on interval $I$ and $(x_0, y_0, y_0') \in I \times \mathbb{R}^2$, there exists a **unique** solution on all of $I$.

_Note: the solution interval is the entire interval of continuity of the coefficients — much stronger than the first-order result._

## 4. First-Order ODEs: Solution Methods

### 4.1 Separable Equations

**Form:** $\frac{dy}{dx} = g(x),h(y)$

**Method:** Separate and integrate.

$$ \frac{dy}{h(y)} = g(x),dx \implies \int \frac{dy}{h(y)} = \int g(x),dx $$

**Example:** $\frac{dy}{dx} = xy$

$$ \frac{dy}{y} = x,dx \implies \ln|y| = \frac{x^2}{2} + C \implies y = Ae^{x^2/2} $$

_This is the Gaussian! It appears as the fundamental solution to the heat equation and as the normal distribution in probability — the solution to the "most entropic" separable ODE._

### 4.2 Linear First-Order: Integrating Factor

**Form:** $\frac{dy}{dx} + P(x)y = Q(x)$

**Integrating factor:** $\mu(x) = e^{\int P(x),dx}$

Multiply both sides by $\mu$:

$$ \frac{d}{dx}[\mu(x),y] = \mu(x),Q(x) $$

Integrate:

$$ \boxed{y = \frac{1}{\mu(x)}\left[\int \mu(x),Q(x),dx + C\right]} $$

**Why does this work?** We are manufacturing a perfect derivative on the left. The product rule gives $\frac{d}{dx}[\mu y] = \mu y' + \mu' y$. For this to equal $\mu(y' + Py)$, we need $\mu' = \mu P$, i.e., $\mu = e^{\int P,dx}$. $\blacksquare$

**Example:** $y' + \frac{2}{x}y = x^2$, $\quad x > 0$

$$ \mu = e^{\int 2/x, dx} = e^{2\ln x} = x^2 $$

$$ \frac{d}{dx}[x^2 y] = x^4 \implies x^2 y = \frac{x^5}{5} + C \implies y = \frac{x^3}{5} + \frac{C}{x^2} $$

### 4.3 Exact Equations

**Form:** $M(x,y),dx + N(x,y),dy = 0$

**Exactness condition:** $\frac{\partial M}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial N}{\partial x}$

If exact, there exists $F(x,y)$ with $F_x = M$, $F_y = N$, and the solution is $F(x,y) = C$ (a family of level curves).

**Finding $F$:**

$$ F = \int M,dx + g(y) \quad \text{then solve } F_y = N \text{ for } g'(y) $$

**Example:** $(2xy + 3),dx + (x^2 - 4y^3),dy = 0$

Check: $M_y = 2x$, $N_x = 2x$. ✓ Exact.

$$ F = \int (2xy+3),dx = x^2 y + 3x + g(y) $$ $$ F_y = x^2 + g'(y) = x^2 - 4y^3 \implies g'(y) = -4y^3 \implies g(y) = -y^4 $$

Solution: $x^2 y + 3x - y^4 = C$

### 4.4 Homogeneous First-Order (Substitution $v = y/x$)

**Form:** $\frac{dy}{dx} = F!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)$

**Substitution:** $v = y/x$, so $y = vx$, $y' = v + xv'$:

$$ v + xv' = F(v) \implies \frac{dv}{F(v) - v} = \frac{dx}{x} $$

Now separable. Solve for $v(x)$, then $y = vx$.

### 4.5 Bernoulli Equation

**Form:** $y' + P(x)y = Q(x)y^n$, $n \neq 0, 1$

**Substitution:** $w = y^{1-n}$, which linearizes the equation:

$$ w' + (1-n)P(x)w = (1-n)Q(x) $$

Now a linear first-order ODE — solve by integrating factor.

### 4.6 Summary Table of First-Order Methods

|Type|Recognizer|Method|
|---|---|---|
|Separable|$y' = g(x)h(y)$|Separate and integrate|
|Linear|$y' + P(x)y = Q(x)$|Integrating factor $e^{\int P,dx}$|
|Exact|$M,dx + N,dy=0$, $M_y=N_x$|Find potential $F$|
|Homogeneous|$y' = F(y/x)$|$v = y/x$|
|Bernoulli|$y' + Py = Qy^n$|$w = y^{1-n}$|
|Logistic|$y' = ry(1-y/K)$|Partial fractions (see [[Models for Population Growth]])|

## 5. Second-Order Linear ODEs

### 5.1 Structure Theorem

The complete general solution of the **non-homogeneous** linear ODE:

$$ y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = G(x) $$

is:

$$ \boxed{y = y_h + y_p} $$

where:

- $y_h = C_1 y_1 + C_2 y_2$ is the **general homogeneous solution** ($y_1, y_2$ linearly independent solutions of the homogeneous equation),
- $y_p$ is **any particular solution** of the full non-homogeneous equation.

**Proof sketch:** If $y$ is any solution, then $y - y_p$ satisfies the homogeneous equation (by linearity), so $y - y_p \in \text{span}{y_1, y_2}$. $\blacksquare$

### 5.2 Constant Coefficients: The Characteristic Equation

For $ay'' + by' + cy = 0$, try $y = e^{rx}$:

$$ ar^2 + br + c = 0 \implies r = \frac{-b \pm \sqrt{b^2 - 4ac}}{2a} $$

The discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$ governs the three cases:

**Case 1: $\Delta > 0$ (two distinct real roots $r_1 \neq r_2$)**

$$ y_h = C_1 e^{r_1 x} + C_2 e^{r_2 x} $$

**Case 2: $\Delta = 0$ (repeated root $r_1 = r_2 = r$)**

$$ y_h = (C_1 + C_2 x),e^{rx} $$

_Why $xe^{rx}$?_ We need two linearly independent solutions. Since $e^{rx}$ is already one, we use **reduction of order**: assume $y_2 = v(x)e^{rx}$, substitute, and solve for $v(x) = x$. $\blacksquare$

**Case 3: $\Delta < 0$ (complex conjugate roots $r = \alpha \pm i\beta$)**

$$ y_h = e^{\alpha x}(C_1\cos\beta x + C_2\sin\beta x) $$

_Euler's formula $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta$ converts complex exponentials to real sinusoids._

### 5.3 The Wronskian and Linear Independence

For two solutions $y_1, y_2$, the **Wronskian** is:

$$ W(y_1, y_2)(x) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \ y_1' & y_2' \end{vmatrix} = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1' $$

**Theorem (Abel):** $W(x) = W(x_0),\exp!\left(-\int_{x_0}^x P(t),dt\right)$

**Consequence:** $W$ is either **identically zero** (dependent) or **never zero** (independent) on $I$. There is no middle ground.

$y_1, y_2$ form a **fundamental set of solutions** $\iff$ $W(y_1, y_2) \neq 0$ on $I$.

### 5.4 Finding $y_p$: Method of Undetermined Coefficients

Applies when coefficients are **constant** and $G(x)$ is of a "nice" type. Guess $y_p$ based on the form of $G(x)$:

|$G(x)$|Trial $y_p$|
|---|---|
|$P_n(x)$ (polynomial degree $n$)|$x^s(A_n x^n + \cdots + A_0)$|
|$e^{\alpha x}$|$x^s A e^{\alpha x}$|
|$\cos\beta x$ or $\sin\beta x$|$x^s(A\cos\beta x + B\sin\beta x)$|
|$e^{\alpha x}\cos\beta x$|$x^s e^{\alpha x}(A\cos\beta x + B\sin\beta x)$|
|Sum of above|Sum of corresponding trials|

The **modification rule:** $s = 0$ normally; $s = 1$ if the trial solution duplicates a term in $y_h$; $s = 2$ if the duplication is with a repeated root.

**Example:** $y'' - 3y' + 2y = e^x$

Homogeneous: $r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0 \Rightarrow r = 1, 2$, so $y_h = C_1 e^x + C_2 e^{2x}$.

$G = e^x$ and $e^x$ already appears in $y_h$ (with $r=1$), so use $y_p = Axe^x$ ($s=1$):

$$ y_p' = Ae^x + Axe^x, \quad y_p'' = 2Ae^x + Axe^x $$

Substitute: $(2Ae^x + Axe^x) - 3(Ae^x + Axe^x) + 2Axe^x = -Ae^x = e^x$

So $A = -1$, giving $y_p = -xe^x$.

### 5.5 Finding $y_p$: Variation of Parameters

Works for **any** $G(x)$ (including non-elementary). Given $y_h = C_1 y_1 + C_2 y_2$, assume:

$$ y_p = u_1(x),y_1(x) + u_2(x),y_2(x) $$

Impose the auxiliary condition $u_1' y_1 + u_2' y_2 = 0$ (suppresses second derivatives of $u$). Then substituting into the ODE gives:

$$ u_1' y_1' + u_2' y_2' = G(x) $$

Solving this $2 \times 2$ linear system via Cramer's rule:

$$ \boxed{u_1' = -\frac{y_2, G}{W}, \qquad u_2' = \frac{y_1, G}{W}} $$

Integrate to get $u_1, u_2$.

**Why this works:** We have two unknowns $u_1', u_2'$ and two equations (the auxiliary condition + the ODE). The Wronskian $W \neq 0$ guarantees the system is non-singular. $\blacksquare$

## 6. Systems of ODEs and Phase Plane Analysis

### 6.1 Converting to First-Order Systems

Any $n$-th order ODE can be converted to a **system** of $n$ first-order ODEs. For $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x)$, let $y_1 = y$, $y_2 = y'$:

$$ \dot{y}_1 = y_2, \qquad \dot{y}_2 = g - py_2 - qy_1 $$

In vector form: $\dot{\mathbf{y}} = A\mathbf{y} + \mathbf{b}$ (if linear), or $\dot{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{y})$ (general).

_This is the standard form for numerical solvers (Euler, Runge–Kutta) and for Neural ODEs._

### 6.2 Linear Autonomous Systems

$$ \dot{\mathbf{x}} = A\mathbf{x}, \qquad A \in \mathbb{R}^{2\times 2} $$

The solution is $\mathbf{x}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{x}_0$, where $e^{At} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(At)^n}{n!}$ (matrix exponential).

The qualitative behavior depends entirely on the **eigenvalues** $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ of $A$:

### 6.3 Classification of Fixed Points (2D)

Let $\tau = \text{tr}(A) = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$ and $\Delta = \det(A) = \lambda_1\lambda_2$.

|Condition|Eigenvalues|Fixed Point Type|Stable?|
|---|---|---|---|
|$\Delta < 0$|Real, opposite signs|**Saddle**|Unstable|
|$\Delta > 0$, $\tau^2 > 4\Delta$, $\tau < 0$|Real, both $< 0$|**Stable node**|✓|
|$\Delta > 0$, $\tau^2 > 4\Delta$, $\tau > 0$|Real, both $> 0$|**Unstable node**|✗|
|$\Delta > 0$, $\tau^2 < 4\Delta$, $\tau < 0$|Complex, $\text{Re}<0$|**Stable spiral**|✓|
|$\Delta > 0$, $\tau^2 < 4\Delta$, $\tau > 0$|Complex, $\text{Re}>0$|**Unstable spiral**|✗|
|$\Delta > 0$, $\tau = 0$|Pure imaginary|**Center**|Neutrally stable|

### 6.5 Stability: The Lyapunov Framework

**Definition.** The fixed point $\mathbf{x}^*$ is:

- **Stable (Lyapunov):** $\forall \varepsilon > 0$, $\exists \delta > 0$ s.t. $|\mathbf{x}(0) - \mathbf{x}^_| < \delta \implies |\mathbf{x}(t) - \mathbf{x}^_| < \varepsilon$ for all $t > 0$.
- **Asymptotically stable:** Stable _and_ $\mathbf{x}(t) \to \mathbf{x}^*$ as $t \to \infty$.
- **Unstable:** Not stable.

**Lyapunov's Direct Method.** If there exists $V: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ with:

1. $V(\mathbf{x}^_) = 0$ and $V(\mathbf{x}) > 0$ for $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{x}^_$,
2. $\dot{V}(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla V \cdot \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) \leq 0$,

then $\mathbf{x}^*$ is stable. If $\dot{V} < 0$ (strictly), it is asymptotically stable.

_$V$ is a "generalized energy" that the system dissipates. Finding $V$ is an art — for quadratic systems, $V = \mathbf{x}^\top P \mathbf{x}$ with $P$ the solution to the Lyapunov matrix equation $A^\top P + PA = -Q$._

## 7. Numerical Methods: The Bridge to Computation

Analytic solutions are the exception. Numerical methods are the rule.

### 7.1 Euler's Method

For $y' = f(x,y)$, $y(x_0) = y_0$, with step $h$:

$$ y_{n+1} = y_n + h,f(x_n, y_n) $$

This is a **first-order Taylor approximation**: $y(x+h) \approx y(x) + hy'(x)$.

**Local truncation error:** $O(h^2)$. **Global error:** $O(h)$.

### 7.2 Improved Euler (Heun's Method)

A **predictor-corrector** scheme:

$$ \tilde{y}_{n+1} = y_n + h,f(x_n, y_n) \qquad \text{(predict)} $$ $$ y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{2}\left[f(x_n, y_n) + f(x_{n+1}, \tilde{y}_{n+1})\right] \qquad \text{(correct)} $$

Global error: $O(h^2)$.

### 7.3 Runge–Kutta (RK4)

The workhorse of scientific computing. Four slope evaluations per step:

$$ k_1 = f(x_n,, y_n) $$ $$ k_2 = f!\left(x_n + \tfrac{h}{2},, y_n + \tfrac{h}{2}k_1\right) $$ $$ k_3 = f!\left(x_n + \tfrac{h}{2},, y_n + \tfrac{h}{2}k_2\right) $$ $$ k_4 = f!\left(x_n + h,, y_n + h,k_3\right) $$ $$ \boxed{y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{6}\left(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4\right)} $$

Global error: $O(h^4)$. The weighted average $\frac{1}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4)$ is a **Simpson's rule approximation** to the integral $\int_0^h f,dt$.

### 7.4 Comparison

```
Improved Euler  |   2   |        2         | Quick estimates
RK4             |   4   |        4         | Most problems
RK45 (adaptive) |  4–5  |       6–7        | Production (scipy)
```

## 8. A Brief Look at PDEs

The most important PDEs in science all arise from ODE-like local balance laws.

### 8.1 The Big Three

**Heat (diffusion) equation:** $$ \frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} $$ Models heat conduction, diffusion of particles, probability density evolution (Fokker–Planck).

**Wave equation:** $$ \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2} $$ Models sound, light, vibrating strings. Second-order in _both_ $t$ and $x$ — the spring ODE extended to a continuum.

**Laplace/Poisson equation:** $$ \nabla^2 u = 0 \quad \text{(Laplace)}, \qquad \nabla^2 u = f \quad \text{(Poisson)} $$ Models steady-state heat, electrostatics, fluid potential. Solutions are **harmonic functions**.

### 8.2 Separation of Variables for PDEs

The standard technique: assume $u(x,t) = X(x)T(t)$. For the heat equation:

$$ \frac{T'}{T} = \alpha\frac{X''}{X} = -\lambda \quad \text{(separation constant)} $$

This yields two ODEs: $$ T' = -\lambda T \implies T(t) = e^{-\lambda t} $$ $$ X'' = -\frac{\lambda}{\alpha}X \implies X(x) = A\cos(\mu x) + B\sin(\mu x), \quad \mu = \sqrt{\lambda/\alpha} $$

Boundary conditions on $X$ force $\lambda$ to take discrete values — **eigenvalues** — and the solution is a **Fourier series**. The PDE reduces to infinitely many decoupled ODEs.

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _The ODE is not a detour from ML — it is ML's hidden foundation._

### 9.1 Every Gradient Flow Is an ODE

Training a neural network by gradient descent on loss $L(\theta)$ is, in the continuous limit, solving:

$$ \frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\nabla_\theta L(\theta) $$

This is a **first-order autonomous ODE** — a gradient flow. The minima of $L$ are exactly the **stable fixed points** of this ODE. Saddle points are unstable fixed points. The entire theory of §6 (stability, phase portraits, Lyapunov functions) applies to understanding optimization.

### 9.2 Neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018)

A ResNet layer: $h_{t+1} = h_t + f_\theta(h_t)$ is Euler's method with $h=1$ applied to $\dot{h} = f_\theta(h)$.

The **Neural ODE** takes the continuous limit: define the hidden state by the IVP:

$$ \frac{dh}{dt} = f_\theta(h(t), t), \qquad h(0) = x $$

The network "depth" becomes a **continuous time variable**. Output is $h(T)$, computed by any ODE solver (e.g., RK45). Backpropagation through the solver uses the **adjoint method** — a classical ODE technique for sensitivity analysis.

The adjoint $\mathbf{a}(t) = \partial L / \partial h(t)$ satisfies a _backward ODE_:

$$ \frac{d\mathbf{a}}{dt} = -\mathbf{a}^\top \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial h} $$

This is variation of parameters / the transpose of the linearized forward equation.

### 9.3 Stability ↔ Generalization

The damping theory of §5 (Spring note) and the stability theory of §6 both say: **negative real parts of eigenvalues** $\Rightarrow$ convergence to a fixed point.

In neural networks:

- The **Jacobian** $J = \partial f_\theta / \partial h$ at a fixed point plays the role of the matrix $A$.
- If all eigenvalues of $J$ have negative real parts: the network's representation is stable — small input perturbations don't explode. This is related to **robustness and adversarial stability**.
- If eigenvalues have positive real parts: **exploding activations** or **sensitivity** to inputs.

### 9.4 Diffusion Models (Score-Based Generative Models)

The **forward process** of a diffusion model (DDPM, score matching) is the **stochastic differential equation**:

$$ dx = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t),x,dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)},dW_t $$

This is the Ornstein–Uhlenbeck process — a **damped linear ODE with noise**. Specifically:

- The deterministic part $-\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x$ is an exponentially decaying ODE (overdamped spring with time-varying $k$).
- The noise $dW_t$ is Brownian motion.

The **reverse process** (generation = denoising) solves a backward SDE derived from the **score function** $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$. Learning this score is exactly learning the drift of an ODE.

Without understanding the forward/backward ODE, diffusion models are a black box. With it, they become a natural extension of the framework we've built.

### 9.5 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

PINNs solve PDEs by embedding the equation as a **loss term**. For PDE $\mathcal{N}[u] = 0$:

$$ L(\theta) = \underbrace{\frac{1}{N}\sum_i |u_\theta(x_i, t_i) - u_{\text{data}}(x_i, t_i)|^2}_{\text{data loss}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{M}\sum_j |\mathcal{N}[u_\theta](x_j, t_j)|^2}_{\text{physics loss}} $$

The neural network $u_\theta$ is trained to satisfy both data and the differential equation simultaneously. The network is the _approximate solution_ to the PDE, and the ODE/PDE theory determines whether the solution is physically reasonable.

### 9.6 Numerical Solvers Inside ML

Modern ML frameworks (JAX, PyTorch) include **differentiable ODE solvers** (`torchdiffeq`, `diffrax`). These compute $\frac{\partial}{\partial \theta}\int_0^T f_\theta(h,t),dt$ using the adjoint method. Every concept from §7 (Euler, RK4, step size, stability) is directly relevant to training speed and numerical accuracy.

## 10. The Existence–Methods–Qualitative Trinity

The most important meta-lesson of this subject:

```
                    DIFFERENTIAL EQUATION
                           │
           ┌───────────────┼───────────────┐
           │               │               │
     EXISTENCE &      ANALYTIC         QUALITATIVE
     UNIQUENESS       SOLUTIONS        ANALYSIS
     (Picard–        (separation,      (phase plane,
      Lindelöf)       integrating       fixed points,
                      factor,           Lyapunov,
                      char. eq.,        stability)
                      var. params.)
           │               │               │
           └───────────────┴───────────────┘
                           │
                    NUMERICAL METHODS
                    (Euler, RK4, etc.)
                    when analytic fails
```

Many ODEs have no closed-form solution. Qualitative and numerical methods are not fallbacks — they are the primary tools for most real-world problems. Analytic solutions, when they exist, provide exact benchmarks and structural insight.

## 11. Quick-Reference: Solution Forms

**First-order:** $$ y' = ky \implies y = Ce^{kx} $$ $$ y' + P(x)y = Q(x) \implies y = e^{-\int P,dx}!\left[\int Q,e^{\int P,dx},dx + C\right] $$

**Second-order, constant coefficients $ay''+by'+cy=0$:**

|Discriminant|Roots|Solution|
|---|---|---|
|$b^2-4ac>0$|$r_1\neq r_2$ real|$C_1e^{r_1 x}+C_2 e^{r_2 x}$|
|$b^2-4ac=0$|$r$ repeated|$(C_1+C_2 x)e^{rx}$|
|$b^2-4ac<0$|$\alpha\pm i\beta$|$e^{\alpha x}(C_1\cos\beta x+C_2\sin\beta x)$|

**Variation of parameters:** $$ u_1' = -\frac{y_2 G}{W}, \qquad u_2' = \frac{y_1 G}{W}, \qquad W = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1' $$

**Matrix exponential (linear system $\dot{\mathbf{x}}=A\mathbf{x}$):** $$ \mathbf{x}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{x}_0, \qquad e^{At} = P,\text{diag}(e^{\lambda_i t}),P^{-1} \quad \text{(when } A \text{ diagonalizable)} $$

## 12. Exercises (Self-Check)

1. **Classification.** Classify each as order/linear/homogeneous: (a) $y'' + (\sin x)y = e^x$, (b) $yy' = x$, (c) $y''' - y = 0$, (d) $y' + y^2 = 1$.
    
2. **Non-uniqueness.** Verify that both $y=0$ and $y=(x/2)^2$ solve $y' = \sqrt{y}$, $y(0)=0$. Where does Picard–Lindelöf fail?
    
3. **Integrating factor.** Solve $y' + (\cot x)y = \cos x$. (Hint: $\int\cot x,dx = \ln|\sin x|$.)
    
4. **Exact equation.** Show $(e^x\sin y + 2x)dx + (e^x\cos y + 2y)dy = 0$ is exact and find the general solution.
    
5. **Wronskian.** For $y'' - y = 0$ with solutions $y_1 = e^x$, $y_2 = e^{-x}$: compute $W$, verify Abel's theorem, and confirm the discriminant of the characteristic equation matches.
    
6. **Variation of parameters.** Solve $y'' + y = \sec x$ using variation of parameters. (The method of undetermined coefficients fails here — why?)
    
7. **Phase portrait.** For $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 2 \ 0 & -3 \end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x}$: find eigenvalues, classify the fixed point, sketch the phase portrait.
    
8. **Neural ODE.** Write the spring equation $\ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0$ as a first-order system. Apply Euler's method with step $h$. For what values of $h$ does the discrete system remain stable? (Compare $|\lambda h| \leq 2$ for Euler stability.)
    
9. **Gradient flow.** For $L(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}(\theta - \theta^_)^2$, solve the gradient flow ODE $\dot{\theta} = -L'(\theta)$ explicitly. Show it is an integrating-factor problem. At what rate does $\theta(t) \to \theta^_$?
    
10. **Diffusion intuition.** The forward diffusion SDE has deterministic part $\dot{x} = -\frac{\beta}{2}x$. Solve this ODE. What does the solution say about the long-term fate of any initial data point? Why is this desirable for a generative model?
