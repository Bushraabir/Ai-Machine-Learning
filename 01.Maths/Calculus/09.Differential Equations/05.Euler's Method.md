## 0. Orientation

Euler's method is simultaneously the simplest numerical ODE solver, the geometric act of _walking a direction field_, the conceptual ancestor of every modern integrator, and — as we will see — the exact mathematical operation performed by a residual neural network at each layer.

It is tempting to dismiss Euler's method as a toy. Resist that temptation. Its errors are instructive, its geometry is exact, and understanding _why it fails_ is the prerequisite for understanding why RK4, adaptive solvers, and implicit methods work.

---

## 1. The Setup

We want to approximate the solution to the **initial value problem (IVP)**:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = f(x, y), \qquad y(x_0) = y_0 $$

We cannot (or choose not to) find the exact solution analytically. Instead, we approximate $y(x)$ at a discrete sequence of points:

$$ x_0,; x_1 = x_0 + h,; x_2 = x_0 + 2h,; \ldots,; x_n = x_0 + nh $$

where $h > 0$ is the **step size**.

---

## 2. Derivation: Three Equivalent Viewpoints

The formula is the same from all three angles. Understanding all three is what separates a user of Euler's method from someone who truly knows it.

### 2.1 Taylor Series

Expand $y(x + h)$ around $x$:

$$ y(x+h) = y(x) + h,y'(x) + \frac{h^2}{2},y''(x) + \frac{h^3}{6},y'''(x) + \cdots $$

We know $y'(x) = f(x, y(x))$ from the ODE. Drop all terms of order $h^2$ and higher:

$$ y(x+h) \approx y(x) + h,f(x,,y(x)) $$

This is Euler's method. The dropped terms constitute the **local truncation error**.

### 2.2 Geometric / Direction Field

At the current point $(x_n, y_n)$, the ODE tells us the slope: $f(x_n, y_n)$. Walk along the tangent line for horizontal distance $h$:

$$ y_{n+1} = y_n + h \cdot \underbrace{f(x_n, y_n)}_{\text{slope at current point}} $$

We are navigating by the direction field — reading the compass at $(x_n, y_n)$ and walking in that direction until $x_{n+1}$, then re-reading.

### 2.3 Integral Approximation (Rectangle Rule)

Integrate the ODE from $x_n$ to $x_{n+1}$:

$$ y(x_{n+1}) = y(x_n) + \int_{x_n}^{x_{n+1}} f(x, y(x)),dx $$

Approximate the integral using the **left-endpoint rectangle rule**:

$$ \int_{x_n}^{x_{n+1}} f(x, y(x)),dx \approx h \cdot f(x_n, y_n) $$

This gives exactly the Euler update. The error in Euler's method is precisely the error in the rectangle rule — and we can control it by shrinking $h$.

**The master formula:**

$$ \boxed{y_{n+1} = y_n + h,f(x_n, y_n), \qquad n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots} $$

## 3. Geometric Picture

```
y
 |
 |                    * ← true solution y(x_{n+2})
 |              *  · ↗
 |         *  ·  ↗  ← Euler step 2 (wrong slope again)
 |    *  ·  ↗ ← y_{n+2} (Euler)
 |  · ↗  ↗ ← Euler step 1 (tangent line at (x_n, y_n))
 |↗  ← y_{n+1} (Euler)
 ●──────────────────────────→ x
(x_n, y_n)    x_{n+1}   x_{n+2}

· · · = true solution curve
──── = Euler polygonal path (piecewise linear)
```

**The key geometric defect:** After taking a step, we are no longer on the true solution curve. We restart from the wrong point $(x_{n+1}, y_{n+1})$ and read the direction field there — but since $y_{n+1} \neq y(x_{n+1})$, we are reading the field at the wrong height. Each step compounds the error from all previous steps.

## 4. Error Analysis

### 4.1 Local Truncation Error (LTE)

The **local truncation error** is the error made in a single step, assuming we start exactly on the true solution ($y_n = y(x_n)$).

From the Taylor expansion:

$$ y(x_{n+1}) = y(x_n) + h,y'(x_n) + \frac{h^2}{2},y''(x_n) + O(h^3) $$

Euler gives $y_{n+1} = y(x_n) + h,f(x_n, y(x_n)) = y(x_n) + h,y'(x_n)$.

Therefore:

$$ \text{LTE} = y(x_{n+1}) - y_{n+1} = \frac{h^2}{2},y''(x_n) + O(h^3) = O(h^2) $$

LTE is **second order** in $h$.

### 4.2 Global Truncation Error (GTE)

The **global truncation error** at $x_N = x_0 + Nh$ is the accumulated error over all $N$ steps.

Taking $N$ steps of size $h$ to reach a fixed point $x_N$: there are $N = (x_N - x_0)/h$ steps. Each contributes LTE $\approx Ch^2$, so:

$$ \text{GTE} \approx N \cdot Ch^2 = \frac{x_N - x_0}{h} \cdot Ch^2 = C(x_N - x_0),h = O(h) $$

**Euler's method is a first-order method**: halving $h$ halves the global error.

### 4.3 The Error Constant

More precisely, if $|y''(x)| \leq M$ on $[x_0, x_N]$, then:

$$ |y(x_N) - y_N| \leq \frac{Mh}{2L}\left(e^{L(x_N - x_0)} - 1\right) $$

where $L$ is the Lipschitz constant of $f$ in $y$. The exponential factor $e^{L(x_N-x_0)}$ is the **error amplification** — errors grow exponentially over long intervals, making Euler's method unreliable for large integration windows unless $h$ is very small.

### 4.4 Geometric Interpretation of Error

Euler underestimates or overestimates depending on the **concavity** of the solution:

```
Concave-up (y'' > 0):         Concave-down (y'' < 0):

  true ___                        true
      /                    ___---
Euler/  ← underestimates   Euler   ← overestimates
   /                             \
```

- If $y'' > 0$ (solution bends upward): Euler's tangent-line approximation lies _below_ the curve → underestimate.
- If $y'' < 0$ (solution bends downward): tangent lies _above_ → overestimate.

The second derivative $y''$ is exactly what the LTE formula $\frac{h^2}{2}y''$ captures.

## 5. Fully Worked Examples

### 5.1 Example 1 — Exponential Growth

**IVP:** $y' = y$, $y(0) = 1$. Exact solution: $y = e^x$.

Apply Euler with $h = 0.5$ on $[0, 1]$:

|$n$|$x_n$|$y_n$ (Euler)|$f(x_n, y_n) = y_n$|$y(x_n) = e^{x_n}$|Error|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|0|0.0|1.0000|1.0000|1.0000|0.0000|
|1|0.5|$1 + 0.5(1) = 1.5000$|1.5000|1.6487|0.1487|
|2|1.0|$1.5 + 0.5(1.5) = 2.2500$|—|2.7183|0.4683|

Error at $x=1$: $|2.7183 - 2.2500| = 0.4683$ (about 17%).

Now with $h = 0.25$:

|$x_n$|$y_n$ (Euler)|$e^{x_n}$|Error|
|---|---|---|---|
|0.00|1.0000|1.0000|0.0000|
|0.25|1.2500|1.2840|0.0340|
|0.50|1.5625|1.6487|0.0862|
|0.75|1.9531|2.1170|0.1639|
|1.00|2.4414|2.7183|0.2769|

Halving $h$ from 0.5 to 0.25 reduced the error at $x=1$ from 0.4683 to 0.2769 — roughly halved, confirming $O(h)$ global error.

### 5.2 Example 2 — Logistic Equation

**IVP:** $y' = y(1-y)$, $y(0) = 0.1$. Exact solution: $y(x) = \frac{1}{1 + 9e^{-x}}$.

Apply Euler with $h = 0.5$ on $[0, 3]$:

|$n$|$x_n$|$y_n$|$f = y_n(1-y_n)$|$y_{\text{exact}}$|Error|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|0|0.0|0.1000|0.0900|0.1000|0.0000|
|1|0.5|0.1450|0.1240|0.1480|0.0030|
|2|1.0|0.2070|0.1642|0.2142|0.0072|
|3|1.5|0.2891|0.2057|0.3100|0.0209|
|4|2.0|0.3920|0.2384|0.4378|0.0458|
|5|2.5|0.5112|0.2497|0.5750|0.0638|
|6|3.0|0.6361|—|0.7109|0.0748|

The Euler path follows the S-curve but lags behind — it consistently underestimates because the logistic curve is concave up in its initial phase (below the inflection point $y = 0.5$).

### 5.3 Example 3 — A Cautionary Case: Stiff ODE

**IVP:** $y' = -100y$, $y(0) = 1$. Exact solution: $y = e^{-100x}$ (decays to 0 very fast).

Euler: $y_{n+1} = y_n + h(-100y_n) = y_n(1 - 100h)$.

- If $h = 0.015$: $1 - 100(0.015) = -0.5$. Each step multiplies by $-0.5$: $1, -0.5, 0.25, -0.125, \ldots$ — oscillating but decaying. ✓ (barely stable)
- If $h = 0.025$: $1 - 100(0.025) = -1.5$. Each step multiplies by $-1.5$: $1, -1.5, 2.25, -3.375, \ldots$ — **diverging**. ✗

The exact solution is essentially 0 after $x = 0.1$, yet Euler _explodes_ for step sizes that seem small. This is **stiffness** — Euler's method requires $h < 2/100 = 0.02$ for stability, which may be far smaller than accuracy requires. This motivates implicit methods.

## 6. Stability Analysis

### 6.1 The Test Equation

The standard stability analysis uses the **test equation** (Dahlquist's test problem):

$$ y' = \lambda y, \qquad \lambda \in \mathbb{C}, \quad y(0) = 1 $$

Exact solution: $y = e^{\lambda x}$. For $\text{Re}(\lambda) < 0$, the solution decays — and we want the numerical method to also decay.

Euler's method on the test equation:

$$ y_{n+1} = y_n + h\lambda y_n = (1 + h\lambda)y_n $$

So $y_n = (1 + h\lambda)^n$. This decays to 0 iff $|1 + h\lambda| < 1$.

### 6.2 Region of Absolute Stability

The **region of absolute stability** is:

$$ \mathcal{S}_{\text{Euler}} = {z \in \mathbb{C} : |1 + z| < 1} $$

where $z = h\lambda$. This is a **disk of radius 1 centered at $z = -1$** in the complex plane.

```
Im(z)
  ↑
1 ─ ┤      · · ·
    ┤    ·       ·
    ┤   ·    ●    ·     ← stability region (disk)
    ┤   ·  (-1,0)  ·      center: (-1,0), radius: 1
    ┤    ·       ·
-1 ─ ┤      · · ·
    ─┼──────────────→ Re(z)
   -2    -1    0

Note: the right half-plane (Re(z) > 0) is entirely OUTSIDE.
For real λ < 0: need hλ ∈ (-2, 0), i.e., h < 2/|λ|.
```

**Consequence for stiff ODEs:** If $\lambda \ll 0$ (large negative, rapid decay), the stability condition $h < 2/|\lambda|$ forces _extremely small_ step sizes — even though accuracy doesn't require them. This is the stiffness problem.

### 6.3 Comparison with Other Methods

|Method|Stability region contains...|Order|
|---|---|---|
|Euler (explicit)|Disk of radius 1 at $(-1,0)$|1|
|Trapezoidal (implicit)|Entire left half-plane|2|
|Backward Euler (implicit)|Complement of unit disk at $(1,0)$|1|
|RK4|Much larger region (extends to $\approx -2.8$ on real axis)|4|

**Backward Euler** ($y_{n+1} = y_n + hf(x_{n+1}, y_{n+1})$) uses the slope at the _next_ point — it is unconditionally stable for the test equation ($A$-stable). The cost: solving an implicit equation at each step.

## 7. Refinements and Relatives

### 7.1 Improved Euler (Heun's Method)

The central flaw of Euler: it uses the slope only at the _left_ endpoint. Heun's method averages the slopes at both endpoints:

$$ \tilde{y}_{n+1} = y_n + h,f(x_n, y_n) \qquad \text{(predictor: Euler)} $$ $$ y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{2}!\left[f(x_n, y_n) + f(x_{n+1}, \tilde{y}_{n+1})\right] \qquad \text{(corrector: trapezoid)} $$

LTE: $O(h^3)$. GTE: $O(h^2)$. Second-order method — same cost as two Euler steps, but far more accurate.

Geometric interpretation: use the average of the field at the current and predicted next point — a more faithful reading of the direction field.

### 7.2 Midpoint Method

$$ y_{n+1} = y_n + h,f!\left(x_n + \frac{h}{2},; y_n + \frac{h}{2}f(x_n, y_n)\right) $$

Evaluate the slope at the _midpoint_ of the interval. Also second-order. Geometrically: take a half-step of Euler to estimate the midpoint, read the field there, then use that slope for the full step.

### 7.3 The Runge–Kutta Family

All Runge–Kutta methods are refinements of Euler: evaluate $f$ at multiple intermediate points, then take a weighted average. Euler is RK1 — one evaluation, weight 1. See [[General Differential Equations]] §7 for RK4.

### 7.4 The Euler Family Tree

```
                   Euler (RK1, explicit)
                   h·f(xₙ, yₙ)
                         │
          ┌──────────────┼──────────────┐
          │                             │
    Midpoint method              Heun's method
    (evaluate at midpoint)       (average of two slopes)
          │                             │
          └──────────┬──────────────────┘
                     │
              Runge–Kutta RK4
           (weighted 4-point average)
                     │
              Adaptive RK45
           (variable step size,
            error control)
```

## 8. Euler's Method for Systems

First-order systems $\dot{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{f}(x, \mathbf{y})$, $\mathbf{y}(x_0) = \mathbf{y}_0$ (where $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^n$):

$$ \boxed{\mathbf{y}_{n+1} = \mathbf{y}_n + h,\mathbf{f}(x_n, \mathbf{y}_n)} $$

**Identical formula, vectorized.** Every component is updated simultaneously using the current values of all components.

**Example (spring, from [[Models for the Motion of a Spring]]):**

$\ddot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0$ written as system: $y_1 = x$, $y_2 = \dot{x}$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix}y_1 \ y_2\end{pmatrix}_{n+1} = \begin{pmatrix}y_1 \ y_2\end{pmatrix}_n + h\begin{pmatrix}y_2 \ -\omega_0^2 y_1\end{pmatrix}_n = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}1 & h \ -\omega_0^2 h & 1\end{pmatrix}}_{M}\begin{pmatrix}y_1 \ y_2\end{pmatrix}_n $$

The update is a **linear map** $M$. The eigenvalues of $M$ are $1 \pm ih\omega_0$ — magnitude $\sqrt{1 + h^2\omega_0^2} > 1$.

**The Euler method for a conservative oscillator always grows the amplitude.** Each step the state spirals _outward_:

```
y₂ (velocity)
  ↑
  |      /‾‾‾→ Euler spirals outward!
  |     /  ↗
  |    /  ↗
  |   ·  ●  ← true orbit: closed ellipse
  |    ↖  ↘
  |     ↖  ↘
  +───────────→ y₁ (position)

Euler: outward spiral (energy grows)
True:  closed ellipse (energy conserved)
```

This energy drift is a fundamental flaw of Euler for conservative systems — it creates _numerical dissipation_ in the wrong direction. **Symplectic integrators** (e.g., symplectic Euler, Störmer–Verlet) fix this by preserving a discrete energy-like quantity.

## 9. Step-Size Control and Adaptive Methods

In practice, we don't use fixed $h$. Adaptive solvers (RK45, Dormand–Prince) estimate the local error at each step and adjust $h$ accordingly:

- **Error too large:** reduce $h$, repeat step.
- **Error small enough:** accept step, possibly increase $h$ next step.

The error estimate uses two approximations of different orders (e.g., RK4 and RK5) and takes their difference as a proxy for the true error.

**Why Euler's analysis matters here:** The step-size selection formula in adaptive solvers is derived from the same Taylor-expansion argument as the LTE. If target tolerance is $\varepsilon$ and current error estimate is $e$, the new step size satisfies:

$$ h_{\text{new}} = h_{\text{old}}\left(\frac{\varepsilon}{e}\right)^{1/(p+1)} $$

where $p$ is the method order. For Euler ($p=1$): $h_{\text{new}} = h_{\text{old}}\sqrt{\varepsilon/e}$.

## 10. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _This section is not an analogy. It is a mathematical identity._

### 10.1 ResNets Are Euler's Method

A **ResNet** (He et al., 2016) layer performs:

$$ h_{n+1} = h_n + \mathcal{F}_\theta(h_n) $$

where $\mathcal{F}_\theta$ is a neural network block (convolutions + batch norm + ReLU). Compare with Euler:

$$ y_{n+1} = y_n + h,f(x_n, y_n) $$

They are **identical** with $h = 1$ and $f_\theta \equiv \mathcal{F}_\theta$. The residual connection $h_n \to h_{n+1}$ is not a heuristic engineering trick — it is Euler's method applied to the ODE $\dot{h} = f_\theta(h)$.

This correspondence (formalized in Neural ODEs, Chen et al. 2018) means:

- **Network depth** = number of Euler steps.
- **Layer width** = dimensionality of the state space $\mathbb{R}^d$.
- **Skip connections** = the $y_n$ term in Euler's update.
- **Step size $h$** = implicit; can be absorbed into the parameterization of $\mathcal{F}_\theta$.

### 10.2 Gradient Descent Is Euler on the Gradient Flow

The gradient flow ODE is $\dot{\theta} = -\nabla L(\theta)$. Euler's method on this:

$$ \theta_{n+1} = \theta_n + h,(-\nabla L(\theta_n)) = \theta_n - \eta,\nabla L(\theta_n) $$

This is exactly **stochastic gradient descent** with learning rate $\eta = h$.

Every property of Euler's method translates:

|Euler Property|SGD Property|
|---|---|
|Step size $h$|Learning rate $\eta$|
|LTE $\propto h^2$|Gradient noise $\propto \eta^2$ in loss landscape|
|Stability requires small $h$|SGD diverges for large $\eta$|
|$O(h)$ global error|Suboptimal convergence rate $O(1/\sqrt{T})$|
|Euler spirals for oscillators|SGD oscillates in narrow loss valleys|
|Stiffness $\Rightarrow$ tiny $h$|Ill-conditioned loss $\Rightarrow$ tiny $\eta$|

### 10.3 Implicit Euler and Proximal Methods

The **proximal gradient method** in optimization is backward (implicit) Euler on gradient flow:

$$ \theta_{n+1} = \arg\min_\theta\left[L(\theta) + \frac{1}{2\eta}|\theta - \theta_n|^2\right] $$

The first-order condition gives $\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n - \eta\nabla L(\theta_{n+1})$ — implicit Euler. The proximal step is unconditionally stable (large $\eta$ allowed), just as backward Euler is $A$-stable.

**Proximal algorithms** (ISTA, ADMM, proximal gradient descent) are the optimization analogues of implicit ODE integrators.

### 10.4 Euler Instability ↔ Training Instability

For the test equation $y' = \lambda y$, Euler is stable iff $|1 + h\lambda| < 1$. For a neural network trained on a loss with Hessian eigenvalue $\Lambda_{\max}$ (the curvature), the gradient flow has $\lambda \approx -\Lambda_{\max}$, and SGD (= Euler) is stable iff:

$$ \eta < \frac{2}{\Lambda_{\max}} $$

This is exactly the **spectral radius condition** for gradient descent convergence derived in convex optimization. It is the same stability criterion as Euler's method on the test equation — not a coincidence, but the same theorem.

When training explodes for large learning rates, it is not mysterious: Euler's method has left its stability region.

### 10.5 Higher-Order Methods in ML

If Euler = ResNet = SGD, what are the higher-order methods?

|Numerical Method|ML Analogue|
|---|---|
|Euler (RK1)|SGD, 1-layer ResNet|
|Midpoint / Heun|2-stage ResNet blocks|
|RK4|Multi-step ResNets / dense connections|
|Adaptive RK45|Neural ODEs with adaptive depth|
|Implicit / symplectic Euler|Proximal methods, Hamiltonian NNs|
|Symplectic integrators|Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC)|

Symplectic integrators preserve the discrete energy (the Hamiltonian), just as HMC preserves volume in phase space — the same mathematical structure underlies both.

### 10.6 Euler Discretization and the Diffusion Model Timestep

In **DDPM** (Ho et al., 2020), the forward noising process is the SDE:

$$ dx = -\frac{\beta(t)}{2}x,dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)},dW_t $$

The discrete forward process is Euler–Maruyama discretization of this SDE:

$$ x_{t+1} = x_t - \frac{\beta_t}{2}x_t\Delta t + \sqrt{\beta_t \Delta t},\varepsilon_t, \qquad \varepsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I) $$

The number of diffusion timesteps $T$ is the number of Euler steps; the noise schedule $\beta_t$ is the step-size sequence. The reverse (denoising) process is a numerical solver running the reverse SDE — and the quality vs. speed tradeoff of diffusion sampling is literally the accuracy vs. cost tradeoff of ODE/SDE solvers, with DDIM (Song et al., 2020) corresponding to higher-order solvers.

## 11. Quick-Reference

**The update:** $$ y_{n+1} = y_n + h,f(x_n, y_n) $$

**LTE:** $\tau_n = \frac{h^2}{2}y''(x_n) + O(h^3) = O(h^2)$

**GTE:** $O(h)$ — first-order method.

**Error bound (exact):** $$ |y(x_N) - y_N| \leq \frac{Mh}{2L}!\left(e^{L(x_N - x_0)} - 1\right), \qquad M = \max|y''|,; L = \text{Lipschitz const.} $$

**Stability (test equation $y' = \lambda y$):**  
Stable iff $|1 + h\lambda| < 1$ (disk of radius 1 at $z=-1$ in $z = h\lambda$ plane).

**Real $\lambda < 0$:** stable iff $h < 2/|\lambda|$.

**Vectorized (systems):** $$ \mathbf{y}_{n+1} = \mathbf{y}_n + h,\mathbf{f}(x_n, \mathbf{y}_n) $$

**Defect for conservative systems:** Euler grows energy. Use symplectic Euler instead: $$ p_{n+1} = p_n - h,\nabla_q H(q_n, p_n), \qquad q_{n+1} = q_n + h,\nabla_p H(q_n, p_{n+1}) $$
