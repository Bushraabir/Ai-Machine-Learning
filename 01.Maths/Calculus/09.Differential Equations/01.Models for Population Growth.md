## 0. Why This Matters Before We Begin

Population models are the _pedagogical spine_ of first-order ODEs. They are also, quietly, the conceptual skeleton of a surprising slice of modern machine learning — activation functions, learning-rate schedules, neural population codes, epidemic-spread on graphs. The mathematics here is not a warm-up act; it _is_ the act.

---

## 1. The Exponential (Malthusian) Model

### 1.1 Derivation from First Principles

Let $P(t)$ denote population at time $t$. The **Malthusian hypothesis** is that the instantaneous rate of change is proportional to current population:

$$ \frac{dP}{dt} = kP, \qquad P(0) = P_0 $$

where $k \in \mathbb{R}$ is the **intrinsic growth rate** (births minus deaths per individual per unit time).

**Solving by separation of variables:**

$$ \frac{dP}{P} = k,dt \implies \int_{P_0}^{P} \frac{du}{u} = \int_0^t k,ds \implies \ln!\left(\frac{P}{P_0}\right) = kt $$

$$ \boxed{P(t) = P_0, e^{kt}} $$

- $k > 0$: unbounded exponential growth.
- $k < 0$: exponential decay (radioactive decay, drug clearance — _same equation_).
- $k = 0$: $P(t) = P_0$, a fixed point (trivially stable).

### 1.2 The Fixed Point and Stability

The ODE $\dot{P} = kP$ has a single equilibrium: $P^* = 0$.

The linearisation around $P^*$ is trivial — the equation _is_ linear. Stability:

- $k < 0$: $P^* = 0$ is **stable** (all trajectories decay to it).
- $k > 0$: $P^* = 0$ is **unstable** (trajectories flee from it).

### 1.3 Phase Line

```
k > 0:          k < 0:

P                P
↑    →  →  →    ↑   ←  ←  ←
|                |
0                0
```

All trajectories on the real line either all flow away from (or toward) the origin. No complexity here — that comes next.

### 1.4 Concrete Example

> **World population (crude estimate):** In 1800, $P_0 \approx 10^9$. Suppose $k = 0.014$ yr$^{-1}$ (roughly 1.4% annual growth). Predict population in 1900.

$$ P(100) = 10^9 \cdot e^{0.014 \times 100} = 10^9 \cdot e^{1.4} \approx 10^9 \times 4.055 \approx 4.05 \times 10^9 $$

Historical reality: ~1.6 billion in 1900. The model **overshoots** drastically — because $k$ isn't constant, and resources are finite. This failure motivates §2.

## 2. The Logistic Model

### 2.1 The Key Insight: Carrying Capacity

Verhulst (1838) proposed that $k$ is not constant but **decreases linearly** with population — as $P$ grows, competition for resources reduces the effective per-capita growth rate:

$$ \text{effective rate} = r\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) $$

where:

- $r > 0$: intrinsic (maximum) growth rate,
- $K > 0$: **carrying capacity** (environmental ceiling).

This yields the **logistic equation**:

$$ \boxed{\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)} $$

### 2.2 Qualitative Analysis Before Solving

**Fixed points:** Set $\dot{P} = 0$: $$ P^* = 0 \quad \text{(trivial, unstable)} \qquad \text{and} \qquad P^* = K \quad \text{(non-trivial)} $$

**Phase line analysis:**

|Region|Sign of $\dot{P}$|Direction|
|---|---|---|
|$P < 0$|negative (biologically meaningless)|—|
|$0 < P < K$|**positive**|$P$ increases|
|$P = K$|zero|equilibrium|
|$P > K$|**negative**|$P$ decreases|

So $K$ is a **stable equilibrium** (globally attracting for $P > 0$). $P^* = 0$ is unstable.

**Phase line:**

```
←  (0)  →  →  →  →  [K]  ←  ←  ←
```

### 2.3 Exact Solution

Separate variables:

$$ \frac{dP}{P(1 - P/K)} = r,dt $$

Use partial fractions on the left:

$$ \frac{1}{P(1-P/K)} = \frac{1}{P} + \frac{1/K}{1-P/K} $$

Integrate both sides:

$$ \ln P - \ln!\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right) = rt + C $$

$$ \ln!\left(\frac{P}{K - P}\right) = rt + C $$

Apply initial condition $P(0) = P_0$, so $C = \ln!\left(\frac{P_0}{K-P_0}\right)$. Exponentiate and solve for $P$:

$$ \boxed{P(t) = \frac{K P_0}{P_0 + (K - P_0),e^{-rt}}} $$

This is the **logistic function** — an S-shaped (sigmoidal) curve.

### 2.4 Asymptotic Behavior

$$ \lim_{t \to \infty} P(t) = K \qquad (\text{regardless of } P_0 \in (0, K]) $$

$$ \lim_{t \to -\infty} P(t) = 0 $$

The inflection point (where growth is fastest, $\ddot{P} = 0$) occurs at $P = K/2$.

_Proof of inflection:_ Differentiate $\dot{P} = rP(1-P/K)$ with respect to $t$:

$$ \ddot{P} = r\dot{P}\left(1 - \frac{2P}{K}\right) = r^2 P!\left(1-\frac{P}{K}\right)!\left(1 - \frac{2P}{K}\right) $$

Setting $\ddot{P} = 0$ (with $P > 0$, $P \neq K$) gives $P = K/2$. $\blacksquare$

### 2.5 Visualization: The S-Curve

```
P(t)
 K  ──────────────────────────── (asymptote)
    |                   /‾‾‾‾‾‾‾
K/2 |................../  ← inflection (fastest growth)
    |              /
    |           /
    |        /
    |     /
    |  /
    |/
    0──────────────────────────── t
```

- Below $K/2$: growth is **accelerating** (convex).
- Above $K/2$: growth is **decelerating** (concave).
- The curve is symmetric about the inflection point in log-odds space.

### 2.6 Concrete Example

> **U.S. population:** Stewart uses $P_0 = 3.9 \times 10^6$ (1790), $K \approx 197 \times 10^6$, and fits $r$ to historical data.  
> At $t = 0$ (1790): rapid growth. By 1900, growth slows — logistic fits better than exponential.

## 3. The Harvesting Extension (Brief)

A natural perturbation: suppose we remove $h$ individuals per unit time (fishing, hunting):

$$ \frac{dP}{dt} = rP!\left(1-\frac{P}{K}\right) - h $$

The equilibria satisfy a quadratic. For $h < rK/4$ (below the **maximum sustainable yield**), two equilibria exist; above it, population collapses. MSY is achieved at $P = K/2$ — the same inflection point, not a coincidence.

$$ h_{\max} = \frac{rK}{4} $$

## 5. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _This is the part most textbooks skip. Don't._

### 5.1 The Logistic Function _Is_ the Sigmoid

Rewrite the logistic solution. Let $P_0 = K/2$ (starting at inflection), $u = rt$:

$$ P(t) = \frac{K}{1 + e^{-rt}} = K \cdot \sigma(rt) $$

where $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ is the **sigmoid (logistic) activation function** in neural networks.

This is not a notational coincidence. The sigmoid activation was originally motivated by biological neuron firing rates — neurons saturate at maximum firing (analogous to $K$) and have a threshold below which they barely fire. The logistic ODE describes exactly this saturation dynamic.

**In practice:** every time a neural network applies $\sigma(w^\top x + b)$, it is evaluating the logistic function — the solution to a population ODE.

### 5.2 Logistic Regression

Binary classification via logistic regression models the _probability_ that output $y=1$:

$$ P(y=1 \mid x) = \sigma(w^\top x + b) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(w^\top x + b)}} $$

The cross-entropy loss is the negative log-likelihood of this model. Training via gradient descent is literally fitting the parameters of a logistic (population-growth) curve to data.

### 5.3 Neural ODE and Continuous-Depth Networks

In the 2018 **Neural ODE** framework (Chen et al.), the hidden state $h(t)$ of a network satisfies:

$$ \frac{dh}{dt} = f_\theta(h(t), t) $$

where $f_\theta$ is a neural network. This is a _learned_ ODE. The logistic equation is the simplest non-trivial case of such a system with a single hidden unit.

Euler discretization of $\dot{h} = f_\theta(h)$ with step $\Delta t = 1$ gives exactly a **ResNet layer**:

$$ h_{t+1} = h_t + f_\theta(h_t) $$

Understanding population ODEs qualitatively — fixed points, stability, phase lines — is the conceptual prerequisite for reasoning about the dynamics of deep networks.

### 5.4 Epidemic/Information Diffusion (SIR Model)

The spread of information, memes, or viruses in a network follows the **SIR model**:

$$ \frac{dS}{dt} = -\beta SI, \qquad \frac{dI}{dt} = \beta SI - \gamma I, \qquad \frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I $$

This is a direct generalization of population ODEs to a 3-compartment system. Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) trained on epidemiological or social data are learning to approximate the solutions to ODE systems like this one.

### 5.5 Learning Rate Schedules

Many modern optimizers use **exponential decay** of the learning rate:

$$ \eta(t) = \eta_0, e^{-\lambda t} $$

This is the solution to $\dot{\eta} = -\lambda \eta$ — a population decay ODE applied to an optimization hyperparameter. "Cosine annealing" is a smooth perturbation of this.

### 5.6 Summary of ML Connections

|Population Model|ML/DL Analogue|
|---|---|
|Logistic function $\sigma$|Sigmoid activation, logistic regression|
|Exponential growth/decay|Learning rate schedules, weight decay|
|Carrying capacity $K$|Saturation, vanishing gradient in deep nets|
|Phase-line stability analysis|Loss landscape fixed-point analysis|
|Logistic ODE|Continuous-depth networks (Neural ODEs)|
|SIR generalization|Epidemic GNNs, diffusion on graphs|

## 6. Proofs and Technicalities Worth Knowing

### 6.1 Existence and Uniqueness (Picard–Lindelöf)

The right-hand side $f(t, P) = rP(1-P/K)$ is a polynomial in $P$, hence **continuously differentiable** (Lipschitz in $P$ on any compact set). By the Picard–Lindelöf theorem, the IVP has a **unique solution** on some interval around $t_0$.

For the logistic equation, the explicit formula shows the solution exists for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

### 6.2 Global Stability of $K$ (Lyapunov Argument)

Define the Lyapunov function $V(P) = (P - K)^2$ for $P > 0$.

$$ \dot{V} = 2(P-K)\dot{P} = 2(P-K) \cdot rP\left(1-\frac{P}{K}\right) $$

Note $1 - P/K = -(P-K)/K$, so:

$$ \dot{V} = 2(P-K) \cdot rP \cdot \left(-\frac{P-K}{K}\right) = -\frac{2rP}{K}(P-K)^2 \leq 0 $$

with equality only when $P = K$ or $P = 0$. Since $P > 0$, $\dot{V} < 0$ whenever $P \neq K$. So $K$ is **Lyapunov stable** and the trajectory converges to it. $\blacksquare$
