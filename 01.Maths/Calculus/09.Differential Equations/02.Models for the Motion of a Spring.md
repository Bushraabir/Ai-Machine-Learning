## 0. Why This Is the Most Important ODE in Physics

The spring вЂ” specifically, the **second-order linear ODE** it produces вЂ” is arguably the single most recurring equation in all of science and engineering. It governs sound, light, electrical circuits (RLC), quantum harmonic oscillators, climate oscillations, and the small-oscillation limit of _every_ potential well in classical mechanics. If you understand the spring, you understand oscillations universally.

And, quietly, it underlies **recurrent neural networks**, **attention mechanisms with positional encodings**, and **second-order optimizers** in ML. We will get there.

---

## 1. Setting Up the Model

### 1.1 Physical Setup

A mass $m > 0$ hangs (or slides) on a spring with **spring constant** $k > 0$. Let $x(t)$ denote displacement from the **natural equilibrium position** (so $x = 0$ is already the rest position with gravity balanced by the spring's static extension, if vertical).

**Forces acting on $m$:**

|Force|Expression|Direction|
|---|---|---|
|Spring restoring|$-kx$|Always toward $x=0$|
|Damping (friction/resistance)|$-c\dot{x}$, $c \geq 0$|Opposes velocity|
|External driving|$F(t)$|Prescribed|

By Newton's second law, $\sum F = ma = m\ddot{x}$:

$$ \boxed{m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)} $$

This is the **master equation** for all cases below. Divide through by $m$:

$$ \ddot{x} + \frac{c}{m}\dot{x} + \frac{k}{m}x = \frac{F(t)}{m} $$

Define the standard parameters: $$ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}} \quad \text{(natural angular frequency)}, \qquad \gamma = \frac{c}{2m} \quad \text{(damping coefficient)} $$

The equation becomes:

$$ \ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2, x = \frac{F(t)}{m} $$

## 2. Case I вЂ” Simple Harmonic Motion (Undamped, Unforced)

Set $c = 0$, $F(t) = 0$:

$$ \ddot{x} + \omega_0^2, x = 0 $$

### 2.1 Characteristic Equation and General Solution

Substitute $x = e^{rt}$:

$$ r^2 + \omega_0^2 = 0 \implies r = \pm, i\omega_0 $$

Complex roots $\Rightarrow$ general solution:

$$ \boxed{x(t) = C_1\cos(\omega_0 t) + C_2\sin(\omega_0 t)} $$

Equivalently, using amplitude-phase form:

$$ x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t - \phi) $$

where $A = \sqrt{C_1^2 + C_2^2}$ is the **amplitude** and $\phi = \arctan(C_2/C_1)$ is the **phase angle**.

### 2.2 Initial Conditions

Given $x(0) = x_0$, $\dot{x}(0) = v_0$:

$$ C_1 = x_0, \qquad C_2 = \frac{v_0}{\omega_0} $$

$$ A = \sqrt{x_0^2 + \frac{v_0^2}{\omega_0^2}}, \qquad \phi = \arctan!\left(\frac{v_0}{\omega_0 x_0}\right) $$

### 2.3 Energy Conservation

Kinetic + Potential energy is conserved:

$$ E = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 = \text{const} $$

_Verification:_ $\dot{E} = m\dot{x}\ddot{x} + kx\dot{x} = \dot{x}(m\ddot{x} + kx) = \dot{x} \cdot 0 = 0$. $\blacksquare$

### 2.4 Phase Plane: An Ellipse

Let $v = \dot{x}$. The system becomes:

$$ \dot{x} = v, \qquad \dot{v} = -\omega_0^2 x $$

Eliminating $t$: $\frac{dv}{dx} = \frac{-\omega_0^2 x}{v}$, so $v,dv = -\omega_0^2 x,dx$, giving:

$$ \frac{v^2}{\omega_0^2} + x^2 = A^2 \qquad \text{(family of ellipses)} $$

## 3. Case II вЂ” Damped Free Oscillation

Set $F(t) = 0$, $c > 0$:

$$ \ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2, x = 0 $$

### 3.1 Characteristic Equation

$$ r^2 + 2\gamma r + \omega_0^2 = 0 \implies r = -\gamma \pm \sqrt{\gamma^2 - \omega_0^2} $$

The discriminant $\Delta = \gamma^2 - \omega_0^2$ governs the qualitative behavior.

---

### 3.2 Subcase: Underdamping ($\gamma < \omega_0$, i.e., $\Delta < 0$)

$$ r = -\gamma \pm i\omega_d, \qquad \omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \gamma^2} \quad \text{(damped frequency)} $$

General solution:

$$ \boxed{x(t) = e^{-\gamma t}!\left[C_1\cos(\omega_d t) + C_2\sin(\omega_d t)\right] = A e^{-\gamma t}\cos(\omega_d t - \phi)} $$

The system **oscillates** but the amplitude decays exponentially. The factor $e^{-\gamma t}$ is an **envelope** squeezing the oscillation to zero.

### 3.3 Subcase: Overdamping ($\gamma > \omega_0$, i.e., $\Delta > 0$)

Two distinct negative real roots $r_1, r_2 < 0$:

$$ \boxed{x(t) = C_1 e^{r_1 t} + C_2 e^{r_2 t}} $$

No oscillation вЂ” the system **creeps** back to equilibrium. Think of a door closer in a thick oil: it closes, but slowly and without bouncing.

---

### 3.4 Subcase: Critical Damping ($\gamma = \omega_0$, i.e., $\Delta = 0$)

Repeated root $r = -\gamma$:

$$ \boxed{x(t) = (C_1 + C_2 t),e^{-\gamma t}} $$

**Critical damping returns to equilibrium as fast as possible without oscillating.** This is why car shock absorbers and galvanometers are tuned to near-critical damping.

_Why the extra $t$?_ When the characteristic equation has a repeated root $r_0$, the two linearly independent solutions are $e^{r_0 t}$ and $t e^{r_0 t}$. Their Wronskian is $e^{2r_0 t} \neq 0$, confirming independence. $\blacksquare$

## 4. Case III вЂ” Forced Oscillations and Resonance

Now $F(t) = F_0\cos(\omega t)$, a periodic driving force at frequency $\omega$:

$$ m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F_0\cos(\omega t) $$

### 4.1 Method of Undetermined Coefficients

The general solution is:

$$ x(t) = x_h(t) + x_p(t) $$

where $x_h$ is the (decaying) homogeneous solution and $x_p$ is a **particular solution** we seek.

Try $x_p = D\cos(\omega t) + E\sin(\omega t)$. Substitute and match coefficients:

$$ D = \frac{F_0/m,(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + 4\gamma^2\omega^2}, \qquad E = \frac{2\gamma\omega, F_0/m}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + 4\gamma^2\omega^2} $$

The amplitude of the steady-state response is:

$$ \boxed{A_p(\omega) = \frac{F_0/m}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + 4\gamma^2\omega^2}}} $$

### 4.2 Resonance

As $\omega \to \omega_0$ with $\gamma \to 0$ (undamped), the denominator $\to 0$, so $A_p \to \infty$.

**Pure resonance ($c = 0$, $\omega = \omega_0$):** The method of undetermined coefficients fails (forcing frequency equals natural frequency). Use **variation of parameters** or the standard resonance ansatz $x_p = t(D\cos\omega_0 t + E\sin\omega_0 t)$:

$$ \boxed{x_p(t) = \frac{F_0}{2m\omega_0},t\sin(\omega_0 t)} $$

The amplitude grows **linearly in time** вЂ” unbounded oscillation. This is the **Tacoma Narrows Bridge** phenomenon (1940), and why soldiers break step when crossing bridges.

## 5. The RLC Circuit вЂ” Same Equation

The **series RLC circuit** (resistor $R$, inductor $L$, capacitor $C$) with EMF $E(t)$ satisfies:

$$ L\ddot{q} + R\dot{q} + \frac{1}{C}q = E(t) $$

Identify: $L \leftrightarrow m$, $R \leftrightarrow c$, $1/C \leftrightarrow k$, $q \leftrightarrow x$. **Identical ODE.** Every result about mechanical oscillators translates immediately to electrical circuits. This is **mathematical universality** вЂ” a single equation ruling two different physical worlds.

|Mechanical|Electrical|
|---|---|
|Mass $m$|Inductance $L$|
|Damping $c$|Resistance $R$|
|Spring constant $k$|$1/C$|
|Displacement $x$|Charge $q$|
|Velocity $\dot{x}$|Current $\dot{q} = I$|
|Driving force $F(t)$|EMF $E(t)$|

## 6. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _Here the spring stops being a toy and becomes a lens._

### 6.1 Sinusoidal Positional Encodings (Transformers)

The **Transformer** architecture (Vaswani et al., 2017 вЂ” _Attention Is All You Need_) encodes position $pos$ in a sequence using:

$$ PE_{(pos, 2i)} = \sin!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \qquad PE_{(pos, 2i+1)} = \cos!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right) $$

These are exactly the solutions to $\ddot{x} + \omega_i^2 x = 0$ вЂ” undamped simple harmonic motion at frequency $\omega_i = 10000^{-2i/d}$. Each "dimension" of the positional encoding is an independent oscillator. The Transformer's ability to represent relative position relies on the trigonometric identities $\cos(\alpha - \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta + \sin\alpha\sin\beta$ вЂ” which are _addition formulas for SHM solutions_.

The recently proposed **RoPE** (Rotary Position Embedding, Su et al. 2021) used in LLaMA, GPT-NeoX, and many modern LLMs encodes position via a rotation matrix вЂ” again, the rotation group underlying SHM in the phase plane.

### 6.2 Recurrent Neural Networks as Discretized Oscillators

A second-order ODE $\ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0$ written as a first-order system:

$$ \frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix}x \ v\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \ -\omega_0^2 & -2\gamma\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}x \ v\end{pmatrix} $$

Euler discretize with step $\Delta t$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix}x_{t+1} \ v_{t+1}\end{pmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}1 & \Delta t \ -\omega_0^2\Delta t & 1-2\gamma\Delta t\end{pmatrix}}_{W}\begin{pmatrix}x_t \ v_t\end{pmatrix} $$

This is a **linear RNN update** $h_{t+1} = Wh_t$. The eigenvalues of $W$ determine stability вЂ” _exactly_ as in the damping regimes. Underdamped $\leftrightarrow$ complex eigenvalues (oscillatory hidden state), overdamped $\leftrightarrow$ real eigenvalues (monotone forgetting).

The **vanishing gradient problem** in RNNs is the overdamped regime: if $|eigenvalues(W)| < 1$, gradients decay exponentially through time вЂ” information is damped away. LSTMs and GRUs are engineered specifically to stay near the **critically damped** or **marginally stable** boundary.

### 6.3 Second-Order Optimization (Heavy Ball, Momentum)

Standard SGD: $\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta\nabla L(\theta_t)$ вЂ” Euler discretization of **gradient flow** $\dot{\theta} = -\nabla L$.

**SGD with momentum** (Polyak's Heavy Ball method):

$$ v_{t+1} = \beta v_t - \eta\nabla L(\theta_t), \qquad \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + v_{t+1} $$

This is the discretization of the **damped oscillator equation**:

$$ m\ddot{\theta} + c\dot{\theta} = -\nabla L(\theta) $$

where $m \sim \eta/(1-\beta)$ and $c \sim 1-\beta$. Momentum "accumulates" velocity like a mass, helping escape shallow minima and accelerating convergence in narrow valleys вЂ” exactly the behavior of an underdamped spring released near a potential minimum.

**Nesterov Accelerated Gradient** is the critically-damped version of this, achieving the optimal convergence rate $O(1/t^2)$ for convex $L$ вЂ” a consequence of tuning to the equivalent of critical damping.

### 6.4 Neural ODEs Revisited

In the Neural ODE framework, the second-order spring can be cast as a learned system:

$$ \frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix}x \ v\end{pmatrix} = f_\theta!\left(\begin{pmatrix}x \ v\end{pmatrix}, t\right) $$

where $f_\theta$ is a neural network. The network learns to mimic (or generalize) spring-like dynamics from data. Hamiltonian Neural Networks (Greydanus et al., 2019) and Lagrangian Neural Networks enforce conservation of the energy $E = \frac{1}{2}v^2 + \frac{1}{2}\omega_0^2 x^2$ as a structural prior вЂ” the phase-plane ellipses become the invariant set the network is constrained to respect.

## 7. Formal Proofs and Technicalities

### 7.1 Linear Independence of Solutions (Wronskian)

For the underdamped case, solutions $x_1 = e^{-\gamma t}\cos\omega_d t$ and $x_2 = e^{-\gamma t}\sin\omega_d t$:

$$ W(x_1, x_2) = \begin{vmatrix} x_1 & x_2 \ \dot{x}_1 & \dot{x}_2 \end{vmatrix} $$

Computing: $$ W = e^{-\gamma t}\cos\omega_d t \cdot e^{-\gamma t}(-\gamma\sin\omega_d t + \omega_d\cos\omega_d t) - e^{-\gamma t}\sin\omega_d t \cdot e^{-\gamma t}(-\gamma\cos\omega_d t - \omega_d\sin\omega_d t) $$

$$ = e^{-2\gamma t}\left[\omega_d\cos^2\omega_d t + \omega_d\sin^2\omega_d t\right] = \omega_d, e^{-2\gamma t} \neq 0 $$

So ${x_1, x_2}$ forms a fundamental set of solutions. $\blacksquare$

### 7.2 Abel's Identity (Why the Wronskian Decays)

For $\ddot{x} + p(t)\dot{x} + q(t)x = 0$, Abel's theorem gives:

$$ W(t) = W(t_0)\exp!\left(-\int_{t_0}^t p(s),ds\right) $$

For our equation, $p = 2\gamma$, so $W(t) = W_0 e^{-2\gamma t}$ вЂ” confirming the calculation above.

### 7.3 Stability via Characteristic Roots

The origin $(x, v) = (0,0)$ is **asymptotically stable** if and only if all characteristic roots have **negative real parts**.

For $r = -\gamma \pm \sqrt{\gamma^2 - \omega_0^2}$: since $\gamma > 0$ and $\omega_0 > 0$:

- Real parts are $-\gamma \pm \text{Re}!\left(\sqrt{\gamma^2 - \omega_0^2}\right)$.
- If $\gamma \geq \omega_0$: both roots real and $< 0$. вњ“
- If $\gamma < \omega_0$: roots are $-\gamma \pm i\omega_d$, real part $= -\gamma < 0$. вњ“

So for **any** $\gamma > 0$, the equilibrium is asymptotically stable. $\blacksquare$

## 8. Quick-Reference Formulas

$$ \omega_0 = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}, \qquad \gamma = \frac{c}{2m}, \qquad \omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \gamma^2} $$

$$ \text{Underdamped } (\gamma < \omega_0): \quad x(t) = Ae^{-\gamma t}\cos(\omega_d t - \phi) $$

$$ \text{Critically damped } (\gamma = \omega_0): \quad x(t) = (C_1 + C_2 t)e^{-\gamma t} $$

$$ \text{Overdamped } (\gamma > \omega_0): \quad x(t) = C_1 e^{r_1 t} + C_2 e^{r_2 t}, \quad r_{1,2} = -\gamma \pm \sqrt{\gamma^2 - \omega_0^2} $$

$$ \text{Resonance amplitude}: \quad A_p(\omega) = \frac{F_0/m}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2-\omega^2)^2 + 4\gamma^2\omega^2}} $$

$$ \text{Pure resonance particular solution}: \quad x_p(t) = \frac{F_0}{2m\omega_0},t\sin(\omega_0 t) $$

$$ \text{Energy (SHM)}: \quad E = \frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2 + \frac{1}{2}kx^2 = \frac{1}{2}kA^2 $$
