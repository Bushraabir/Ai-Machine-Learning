## Historical Context

**Johannes Kepler** (1571–1630) extracted these three laws empirically from **Tycho Brahe's** extraordinarily precise naked-eye observations of Mars. He did not know _why_ they were true.

**Isaac Newton** (1687, _Principia_) proved all three as mathematical consequences of:

1. $F = ma$ (second law)
2. $F = -GMm/r^2$ (universal gravitation, inverse-square)
3. Conservation of angular momentum

This is one of the great moments in the history of science: observation → law → derivation from deeper principle.

> **Note to self:** When Newton derived Kepler's First Law, he had to _invent_ the calculus to do it. The orbit equation is a second-order nonlinear ODE — and its general solution is a conic section. This is not a coincidence; it is a theorem.

## The Three Laws — Statement

> **Kepler's First Law (The Law of Ellipses).** The orbit of each planet is an **ellipse**, with the Sun at one **focus**.

> **Kepler's Second Law (The Law of Equal Areas).** A line segment joining a planet to the Sun sweeps out **equal areas** in equal time intervals.

> **Kepler's Third Law (The Harmonic Law).** The square of the orbital period $T$ is proportional to the cube of the semi-major axis $a$: $$T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} a^3$$

These are connected. The second law is purely about angular momentum conservation — it requires only a **central force** (not necessarily inverse-square). The first and third laws require the specific $1/r^2$ form.

## Law I: Orbits are Ellipses

### Deriving the Orbit Equation from Newton's Law

**Setup.** Use polar coordinates $(r, \theta)$ centered at the Sun (mass $M$), planet (mass $m$). Newton's second law in polar coordinates:

Radial: $$m(\ddot{r} - r\dot{\theta}^2) = -\frac{GMm}{r^2}$$

Tangential: $$m(r\ddot{\theta} + 2\dot{r}\dot{\theta}) = 0$$

The tangential equation says: $$\frac{d}{dt}(r^2 \dot{\theta}) = 0 \implies r^2\dot{\theta} = h = \text{const}$$

This constant $h$ is the **specific angular momentum** (angular momentum per unit mass). This is already Law II in disguise.

**Substitution.** Let $u = 1/r$. Then: $$\dot{r} = -\frac{1}{u^2}\dot{u} = -\frac{h}{u^2} \cdot \frac{du}{d\theta} \cdot \frac{\dot{\theta}}{1} = -h\frac{du}{d\theta}$$

$$\ddot{r} = -h\frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2}\dot{\theta} = -h^2 u^2 \frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2}$$

Substituting into the radial equation: $$-h^2 u^2 \frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2} - r \cdot h^2u^4 = -GMu^2$$

Wait — let me be careful. With $r = 1/u$ and $r\dot\theta^2 = (1/u) \cdot (hu^2)^2 = h^2 u^3$:

$$m\left(-h^2u^2\frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2} - h^2u^3 \cdot \frac{1}{u}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{1} = -GMmu^2$$

Simplifying the radial equation becomes the **Binet equation**:

$$\boxed{\frac{d^2u}{d\theta^2} + u = \frac{GM}{h^2}}$$

This is a **simple harmonic oscillator** equation with a constant forcing term! Its general solution is:

$$u = \frac{GM}{h^2} + C\cos(\theta - \theta_0)$$

Choosing $\theta_0 = 0$ (orienting periapsis along polar axis) and setting $\ell = h^2/(GM)$:

$$u = \frac{1}{\ell}(1 + e\cos\theta) \implies r = \frac{\ell}{1 + e\cos\theta}$$

where $e = C\ell \geq 0$ is determined by initial conditions (total energy). This is exactly the polar conic equation with semi-latus rectum $\ell = h^2/(GM)$. $\square$

> **This is Kepler's First Law as a theorem.** The orbit is a conic section. It's an ellipse when $e < 1$, i.e., when the total energy is negative (bound orbit).

### The Polar Conic Connection

Comparing with our [[Polar Equations in Conics]] note:

$$r = \frac{ed}{1 + e\cos\theta} \quad \longleftrightarrow \quad r = \frac{\ell}{1 + e\cos\theta}$$

So $\ell = ed$ is the **semi-latus rectum** — the value of $r$ at $\theta = \pi/2$, i.e., the half-chord through the focus perpendicular to the major axis.

|Quantity|Orbital meaning|Conic meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$h$|Specific angular momentum|$\sqrt{GM\ell}$|
|$e$|Eccentricity|Shape parameter|
|$\ell = h^2/GM$|Semi-latus rectum|$r$ at $\theta = \pi/2$|
|$e = 0$|Circular orbit|Circle|
|$0 < e < 1$|Bound elliptical orbit|Ellipse|
|$e = 1$|Parabolic escape|Parabola|
|$e > 1$|Hyperbolic flyby|Hyperbola|

## Law II: Equal Areas in Equal Times

### Proof via Angular Momentum Conservation

**Areal velocity.** In time $dt$, the planet sweeps out a small triangle with the Sun. In polar coordinates, the area of this triangle is:

$$dA = \frac{1}{2} r \cdot (r, d\theta) = \frac{1}{2}r^2, d\theta$$

Therefore:

$$\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{1}{2}r^2\dot{\theta} = \frac{h}{2}$$

Since $h = r^2\dot{\theta}$ is conserved (for _any_ central force, not just $1/r^2$), the areal velocity is **constant**.

$$\boxed{\frac{dA}{dt} = \frac{h}{2} = \text{const}}$$

Equal times $\Rightarrow$ equal $\Delta A$. **QED.** $\square$

> **This is the deepest of the three laws conceptually.** It is equivalent to conservation of angular momentum, which follows from the fact that gravity is a **central force** (directed along the line joining Sun and planet). No specific $r$-dependence is needed. It would hold for $1/r^3$, $1/r^5$, anything central.

### The Areal Velocity

The total area of an ellipse is $A = \pi ab$. Over one period $T$, the full area is swept:

$$\pi ab = \frac{h}{2} \cdot T \implies T = \frac{2\pi ab}{h}$$

This feeds directly into Law III.

## Law III: The Harmonic Law

### Proof for Circular Orbits (Warm-Up)

For a circular orbit of radius $r$, the gravitational force provides the centripetal acceleration:

$$\frac{GMm}{r^2} = \frac{mv^2}{r} = mr\omega^2 = mr\left(\frac{2\pi}{T}\right)^2$$

Solving:

$$\frac{GM}{r^2} = \frac{4\pi^2 r}{T^2} \implies T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} r^3$$

For a circle, $r = a$, so $T^2 \propto a^3$. $\square$

### Proof for Elliptical Orbits (Full)

From the period formula derived in Law II:

$$T = \frac{2\pi ab}{h}$$

For an ellipse:

- Semi-latus rectum: $\ell = b^2/a$
- So $b^2 = a\ell = ah^2/(GM)$
- Therefore $b = a^{1/2} \cdot h / \sqrt{GM}$

Substituting:

$$T = \frac{2\pi a \cdot \left(\dfrac{a^{1/2} h}{\sqrt{GM}}\right)}{h} = \frac{2\pi a^{3/2}}{\sqrt{GM}}$$

$$\boxed{T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM} a^3}$$

$\square$

> **This is remarkable.** The period depends only on $a$, the semi-major axis — not on the eccentricity. Two planets with the same semi-major axis but wildly different eccentricities have the same period. The shape doesn't matter, only the "size" in one direction.

## Unified Picture: Energy and Orbit Shape

The total mechanical energy per unit mass determines the orbit:

$$E = \frac{1}{2}v^2 - \frac{GM}{r} = -\frac{GM}{2a}$$

The eccentricity is related to $E$ and $h$ by:

$$e = \sqrt{1 + \frac{2Eh^2}{G^2M^2}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{b^2}{a^2}}$$

|Energy $E$|Eccentricity $e$|Orbit|$a$|
|---|---|---|---|
|$E < 0$|$0 \leq e < 1$|Ellipse (bound)|$a = -GM/(2E) > 0$|
|$E = 0$|$e = 1$|Parabola|$a \to \infty$|
|$E > 0$|$e > 1$|Hyperbola (unbound)|$a = GM/(2E) > 0$*|

*For hyperbola, $a$ is defined as the distance from center to vertex.

**Escape velocity.** Setting $E = 0$ at surface radius $R$: $$\frac{1}{2}v_{\text{esc}}^2 = \frac{GM}{R} \implies v_{\text{esc}} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}$$

At Earth's surface: $v_{\text{esc}} \approx 11.2$ km/s.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Earth's Orbit

Earth: $a = 1$ AU $= 1.496 \times 10^{11}$ m, $e \approx 0.0167$.

**Period check:** $T^2 = \frac{4\pi^2}{GM_\odot} a^3$

With $GM_\odot = 1.327 \times 10^{20}$ m³/s²:

$$T = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{a^3}{GM_\odot}} = 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{(1.496\times10^{11})^3}{1.327\times10^{20}}} \approx 3.156\times10^7 \text{ s} \approx 365.25 \text{ days} \checkmark$$

**Perihelion and aphelion:** $$r_{\min} = a(1-e) = 0.9833 \text{ AU}, \qquad r_{\max} = a(1+e) = 1.0167 \text{ AU}$$

About 3.3% variation in Earth-Sun distance. Not why we have seasons.

---

### Example 2 — Halley's Comet

$T = 75.3$ years, $e = 0.967$.

**Semi-major axis:** $$a = \left(\frac{GM_\odot T^2}{4\pi^2}\right)^{1/3}$$

In astronomical units: $a^3 = T^2$ (when $T$ in years, $a$ in AU) gives $a = 75.3^{2/3} \approx 17.8$ AU.

**Perihelion distance:** $r_{\min} = a(1-e) = 17.8(0.033) \approx 0.587$ AU (inside Venus's orbit).

**Aphelion distance:** $r_{\max} = a(1+e) = 17.8(1.967) \approx 35$ AU (beyond Neptune).

**Polar equation** (Sun at focus, periapsis at $\theta = 0$): $$\ell = a(1-e^2) = 17.8(1 - 0.967^2) \approx 1.15 \text{ AU}$$ $$r = \frac{1.15}{1 + 0.967\cos\theta} \text{ AU}$$

---

### Example 3 — Geostationary Orbit

A satellite in geostationary orbit ($T = 24$ h) around Earth. Find the orbital radius.

$$r^3 = \frac{GM_\oplus T^2}{4\pi^2}$$

With $GM_\oplus = 3.986 \times 10^{14}$ m³/s² and $T = 86400$ s:

$$r = \left(\frac{3.986\times10^{14} \cdot (86400)^2}{4\pi^2}\right)^{1/3} \approx 4.22 \times 10^7 \text{ m} \approx 42{,}200 \text{ km}$$

This is about 6.6 Earth radii from the center, or 35,800 km above the surface.

---

### Example 4 — Interplanetary Transfer (Hohmann Transfer)

To travel from Earth ($a_1 = 1$ AU) to Mars ($a_2 = 1.524$ AU) on a Hohmann transfer ellipse:

- Transfer orbit semi-major axis: $a_t = (a_1 + a_2)/2 = 1.262$ AU
- Transfer time (half-period): $t = T_t/2 = \pi\sqrt{a_t^3/GM_\odot}$

$$t = \pi\sqrt{\frac{(1.262)^3}{1}} \approx \pi \cdot 1.418 \approx 4.454 \text{ years}/\text{(2π)} \approx 259 \text{ days}$$

This is the _minimum energy_ trajectory — the one that uses least fuel. It follows an ellipse with perihelion at Earth's orbit and aphelion at Mars's orbit.

## Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

The connections here go deeper than might be expected.

---

### 1. Inverse Problems & Orbital Mechanics in ML

Given noisy observations of a planet's position $(r_i, \theta_i)$, recover the orbital parameters $(e, \ell)$ — this is a **nonlinear least squares** problem:

$$\min_{e,\ell} \sum_i \left(r_i - \frac{\ell}{1 + e\cos\theta_i}\right)^2$$

Modern approaches use **Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)** to solve orbital mechanics problems, where the Kepler equations are embedded as _constraints_ or _regularizers_ in the loss function. The network must learn solutions consistent with Kepler's laws.

---

### 2. Hamiltonian Neural Networks — Learning Conservation Laws

Kepler's Second Law is conservation of angular momentum: $L = r^2\dot\theta = h$. Kepler's Third Law encodes conservation of energy: $E = -GM/(2a)$.

**Hamiltonian Neural Networks** (Greydanus et al., 2019) learn to respect these conservation laws by parameterizing dynamics through the Hamiltonian $\mathcal{H}(q, p)$:

$$\dot{q} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial p}, \qquad \dot{p} = -\frac{\partial \mathcal{H}}{\partial q}$$

Trained on trajectory data, they provably conserve energy — unlike standard neural ODEs which drift. The orbital mechanics case (2-body problem) is the canonical benchmark.

> The Hamiltonian for the Kepler problem is $\mathcal{H} = p_r^2/2 + p_\theta^2/(2r^2) - GM/r$. The $p_\theta = h = \text{const}$ is Law II; the value of $\mathcal{H} = E = \text{const}$ plus the orbit equation gives Law I; Law III follows from integrating Law II over a full period.

---

### 3. Neural ODEs and Symplectic Integrators

The equations of orbital motion form a **Hamiltonian system**. Numerically integrating them requires **symplectic integrators** (Störmer-Verlet, leapfrog) that preserve phase-space volume (Liouville's theorem) and the symplectic structure.

**Neural ODEs** use neural networks as the vector field: $$\frac{dz}{dt} = f_\theta(z, t)$$

When applied to physical systems like orbital mechanics, vanilla Neural ODEs violate conservation laws over long integration. **Symplectic Neural ODEs** and **Lagrangian Neural Networks** are architectures specifically designed to respect these geometric structures.

Kepler orbits are the most-studied test case — if your "physics-aware" architecture can't reproduce elliptical orbits and the $T^2 \propto a^3$ relation, it's wrong.

---

### 4. Symmetry and Equivariant Neural Networks

Kepler's laws are consequences of symmetries:

- **Rotational symmetry** of gravity → conservation of angular momentum (Law II) via Noether's theorem
- **Time-translation symmetry** → conservation of energy (Law I, orbit is fixed)
- **Hidden SO(4) symmetry** (Laplace-Runge-Lenz vector) → closed elliptical orbits specifically (only for $1/r^2$!)

**Equivariant neural networks** (SE(3)-equivariant, E(n)-equivariant) are architectures that bake in these symmetries by design. They appear in:

- **AlphaFold 2** — SE(3)-equivariant attention for protein structure
- **NequIP, MACE** — equivariant graph networks for molecular dynamics
- **Orbital mechanics simulators** — SO(3)-equivariant networks for n-body problems

The lesson: knowing the symmetry group of your problem → better inductive bias → more data-efficient learning.

---

### 5. Areal Velocity = Cross Product = Attention Mechanism?

The areal velocity $dA/dt = \frac{1}{2}|r \times v|$ is a **cross product** in 2D — equivalently, the determinant of a $2 \times 2$ matrix:

$$h = \left|\begin{matrix} r_x & r_y \ v_x & v_y \end{matrix}\right|$$

In the **attention mechanism** of transformers:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

The $QK^T$ matrix is a matrix of inner products between queries and keys. The softmax produces a probability distribution that "selects" values — it's an angular weighting, related to projections. The connection is loose but real: **both are fundamentally about inner-product geometry and selective integration over a space**, with the dot product playing the role of the angular velocity in determining how much "area" (attention) is swept.

---

### 6. Orbital Mechanics in Satellite Scheduling — Combinatorial Optimization

Modern ML uses Kepler's laws extensively in:

- **Satellite imagery scheduling** (what can a satellite see and when, given its orbit)
- **Collision avoidance** (predicting conjunction events)
- **Starlink/satellite constellation optimization** — large-scale combinatorial problems where Keplerian propagation is the dynamics model and RL/evolutionary algorithms search for optimal configurations

The orbital propagator (numerical integration of Kepler's equations) is literally called thousands of times per second in reinforcement learning training loops for these applications.

## Summary

|---|---|---|---|

|**II**|$dA/dt = h/2 = \text{const}$|Equal areas in equal times|Any central force|
|**III**|$T^2 = (4\pi^2/GM)a^3$|Period² ∝ (semi-major axis)³|$F \propto 1/r^2$|

**Key quantities:**

$$h = r^2\dot\theta \quad \text{(specific angular momentum, conserved)}$$

$$E = \frac{1}{2}v^2 - \frac{GM}{r} = -\frac{GM}{2a} \quad \text{(specific energy, conserved)}$$

$$\ell = \frac{h^2}{GM} = a(1-e^2) \quad \text{(semi-latus rectum)}$$

$$e = \sqrt{1 + \frac{2Eh^2}{G^2M^2}} \quad \text{(eccentricity from energy and momentum)}$$
