## §0 — Motivation

In Cartesian calculus, arc length of $y = F(x)$ from $x=a$ to $x=b$ is:

$$ L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}, dx $$

But this formula secretly assumes $x$ increases monotonically along the curve. The moment the curve doubles back, spirals, or loops — as virtually every interesting curve in science and engineering does — this formula breaks down entirely.

The parametric formulation fixes this. Instead of describing the curve as a graph, we describe it as a **trajectory**: a particle moving through the plane, its position $(x(t), y(t))$ varying with time $t$. Arc length becomes the **total distance traveled** — and _that_ is a concept that makes sense regardless of how the curve bends, reverses, or loops.

---

## §1 — Derivation from First Principles

### 1.1 Polygonal Approximation

Partition $[\alpha, \beta]$ into $n$ subintervals by $\alpha = t_0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n = \beta$. The curve is approximated by the **inscribed polygon** with vertices at $(f(t_i), g(t_i))$.

The length of the $i$-th chord is:

$$ \ell_i = \sqrt{(f(t_i) - f(t_{i-1}))^2 + (g(t_i) - g(t_{i-1}))^2} $$

By the **Mean Value Theorem**, applied to $f$ and $g$ separately on $[t_{i-1}, t_i]$: there exist $t_i^*, t_i^{**} \in (t_{i-1}, t_i)$ such that

$$ f(t_i) - f(t_{i-1}) = f'(t_i^*),\Delta t_i, \qquad g(t_i) - g(t_{i-1}) = g'(t_i^{**}),\Delta t_i $$

So:

$$ \ell_i = \sqrt{f'(t_i^*)^2 + g'(t_i^{**})^2},\Delta t_i $$

Summing:

$$ L \approx \sum_{i=1}^n \sqrt{f'(t_i^*)^2 + g'(t_i^{**})^2},\Delta t_i $$

> **Subtlety.** $t_i^*$ and $t_i^{**}$ are generally _different_ sample points — so this is not quite a Riemann sum for $\sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2}$. The rigorous argument uses the fact that if $f'$ and $g'$ are **continuous**, then as $|\Delta| \to 0$, both sample points converge to the same limiting behavior, and the sum converges to the integral below. This is the content of the arc length theorem in real analysis.

Taking $n \to \infty$ (and $|\Delta| \to 0$):

$$ \boxed{L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}, dt = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}, dt} $$

### 1.2 The Infinitesimal Arc Element

Define the **arc length element** $ds$:

$$ ds = \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}, dt = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|, dt $$

This is the **speed** of the particle times the time increment: $ds = |\mathbf{v}(t)|,dt$. Arc length is just the integral of speed over time — total distance traveled.

$$ L = \int_\alpha^\beta |\mathbf{r}'(t)|, dt = \int_\alpha^\beta |\mathbf{v}(t)|, dt $$

This is the fundamental formula of kinematics, reinterpreted as geometry.

---

## §2 — The Speed Vector Perspective

Let $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle f(t), g(t) \rangle$. Then:

- **Velocity vector:** $\mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle f'(t), g'(t) \rangle$
- **Speed (scalar):** $|\mathbf{v}(t)| = \sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}$
- **Arc length:** $L = \int_\alpha^\beta |\mathbf{v}(t)|, dt$

The speed $|\mathbf{v}(t)|$ is **independent of how the curve is parametrized** — it depends on both the curve's shape _and_ the rate of traversal. This is why two different parametrizations of the same geometric curve can give the same $L$ but different integrands.

### 2.1 Natural (Arc Length) Parametrization

Define the **arc length function** from a base point $t = \alpha$:

$$ s(t) = \int_\alpha^t \sqrt{f'(u)^2 + g'(u)^2}, du $$

Then $\dfrac{ds}{dt} = \sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2} = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|$.

If we reparametrize the curve by $s$ (arc length), we get $\mathbf{r}(s)$ with the remarkable property:

$$ \left|\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{ds}\right| = 1 \quad \text{for all } s $$

The **unit tangent vector** is then simply $\hat{\mathbf{T}} = \dfrac{d\mathbf{r}}{ds}$. This is the **natural parametrization** — the curve traversed at unit speed — and it is the foundation of differential geometry (curvature, Frenet–Serret frames, etc.).

> **Remark.** In practice, $s(t)$ is rarely expressible in closed form, so reparametrizing explicitly is often impossible. But the _existence_ of arc length parametrization is conceptually essential.

---

## §3 — Relation to the Cartesian Formula

Suppose $y = F(x)$, i.e., $x = t$, $y = F(t)$. Then $f'(t) = 1$, $g'(t) = F'(t)$, and:

$$ L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + F'(t)^2}, dt = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}, dx $$

The Cartesian formula is a **special case** of the parametric formula — with the trivial parametrization $x = t$.

## §4 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Circumference of a Circle

$$ x = r\cos t, \quad y = r\sin t, \quad t \in [0, 2\pi] $$

$$ f'(t) = -r\sin t, \quad g'(t) = r\cos t $$

$$ L = \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{r^2\sin^2 t + r^2\cos^2 t}, dt = \int_0^{2\pi} r, dt = 2\pi r \checkmark $$

A sanity check: the parametric formula recovers the most ancient result in geometry.

---

### Example 2 — Arc Length of One Arch of the Cycloid

$$ x = r(t - \sin t),\quad y = r(1-\cos t),\quad t\in[0,2\pi] $$

$$ f'(t) = r(1-\cos t),\quad g'(t) = r\sin t $$

$$ f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2 = r^2\bigl[(1-\cos t)^2 + \sin^2 t\bigr] = r^2\bigl[1 - 2\cos t + \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t\bigr] = r^2[2 - 2\cos t] $$

Using the half-angle identity $1 - \cos t = 2\sin^2(t/2)$:

$$ = 2r^2(1 - \cos t) = 4r^2\sin^2!\left(\frac{t}{2}\right) $$

Therefore $\sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2} = 2r\left|\sin(t/2)\right| = 2r\sin(t/2)$ (since $\sin(t/2) \geq 0$ for $t \in [0,2\pi]$).

$$ L = \int_0^{2\pi} 2r\sin!\left(\frac{t}{2}\right) dt = 2r\left[-2\cos!\left(\frac{t}{2}\right)\right]_0^{2\pi} = 2r\bigl[-2\cos\pi + 2\cos 0\bigr] = 2r(2 + 2) = 8r $$

$$ \boxed{L = 8r} $$

The arc length of one cycloid arch is **exactly 8 radii** of the rolling circle — a beautiful, clean answer from what looked like a messy integral.

---

### Example 3 — The Astroid

$$ x = a\cos^3 t,\quad y = a\sin^3 t,\quad t\in[0,2\pi] $$

$$ f'(t) = -3a\cos^2 t\sin t,\quad g'(t) = 3a\sin^2 t\cos t $$

$$ f'^2 + g'^2 = 9a^2\cos^4 t\sin^2 t + 9a^2\sin^4 t\cos^2 t = 9a^2\sin^2 t\cos^2 t(\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t) = 9a^2\sin^2 t\cos^2 t $$

$$ \sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2} = 3a|\sin t\cos t| = \frac{3a}{2}|\sin 2t| $$

By symmetry across the four quadrants:

$$ L = 4\int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{3a}{2}\sin 2t, dt = 6a\left[-\frac{\cos 2t}{2}\right]_0^{\pi/2} = 6a\left[\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\right] = 6a $$

$$ \boxed{L = 6a} $$

The astroid's perimeter is $6a$ — exactly $\dfrac{6}{\pi}$ times the circumference of its circumscribed circle. Remarkably clean.

---

### Example 4 — Logarithmic Spiral

$$ x = e^t\cos t,\quad y = e^t\sin t,\quad t\in[0,T] $$

$$ f'(t) = e^t(\cos t - \sin t),\quad g'(t) = e^t(\sin t + \cos t) $$

$$ f'^2 + g'^2 = e^{2t}[(\cos t-\sin t)^2 + (\sin t+\cos t)^2] = e^{2t}[2\cos^2 t + 2\sin^2 t] = 2e^{2t} $$

$$ L = \int_0^T \sqrt{2},e^t, dt = \sqrt{2}(e^T - 1) $$

The logarithmic spiral has finite arc length to any finite $T$, and it grows exponentially with $T$.

## §6 — Surface Area of Revolution (Extension)

When the parametric curve is revolved around the $x$-axis, the surface area is:

$$ S = \int_\alpha^\beta 2\pi, |g(t)|, \sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}, dt = \int_\alpha^\beta 2\pi, |y|, ds $$

Around the $y$-axis:

$$ S = \int_\alpha^\beta 2\pi, |f(t)|, \sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}, dt = \int_\alpha^\beta 2\pi, |x|, ds $$

The integrand $2\pi |y|, ds$ has a clear geometric meaning: each infinitesimal arc element $ds$ sweeps out a thin band of circumference $2\pi|y|$ — a "ring" of area $2\pi|y|,ds$.

**Example.** Sphere from semicircle $x = r\cos t,, y = r\sin t,, t\in[0,\pi]$, revolved around $x$-axis:

$$ S = \int_0^\pi 2\pi r\sin t \cdot r, dt = 2\pi r^2 \int_0^\pi \sin t, dt = 2\pi r^2 \cdot 2 = 4\pi r^2 \checkmark $$

## §7 — Why Most Arc Length Integrals Are Non-Elementary

The integrand $\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}$ involves a square root of a sum of squares. For most natural choices of $f$ and $g$, this does not integrate in closed form. Specifically:

- **Ellipse** $x = a\cos t,, y = b\sin t$: the integrand becomes $\sqrt{a^2\sin^2 t + b^2\cos^2 t}$, which integrates to an **elliptic integral** — a transcendental function not expressible in elementary terms. This is why the perimeter of an ellipse has no simple formula.
- **Parabola**, **sine curve**, most polynomials: similar non-elementary integrands.

The fact that the circle ($a = b = r$), the cycloid, and the astroid _do_ yield clean answers is remarkable — and is why these curves appeared repeatedly in 17th–19th century mathematics.

> This is not a failure of technique. Liouville's theorem in differential algebra proves that such integrals are genuinely non-elementary for most curves.

## §8 — Rigorous Summary

> **Theorem (Parametric Arc Length).** Let $\mathbf{r}(t) = (f(t), g(t))$ where $f'$ and $g'$ are continuous on $[\alpha, \beta]$. Then the arc length of the curve from $t = \alpha$ to $t = \beta$ is: $$L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}, dt$$

> **Theorem (Parametrization Independence).** If $\mathbf{r}(t)$ and $\mathbf{R}(u)$ are two smooth parametrizations of the same oriented curve, related by a smooth bijection $t = \phi(u)$ with $\phi'(u) > 0$, then both give the same arc length $L$.

_Proof sketch._ Substituting $t = \phi(u)$, $dt = \phi'(u),du$, and $f'(t) = \frac{df}{du}\cdot\frac{1}{\phi'(u)}$ — the $\phi'(u)$ factors cancel, leaving the same integrand in $u$. $\square$

> **Corollary.** Arc length is an intrinsic geometric property of the curve, independent of parametrization.

## §9 — Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 9.1 Gradient Descent Trajectory Length

Training dynamics in neural networks define a parametric curve $\theta(t)$ in weight space $\mathbb{R}^n$. The **total path length** of the optimization trajectory is:

$$ L = \int_0^T |\theta'(t)|, dt = \int_0^T |\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta(t))|\cdot\eta, dt $$

(in continuous gradient flow). This is the arc length of the loss descent curve. Research has shown that **flatter loss landscapes** (small $|\nabla \mathcal{L}|$, i.e., low speed along the trajectory) correlate with better generalization — and the total trajectory length is a measure of how much the optimizer "traveled" to reach a solution.

### 9.2 Elastic Weight Consolidation (EWC) and Path Length

In **continual learning**, EWC penalizes large moves in weight space between tasks. The penalty is related to the squared $\ell_2$ distance traveled:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{EWC}} = \mathcal{L}_B(\theta) + \frac{\lambda}{2}\sum_i F_i(\theta_i - \theta_{A,i}^*)^2 $$

where $F_i$ approximates the local metric (Fisher information). This is a **discretization of arc length** in a Riemannian metric on weight space — the Fisher metric. The arc length integral $\int |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt$ generalizes to $\int \sqrt{\mathbf{r}'(t)^T \mathbf{F}(\theta(t)), \mathbf{r}'(t)},dt$ in the Fisher geometry.

### 9.3 Natural Parametrization and Positional Encodings

In **Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE)** — used in LLaMA, Mistral, and most modern LLMs — the position encoding rotates token embeddings by an angle proportional to position:

$$ \mathbf{q}_m = R_{\Theta,m},\mathbf{q}_m $$

where $R_{\Theta,m}$ is a block-diagonal rotation matrix. The positions $m$ are the **arc length parameter** of a curve on the unit circle in each frequency subspace. The choice of unit-speed parametrization (equal angular increments per position) is precisely the **natural (arc length) parametrization** of the circular trajectory — ensuring uniform spacing in the embedding space.

### 9.4 Curve Length as a Regularizer in Shape Models

In **active contour models** (Snakes, used in medical image segmentation), the energy functional to minimize includes an **arc length regularization term**:

$$ E[\mathbf{r}] = \int_0^1 \left[\alpha|\mathbf{r}'(s)|^2 + \beta|\mathbf{r}''(s)|^2\right] ds + E_{\text{image}}[\mathbf{r}] $$

The first term $\alpha\int|\mathbf{r}'|^2,ds$ penalizes long contours (arc length proxy). The second penalizes high curvature (bending). This is a **calculus of variations** problem over parametric curves — and modern deep learning versions (DeepSnake, E2EC) learn to minimize similar energies via neural networks.

### 9.5 Riemannian Geometry and Geodesics in Latent Space

In **VAEs** and **generative models**, the latent space carries a non-Euclidean geometry induced by the decoder. The **geodesic** (shortest path) between two latent points $z_0$ and $z_1$ is the curve $\gamma(t)$ minimizing:

$$ L[\gamma] = \int_0^1 \sqrt{\gamma'(t)^T \mathbf{G}(\gamma(t)),\gamma'(t)}, dt $$

where $\mathbf{G}(z)$ is the **pullback metric** (Riemannian metric induced by the decoder Jacobian). This is the **parametric arc length formula in a curved space** — the direct generalization of $\int\sqrt{f'^2+g'^2},dt$ to a Riemannian manifold. Interpolating in latent space along geodesics (instead of straight lines) produces semantically smoother interpolations between generated images.

### 9.6 Sequence Length and Attention Complexity

In transformers, the **sequence length** $n$ is treated as a discrete arc length — the number of tokens is the number of steps along the "curve" of a document. The quadratic $O(n^2)$ complexity of self-attention comes from computing all pairwise interactions along this sequence. Efficient transformers (Longformer, Big Bird) approximate this by attending only to nearby positions — mimicking the **local** nature of the arc length element $ds$, which only sees infinitesimal neighborhoods.

## §11 — Common Pitfalls

1. **Forgetting the square root is of a sum, not a sum of square roots.** $\sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2} \neq |f'| + |g'|$.
    
2. **Assuming the formula gives displacement.** Arc length is total distance traveled, not $|\mathbf{r}(\beta) - \mathbf{r}(\alpha)|$. A curve that loops back still accumulates length on the return trip.
    
3. **Missing absolute values in $|\sin(t/2)|$.** In the cycloid derivation, $\sin(t/2) \geq 0$ on $[0, 2\pi]$, so it's fine — but on extended intervals, check the sign.
    
4. **Confusing arc length with area.** Area: $\int g(t),f'(t),dt$. Arc length: $\int\sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2},dt$. The geometric content is completely different.
    
5. **Parametrization independence.** Two different parametrizations of the same curve give the same $L$. The integrand changes, but the integral does not.
