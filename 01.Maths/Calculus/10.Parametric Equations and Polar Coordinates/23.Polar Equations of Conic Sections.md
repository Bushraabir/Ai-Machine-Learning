## Motivation & Setup

In Cartesian coordinates, the three conic sections — ellipse, parabola, hyperbola — each wear their own algebraic costume:

$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1, \qquad y = ax^2, \qquad \frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$$

They look like strangers. Polar coordinates, with a **focus at the origin**, reveal them as members of a single family, parameterised by one number: the **eccentricity** $e$.

> **Personal note:** This is one of those moments in mathematics where the "right" coordinate system collapses three theorems into one. Always ask: _what is the natural home of this object?_

---

## The Unified Polar Form

> **Theorem (Unified Polar Equation of a Conic).** Let $F$ be a focus placed at the origin, and let $\ell$ be a directrix at perpendicular distance $d$ from $F$. Then the conic with eccentricity $e > 0$ has polar equation
> 
> $$\boxed{r = \frac{ed}{1 + e\cos\theta}}$$
> 
> (directrix to the **right** of the focus). Analogous forms hold for other directrix orientations.

|$e$|Conic|
|---|---|
|$0 < e < 1$|Ellipse|
|$e = 1$|Parabola|
|$e > 1$|Hyperbola|

The **four standard forms** depending on directrix placement:

|Directrix|Polar Equation|
|---|---|
|$x = d > 0$ (right of focus)|$r = \dfrac{ed}{1 + e\cos\theta}$|
|$x = -d < 0$ (left of focus)|$r = \dfrac{ed}{1 - e\cos\theta}$|
|$y = d > 0$ (above focus)|$r = \dfrac{ed}{1 + e\sin\theta}$|
|$y = -d < 0$ (below focus)|$r = \dfrac{ed}{1 - e\sin\theta}$|

## Proof of the Unified Form

**Setup.** Place the focus $F$ at the origin. Let the directrix be the vertical line $x = d$ (so it lies to the right, at distance $d$ from $F$). Let $P = (r, \theta)$ be a point on the conic.

**Focus-Directrix Definition.** By definition, a conic is the locus of points where:

$$\frac{\text{distance to focus}}{\text{distance to directrix}} = e$$

- Distance from $P$ to focus: $\overline{PF} = r$
- Distance from $P$ to directrix $x = d$: The point $P$ has Cartesian $x$-coordinate $x = r\cos\theta$, so the horizontal distance to $x = d$ is $d - r\cos\theta$.

The focus-directrix condition gives:

$$\frac{r}{d - r\cos\theta} = e$$

**Solving for $r$:**

$$r = e(d - r\cos\theta) = ed - er\cos\theta$$

$$r + er\cos\theta = ed$$

$$r(1 + e\cos\theta) = ed$$

$$\boxed{r = \frac{ed}{1 + e\cos\theta}}$$

$\square$

> **Remark.** Note carefully that when $e > 1$ (hyperbola), the denominator $1 + e\cos\theta$ can equal zero — this corresponds to the asymptotic directions. At those angles $r \to \infty$, which is geometrically correct.

## The Three Conics in Detail

### 1. Parabola ($e = 1$)

The equation becomes:

$$r = \frac{d}{1 + \cos\theta}$$

**Key features:**

- At $\theta = 0$: $r = d/2$ — this is the **vertex** (the point of closest approach to the focus).
- At $\theta = \pi/2$ or $\theta = 3\pi/2$: $r = d$ — these are the endpoints of the **latus rectum** (the chord through the focus perpendicular to the axis).
- As $\theta \to \pi$: $r \to \infty$ — the parabola opens to the left.

**Worked Example.** Find the polar equation of the parabola with focus at origin and directrix $x = 3$.

Here $e = 1$, $d = 3$: $$r = \frac{3}{1 + \cos\theta}$$

Vertex at $r = 3/2$ when $\theta = 0$, i.e., point $(3/2, 0)$.

---

### 2. Ellipse ($0 < e < 1$)

$$r = \frac{ed}{1 + e\cos\theta}$$

**Key features:**

- **Periapsis** (closest point to focus, $\theta = 0$): $r_{\min} = \dfrac{ed}{1+e}$
- **Apoapsis** (farthest point, $\theta = \pi$): $r_{\max} = \dfrac{ed}{1-e}$
- Semi-major axis: $a = \dfrac{r_{\min} + r_{\max}}{2} = \dfrac{ed}{1-e^2}$
- The **other** focus lies at distance $2ae$ from the origin along the axis.

**Worked Example.** Sketch and identify $r = \dfrac{6}{3 + 2\cos\theta}$.

Divide numerator and denominator by 3: $$r = \frac{2}{1 + \frac{2}{3}\cos\theta}$$

So $e = 2/3 < 1$ → **ellipse**, and $ed = 2 \Rightarrow d = 3$.

- $r_{\min} = 2/(1 + 2/3) = 6/5$ at $\theta = 0$
- $r_{\max} = 2/(1 - 2/3) = 6$ at $\theta = \pi$
- Semi-major axis: $a = (6/5 + 6)/2 = 18/5$

---

### 3. Hyperbola ($e > 1$)

$$r = \frac{ed}{1 + e\cos\theta}$$

**Key features:**

- The denominator $1 + e\cos\theta = 0$ when $\cos\theta = -1/e$, giving **asymptotic angles** $\theta^* = \pm\arccos(-1/e)$.
- Only one branch is traced by $r > 0$. The other branch appears if we allow $r < 0$, or equivalently use the form with $1 - e\cos\theta$.
- **Periapsis**: $r_{\min} = \dfrac{ed}{1+e}$

**Worked Example.** Identify $r = \dfrac{10}{1 - 2\cos\theta}$.

Rewrite: this has form $\dfrac{ed}{1 - e\cos\theta}$, so $e = 2 > 1$ → **hyperbola** with directrix to the left.

- $ed = 10 \Rightarrow d = 5$
- Asymptotic angles: $\cos\theta = 1/2 \Rightarrow \theta = \pm 60°$

## Rotating Conics

If we want the **major axis tilted** by angle $\alpha$, replace $\theta$ with $\theta - \alpha$:

$$r = \frac{ed}{1 + e\cos(\theta - \alpha)}$$

**Example.** The ellipse $r = \dfrac{6}{1 + \frac{1}{2}\cos(\theta - \pi/4)}$ has its major axis tilted $45°$ from the horizontal. Periapsis is at $\theta = \pi/4$ (not $\theta = 0$).

This is essentially a **change of basis** in angle — an idea that recurs constantly in signal processing and Fourier analysis.

## Connection to Kepler's Laws

> **Kepler's First Law:** The orbit of a planet is an ellipse, with the Sun at one focus.

This is where polar conics become physics. In Newtonian mechanics, the solution to the two-body gravitational problem gives:

$$r = \frac{\ell}{1 + e\cos\theta}, \qquad \ell = \frac{h^2}{GM}$$

where:

- $h$ = specific angular momentum (conserved quantity)
- $G$ = gravitational constant, $M$ = mass of central body
- $e$ = orbital eccentricity (determined by total energy)

|$e$|Energy|Orbit|
|---|---|---|
|$0 \leq e < 1$|$E < 0$ (bound)|Ellipse / Circle|
|$e = 1$|$E = 0$|Parabola|
|$e > 1$|$E > 0$ (unbound)|Hyperbola|

The polar form **literally** is the solution to Newton's gravitational equation — not a coincidence, but a deep structural fact.

## Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

This is not a superficial connection. Conic sections and their polar representations appear in several places across modern machine learning.

---

### 1. Support Vector Machines (SVMs) — Quadratic Decision Boundaries

The general conic $Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$ is the most general **degree-2 decision boundary**. The discriminant $B^2 - 4AC$ tells you the type:

|$B^2 - 4AC$|Shape|
|---|---|
|$< 0$|Ellipse|
|$= 0$|Parabola|
|$> 0$|Hyperbola|

When SVMs use a **polynomial kernel of degree 2**, $k(x, z) = (x \cdot z + c)^2$, the resulting decision boundary in input space is exactly a conic section. The **kernel trick** is geometrically a projection that straightens out these curved boundaries.

> The Mercer condition for kernel validity is essentially asking: is the kernel a valid inner product in some (possibly infinite-dimensional) feature space? For degree-2 polynomial kernels, the geometry is pure conic section theory.

---

### 2. Gaussian Distributions — Ellipses as Level Sets

A 2D Gaussian with covariance matrix $\Sigma$:

$$p(x) \propto \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}(x - \mu)^T \Sigma^{-1}(x - \mu)\right)$$

The level sets ${x : (x-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu) = c}$ are **ellipses** — their axes are the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$, and their radii are $\sqrt{c \lambda_i}$ where $\lambda_i$ are eigenvalues.

In **Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs)**, **Gaussian Processes**, and **Kalman filters**, understanding the geometry of these ellipsoidal level sets is essential. The polar form gives you the radial profile in any direction:

$$r(\theta) = \frac{c}{\sqrt{e_1^2 \cos^2\theta / \lambda_1 + e_2^2 \sin^2\theta / \lambda_2}}$$

which is precisely an ellipse in polar form.

---

### 3. Gradient Descent on Quadratic Loss — Elliptic Contours

The loss function of linear regression is:

$$\mathcal{L}(w) = |Xw - y|^2 = w^T(X^TX)w - 2y^T X w + |y|^2$$

The sublevel sets ${\mathcal{L}(w) \leq c}$ are **ellipsoids** (in high dimension) whose axes are the right singular vectors of $X$, with radii proportional to $1/\sigma_i$ (singular values).

**Why this matters for optimization:**

- The eccentricity of these ellipses determines how badly conditioned the problem is.
- A highly eccentric ellipse (thin, elongated) → **slow convergence** of gradient descent.
- This is why **preconditioning / adaptive methods** (Adam, RMSProp) try to make the loss landscape more "circular" (lower eccentricity).

> The **condition number** $\kappa = \sigma_{\max}/\sigma_{\min}$ of $X^TX$ is essentially the eccentricity of the loss ellipsoid. The convergence rate of gradient descent is $\left(\frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}\right)^k \to 0$, which degrades exactly as the ellipse becomes more elongated.

---

### 4. Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK) — Infinite-Width Limits

In the infinite-width limit of neural networks, training dynamics are governed by the **Neural Tangent Kernel** $K(x, x')$. For networks trained on structured data, the empirical NTK matrix has eigenvalue spectra whose geometric meaning in feature space connects to ellipsoidal geometry and conic sections.

This is an active research area (2019–present), but the key point is: **kernel methods, of which NTK theory is a limiting case, are fundamentally about inner products and conic-section geometry**.

---

### 5. Coordinate Systems in Robotics / RL — Polar Representations

In **reinforcement learning** for robotics, the state of a robotic arm or satellite is naturally expressed in polar or spherical coordinates. The **reachable set** of a robot — the set of positions it can reach — is often an ellipse or more general conic section in Cartesian space.

Polar-conic representations allow:

- Efficient parametrization of goal regions
- Natural formulation of Lyapunov stability conditions (ellipsoidal invariant sets)
- Geometric model predictive control

## Key Formulas to Memorize

$$r = \frac{ed}{1 \pm e\cos\theta} \qquad \text{or} \qquad r = \frac{ed}{1 \pm e\sin\theta}$$

$$\text{Semi-latus rectum: } \ell = ed = r\big|_{\theta = \pi/2}$$

$$\text{For ellipse: } a = \frac{ed}{1-e^2}, \quad b = \frac{ed}{\sqrt{1-e^2}}, \quad c = ae$$

$$\text{Eccentricity from } r_{\min}, r_{\max}: \quad e = \frac{r_{\max} - r_{\min}}{r_{\max} + r_{\min}}$$

## Practice Problems

> 1. Convert $r = \dfrac{4}{2 - \cos\theta}$ to standard form. Identify the conic, find $e$, $d$, and sketch.

> 2. Find the polar equation of the ellipse with eccentricity $1/3$ and directrix $y = -6$.

> 3. Show that the semi-latus rectum $\ell = ed$ equals the value of $r$ when $\theta = \pi/2$. Why is this geometrically natural?

> 4. A comet follows a parabolic orbit with the Sun at the focus. At perihelion (closest approach), $r = 0.5$ AU. Find the polar equation of its orbit. At what angle does $r = 1$ AU?

> 5. (**ML connection**) The level sets of a 2D Gaussian are ellipses. Express the level set ${(x_1, x_2) : x_1^2/4 + x_2^2/9 = 1}$ in polar form $r(\theta)$. What is the eccentricity?
