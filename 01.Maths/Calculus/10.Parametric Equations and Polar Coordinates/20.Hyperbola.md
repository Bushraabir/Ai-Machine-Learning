

> _The hyperbola is the ellipse's shadow — same two foci, same defining distance condition, but subtracted instead of summed. That single sign change tears the curve open into two infinite branches._

---

## 1. The Geometric Definition

> **Definition.** A _hyperbola_ is the set of all points $P$ in the plane such that the **absolute difference of distances** from $P$ to two fixed points $F_1$ and $F_2$ (the **foci**) is constant.

$$\text{Hyperbola} = {, P \in \mathbb{R}^2 : \big||PF_1| - |PF_2|\big| = 2a ,}$$

where $2a > 0$ is the constant difference.

**Compare with the ellipse:**

|Conic|Condition|Curve|
|---|---|---|
|Ellipse|$|PF_1|
|**Hyperbola**|$\big||

The two branches correspond to the two cases: $|PF_1| - |PF_2| = 2a$ (branch near $F_1$) and $|PF_2| - |PF_1| = 2a$ (branch near $F_2$).

```
        branch 1          branch 2
           |                 |
     *     |                 |     *
    * *    |                 |    * *
   *   *   |                 |   *   *
  *     *--F₁               F₂--*     *
   *   *   |                 |   *   *
    * *    |                 |    * *
     *     |                 |     *
           |                 |

  On branch 1: |PF₂| - |PF₁| = 2a
  On branch 2: |PF₁| - |PF₂| = 2a
```

**Three fundamental constants:**

|Symbol|Meaning|Constraint|
|---|---|---|
|$a$|semi-transverse axis|$a > 0$|
|$b$|semi-conjugate axis|$b > 0$|
|$c$|center-to-focus distance|$c > a$|

**The fundamental relation (note the sign flip from the ellipse!):**

$$\boxed{c^2 = a^2 + b^2}$$

_Proof._ Place $P$ at a vertex $(a, 0)$. Then $|PF_1| = c - a$ and $|PF_2| = c + a$, so the difference is $2a$. ✓  
Place $P$ far out along an asymptote — the relation $c^2 = a^2 + b^2$ is forced by requiring the asymptote slope to be $b/a$ (derived in §5). $\square$

## 2. Deriving the Equation

Place center at origin, foci at $F_1 = (-c, 0)$ and $F_2 = (c, 0)$. Take the branch with $|PF_2| - |PF_1| = 2a$:

$$\sqrt{(x+c)^2 + y^2} - \sqrt{(x-c)^2 + y^2} = 2a$$

Move one radical and square (same algebra as the ellipse derivation):

$$\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2} = 2a + \sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}$$

$$(x+c)^2+y^2 = 4a^2 + 4a\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2} + (x-c)^2+y^2$$

$$4cx - 4a^2 = 4a\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}$$

$$cx - a^2 = a\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}$$

Square again (requires $cx - a^2 \geq 0$, i.e., $x \geq a^2/c$, satisfied on this branch):

$$c^2x^2 - 2a^2cx + a^4 = a^2(x^2 - 2cx + c^2 + y^2)$$

$$(c^2 - a^2)x^2 - a^2y^2 = a^2(c^2 - a^2)$$

Since $b^2 = c^2 - a^2 > 0$:

$$b^2x^2 - a^2y^2 = a^2b^2$$

$$\boxed{\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1}$$

**Key observation:** The hyperbola equation differs from the ellipse's $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ only in the sign of the $y^2$ term. That minus sign is the entire geometric difference.

## 3. Standard Forms

### Horizontal transverse axis (opening left/right)

$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$$

- Center: $(0, 0)$
- Vertices: $(\pm a, 0)$
- Foci: $(\pm c, 0)$, $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$
- Asymptotes: $y = \pm\dfrac{b}{a}x$
- Transverse axis along $x$-axis

### Vertical transverse axis (opening up/down)

$$\frac{y^2}{a^2} - \frac{x^2}{b^2} = 1$$

- Center: $(0, 0)$
- Vertices: $(0, \pm a)$
- Foci: $(0, \pm c)$, $c = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$
- Asymptotes: $y = \pm\dfrac{a}{b}x$
- Transverse axis along $y$-axis

**Identification rule:** The positive term's variable tells you the direction the hyperbola opens.

### Shifted hyperbola (center at $(h, k)$)

$$\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1 \quad \text{(horizontal)}$$

$$\frac{(y-k)^2}{a^2} - \frac{(x-h)^2}{b^2} = 1 \quad \text{(vertical)}$$

## 4. Eccentricity

$$\boxed{e = \frac{c}{a} > 1}$$

$$c = ae, \qquad b^2 = a^2(e^2 - 1), \qquad b = a\sqrt{e^2 - 1}$$

|$e$|Shape|
|---|---|
|$e \to 1^+$|Very narrow, nearly parabolic branches|
|$e = \sqrt{2}$|Rectangular hyperbola ($a = b$, asymptotes at $90°$)|
|$e \to \infty$|Branches open very wide, nearly flat|

**Contrast with other conics:**

$$e < 1 \text{ (ellipse)}, \quad e = 1 \text{ (parabola)}, \quad e > 1 \text{ (hyperbola)}$$

**Focus-directrix property:** For each focus $F$ and its corresponding directrix $\ell$:

$$\frac{|PF|}{d(P, \ell)} = e > 1 \quad \text{for every } P \text{ on the hyperbola}$$

Directrices: $x = \pm\dfrac{a}{e} = \pm\dfrac{a^2}{c}$ (for horizontal hyperbola). Note these lie _between_ the two vertices — inside the hyperbola.

---

## 5. The Asymptotes — The Defining Feature

The asymptotes are the lines the branches approach but never touch. For $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} - \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$:

$$\boxed{y = \pm \frac{b}{a}x}$$

**Derivation.** Solve for $y$:

$$y = \pm \frac{b}{a}\sqrt{x^2 - a^2} = \pm \frac{b}{a}x\sqrt{1 - \frac{a^2}{x^2}}$$

As $x \to \infty$: $y \to \pm\frac{b}{a}x$. The gap between the curve and the asymptote:

$$\frac{b}{a}x - \frac{b}{a}\sqrt{x^2-a^2} = \frac{b}{a}\cdot\frac{a^2}{x + \sqrt{x^2-a^2}} \to 0$$

The approach is asymptotic but never reaches zero for finite $x$. $\square$

**Compact form:** The asymptotes are encoded in the equation

$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 0 \implies \left(\frac{x}{a} - \frac{y}{b}\right)\left(\frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b}\right) = 0$$

i.e., the asymptotes are what the hyperbola "degenerates to" when the right-hand side is $0$ instead of $1$.

**Angle between asymptotes:**

$$\tan\theta = \frac{2ab}{a^2 - b^2} \quad \text{(when } a \neq b\text{)}$$

For the **rectangular hyperbola** ($a = b$): asymptotes are perpendicular ($\theta = 90°$).

---

## 6. The Rectangular Hyperbola

When $a = b$, the asymptotes are perpendicular. Equation:

$$x^2 - y^2 = a^2$$

Rotating $45°$ (substituting $x = \frac{u+v}{\sqrt{2}}$, $y = \frac{u-v}{\sqrt{2}}$):

$$uv = \frac{a^2}{2} = k$$

The familiar form $xy = k$ is a **rectangular hyperbola with asymptotes along the coordinate axes**. Its eccentricity is $e = \sqrt{2}$.

This is the curve behind:

- Boyle's law: $PV = \text{const}$
- Supply-demand curves in economics
- Harmonic sequences

## 7. Key Geometric Properties

### 7.1 Latus Rectum

The chord through a focus perpendicular to the transverse axis. At $x = c$ in $\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$:

$$\frac{c^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1 \implies y^2 = b^2!\left(\frac{c^2}{a^2} - 1\right) = b^2 \cdot \frac{c^2 - a^2}{a^2} = \frac{b^4}{a^2}$$

$$\text{Length of latus rectum} = \frac{2b^2}{a}$$

Identical in form to the ellipse — the sign difference in $c^2 = a^2 \pm b^2$ is absorbed.

### 7.2 The Reflective Property

> **Theorem.** A ray directed toward one focus reflects off the hyperbola and appears to come from the other focus.

_Proof sketch._ At point $P$ on the hyperbola, the tangent line bisects the angle $\angle F_1PF_2$ internally (unlike the ellipse, where it bisects externally). By the reflection law, an incoming ray aimed at $F_2$ reflects as if it came from $F_1$. $\square$

**Application:** Cassegrain telescopes use a parabolic primary mirror and a hyperbolic secondary mirror. Light focused by the parabola toward its focus is intercepted by the hyperbola, which redirects it to the other hyperbolic focus, passing through a hole in the primary mirror.

```
  Ray aimed at F₂         reflects away from F₁

       →→→ F₂
      ↗
  ___/___
 /       \     ← hyperbola branch
  \     /
   \___/
         \
          F₁ (reflected ray diverges as if from F₁)
```

### 7.3 Tangent Line

At point $(x_0, y_0)$ on $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} - \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$:

$$\frac{x,x_0}{a^2} - \frac{y,y_0}{b^2} = 1$$

**Slope of tangent:**

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{b^2 x_0}{a^2 y_0}$$

**Tangent with slope $m$:**

$$y = mx \pm \sqrt{a^2m^2 - b^2} \quad \text{(requires } |m| > b/a\text{)}$$

_(Condition: $a^2m^2 - b^2 > 0$, i.e., slope steeper than the asymptotes.)_

## 8. Parametric Equations

Two equivalent parametrizations:

**Hyperbolic functions (natural):**

$$x = a\cosh t, \quad y = b\sinh t, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

_Right branch only._ Verify: $\frac{a^2\cosh^2 t}{a^2} - \frac{b^2\sinh^2 t}{b^2} = \cosh^2 t - \sinh^2 t = 1$. ✓

For left branch: $x = -a\cosh t$, $y = b\sinh t$.

**Trigonometric (both branches, $\sec$ parametrization):**

$$x = a\sec t, \quad y = b\tan t, \quad t \in \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right) \cup \left(\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{3\pi}{2}\right)$$

Verify: $\frac{a^2\sec^2 t}{a^2} - \frac{b^2\tan^2 t}{b^2} = \sec^2 t - \tan^2 t = 1$. ✓

---

## 9. Polar Form (Focus at Pole)

With one focus at the origin:

$$r = \frac{\ell}{1 - e\cos\theta}, \quad \ell = \frac{b^2}{a}, \quad e > 1$$

|$\theta$|$r$|
|---|---|
|$0$|$r = \dfrac{\ell}{1-e} < 0$ (other branch)|
|$\pi$|$r = \dfrac{\ell}{1+e} > 0$ (near branch vertex)|
|$\pi/2$|$r = \ell = b^2/a$|

All four orientations:

|Opening direction|Polar equation|
|---|---|
|Right|$r = \dfrac{\ell}{1 - e\cos\theta}$|
|Left|$r = \dfrac{\ell}{1 + e\cos\theta}$|
|Up|$r = \dfrac{\ell}{1 - e\sin\theta}$|
|Down|$r = \dfrac{\ell}{1 + e\sin\theta}$|

## 10. Area and Arc Length

**Area between the hyperbola and its transverse axis** (from $x = a$ to $x = x_1$):

$$A = \int_a^{x_1} \frac{b}{a}\sqrt{x^2 - a^2};dx = \frac{b}{a}\left[\frac{x\sqrt{x^2-a^2}}{2} - \frac{a^2}{2}\ln!\left|x + \sqrt{x^2-a^2}\right|\right]_a^{x_1}$$

**Arc length** of one branch from vertex to $(x_1, y_1)$:

$$L = \int_a^{x_1}\sqrt{1 + \frac{b^2x^2}{a^2(x^2-a^2)}},dx$$

(No closed form in general; expressed via elliptic integrals.)

**Sector area** (analogous to the $\frac{1}{2}r^2,d\theta$ formula): in the $(\cosh t, \sinh t)$ parametrization, the area of the sector swept from $t=0$ to $t=T$ is $\frac{1}{2}ab,T$ — the hyperbolic angle is literally the area.

---

## 11. Curvature

At parametric point $t$ (hyperbolic parametrization):

$$\kappa(t) = \frac{ab}{(a^2\sinh^2 t + b^2\cosh^2 t)^{3/2}}$$

At **vertex** ($t = 0$, $x = a$):

$$\kappa_{\max} = \frac{a}{b^2}, \quad \rho_{\min} = \frac{b^2}{a}$$

_(Same form as the ellipse vertex formula — and same as the parabola vertex formula $\rho = 2p$ with $p = b^2/(2a)$.)_

## 12. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Find Everything from the Equation

Given $\dfrac{x^2}{9} - \dfrac{y^2}{16} = 1$.

- $a^2 = 9 \Rightarrow a = 3$ (positive term → opens left/right)
- $b^2 = 16 \Rightarrow b = 4$
- $c = \sqrt{9 + 16} = \sqrt{25} = 5$
- $e = c/a = 5/3 \approx 1.667$
- Vertices: $(\pm 3, 0)$
- Foci: $(\pm 5, 0)$
- Asymptotes: $y = \pm\dfrac{4}{3}x$
- Latus rectum: $\dfrac{2(16)}{3} = \dfrac{32}{3}$

### Example 2: Find the Equation

Foci at $(0, \pm 13)$, vertices at $(0, \pm 5)$.

- Foci on $y$-axis → vertical transverse axis
- $a = 5$, $c = 13$
- $b^2 = c^2 - a^2 = 169 - 25 = 144$, so $b = 12$

$$\frac{y^2}{25} - \frac{x^2}{144} = 1$$

- Asymptotes: $y = \pm\dfrac{5}{12}x$

### Example 3: Shifted Hyperbola

$9x^2 - 4y^2 - 36x + 8y - 4 = 0$

Complete the square:

$9(x^2 - 4x) - 4(y^2 - 2y) = 4$

$9(x-2)^2 - 36 - 4(y-1)^2 + 4 = 4$

$9(x-2)^2 - 4(y-1)^2 = 36$

$$\frac{(x-2)^2}{4} - \frac{(y-1)^2}{9} = 1$$

Center $(2,1)$, $a = 2$, $b = 3$, $c = \sqrt{13}$, horizontal transverse axis.

Asymptotes: $y - 1 = \pm\dfrac{3}{2}(x - 2)$.

## 14. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 14.1 Hyperbolic Space and Hyperbolic Neural Networks

Euclidean space has flat geometry; **hyperbolic space** has constant negative curvature. Poincaré disk and hyperboloid models of hyperbolic space are built on the equation:

$$-t^2 + x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2 = -1$$

This is a hyperboloid — the $n$-dimensional generalization of the hyperbola. **Poincaré embeddings** (Nickel & Kiela, 2017) embed hierarchical data (trees, knowledge graphs, taxonomies) into hyperbolic space because hyperbolic geometry grows exponentially with radius, perfectly matching the exponential branching structure of trees.

The core geometry: in 2D hyperbolic space, circles of radius $r$ have circumference $2\pi\sinh r \approx \pi e^r$ — exponential growth vs the Euclidean $2\pi r$. This is why hyperbolic embeddings can represent large hierarchies with low distortion in few dimensions.

### 14.2 The Hyperboloid Model and Lorentz Embeddings

The hyperboloid model of $n$-dimensional hyperbolic space is the upper sheet of:

$$-x_0^2 + x_1^2 + \cdots + x_n^2 = -1, \quad x_0 > 0$$

This is a direct generalization of the hyperbola to $n+1$ dimensions, with the **Minkowski inner product** $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}\rangle_\mathcal{L} = -u_0v_0 + \sum_{i=1}^n u_iv_i$.

**Lorentz embeddings** use this space to embed relational data and have shown state-of-the-art performance on link prediction in knowledge graphs. The geodesic distance in this space:

$$d(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \text{arccosh}(-\langle\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v}\rangle_\mathcal{L})$$

is the "hyperbolic distance" — directly encoding the hyperbola's geometry into the similarity measure.

### 14.3 Hyperbolic Activation Functions

The **hyperbolic tangent**:

$$\tanh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}} = \frac{\sinh x}{\cosh x}$$

is the most ubiquitous activation function in recurrent networks (LSTMs, GRUs). Its shape — bounded in $(-1,1)$, odd, saturating — comes directly from the parametrization of the hyperbola $x^2 - y^2 = 1$: the point $(\cosh t, \sinh t)$ traces the right branch, and $\tanh t = y/x$ is the slope to that point.

The **sigmoid** $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ is a shifted, rescaled $\tanh$: $\tanh(x) = 2\sigma(2x) - 1$.

### 14.4 SVM Decision Boundaries and Hyperbolic Separation

In a **two-class SVM**, the margin boundary between the two support hyperplanes:

$$\mathbf{w}^\top\mathbf{x} + b = \pm 1$$

When data is not linearly separable and a **radial basis function (RBF) kernel** is used, the decision boundary in input space can be a **conic section** — including a hyperbola in 2D. The hyperbolic boundary arises when the two classes' centroids in feature space are separated in a way that produces a difference-of-distances condition, mirroring the hyperbola's definition exactly.

### 14.5 Contrastive Learning and Hyperbolic Distance

Modern **contrastive learning** (SimCLR, MoCo, CLIP) learns representations by pulling positive pairs together and pushing negative pairs apart. When the representation space is taken to be **hyperbolic** rather than Euclidean, the contrastive loss becomes:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\log\frac{\exp(-d_\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{z}_j)/\tau)}{\sum_k \exp(-d_\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{z}_i, \mathbf{z}_k)/\tau)}$$

where $d_\mathcal{H}$ is the hyperbolic distance. The level sets of $d_\mathcal{H}(\mathbf{z}, \mathbf{z}_0) = r$ are circles in the Poincaré disk model, but correspond to **horocycles** — curves asymptotically approaching the boundary, which are the hyperbolic analogs of the hyperbola's branches approaching their asymptotes.

### 14.6 Asymptotic Behavior and Generalization Bounds

In learning theory, **PAC-Bayes bounds** and **VC dimension** bounds take the form:

$$\text{Generalization error} \leq \text{Training error} + C\sqrt{\frac{d \log(n/d)}{n}}$$

The behavior of these bounds as $n \to \infty$ (fixed $d$) is asymptotically hyperbolic in the $(n, \text{error})$ plane: the bound curve approaches the $x$-axis asymptotically, never reaching zero — exactly like a hyperbola approaching its asymptote. The "price of complexity" $d$ controls the eccentricity of this hyperbolic decay.

## 15. Summary of Key Distinctions

|Feature|Ellipse|Hyperbola|
|---|---|---|
|Definition|$\|PF_1\| + \|PF_2\| = 2a$|$\big\|PF_1\| - \|PF_2\|\big\| = 2a$|
|Relation|$c^2 = a^2 - b^2$|$c^2 = a^2 + b^2$|
|Eccentricity|$0 < e < 1$|$e > 1$|
|Branches|1 (closed)|2 (open)|
|Asymptotes|None|$y = \pm\frac{b}{a}x$|
|Foci position|Inside curve|Outside curve, beyond vertices|
|Area|$\pi ab$|Infinite|
|Latus rectum|$2b^2/a$|$2b^2/a$|

## 16. Conic Sections — The Complete Picture

$$B^2 - 4AC \begin{cases} < 0 & \text{ellipse (or circle)} \ = 0 & \text{parabola} \ > 0 & \text{hyperbola} \end{cases}$$

All three arise from slicing a double cone with a plane — the hyperbola is the only conic that cuts **both nappes** of the cone.

```
        Cone (double-napped):

          /\        /\
         /  \      /  \
        / el \    / hyp\   ← plane cuts both nappes
       /______\  /______\
              \/
         (vertex)
              /\
             /  \
            /    \
```
