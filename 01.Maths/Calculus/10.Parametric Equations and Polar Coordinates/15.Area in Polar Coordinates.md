

> _A region bounded by a polar curve has a shape no Cartesian rectangle can cleanly tile. We need a new slicing strategy ŌĆö and the geometry of circular sectors gives it to us._

---

## 1. Why Cartesian Integration Breaks Down

In Cartesian coordinates, we integrate by slicing a region into thin **vertical rectangles** of width $dx$ and height $f(x)$. The key geometric fact exploited is:

$$\text{Area of a thin strip} \approx f(x),dx$$

A polar curve $r = f(\theta)$ sweeps out its region by **rotation**, not translation. The natural "thin piece" is not a rectangle ŌĆö it is a thin **circular sector** swept out by a small angular increment $d\theta$.

---

## 2. The Geometric Engine: Area of a Circular Sector

**Lemma.** A circular sector of radius $r$ subtending angle $\Delta\theta$ (in radians) has area

$$A_{\text{sector}} = \frac{1}{2}r^2,\Delta\theta.$$

_Proof._ A full disk of radius $r$ has area $\pi r^2$ and subtends $2\pi$ radians. By proportionality,

$$A_{\text{sector}} = \frac{\Delta\theta}{2\pi}\cdot\pi r^2 = \frac{1}{2}r^2,\Delta\theta. \qquad \square$$

This is the exact geometric fact that drives everything that follows.

---

## 3. Setting Up the Riemann Sum

Let $r = f(\theta)$ be a continuous, non-negative function on $[\alpha,\beta]$, where $0 < \beta - \alpha \leq 2\pi$. Consider the region $\mathcal{R}$ bounded by the curve and the two rays $\theta = \alpha$, $\theta = \beta$.

**Partition** $[\alpha, \beta]$: choose $\alpha = \theta_0 < \theta_1 < \cdots < \theta_n = \beta$, with $\Delta\theta_i = \theta_i - \theta_{i-1}$.

**Approximate** the $i$-th sub-region by a circular sector with:

- radius $= f(\theta_i^_)$ for some sample point $\theta_i^_ \in [\theta_{i-1}, \theta_i]$,
- angle $= \Delta\theta_i$.

The area of this approximating sector is $\tfrac{1}{2}[f(\theta_i^*)]^2,\Delta\theta_i$.

**Sum:** $\displaystyle A \approx \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{2}[f(\theta_i^*)]^2,\Delta\theta_i$

This is a Riemann sum for $\tfrac{1}{2}[f(\theta)]^2$ over $[\alpha, \beta]$.

---

## 4. The Main Theorem

**Theorem (Area in Polar Coordinates).** If $f$ is continuous and $f(\theta) \geq 0$ on $[\alpha, \beta]$, then the area of the region bounded by the polar curve $r = f(\theta)$ and the rays $\theta = \alpha$, $\theta = \beta$ is

$$\boxed{A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta [f(\theta)]^2,d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2,d\theta}$$

_Proof._ Since $f$ is continuous on a closed interval, $[f(\theta)]^2$ is also continuous, hence Riemann integrable. The Riemann sums above converge to the integral as $|\text{partition}| \to 0$. $\square$

**Remark.** The integrand is $\tfrac{1}{2}r^2$, not $r$ ŌĆö this is a quadratic functional in the radius, reflecting the fact that sectors grow in area quadratically with radius (just as disk area grows as $r^2$).

## 5. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Full Rose ŌĆö $r = \cos(2\theta)$ (Four-Petal Rose)

The curve $r = \cos(2\theta)$ traces four petals. One petal lies over $\theta \in [-\pi/4,, \pi/4]$ (where $r \geq 0$).

$$A_{\text{one petal}} = \frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\cos^2(2\theta),d\theta$$

Use $\cos^2(u) = \frac{1+\cos(2u)}{2}$:

$$= \frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{1+\cos(4\theta)}{2},d\theta = \frac{1}{4}\left[\theta + \frac{\sin(4\theta)}{4}\right]_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}$$

$$= \frac{1}{4}\left[\frac{\pi}{4} + 0 - \left(-\frac{\pi}{4} + 0\right)\right] = \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi}{8}$$

Total area of all four petals: $4 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{8} = \dfrac{\pi}{2}$.

---

### Example 2: Cardioid ŌĆö $r = 1 + \cos\theta$

The cardioid $r = 1 + \cos\theta$ traces a complete, closed curve as $\theta$ ranges over $[0, 2\pi]$.

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(1+\cos\theta)^2,d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta),d\theta$$

Over a full period: $\int_0^{2\pi}\cos\theta,d\theta = 0$ and $\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^2\theta,d\theta = \pi$.

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\left[2\pi + 0 + \pi\right] = \frac{3\pi}{2}$$

---

### Example 3: Area Between Two Curves

**Setup.** Given two polar curves $r = f(\theta)$ (outer) and $r = g(\theta)$ (inner), with $f(\theta) \geq g(\theta) \geq 0$ on $[\alpha, \beta]$, the area between them is:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \left([f(\theta)]^2 - [g(\theta)]^2\right)d\theta$$

**Example.** Find the area inside $r = 3\sin\theta$ and outside $r = 1 + \sin\theta$.

Find intersections: $3\sin\theta = 1 + \sin\theta \Rightarrow 2\sin\theta = 1 \Rightarrow \sin\theta = \tfrac{1}{2} \Rightarrow \theta = \tfrac{\pi}{6}, \tfrac{5\pi}{6}$.

On $[\pi/6, 5\pi/6]$, $3\sin\theta \geq 1 + \sin\theta$.

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[(3\sin\theta)^2 - (1+\sin\theta)^2\right]d\theta$$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[9\sin^2\theta - 1 - 2\sin\theta - \sin^2\theta\right]d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[8\sin^2\theta - 2\sin\theta - 1\right]d\theta$$

Using $8\sin^2\theta = 4(1-\cos 2\theta) = 4 - 4\cos 2\theta$:

$$= \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[3 - 4\cos 2\theta - 2\sin\theta\right]d\theta$$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\left[3\theta - 2\sin 2\theta + 2\cos\theta\right]_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6} = \pi$$

## 7. Common Pitfalls and Subtleties

**Pitfall 1: Curve traced multiple times.** The lemniscate $r^2 = \cos(2\theta)$ is only real when $\cos(2\theta) \geq 0$, i.e., $\theta \in [-\pi/4, \pi/4] \cup [3\pi/4, 5\pi/4]$. Integrating over $[0, 2\pi]$ naively would double-count the area. Always sketch the curve and identify the correct angular range for one complete tracing.

**Pitfall 2: Negative $r$ values.** If $f(\theta) < 0$ on part of $[\alpha, \beta]$, the formula still makes sense geometrically because $[f(\theta)]^2 \geq 0$. However, the curve is reflected through the pole. Be careful about which region you are actually computing.

**Pitfall 3: Finding intersection angles.** Polar intersections are subtle. Two curves can intersect at the pole with _different_ $\theta$ values (since both reach $r = 0$ at different angles). Always check whether the pole is an intersection separately.

## 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

Polar coordinates and the area formula appear ŌĆö sometimes overtly, often structurally ŌĆö in several corners of modern ML.

### 8.1 Radial Basis Functions (RBFs) and Kernel Methods

Support Vector Machines and kernel methods frequently use **radial kernels** of the form

$$K(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \phi(|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}|)$$

The _effective receptive area_ of such a kernel ŌĆö how much of feature space it "sees" ŌĆö is computed as an integral in polar/spherical coordinates. In 2D, this is exactly the polar area integral. For the Gaussian kernel $\phi(r) = e^{-r^2/(2\sigma^2)}$, the normalization constant requires:

$$\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2/(2\sigma^2)} r,dr$$

which emerges from writing $\int_{\mathbb{R}^2} K,dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2/(2\sigma^2)},r,dr,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2/(2\sigma^2)},r,dr$.

The factor of $r$ (the Jacobian) is the polar-coordinate remnant of the sector area element $r,dr,d\theta$ ŌĆö directly analogous to $\tfrac{1}{2}r^2,d\theta$ in our formula.

### 8.2 Polar Decomposition and Neural Network Geometry

The geometry of weight spaces in neural networks is often studied in polar/spherical coordinates. The unit hypersphere $\mathbb{S}^{n-1}$ is parameterized angularly, and integration over weight vectors decomposes as:

$$\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} f(\mathbf{w}),d\mathbf{w} = \int_0^\infty \int_{\mathbb{S}^{n-1}} f(r,\boldsymbol{\omega}),r^{n-1},dS(\boldsymbol{\omega}),dr$$

The angular integral over $\mathbb{S}^{n-1}$ is the higher-dimensional generalization of our $\int_\alpha^\beta r^2,d\theta$ formula. Understanding the geometry of parameter space ŌĆö how much "volume" of weight space corresponds to good solutions ŌĆö uses exactly this machinery.

### 8.3 Fourier Features and Directional Statistics

**Random Fourier Features** (Rahimi & Recht, 2007) approximate shift-invariant kernels by sampling frequencies $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ from a spectral distribution. The spectral density of a radial kernel is itself a radial function, and sampling from it efficiently uses the polar decomposition: sample angle $\theta \sim \text{Uniform}[0, 2\pi]$ independently from the radial component $r \sim p_r$.

**Directional statistics** (von Mises distribution, wrapped Cauchy) model data on circles and spheres. Computing normalizing constants and expected values under these distributions requires polar area integrals. This appears in:

- orientation estimation in computer vision,
- molecule conformation prediction (AlphaFold-style models),
- circular embeddings for temporal/angular data.

### 8.4 Activation Function Analysis and Angular Regions

In a two-layer ReLU network, the _decision boundary_ of a single neuron is a hyperplane, but the _activation region_ (where the neuron fires) is a half-space. In 2D, this corresponds to a wedge ŌĆö an angular sector ŌĆö and the probability that a random Gaussian input activates a neuron is:

$$P(\text{neuron fires}) = \frac{\Delta\theta}{2\pi}$$

where $\Delta\theta$ is the angular measure of the sector. Computing the expected number of active neurons, or the measure of the activation region, reduces to polar area calculations.

### 8.5 Diffusion Models and Score Functions

Modern generative models (score-based diffusion models) define a noising process on $\mathbb{R}^n$. The **score function** $\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p_t(\mathbf{x})$ points radially inward toward regions of high probability. The normalization of the forward process uses Gaussian integrals in $n$-dimensional polar coordinates. Specifically,

$$\int_{\mathbb{R}^n} |\mathbf{x}|^2,e^{-|\mathbf{x}|^2/(2\sigma^2)},d\mathbf{x} = n\sigma^2(2\pi\sigma^2)^{n/2}$$

derived by switching to polar coordinates ŌĆö the 2D version of this is our $\int r^2,d\theta$ formula elevated to $n$ dimensions.

## 10. Key Formulas at a Glance

$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2,d\theta$$

$$\text{Area between curves} = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \left(r_{\text{outer}}^2 - r_{\text{inner}}^2\right)d\theta$$

$$\text{Arc length} = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2},d\theta \quad \text{(for reference)}$$
