## 0. Preliminary Remarks

Every curve you have ever drawn — a circle, a parabola, a spiral, a cycloid — can be described in two fundamentally different ways: as a **relation** between $x$ and $y$ (implicit or explicit), or as the **image of a path** traced by a moving point. The second description is parametric, and it is strictly more powerful.

Parametric equations remember **direction**, **speed**, and **how many times** a curve is traversed. They are the natural language for kinematics, differential geometry, computer graphics, and — as we shall see — the geometry of neural network training.

This note builds the full theory: definitions → calculus (tangents, arc length, area) → curvature → classical curves → connection to ML.

---

## 1. Definitions

> **Definition 1.1 (Parametric Curve).** A _parametric curve_ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a continuous map $$\gamma: I \to \mathbb{R}^2, \quad t \mapsto (x(t),, y(t)),$$ where $I \subseteq \mathbb{R}$ is an interval (open, closed, or all of $\mathbb{R}$), and $x, y: I \to \mathbb{R}$ are continuous functions called the _coordinate functions_ or _parametric equations_.

The variable $t$ is the **parameter**. It often represents time, but it need not — it is simply a bookkeeping variable for tracing the curve.

> **Definition 1.2 (Smooth Curve).** A parametric curve $\gamma$ is _smooth_ on $I$ if $x$ and $y$ are differentiable and the velocity vector $$\dot{\gamma}(t) = (x'(t),, y'(t)) \neq (0, 0) \quad \text{for all } t \in I.$$ The condition $\dot{\gamma}(t) \neq \mathbf{0}$ rules out **cusps** (points where the curve momentarily stops and reverses).

> **Definition 1.3 (Simple and Closed Curves).**
> 
> - $\gamma$ is _simple_ if it does not cross itself: $\gamma(t_1) = \gamma(t_2) \Rightarrow t_1 = t_2$ (except possibly at endpoints).
> - $\gamma$ is _closed_ if $\gamma(a) = \gamma(b)$ for a compact interval $[a,b]$.
> - A simple closed curve is a _Jordan curve_.

> **Jordan Curve Theorem** _(stated without proof)._ Every Jordan curve in $\mathbb{R}^2$ divides the plane into exactly two regions — a bounded interior and an unbounded exterior.

## 2. Eliminating the Parameter

Given $(x(t), y(t))$, one can sometimes solve for $t$ from one equation and substitute into the other, obtaining an **implicit** or **explicit** Cartesian equation $F(x,y) = 0$.

> **Warning.** Elimination loses information: it discards direction, speed, and multiplicity. Two parametrizations can give the same Cartesian curve.

### Examples

**Example 2.1.** $x = t^2,\ y = t^3$, $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

From the first: $t = x^{1/2}$ (for $t \geq 0$) or $t = -x^{1/2}$ (for $t \leq 0$). Substituting into $y = t^3$: $$y^2 = t^6 = (t^2)^3 = x^3 \implies y^2 = x^3.$$

This is a **cuspidal cubic** (semicubical parabola). At $t = 0$: $(x,y) = (0,0)$, and $\dot{\gamma}(0) = (0, 0)$ — a cusp, which the Cartesian equation displays as a singularity ($y^2 = x^3$ is not smooth at the origin).

**Example 2.2.** $x = \cos t,\ y = \sin t$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$.

$x^2 + y^2 = \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t = 1$. The unit circle. The parametrization traverses it **counterclockwise**, once.

**Example 2.3.** $x = \cos 2t,\ y = \sin 2t$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$.

Same Cartesian curve $x^2 + y^2 = 1$, but traversed **twice** as fast, **twice** in total. Elimination cannot see this.

**Example 2.4** _(no elimination possible)._ $x = t - \sin t,\ y = 1 - \cos t$ (the **cycloid**). There is no closed-form Cartesian equation. Parametric is the only natural description.

## 3. Calculus of Parametric Curves

### 3.1 Tangent Lines and $dy/dx$

> **Theorem 3.1.** If $\gamma(t) = (x(t), y(t))$ is smooth at $t = t_0$ and $x'(t_0) \neq 0$, then the slope of the tangent line to $\gamma$ at $t_0$ is: $$\frac{dy}{dx}\Bigg|_{t=t_0} = \frac{y'(t_0)}{x'(t_0)}.$$

**Proof.** By the chain rule, if $y$ is a differentiable function of $x$ (guaranteed locally by the Inverse Function Theorem when $x'(t_0) \neq 0$): $$\frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt} = \frac{y'(t)}{x'(t)}. \quad \blacksquare$$

> **Theorem 3.2 (Second Derivative).** The second derivative of $y$ with respect to $x$ along the parametric curve is: $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{\frac{d}{dt}!\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)}{x'(t)} = \frac{\frac{d}{dt}!\left(\frac{y'}{x'}\right)}{x'(t)}.$$

**Proof.** Apply Theorem 3.1 with $dy/dx$ in place of $y$: $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{d}{dx}!\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = \frac{\frac{d}{dt}!\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)}{\frac{dx}{dt}}. \quad \blacksquare$$

**Example.** For $x = t^2,\ y = t^3$: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3t^2}{2t} = \frac{3t}{2}, \quad \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{3/2}{2t} = \frac{3}{4t}.$$

At $t = 1$: slope $= 3/2$, concave up ($d^2y/dx^2 = 3/4 > 0$).

### 3.2 Horizontal and Vertical Tangents

|Condition|Tangent|
|---|---|
|$y'(t_0) = 0$, $x'(t_0) \neq 0$|**Horizontal** tangent at $\gamma(t_0)$|
|$x'(t_0) = 0$, $y'(t_0) \neq 0$|**Vertical** tangent at $\gamma(t_0)$|
|$x'(t_0) = y'(t_0) = 0$|Need higher-order analysis (possible cusp)|

**Example.** Cycloid $x = t - \sin t$, $y = 1 - \cos t$: $$x'(t) = 1 - \cos t, \quad y'(t) = \sin t.$$

Horizontal tangents: $\sin t = 0$ and $1 - \cos t \neq 0 \Rightarrow t = \pi + 2k\pi$. At these $t$: $(x,y) = (\pi + 2k\pi, 2)$ — tops of the arches.

Vertical tangents (cusps): $1 - \cos t = 0 \Rightarrow t = 2k\pi$, and $\sin(2k\pi) = 0$ also. Both vanish — these are cusps at $t = 2k\pi$, corresponding to $(x,y) = (2k\pi, 0)$ — the touching points on the ground.

### 3.3 Arc Length

> **Theorem 3.3 (Arc Length).** If $\gamma: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}^2$ is smooth, the arc length of $\gamma$ is: $$L = \int_a^b |\dot{\gamma}(t)|,dt = \int_a^b \sqrt{x'(t)^2 + y'(t)^2},dt.$$

**Proof.** Partition $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals with $\Delta t = (b-a)/n$. The polygonal approximation has length: $$L_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \sqrt{(\Delta x_k)^2 + (\Delta y_k)^2} = \sum_{k=1}^n \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Delta x_k}{\Delta t}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\Delta y_k}{\Delta t}\right)^2},\Delta t.$$

By the Mean Value Theorem, $\Delta x_k = x'(\xi_k)\Delta t$ and $\Delta y_k = y'(\eta_k)\Delta t$ for some $\xi_k, \eta_k$ in the $k$-th subinterval. As $n \to \infty$ (and since $x', y'$ are continuous), $L_n \to \int_a^b \sqrt{x'(t)^2 + y'(t)^2},dt$. $\blacksquare$

**Example — Circumference of a circle.** $x = r\cos t$, $y = r\sin t$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$: $$L = \int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt{r^2\sin^2 t + r^2\cos^2 t},dt = \int_0^{2\pi} r,dt = 2\pi r. \quad \checkmark$$

**Example — Length of one cycloid arch.** $x = t - \sin t$, $y = 1 - \cos t$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$: $$x'(t)^2 + y'(t)^2 = (1-\cos t)^2 + \sin^2 t = 1 - 2\cos t + \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t = 2 - 2\cos t = 4\sin^2\frac{t}{2}.$$ $$L = \int_0^{2\pi} 2\left|\sin\frac{t}{2}\right|dt = 2\int_0^{2\pi}\sin\frac{t}{2},dt = 2\left[-2\cos\frac{t}{2}\right]_0^{2\pi} = 2(2+2) = 8.$$

One cycloid arch has length $8r$ (here $r=1$) — exactly **8 times the radius**. A beautiful result.

### 3.4 Area Under a Parametric Curve

> **Theorem 3.4 (Area).** If $\gamma: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}^2$ traces a curve from left to right (i.e., $x'(t) > 0$), the area under the curve and above the $x$-axis is: $$A = \int_\alpha^\beta y,dx = \int_a^b y(t),x'(t),dt.$$

**Proof.** Standard substitution $x = x(t)$, $dx = x'(t),dt$. $\blacksquare$

**Example — Area enclosed by a cycloid arch and the $x$-axis:** $$A = \int_0^{2\pi} (1 - \cos t)(1 - \cos t),dt = \int_0^{2\pi}(1 - \cos t)^2,dt.$$ $$= \int_0^{2\pi}\left(1 - 2\cos t + \cos^2 t\right)dt = 2\pi - 0 + \pi = 3\pi.$$

The cycloid arch encloses exactly $3\pi$ — three times the area of the rolling circle ($\pi r^2 = \pi$).

## 4. Curvature

> **Definition 4.1 (Curvature).** The _curvature_ of a smooth curve $\gamma$ at $t$ is: $$\kappa(t) = \frac{|x'y'' - y'x''|}{(x'^2 + y'^2)^{3/2}},$$ where primes denote $d/dt$.

**Derivation.** Curvature measures the rate of change of the unit tangent vector $\mathbf{T} = \dot{\gamma}/|\dot{\gamma}|$ with respect to arc length $s$: $\kappa = |d\mathbf{T}/ds|$. One computes $d\mathbf{T}/ds = (d\mathbf{T}/dt)/(ds/dt)$ and takes the norm, arriving at the formula above.

**Geometric meaning:**

- $\kappa = 0$: straight line (no bending)
- $\kappa = 1/r$: circle of radius $r$ (constant curvature)
- Large $\kappa$: sharp bend; small $\kappa$: gentle curve

**Radius of curvature:** $R = 1/\kappa$ — the radius of the **osculating circle** (the best-fit circle at that point).

**Example — Curvature of $x = t^2,\ y = t^3$:** $$x' = 2t,\ y' = 3t^2,\ x'' = 2,\ y'' = 6t.$$ $$\kappa = \frac{|2t\cdot 6t - 3t^2\cdot 2|}{(4t^2 + 9t^4)^{3/2}} = \frac{|12t^2 - 6t^2|}{t^3(4 + 9t^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{6t^2}{t^3(4+9t^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{6}{t(4+9t^2)^{3/2}}.$$

As $t \to 0^+$, $\kappa \to \infty$ — infinite curvature at the cusp. $\checkmark$

## 5. Classical Curves

### 5.1 The Cycloid

Rolling circle of radius $r$: the path traced by a point on the rim.

$$x = r(t - \sin t), \qquad y = r(1 - \cos t), \quad t \in \mathbb{R}.$$

**Tautochrone property:** A bead slides to the bottom of a cycloid arch in the **same time** regardless of starting position (Huygens, 1659). The period is $T = \pi\sqrt{r/g}$ — independent of amplitude.

**Brachistochrone property:** The cycloid is the curve of **fastest descent** under gravity between two points (Johann Bernoulli, 1696) — the first variational problem in history.

$$x = A\sin(\omega_x t + \delta), \qquad y = B\sin(\omega_y t).$$

The shape depends on the **frequency ratio** $\omega_x/\omega_y$:

### 5.3 Epicycloids and Hypocycloids

A circle of radius $r$ rolling **outside** (epicycloid) or **inside** (hypocycloid) a circle of radius $R$:

**Epicycloid:** $$x = (R+r)\cos t - r\cos!\left(\frac{R+r}{r}t\right), \quad y = (R+r)\sin t - r\sin!\left(\frac{R+r}{r}t\right).$$

**Hypocycloid** (rolling inside): $$x = (R-r)\cos t + r\cos!\left(\frac{R-r}{r}t\right), \quad y = (R-r)\sin t - r\sin!\left(\frac{R-r}{r}t\right).$$

Special case $R = 4r$ (hypocycloid): the **astroid** $x = R\cos^3 t$, $y = R\sin^3 t$, whose Cartesian equation is $x^{2/3} + y^{2/3} = R^{2/3}$.

## 6. Parametric Curves in $\mathbb{R}^n$

The definition extends immediately:

> **Definition 6.1.** A _parametric curve_ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a map $\gamma: I \to \mathbb{R}^n$, $t \mapsto (x_1(t), \ldots, x_n(t))$.

All the calculus of §3 generalizes. The arc length formula becomes: $$L = \int_a^b |\dot{\gamma}(t)|,dt = \int_a^b \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n x_i'(t)^2},dt.$$

This is the setting for **curves in parameter space** — the key bridge to machine learning.

---

## 7. Reparametrization and Arc-Length Parametrization

> **Definition 7.1 (Reparametrization).** Given $\gamma: I \to \mathbb{R}^2$ and a diffeomorphism $\phi: J \to I$ (bijective, smooth, with smooth inverse), the curve $\tilde{\gamma} = \gamma \circ \phi: J \to \mathbb{R}^2$ is a _reparametrization_ of $\gamma$.

Reparametrizations trace the same image at different speeds. The **arc-length parametrization** is the canonical one:

> **Definition 7.2.** The _arc-length parameter_ is $s(t) = \int_{t_0}^t |\dot{\gamma}(\tau)|,d\tau$. The curve reparametrized by $s$ satisfies $|\gamma'(s)| = 1$ — unit speed.

**Why it matters:** In the arc-length parametrization, curvature takes the simplest form $\kappa = |\gamma''(s)|$, and the Frenet–Serret formulas hold. It is the "natural" parametrization intrinsic to the curve.

## 8. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 8.1 Training Trajectories as Parametric Curves

During gradient descent, the model parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^n$ evolve in time. This is a **parametric curve in parameter space**: $$t \mapsto \boldsymbol{\theta}(t) = (\theta_1(t), \theta_2(t), \ldots, \theta_n(t)).$$

For a neural network with $n = 10^9$ parameters, this is a curve in a billion-dimensional space. Every concept from §3 and §6 applies:

- **Velocity** $\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(t) = -\eta,\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t))$: gradient descent is literally the derivative of this curve.
- **Arc length** of the training trajectory: measures total parameter movement. Regularization (weight decay, gradient clipping) controls this arc length.
- **Curvature** of the loss landscape along $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$: related to the Hessian $\nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$, governs convergence speed.

### 8.2 Loss Landscape Curves and the Saddle Point Problem

The function $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ defines a **surface** in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$. A gradient descent trajectory $t \mapsto (\boldsymbol{\theta}(t), \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)))$ is a **parametric curve on this surface**. The curvature of this curve at a saddle point is related to the eigenvalues of the Hessian — exactly the Jacobian/linearization analysis from [[Predator–Prey System]] §3.

### 8.3 Bezier Curves — Parametric Curves in Computer Graphics and ML

> **Definition 8.1 (Bézier Curve).** A _Bézier curve_ of degree $n$ with control points $\mathbf{P}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_n \in \mathbb{R}^2$ is: $$\gamma(t) = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}(1-t)^{n-k}t^k,\mathbf{P}_k, \quad t \in [0,1].$$

This is a **parametric curve**: $t \mapsto (x(t), y(t))$ where $x$ and $y$ are Bernstein polynomials in $t$.

**In ML:** Bézier curves model smooth interpolation between checkpoints in training (learning rate schedules as Bézier curves), trajectory planning in robotics, and font design (TrueType fonts use quadratic Bézier curves).

**Cubic Bézier example:** $\mathbf{P}_0 = (0,0)$, $\mathbf{P}_1 = (1,2)$, $\mathbf{P}_2 = (2,2)$, $\mathbf{P}_3 = (3,0)$: $$\gamma(t) = (1-t)^3(0,0) + 3(1-t)^2 t(1,2) + 3(1-t)t^2(2,2) + t^3(3,0).$$ $$x(t) = 3t(1-t)^2\cdot 1 + 3(1-t)t^2\cdot 2 + t^3\cdot 3 = 3t, \quad y(t) = 6t(1-t)^2 + 6(1-t)t^2 = 6t(1-t).$$

### 8.4 Neural ODEs — Parametric Curves as Hidden State Trajectories

A **Neural ODE** (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2018) defines the hidden state $\mathbf{h}(t)$ as a parametric curve: $$\frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}(t), t), \qquad \mathbf{h}(t_1) = \mathbf{h}(t_0) + \int_{t_0}^{t_1} f_\theta(\mathbf{h}(t), t),dt.$$

The **arc length** of this curve $\int_{t_0}^{t_1}|f_\theta|,dt$ measures the total computational "work" done by the network. Minimizing it is a form of **regularization by path length**.

The **curvature** of $\mathbf{h}(t)$ determines how much the trajectory bends — high curvature signals rapid feature transformation at that layer-depth.

### 8.5 Generative Models — Latent Space Curves

In a **Variational Autoencoder (VAE)** or diffusion model, the latent space $\mathcal{Z} \subset \mathbb{R}^k$ is a continuous manifold. **Interpolation** between two latent codes $\mathbf{z}_0$ and $\mathbf{z}_1$ is a parametric curve:

$$\gamma: [0,1] \to \mathcal{Z}, \quad \gamma(t) = (1-t)\mathbf{z}_0 + t\mathbf{z}_1 \quad \text{(linear — bad)}.$$

Linear interpolation in latent space is naive. **Geodesic interpolation** finds the shortest curve $\gamma$ on the data manifold (minimizing arc length with respect to the metric induced by the decoder). This is the **arc-length minimization problem** — a direct application of §3.3.

The decoded images $\text{Decoder}(\gamma(t))$ then interpolate **smoothly along the data manifold** rather than cutting through empty space.

### 8.6 Reparametrization Trick in VAEs

The **reparametrization trick** is literally Definition 7.1:

$$\mathbf{z} = \boldsymbol{\mu} + \boldsymbol{\sigma} \odot \boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I).$$

One reparametrizes the random variable $\mathbf{z}$ (a point on a stochastic curve) so that the randomness flows through $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ rather than $\mathbf{z}$ itself. This makes the map $(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \boldsymbol{\sigma}) \mapsto \mathbf{z}$ differentiable — enabling backpropagation through sampling.

This is a **reparametrization** in the exact sense of Definition 7.1: same point, different parameter. The Jacobian of the reparametrization is what backprop computes.

## 10. Things to Prove Yourself

1. **Prove** the arc length formula (Theorem 3.3) rigorously using the MVT and the definition of the Riemann integral.
2. **Compute** the curvature of the unit circle $x = \cos t$, $y = \sin t$ using the formula in Definition 4.1. Verify $\kappa = 1$ everywhere.
3. **Show** that the astroid $x = R\cos^3 t$, $y = R\sin^3 t$ satisfies the Cartesian equation $x^{2/3} + y^{2/3} = R^{2/3}$. Find all four cusps and verify $\dot{\gamma} = \mathbf{0}$ at each.
4. **Prove** that the area enclosed by a **closed** parametric curve $\gamma: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}^2$ (traversed counterclockwise) is: $$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_a^b (x,y' - y,x'),dt.$$ (Hint: Green's Theorem with $P = -y/2$, $Q = x/2$.)
5. **Derive** the curvature formula $\kappa = |x'y'' - y'x''|/(x'^2+y'^2)^{3/2}$ from $\kappa = |d\mathbf{T}/ds|$ where $\mathbf{T} = \dot{\gamma}/|\dot{\gamma}|$.
6. **Compute** the arc length of one arch of the cycloid and verify $L = 8r$. (The key identity is $(1-\cos t)^2 + \sin^2 t = 2(1-\cos t)$.)
7. **For the Bézier curve** in §8.3, verify that $\gamma(0) = \mathbf{P}_0$ and $\gamma(1) = \mathbf{P}_n$ (endpoints are interpolated). Show that $\gamma'(0)$ is parallel to $\mathbf{P}_1 - \mathbf{P}_0$ (tangent at start points toward first control point).
