## §0 — Historical Prelude

**Pierre Bézier** (Renault, 1962) and **Paul de Casteljau** (Citroën, 1959) independently discovered these curves while designing automobile body panels — a problem where you need smooth, controllable curves that an engineer can adjust by pulling on "handles."

De Casteljau's algorithm came first but was classified by Citroën. Bézier published, got his name on the curves. The algorithm remains de Casteljau's.

The setting: we want a curve in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (or $\mathbb{R}^n$) that:

1. Passes through two endpoints (interpolates boundary data)
2. Is "attracted to" intermediate **control points** (approximates, not interpolates)
3. Is smooth (infinitely differentiable, in fact polynomial)
4. Is easy to compute, subdivide, and reason about geometrically

---

## §1 — Linear Bézier: The Foundation

The simplest case: two control points $\mathbf{P}_0, \mathbf{P}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^2$.

$$ \mathbf{B}(t) = (1-t),\mathbf{P}_0 + t,\mathbf{P}_1, \quad t \in [0,1] $$

This is just **linear interpolation** (lerp). As $t$ goes from $0$ to $1$:

- $t=0$: $\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{P}_0$
- $t=1$: $\mathbf{B} = \mathbf{P}_1$
- $t=1/2$: $\mathbf{B} = \frac{\mathbf{P}_0 + \mathbf{P}_1}{2}$ (midpoint)

The curve is a straight line segment. This is the atomic building block of everything that follows.

**Notation:** Write $\text{lerp}(\mathbf{P}, \mathbf{Q}, t) = (1-t)\mathbf{P} + t\mathbf{Q}$.

## §2 — Quadratic Bézier: Three Control Points

Three points $\mathbf{P}_0, \mathbf{P}_1, \mathbf{P}_2$.

### 2.1 de Casteljau's Construction

At parameter $t$:

**Step 1** — Lerp between adjacent pairs: $$ \mathbf{Q}_0(t) = (1-t)\mathbf{P}_0 + t\mathbf{P}_1, \qquad \mathbf{Q}_1(t) = (1-t)\mathbf{P}_1 + t\mathbf{P}_2 $$

**Step 2** — Lerp between the results: $$ \mathbf{B}(t) = (1-t)\mathbf{Q}_0(t) + t\mathbf{Q}_1(t) $$

### 2.2 Explicit Formula

Substitute Step 1 into Step 2: $$ \mathbf{B}(t) = (1-t)^2\mathbf{P}_0 + 2t(1-t)\mathbf{P}_1 + t^2\mathbf{P}_2 $$

Recognize: these are the **binomial coefficients** $\binom{2}{0}, \binom{2}{1}, \binom{2}{2}$ weighted by $(1-t)^{2-k}t^k$.

```
de Casteljau at t = 0.5:

P0 -----Q0----- P1
         \
          B(t)       ← the curve point
         /
P1 -----Q1----- P2
```

## §3 — Cubic Bézier: Four Control Points

The workhorse of computer graphics. Four points $\mathbf{P}_0, \mathbf{P}_1, \mathbf{P}_2, \mathbf{P}_3$.

### 3.1 de Casteljau's Algorithm

**Level 1** (3 points): $$ \mathbf{Q}_i(t) = (1-t)\mathbf{P}_i + t\mathbf{P}_{i+1}, \quad i = 0,1,2 $$

**Level 2** (2 points): $$ \mathbf{R}_i(t) = (1-t)\mathbf{Q}_i(t) + t\mathbf{Q}_{i+1}(t), \quad i = 0,1 $$

**Level 3** (1 point — the curve): $$ \mathbf{B}(t) = (1-t)\mathbf{R}_0(t) + t\mathbf{R}_1(t) $$

### 3.2 Explicit Formula

$$ \boxed{\mathbf{B}(t) = (1-t)^3\mathbf{P}_0 + 3t(1-t)^2\mathbf{P}_1 + 3t^2(1-t)\mathbf{P}_2 + t^3\mathbf{P}_3} $$

### 3.3 Matrix Form

Write $\mathbf{B}(t) = \mathbf{T},M_{\text{Bézier}},\mathbf{P}$:

$$ \mathbf{B}(t) = \begin{bmatrix}1 & t & t^2 & t^3\end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \ -3 & 3 & 0 & 0 \ 3 & -6 & 3 & 0 \ -1 & 3 & -3 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix}\mathbf{P}_0 \ \mathbf{P}_1 \ \mathbf{P}_2 \ \mathbf{P}_3\end{bmatrix} $$

This matrix form is used in GPU shader programs for fast evaluation.

## §4 — The General Degree-$n$ Bézier Curve

### 4.1 Bernstein Polynomials

Define the **Bernstein basis polynomials** of degree $n$:

$$ B_{k,n}(t) = \binom{n}{k}t^k(1-t)^{n-k}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n $$

**Key properties:**

- $B_{k,n}(t) \geq 0$ for $t \in [0,1]$ → **non-negativity**
- $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^n B_{k,n}(t) = 1$ for all $t$ → **partition of unity** (follows from the Binomial Theorem: $[(1-t)+t]^n = 1$)
- $B_{k,n}(t)$ peaks near $t = k/n$ → **localization**

### 4.2 The Bézier Curve

Given control points $\mathbf{P}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$:

$$ \boxed{\mathbf{B}(t) = \sum_{k=0}^n B_{k,n}(t),\mathbf{P}_k = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}(1-t)^{n-k}t^k,\mathbf{P}_k, \quad t \in [0,1]} $$

This is a **weighted average** of the control points — the weights $B_{k,n}(t)$ sum to 1 (partition of unity) and are all non-negative. The curve is always inside the **convex hull** of the control points.

## §5 — Fundamental Properties (with Proofs)

### 5.1 Endpoint Interpolation

**Claim:** $\mathbf{B}(0) = \mathbf{P}_0$ and $\mathbf{B}(1) = \mathbf{P}_n$.

**Proof.** At $t=0$: $B_{k,n}(0) = \binom{n}{k}0^k\cdot 1^{n-k} = 0$ for $k \geq 1$, and $B_{0,n}(0) = 1$. So $\mathbf{B}(0) = \mathbf{P}_0$. Similarly at $t=1$. $\square$

### 5.2 Convex Hull Property

**Claim:** $\mathbf{B}(t)$ lies in the convex hull of ${\mathbf{P}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_n}$ for all $t \in [0,1]$.

**Proof.** The $B_{k,n}(t)$ are non-negative and sum to 1, so $\mathbf{B}(t)$ is a convex combination of the control points. $\square$

> **Consequence.** This is the fundamental **containment guarantee** — the curve can never escape the convex hull of its control points. Essential for collision detection in graphics.

### 5.3 Tangent at Endpoints

**Claim:** $\mathbf{B}'(0) = n(\mathbf{P}_1 - \mathbf{P}_0)$ and $\mathbf{B}'(1) = n(\mathbf{P}_n - \mathbf{P}_{n-1})$.

**Proof (for $t=0$).** Differentiate $B_{k,n}(t) = \binom{n}{k}t^k(1-t)^{n-k}$: $$ B_{k,n}'(t) = \binom{n}{k}\left[kt^{k-1}(1-t)^{n-k} - (n-k)t^k(1-t)^{n-k-1}\right] $$ At $t=0$: only the $k=1$ term survives (all $k \geq 2$ terms have $t^{k-1}$ with $k-1\geq 1$, so vanish; the $k=0$ term has $-n(1-t)^{n-1}$ which at $t=0$ gives $-n$): $$ B_{0,n}'(0) = -n,\quad B_{1,n}'(0) = n, \quad B_{k,n}'(0) = 0 \text{ for } k \geq 2 $$ Therefore: $$ \mathbf{B}'(0) = -n\mathbf{P}_0 + n\mathbf{P}_1 = n(\mathbf{P}_1 - \mathbf{P}_0). \quad \square $$

**Geometric meaning:** The tangent at $\mathbf{P}_0$ points directly toward $\mathbf{P}_1$. Moving $\mathbf{P}_1$ steers the curve's initial direction.

### 5.4 Affine Invariance

**Claim:** Applying any affine map $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}$ to the control points gives the transformed curve: $\phi(\mathbf{B}(t)) = \text{Bézier}(\phi(\mathbf{P}_0), \ldots, \phi(\mathbf{P}_n))(t)$.

**Proof.** Affine maps commute with convex combinations (partition of unity): $$ \phi(\mathbf{B}(t)) = \phi!\left(\sum_k B_{k,n}(t)\mathbf{P}_k\right) = A\sum_k B_{k,n}(t)\mathbf{P}_k + \mathbf{b} = \sum_k B_{k,n}(t)(A\mathbf{P}_k + \mathbf{b}) = \sum_k B_{k,n}(t)\phi(\mathbf{P}_k). \quad \square $$

> **Consequence.** To rotate/translate/scale a Bézier curve, just transform the control points. You don't need to re-evaluate the curve. This is why Bézier curves dominate computer graphics.

### 5.5 Variation Diminishing Property

A Bézier curve crosses any line **no more times** than its control polygon does. Intuitively: the curve is "smoother" than the jagged polygon.

## §6 — de Casteljau's Algorithm: The Full Picture

For degree $n$ with control points $\mathbf{P}_0^{(0)}, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_n^{(0)}$, define the **triangular array**:

$$ \mathbf{P}_i^{(r)}(t) = (1-t),\mathbf{P}_i^{(r-1)} + t,\mathbf{P}_{i+1}^{(r-1)}, \quad r = 1, \ldots, n,\quad i = 0, \ldots, n-r $$

The curve point at parameter $t$ is: $$ \mathbf{B}(t) = \mathbf{P}_0^{(n)}(t) $$

For cubic ($n=3$), the full triangle:

```
Level 0 (given):   P0    P1    P2    P3
                     \   / \   / \   /
Level 1:            Q0    Q1    Q2
                      \   / \   /
Level 2:              R0    R1
                        \   /
Level 3 (curve pt):      B(t)
```

Each step is a lerp. The algorithm uses $\frac{n(n+1)}{2}$ lerp operations total.

### 6.1 Subdivision Property ⭐

**The most important algorithmic property.** Evaluating $\mathbf{B}(t_0)$ via de Casteljau also gives the control points of the **two sub-curves**:

- Left sub-curve $[0, t_0]$: control points are the **left edge** of the triangle: $\mathbf{P}_0^{(0)}, \mathbf{P}_0^{(1)}, \mathbf{P}_0^{(2)}, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_0^{(n)}$
- Right sub-curve $[t_0, 1]$: control points are the **right edge**: $\mathbf{P}_0^{(n)}, \mathbf{P}_1^{(n-1)}, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_n^{(0)}$

This is the basis of **adaptive subdivision** — the key algorithm in font rendering, path clipping, and intersection testing.

## §7 — Derivatives and Curvature

### 7.1 Derivative Formula

The derivative of a degree-$n$ Bézier is a degree-$(n-1)$ Bézier:

$$ \mathbf{B}'(t) = n\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} B_{k,n-1}(t),(\mathbf{P}_{k+1} - \mathbf{P}_k) $$

**Proof sketch.** Differentiate $B_{k,n}(t)$ and use the identity $B_{k,n}'(t) = n[B_{k-1,n-1}(t) - B_{k,n-1}(t)]$.

Let $\Delta\mathbf{P}_k = \mathbf{P}_{k+1} - \mathbf{P}_k$ (forward differences). Then:

$$ \mathbf{B}'(t) = n,\mathbf{B}_{\Delta}(t) $$

where $\mathbf{B}_\Delta$ is the Bézier curve with control points $\Delta\mathbf{P}_0, \ldots, \Delta\mathbf{P}_{n-1}$.

### 7.2 Curvature at Endpoints

At $t = 0$, for a cubic:

$$ \kappa(0) = \frac{|\mathbf{B}'(0) \times \mathbf{B}''(0)|}{|\mathbf{B}'(0)|^3} $$

where $\times$ denotes the 2D "cross product" (scalar: $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = u_x v_y - u_y v_x$), and:

$$ \mathbf{B}'(0) = 3(\mathbf{P}_1 - \mathbf{P}_0), \qquad \mathbf{B}''(0) = 6(\mathbf{P}_0 - 2\mathbf{P}_1 + \mathbf{P}_2) $$

## §8 — Joining Bézier Curves: Continuity Conditions

A single cubic Bézier has limited shape vocabulary. Complex shapes (fonts, paths) are built from **piecewise Bézier splines** — sequences of cubic segments joined end-to-end.

Let segment 1 end at $\mathbf{P}_3$ with last handle $\mathbf{P}_2$, and segment 2 start at $\mathbf{Q}_0 = \mathbf{P}_3$ with first handle $\mathbf{Q}_1$.

|Continuity Class|Condition|Geometric Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$C^0$|$\mathbf{P}_3 = \mathbf{Q}_0$|Curves share an endpoint — no gap|
|$G^1$|$\mathbf{Q}_1 - \mathbf{P}_3$ parallel to $\mathbf{P}_3 - \mathbf{P}_2$|Same tangent _direction_ — no kink|
|$C^1$|$\mathbf{Q}_1 - \mathbf{P}_3 = \mathbf{P}_3 - \mathbf{P}_2$|Same tangent direction _and speed_|
|$C^2$|$C^1$ + second derivative matches|Matching curvature — the join is invisible|

> **Design implication (Illustrator, Figma).** When you drag a "smooth" handle in a vector editor, you're enforcing $G^1$. When you hold a modifier key to enforce equal handle lengths, you're enforcing $C^1$. The editor is managing these conditions for you.

```
G¹ join (smooth, different handle lengths):

    P2    P3=Q0    Q1
     o----●--------o
          ↑
      tangent direction continuous,
      speeds may differ (‖P3-P2‖ ≠ ‖Q1-Q0‖)

C¹ join (equal handle lengths):

    P2    P3=Q0    Q1
     o----●----o
          ↑
      P3 - P2 = Q1 - P3 (exact reflection)
```

## §9 — Rational Bézier Curves and Conics

Standard Bézier curves cannot represent a **circle arc** exactly — they are polynomials, and circle arcs are algebraic of a different kind. The fix: **rational Bézier curves**.

Attach a weight $w_k > 0$ to each control point:

$$ \mathbf{B}(t) = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^n w_k B_{k,n}(t),\mathbf{P}_k}{\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^n w_k B_{k,n}(t)} $$

This is a **projective** generalization. For $n=2$ with weights $(1, w, 1)$:

- $w = 1$: parabola arc
- $w < 1$: ellipse arc
- $w = \cos(\theta/2)$ for arc subtending angle $\theta$: exact circle arc
- $w > 1$: hyperbola arc

**NURBS** (Non-Uniform Rational B-Splines) — the industry standard in CAD (SolidWorks, CATIA, Rhino) — are piecewise rational Bézier curves with a knot vector controlling the join structure.

## §11 — Bézier Curves in AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 11.1 Font Rendering and OCR

TrueType fonts store every glyph as piecewise **quadratic** Bézier curves; PostScript/PDF uses **cubic** Bézier curves. The entire Latin alphabet, CJK character sets, Arabic script — everything is Bézier control points.

**OCR neural networks** (Tesseract 4+, TrOCR) must decode these curves. Training data for OCR is generated by rendering Bézier-encoded fonts. Understanding what the network "sees" requires understanding how Bézier curves are rasterized.

### 11.2 Trajectory Planning in Reinforcement Learning

In **robot learning** and **autonomous driving**, smooth reference trajectories are represented as Bézier curves. A policy network predicts **control points** $\mathbf{P}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_n$ rather than a sequence of waypoints, because:

- The convex hull property guarantees the trajectory stays within a safe region
- The tangent conditions enforce smooth acceleration/deceleration
- Subdivision allows adaptive refinement at high-curvature regions
- The entire trajectory is represented by $4(n+1)$ numbers (compact)

**Example.** In Waymo's motion forecasting, predicted agent trajectories are parametrized as cubic Bézier curves. The loss function penalizes deviation between predicted and ground-truth control points.

### 11.3 Bézier Curves as Learnable Parameters

**BézierFormer** and related models directly output Bézier control points from attention-based decoders:

$$ \hat{\mathbf{P}}_0, \ldots, \hat{\mathbf{P}}_n = \text{Decoder}(\text{image features}) $$

The predicted curve is $\hat{\mathbf{B}}(t) = \sum_k B_{k,n}(t)\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k$. The loss is:

$$ \mathcal{L} = \int_0^1 |\hat{\mathbf{B}}(t) - \mathbf{B}^_(t)|^2, dt \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^N |\hat{\mathbf{B}}(t_j) - \mathbf{B}^_(t_j)|^2 $$

This integral loss is **differentiable** with respect to the control points $\hat{\mathbf{P}}_k$ because $B_{k,n}(t)$ is a smooth function of $t$, and the chain rule flows through:

$$ \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{\mathbf{P}}_k} = \frac{2}{N}\sum_j B_{k,n}(t_j),(\hat{\mathbf{B}}(t_j) - \mathbf{B}^*(t_j)) $$

### 11.4 Text Detection: Scene Text in the Wild

**ABCNet** and **TextBPN** use Bézier curves to detect arbitrarily curved text in natural images (menus, storefronts, logos). The network predicts control points of a Bézier curve that traces the text baseline, enabling recognition of text that cannot be enclosed in a simple bounding box.

### 11.5 Neural Architecture: Bézier Activation Functions

Some recent work uses **learnable Bézier activation functions**:

$$ \sigma(x) = \sum_{k=0}^n B_{k,n}!\left(\frac{x - x_{\min}}{x_{\max} - x_{\min}}\right) w_k $$

where the weights $w_k$ are learned. This subsumes ReLU, sigmoid, tanh, and GELU as special cases, with the shape learned from data. The **partition of unity** property ensures $\sigma$ is a well-normalized blending of the weights.

### 11.6 Latent Space Interpolation

In **GANs and VAEs**, interpolating between two latent codes $\mathbf{z}_0$ and $\mathbf{z}_1$ via a straight line often crosses low-density regions (producing blurry or out-of-distribution images). **Bézier interpolation** with control points placed in high-density regions:

$$ \mathbf{z}(t) = (1-t)^2\mathbf{z}_0 + 2t(1-t)\mathbf{z}_c + t^2\mathbf{z}_1 $$

where $\mathbf{z}_c$ is chosen to stay near the data manifold, produces semantically smoother interpolations. The convex hull property keeps the path in the convex hull of ${\mathbf{z}_0, \mathbf{z}_c, \mathbf{z}_1}$ — a region more likely to be in-distribution.

### 11.7 Attention Weight Smoothing and Positional Encodings

The Bernstein basis functions $B_{k,n}(t)$ are a **smooth partition of unity** — they sum to 1, are non-negative, and have compact support on $[0,1]$. This is exactly the property required of **attention weights** (softmax output). Some continuous attention models replace the softmax with a Bernstein mixture:

$$ \alpha_k = \frac{B_{k,n}(\tau)}{\sum_j B_{j,n}(\tau)} $$

where $\tau$ is a learned "focus" parameter. This gives smooth, controllable attention as a function of position.

### 11.8 Diffusion Model Trajectory Parametrization

In **diffusion models**, the noising/denoising path $\mathbf{x}(t)$ for $t \in [0,1]$ is typically linear (DDPM) or follows a learned schedule. Emerging work (2024) parametrizes the denoising trajectory as a **Bézier curve** in data space:

$$ \mathbf{x}(t) = \sum_{k=0}^n B_{k,n}(t),\mathbf{C}_k $$

where $\mathbf{C}_0 = \mathbf{x}_{\text{noise}}$, $\mathbf{C}_n = \mathbf{x}_{\text{data}}$, and intermediate control points $\mathbf{C}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{C}_{n-1}$ are predicted by the network. The subdivision property allows adaptive step sizing; the convex hull property bounds the trajectory.

## §12 — Rigorous Summary

> **Definition.** The **Bézier curve of degree $n$** with control points $\mathbf{P}_0, \ldots, \mathbf{P}_n \in \mathbb{R}^d$ is: $$\mathbf{B}: [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}^d, \qquad \mathbf{B}(t) = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}(1-t)^{n-k}t^k,\mathbf{P}_k$$

> **Theorem (Properties).** $\mathbf{B}$ satisfies:
> 
> 1. **(Endpoint interpolation)** $\mathbf{B}(0) = \mathbf{P}_0$, $\mathbf{B}(1) = \mathbf{P}_n$
> 2. **(Convex hull)** $\mathbf{B}(t) \in \text{conv}(\mathbf{P}_0,\ldots,\mathbf{P}_n)$ for all $t \in [0,1]$
> 3. **(Tangents)** $\mathbf{B}'(0) = n(\mathbf{P}_1-\mathbf{P}_0)$, $\mathbf{B}'(1) = n(\mathbf{P}_n - \mathbf{P}_{n-1})$
> 4. **(Affine invariance)** For any affine $\phi$: $\phi(\mathbf{B}(t)) = \text{Bézier}(\phi(\mathbf{P}_0),\ldots,\phi(\mathbf{P}_n))(t)$
> 5. **(Smoothness)** $\mathbf{B} \in C^\infty([0,1])$; in fact $\mathbf{B}$ is a polynomial of degree $\leq n$
> 6. **(Subdivision)** de Casteljau at $t_0$ splits $\mathbf{B}$ into two Bézier sub-curves with explicitly computable control points
