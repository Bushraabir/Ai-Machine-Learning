

> _"Before you integrate, before you differentiate — you must see. A polar curve that you cannot sketch from its equation is a curve you do not yet understand."_

## 0. The Philosophy of Polar Graphing

In Cartesian coordinates, graphing $y = f(x)$ is mechanical: sweep $x$ left to right, plot height $f(x)$. The geometry is transparent because the axes are the geometry.

In polar coordinates, graphing $r = f(\theta)$ is genuinely different. As $\theta$ increases, you rotate around the origin, and the curve is traced by a point at distance $r(\theta)$ from the pole. When $r < 0$, the point appears on the **opposite** side of the pole. The curve can cross itself, loop back, retrace, and produce far more intricate shapes than the equation suggests.

**The discipline required:** You must think simultaneously about _angle_ (direction) and _radius_ (distance), and you must track the sign of $r$ carefully. The reward is that a one-line equation like $r = \cos(2\theta)$ produces a four-petalled rose — and once you understand _why_, no memorization is needed.

---

## 1. The Systematic Graphing Procedure

Here is the procedure I always follow, in order. Do not skip steps.

**Step 1 — Check symmetry.** Apply the three symmetry tests. Every symmetry you establish halves (or quarters) the work. (See: [[Symmetry in Polar Curves]])

**Step 2 — Find zeros and maximum $|r|$.** Solve $f(\theta) = 0$ — these are the angles where the curve passes through the pole. Find $\theta$ where $|f(\theta)|$ is maximum — these are the farthest points from the pole.

**Step 3 — Find where $r$ changes sign.** Between consecutive zeros of $f$, determine the sign of $r$. When $r > 0$, the point is in the direction $\theta$. When $r < 0$, it is in the direction $\theta + \pi$.

**Step 4 — Build a table of key values.** Evaluate $r$ at $\theta = 0, \pi/6, \pi/4, \pi/3, \pi/2, 2\pi/3, 3\pi/4, 5\pi/6, \pi$ and by symmetry fill in the rest. Add any special angles from Steps 2–3.

**Step 5 — Plot the key points in polar form.** Mark them on a polar grid (concentric circles at $r = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$ and rays at multiples of $\pi/6$ or $\pi/4$).

**Step 6 — Connect smoothly.** Join the points with a smooth curve, respecting the direction of increasing $\theta$. Note where the curve is approaching or leaving the pole.

**Step 7 — Verify with Cartesian conversion (optional).** Convert special points to $(x, y)$ and check they lie in the expected position.

## 3. Graphing Circles and Lines

These are the base cases — understand them completely before moving to more complex curves.

### 3.1 Circle $r = a$

All points at distance $|a|$ from the pole. This is the circle $x^2 + y^2 = a^2$.

```
r = 2:

      ╭───╮
     │  O  │   radius 2, centered at pole
      ╰───╯
```

### 3.2 Circle $r = 2a\cos\theta$

Convert: $r^2 = 2ar\cos\theta \Rightarrow x^2 + y^2 = 2ax \Rightarrow (x-a)^2 + y^2 = a^2$.

Circle of radius $|a|$, centered at $(a, 0)$. Passes through the pole (since $r = 0$ when $\theta = \pi/2$).

```
r = 2cosθ  (a = 1):

       ╭──╮
      │    │
   ─O─╯    ╰   center at (1, 0), radius 1
            
Traced once as θ goes from -π/2 to π/2.
At θ = π/2 or -π/2: r = 0 (at the pole).
At θ = 0: r = 2 (rightmost point).
```

### 3.3 Circle $r = 2a\sin\theta$

Similarly: $(x)^2 + (y-a)^2 = a^2$. Circle centered at $(0, a)$, passes through pole.

### 3.4 Line $\theta = \alpha$

All points at angle $\alpha$ from the polar axis, for any $r$ (including $r < 0$, giving the opposite ray). This is the full line through the pole at angle $\alpha$.

### 3.5 Line $r = \frac{d}{\cos(\theta - \alpha)}$

A line at perpendicular distance $d$ from the pole, with the perpendicular foot in direction $\alpha$.

## 4. Graphing Limaçons: $r = b \pm a\cos\theta$ or $r = b \pm a\sin\theta$

The limaçon family is controlled by the ratio $k = a/b$:

|Condition|Shape|
|---|---|
|$k < 1$ (i.e., $a < b$)|Convex limaçon (no dimple, no loop)|
|$k = 1$ (i.e., $a = b$)|**Cardioid**|
|$1 < k < 2$|Limaçon with inner dimple|
|$k \geq 2$ (i.e., $a \geq 2b$)|Limaçon with inner loop|

### 4.1 Full Analysis: Cardioid $r = 1 + \cos\theta$

**Symmetry:** $f(-\theta) = 1 + \cos\theta = f(\theta)$ → symmetric about polar axis.

**Zeros:** $r = 0$ when $\cos\theta = -1$, i.e., $\theta = \pi$. Passes through pole once.

**Maximum:** $r = 2$ at $\theta = 0$.

**Key values (upper half only, by symmetry):**

|$\theta$|$r = 1 + \cos\theta$|Approx|Cartesian $(x, y)$|
|---|---|---|---|
|$0$|$2$|$2.00$|$(2, 0)$|
|$\pi/6$|$1 + \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$|$1.87$|$(1.62, 0.93)$|
|$\pi/3$|$3/2$|$1.50$|$(0.75, 1.30)$|
|$\pi/2$|$1$|$1.00$|$(0, 1)$|
|$2\pi/3$|$1/2$|$0.50$|$(-0.25, 0.43)$|
|$5\pi/6$|$1 - \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}$|$0.13$|$(-0.11, 0.07)$|
|$\pi$|$0$|$0$|$(0, 0)$ ← pole|

**Graphing narrative:**

- Start at $\theta = 0$: point $(2, 0)$, the rightmost tip.
- As $\theta$ increases from $0$ to $\pi$: $r$ decreases from $2$ to $0$. The point spirals inward counter-clockwise, tracing the upper half of the cardioid.
- At $\theta = \pi$: arrives at the pole, forming a cusp (since $f'(\pi) = -\sin(\pi) = 0$).
- From $\theta = \pi$ to $2\pi$: by symmetry with the upper half, the lower half is traced.

```
Cardioid r = 1 + cosθ:

            (0,1)
             •
           /   \
         /       \
(-.25,.43)•      •(2,0)    ← maximum radius
         \       /
           \   /
             •
           (pole, cusp)
           
Shape: like a heart, cusp pointing LEFT,
       maximum extent to the RIGHT.
```

### 4.2 Full Analysis: Limaçon with Inner Loop $r = 1 + 2\cos\theta$

Here $a/b = 2 > 1$, so there is an inner loop.

**Zeros:** $r = 0$ when $\cos\theta = -1/2$, i.e., $\theta = 2\pi/3$ and $\theta = 4\pi/3$.

**Sign of $r$:**

- $\theta \in [0, 2\pi/3)$: $\cos\theta > -1/2$, so $r > 0$.
- $\theta \in (2\pi/3, 4\pi/3)$: $\cos\theta < -1/2$, so $r < 0$ → **inner loop** (points reflect through pole).
- $\theta \in (4\pi/3, 2\pi]$: $r > 0$ again.

**Maximum:** $r = 3$ at $\theta = 0$. Inner loop maximum: $|r| = 1$ at $\theta = \pi$ ($r = -1$, so $|r| = 1$).

|$\theta$|$r$|Sign|Direction plotted|
|---|---|---|---|
|$0$|$3$|$+$|$\theta = 0$|
|$\pi/3$|$2$|$+$|$\theta = \pi/3$|
|$\pi/2$|$1$|$+$|$\theta = \pi/2$|
|$2\pi/3$|$0$|—|pole|
|$5\pi/6$|$-\sqrt{3}+1 \approx -0.73$|$-$|$\theta = 5\pi/6 + \pi = 11\pi/6$|
|$\pi$|$-1$|$-$|$\theta = 0$ (inner loop on pos. $x$-axis)|
|$7\pi/6$|$-\sqrt{3}+1$|$-$|$\theta = \pi/6$|
|$4\pi/3$|$0$|—|pole|

**Inner loop narrative:**

When $\theta \in (2\pi/3, 4\pi/3)$, we have $r < 0$. The point plots at angle $\theta + \pi$, with distance $|r|$ from the pole. So:

- At $\theta = \pi$: $r = -1$, point at $(-(-1), \pi + \pi) = (1, 0)$ in polar → the Cartesian point $(1, 0)$. This is _inside_ the outer loop.

The inner loop is traced as $\theta$ sweeps from $2\pi/3$ to $4\pi/3$, but the actual point moves in the angular range $[2\pi/3 + \pi, 4\pi/3 + \pi] = [5\pi/3, 7\pi/3]$, i.e., back around the right side of the plane.

```
Limaçon r = 1 + 2cosθ:

         outer loop
        ╭────────╮
       │           │
  ─────●─────────────●────  (r=3)
       │   ╭───╮   │
        ╰──│   │──╯
           ╰───╯
           inner loop
           (traced when r < 0)
           
Outer loop: r > 0, θ ∈ [0,2π/3] ∪ [4π/3, 2π]
Inner loop: r < 0, θ ∈ [2π/3, 4π/3]
```

## 5. Graphing Rose Curves: $r = a\cos(n\theta)$ and $r = a\sin(n\theta)$

**The petal count rule:**

- $n$ **odd**: $n$ petals.
- $n$ **even**: $2n$ petals.

**Why?** (See the rigorous argument in [[Symmetry in Polar Curves]].)

For odd $n$: when $r < 0$ during $\theta \in (\pi/(2n), 3\pi/(2n))$ etc., the negative-$r$ portions _retrace_ petals that were already drawn with $r > 0$, just approached from the opposite direction.

For even $n$: the negative-$r$ portions draw _new_ petals in the gaps, doubling the count.

### 5.1 Full Analysis: 4-Petal Rose $r = \cos(2\theta)$

**Symmetry:** $f(-\theta) = \cos(-2\theta) = \cos(2\theta)$ → about polar axis. $f(\pi - \theta) = \cos(2\pi - 2\theta) = \cos(2\theta)$ → about $\theta = \pi/2$. By the proposition (any two → all three): also about the pole.

**Zeros:** $\cos(2\theta) = 0 \Rightarrow 2\theta = \pi/2 + k\pi \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/4, 3\pi/4, 5\pi/4, 7\pi/4$. Four zeros → four passages through the pole.

**Maxima:** $r = \pm 1$ at $\theta = 0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2$.

**Sign analysis over $[0, 2\pi]$:**

|$\theta$ interval|$2\theta$ interval|$\cos(2\theta)$|$r$ sign|Petal|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|$[0, \pi/4]$|$[0, \pi/2]$|$+$|$+$|Petal 1 (right)|
|$[\pi/4, 3\pi/4]$|$[\pi/2, 3\pi/2]$|$-$|$-$|Petal 2* (upper, via $r<0$)|
|$[3\pi/4, 5\pi/4]$|$[3\pi/2, 5\pi/2]$|$+$|$+$|Petal 3 (left)|
|$[5\pi/4, 7\pi/4]$|$[5\pi/2, 7\pi/2]$|$-$|$-$|Petal 4* (lower, via $r<0$)|
|$[7\pi/4, 2\pi]$|$[7\pi/2, 4\pi]$|$+$|$+$|Returns to Petal 1|

*Petals 2 and 4 are drawn with $r < 0$. At $\theta = \pi/2$, $r = \cos(\pi) = -1$, so the point is at distance 1 in direction $\pi/2 + \pi = 3\pi/2$ — the downward direction. But by the full sign table, the upper petal ($r < 0$, $\theta \in [\pi/4, 3\pi/4]$) actually traces upward since the reflected direction points upward.

Let me be explicit. At $\theta = \pi/2$: $r = -1$. Point: distance 1, direction $\pi/2 + \pi = 3\pi/2$. That is $(0, -1)$ in Cartesian — **downward**, not upward. So the petal drawn during $\theta \in [\pi/4, 3\pi/4]$ with $r < 0$ is actually the **downward** petal (Petal 4), not the upper one.

Wait — let's redo this carefully.

At $\theta = \pi/2$: $r = \cos(\pi) = -1$ → point at $(|r|, \theta + \pi) = (1, 3\pi/2)$ → Cartesian $(0, -1)$.

So during $\theta \in [\pi/4, 3\pi/4]$: $r < 0$, actual direction = $\theta + \pi \in [5\pi/4, 7\pi/4]$ — the **lower half**. This draws the **bottom two petals**.

The full tracing order:

1. $\theta \in [0, \pi/4]$: Right petal traced outward.
2. $\theta \in [\pi/4, 3\pi/4]$: $r < 0$ → **bottom two petals** partially traced.
3. $\theta \in [3\pi/4, 5\pi/4]$: Left petal.
4. $\theta \in [5\pi/4, 7\pi/4]$: $r < 0$ → **top two petals** partially traced.
5. $\theta \in [7\pi/4, 2\pi]$: Back to right petal start.

The standard description (right, top, left, bottom petals at $\theta = 0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2$) is correct; it's the _order of tracing_ that requires care.

```
4-petal rose r = cos(2θ):

         top petal
            •
            |
left  •──── O ────•  right
  petal     |      petal
            •
         bottom petal

Petal tips at θ = 0 (right), π/2 (top? — check!),
π (left), 3π/2 (bottom).

At θ = π/2: r = cos(π) = -1 → point at (1, 3π/2) = (0,-1).
So the petal at θ = π/2 is actually the BOTTOM petal (via r < 0).
The TOP petal is traced at θ = 3π/2: r = cos(3π) = -1 → (1, π/2) = (0,1). ✓
```

### 5.2 Full Analysis: 3-Petal Rose $r = \cos(3\theta)$

**Zeros:** $3\theta = \pi/2 + k\pi \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/6, \pi/2, 5\pi/6, 7\pi/6, 3\pi/2, 11\pi/6$.

Six passages through the pole during $[0, 2\pi]$.

**Sign over $[0, \pi]$ (then check if lower half retraces):**

|$\theta$|$r = \cos(3\theta)$|Sign|
|---|---|---|
|$[0, \pi/6]$|positive|$+$ → Petal 1 (right)|
|$[\pi/6, \pi/2]$|negative|$-$ → points in $[\pi/6+\pi, \pi/2+\pi] = [7\pi/6, 3\pi/2]$|
|$[\pi/2, 5\pi/6]$|positive|$+$ → Petal 2 (upper left)|
|$[5\pi/6, \pi]$|negative|$-$ → points in $[5\pi/6+\pi, 2\pi] = [11\pi/6, 2\pi]$|

During $\theta \in [\pi/6, \pi/2]$ with $r < 0$: actual direction $\in [7\pi/6, 3\pi/2]$ (lower left to downward). This traces the **lower left petal** (Petal 3's bottom half).

For odd $n = 3$: petals lie at $\theta = 0, 2\pi/3, 4\pi/3$ (where $r = 1$). The three petals are equally spaced at $120°$. The $r < 0$ segments retrace each petal from the other side, not creating new petals.

```
3-petal rose r = cos(3θ):

      Petal 2 ↗
         •
        / \
       /   \
   ───O─────•──  Petal 1 (θ=0, r=1, point at (1,0))
       \   /
        \ /
         •
      Petal 3 ↘

Petals at angles 0°, 120°, 240°.
Each petal has length r = 1.
Odd n → n petals (the r<0 portions retrace existing petals).
```

### 5.3 The General Rose $r = a\cos(n\theta)$: Key Facts Summary

```
ROSE CURVE FACTS:
                                                  
  r = a·cos(nθ):                                  
  ┌─────────────────────────────────────────────┐
  │  n odd:  n petals, each of length a         │
  │          Petals at θ = 0, 2π/n, 4π/n, ...  │
  │          Symmetric about polar axis         │
  │                                             │
  │  n even: 2n petals, each of length a        │
  │          Petals at θ = 0, π/n, 2π/n, ...   │

    n even: 2n · πa²/(4n) = πa²/2
```

## 6. Graphing the Lemniscate: $r^2 = a^2\cos(2\theta)$

This is qualitatively different — $r^2$ rather than $r$, so $r$ is only defined where $\cos(2\theta) \geq 0$.

**Domain:** $\cos(2\theta) \geq 0 \Leftrightarrow 2\theta \in [-\pi/2, \pi/2] \cup [3\pi/2, 5\pi/2] \Leftrightarrow \theta \in [-\pi/4, \pi/4] \cup [3\pi/4, 5\pi/4]$.

The curve simply **does not exist** for $\theta \in (\pi/4, 3\pi/4) \cup (5\pi/4, 7\pi/4)$.

**Graphing:**

- At $\theta = 0$: $r = a$. Rightmost point $(a, 0)$.
- As $\theta \to \pi/4$: $r \to 0$. Arrives at pole.
- The curve traces the **right lobe** for $\theta \in [-\pi/4, \pi/4]$.
- For $\theta \in [3\pi/4, 5\pi/4]$: traces the **left lobe**.

```
Lemniscate r² = a²cos(2θ):

        does not     does not
        exist here   exist here
          ↓               ↓
  ←──────── (π/4 to 3π/4: no curve) ────────→
  
Left lobe:  •────────────O────────────•  Right lobe
  (traced                             (traced
  θ∈[3π/4,5π/4])                    θ∈[-π/4, π/4])
  
Figure-eight shape. Each lobe is a closed curve
passing through the pole.
Resembles the symbol ∞.
```

**Cartesian form:** $r^4 = a^2 r^2 \cos(2\theta) = a^2(r^2\cos^2\theta - r^2\sin^2\theta) = a^2(x^2 - y^2)$. Since $r^2 = x^2 + y^2$: $(x^2 + y^2)^2 = a^2(x^2 - y^2)$.

## 7. Graphing Spirals

### 7.1 Archimedean Spiral $r = a\theta$, $\theta \geq 0$

- $r$ increases linearly with $\theta$. Each full revolution ($\Delta\theta = 2\pi$) increases $r$ by $2\pi a$.
- The spacing between successive arms is constant: $\Delta r = 2\pi a$.

```
Archimedean spiral r = θ (a=1):

         r increases by 2π per revolution
         
         ╭──────╮
        ╱    ╭──╯
       │    ╱
       ╰───O──────→
       
Arms equally spaced: Δr = 2π between adjacent arms.
No symmetry (r ≠ f(-θ) since θ ≥ 0 changes domain).
```

### 7.2 Logarithmic Spiral $r = ae^{b\theta}$

- For $b > 0$: expands outward.
- For $b < 0$: contracts toward the pole.
- **Self-similar**: $\tan\psi = 1/b$ is constant — the curve cuts every ray at the same angle.
- As $\theta \to -\infty$ (for $b > 0$): $r \to 0$, spiraling into the pole infinitely.

```
Log spiral r = e^{0.1θ}:

Arms get farther apart exponentially.
Same shape at every scale (self-similar).
        
        ╭────────╮
       ╱    ╭───╯
      │   ╭─╯
      ╰──O
      
The golden spiral (nautilus shell) is a log spiral.
```

## 8. A Systematic Graphing Example: $r = 2\sin(3\theta)$

This is a 3-petal rose (odd $n = 3$), using sine instead of cosine, so it is rotated.

**Step 1 — Symmetry.**

$f(\pi - \theta) = 2\sin(3\pi - 3\theta) = 2\sin(3\pi)\cos(3\theta) - 2\cos(3\pi)\sin(3\theta) = 0 + 2\sin(3\theta) = f(\theta)$. ✓ Symmetric about $\theta = \pi/2$.

**Step 2 — Zeros.** $2\sin(3\theta) = 0 \Rightarrow 3\theta = k\pi \Rightarrow \theta = 0, \pi/3, 2\pi/3, \pi, 4\pi/3, 5\pi/3$.

**Step 3 — Sign.**

|$\theta$ interval|$\sin(3\theta)$|$r$|Actual direction|
|---|---|---|---|
|$(0, \pi/3)$|$+$|$> 0$|Same as $\theta$|
|$(\pi/3, 2\pi/3)$|$-$|$< 0$|$\theta + \pi$|
|$(2\pi/3, \pi)$|$+$|$> 0$|Same as $\theta$|
|$(\pi, 4\pi/3)$|$-$|$< 0$|$\theta + \pi$|
|$(4\pi/3, 5\pi/3)$|$+$|$> 0$|Same as $\theta$|
|$(5\pi/3, 2\pi)$|$-$|$< 0$|$\theta + \pi$|

For odd $n$: positive-$r$ intervals trace petals; negative-$r$ intervals retrace those same petals from the other direction. Only 3 distinct petals.

**Step 4 — Maximum $r = 2$ at $3\theta = \pi/2, 5\pi/2, 9\pi/2$, i.e., $\theta = \pi/6, 5\pi/6, 3\pi/2$.**

Petal tips: $(\pi/6, 2)$, $(5\pi/6, 2)$, $(3\pi/2, 2)$.

|$2\pi/3$|$0$|
|$5\pi/6$|$2$|
|$\pi$|$0$|

**Step 6 — Sketch:**

## 9. Recognizing Curve Type from Equation: A Quick Reference

Before graphing, identify the curve type from the equation's structure:

```
EQUATION RECOGNITION GUIDE:

  r = a                         → Circle (center at pole)
  r = 2a·cosθ or r = 2a·sinθ   → Circle (through pole)
  r = a ± b·cosθ (or sinθ)     → Limaçon family:
       a = b  →  Cardioid
       a > b  →  Convex/dimpled limaçon
       a < b  →  Limaçon with inner loop
  r = a·cos(nθ) or a·sin(nθ)   → Rose curve:
       n odd  →  n petals
       n even →  2n petals
  r² = a²·cos(2θ)               → Lemniscate (figure-eight)
  r² = a²·sin(2θ)               → Lemniscate (rotated 45°)
  r = aθ                        → Archimedean spiral
  r = ae^{bθ}                   → Logarithmic spiral
  r·cosθ = a  (i.e., x = a)    → Vertical line
  r·sinθ = b  (i.e., y = b)    → Horizontal line
```

## 10. Common Graphing Errors and How to Avoid Them

**Error 1: Ignoring $r < 0$.**

The most common mistake. When $r < 0$, the point is NOT at $(\text{negative distance}, \theta)$ — it is at $(|r|, \theta + \pi)$. Always check the sign of $r$ in each interval.

_Fix:_ In your sign table, explicitly write the "actual direction" column as in §8.

**Error 2: Wrong petal count on rose curves.**

Students often say "$r = \cos(4\theta)$ has 4 petals" when it has 8.

_Fix:_ Memorize the rule — even $n$ gives $2n$ petals. Alternatively, find all zeros of $r$ in $[0, 2\pi]$ and count: $r = \cos(n\theta) = 0$ at $2n$ values of $\theta$, and consecutive zeros bound one petal each.

**Error 3: Conflating the parameter interval with the full curve.**

For $r = \cos(2\theta)$, the full curve is traced as $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$. For $r = \cos\theta$, the full circle is traced in $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ (the second half retraces).

_Fix:_ Find the period of $r = f(\theta)$. For $f(\theta) = \cos(n\theta)$, the period is $2\pi/n$. Trace over $[0, 2\pi]$ but note when retracing begins.

**Error 4: Missing the symmetry → overworking.**

Students compute all of $[0, 2\pi]$ when they could exploit symmetry to do half.

_Fix:_ Always run the symmetry check first. Symmetric about polar axis → compute $[0, \pi]$ and reflect.

**Error 5: Incorrect orientation of rotated curves.**

$r = a\cos\theta$ → circle on the right of the pole. $r = a\sin\theta$ → circle on top. $r = a\cos(n\theta)$ → first petal along positive $x$-axis. $r = a\sin(n\theta)$ → first petal rotated by $\pi/(2n)$.

_Fix:_ The cos-version always has a feature on the polar axis. The sin-version is the cos-version rotated by $\pi/(2n)$ (for rose) or $\pi/2$ (for cardioid).

## 12. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 12.1 Polar Graphs as Visualization Tools in ML

In ML model evaluation, **radar charts** (also called spider charts or polar charts) are direct applications of polar graphing: each metric (accuracy, precision, recall, F1, AUC) is assigned an angular direction, and performance is plotted as a polar curve. Comparing two models is equivalent to asking which polar region is larger — the area formula $A = \frac{1}{2}\int r^2, d\theta$ quantifies this.

### 12.2 Feature Map Visualization via Rose Patterns

In **convolutional neural networks**, filters in the first layer learn oriented features — edges at various angles. Visualizing the orientation tuning of a neuron as a polar plot (response $r$ as a function of angle $\theta$ of the input edge) produces a rose-like curve. A neuron tuned to a single orientation produces a 2-petal rose ($r = \cos^2\theta$ roughly); a neuron with broader tuning produces a more circular plot.

This is not metaphor — it is literally how **orientation tuning curves** are displayed in computational neuroscience and CNN interpretability research.

### 12.3 Limaçon-Shaped Decision Boundaries

A linear classifier has a straight-line decision boundary. A kernel SVM or a neural network can produce curved boundaries. In polar coordinates centered at a cluster center, a smooth convex boundary looks like a limaçon (cardioid when the boundary passes through the origin; convex limaçon when it doesn't). Classifiers that produce boundaries with inner loops effectively create "islands" of one class inside the other — the inner loop encloses a pocket of the positive class inside the negative class.

### 12.4 Lemniscate and Information Geometry

The lemniscate $r^2 = a^2\cos(2\theta)$ satisfies $(x^2 + y^2)^2 = a^2(x^2 - y^2)$. In **information geometry**, the Fisher information metric on a family of probability distributions can produce level curves with this shape near saddle points of the KL divergence. The lemniscate's figure-eight form means there are two "basins" of nearby distributions, meeting at a point (the pole) — a geometric picture of bimodal model families.

### 12.5 Spiral Dynamics in Gradient Descent

The trajectory of gradient descent with momentum in a 2D quadratic loss $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}) = \frac{1}{2}(\lambda_1 w_1^2 + \lambda_2 w_2^2)$ with $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$ is an inward spiral. In polar coordinates centered at the minimum, this trajectory approximates a logarithmic spiral $r = ae^{-b\theta}$ (contracting, $b > 0$). The spiral's winding is tighter when the condition number $\lambda_2/\lambda_1$ is large — an ill-conditioned loss makes the spiral oscillate widely before converging. This is precisely why ill-conditioned problems are hard: the log spiral has many turns before $r \approx 0$.

**Preconditioning** (used in Adam, AdaGrad, natural gradient) effectively makes $\lambda_1 \approx \lambda_2$, converting the tight spiral into a nearly radial path — the angle $\psi \approx \pi/2$ everywhere (perpendicular to the level curves), which is the steepest descent direction.

### 12.6 Sinusoidal Positional Encodings as Polar Plots

The positional encoding in the original Transformer ($\text{PE}_{k,2i} = \sin(k/10000^{2i/d})$, $\text{PE}_{k,2i+1} = \cos(k/10000^{2i/d})$) can be read as: for each frequency band $i$, the 2D vector $(\cos(\omega_i k), \sin(\omega_i k))$ traces a circle (the polar curve $r = 1$) as $k$ increases. Different frequency bands trace this circle at different angular rates $\omega_i$. The full positional encoding is a stack of these circular traces — a multi-frequency polar representation of position.

**RoPE** (Rotary Position Embedding) makes this exact: it applies a rotation by $\omega_i k$ to each 2D subspace of the embedding, which is literally multiplication by the rotation matrix in polar form $e^{i\omega_i k}$. The polar curve $r = 1$ (unit circle) is the geometry underlying RoPE.
