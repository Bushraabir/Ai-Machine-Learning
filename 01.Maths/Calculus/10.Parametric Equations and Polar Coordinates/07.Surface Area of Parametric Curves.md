

> _"Arc length measures how far you travel. Surface area measures the skin of the world you sweep out as you travel."_

---

## §0 — Motivation & The Core Idea

When a parametric curve is **revolved** about an axis, it sweeps out a **surface of revolution**. Computing the area of that surface is the central problem of this note.

The key geometric insight: as a tiny arc element $ds$ of the curve sweeps around an axis, it traces out a **thin band** (a frustum of a cone). The area of that band is:

$$ dS = 2\pi \cdot (\text{radius}) \cdot ds $$

where $ds$ is the arc length element and "radius" is the perpendicular distance from the curve to the axis of revolution.

Everything else is careful bookkeeping of what $ds$ looks like in parametric form.

---

## §1 — The Arc Length Element

Before surface area, we need $ds$ for a parametric curve $x = f(t),, y = g(t)$.

A small displacement along the curve: $$ ds = \sqrt{(dx)^2 + (dy)^2} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2}, dt = \sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}, dt $$

This is the **speed** of the parametric point times $dt$: $$ ds = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|, dt, \quad \text{where } \mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle f'(t), g'(t)\rangle $$

---

## §2 — Surface Area Formula: Revolution About the $x$-axis

### 2.1 Derivation

Revolve the curve about the **$x$-axis**. The radius of revolution at parameter $t$ is the $y$-coordinate: $\text{radius} = y = g(t)$ (assuming $g(t) \geq 0$).

Each arc element $ds$ sweeps out a band of area: $$ dS = 2\pi, g(t), ds = 2\pi, g(t),\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}, dt $$

Integrating:

$$ \boxed{S = 2\pi\int_\alpha^\beta g(t),\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}; dt} $$

> **Condition:** $g(t) \geq 0$ on $[\alpha, \beta]$ (the curve must be on or above the $x$-axis, so the radius is non-negative).

### 2.2 Derivation via Frustum Approximation

More rigorously: partition $[\alpha, \beta]$ into $n$ subintervals. On each subinterval, the curve is approximated by a **line segment** of length $\Delta s_k$. Revolving it about the $x$-axis gives a **frustum** (truncated cone) with average radius $\bar{y}_k$ and slant height $\Delta s_k$:

$$ \Delta S_k = 2\pi, \bar{y}_k, \Delta s_k $$

Summing and taking $n \to \infty$: $$ S = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n 2\pi,\bar{y}_k,\Delta s_k = 2\pi\int_\alpha^\beta g(t),\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}; dt $$

This is a **Riemann sum** for the integral — the passage to the limit is justified by the continuity of $g$ and $\sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2}$.

---

## §3 — Surface Area Formula: Revolution About the $y$-axis

Revolve about the **$y$-axis**. Now the radius of revolution is the $x$-coordinate: $\text{radius} = x = f(t)$ (assuming $f(t) \geq 0$).

$$ \boxed{S = 2\pi\int_\alpha^\beta f(t),\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}; dt} $$

> **Mnemonic.** The formula is always $2\pi \cdot (\text{distance to axis}) \cdot ds$. Identify your axis, identify your distance, write $ds$ in parametric form.

---

## §4 — Special Case: Cartesian Curve $y = F(x)$

When $x = t,, y = F(t)$, then $f'(t) = 1,, g'(t) = F'(t)$. The surface area about the $x$-axis becomes:

$$ S = 2\pi\int_a^b F(x),\sqrt{1 + F'(x)^2}; dx $$

The parametric formula reduces to the standard Cartesian formula. Good. ✓

## §5 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Sphere from a Semicircle

Revolve the semicircle $x = r\cos t,, y = r\sin t,, t \in [0, \pi]$ about the $x$-axis.

$$ f'(t) = -r\sin t,\quad g'(t) = r\cos t $$ $$ \sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2} = \sqrt{r^2\sin^2 t + r^2\cos^2 t} = r $$

$$ S = 2\pi\int_0^\pi r\sin t \cdot r, dt = 2\pi r^2 \int_0^\pi \sin t, dt = 2\pi r^2 \cdot [-\cos t]_0^\pi = 2\pi r^2 \cdot 2 $$

$$ \boxed{S = 4\pi r^2} $$

The surface area of a sphere. The parametric derivation is cleaner than any Cartesian approach — no awkward $\sqrt{1 - x^2/r^2}$ denominators to wrestle with.

---

### Example 2 — Surface of Revolution of the Cycloid

Revolve one arch of the cycloid $x = r(t - \sin t),, y = r(1 - \cos t),, t \in [0, 2\pi]$ about the $x$-axis.

$$ f'(t) = r(1 - \cos t),\quad g'(t) = r\sin t $$

$$ f'^2 + g'^2 = r^2(1-\cos t)^2 + r^2\sin^2 t = r^2(1 - 2\cos t + \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t) = r^2(2 - 2\cos t) $$

Using the half-angle identity $1 - \cos t = 2\sin^2(t/2)$:

$$ \sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2} = r\sqrt{2 \cdot 2\sin^2(t/2)} = 2r\sin(t/2) \quad (t \in [0,2\pi],\ \sin(t/2) \geq 0) $$

$$ S = 2\pi\int_0^{2\pi} r(1-\cos t)\cdot 2r\sin(t/2), dt = 2\pi\int_0^{2\pi} r \cdot 2\sin^2!\frac{t}{2} \cdot 2r\sin\frac{t}{2}, dt $$

$$ = 8\pi r^2\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^3!\frac{t}{2}, dt $$

Substitute $u = t/2$, $du = dt/2$:

$$ = 16\pi r^2\int_0^{\pi}\sin^3 u, du $$

Using $\displaystyle\int_0^\pi \sin^3 u, du = \frac{4}{3}$:

$$ \boxed{S = 16\pi r^2 \cdot \frac{4}{3} = \frac{64\pi r^2}{3}} $$

---

### Example 3 — Astroid Revolved About the $x$-axis

$$ x = a\cos^3 t,\quad y = a\sin^3 t,\quad t \in [0, \pi/2] $$ (use one quarter and multiply by 2 for the upper half, which generates the full surface)

$$ f'(t) = -3a\cos^2 t\sin t,\quad g'(t) = 3a\sin^2 t\cos t $$

$$ f'^2 + g'^2 = 9a^2\cos^4 t\sin^2 t + 9a^2\sin^4 t\cos^2 t = 9a^2\sin^2 t\cos^2 t(\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t) = 9a^2\sin^2 t\cos^2 t $$

$$ \sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2} = 3a\sin t\cos t = \frac{3a}{2}\sin 2t $$

By symmetry (revolve the upper half $t \in [0,\pi]$, use $t\in[0,\pi/2]$ doubled):

$$ S = 2\pi \cdot 2\int_0^{\pi/2} a\sin^3 t \cdot 3a\sin t\cos t, dt = 12\pi a^2\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^4 t\cos t, dt $$

$$ = 12\pi a^2 \cdot \left[\frac{\sin^5 t}{5}\right]_0^{\pi/2} = 12\pi a^2 \cdot \frac{1}{5} $$

$$ \boxed{S = \frac{12\pi a^2}{5}} $$

---

### Example 4 — Cone from a Line Segment (Sanity Check)

Revolve the line segment $x = t,, y = mt,, t\in[0,h]$ (slope $m > 0$) about the $x$-axis. This should give a cone.

$$ \sqrt{f'^2 + g'^2} = \sqrt{1 + m^2} $$

$$ S = 2\pi\int_0^h mt \cdot \sqrt{1+m^2}, dt = 2\pi m\sqrt{1+m^2}\cdot\frac{h^2}{2} = \pi m h^2\sqrt{1+m^2} $$

The base radius is $R = mh$ and slant height is $\ell = h\sqrt{1+m^2}$, so:

$$ S = \pi R \ell \quad \checkmark $$

The parametric formula recovers the elementary cone lateral-area formula.

## §7 — The Speed Factor and Why It's Not Just $\int g(t),dt$

A conceptual trap: why isn't surface area just $2\pi\int g(t),dt$?

Because $t$ is not arc length. The parameter $t$ might make the curve move fast in some regions and slow in others. The factor $\sqrt{f'^2+g'^2}$ is the **speed** — it converts "parameter time" into "geometric length". Without it, we'd be weighting fast-moving portions of the curve the same as slow-moving portions, which gives the wrong answer.

**Example of the trap.** Reparametrize the sphere semicircle as $x = r\cos(t^3),, y = r\sin(t^3),, t\in[0, \pi^{1/3}]$. Now $f'(t) = -3rt^2\sin(t^3),, g'(t) = 3rt^2\cos(t^3)$, and $\sqrt{f'^2+g'^2} = 3rt^2$. The integral:

$$ 2\pi\int_0^{\pi^{1/3}} r\sin(t^3)\cdot 3rt^2, dt $$

Substitute $u = t^3$: recovers exactly $2\pi r^2\int_0^\pi \sin u, du = 4\pi r^2$ ✓

Surface area is **parametrization-independent** — a property guaranteed by the $ds$ factor.

> **This is the geometric content of the formula: $ds$ is intrinsic to the curve, not to the parametrization.**

## §8 — Surfaces in 3D: The Full Generalization

For completeness (and connection to multivariable calculus / deep learning), the surface area formula generalizes to a **parametric surface** $\mathbf{r}(u,v) = \langle x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v) \rangle$:

$$ S = \iint_D \left|\frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v}\right|, du, dv $$

The cross product $\partial\mathbf{r}/\partial u \times \partial\mathbf{r}/\partial v$ is the **normal vector** to the surface, and its magnitude is the area element $dS$. This is the MIT 18.02 generalization — the 2D surface area formula is the 1D special case when the surface is a surface of revolution.

The surface of revolution formula follows from this with $u = t$, $v = \theta$ (the revolution angle), and explicit computation of the cross product.

## §9 — Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 9.1 Mesh Surface Area in 3D Deep Learning

In **3D shape learning** — PointNet, MeshCNN, Neural Implicit Surfaces — a learned 3D shape is often represented as a mesh (a collection of triangular faces). The **surface area of the mesh** is computed as:

$$ S = \sum_{\text{triangles}} \frac{1}{2}\left|\mathbf{e}_1 \times \mathbf{e}_2\right| $$

where $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2$ are two edge vectors of each triangle. This is the **discrete version** of $|\partial\mathbf{r}/\partial u \times \partial\mathbf{r}/\partial v|, du, dv$ — the parametric surface area formula with a triangular parametrization.

This is **differentiable** with respect to vertex positions, enabling surface-area regularization losses in 3D generative models.

### 9.2 Neural Implicit Surfaces and the Eikonal Equation

**NeRF** (Neural Radiance Fields) and **NeuS** represent surfaces implicitly as zero level sets of a learned function $F: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$. The surface area element at a point is:

$$ dS = \frac{|\nabla F|}{\left|\partial F/\partial n\right|} $$

Training includes a loss term enforcing $|\nabla F| = 1$ (the **Eikonal equation**), which ensures the level set is a properly-normalized surface — directly analogous to ensuring $|\mathbf{r}'(t)| = 1$ (arc-length parametrization) in 1D curves. The surface area formula requires the curve/surface to be metrically honest; the Eikonal constraint enforces this in neural implicit representations.

### 9.3 Isoperimetric Constraints in Generative Models

The **isoperimetric inequality** states: $$ 4\pi A \leq S^2 $$

for a surface of area $S$ enclosing volume $A$ (with equality for spheres). In **3D shape generation** and **GANs for 3D objects**, regularization losses enforcing near-spherical geometry or bounding surface area are related to this inequality. Minimizing surface area subject to fixed volume is a classical variational problem — and its discrete, differentiable version appears in shape optimization layers.

### 9.4 Riemannian Geometry in Latent Spaces

Modern **latent space geometry** (Arvanitidis et al., 2018 — "Latent Space Oddity") treats the latent space of a VAE as a **Riemannian manifold**. The metric tensor is:

$$ G(z) = J(z)^\top J(z) + \sigma^2 I $$

where $J(z)$ is the Jacobian of the decoder. The **surface area** of a region in latent space, measured under this metric, is:

$$ S = \int!\sqrt{\det G(z)}, dz $$

This is exactly $\int|\partial\mathbf{r}/\partial u \times \partial\mathbf{r}/\partial v|,du,dv$ with $\sqrt{\det G}$ playing the role of the cross-product magnitude. Geodesic interpolation in this Riemannian latent space is used to generate smoother, more semantically meaningful interpolations than straight-line Euclidean interpolation.

### 9.5 Speed Normalization and Batch Normalization

The parametrization-independence of surface area ($ds$ absorbs speed, not parameter values) has a deep parallel in **normalization in neural networks**. Batch normalization, layer normalization, and weight normalization all enforce a kind of "unit speed" on the network's internal representations — ensuring that the magnitude of activations or weights doesn't distort the geometry of the learned manifold. The surface area formula teaches: **measure geometry intrinsically, not via arbitrary parametrizations.**

### 9.6 Differentiable Rendering

In **differentiable rendering** (used in inverse graphics and 3D-aware GANs), the rendered image depends on the surface area and orientation of mesh faces. Gradients of the rendered loss with respect to vertex positions flow through the surface area formula:

$$ \frac{\partial S}{\partial \mathbf{v}_i} = \sum_{\text{triangles adjacent to } \mathbf{v}_i} \frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{v}_i}\left(\frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{e}_1 \times \mathbf{e}_2|\right) $$

This is the discrete gradient of the parametric surface area integral — the fundamental quantity that allows 3D shape learning from 2D image supervision.

## §10 — Rigorous Summary

> **Theorem (Surface Area of Revolution).** Let $x = f(t),, y = g(t)$, $t\in[\alpha,\beta]$, where $f, g$ are continuously differentiable and $g(t) \geq 0$. Assume the curve does not cross itself. Then the area of the surface generated by revolving the curve about the $x$-axis is: $$S = 2\pi\int_\alpha^\beta g(t),\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}; dt$$ This formula is **independent of the choice of parametrization** (reparametrization-invariant).

> **Corollary.** For revolution about the $y$-axis (with $f(t) \geq 0$): $$S = 2\pi\int_\alpha^\beta f(t),\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}; dt$$

## §12 — Common Pitfalls

1. **Forgetting $\sqrt{f'^2+g'^2}$**: Surface area is $2\pi\int y,ds$, not $2\pi\int y,dt$. The speed factor is non-negotiable.
    
2. **Wrong axis, wrong radius**: Revolving about the $x$-axis → radius is $y$. About the $y$-axis → radius is $x$. Confusing these is the most common error.
    
3. **Negative $g(t)$**: If the curve dips below the $x$-axis, the formula breaks (negative radius). Either restrict the interval or use $|g(t)|$.
    
4. **Confusing surface area with volume**: Surface area of revolution is $2\pi\int y,ds$. Volume of revolution (disk/washer method) is $\pi\int y^2, dx$. Different powers, different geometric meanings.
    
5. **Confusing surface area with arc length**: Arc length is $\int\sqrt{f'^2+g'^2},dt$ (no $2\pi g(t)$ factor). Surface area wraps arc length around an axis.
