

> _The ellipse is a circle that has been honestly stretched. Everything about it — its foci, its reflective property, its area — flows from one number: how much it has been stretched._

---

## 1. The Geometric Definition

> **Definition.** An _ellipse_ is the set of all points $P$ in the plane such that the **sum of distances** from $P$ to two fixed points $F_1$ and $F_2$ (the **foci**) is constant.

$$\text{Ellipse} = {, P \in \mathbb{R}^2 : |PF_1| + |PF_2| = 2a ,}$$

where $2a$ is the constant sum, $a > 0$.

**Why $2a$?** When $P$ is at a vertex (end of the major axis), one distance is $a - c$ and the other is $a + c$, summing to $2a$. This is the calibration.

```
        P
       /|\
      / | \
     /  |  \
    /   |   \
F₁ *---+---* F₂        |F₁F₂| = 2c
    \   |   /
     \  |  /
      \ | /
       \|/

  |PF₁| + |PF₂| = 2a  (constant, for every P on the ellipse)
```

**Three fundamental constants:**

|Symbol|Meaning|Constraint|
|---|---|---|
|$a$|semi-major axis (half the long diameter)|$a > 0$|
|$b$|semi-minor axis (half the short diameter)|$0 < b \leq a$|
|$c$|focal distance (center to focus)|$c \geq 0$|

**The fundamental relation:**

$$\boxed{a^2 = b^2 + c^2}$$

_Proof._ Place $P$ at the end of the minor axis: $(0, b)$ with foci at $(\pm c, 0)$. Then $|PF_1| = |PF_2| = a$ (by symmetry), and by Pythagoras: $a^2 = b^2 + c^2$. $\square$

---

## 2. Deriving the Equation

Place center at origin, foci at $F_1 = (-c, 0)$ and $F_2 = (c, 0)$.

Let $P = (x, y)$. The definition gives:

$$\sqrt{(x+c)^2 + y^2} + \sqrt{(x-c)^2 + y^2} = 2a$$

Move one radical: $\sqrt{(x+c)^2+y^2} = 2a - \sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}$

Square: $(x+c)^2 + y^2 = 4a^2 - 4a\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2} + (x-c)^2 + y^2$

Simplify the left minus right: $4cx - 4a^2 = -4a\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}$

$$a^2 - cx = a\sqrt{(x-c)^2+y^2}$$

Square again: $a^4 - 2a^2cx + c^2x^2 = a^2(x^2 - 2cx + c^2 + y^2)$

$$a^4 - c^2x^2 - a^2y^2 = a^4 - a^2c^2 \implies (a^2-c^2)x^2 + a^2y^2 = a^2(a^2-c^2)$$

Since $b^2 = a^2 - c^2$:

$$b^2x^2 + a^2y^2 = a^2b^2$$

$$\boxed{\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1}$$

## 3. Standard Forms

### Horizontal major axis ($a > b > 0$, foci on $x$-axis)

$$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$$

- Center: $(0,0)$
- Vertices: $(\pm a, 0)$
- Co-vertices: $(0, \pm b)$
- Foci: $(\pm c, 0)$, where $c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}$

### Vertical major axis ($a > b > 0$, foci on $y$-axis)

$$\frac{x^2}{b^2} + \frac{y^2}{a^2} = 1$$

- Center: $(0,0)$
- Vertices: $(0, \pm a)$
- Co-vertices: $(\pm b, 0)$
- Foci: $(0, \pm c)$, where $c = \sqrt{a^2 - b^2}$

**Rule:** The larger denominator is always $a^2$, and the foci lie on the axis of the larger denominator.

### Shifted ellipse (center at $(h, k)$)

$$\frac{(x-h)^2}{a^2} + \frac{(y-k)^2}{b^2} = 1$$

## 4. Eccentricity

$$\boxed{e = \frac{c}{a}, \quad 0 \leq e < 1}$$

|$e$|Shape|
|---|---|
|$e = 0$|Perfect circle ($c = 0$, $a = b$)|
|$e \to 0$|Nearly circular|
|$e \to 1$|Very elongated (nearly degenerate)|

Eccentricity measures _how far the foci are from center relative to the semi-major axis_. It is the single number encoding the "shape" of the ellipse — all ellipses with the same $e$ are similar.

**Alternative definition via focus-directrix:**

Each focus has a corresponding directrix. For focus $F_1 = (-c, 0)$, the directrix is $x = -\dfrac{a}{e} = -\dfrac{a^2}{c}$. The ellipse satisfies:

$$\frac{|PF_1|}{d(P,\ell_1)} = e \quad \text{for every } P \text{ on the ellipse}$$

## 5. Key Geometric Features

### 5.1 Latus Rectum

The chord through a focus perpendicular to the major axis. Its half-length:

At $x = c$ in $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$:

$$\frac{c^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1 \implies y^2 = b^2\left(1 - \frac{c^2}{a^2}\right) = \frac{b^4}{a^2}$$

$$\text{Length of latus rectum} = \frac{2b^2}{a}$$

### 5.2 The Reflective (Optical) Property

> **Theorem.** A ray from one focus reflects off the ellipse and passes through the other focus.

_Proof sketch._ At point $P$ on the ellipse, the tangent line makes equal angles with $\overrightarrow{PF_1}$ and $\overrightarrow{PF_2}$. This follows because the tangent is the external angle bisector of $\angle F_1PF_2$ — a consequence of the fact that the ellipse is the locus where $|PF_1| + |PF_2|$ is constant (Fermat's principle / equal-angle reflection). $\square$

**Applications:** Whispering galleries (St. Paul's Cathedral, Capitol building), lithotripsy (focusing ultrasound to shatter kidney stones), ellipsoidal mirrors in optics.

```
       F₁ ----→ reflects → F₂
       
  ___________________________
 /                           \
|    F₁ *         * F₂       |
 \___________________________/

  Every ray from F₁ bounces to F₂.
```

### 5.3 Tangent Line

At point $(x_0, y_0)$ on $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2} + \dfrac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$:

$$\boxed{\frac{x,x_0}{a^2} + \frac{y,y_0}{b^2} = 1}$$

This is the **contact equation** — symmetric and elegant. Compare with the tangent to a circle $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ at $(x_0, y_0)$: $xx_0 + yy_0 = r^2$. The ellipse tangent is the same with denominators $a^2, b^2$.

**Tangent with slope $m$:**

$$y = mx \pm \sqrt{a^2m^2 + b^2}$$

## 6. Area and Perimeter

### Area

$$\boxed{A = \pi a b}$$

_Derivation._ The ellipse $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$ is a circle $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ scaled by $x \mapsto ax$, $y \mapsto by$. The Jacobian of this map is $ab$. Since a unit disk has area $\pi$:

$$A = \pi \cdot ab$$

When $a = b = r$: $A = \pi r^2$. ✓

### Perimeter (No Closed Form!)

The perimeter of an ellipse **cannot be expressed in elementary functions**. It is:

$$P = 4a\int_0^{\pi/2}\sqrt{1 - e^2\sin^2\theta},d\theta = 4aE(e)$$

where $E(e)$ is the **complete elliptic integral of the second kind** — a transcendental function.

**Ramanujan's approximation** (remarkably accurate):

$$P \approx \pi\left[3(a+b) - \sqrt{(3a+b)(a+3b)}\right]$$

**Simple bounds:**

$$2\pi b \leq P \leq 2\pi a \quad \text{(from circumscribed/inscribed circles)}$$

$$P \approx 2\pi\sqrt{\frac{a^2+b^2}{2}} \quad \text{(crude approximation)}$$

## 7. Parametric Form

$$x = a\cos t, \quad y = b\sin t, \quad t \in [0, 2\pi)$$

Verify: $\frac{a^2\cos^2 t}{a^2} + \frac{b^2\sin^2 t}{b^2} = \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t = 1$. ✓

- $t = 0$: right vertex $(a, 0)$
- $t = \pi/2$: top co-vertex $(0, b)$
- $t = \pi$: left vertex $(-a, 0)$

**Velocity:** $\dot{\mathbf{r}} = (-a\sin t,, b\cos t)$

**Speed:** $|\dot{\mathbf{r}}| = \sqrt{a^2\sin^2 t + b^2\cos^2 t}$

The arc length integral $\int_0^{2\pi}|\dot{\mathbf{r}}|,dt$ is exactly the elliptic integral $4aE(e)$ above.

---

## 8. Polar Form (Focus at Pole)

With one focus at the origin:

$$r = \frac{a(1-e^2)}{1 - e\cos\theta} = \frac{b^2/a}{1 - e\cos\theta} = \frac{\ell}{1 - e\cos\theta}$$

where $\ell = \frac{b^2}{a}$ is the **semi-latus rectum**.

|$\theta$|$r$|
|---|---|
|$0$ (periapsis)|$r_{\min} = a(1-e) = a - c$|
|$\pi$ (apoapsis)|$r_{\max} = a(1+e) = a + c$|
|$\pi/2$|$r = \ell = b^2/a$|

Note: $r_{\min} + r_{\max} = 2a$, consistent with $|PF_1| + |PF_2| = 2a$ at the vertices.

## 9. Conjugate Diameters and the General Equation

### General Second-Degree Form

$$Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$$

is an ellipse when $B^2 - 4AC < 0$ (and the conic is non-degenerate).

Reduce by completing the square → shifted standard form.

### Matrix Form

$$\mathbf{x}^\top M \mathbf{x} = 1, \quad M = \begin{pmatrix} 1/a^2 & 0 \ 0 & 1/b^2 \end{pmatrix}$$

For a rotated ellipse: $M$ is a positive definite symmetric matrix. Its eigenvalues are $1/a^2$ and $1/b^2$; its eigenvectors are the axes of the ellipse.

## 10. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Find Everything from the Equation

Given $\dfrac{x^2}{25} + \dfrac{y^2}{9} = 1$.

- $a^2 = 25 \Rightarrow a = 5$ (major axis horizontal, larger denominator)
- $b^2 = 9 \Rightarrow b = 3$
- $c = \sqrt{25 - 9} = \sqrt{16} = 4$
- $e = c/a = 4/5 = 0.8$
- Foci: $(\pm 4, 0)$
- Vertices: $(\pm 5, 0)$
- Co-vertices: $(0, \pm 3)$
- Latus rectum: $2b^2/a = 18/5 = 3.6$
- Area: $\pi \cdot 5 \cdot 3 = 15\pi$

### Example 2: Find the Equation

Foci at $(0, \pm 3)$, sum of distances $= 10$.

- Foci on $y$-axis → vertical major axis
- $2a = 10 \Rightarrow a = 5$, so $a^2 = 25$
- $c = 3$, so $b^2 = a^2 - c^2 = 25 - 9 = 16$

$$\frac{x^2}{16} + \frac{y^2}{25} = 1$$

### Example 3: Shifted Ellipse

$4x^2 + 9y^2 - 16x + 18y - 11 = 0$

Complete the square:

$4(x^2 - 4x) + 9(y^2 + 2y) = 11$

$4(x-2)^2 - 16 + 9(y+1)^2 - 9 = 11$

$4(x-2)^2 + 9(y+1)^2 = 36$

$$\frac{(x-2)^2}{9} + \frac{(y+1)^2}{4} = 1$$

Center $(2, -1)$, $a = 3$, $b = 2$, $c = \sqrt{5}$, horizontal major axis.

## 12. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 12.1 Ellipsoidal Level Sets of the Gaussian Distribution

The multivariate Gaussian:

$$p(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

has **ellipsoidal level sets**. The equation $(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}) = k^2$ is the $d$-dimensional ellipsoid (ellipse in 2D) with axes aligned to the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$, with semi-axis lengths $k\sqrt{\lambda_i}$ where $\lambda_i$ are eigenvalues of $\Sigma$.

Every time you compute a Gaussian distribution — in a VAE, a Kalman filter, a Gaussian process, naive Bayes — you are computing with ellipses.

### 12.2 Covariance Ellipse and Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

PCA finds the axes of the **covariance ellipse** of the data:

$$\hat{\Sigma} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}})(\mathbf{x}_i - \bar{\mathbf{x}})^\top$$

The principal components are the eigenvectors of $\hat{\Sigma}$, and the eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \cdots$ are the **squared semi-axes** of the data's covariance ellipse. The eccentricity of this ellipse measures how much the data varies in one direction vs another — a nearly circular covariance ellipse means all directions are equally informative; a thin, elongated ellipse means the data lies nearly on a line.

Dimensionality reduction = projecting onto the longest axes of the ellipse.

### 12.3 Trust Region Methods in Optimization

**Trust region** optimization (used in second-order methods like TRPO in reinforcement learning) constrains the update step $\Delta\mathbf{w}$ to lie inside an ellipsoidal region:

$$\Delta\mathbf{w}^\top H,\Delta\mathbf{w} \leq \delta$$

where $H$ is the Hessian (or Fisher information matrix). This is an ellipsoid in parameter space — its shape is determined by the local curvature of the loss. Directions of high curvature get short semi-axes (small allowed steps); directions of low curvature get long semi-axes. The optimal step is the eigenvector of $H$ corresponding to the steepest descent direction, constrained to this ellipsoid.

**TRPO** (Trust Region Policy Optimization) uses a KL-divergence ellipsoid to constrain policy updates — the geometry is fundamentally ellipsoidal.

### 12.4 The Fisher Information Ellipse and Natural Gradient

The **Fisher information matrix** $\mathcal{F}$ defines a Riemannian metric on the parameter space of a statistical model. Its level sets are ellipses. The **natural gradient**:

$$\tilde{\nabla}\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}$$

rescales the gradient to account for the ellipsoidal geometry of the parameter space. Standard gradient descent treats parameter space as isotropic (circular level sets); natural gradient descent uses the true ellipsoidal geometry. This is why natural gradient / K-FAC methods converge faster — they respect the intrinsic ellipsoidal structure.

### 12.5 Elliptic Embeddings and Knowledge Graphs

Recent work (Vilnis et al., Box Embeddings; Peng et al., Hyperbolic Embeddings) embeds entities in ellipsoidal regions rather than points. An entity is represented as a Gaussian (equivalently, an ellipse in 2D), and entailment/subsumption is modeled as **containment of ellipses**:

$$\text{"A is-a B"} \iff \text{ellipse}_A \subset \text{ellipse}_B$$

The volume of the ellipse ($\pi ab$) encodes the generality of the concept: large ellipses = general concepts, small ellipses = specific ones.

### 12.6 Elliptical Attention and Anisotropic Kernels

Standard dot-product attention computes $\text{softmax}(\mathbf{q}^\top\mathbf{k}/\sqrt{d})$ — a spherical similarity. **Elliptical attention** generalizes this to:

$$\text{score}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}) = \exp!\left(-(\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{k})^\top M (\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{k})\right)$$

where $M$ is a learned positive definite matrix. The similarity decays along ellipsoidal contours, allowing the model to learn anisotropic feature relevance — some feature dimensions matter more than others for a given query.
