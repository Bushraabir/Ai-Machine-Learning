## 0. Why Bother? A Motivating Remark

Cartesian coordinates encode a point as a _displacement_ from an origin: how far right, how far up. This is natural when geometry is built from rectangles and grids. But many curves — spirals, roses, cardioids, limaçons — are _intrinsically radial_: they are defined by how far you are from a fixed center, and in which direction. For such curves, the Cartesian description is grotesque while the polar description is one line.

In machine learning, the same philosophy recurs constantly: the right representation collapses complexity. A kernel function, an attention mechanism, a Fourier feature map — all are attempts to find coordinates in which the problem becomes simple.

## 1. The Polar Coordinate System

**Definition.** A point $P$ in the plane is assigned **polar coordinates** $(r, \theta)$ where:

- $r$ = the _radial distance_ from the **pole** (origin) $O$ to $P$
- $\theta$ = the _polar angle_ measured counter-clockwise from the **polar axis** (positive $x$-axis)

The conversion identities are exact:

$$x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta$$

$$r^2 = x^2 + y^2, \qquad \tan\theta = \frac{y}{x}$$

**Remark on uniqueness.** Unlike Cartesian coordinates, polar representations are _not_ unique:

$$\left(r,, \theta\right) = \left(r,, \theta + 2\pi k\right) \quad \text{for any } k \in \mathbb{Z}$$

$$\left(r,, \theta\right) = \left(-r,, \theta + \pi\right)$$

This non-uniqueness is not a bug — it is the source of the richness we exploit when finding intersections of polar curves.

## 2. Polar Curves: Definition and Graphing

A **polar curve** is the set of points whose polar coordinates satisfy an equation $r = f(\theta)$, or more generally $F(r, \theta) = 0$.

### 2.1 Standard Toolkit of Polar Curves

|Curve|Equation|Description|
|---|---|---|
|Circle (centered at pole)|$r = a$|Radius $a$|
|Line through pole|$\theta = \alpha$|Angle $\alpha$|
|Circle passing through pole|$r = 2a\cos\theta$|Diameter $2a$, center $(a, 0)$|
|Cardioid|$r = a(1 + \cos\theta)$|Heart-shaped, passes through pole|
|Limaçon|$r = b + a\cos\theta$, $b \neq a$|Inner loop if $b < a$|
|Rose curve|$r = a\cos(n\theta)$|$n$ petals ($n$ odd), $2n$ petals ($n$ even)|
|Lemniscate of Bernoulli|$r^2 = a^2\cos(2\theta)$|Figure-eight|
|Archimedean spiral|$r = a\theta$|Uniform radial expansion|
|Logarithmic spiral|$r = ae^{b\theta}$|Self-similar, appears in nature|

### 2.2 Worked Example — The Cardioid $r = 1 + \cos\theta$

Build the table of values:

|$\theta$|$\cos\theta$|$r$|
|---|---|---|
|$0$|$1$|$2$|
|$\pi/3$|$1/2$|$3/2$|

|$2\pi/3$|$-1/2$|$1/2$|

**Symmetry test:** $f(-\theta) = 1 + \cos(-\theta) = 1 + \cos\theta = f(\theta)$, so the cardioid is symmetric about the polar axis (the $x$-axis). ✓

### 2.3 Worked Example — Rose Curve $r = \cos(2\theta)$

This has $n = 2$ (even), so it produces $2 \times 2 = 4$ petals. The petals appear along the lines $\theta = 0, \pi/2, \pi, 3\pi/2$.

Petal tips occur where $|r| = 1$:

- $\theta = 0$: $r = 1$ → petal along positive $x$-axis
- $\theta = \pi/2$: $r = \cos(\pi) = -1$ → $r < 0$ means the point plots in the direction $\theta = \pi/2 + \pi = 3\pi/2$ … actually let's be careful.

At $\theta = \pi/4$: $r = \cos(\pi/2) = 0$ → crosses the pole (start/end of petal).

## 3. Tangents to Polar Curves

### 3.1 Derivation

Given $r = f(\theta)$, the Cartesian parametrization is:

$$x(\theta) = f(\theta)\cos\theta, \qquad y(\theta) = f(\theta)\sin\theta$$

By the chain rule:

$$\frac{dy}{d\theta} = f'(\theta)\sin\theta + f(\theta)\cos\theta$$

$$\frac{dx}{d\theta} = f'(\theta)\cos\theta - f(\theta)\sin\theta$$

Therefore, the slope of the tangent at a point is:

$$\boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{f'(\theta)\sin\theta + f(\theta)\cos\theta}{f'(\theta)\cos\theta - f(\theta)\sin\theta}}$$

### 3.2 Horizontal and Vertical Tangents

- **Horizontal tangent**: $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} = 0$ and $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} \neq 0$
- **Vertical tangent**: $\dfrac{dx}{d\theta} = 0$ and $\dfrac{dy}{d\theta} \neq 0$

**Example.** For the cardioid $r = 1 + \cos\theta$, find horizontal tangents.

$f(\theta) = 1 + \cos\theta$, $f'(\theta) = -\sin\theta$.

$$\frac{dy}{d\theta} = -\sin\theta \cdot \sin\theta + (1+\cos\theta)\cos\theta = -\sin^2\theta + \cos\theta + \cos^2\theta$$

$$= \cos(2\theta) + \cos\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1 + \cos\theta$$

Setting equal to zero: $2\cos^2\theta + \cos\theta - 1 = 0$, which factors as $(2\cos\theta - 1)(\cos\theta + 1) = 0$.

So $\cos\theta = 1/2 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/3, 5\pi/3$ and $\cos\theta = -1 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi$ (the pole — needs separate analysis).

At $\theta = \pi/3$: $r = 3/2$, giving the Cartesian point $(3/4, 3\sqrt{3}/4)$. ✓

## 4. Area Enclosed by Polar Curves

### 4.1 The Sector Formula

The key idea: a thin "wedge" of angle $d\theta$ and radius $r$ has area $\approx \frac{1}{2}r^2, d\theta$ (the area of a circular sector with angle $d\theta$ and radius $r$).

**Theorem (Area in Polar Coordinates).** The area enclosed by $r = f(\theta)$ between $\theta = \alpha$ and $\theta = \beta$ is:

$$\boxed{A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2, d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta [f(\theta)]^2, d\theta}$$

**Proof sketch.** Partition $[\alpha, \beta]$ into $n$ subintervals. On the $i$-th subinterval $[\theta_{i-1}, \theta_i]$, approximate the region by a circular sector of radius $f(\theta_i^_)$ and angle $\Delta\theta_i$. Its area is $\frac{1}{2}[f(\theta_i^_)]^2 \Delta\theta_i$. Summing and taking $n \to \infty$ gives the integral. $\square$

### 4.2 Example: Area Inside the Cardioid $r = 1 + \cos\theta$

By symmetry (symmetric about $x$-axis), compute the upper half and double:

$$A = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \int_0^\pi (1 + \cos\theta)^2, d\theta = \int_0^\pi (1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta), d\theta$$

Use $\cos^2\theta = \frac{1 + \cos(2\theta)}{2}$:

$$= \int_0^\pi \left(1 + 2\cos\theta + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\cos(2\theta)}{2}\right)d\theta = \int_0^\pi \left(\frac{3}{2} + 2\cos\theta + \frac{\cos(2\theta)}{2}\right)d\theta$$

$$= \left[\frac{3\theta}{2} + 2\sin\theta + \frac{\sin(2\theta)}{4}\right]_0^\pi = \frac{3\pi}{2} + 0 + 0 = \frac{3\pi}{2}$$

### 4.3 Area Between Two Polar Curves

If $f(\theta) \geq g(\theta) \geq 0$ on $[\alpha, \beta]$, the area between $r = f(\theta)$ (outer) and $r = g(\theta)$ (inner) is:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \left([f(\theta)]^2 - [g(\theta)]^2\right)d\theta$$

**Example.** Area inside $r = 3\sin\theta$ and outside $r = 1 + \sin\theta$.

First, find intersections: $3\sin\theta = 1 + \sin\theta \Rightarrow \sin\theta = 1/2 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/6, 5\pi/6$.

On $[\pi/6, 5\pi/6]$, $3\sin\theta \geq 1 + \sin\theta$. By symmetry:

$$A = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2}\left[(3\sin\theta)^2 - (1+\sin\theta)^2\right]d\theta$$

$$= \int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2}\left[9\sin^2\theta - 1 - 2\sin\theta - \sin^2\theta\right]d\theta = \int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2}\left[8\sin^2\theta - 2\sin\theta - 1\right]d\theta$$

Expand $8\sin^2\theta = 4(1-\cos 2\theta)$:

$$= \int_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2}[3 - 4\cos(2\theta) - 2\sin\theta], d\theta = \left[3\theta - 2\sin(2\theta) + 2\cos\theta\right]_{\pi/6}^{\pi/2}$$

$$= \left(\frac{3\pi}{2} - 0 + 0\right) - \left(\frac{\pi}{2} - \sqrt{3} + \sqrt{3}\right) = \pi$$

## 5. Arc Length of a Polar Curve

### 5.1 Formula

Starting from the general arc-length formula $L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{d\theta}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{d\theta}\right)^2}, d\theta$, compute:

$$\left(\frac{dx}{d\theta}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{d\theta}\right)^2 = (f')^2\cos^2\theta - 2ff'\sin\theta\cos\theta + f^2\sin^2\theta$$ $$\quad + (f')^2\sin^2\theta + 2ff'\sin\theta\cos\theta + f^2\cos^2\theta$$ $$= (f')^2 + f^2$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2}, d\theta}$$

**Example.** Arc length of the cardioid $r = a(1 + \cos\theta)$:

$dr/d\theta = -a\sin\theta$.

$$r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2 = a^2(1+\cos\theta)^2 + a^2\sin^2\theta = a^2(1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta)$$ $$= a^2(2 + 2\cos\theta) = 4a^2\cos^2(\theta/2)$$

using the half-angle identity $1 + \cos\theta = 2\cos^2(\theta/2)$.

$$L = \int_0^{2\pi} 2a\left|\cos(\theta/2)\right|, d\theta = 4a\int_0^\pi \cos(\theta/2), d\theta = 4a\left[2\sin(\theta/2)\right]_0^\pi = 8a$$

## 6. Intersection of Polar Curves

This is where most students get burned. The issue is **non-uniqueness of representation**.

**To find all intersections of $r = f(\theta)$ and $r = g(\theta)$:**

**Step 1.** Solve $f(\theta) = g(\theta)$ simultaneously. These give "genuine" intersections.

**Step 2.** Check whether the pole lies on both curves: $f(\theta_1) = 0$ for some $\theta_1$, and $g(\theta_2) = 0$ for some $\theta_2$. If so, the pole is an intersection point (even if $\theta_1 \neq \theta_2$).

**Step 3.** Check equivalent representations. If $r = f(\theta)$, also check $r = f(\theta + 2\pi k)$ and $-r = f(\theta + \pi)$ against $g$.

**Example.** Find all intersections of $r = \cos\theta$ and $r = 1 - \cos\theta$.

Step 1: $\cos\theta = 1 - \cos\theta \Rightarrow 2\cos\theta = 1 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/3, 5\pi/3$.

Points: $r = 1/2$ at $\theta = \pi/3$: Cartesian $(1/4, \sqrt{3}/4)$ and at $\theta = 5\pi/3$: $(1/4, -\sqrt{3}/4)$.

Step 2: $r = \cos\theta = 0$ when $\theta = \pi/2$. $r = 1 - \cos(\pi/2) = 1 \neq 0$. But $r = 1 - \cos\theta = 0$ when $\theta = 0$, and $r = \cos(0) = 1 \neq 0$. The pole is NOT on $r = 1 - \cos\theta$... actually wait: $1 - \cos\theta = 0 \Rightarrow \theta = 0$, so the pole lies on $r = 1 - \cos\theta$ (at $\theta = 0$). And $r = \cos(\pi/2) = 0$, so the pole lies on $r = \cos\theta$ (at $\theta = \pi/2$). Hence the **pole** is also an intersection.

Total: 3 intersection points.

## 8. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

Polar coordinates and polar curve theory connect to modern ML in several deep and concrete ways. This is not superficial analogy — these are genuine mathematical bridges.

### 8.1 Radial Basis Functions (RBFs) and Kernel Methods

A **radial basis function** $\phi(x) = \phi(|x|)$ depends only on the distance from a center — it is constant on spheres, which are the "circles" of Euclidean space. This is exactly the polar symmetry $r = \text{const}$.

The **Gaussian RBF kernel** $k(x, x') = e^{-|x - x'|^2 / 2\sigma^2}$ is radially symmetric. In polar-like coordinates centered at $x'$, it becomes $k = e^{-r^2/2\sigma^2}$, a function of $r$ alone — identical in structure to polar curves like $r = e^{-a\theta^2}$.

SVM with RBF kernels implicitly map data into an infinite-dimensional feature space where linearly inseparable data (a donut-shaped class, say) becomes separable. The donut has polar equation $r_1 \leq r \leq r_2$ — it is a polar region.

### 8.2 Fourier Features and Periodic Representations

The rose curve $r = \cos(n\theta)$ is built from $\cos(n\theta)$ — a **Fourier basis function**. In **Random Fourier Features** (Rahimi & Recht, 2007), RBF kernels are approximated by random projections of the form $z(x) = \cos(\omega^T x + b)$ where $\omega$ is sampled from a specific distribution. These feature maps live naturally in polar/angular space.

**Positional encodings** in Transformers (e.g., $\sin(k / 10000^{2i/d})$, $\cos(k / 10000^{2i/d})$) are essentially polar angle encodings applied to the token index $k$. The self-attention mechanism computes $Q K^T$, and the quality of this dot product depends critically on how angles are preserved — RoPE (Rotary Position Embedding) makes this explicit by applying a rotation matrix in 2D blocks, i.e., a rotation in polar coordinates.

### 8.3 Loss Landscape Geometry and Angular Structure

The **loss landscape** $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ of a neural network is a high-dimensional scalar field. Understanding its critical points (saddles, minima) requires analyzing the directional behavior of $\mathcal{L}$ — in polar coordinates centered at a critical point, this means studying $\mathcal{L}(r, \hat{\theta})$ as $r \to 0$ from different directions $\hat{\theta}$.

The **sharpness** of a minimum (related to the Hessian's largest eigenvalue) corresponds to the maximum of $\partial^2\mathcal{L}/\partial r^2$ over all directions $\hat{\theta}$. Flat minima (small sharpness) generalize better — this is the content of SAM (Sharpness-Aware Minimization).

### 8.4 Circular/Torus Data Manifolds

In data with periodic structure — time-of-day features, molecular bond angles, protein dihedral angles, directions in 2D space — the natural representation is **angular**: the data lives on a circle $S^1$ or torus $S^1 \times S^1$, described by polar/toroidal coordinates.

**von Mises distribution** is the analogue of the Gaussian for circular data: $p(\theta) \propto e^{\kappa \cos(\theta - \mu)}$. Neural networks for protein structure prediction (AlphaFold) must handle such circular outputs — a naive MSE loss on angles is metrically wrong (the angle 359° is close to 1°, but their difference is 358°).

### 8.5 Equivariant Networks and Rotational Symmetry

A CNN that must be equivariant to rotation is most naturally designed in polar coordinates. **Steerable CNNs** and **E(2)-equivariant networks** decompose feature maps into irreducible representations of SO(2) — i.e., into Fourier modes $e^{in\theta}$, the complex version of polar rose harmonics. The $n$-petal rose $r = \cos(n\theta)$ is the real part of the $n$-th Fourier mode.

### 8.6 Summary Table

|ML Concept|Polar Connection|
|---|---|
|RBF Kernel / SVM|Radial symmetry $r = f(\|x\|)$|
|Random Fourier Features|Cosine modes $\cos(n\theta)$|
|Rotary Positional Embedding (RoPE)|2D rotation in polar form|
|Protein angle regression|Circular statistics on $S^1$|

|E(2)-equivariant CNNs|SO(2) Fourier decomposition|

|Quantity|Formula|
|---|---|
|Cartesian from polar|$x = r\cos\theta,; y = r\sin\theta$|
|Polar from Cartesian|$r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2},; \tan\theta = y/x$|
|Slope of tangent|$\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \dfrac{r'\sin\theta + r\cos\theta}{r'\cos\theta - r\sin\theta}$|
