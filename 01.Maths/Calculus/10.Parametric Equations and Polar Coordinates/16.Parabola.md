

> _Every parabola is the same parabola — just scaled and rotated. This single geometric fact, once internalized, unlocks the whole theory._

---

## 1. The Geometric Definition (Start Here, Always)

> **Definition.** A _parabola_ is the set of all points in the plane equidistant from a fixed point $F$ (the **focus**) and a fixed line $\ell$ (the **directrix**).

$$\text{Parabola} = {, P \in \mathbb{R}^2 : |PF| = d(P, \ell) ,}$$

This is the _true_ definition — not "$y = x^2$". The algebraic form is a _consequence_.

**The vertex** is the unique point on the parabola closest to both focus and directrix simultaneously. By symmetry it lies exactly halfway between them.

```
          Focus F
            *
            |  ← p (focal distance)
            |
  ──────────V────────── ← Vertex
            |
            |  ← p
  ──────────────────── ← Directrix ℓ
```

The distance $p > 0$ from vertex to focus (= vertex to directrix) is called the **focal parameter** or **focal length**.

---

## 2. Deriving the Equation from the Definition

Place the vertex at the origin, focus at $F = (0, p)$, directrix $y = -p$.

Let $P = (x, y)$ be any point on the parabola. Then:

$$|PF| = d(P, \ell)$$

$$\sqrt{x^2 + (y-p)^2} = |y + p|$$

Square both sides (both sides non-negative for $y \geq -p$):

$$x^2 + (y-p)^2 = (y+p)^2$$

$$x^2 + y^2 - 2py + p^2 = y^2 + 2py + p^2$$

$$x^2 = 4py$$

$$\boxed{x^2 = 4py}$$

This is the **standard form**. Every coefficient is geometrically meaningful: $4p$ encodes the focal length.

**Four standard orientations:**

|Opens|Equation|Focus|Directrix|
|---|---|---|---|
|Up|$x^2 = 4py$, $p>0$|$(0, p)$|$y = -p$|
|Down|$x^2 = -4py$, $p>0$|$(0, -p)$|$y = p$|
|Right|$y^2 = 4px$, $p>0$|$(p, 0)$|$x = -p$|
|Left|$y^2 = -4px$, $p>0$|$(-p, 0)$|$x = p$|

## 3. The General Second-Degree Parabola

The general conic:

$$Ax^2 + Bxy + Cy^2 + Dx + Ey + F = 0$$

is a parabola when the **discriminant** $B^2 - 4AC = 0$.

When $B = 0$ (axis-aligned), the conditions reduce to: exactly one of $A, C$ is zero.

**Completing the square** reduces any axis-aligned parabola to standard form. Example:

$$y = 2x^2 - 8x + 5$$

$$= 2(x^2 - 4x) + 5 = 2(x^2 - 4x + 4 - 4) + 5 = 2(x-2)^2 - 3$$

Vertex at $(2, -3)$, opens up, $4p = \tfrac{1}{2} \Rightarrow p = \tfrac{1}{8}$.

## 4. Key Geometric Properties

### 4.1 The Latus Rectum

The **latus rectum** is the chord through the focus perpendicular to the axis of symmetry. For $x^2 = 4py$:

At $y = p$: $x^2 = 4p^2 \Rightarrow x = \pm 2p$.

$$\text{Length of latus rectum} = 4p$$

This is the natural "width" of the parabola at focus level — a clean intrinsic measurement.

### 4.2 Reflective Property

> **Theorem.** Any ray parallel to the axis of a parabola, upon reflection off the parabola, passes through the focus.

_Proof sketch._ At a point $P = (x_0, y_0)$ on $x^2 = 4py$, the tangent line has slope $\frac{dy}{dx}\big|_{x_0} = \frac{x_0}{2p}$. The normal bisects the angle between the incoming ray (parallel to the $y$-axis) and the line $PF$. One verifies by direct computation that the angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection with respect to this normal. $\square$

This is why parabolic mirrors focus parallel light rays (telescopes, satellite dishes) and parabolic reflectors broadcast a point source as parallel rays (flashlights, headlights).

```
  Incoming rays (parallel to axis)
   ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓  ↓
   ↘  ↘  ↘  ↘  ↘
    \  \  |  /  /       ← parabola
     \  \ | / /
      \   F   /         ← all rays converge at F
```

### 4.3 Tangent Line at $(x_0, y_0)$

For $x^2 = 4py$, differentiating implicitly: $2x = 4py' \Rightarrow y' = \frac{x}{2p}$.

Tangent at $(x_0, y_0)$:

$$y - y_0 = \frac{x_0}{2p}(x - x_0)$$

Since $x_0^2 = 4py_0$, this simplifies to:

$$xx_0 = 2p(y + y_0)$$

A beautiful symmetric form — $x$ and $x_0$ appear symmetrically on the left.

## 5. Parabola as a Conic Section

A parabola arises when a plane cuts a (double-napped) cone **parallel to exactly one generator** (ruling line).

```
        /\
       /  \
      / ·· \     ← plane cuts parallel to one side
     /  ··  \
    /___··___\
       (parabola)
```

The three conic sections and their discriminant conditions ($B^2 - 4AC$):

|Conic|Discriminant|Eccentricity|
|---|---|---|
|Ellipse (incl. circle)|$< 0$|$e < 1$|
|**Parabola**|$= 0$|$e = 1$|
|Hyperbola|$> 0$|$e > 1$|

The parabola is the **boundary case** $e = 1$: one focus, one directrix, and the definition of distance-from-focus $=$ distance-from-directrix is exactly eccentricity $1$.

## 6. Parametric Form

The natural parametrization of $x^2 = 4py$:

$$x = 2pt, \quad y = pt^2, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

Verify: $(2pt)^2 = 4p^2t^2 = 4p(pt^2)$. ✓

Velocity vector: $\dot{\mathbf{r}} = (2p,, 2pt)$. At $t=0$ (vertex), $\dot{\mathbf{r}} = (2p, 0)$ — horizontal tangent, as expected.

Curvature at the vertex ($t = 0$):

$$\kappa = \frac{|\dot{x}\ddot{y} - \dot{y}\ddot{x}|}{(\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{|2p \cdot 2p - 0|}{(4p^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{4p^2}{8p^3} = \frac{1}{2p}$$

So the **radius of curvature at the vertex** is $\rho = 2p$ — the diameter of the osculating circle equals the latus rectum length. Another clean intrinsic fact.

## 7. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Find Focus and Directrix

Given $y = -\frac{1}{12}x^2 + 3$.

Rewrite: $x^2 = -12(y - 3)$.

Compare with $x^2 = -4p(y - k)$: $4p = 12 \Rightarrow p = 3$.

- Vertex: $(0, 3)$
- Opens **down**
- Focus: $(0, 3 - 3) = (0, 0)$
- Directrix: $y = 3 + 3 = 6$

### Example 2: Find the Equation

Focus $F = (2, 3)$, directrix $x = -4$.

The axis is horizontal (focus and directrix are separated horizontally). Vertex is halfway: $x_V = \frac{2 + (-4)}{2} = -1$, $y_V = 3$.

Focal length $p = 2 - (-1) = 3$ (opens right).

$$\boxed{(y - 3)^2 = 12(x + 1)}$$

### Example 3: Arc Length of $y = x^2$ from $x=0$ to $x=1$

$$L = \int_0^1 \sqrt{1 + (y')^2},dx = \int_0^1\sqrt{1 + 4x^2},dx$$

Let $x = \frac{1}{2}\sinh t$, $dx = \frac{1}{2}\cosh t,dt$, $\sqrt{1+4x^2} = \cosh t$:

$$L = \int_0^{\sinh^{-1}(2)}\cosh^2 t,\frac{dt}{2} = \frac{1}{4}\left[\sinh t\cosh t + t\right]_0^{\sinh^{-1}(2)}$$

Numerically: $L \approx 1.4789$.

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 9.1 Quadratic Loss and Parabolic Geometry

The **mean squared error (MSE) loss** for a single parameter $w$:

$$\mathcal{L}(w) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - wx_i)^2$$

is a parabola (or paraboloid in higher dimensions) in $w$. This is not a coincidence — the square loss is the _unique_ loss (up to scaling) whose level sets are spheres and whose geometry is Euclidean. The vertex of the parabola is the **global minimum**, and gradient descent on a parabola converges in one step with the right learning rate.

For the 1D case with exact solution $w^* = \frac{\sum x_i y_i}{\sum x_i^2}$, the loss surface is:

$$\mathcal{L}(w) = \mathcal{L}(w^_) + \frac{\sum x_i^2}{n}(w - w^_)^2$$

Literally a parabola with vertex at the optimum. The coefficient $\frac{\sum x_i^2}{n}$ (the curvature) determines how sharply the loss rises away from the minimum — this is the **Hessian** in 1D.

### 9.2 The Hessian and Paraboloid Approximations

The **second-order Taylor approximation** of any smooth loss $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})$ around a point $\mathbf{w}_0$ is:

$$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}) \approx \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}_0) + \nabla\mathcal{L}^\top\Delta\mathbf{w} + \frac{1}{2}\Delta\mathbf{w}^\top H,\Delta\mathbf{w}$$

where $H = \nabla^2\mathcal{L}$ is the Hessian. The last term is a **paraboloid** — the $n$-dimensional analog of a parabola. Newton's method exploits this: it finds the vertex of the local paraboloid approximation in one step:

$$\mathbf{w}_{\text{new}} = \mathbf{w}_0 - H^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}$$

All second-order optimization methods (L-BFGS, natural gradient, Adam to some degree) are approximations to navigating parabolic loss geometry.

### 9.3 SVM and Quadratic Programming

The hard-margin **Support Vector Machine** dual problem is:

$$\max_{\boldsymbol{\alpha}} \sum_i \alpha_i - \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i,j}\alpha_i\alpha_j y_i y_j K(\mathbf{x}_i, \mathbf{x}_j)$$

subject to $\alpha_i \geq 0$, $\sum_i \alpha_i y_i = 0$.

The objective is a **quadratic** (paraboloid) in $\boldsymbol{\alpha}$. The feasible set is a convex polytope. Finding the SVM solution means finding the vertex of a paraboloid above a polytope — a quadratic programming problem. The geometry of the parabola (unique minimum, no local minima) guarantees the solution is unique.

### 9.4 Parabolic Activation Functions and Squaring Operations

The **squared activation** $\sigma(x) = x^2$ (and related monomials) appears in:

- **Polynomial networks** and tensor decomposition
- **Attention mechanisms**: the dot-product $\mathbf{q}^\top\mathbf{k}$ is linear, but before softmax it creates a quadratic scoring surface
- **Quadratic neurons** (recent research): neurons computing $(\mathbf{w}_1^\top\mathbf{x})(\mathbf{w}_2^\top\mathbf{x})$ whose composition creates parabolic response surfaces

The **variance** of activations: $\text{Var}(wx) = w^2\text{Var}(x)$ — the dependence on $w^2$ (not $w$) means weight initialization schemes (He, Xavier) must account for the squaring. The safe initialization radius in weight space is the _focus_ of a related parabola in the variance-vs-depth relationship.

### 9.5 Parabolic Learning Rate Schedules

Several learning rate annealing strategies follow parabolic curves. The **cosine annealing with warm restarts** is approximately parabolic near its minima. The **polynomial decay** schedule:

$$\eta_t = \eta_0\left(1 - \frac{t}{T}\right)^k$$

with $k=2$ is literally parabolic decay. The rationale: the loss surface near the optimum is parabolic (as in §9.1), so a learning rate that decays quadratically matches the curvature of the landscape near convergence.

### 9.6 Paraboloids in Gaussian Processes

A **Gaussian process** with squared-exponential kernel has log-likelihood whose dependence on the length-scale hyperparameter $\ell$ is approximately parabolic near its optimum. Marginal likelihood maximization (type-II maximum likelihood) thus looks like finding the vertex of a paraboloid in hyperparameter space — again a parabolic optimization problem.
