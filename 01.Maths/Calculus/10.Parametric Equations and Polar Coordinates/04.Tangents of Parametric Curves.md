

> _"A curve does not know it is parametric — but we do, and that changes everything."_

---

## §0 — Motivation & Setup

In standard Cartesian calculus, a curve is a graph $y = f(x)$. The tangent slope is $f'(x)$ — clean and immediate.

But reality is richer. A particle tracing an ellipse, a robot arm sweeping an arc, a neural network's loss surface following a trajectory in weight space — none of these come with $y$ written neatly as a function of $x$. They come **parametrically**:

$$ x = f(t), \quad y = g(t), \quad t \in [a, b] $$

Here $t$ is the **parameter** — often time, but need not be. The curve is the image of the map $t \mapsto (f(t), g(t))$ in the plane.

**The central question:** What is the slope of the tangent line at a point on such a curve?

---

## §1 — Deriving the Tangent Formula

### 1.1 The Core Idea: Chain Rule in Disguise

Suppose $y$ is (locally) expressible as a function of $x$, i.e., $y = F(x)$ near some point. Then by the **Chain Rule**:

$$ \frac{dy}{dt} = \frac{dy}{dx} \cdot \frac{dx}{dt} $$

Solving for the quantity we want:

$$ \boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{,dy/dt,}{dx/dt} = \frac{g'(t)}{f'(t)}, \quad \text{provided } f'(t) \neq 0} $$

> **Remark (Implicit Function Theorem backbone).** The assumption that $y = F(x)$ exists locally is guaranteed whenever $f'(t) \neq 0$ — by the Implicit Function Theorem, the map $t \mapsto x = f(t)$ is locally invertible, so $t = f^{-1}(x)$ exists and $y = g(f^{-1}(x))$.

This is not just symbol manipulation. We are asserting that _locally_, the parametric curve looks like a regular graph.

### 1.2 Tangent Line at a Point

At parameter value $t = t_0$, the point on the curve is $(x_0, y_0) = (f(t_0),, g(t_0))$.

The **tangent line** is:

$$ y - g(t_0) = \frac{g'(t_0)}{f'(t_0)},(x - f(t_0)) $$

---

## §2 — Second Derivatives and Concavity

To understand _concavity_, we need $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$. One might guess it's $g''(t)/f''(t)$ — **this is wrong.** Here's the correct derivation.

We treat $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ as a new function of $t$, say $h(t) = \dfrac{g'(t)}{f'(t)}$. Then:

$$ \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{d}{dx}!\left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = \frac{\dfrac{d}{dt}!\left(\dfrac{dy}{dx}\right)}{\dfrac{dx}{dt}} $$

Substituting:

$$ \boxed{\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{\dfrac{d}{dt}!\left[\dfrac{g'(t)}{f'(t)}\right]}{f'(t)}} $$

> **Warning.** Notice: $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2} \neq \dfrac{g''(t)}{f''(t)}$. The denominator involves $f'(t)$, not $f''(t)$.

## §3 — Special Cases: Vertical & Horizontal Tangents

|Condition|Geometric Meaning|
|---|---|
|$g'(t_0) = 0,\ f'(t_0) \neq 0$|**Horizontal tangent** at $(f(t_0), g(t_0))$|
|$f'(t_0) = 0,\ g'(t_0) \neq 0$|**Vertical tangent** at $(f(t_0), g(t_0))$|
|$f'(t_0) = 0,\ g'(t_0) = 0$|**Singular point** — need L'Hôpital or higher-order analysis|

## §4 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — The Cycloid

A circle of radius $r$ rolls along the $x$-axis. A point on its rim traces a **cycloid**:

$$ x = r(t - \sin t), \quad y = r(1 - \cos t) $$

**Derivatives:** $$ \frac{dx}{dt} = r(1 - \cos t), \qquad \frac{dy}{dt} = r \sin t $$

**Slope:** $$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{r\sin t}{r(1 - \cos t)} = \frac{\sin t}{1 - \cos t} $$

Using the half-angle identities $\sin t = 2\sin(t/2)\cos(t/2)$ and $1 - \cos t = 2\sin^2(t/2)$:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{2\sin(t/2)\cos(t/2)}{2\sin^2(t/2)} = \cot!\left(\frac{t}{2}\right) $$

**Special points:**

- At $t = \pi$ (top of arch): $\cot(\pi/2) = 0$ → **horizontal tangent** ✓
- As $t \to 0^+$ or $t \to 2\pi^-$ (cusps on $x$-axis): $\cot(t/2) \to \infty$ → **vertical tangent** ✓ (the curve has cusps where it touches the ground)

---

### Example 2 — Ellipse

$$ x = a\cos t, \quad y = b\sin t \quad (a, b > 0) $$

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{b\cos t}{-a\sin t} = -\frac{b}{a}\cot t $$

At $t = \pi/4$: slope $= -b/a$, point $= (a/\sqrt{2},, b/\sqrt{2})$.

Tangent line: $$ y - \frac{b}{\sqrt{2}} = -\frac{b}{a}\left(x - \frac{a}{\sqrt{2}}\right) \implies \frac{x}{a\sqrt{2}} + \frac{y}{b\sqrt{2}} = 1 $$

This is the classical tangent-to-ellipse formula, recovered cleanly.

---

### Example 3 — Lissajous Figure (AI-relevant)

$$ x = \sin(2t), \quad y = \sin(3t) $$

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3\cos(3t)}{2\cos(2t)} $$

Horizontal tangents: $\cos(3t) = 0 \Rightarrow t = \pi/6, \pi/2, 5\pi/6, \ldots$

Vertical tangents: $\cos(2t) = 0 \Rightarrow t = \pi/4, 3\pi/4, \ldots$

These figures appear in signal processing and are related to **Fourier analysis** — the backbone of transformers' positional encodings.

## §6 — The Tangent Vector Perspective

There is a deeper, coordinate-free way to see this. The **velocity vector** (tangent vector) at parameter $t$ is:

$$ \mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle f'(t),, g'(t) \rangle $$

This vector is **tangent to the curve** at the point $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle f(t), g(t) \rangle$.

The slope $dy/dx$ is simply the **ratio of the components** of this tangent vector:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{g'(t)}{f'(t)} = \frac{[\mathbf{r}'(t)]_y}{[\mathbf{r}'(t)]_x} $$

The **unit tangent vector** is:

$$ \hat{\mathbf{T}}(t) = \frac{\mathbf{r}'(t)}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|} = \frac{\langle f'(t), g'(t) \rangle}{\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}} $$

> **Key Insight.** The slope $dy/dx$ encodes the _direction_ of $\mathbf{r}'(t)$, but loses magnitude (speed). The full tangent vector carries both direction and speed — crucial in physics and in optimization.

---

## §7 — Singular Points & L'Hôpital

When $f'(t_0) = g'(t_0) = 0$, the formula $g'(t)/f'(t)$ is a $0/0$ indeterminate form. We apply **L'Hôpital's Rule**:

$$ \lim_{t \to t_0} \frac{g'(t)}{f'(t)} = \lim_{t \to t_0} \frac{g''(t)}{f''(t)} \quad \text{(if this limit exists)} $$

**Example.** The _cusp_ of $x = t^2,\ y = t^3$ at $t = 0$:

$$ \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{3t^2}{2t} = \frac{3t}{2} \xrightarrow{t \to 0} 0 $$

Slope approaches $0$, confirming a **cusp with horizontal tangent direction** (but not a smooth tangent — the curve reverses direction).

## §8 — Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

This is where parametric curves cease to be a calculus curiosity and become **foundational infrastructure**.

### 8.1 Gradient Descent as a Parametric Curve

Training a neural network is **trajectory tracing** in parameter space. Let $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be the weight vector. Gradient descent defines:

$$ \theta(t+1) = \theta(t) - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta(t)) $$

In continuous time (gradient flow):

$$ \frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta) $$

This is a **parametric curve** $t \mapsto \theta(t)$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. The **tangent vector** at each point is exactly the negative gradient:

$$ \theta'(t) = -\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta(t)) $$

The geometry of this curve — how it turns, where it slows, what tangent directions it follows — determines convergence behavior.

### 8.2 Neural ODEs

**Neural ODEs** (Chen et al., 2018) make this explicit: the hidden state of a "network" is a parametric curve satisfying:

$$ \frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}(t), t) $$

The tangent vector $\mathbf{h}'(t)$ is _learned_. Understanding $dy/dx$ in parametric form is the 2D toy version of understanding $d\mathbf{h}/dt$ in high-dimensional feature space.

### 8.3 Bezier Curves in Generative Models

**Bezier curves** are parametric curves defined by control points:

$$ \mathbf{B}(t) = \sum_{k=0}^n \binom{n}{k}(1-t)^{n-k}t^k,\mathbf{P}_k, \quad t \in [0,1] $$

Their tangent at $t=0$ is $n(\mathbf{P}_1 - \mathbf{P}_0)$, at $t=1$ is $n(\mathbf{P}_n - \mathbf{P}_{n-1})$.

These appear in:

- **Font rendering** (TrueType/OpenType paths)
- **Diffusion model** trajectory interpolation
- **NeRF** (Neural Radiance Fields) camera path planning
- **Latent space traversal** in VAEs and GANs

### 8.4 Positional Encodings & Lissajous-Like Structure

Transformer **positional encodings** use: $$ \text{PE}(pos, 2i) = \sin!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \quad \text{PE}(pos, 2i+1) = \cos!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right) $$

Each position traces a parametric curve on the unit circle in each 2D frequency subspace. The **relative angle** between two positions is captured in the inner product — a fact proved using the parametric structure. This is exactly the ellipse/Lissajous machinery.

### 8.5 Curvature and Loss Landscape Sharpness

The **curvature** of the optimization trajectory is related to the **sharpness** of the loss landscape. Sharp minima (high curvature) generalize poorly; flat minima (low curvature) generalize well. Measuring curvature of the training trajectory in weight space uses the second-derivative formula:

$$ \kappa = \frac{|f'g'' - g'f''|}{(f'^2 + g'^2)^{3/2}} $$

In high dimensions, this generalizes to the **Hessian spectrum** — the subject of modern work on loss landscape geometry.

## §9 — Rigorous Summary

> **Theorem.** Let $\mathbf{r}(t) = (f(t), g(t))$ be a parametric curve where $f, g$ are differentiable on $(a,b)$.
> 
> (i) If $f'(t_0) \neq 0$, then $\dfrac{dy}{dx}\Big|_{t=t_0} = \dfrac{g'(t_0)}{f'(t_0)}$.
> 
> (ii) If $f'(t_0) \neq 0$ and $f, g$ are twice differentiable, then $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}\Bigg|_{t=t_0} = \frac{\left[\dfrac{d}{dt}!\left(\dfrac{g'}{f'}\right)\right]_{t=t_0}}{f'(t_0)}$$
> 
> (iii) The curve has a **horizontal tangent** at $t_0$ iff $g'(t_0) = 0 \neq f'(t_0)$, and a **vertical tangent** iff $f'(t_0) = 0 \neq g'(t_0)$.
