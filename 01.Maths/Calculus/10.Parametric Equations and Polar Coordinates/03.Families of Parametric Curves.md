## 0. Preliminary Remarks

A **family of curves** arises when a parametric curve depends not only on a parameter $t$ (which traces the curve) but also on one or more **constants** вҖ” call them $c$, $a$, $k$, $n$, etc. вҖ” whose different values generate an entire collection of related curves.

The distinction is crucial and worth stating precisely from the start:

|Symbol|Role|What changes when it varies|
|---|---|---|
|$t$|**Tracing parameter**|Which point on a fixed curve|
|$c, a, k, \ldots$|**Family parameter**|Which curve in the family|

Families appear everywhere: in physics (orbits with different energies), in geometry (spirals with different growth rates), in engineering (Lissajous figures with different frequency ratios), and in machine learning (curves traced by training dynamics under different hyperparameters).

The deep mathematical question is: **what geometric property is shared across a family, and what changes?** Answering this requires studying how the curve morphs as the family parameter varies вҖ” which is calculus in disguise.

---

## 1. Formal Definition

> **Definition 1.1 (Family of Parametric Curves).** A _one-parameter family of curves_ is a map $$F: I \times J \to \mathbb{R}^2, \quad (t, c) \mapsto (x(t,c),, y(t,c)),$$ where $I$ is the tracing interval (in $t$), $J$ is the family interval (in $c$), and $x, y$ are continuous functions of both variables. For each fixed $c \in J$, the curve $$\gamma_c: I \to \mathbb{R}^2, \quad t \mapsto (x(t,c), y(t,c))$$ is called a _member_ of the family.

A **two-parameter family** allows both $c_1$ and $c_2$ to vary, generating a two-dimensional collection of curves. In general, an $n$-parameter family fills an $n$-dimensional space of curves.

> **Remark.** In the [[PredatorвҖ“Prey System]], the level sets of the conserved quantity $H(x,y) = k$ form a **one-parameter family** of closed orbits, indexed by $k > H(E^*)$. This is the standard way families arise from conservation laws.

All members are geometrically similar вҖ” same shape, different scale. This is the behavior when the family parameter appears **multiplicatively** (pure scaling).

**Key observation.** The number of **horizontal tangencies** is $\omega$ and the number of **vertical tangencies** is $\omega - 1$ (for $\delta = 0$, integer $\omega$). This is how engineers read $\omega$ from an oscilloscope: count the tangencies.

**When does the curve close?** The curve $(\cos(\omega t + \delta), \sin t)$ is periodic iff $\omega$ is rational. If $\omega = p/q$ in lowest terms, the curve closes after $t = 2\pi q$ and has $p$ vertical loops and $q$ horizontal loops.

> **Proof.** The curve returns to its initial position when $t \mapsto t + T$ satisfies $\sin t = \sin(t+T)$ and $\cos(\omega t) = \cos(\omega(t+T))$. The first requires $T = 2\pi k$ for integer $k$; the second requires $\omega T = 2\pi m$ for integer $m$. So $\omega = m/k \in \mathbb{Q}$. The minimal period is $T = 2\pi q$ where $\omega = p/q$ in lowest terms. $\blacksquare$

**Irrational $\omega$:** The curve never closes and is **dense** in the rectangle $[-1,1]^2$ вҖ” it fills the rectangle in the limit (Kronecker's theorem / equidistribution).

As $\delta$ increases from $0$ to $\pi/2$, the figure-8 **rotates continuously** from vertical to horizontal. The family ${\gamma_\delta}$ is a **continuous deformation** вҖ” a homotopy вҖ” of curves.

> **Definition 4.1 (Homotopy of Curves).** Two curves $\gamma_0$ and $\gamma_1$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ are _homotopic_ if there exists a continuous family ${\gamma_c}_{c \in [0,1]}$ connecting them вҖ” i.e., a continuous map $F: [a,b] \times [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}^2$ with $F(\cdot, 0) = \gamma_0$ and $F(\cdot, 1) = \gamma_1$.

Families of parametric curves **are** homotopies. Phase-shifting deformations are among the most natural examples.

### 5.2 Logarithmic (Equiangular) Spiral

$$x(t, b) = e^{bt}\cos t, \quad y(t, b) = e^{bt}\sin t, \quad t \in \mathbb{R},\ b > 0.$$

The family parameter $b$ controls the **tightness** of the spiral:

- $b$ large: fast expansion (loose spiral)
- $b$ small: slow expansion (tight spiral)
- $b \to 0$: circle

```
  b = 0.1 (tight):     b = 0.3 (medium):    b = 0.7 (loose):
      в•ӯв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв•®              в•ӯв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв•®               в•ӯв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв”Җв•®
    в•ӯв”Җв•Ҝ  в•ӯв”Җв”Җв•®в•°в”Җв•®          в•ӯв”Җв•Ҝ в•ӯв”Җв•® в•°в”Җв•®           в•ӯв”Җв•Ҝ    в•ӯв”Җв•®    в•°в”Җв”Җв•®
   в”Җв•Ҝ   в”Җв•Ҝ в•°в”Җ в•°в”Җ          в•Ҝ  в”Җв•Ҝ  в•°в”Җ  в•°           в•Ҝ      в•°в”Җв•Ҝ       в•°в”Җ
```

**Self-similarity:** The logarithmic spiral looks the same at every scale вҖ” it is **scale-invariant**. Formally, $\gamma_{b}(t + t_0) = e^{bt_0} \gamma_b(t)$ вҖ” a shift in $t$ is a scaling of the spiral.

**Angle property:** The angle $\psi$ between the tangent to the spiral and the radial direction satisfies $\cot\psi = b$ вҖ” **constant** along the entire curve. This is why it is called equiangular.

**Proof.** The position vector is $\mathbf{r}(t) = e^{bt}(\cos t, \sin t)$. The tangent vector is $\dot{\mathbf{r}} = e^{bt}(b\cos t - \sin t, b\sin t + \cos t)$. Compute $\cos\psi = \frac{\mathbf{r}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}}}{|\mathbf{r}||\dot{\mathbf{r}}|}$: $$\mathbf{r}\cdot\dot{\mathbf{r}} = e^{2bt}(b\cos^2 t - \cos t\sin t + b\sin^2 t + \sin t\cos t) = be^{2bt}.$$ $$|\mathbf{r}| = e^{bt},\quad |\dot{\mathbf{r}}| = e^{bt}\sqrt{b^2+1}.$$ $$\cos\psi = \frac{be^{2bt}}{e^{bt}\cdot e^{bt}\sqrt{b^2+1}} = \frac{b}{\sqrt{b^2+1}}, \quad \text{independent of } t. \quad \blacksquare$$

The transition $h = r$ (cusp appearance) and (implicitly) the appearance of loops for $h > r$ are both bifurcations.

## 7. Families of Conics as Parametric Curves

The conic sections form a one-parameter family when expressed parametrically in terms of **eccentricity** $e \geq 0$:

$$x(t, e) = \frac{\cos t - e}{1 - e\cos t}, \quad y(t, e) = \frac{\sqrt{1-e^2},\sin t}{1 - e\cos t} \quad (e < 1).$$

More cleanly in polar: $r = \frac{\ell}{1 + e\cos\theta}$ (semi-latus rectum $\ell$), then:

$$x(\theta, e) = \frac{\ell\cos\theta}{1 + e\cos\theta}, \quad y(\theta, e) = \frac{\ell\sin\theta}{1 + e\cos\theta}.$$

|$e$|Curve|Character|
|---|---|---|
|$0$|Circle|Compact, closed|
|$0 < e < 1$|Ellipse|Compact, closed|
|$e = 1$|Parabola|Open, one branch|
|$e > 1$|Hyperbola|Open, two branches|


## 8. Orthogonal Families вҖ” The Calculus Connection

Two families ${\gamma_c}$ and ${\tilde{\gamma}_k}$ are **orthogonal** if at every intersection point, the tangent vectors of the two families are perpendicular.

> **Definition 8.1.** Families ${F(x,y) = c}$ and ${G(x,y) = k}$ are _orthogonal trajectories_ if $\nabla F \cdot \nabla G = 0$ at every intersection.

**How to find orthogonal trajectories of ${F(x,y) = c}$:**

1. Differentiate $F(x,y) = c$ implicitly: find slope $dy/dx = m(x,y)$ of the family.
2. The orthogonal family has slope $dy/dx = -1/m(x,y)$.
3. Solve this ODE.

**Example.** Family: ${y = cx^2}$ (parabolas through the origin).

Step 1: $dy/dx = 2cx = 2(y/x^2)\cdot x = 2y/x$.

Step 2: Orthogonal family satisfies $dy/dx = -x/(2y)$.

Step 3: $2y,dy = -x,dx \Rightarrow y^2 = -x^2/2 + k \Rightarrow x^2/2 + y^2 = k$ вҖ” **ellipses** with semi-axes ratio $\sqrt{2}:1$.

## 9. Envelope of a Family

> **Definition 9.1 (Envelope).** The _envelope_ of a one-parameter family ${F(x, y, c) = 0}$ is a curve that is **tangent to every member** of the family.

The envelope is found by eliminating $c$ from the system: $$F(x, y, c) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{\partial F}{\partial c}(x, y, c) = 0.$$

**Derivation.** The envelope touches $\gamma_c$ at the point where $\gamma_{c+\varepsilon}$ and $\gamma_c$ intersect as $\varepsilon \to 0$. Formally: the intersection of $F = 0$ and $F + \varepsilon \partial F/\partial c + O(\varepsilon^2) = 0$ gives $\partial F/\partial c = 0$ in the limit. $\blacksquare$

### 9.1 Example вҖ” Family of Lines

Family: ${y = cx - c^2}$, i.e., $F(x, y, c) = cx - c^2 - y = 0$.

$\partial F/\partial c = x - 2c = 0 \Rightarrow c = x/2$.

Substitute: $y = (x/2)\cdot x - (x/2)^2 = x^2/2 - x^2/4 = x^2/4$.

**Envelope:** $y = x^2/4$ вҖ” a **parabola** tangent to every line in the family.


### 9.2 Example вҖ” Family of Circles (Caustics)

Family: circles of radius $r$ centered on the $x$-axis: $(x-c)^2 + y^2 = r^2$.

$\partial F/\partial c = -2(x-c) = 0 \Rightarrow x = c$.

Substitute: $y^2 = r^2 \Rightarrow y = \pm r$.

**Envelope:** the two horizontal lines $y = \pm r$ вҖ” the common external tangent lines.

### 9.3 Discriminant Interpretation

The system $F = 0$, $\partial F/\partial c = 0$ is the condition for $c$ to be a **repeated root** of $F(x,y,c) = 0$ (viewed as a polynomial in $c$). The envelope is the **discriminant locus** вҖ” a classical algebro-geometric object.

### Example 10.2 вҖ” Rhodonea (Rose Curves)

$$x(t, n) = \cos(nt)\cos t, \quad y(t, n) = \cos(nt)\sin t, \quad t \in [0, \pi \text{ or } 2\pi].$$

In polar: $r = \cos(n\theta)$.

|$n$|Petals|Interval needed|
|---|---|---|
|Odd integer|$n$ petals|$[0, \pi]$|
|Even integer|$2n$ petals|$[0, 2\pi]$|
|$p/q$ (rational)|$p+q$ or $2(p+q)$ petals|$[0, q\pi]$ or $[0, 2q\pi]$|

```
  n=1          n=2          n=3          n=4
  (1 petal)   (4 petals)   (3 petals)   (8 petals)

    *            * *           *             * * *
   * *          *   *         * *          *       *
    *            * *          * * *         *       *
                                *           * * * * *
```

**Count formula:**

- $n$ odd $\Rightarrow$ $n$ petals (the curve traces each petal once on $[0,\pi]$)
- $n$ even $\Rightarrow$ $2n$ petals (the curve traces each petal twice on $[0,2\pi]$)

**Proof for $n$ odd.** $r = \cos(n\theta) = 0$ at $\theta = \pi/(2n) + k\pi/n$ for $k = 0, \ldots, n-1$: exactly $n$ zeros on $[0, \pi)$, giving $n$ petals. For $n$ even, zeros occur on both $[0,\pi)$ and $[\pi, 2\pi)$, each repeated with reversed sign вҖ” giving $2n$ distinct petals. $\blacksquare$

### Example 10.3 вҖ” Butterfly Curve

$$x(t, a) = \sin t\left(e^{\cos t} - 2\cos(4t) - \sin^5!\frac{t}{12}\right), \quad y(t, a) = \cos t\left(e^{\cos t} - 2\cos(4t) - \sin^5!\frac{t}{12}\right).$$

Varying the coefficients inside gives a family of butterfly-like curves вҖ” an example of a family where the **topology is constant** (always a butterfly-like figure) but the **metric shape** deforms continuously.

## 12. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 12.1 Hyperparameter Families вҖ” Training Curves as Parametric Families

When training a neural network with learning rate $\eta$, the loss trajectory $t \mapsto \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t; \eta))$ is a **parametric curve in loss space indexed by $\eta$**. Varying $\eta$ gives a **family of training curves**:

```
  Loss L
  в”Ӯ

  в”Ӯ \  О· too large (diverges)
  в”Ӯ  в•І
  в”Ӯ   в•І__в•ұв•І__в•ұ___  (oscillates)
  в”Ӯ         О· optimal
  в”Ӯ          в•І___________

```

The **envelope** of this family (Definition 9.1) is the curve of **best achievable loss at each epoch** вҖ” the Pareto frontier of training efficiency. Finding it is the problem of **learning rate scheduling**.

### 12.2 Superellipses and $L^p$ Geometry

The superellipse family $|x|^p + |y|^p = 1$ (Example 10.1 with $n = 2/p$) is fundamental in ML:

|$p$|Norm unit ball|Geometry|ML use|
|---|---|---|---|
|$1$|Diamond ($L^1$)|Sparse solutions|LASSO, sparse coding|
|$2$|Circle ($L^2$)|Isotropic|Ridge regression, standard DL|
|$\infty$|Square ($L^\infty$)|Coordinate-aligned|Adversarial robustness|
|$p \in (1,2)$|Intermediate|вҖ”|Elastic net|

The family ${|x|^p + |y|^p = 1}_{p > 0}$ is a one-parameter family of curves that interpolates between $L^1$ and $L^\infty$ geometry. **The choice of $p$ in regularization is the choice of which member of this family to use.**

### 12.3 Activation Function Families

Many neural network activation functions form parametric families:

**Parametric ReLU (PReLU):** $$\sigma(x, \alpha) = \begin{cases} x & x \geq 0 \ \alpha x & x < 0 \end{cases}, \quad \alpha \in [0, 1].$$

As $\alpha$ varies:

- $\alpha = 0$: ReLU
- $\alpha = 1$: linear (identity)
- $\alpha = 0.01$: Leaky ReLU

The family ${\sigma(\cdot, \alpha)}_{\alpha \in [0,1]}$ is a one-parameter family of piecewise-linear functions. The input-output curve $x \mapsto \sigma(x, \alpha)$ is a parametric curve in $(x, \sigma)$-space indexed by $\alpha$.

**ELU family:** $$\sigma(x, \alpha) = \begin{cases} x & x \geq 0 \ \alpha(e^x - 1) & x < 0 \end{cases}.$$

The graph of $\sigma(\cdot, \alpha)$ is a smooth curve for each $\alpha$; the family morphs the negative half continuously.

### 12.4 Diffusion Model Score Curves

In a **diffusion model** (DDPM), data $\mathbf{x}_0$ is gradually noised: $$\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar{\alpha}_t},\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1 - \bar{\alpha}_t},\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad t \in [0, T].$$

For fixed $\mathbf{x}_0$, the path $t \mapsto \mathbf{x}_t$ is a **parametric curve in data space** вҖ” a straight-line interpolation in distribution space between data and noise. The **family** of such paths (over all $\mathbf{x}_0$ in the training set) is a family of curves in $\mathbb{R}^d$.

The **score function** $\nabla_\mathbf{x} \log p_t(\mathbf{x})$ is the **envelope** of this family in a precise sense: it is the direction field that is tangent to the denoising paths. Learning the score = learning the envelope.

### 12.5 Neural Architecture as Curve Families

A neural network with residual connections computes: $$\mathbf{h}_{l+1} = \mathbf{h}_l + f_\theta(\mathbf{h}_l), \quad l = 0, 1, \ldots, L-1.$$

In the continuous limit (Neural ODE from [[Parametric Curves]] В§8.4): $$\frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}(t), t).$$

For each **input** $\mathbf{x}$, the hidden state traces a different curve $t \mapsto \mathbf{h}(t; \mathbf{x})$. The **family of all such curves** (over all inputs $\mathbf{x}$) is a family of curves in feature space. The network learns to **warp this family** so that curves corresponding to the same class cluster together at $t = 1$.

```
  Feature space (t=0):         Feature space (t=1):
  
  class A в—Ҹ  в—Ҹ class B         class A в—Ҹв—Ҹв—Ҹ
     в—Ҹ в—Ҹ   в—Ҹ в—Ҹ                          |  вҶҗ  (separated)
       в—Ҹ в—Ҹ в—Ҹ                  class B   в—Ҹв—Ҹв—Ҹ
  (mixed)
  
  Neural ODE warps the family of curves so classes separate.
```

### 12.6 GANs and the Envelope of Generated Distributions

The **generator** $G_\theta: \mathcal{Z} \to \mathcal{X}$ of a GAN traces a curve in data space as $\mathbf{z}$ varies along a path in latent space. The **envelope** of all such curves (as $\theta$ varies over the training trajectory) approximates the **support of the real data distribution**.

Training convergence $=$ the family of generator curves' envelope matching the data manifold.

4. **Prove** that the orthogonal trajectories of the family of **circles** $x^2 + y^2 = c^2$ are the family of **lines** $y = kx$ through the origin.
    
5. **For the limaГ§on family** $r = 1 + c\cos\theta$: find the value of $c$ at which the inner loop first appears (bifurcation point) by finding when the curve becomes self-intersecting. (Hint: find when $r = 0$ has a solution.)
