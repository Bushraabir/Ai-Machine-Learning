

> _"Integration is just summation taken to its logical extreme — and parametric curves force us to be honest about what we're really summing."_

## §0 — The Problem Setup

In standard Cartesian calculus, the area under a curve $y = F(x)$ from $x = a$ to $x = b$ is:

$$ A = \int_a^b F(x), dx $$

This formula silently assumes $x$ is the natural variable of integration — that we sweep from left to right in $x$.

Now suppose the curve is given **parametrically**:

$$ x = f(t), \quad y = g(t), \quad t \in [\alpha, \beta] $$

We want the area between this curve and the $x$-axis. The key move: **change the variable of integration from $x$ to $t$**.

---

## §1 — Derivation of the Area Formula

### 1.1 The Substitution

Start from the Cartesian formula:

$$ A = \int_{x_1}^{x_2} y, dx $$

Since $x = f(t)$, we have $dx = f'(t),dt$. When $x = x_1 = f(\alpha)$ and $x = x_2 = f(\beta)$, the substitution yields:

$$ \boxed{A = \int_\alpha^\beta g(t), f'(t), dt} $$

> **Remark.** This is _exactly_ the substitution rule for definite integrals — nothing more exotic. The formula is rigorous provided $f$ and $g$ are continuous on $[\alpha, \beta]$ and $f$ is differentiable there.

### 1.2 Orientation Matters — A Critical Warning

The sign of $f'(t)$ encodes **direction of travel** along $x$:

- If $f'(t) > 0$ throughout: $x$ increases as $t$ increases → the formula gives **positive area** (assuming $y > 0$).
- If $f'(t) < 0$ throughout: $x$ decreases as $t$ increases → the integral gives a **negative** value; take absolute value for geometric area.
- If the curve **doubles back** (changes direction in $x$): areas on forward and backward passes may **cancel**. To get total enclosed area, split at turning points or take $|g(t),f'(t)|$.

> **The signed vs. geometric area distinction is the single most common source of errors in this topic.** Stewart §10.2 is explicit about this.

### 1.3 The Fully Careful Formula

For the **geometric** (always positive) area between the curve and the $x$-axis:

$$ A = \int_\alpha^\beta \bigl|g(t)\bigr|,\bigl|f'(t)\bigr|, dt $$

or equivalently, decompose into sub-intervals where $f'(t)$ doesn't change sign.

## §2 — Area Enclosed by a Closed Curve

When the parametric curve is **closed** — $\mathbf{r}(\alpha) = \mathbf{r}(\beta)$ — and encloses a region, we want the area of that region.

### 2.1 Via Green's Theorem

This is the rigorous path. **Green's Theorem** states that for a positively oriented (counterclockwise), simple closed curve $C$ bounding region $D$:

$$ \oint_C P, dx + Q, dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dA $$

Choose $P = 0,\ Q = x$: then $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = 1$, so:

$$ A = \oint_C x, dy = \int_\alpha^\beta f(t), g'(t), dt $$

Choose $P = -y,\ Q = 0$: then $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = 1$, so:

$$ A = -\oint_C y, dx = -\int_\alpha^\beta g(t), f'(t), dt $$

Average the two (a useful check formula):

$$ \boxed{A = \frac{1}{2} \oint_C \bigl(x,dy - y,dx\bigr) = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \bigl[f(t),g'(t) - g(t),f'(t)\bigr], dt} $$

> **Remark on orientation.** If the curve is traversed **clockwise** (negative orientation), the formula gives $-A$. Always check orientation before computing. A counterclockwise-traversed region encloses positive area.

### 2.2 Sign Convention Diagram

```
Counterclockwise (CCW) → positive area → formula gives +A

        y
        |    ___
        |   /   \
        |  |  +  |  ← area counted positively
        |   \___/
        |_____________ x
        
Clockwise (CW) → formula gives -A → take absolute value
```

## §3 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Area Under One Arch of the Cycloid

The cycloid: $$ x = r(t - \sin t), \quad y = r(1 - \cos t), \quad t \in [0, 2\pi] $$

One arch sweeps from $(0,0)$ to $(2\pi r, 0)$ as $t: 0 \to 2\pi$. Since $f'(t) = r(1-\cos t) \geq 0$ and $g(t) = r(1-\cos t) \geq 0$, no sign issues.

$$ A = \int_0^{2\pi} g(t),f'(t), dt = \int_0^{2\pi} r(1-\cos t)\cdot r(1-\cos t), dt = r^2\int_0^{2\pi}(1-\cos t)^2, dt $$

Expand: $$ (1-\cos t)^2 = 1 - 2\cos t + \cos^2 t = 1 - 2\cos t + \frac{1+\cos 2t}{2} = \frac{3}{2} - 2\cos t + \frac{\cos 2t}{2} $$

Integrate over $[0, 2\pi]$: $$ \int_0^{2\pi}!\left(\frac{3}{2} - 2\cos t + \frac{\cos 2t}{2}\right)dt = \frac{3}{2}(2\pi) - 0 + 0 = 3\pi $$

Therefore: $$ \boxed{A = 3\pi r^2} $$

This is exactly **three times the area of the rolling circle** ($\pi r^2$). A celebrated result — first proved by Galileo (incorrectly) and then by Roberval in 1634.

---

### Example 2 — Area of an Ellipse

$$ x = a\cos t,\quad y = b\sin t,\quad t \in [0, 2\pi] $$

The ellipse is traversed **counterclockwise** (check: at $t=0$, $x=a,,y=0$; at $t=\pi/2$, $x=0,,y=b$ — yes, CCW). Using the Green's Theorem formula:

$$ A = -\int_0^{2\pi} g(t),f'(t), dt = -\int_0^{2\pi} b\sin t \cdot (-a\sin t), dt = ab\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^2 t, dt $$

$$ = ab \int_0^{2\pi} \frac{1-\cos 2t}{2}, dt = ab \cdot \pi $$

$$ \boxed{A = \pi ab} $$

Recovers the standard formula. For $a = b = r$: $A = \pi r^2$. ✓

---

### Example 3 — Area of a Rose Petal

The three-petaled rose $r = \cos(3\theta)$ in polar can be written parametrically: $$ x = \cos(3t)\cos t,\quad y = \cos(3t)\sin t,\quad t \in [0, \pi/3] $$ (one petal from $t=0$ to $t=\pi/3$ where $r$ goes $1 \to 0 \to 0$, tracing the petal)

Area of one petal using the polar-area formula (which _is_ a special case of parametric area — see §4):

$$ A_{\text{petal}} = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi/3} \cos^2(3t), dt = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{\pi/3}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\text{(needs full calc)} = \frac{\pi}{12} $$

Full calculation: $$ \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi/3}!\frac{1+\cos 6t}{2},dt = \frac{1}{4}\left[t + \frac{\sin 6t}{6}\right]_0^{\pi/3} = \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\pi}{3} = \frac{\pi}{12} $$

Total area (3 petals): $\boxed{A = \pi/4}$.

---

### Example 4 — The Astroid

$$ x = a\cos^3 t,\quad y = a\sin^3 t,\quad t \in [0, 2\pi] $$

$$ f'(t) = -3a\cos^2 t\sin t,\quad g(t) = a\sin^3 t $$

By symmetry, compute area of the first quadrant ($t: 0 \to \pi/2$) and multiply by 4:

$$ A = 4\int_0^{\pi/2} g(t),|f'(t)|,dt = 4\int_0^{\pi/2} a\sin^3 t \cdot 3a\cos^2 t\sin t, dt = 12a^2\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^4 t\cos^2 t, dt $$

Using the Wallis-type reduction: $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^4 t\cos^2 t, dt = \frac{3\cdot1\cdot1}{6\cdot4\cdot2}\cdot\pi = \frac{\pi}{32}$

$$ \boxed{A = 12a^2 \cdot \frac{\pi}{32} = \frac{3\pi a^2}{8}} $$

This is $\dfrac{3}{8}$ the area of the circumscribed circle of radius $a$.

---

## §4 — Polar Coordinates as a Special Case

Polar curves $r = h(\theta)$ are parametric curves with $t = \theta$:

$$ x = h(\theta)\cos\theta,\quad y = h(\theta)\sin\theta $$

Then: $$ f'(\theta) = h'(\theta)\cos\theta - h(\theta)\sin\theta,\quad g(\theta) = h(\theta)\sin\theta $$

Plugging into the Green's Theorem area formula and simplifying (the cross terms collapse elegantly):

$$ A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \bigl[f(\theta)g'(\theta) - g(\theta)f'(\theta)\bigr], d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \bigl[h(\theta)\bigr]^2, d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2, d\theta $$

So the **polar area formula is a theorem**, not a new postulate — it follows directly from parametric area + Green's Theorem.

## §6 — The Shoelace Formula Connection

The discrete analog of the Green's Theorem area formula is the **Shoelace Formula** for a polygon with vertices $(x_1,y_1),\ldots,(x_n,y_n)$:

$$ A = \frac{1}{2}\left|\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i y_{i+1} - x_{i+1} y_i)\right| $$

This is literally the Riemann sum approximation to $\dfrac{1}{2}\displaystyle\oint(x,dy - y,dx)$. The parametric area formula is the **continuous limit** of the Shoelace Formula as the polygon is refined to a smooth curve.

---

## §7 — Rigorous Summary

> **Theorem (Area Under Parametric Curve).** Let $x = f(t),, y = g(t)$, $t \in [\alpha,\beta]$, with $f,g$ continuous and $f$ differentiable. If $f'(t)$ does not change sign, then the area bounded by the curve, the $x$-axis, and the lines $x = f(\alpha),, x = f(\beta)$ is: $$A = \left|\int_\alpha^\beta g(t),f'(t),dt\right|$$

> **Theorem (Enclosed Area via Green's Theorem).** Let $C$ be a positively oriented (CCW), simple, closed, piecewise-smooth parametric curve $x=f(t),,y=g(t)$, $t\in[\alpha,\beta]$, with $f(\alpha)=f(\beta)$, $g(\alpha)=g(\beta)$. Then the area enclosed is: $$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \bigl[f(t),g'(t) - g(t),f'(t)\bigr],dt$$

## §8 — Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 8.1 The Shoelace Formula in Polygon-Based Neural Networks

In **PolyWorld** and **PolygonRNN** (polygon-prediction networks for image segmentation), the model predicts a sequence of vertices $(x_1,y_1),\ldots,(x_n,y_n)$ tracing the boundary of an object. The area of the predicted polygon is computed via the Shoelace Formula — the discrete parametric area formula — and used as part of the **loss function** to enforce area constraints.

The differentiability of the Shoelace formula w.r.t. vertex coordinates is what makes this end-to-end trainable via backpropagation: $$ \frac{\partial A}{\partial x_i} = \frac{1}{2}(y_{i+1} - y_{i-1}), \quad \frac{\partial A}{\partial y_i} = \frac{1}{2}(x_{i-1} - x_{i+1}) $$

### 8.2 ROC Curve Area (AUC) as Parametric Area

The **ROC (Receiver Operating Characteristic) curve** is a parametric curve: $$ \text{TPR} = g(\tau),\quad \text{FPR} = f(\tau),\quad \tau \in [0, 1] $$

where $\tau$ is the classification threshold. The **AUC (Area Under the Curve)** is:

$$ \text{AUC} = \int_0^1 \text{TPR}, d(\text{FPR}) = \int_0^1 g(\tau), f'(\tau), d\tau $$

This is **exactly** the parametric area formula $\displaystyle\int g(t),f'(t),dt$. Every time you compute AUC to evaluate a classifier, you are computing a parametric area integral.

### 8.3 Precision-Recall Area (AP)

Similarly, **Average Precision (AP)** — the area under the Precision-Recall curve — is a parametric area integral where the parameter is the confidence threshold. It is the standard evaluation metric for object detection (COCO, Pascal VOC benchmarks).

### 8.4 Normalizing Flows and Change of Variables

**Normalizing flows** transform a simple density $p_z(z)$ into a complex density $p_x(x)$ via a differentiable bijection $x = f(z)$. The fundamental formula is:

$$ p_x(x) = p_z(f^{-1}(x))\left|\det\frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial x}\right| $$

The **Jacobian determinant** $|\det \partial f^{-1}/\partial x|$ is the multi-dimensional generalization of $|f'(t)|$ in the change-of-variables formula for parametric area. In 2D, the area element transforms as:

$$ dA_x = |f'(t)|, dA_t $$

which is precisely what we use when converting from $dx$ to $f'(t),dt$. The Jacobian _is_ the generalization of $f'(t)$ to higher dimensions.

### 8.5 Contour Integration in Signal Processing

In **complex neural networks** and signal processing layers, integrals over parametric contours in the complex plane arise naturally. The area enclosed by such contours, computed via Green's Theorem, determines **winding numbers** — which govern how many times a learned representation wraps around a singularity, with implications for phase-based neural representations.

### 8.6 Attention as Area Under a Curve

In **soft attention mechanisms**, the attention weight $\alpha_t$ integrates over a continuous time axis. In **continuous attention** models (e.g., Luong, monotonic attention), the attended context is:

$$ c = \int_0^T \alpha(t), h(t), dt $$

where $\alpha(t)$ defines a probability distribution over a parametric trajectory $t \mapsto h(t)$. This is a parametric integral — the weights $\alpha(t)$ play the role of $f'(t)$ (the measure), and $h(t)$ plays the role of $g(t)$ (the height).

## §10 — Common Pitfalls

1. **Sign of $f'(t)$**: Always determine whether $x$ is increasing or decreasing. A curve traversed right-to-left gives a negative integral for a positive region.
    
2. **Closed curve orientation**: CCW → positive area. CW → formula gives $-A$. Check by parameterization: does $x$ increase when $y > 0$ (CCW) or decrease (CW)?
    
3. **Self-intersecting curves**: The formula counts areas with multiplicity. For a figure-eight, the two lobes may cancel. Split at self-intersection points.
    
4. **$d^2y/dx^2 \neq g''/f''$** (from the tangents note): analogously, area is NOT $\int g(t),f(t),dt$ — it is $\int g(t),f'(t),dt$. The derivative $f'(t)$ comes from the change of variable $dx = f'(t),dt$.
    
5. **Confusing arc length with area**: Area uses $g(t),f'(t)$; arc length uses $\sqrt{f'(t)^2 + g'(t)^2}$. They come from entirely different geometric ideas.
