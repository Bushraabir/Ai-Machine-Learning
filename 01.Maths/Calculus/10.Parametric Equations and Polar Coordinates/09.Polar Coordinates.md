

> _"Cartesian coordinates ask: how far right, how far up? Polar coordinates ask: how far, and in which direction? The second question is often the more natural one."_

---

## ┬¦0 ŌĆö Motivation

Cartesian coordinates $(x, y)$ are adapted to **translation symmetry** ŌĆö grids, rectangles, straight lines. But many natural phenomena have **rotational symmetry**: planetary orbits, sound waves, electromagnetic fields, the receptive fields of visual neurons. For these, insisting on $(x, y)$ is like measuring angles with a ruler.

**Polar coordinates** are the natural language of rotational geometry.

## ┬¦1 ŌĆö Definition

A point $P$ in the plane is specified by:

$$ (r,, \theta) $$

where:

- $r$ = **radial distance** from the origin (the **pole**) to $P$
- $\theta$ = **polar angle** measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis (the **polar axis**) to the ray $OP$

```

                | /

              origin
              (pole)

  ╬Ė = angle from positive x-axis (CCW positive)
```

### 1.1 Conversion Formulas

**Polar ŌåÆ Cartesian:** $$ x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta $$

**Cartesian ŌåÆ Polar:** $$ r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}, \qquad \theta = \arctan!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) \quad \text{(adjusted for quadrant)} $$

> **Quadrant adjustment.** $\arctan(y/x)$ only gives $\theta \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2)$. Use $\text{atan2}(y, x)$ (the two-argument arctangent) for the correct quadrant: $$\theta = \begin{cases} \arctan(y/x) & x > 0 \ \arctan(y/x) + \pi & x < 0,\ y \geq 0 \ \arctan(y/x) - \pi & x < 0,\ y < 0 \ +\pi/2 & x = 0,\ y > 0 \ -\pi/2 & x = 0,\ y < 0 \end{cases}$$

### 1.2 Non-Uniqueness ŌĆö A Feature, Not a Bug

Unlike Cartesian coordinates, polar representation is **not unique**:

$$ (r, \theta) \equiv (r, \theta + 2\pi k) \quad \text{for any integer } k $$ $$ (r, \theta) \equiv (-r, \theta + \pi) $$

The same point is represented by infinitely many polar pairs. This matters when:

- Finding intersections of polar curves (a point may lie on both curves but with _different_ $(r, \theta)$ values)
- Integrating over a region (must avoid double-counting)

## ┬¦2 ŌĆö The Polar Coordinate System

### 2.1 Coordinate Curves

In Cartesian coordinates, $x = c$ (vertical line) and $y = c$ (horizontal line) are the coordinate curves.

In polar coordinates:

- $r = c$ (constant): a **circle** of radius $c$ centered at the origin
- $\theta = c$ (constant): a **ray** from the origin at angle $c$

### 2.2 The Polar Area Element

This is crucial for integration. In Cartesian: $dA = dx,dy$.

In polar, a small "polar rectangle" with sides $dr$ and $r,d\theta$ has area:

$$ dA = r, dr, d\theta $$

The factor of $r$ arises because the arc length of a circular arc of radius $r$ and angle $d\theta$ is $r,d\theta$, not $d\theta$.

```
        r+dr ____
            /    \   ŌåÉ arc length = r┬Ęd╬Ė
           /      \
         r/___d╬Ė___\
        
  Area of this sector Ōēł r┬Ędr┬Ęd╬Ė
  (area of annular sector, exact in the limit)
```

**Proof via Jacobian.** The change of variables $(x,y) \mapsto (r,\theta)$ has Jacobian:

$$ \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)} = \begin{vmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{vmatrix} = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r $$

So $dx,dy = |J|,dr,d\theta = r,dr,d\theta$. Ō£ō

## ┬¦3 ŌĆö Polar Curves

A **polar curve** is defined by $r = h(\theta)$ for $\theta \in [\alpha, \beta]$.

### 3.1 A Gallery of Standard Curves

**Circle through origin:** $$ r = 2a\cos\theta \quad \text{(circle of radius } |a|, \text{ centered at } (a, 0)) $$ $$ r = 2a\sin\theta \quad \text{(circle of radius } |a|, \text{ centered at } (0, a)) $$

**Lima├¦on:** $r = a + b\cos\theta$

- $|a| > |b|$: no inner loop (dimpled or convex)
- $|a| = |b|$: **cardioid** $r = a(1 + \cos\theta)$
- $|a| < |b|$: inner loop

**Rose curves:** $r = a\cos(n\theta)$ or $r = a\sin(n\theta)$

- $n$ odd: $n$ petals
- $n$ even: $2n$ petals
- Petal length: $a$

**Lemniscate of Bernoulli:** $r^2 = a^2\cos(2\theta)$

- Figure-eight shape, $r$ real only when $\cos(2\theta) \geq 0$

**Archimedean spiral:** $r = a\theta$ ŌĆö constant increase in $r$ per revolution

**Logarithmic spiral:** $r = ae^{b\theta}$ ŌĆö constant angle between tangent and radial direction

### 3.2 Symmetry Tests

|Symmetry|Test (sufficient, not necessary)|
|---|---|
|About $x$-axis ($\theta = 0$)|$h(-\theta) = h(\theta)$|
|About $y$-axis ($\theta = \pi/2$)|$h(\pi - \theta) = h(\theta)$|
|About origin|$h(\theta + \pi) = h(\theta)$ or $h(-\theta) = -h(\theta)$|

> **Caution.** Due to non-uniqueness of polar coordinates, a curve can be symmetric even if none of these tests pass. Always plot to verify.

## ┬¦4 ŌĆö Calculus in Polar Coordinates

### 4.1 Tangent Lines to Polar Curves

A polar curve $r = h(\theta)$ is a parametric curve with $\theta$ as parameter:

$$ x = h(\theta)\cos\theta, \quad y = h(\theta)\sin\theta $$

By the product rule: $$ \frac{dx}{d\theta} = h'(\theta)\cos\theta - h(\theta)\sin\theta $$ $$ \frac{dy}{d\theta} = h'(\theta)\sin\theta + h(\theta)\cos\theta $$

Tangent slope (from our parametric tangent formula ŌĆö see [[Tangents of Parametric Curves]]):

$$ \boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{h'(\theta)\sin\theta + h(\theta)\cos\theta}{h'(\theta)\cos\theta - h(\theta)\sin\theta}} $$

**Horizontal tangents:** numerator $= 0$, denominator $\neq 0$

**Vertical tangents:** denominator $= 0$, numerator $\neq 0$

### 4.2 Area Enclosed by a Polar Curve

As derived in [[Area of Parametric Curves]] (┬¦4 there), the polar area formula is a theorem ŌĆö it follows from the parametric area formula + Green's Theorem. For $r = h(\theta)$, $\theta \in [\alpha, \beta]$:

$$ \boxed{A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \bigl[h(\theta)\bigr]^2, d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta r^2, d\theta} $$

**Intuition.** A thin sector of angle $d\theta$ and radius $r$ has area $\frac{1}{2}r^2,d\theta$ (like a triangle with base $r,d\theta$ and height $r$). Sum infinitely many such sectors.

```
         * r=h(╬Ė)
        /|
       / |
      /  |  thin sector: dA = ┬Įr┬▓d╬Ė
     /d╬Ė |
    /    |
   O_____|
```

**Area between two curves** $r_1(\theta) \leq r_2(\theta)$ on $[\alpha, \beta]$:

$$ A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta \left[r_2(\theta)^2 - r_1(\theta)^2\right] d\theta $$

### 4.3 Arc Length of a Polar Curve

From the parametric arc length formula with $x = h(\theta)\cos\theta$, $y = h(\theta)\sin\theta$:

$$ L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{d\theta}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{d\theta}\right)^2}, d\theta $$

After expanding using product rule and simplifying (the cross terms cancel via $\sin^2+\cos^2=1$):

$$ \boxed{L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2}, d\theta = \int_\alpha^\beta\sqrt{h(\theta)^2 + h'(\theta)^2}, d\theta} $$

### 4.4 Surface Area (Revolution About the Polar Axis)

Revolve $r = h(\theta)$, $\theta \in [\alpha, \beta]$ about the polar axis ($x$-axis). Radius from the axis is $y = r\sin\theta$:

$$ S = 2\pi\int_\alpha^\beta h(\theta)\sin\theta\cdot\sqrt{h(\theta)^2 + h'(\theta)^2}; d\theta $$

## ┬¦5 ŌĆö Worked Examples

### Example 1 ŌĆö Area of a Cardioid

$$ r = a(1 + \cos\theta), \quad \theta \in [0, 2\pi] $$

$$ A = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} a^2(1+\cos\theta)^2, d\theta = \frac{a^2}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(1 + 2\cos\theta + \cos^2\theta), d\theta $$

$$ = \frac{a^2}{2}\left[2\pi + 0 + \pi\right] = \frac{3\pi a^2}{2} $$

$$ \boxed{A = \frac{3\pi a^2}{2}} $$

---

### Example 2 ŌĆö Area of One Rose Petal

$$ r = \cos(2\theta) \quad \text{(4-petaled rose)} $$

One petal occupies $\theta \in [-\pi/4, \pi/4]$ (where $r \geq 0$):

$$ A_{\text{petal}} = \frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\cos^2(2\theta), d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{1+\cos(4\theta)}{2}, d\theta $$

$$ = \frac{1}{4}\left[\theta + \frac{\sin(4\theta)}{4}\right]_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4} = \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi}{8} $$

Total area (4 petals): $4 \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{8} = \dfrac{\pi}{2}$.

---

### Example 3 ŌĆö Area Between Two Curves

Find the area inside $r = 3\sin\theta$ and outside $r = 1 + \sin\theta$.

**Step 1.** Find intersections: $3\sin\theta = 1 + \sin\theta \Rightarrow \sin\theta = 1/2 \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/6,, 5\pi/6$.

**Step 2.** On $[\pi/6, 5\pi/6]$: $3\sin\theta \geq 1 + \sin\theta$ (check at $\theta = \pi/2$: $3 \geq 2$ Ō£ō).

$$ A = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[(3\sin\theta)^2 - (1+\sin\theta)^2\right]d\theta $$

$$ = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[9\sin^2\theta - 1 - 2\sin\theta - \sin^2\theta\right]d\theta $$

$$ = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[8\sin^2\theta - 2\sin\theta - 1\right]d\theta $$

Using $8\sin^2\theta = 4(1-\cos 2\theta)$:

$$ = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[4 - 4\cos 2\theta - 2\sin\theta - 1\right]d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6}\left[3 - 4\cos 2\theta - 2\sin\theta\right]d\theta $$

$$ = \frac{1}{2}\left[3\theta - 2\sin 2\theta + 2\cos\theta\right]_{\pi/6}^{5\pi/6} = \pi $$

$$ \boxed{A = \pi} $$

---

### Example 4 ŌĆö Arc Length of the Cardioid

$r = a(1+\cos\theta)$, $h'(\theta) = -a\sin\theta$:

$$ r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2 = a^2(1+\cos\theta)^2 + a^2\sin^2\theta = a^2(2 + 2\cos\theta) = 4a^2\cos^2!\frac{\theta}{2} $$

$$ L = \int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt{4a^2\cos^2!\frac{\theta}{2}}, d\theta = 2a\int_0^{2\pi}\left|\cos\frac{\theta}{2}\right|d\theta = 2a\cdot 2\int_0^{\pi}\cos\frac{\theta}{2}, d\theta = 4a\left[2\sin\frac{\theta}{2}\right]_0^{\pi} $$

$$ \boxed{L = 8a} $$

## ┬¦7 ŌĆö Intersection of Polar Curves: A Subtle Problem

Finding intersections of $r = f(\theta)$ and $r = g(\theta)$:

**Step 1.** Solve $f(\theta) = g(\theta)$ ŌĆö gives intersection points with the _same_ $(r, \theta)$.

**Step 2.** Check if both curves pass through the **origin** (pole). The origin is on $r = f(\theta)$ if $f(\theta) = 0$ for some $\theta_1$, and on $r = g(\theta)$ if $g(\theta) = 0$ for some $\theta_2$ (not necessarily the same $\theta$!).

**Step 3.** Use the periodicity/sign equivalence to check for missed intersections.

**Example.** $r = \sin\theta$ and $r = \cos\theta$:

- $\sin\theta = \cos\theta \Rightarrow \theta = \pi/4$: intersection at $(1/\sqrt{2},, \pi/4)$ Ō£ō
- $r = \sin\theta = 0$ at $\theta = 0$; $r = \cos\theta = 0$ at $\theta = \pi/2$: both pass through origin, so origin is another intersection Ō£ō
- Total: **two** intersection points.

## ┬¦8 ŌĆö Polar Coordinates in Multiple Dimensions

### 8.1 Cylindrical Coordinates $(r, \theta, z)$

$$ x = r\cos\theta,\quad y = r\sin\theta,\quad z = z $$ Volume element: $dV = r, dr, d\theta, dz$

### 8.2 Spherical Coordinates $(\rho, \phi, \theta)$

$$ x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\quad y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\quad z = \rho\cos\phi $$ Volume element: $dV = \rho^2\sin\phi, d\rho, d\phi, d\theta$

Both are the MIT 18.02 generalizations of 2D polar coordinates. The $r$ in polar becomes the factor in the Jacobian determinant in all cases.

## ┬¦9 ŌĆö Rigorous Summary

> **Theorem (Polar Area).** Let $r = h(\theta)$ be continuous on $[\alpha, \beta]$ with $\beta - \alpha \leq 2\pi$. The area of the region bounded by the curve and the rays $\theta = \alpha$, $\theta = \beta$ is: $$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_\alpha^\beta h(\theta)^2, d\theta$$

> **Theorem (Polar Arc Length).** If $h$ is continuously differentiable on $[\alpha, \beta]$, the arc length of $r = h(\theta)$ is: $$L = \int_\alpha^\beta\sqrt{h(\theta)^2 + h'(\theta)^2}, d\theta$$

> **Theorem (Polar Jacobian).** The change of variables $(x,y) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ has Jacobian $|\partial(x,y)/\partial(r,\theta)| = r$, so $dx,dy = r,dr,d\theta$.

## ┬¦10 ŌĆö Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 10.1 $\text{atan2}$ Is Everywhere in Geometry-Aware Networks

The two-argument arctangent $\text{atan2}(y, x)$ ŌĆö the correct quadrant-aware polar angle ŌĆö is used in:

- **Optical flow** networks: flow vectors are often decomposed into magnitude and angle
- **Oriented bounding boxes** in object detection (rotated RoI pooling)
- **Phase computation** in complex-valued neural networks
- **SE(2)-equivariant networks** (rotation + translation equivariance)

The derivative $\partial,\text{atan2}(y,x)/\partial x = -y/(x^2+y^2)$, $\partial/\partial y = x/(x^2+y^2)$ flows through backpropagation without issue. The polar angle is a **differentiable operation** on features.

### 10.2 Rotational Equivariance via Polar Coordinates

**Group equivariant CNNs** (Cohen & Welling, 2016; Weiler et al., 2019) design filters that are equivariant to rotations. A core technique: represent filters in **polar coordinates** $(r, \theta)$ and decompose the angular part in a Fourier basis:

$$ f(r, \theta) = \sum_k a_k(r), e^{ik\theta} $$

Because $e^{ik\theta}$ transforms predictably under rotation ($\theta \to \theta + \phi$ gives factor $e^{ik\phi}$), filters written this way are automatically equivariant. **The polar coordinate system is the natural basis for rotation equivariance.**

### 10.3 Polar Representation in Radar / Lidar Processing

**Autonomous driving** perception networks often receive data from **lidar** sensors, which output point clouds in polar / spherical coordinates $(r, \theta, \phi)$ naturally. Networks like **PolarNet**, **RangeNet++**, and **Cylinder3D** operate directly in polar/cylindrical coordinate grids rather than converting to Cartesian ŌĆö exploiting the natural symmetries of the sensor geometry. The polar area element $r,dr,d\theta$ determines how to properly normalize features in these coordinate grids.

### 10.4 Complex Numbers, Polar Form, and Attention

Every complex number $z = x + iy$ has polar form $z = re^{i\theta}$. In **complex-valued neural networks** and in **Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE)** ŌĆö used in modern LLMs including LLaMA, Mistral, and GPT-4 ŌĆö the attention mechanism encodes position as a rotation in the complex plane:

$$ \text{RoPE}(x, m) = x \cdot e^{im\theta} $$

This is polar multiplication: rotate the query/key vector by angle $m\theta$ in each 2D subspace. The relative position between tokens $m$ and $n$ appears as a phase difference $e^{i(m-n)\theta}$ in the dot product ŌĆö a purely polar-coordinate phenomenon. **Every modern LLM using RoPE is doing polar coordinate arithmetic in its attention layers.**

### 10.5 Fourier Transform as Polar Integration

The **2D Discrete Fourier Transform** (used in CNNs via the convolution theorem, in spectral graph networks, and in physics-informed neural networks) can be written in polar coordinates in frequency space:

$$ \hat{f}(\xi_1, \xi_2) = \int_0^\infty\int_0^{2\pi} f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta), e^{-2\pi i r(\xi_1\cos\theta + \xi_2\sin\theta)}, r, dr, d\theta $$

The $r,dr,d\theta$ area element is the polar Jacobian. The **Radon transform** (used in CT scans and some neural network architectures) is a line integral in polar coordinates. These are the analytical foundations of frequency-domain learning.

### 10.6 Gaussian Integrals ŌĆö The Foundation of Probabilistic ML

The most important integral in all of machine learning:

$$ \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}, dx = \sqrt{\pi} $$

The standard proof uses **polar coordinates**:

$$ \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}, dx\right)^2 = \int_{-\infty}^\infty\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x^2+y^2)}, dx, dy = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2}, r, dr, d\theta = \pi $$

Every Gaussian distribution, every normal distribution likelihood, every Bayesian posterior involving Gaussians, every variational autoencoder's KL divergence ŌĆö **all rest on this polar coordinate computation.** The factor of $r$ in $r,dr,d\theta$ is literally what makes Gaussian integrals tractable.

### 10.7 Loss Landscapes and Angular Geometry

In **cosine similarity** ŌĆö used in contrastive learning (SimCLR, MoCo, CLIP), metric learning, and attention:

$$ \text{sim}(u, v) = \frac{u \cdot v}{|u||v|} = \cos\theta_{uv} $$

This is exactly the polar coordinate inner product. The **angular distance** $\theta_{uv}$ is the polar angle between the two vectors. Contrastive losses minimize angular distance between positive pairs and maximize it between negative pairs ŌĆö they are **polar coordinate optimization problems** in high-dimensional embedding space.

**ArcFace** (face recognition) explicitly adds an angular margin $m$ to the polar angle:

$$ \mathcal{L} = -\log\frac{e^{s\cos(\theta_{y_i} + m)}}{e^{s\cos(\theta_{y_i}+m)} + \sum_{j\neq y_i}e^{s\cos\theta_j}} $$

This is purely polar geometry ŌĆö the entire loss is written in terms of $r$ (the norm, scaled by $s$) and $\theta$ (the angle between feature and weight vectors).

## ┬¦12 ŌĆö Common Pitfalls

1. **Missing the Jacobian factor $r$**: The area element is $r,dr,d\theta$, not $dr,d\theta$. Forgetting $r$ is the most common integration error in polar coordinates.
    
2. **Wrong quadrant for $\theta$**: $\arctan(y/x)$ is not $\text{atan2}(y,x)$. Always adjust for the correct quadrant.
    
3. **Double-counting in closed curves**: If a curve is traced twice as $\theta$ goes from $0$ to $2\pi$, the area integral counts it twice. Check how many times the curve is traversed.
    
4. **Missing intersections at the origin**: Two curves can meet at the origin with completely different $\theta$ values. Always check the origin separately.
    
5. **Rose petal count**: $r = \cos(n\theta)$ has $n$ petals if $n$ is odd, $2n$ petals if $n$ is even. It is not always $n$ petals.
    
6. **Confusing $r < 0$**: When $h(\theta) < 0$, the point $(h(\theta), \theta)$ is plotted in the opposite direction ŌĆö at $(|h(\theta)|, \theta + \pi)$. This is valid but counterintuitive.
