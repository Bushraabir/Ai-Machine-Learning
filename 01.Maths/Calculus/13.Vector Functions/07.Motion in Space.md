
> _"Newton saw the planets and asked: what function $\mathbf{r}(t)$ produces an ellipse under the force law $\mathbf{F} = -GMm/r^2,\hat{\mathbf{r}}$? The answer required him to invent calculus. We now have that calculus — let us use it properly."_

## 0. Setup and Philosophy

A **particle moving through $\mathbb{R}^3$** is described by a smooth vector-valued function:

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t),, y(t),, z(t)\rangle, \qquad t \in I \subseteq \mathbb{R}$$

Everything — velocity, acceleration, speed, arc length, force — is derived from this single function by differentiation. The miracle of vector calculus is that **all of Newton's mechanics packages cleanly into vector equations**, and the geometry of the Frenet–Serret frame (see [[Normal and Binormal Vectors]]) tells us _why_ acceleration splits the way it does.

**Convention throughout:** $t$ is time. Prime $'$ denotes $d/dt$. We assume $\mathbf{r}(t)$ is at least twice continuously differentiable.

---

## 1. Velocity

### Definition

The **velocity vector** is the derivative of the position vector:

$$\boxed{\mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle x'(t),, y'(t),, z'(t)\rangle}$$

It is the **instantaneous rate of change of position** — both direction and magnitude carry meaning:

- **Direction of $\mathbf{v}(t)$:** Tangent to the curve at $\mathbf{r}(t)$ — the direction of motion.
- **Magnitude $|\mathbf{v}(t)|$:** The **speed** at time $t$.

### Speed

$$v(t) := |\mathbf{v}(t)| = \sqrt{[x'(t)]^2 + [y'(t)]^2 + [z'(t)]^2}$$

Speed is a scalar; velocity is a vector. This distinction is not pedantry — it is the difference between "60 km/h" and "60 km/h due north."

### Relation to the Unit Tangent Vector

Recall $\mathbf{T}(t) = \mathbf{r}'(t)/|\mathbf{r}'(t)|$. Therefore:

$$\mathbf{v}(t) = |\mathbf{v}(t)|,\mathbf{T}(t) = v(t),\mathbf{T}(t)$$

Velocity is speed times the unit tangent. This is the **canonical decomposition of velocity** — speed tells you _how fast_, $\mathbf{T}$ tells you _which way_.

### Arc Length and Speed

Arc length satisfies $\dfrac{ds}{dt} = |\mathbf{r}'(t)| = v(t)$. So:

$$s(t) = \int_{t_0}^{t} |\mathbf{r}'(u)|,du = \int_{t_0}^{t} v(u),du$$

**Speed is the rate of change of arc length.** This is not a coincidence — it is the definition.

---

## 2. Acceleration

### Definition

The **acceleration vector** is the derivative of velocity:

$$\boxed{\mathbf{a}(t) = \mathbf{v}'(t) = \mathbf{r}''(t) = \langle x''(t),, y''(t),, z''(t)\rangle}$$

Unlike velocity, acceleration does **not** generally point along $\mathbf{T}$. It can point in any direction — and decomposing it into the Frenet frame is the central result of this section.

### Newton's Second Law

For a particle of mass $m$:

$$\mathbf{F}(t) = m,\mathbf{a}(t) = m,\mathbf{r}''(t)$$

The vector equation encodes three scalar ODEs simultaneously. Knowing $\mathbf{F}$ as a function of $\mathbf{r}, \mathbf{v}, t$ gives a second-order ODE system — the equation of motion. The trajectory $\mathbf{r}(t)$ is its solution.

---

## 3. The Tangential and Normal Components of Acceleration

This is the theorem that makes everything click. Acceleration splits into exactly two parts: one that changes your **speed**, and one that changes your **direction**.

### Derivation

Start from $\mathbf{v} = v,\mathbf{T}$. Differentiate using the product rule:

$$\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{v}' = v',\mathbf{T} + v,\mathbf{T}'$$

Now use the first Frenet–Serret formula: $\mathbf{T}' = \dfrac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds}\dfrac{ds}{dt} = \kappa,\mathbf{N}\cdot v$. So:

$$\mathbf{a} = v',\mathbf{T} + \kappa v^2,\mathbf{N}$$

Define:

$$\boxed{a_T = v' = \frac{dv}{dt}, \qquad a_N = \kappa v^2}$$

Then:

$$\boxed{\mathbf{a} = a_T,\mathbf{T} + a_N,\mathbf{N}}$$

**This is exact — no approximation.** Acceleration has zero component along $\mathbf{B}$.

### Geometric Interpretation

|Component|Formula|Physical Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$a_T,\mathbf{T}$|$v',\mathbf{T}$|Change in **speed** (speeding up or slowing down)|
|$a_N,\mathbf{N}$|$\kappa v^2,\mathbf{N}$|Change in **direction** (centripetal, toward center of curvature)|

Key observations:

- If you drive at **constant speed** on a curved road: $a_T = 0$, but $a_N = \kappa v^2 \neq 0$. You are accelerating — your direction changes.
- If you drive in a **straight line** and speed up: $a_N = 0$ (since $\kappa = 0$), only $a_T \neq 0$.
- **Acceleration is never along $\mathbf{B}$**. Torsion (twisting) does not produce force directly — it produces a change in the osculating plane.

### Why $a_N = \kappa v^2$?

Write it as $a_N = v^2/\rho$ where $\rho = 1/\kappa$ is the **radius of curvature**. This is the familiar centripetal acceleration formula $a = v^2/r$ from circular motion — now generalized to any curve, using the instantaneous radius of the osculating circle. Beautiful.

## 4. Computing $a_T$ and $a_N$ Without the Frenet Frame

In practice you rarely know $\kappa$ and $\mathbf{N}$ explicitly. Here are computational formulas:

$$a_T = \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{v}|} = \frac{\mathbf{r}'\cdot\mathbf{r}''}{|\mathbf{r}'|}$$

$$a_N = \frac{|\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{a}|}{|\mathbf{v}|} = \frac{|\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}''|}{|\mathbf{r}'|}$$

**Proof of $a_T$:** Project $\mathbf{a} = a_T\mathbf{T} + a_N\mathbf{N}$ onto $\mathbf{T}$: $$\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{T} = a_T(\mathbf{T}\cdot\mathbf{T}) + a_N(\mathbf{N}\cdot\mathbf{T}) = a_T$$

Since $\mathbf{v} = v\mathbf{T}$, we get $\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{v} = a_T v$, hence $a_T = \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{v}/v$.

**Proof of $a_N$:** Compute $\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{a}$: $$\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{a} = v\mathbf{T}\times(a_T\mathbf{T}+a_N\mathbf{N}) = v,a_T(\mathbf{T}\times\mathbf{T}) + v,a_N(\mathbf{T}\times\mathbf{N}) = v,a_N,\mathbf{B}$$

So $|\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{a}| = v,a_N$ (since $|\mathbf{B}|=1$), giving $a_N = |\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{a}|/v$.

**Pythagoras check:**

$$|\mathbf{a}|^2 = a_T^2 + a_N^2$$

since $\mathbf{T} \perp \mathbf{N}$. This gives a useful consistency check.

## 5. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Circular Motion at Constant Speed

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = (R\cos\omega t,, R\sin\omega t,, 0)$$

where $R > 0$ is the radius and $\omega > 0$ is the angular frequency.

**Velocity:** $$\mathbf{v}(t) = (-R\omega\sin\omega t,, R\omega\cos\omega t,, 0), \qquad v = |\mathbf{v}| = R\omega$$

Constant speed — no speeding up or slowing down.

**Acceleration:** $$\mathbf{a}(t) = (-R\omega^2\cos\omega t,, -R\omega^2\sin\omega t,, 0) = -\omega^2(R\cos\omega t,, R\sin\omega t,, 0) = -\omega^2,\mathbf{r}(t)$$

The acceleration points **toward the origin** — centripetal. Its magnitude: $|\mathbf{a}| = R\omega^2$.

**Decomposition:** $$a_T = \frac{dv}{dt} = 0 \quad \checkmark \qquad a_N = \kappa v^2 = \frac{1}{R}(R\omega)^2 = R\omega^2 \quad \checkmark$$

The centripetal force required: $F = m,a_N = mR\omega^2 = mv^2/R$.

---

### Example 2: Projectile Motion

A ball launched from the origin with initial velocity $\mathbf{v}_0 = \langle v_0\cos\theta,, v_0\sin\theta,, 0\rangle$, under gravity $\mathbf{g} = \langle 0, -g, 0\rangle$:

$$\mathbf{a}(t) = \langle 0,, -g,, 0\rangle$$

**Integrate once** (velocity): $$\mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 + \mathbf{g}t = \langle v_0\cos\theta,; v_0\sin\theta - gt,; 0\rangle$$

**Integrate again** (position): $$\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{g}t^2 = \langle v_0\cos\theta\cdot t,; v_0\sin\theta\cdot t - \tfrac{1}{2}gt^2,; 0\rangle$$

**Speed at time $t$:** $$v(t) = \sqrt{v_0^2\cos^2\theta + (v_0\sin\theta - gt)^2}$$

**Tangential acceleration** (rate of change of speed): $$a_T = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{(v_0\sin\theta - gt)(-g)}{v(t)} = \frac{-g(v_y)}{v}$$

At the **apex** ($v_y = 0$): $a_T = 0$ — speed is momentarily neither increasing nor decreasing. But $a_N = g$ — the ball is still curving.

**Normal acceleration at apex:** $$a_N = \sqrt{|\mathbf{a}|^2 - a_T^2} = \sqrt{g^2 - 0} = g$$

At the apex, the full gravitational acceleration is centripetal — exactly as expected from the geometry.

---

### Example 3: Helix with Increasing Speed

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t^2,, \sin t^2,, t)$$

**Velocity:** $$\mathbf{v}(t) = (-2t\sin t^2,, 2t\cos t^2,, 1)$$ $$v = |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{4t^2 + 1}$$

**Acceleration:** $$\mathbf{a}(t) = (-2\sin t^2 - 4t^2\cos t^2,; 2\cos t^2 - 4t^2\sin t^2,; 0)$$

**$a_T$:** $$a_T = \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{a}}{v} = \frac{(-2t\sin t^2)(-2\sin t^2 - 4t^2\cos t^2) + (2t\cos t^2)(2\cos t^2 - 4t^2\sin t^2)}{\sqrt{4t^2+1}}$$

Expanding the numerator: $$= \frac{4t\sin^2 t^2 + 8t^3\sin t^2\cos t^2 + 4t\cos^2 t^2 - 8t^3\cos t^2\sin t^2}{\sqrt{4t^2+1}} = \frac{4t}{\sqrt{4t^2+1}}$$

Check: $v = \sqrt{4t^2+1}$, so $v' = 4t/\sqrt{4t^2+1} = a_T$. ✓

The speed is increasing (for $t > 0$) while the particle also curves — both $a_T$ and $a_N$ are nonzero.

## 6. Visualization

### The Acceleration Decomposition

```
                 N (principal normal)
                 ↑
                 |  a_N·N (centripetal)
                 |  ↑
                 | /
  ───────────────*──────────→  T (tangent)
                /|
               / |
     a_T·T →  /  |
             /   |
            ↗    
           a (total acceleration)
           = a_T·T + a_N·N

  Note: a has NO component along B (binormal).
```

### Speed vs. Curvature Trade-off

```
High speed, low curvature:        Low speed, high curvature:
  a_N = κv² → large               a_N = κv² → moderate

  ─────────────────────            ╭──────────────╮
  (fast, gently curving road)      (slow, sharp turn)

Both can produce the same |a_N| — the driver feels the same lateral force.
This is why speed limits decrease at sharp curves.
```

### Phase Diagram: Speeding Up vs. Slowing Down

```
  a_T > 0  →  speeding up   (a has component in direction of motion)
  a_T < 0  →  slowing down  (a opposes direction of motion)
  a_T = 0  →  constant speed (a ⊥ T, purely centripetal)

  Example — car on a mountain road:
  Ascending curve:   a_T < 0 (engine fights gravity), a_N ≠ 0 (turning)
  Descending curve:  a_T > 0 (gravity aids motion), a_N ≠ 0 (turning)
  At apex (flat):    a_T ≈ 0 momentarily, a_N from curvature
```

## 7. Integration: Finding Position from Acceleration

Given $\mathbf{a}(t)$ and initial conditions $\mathbf{r}(t_0) = \mathbf{r}_0$, $\mathbf{v}(t_0) = \mathbf{v}_0$:

$$\mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{v}_0 + \int_{t_0}^{t}\mathbf{a}(u),du$$

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + \int_{t_0}^{t}\mathbf{v}(u),du$$

Integration is **componentwise**: each equation is three scalar ODEs integrated independently.

**Key point:** The initial conditions $(\mathbf{r}_0, \mathbf{v}_0)$ — six scalar values — completely determine the trajectory when $\mathbf{a}$ is given. This is the vector form of the initial value problem, and uniqueness follows from Picard–Lindelöf applied componentwise.

## 8. Kepler's First Law (A Taste of What the Formalism Buys)

Consider a planet of mass $m$ orbiting a star of mass $M$ at the origin. Newton's law gives:

$$\mathbf{r}'' = -\frac{GM}{|\mathbf{r}|^3},\mathbf{r}$$

**Claim:** The angular momentum $\mathbf{L} = m(\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{v})$ is conserved.

**Proof:** $$\frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt} = m(\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{v}') = m(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{a}) = m\left(\mathbf{0} + \mathbf{r}\times\left(-\frac{GM}{|\mathbf{r}|^3}\mathbf{r}\right)\right) = \mathbf{0}$$

since $\mathbf{r}\times\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{0}$. Therefore $\mathbf{L} = $ const., which means **the motion is planar** (confined to the plane perpendicular to $\mathbf{L}$). This is the first step in proving the orbit is a conic section — and it falls out of a two-line vector calculation.

This illustrates the power of the vector formalism: what would require pages of component algebra becomes transparent.

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

The concepts of velocity and acceleration in vector calculus map directly onto some of the most important algorithms and ideas in modern machine learning.

### 9.1 Gradient Descent as Motion in Parameter Space

Training a neural network by gradient descent defines a trajectory $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$ in parameter space $\mathbb{R}^p$ (where $p$ can be billions). The analog of Newton's equations:

|Physics|ML Optimization|
|---|---|
|Position $\mathbf{r}(t)$|Parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$|
|Velocity $\mathbf{v}(t) = \mathbf{r}'$|Rate of parameter change $\boldsymbol{\theta}'$|
|Force / Acceleration|Negative gradient $-\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$|
|Mass|Learning rate $\eta$ (inverse analog)|

**Vanilla gradient descent** is literally Euler's method for the ODE: $$\frac{d\boldsymbol{\theta}}{dt} = -\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$

The "velocity" in parameter space is $-\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}$ — you move in the direction of steepest descent.

### 9.2 Momentum Methods — Literally Acceleration

**SGD with momentum** (Polyak, 1964) introduces an auxiliary "velocity" variable:

$$\mathbf{m}_{t+1} = \beta,\mathbf{m}_t - \eta,\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$ $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t + \mathbf{m}_{t+1}$$

This is a **discrete heavy-ball ODE**: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}'' + (1-\beta)\boldsymbol{\theta}' = -\eta,\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}$$

where $\boldsymbol{\theta}''$ is the "acceleration" in parameter space. The momentum term $\beta\mathbf{m}_t$ is literally carrying forward the previous velocity — **inertia**. This is why momentum helps:

- In a long ravine (loss landscape), the tangential component $a_T$ accumulates — you speed up in the ravine direction.
- Noisy gradients partially cancel in the normal component $a_N$ — you don't oscillate wildly across the ravine.

**Nesterov accelerated gradient** looks ahead before computing the gradient — equivalent to a better ODE discretization with faster convergence. Sutskever et al. (2013) showed this achieves the optimal $O(1/t^2)$ convergence rate for convex functions.

### 9.3 Adam and Adaptive Curvature

**Adam** (Kingma & Ba, 2014) maintains:

- $\mathbf{m}_t$: exponential moving average of gradients (≈ velocity)
- $\mathbf{v}_t$: exponential moving average of squared gradients (≈ estimate of local curvature / $a_N$)

The update normalizes the velocity by the estimated curvature: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta,\frac{\hat{\mathbf{m}}_t}{\sqrt{\hat{\mathbf{v}}_t}+\epsilon}$$

This is an **adaptive normal acceleration correction** — in directions of high curvature (oscillation), the effective step is reduced; in directions of low curvature (flat), it is increased. Adam is essentially adapting $a_N$ to the local geometry.

### 9.4 Neural ODEs

**Neural ODEs** (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2018) explicitly model the hidden state as a particle trajectory:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{h}(t)}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}(t), t)$$

Here $f_\theta$ is a neural network that defines the **velocity field** — the analog of $\mathbf{v}(t)$. The forward pass is an ODE solve, not a sequence of layer applications. The backward pass uses the **adjoint method** — a continuous analog of backpropagation derived from the calculus of variations.

The acceleration $\mathbf{h}''(t) = \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial \mathbf{h}}f_\theta + \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial t}$ encodes how the hidden state changes direction in representation space — directly analogous to $\mathbf{a} = a_T\mathbf{T}+a_N\mathbf{N}$.

### 9.5 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

PINNs solve PDEs by embedding the equation of motion into the loss. For instance, learning the trajectory of a charged particle:

$$\mathbf{r}'' = \frac{q}{m}(\mathbf{E} + \mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{B})$$

The loss penalizes violation of this ODE: $$\mathcal{L} = \left|\mathbf{r}''_\theta(t) - \frac{q}{m}\left(\mathbf{E}(t) + \mathbf{r}'_\theta(t)\times\mathbf{B}(t)\right)\right|^2$$

where $\mathbf{r}_\theta(t)$ is a neural network approximating the trajectory. The network must learn both the velocity and acceleration components correctly — $a_T$ (along the trajectory) and $a_N$ (centripetal, from the magnetic force $q\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{B}$, which is always perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}$).

### 9.6 Robotics and Reinforcement Learning

In **model-based RL**, the agent learns a dynamics model: $$\mathbf{s}_{t+1} = f(\mathbf{s}_t, \mathbf{a}_t)$$

which is a discrete version of $\mathbf{r}'' = \mathbf{F}/m$. For robotic arm control:

- **Joint velocities** $\mathbf{v}$ are the control inputs.
- **Joint accelerations** $\mathbf{a}$ are constrained (actuator torque limits).
- The **tangential component** $a_T$ changes the end-effector speed; the **normal component** $a_N$ changes its direction.

Trajectory optimization explicitly minimizes $\int_0^T |\mathbf{a}(t)|^2,dt$ subject to dynamics constraints — minimizing the total acceleration effort, decomposed into $a_T^2 + a_N^2$.

### 9.7 Attention and Transformer Dynamics

Recent theoretical work models the **forward pass of a transformer** as a continuous dynamical system (an ODE in layer depth). The residual stream update at each layer is:

$$\mathbf{x}_{l+1} = \mathbf{x}_l + F_l(\mathbf{x}_l)$$

as $l \to \infty$, this converges to $\mathbf{x}' = F(\mathbf{x})$ — a velocity field on the embedding manifold. The **acceleration** $\mathbf{x}''$ captures how attention heads curve the representation trajectory through token space. This framework (Continuous Depth Networks) enables analysis of representation stability using tools directly from this section.

## 10. Summary

Starting from position $\mathbf{r}(t)$, differentiation gives:

$$\mathbf{r}(t) \xrightarrow{;d/dt;} \mathbf{v}(t) \xrightarrow{;d/dt;} \mathbf{a}(t)$$

The master decomposition of acceleration:

$$\mathbf{a} = \underbrace{v',\mathbf{T}}_{\text{tangential}} + \underbrace{\kappa v^2,\mathbf{N}}_{\text{normal}}$$

with computational formulas:

$$a_T = \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{v}|}, \qquad a_N = \frac{|\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{a}|}{|\mathbf{v}|}, \qquad |\mathbf{a}|^2 = a_T^2 + a_N^2$$

The physical content:

- $a_T$ is what your speedometer feels — rate of speed change.
- $a_N$ is what presses you into your seat on a turn — centripetal, always toward center of curvature.
- **No acceleration ever has a binormal component** — torsion is invisible to Newton's $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$ in 3D.

In machine learning, this framework reappears as: momentum in gradient descent (inertia in parameter space), adaptive curvature correction (Adam), continuous dynamics of neural networks (Neural ODEs), and constrained trajectory optimization in robotics.
