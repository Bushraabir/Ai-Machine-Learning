

> _"Curvature is the rate at which the curve turns — measured not against the clock, but against the road itself."_

## 0. Motivation

We already know $\mathbf{r}'(t)$ is the tangent vector. But two natural questions remain:

1. **How long is the curve?** — this is arc length.
2. **How sharply does the curve bend?** — this is curvature.

Both questions demand we measure _intrinsic_ properties of the curve — properties that don't depend on how fast we happen to traverse it. This forces us to re-parametrize by **arc length**, the natural "ruler" on a curve.

---

## 1. Arc Length

### 1.1 Definition

Let $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle f(t), g(t), h(t)\rangle$ be a smooth curve on $[a, b]$. Partition $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals. The $k$-th chord has length $|\mathbf{r}(t_k) - \mathbf{r}(t_{k-1})|$. Taking the limit of the sum of chord lengths:

$$\boxed{L = \int_a^b |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt = \int_a^b \sqrt{[f'(t)]^2 + [g'(t)]^2 + [h'(t)]^2};dt}$$

**Why $|\mathbf{r}'(t)|$?** By the Mean Value Theorem applied component-wise, $|\mathbf{r}(t_k)-\mathbf{r}(t_{k-1})| \approx |\mathbf{r}'(t_k^*)|,\Delta t_k$. Summing and passing to the limit gives the integral. This is a clean Riemann sum argument.

> **Key insight (MIT 18.02).** $|\mathbf{r}'(t)|$ is the _speed_ of the parametrization — how fast the point moves. Arc length integrates speed over time to get distance. Same formula as $d = \int v,dt$ from physics.

### 1.2 The Arc Length Function

Fix a basepoint $a$. Define:

$$s(t) = \int_a^t |\mathbf{r}'(u)|,du$$

By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus:

$$\frac{ds}{dt} = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|$$

So $s$ measures how much curve has been traversed up to time $t$. The infinitesimal arc length element is:

$$ds = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt$$

### 1.3 Worked Example — Helix

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle \cos t,; \sin t,; t\rangle, \qquad t \in [0, 2\pi]$$

$$\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle -\sin t,; \cos t,; 1\rangle, \qquad |\mathbf{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t + 1} = \sqrt{2}$$

$$L = \int_0^{2\pi} \sqrt{2},dt = 2\sqrt{2},\pi$$

> The helix has constant speed $\sqrt{2}$, so its length is simply speed × time = $\sqrt{2} \cdot 2\pi$. One full turn rises by $2\pi$ in $z$ while wrapping a circle of circumference $2\pi$ — Pythagoras gives $\sqrt{(2\pi)^2 + (2\pi)^2} = 2\sqrt{2}\pi$. ✓

### 1.4 Parametrization Invariance

Arc length is **independent of parametrization**. If $\tilde{\mathbf{r}}(\tau) = \mathbf{r}(t(\tau))$ with $t'(\tau) > 0$, then by the chain rule:

$$|\tilde{\mathbf{r}}'(\tau)| = |\mathbf{r}'(t(\tau))|\cdot t'(\tau)$$

$$\int_{\alpha}^{\beta}|\tilde{\mathbf{r}}'(\tau)|,d\tau = \int_a^b |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt$$

The curve length is a geometric fact — not an artifact of how we label points on it.

## 2. Parametrization by Arc Length

### 2.1 The Natural Parametrization

A curve is **parametrized by arc length** (or **unit-speed**) when:

$$|\mathbf{r}'(s)| = 1 \quad \text{for all } s$$

This is the parametrization where "parameter = distance traveled." It strips away the arbitrary speed of any other parametrization and exposes the curve's pure geometry.

**How to find it:** Given $\mathbf{r}(t)$, compute $s(t) = \int_a^t |\mathbf{r}'(u)|,du$, invert to get $t(s)$, then set $\tilde{\mathbf{r}}(s) = \mathbf{r}(t(s))$.

**Verification:** $|\tilde{\mathbf{r}}'(s)| = |\mathbf{r}'(t(s))| \cdot |t'(s)| = |\mathbf{r}'(t)| \cdot \dfrac{1}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|} = 1$. ✓

### 2.2 Example — Circle

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle R\cos t,; R\sin t\rangle, \qquad |\mathbf{r}'(t)| = R$$

$$s = Rt \implies t = \frac{s}{R}$$

$$\tilde{\mathbf{r}}(s) = \left\langle R\cos\frac{s}{R},; R\sin\frac{s}{R}\right\rangle$$

Check: $|\tilde{\mathbf{r}}'(s)| = \left|\left\langle -\sin\frac{s}{R},; \cos\frac{s}{R}\right\rangle\right| = 1$. ✓

## 3. Curvature

### 3.1 The Core Idea

The unit tangent vector $\mathbf{T} = \mathbf{r}'/|\mathbf{r}'|$ always has magnitude 1, so it lives on the unit sphere. **Curvature measures how rapidly $\mathbf{T}$ turns as we move along the curve.**

But "rapidly" must be measured _per unit of arc length_, not per unit of time — otherwise a fast-moving particle on a straight line would appear to have curvature.

### 3.2 Definition

$$\boxed{\kappa = \left|\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds}\right|}$$

where $s$ is arc length. Equivalently, by the chain rule $\dfrac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \dfrac{d\mathbf{T}/dt}{ds/dt}$:

$$\kappa = \frac{|\mathbf{T}'(t)|}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|}$$

**Geometric reading:** $\kappa$ is large where the curve bends sharply (tight corner); $\kappa = 0$ on a straight line; $\kappa$ is constant on a circle.

```
Low curvature:              High curvature:
                                ╭──╮
────────────────             ───╯  ╰───
κ ≈ 0                           κ >> 0
```

### 3.3 Computational Formula (Stewart §13.3)

Computing $\mathbf{T}$ and then $\mathbf{T}'$ is often messy. The following formula is far more practical:

$$\boxed{\kappa = \frac{|\mathbf{r}'(t) \times \mathbf{r}''(t)|}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|^3}}$$

**Proof.** Let $v = |\mathbf{r}'| = ds/dt$. Then $\mathbf{r}' = v\mathbf{T}$. Differentiate:

$$\mathbf{r}'' = v'\mathbf{T} + v\mathbf{T}'$$

Take the cross product with $\mathbf{r}' = v\mathbf{T}$:

$$\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}'' = v\mathbf{T}\times(v'\mathbf{T} + v\mathbf{T}') = v^2(\mathbf{T}\times\mathbf{T}')$$

Since $\mathbf{T}\perp\mathbf{T}'$ (constant length — recall §7 of the previous note) and $|\mathbf{T}|=1$:

$$|\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}''| = v^2|\mathbf{T}'|$$

But $\kappa = |\mathbf{T}'|/v$, so $|\mathbf{T}'| = \kappa v$, giving:

$$|\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}''| = v^2 \cdot \kappa v = \kappa v^3 = \kappa|\mathbf{r}'|^3$$

$$\therefore \quad \kappa = \frac{|\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}''|}{|\mathbf{r}'|^3} \qquad \square$$

### 3.4 Curvature of a Plane Curve $y = f(x)$

For $\mathbf{r}(x) = \langle x, f(x)\rangle$:

$$\mathbf{r}' = \langle 1, f'\rangle, \quad \mathbf{r}'' = \langle 0, f''\rangle$$

$$\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}'' = \langle 1,f',0\rangle\times\langle 0,f'',0\rangle = \langle 0, 0, f''\rangle \implies |\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}''| = |f''|$$

$$|\mathbf{r}'|^3 = (1+[f']^2)^{3/2}$$

$$\boxed{\kappa = \frac{|f''(x)|}{(1+[f'(x)]^2)^{3/2}}}$$

> At an inflection point $f'' = 0$, curvature is zero — the curve momentarily "straightens." At a local max/min where $f' = 0$, curvature simplifies to $|f''|$ — pure second derivative.

## 4. The Frenet–Serret Frame

Curvature is part of a richer structure — the **moving frame** attached to a curve.

### 4.1 The Three Vectors

|Vector|Definition|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$\mathbf{T}$|$\mathbf{r}'/|\mathbf{r}'|
|$\mathbf{N}$|$\mathbf{T}'/|\mathbf{T}'|
|$\mathbf{B}$|$\mathbf{T}\times\mathbf{N}$|Unit binormal — axis of the osculating plane|

These three form an orthonormal basis at every point of the curve — a "local coordinate system" that travels with the curve.

**Why $\mathbf{N}\perp\mathbf{T}$?** Since $|\mathbf{T}|=1$, we have $\mathbf{T}\cdot\mathbf{T}'=0$, so $\mathbf{T}' \perp \mathbf{T}$ always. $\mathbf{N}$ is just $\mathbf{T}'$ normalized.

### 4.2 The Frenet–Serret Equations

$$\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds} = \kappa,\mathbf{N}$$

$$\frac{d\mathbf{N}}{ds} = -\kappa,\mathbf{T} + \tau,\mathbf{B}$$

$$\frac{d\mathbf{B}}{ds} = -\tau,\mathbf{N}$$

where $\tau$ is the **torsion** — the rate at which the curve twists out of its osculating plane.

- $\kappa > 0$: curve bends
- $\tau = 0$: curve is planar
- $\tau \neq 0$: curve twists in 3D (like a helix)

### 4.3 Geometric Picture

```
                B (binormal)
                ↑
                │
                │   osculating plane
     N ←────── • ──────→ T (tangent)
    (normal)   P
               
   The curve bends in the T-N plane.
   Torsion measures how fast B rotates.
```

## 5. The Osculating Circle

At each point $P$ on a curve, the **osculating circle** (from Latin _osculari_, "to kiss") is the circle that:

- Passes through $P$
- Has the same tangent as the curve at $P$
- Has the same curvature $\kappa$ as the curve at $P$

Its **radius of curvature** is:

$$\rho = \frac{1}{\kappa}$$

Its center lies at $P + \rho,\mathbf{N}$ — a distance $\rho$ along the normal direction.

> **Intuition.** On a straight road, $\kappa = 0$, $\rho = \infty$: the "osculating circle" has infinite radius. On a sharp hairpin, $\kappa$ is large, $\rho$ is small. A circle of radius $R$ has $\kappa = 1/R$ everywhere.

### 5.1 Example — Parabola

$$y = x^2, \quad f'=2x, \quad f''=2$$

$$\kappa(x) = \frac{2}{(1+4x^2)^{3/2}}$$

At $x = 0$ (vertex): $\kappa = 2$, $\rho = 1/2$.

At $x \to \infty$: $\kappa \to 0$ — the parabola flattens out.

```
               ↑ y
          ─────┤─────
        ╱      │      ╲
       ╱   ⊙ ← osculating circle
      ╱    radius 1/2
     ╱─────┴─────╲
                   x
```

## 6. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Curvature of the Helix

$$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle a\cos t,; a\sin t,; bt\rangle \qquad (a,b > 0)$$

$$\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle -a\sin t,; a\cos t,; b\rangle, \qquad |\mathbf{r}'| = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$$

$$\mathbf{r}''(t) = \langle -a\cos t,; -a\sin t,; 0\rangle$$

$$\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}'' = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ -a\sin t & a\cos t & b \ -a\cos t & -a\sin t & 0\end{vmatrix}$$

$$= \langle a\cdot 0 - b(-a\sin t),; b(-a\cos t) - (-a\sin t)\cdot 0,; (-a\sin t)(-a\sin t) - (a\cos t)(-a\cos t)\rangle$$

$$= \langle ab\sin t,; -ab\cos t,; a^2\rangle$$

$$|\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}''| = \sqrt{a^2b^2\sin^2 t + a^2b^2\cos^2 t + a^4} = \sqrt{a^2b^2+a^4} = a\sqrt{a^2+b^2}$$

$$\kappa = \frac{a\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}{(a^2+b^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{a}{a^2+b^2}$$

**The curvature of a helix is constant.** When $b=0$: circle with $\kappa = 1/a$. As $b\to\infty$ (helix stretches vertically): $\kappa\to 0$. ✓

### Example 2 — Curvature of $y = \sin x$

$$f' = \cos x, \quad f'' = -\sin x$$

$$\kappa(x) = \frac{|\sin x|}{(1+\cos^2 x)^{3/2}}$$

- At $x = \pi/2$: $\kappa = \dfrac{1}{(1+0)^{3/2}} = 1$, $\rho = 1$.
- At $x = 0$: $\kappa = 0$ (inflection point — straightening).
- Maximum curvature occurs at the peaks and troughs, as expected.

## 7. Summary of Key Formulas

$$L = \int_a^b |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt \qquad \text{[arc length]}$$

$$\frac{ds}{dt} = |\mathbf{r}'(t)| \qquad \text{[speed = rate of arc length]}$$

$$\kappa = \left|\frac{d\mathbf{T}}{ds}\right| = \frac{|\mathbf{T}'(t)|}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|} = \frac{|\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}''|}{|\mathbf{r}'|^3} \qquad \text{[curvature]}$$

$$\kappa = \frac{|f''|}{(1+[f']^2)^{3/2}} \qquad \text{[for } y=f(x) \text{]}$$

$$\rho = \frac{1}{\kappa} \qquad \text{[radius of curvature]}$$

$$\mathbf{T} = \frac{\mathbf{r}'}{|\mathbf{r}'|}, \quad \mathbf{N} = \frac{\mathbf{T}'}{|\mathbf{T}'|}, \quad \mathbf{B} = \mathbf{T}\times\mathbf{N}$$

## 8. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 8.1 Loss Landscape Geometry — Curvature of Optimization Paths

During gradient descent, the parameter trajectory $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$ is a curve in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Its curvature $\kappa$ measures how sharply the optimization path bends. High curvature near a saddle point means the optimizer is changing direction rapidly — a sign of instability.

The **Hessian** $H = \nabla^2\mathcal{L}$ is the second derivative of the loss, encoding the curvature of the loss _surface_. Second-order optimizers (Newton's method, L-BFGS) explicitly use $H$ — they are computing the osculating paraboloid of $\mathcal{L}$, the surface analogue of the osculating circle.

### 8.2 Natural Gradient — Arc Length in Parameter Space

The ordinary gradient $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ measures steepness in Euclidean parameter space. But the **natural gradient** (Amari, 1998) accounts for the _Riemannian metric_ on the statistical manifold of model distributions:

$$\tilde{\nabla}\mathcal{L} = F^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}$$

where $F$ is the **Fisher information matrix**. This corrects for the fact that equal Euclidean steps in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$-space do not correspond to equal arc lengths in distribution space. The natural gradient is precisely the gradient after re-parametrizing by the arc length of the information geometry.

### 8.3 Attention and Sequence Curvature

In Transformer models, the hidden states $\mathbf{h}_1, \mathbf{h}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{h}_n$ form a discrete curve in representation space. **Attention mechanisms** can be interpreted as computing a weighted average of positions on this curve — a discretized integral operator analogous to $\int \mathbf{r}(t),w(t),dt$.

The curvature of the hidden-state trajectory correlates with how "surprising" or semantically complex the sequence is at that position.

### 8.4 Neural ODEs and Curvature Regularization

In a Neural ODE $\frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = F(\mathbf{h}, t)$, the trajectory $\mathbf{h}(t)$ is a curve in latent space. A natural regularizer is:

$$\mathcal{R} = \int_0^T |\mathbf{h}''(t)|^2,dt$$

This penalizes large acceleration (equivalently, high curvature of the hidden-state trajectory), encouraging the network to learn smooth, gradual transformations. This is directly the squared curvature functional from differential geometry.

### 8.5 Geodesics and Shortest Path in Latent Space

In generative models (VAEs, diffusion models), the **latent space** has a geometry. Interpolating between two latent codes along the **geodesic** (the curve of minimal arc length) produces more natural interpolations than linear interpolation in Euclidean space. This requires computing:

$$L[\gamma] = \int_0^1 |\gamma'(t)|_g,dt$$

where $|\cdot|_g$ is the Riemannian metric induced by the generative model — a direct application of our arc length formula.

### 8.6 Curvature of Decision Boundaries

In classification, the decision boundary is a surface (or curve in 2D). Its curvature measures how "wiggly" the boundary is:

- **High curvature** → overfitting (the model memorized the training data with sharp twists)
- **Low curvature** → smoother generalization

Regularization methods (weight decay, dropout, spectral normalization) implicitly reduce the curvature of decision boundaries.

> **Summary table:**

|Classical Concept|ML / DL Analogue|
|---|---|
|Arc length $L = \int|\mathbf{r}'|
|Curvature $\kappa = \|\mathbf{r}'\times\mathbf{r}''\|/\|\mathbf{r}'\|^3$|Hessian eigenvalues; loss landscape curvature|
|Radius of curvature $\rho = 1/\kappa$|Inverse sharpness of a loss minimum (flat minima generalize better)|
|Osculating circle|Local quadratic approximation used by 2nd-order optimizers|
|Arc-length parametrization|Natural gradient (reparametrize by Fisher information)|
|Frenet–Serret frame ${\mathbf{T,N,B}}$|Principal curvature directions of the loss surface (Hessian eigenvectors)|
|Geodesic|Shortest-path interpolation in latent space (VAE/diffusion)|
|Curvature of plane curve|Curvature of decision boundary; high $\kappa$ = overfitting|
|Curvature regularization $\int \|\mathbf{h}''\|^2$|Smoothness regularization in Neural ODEs|
