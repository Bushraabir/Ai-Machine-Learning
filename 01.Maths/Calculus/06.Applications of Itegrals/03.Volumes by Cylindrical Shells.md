## 0. Motivation вҖ” Why Another Method?

Recall from [[Volumes by Disks and Washers]] that we sliced a solid of revolution **perpendicular** to the axis, getting circular cross-sections. This works beautifully until it doesn't.

**The failure mode.** Consider rotating the region bounded by $y = x - x^2$ and $y = 0$ about the $y$-axis. To use washers, you'd need to express $x$ as a function of $y$ вҖ” which requires solving a quadratic. That's manageable here, but imagine $y = \sin(x^2)$. Inverting that is impossible in closed form.

**The fix.** Instead of slicing perpendicular to the axis, slice _parallel_ to it. Each slice becomes a thin cylindrical shell. This is the **shell method**.

**Volume of one shell.** The shell has:

- Inner radius: $r_1 = x$
- Outer radius: $r_2 = x + \Delta x$
- Height: $h = f(x)$

$$\Delta V = \pi r_2^2 \cdot h - \pi r_1^2 \cdot h = \pi h\bigl[(x+\Delta x)^2 - x^2\bigr]$$

Expand: $$\Delta V = \pi f(x)\bigl[2x,\Delta x + (\Delta x)^2\bigr]$$

As $\Delta x \to 0$, the $(\Delta x)^2$ term is negligible: $$\Delta V \approx 2\pi x \cdot f(x),\Delta x$$

The factor $2\pi x$ is the **circumference** of the shell at radius $x$. We are literally unrolling the shell into a flat slab of dimensions: circumference $\times$ height $\times$ thickness.

$$\boxed{\Delta V \approx \underbrace{2\pi x}_{\text{circumference}} \cdot \underbrace{f(x)}_{\text{height}} \cdot \underbrace{\Delta x}_{\text{thickness}}}$$

## 2. The Main Theorem

> **Theorem (Shell Method вҖ” Rotation about the $y$-axis).** Let $f$ be continuous and $f(x) \geq 0$ on $[a,b]$ with $0 \leq a < b$. The volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region under $y = f(x)$ on $[a,b]$ about the $y$-axis is: $$V = \int_a^b 2\pi x, f(x), dx$$

**Proof sketch.** Partition $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals. The Riemann sum $\sum_{i=1}^n 2\pi x_i^* f(x_i^*),\Delta x_i$ approximates the total volume (as shown in В§1). By the definition of the definite integral, taking $n \to \infty$ (so $|\Delta| \to 0$) gives the integral. $\square$

---

## 3. The Mnemonic вҖ” CHIT

When using the shell method, write down:

$$dV = 2\pi \cdot \underbrace{(\text{shell radius})}_{\text{C}} \cdot \underbrace{(\text{shell height})}_{\text{H}} \cdot \underbrace{dx}_{\text{IT (thickness)}}$$

_Circumference Г— Height Г— thickness_ = **CHIT** (or just think: unroll the shell into a flat box).

## 4. Worked Examples

### Example 1 вҖ” The Classic

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region bounded by $y = x - x^2$ and $y = 0$ about the $y$-axis.

**Solution.**

First, find the $x$-intercepts: $x - x^2 = 0 \Rightarrow x(1-x) = 0$, so $x \in [0,1]$.

Shell radius at position $x$: just $x$ (distance to $y$-axis). Shell height: $f(x) = x - x^2$.

$$V = \int_0^1 2\pi x(x - x^2),dx = 2\pi \int_0^1 (x^2 - x^3),dx$$

$$= 2\pi \left[\frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^1 = 2\pi\left(\frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{4}\right) = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{12} = \frac{\pi}{6}$$

> **Cross-check intuition.** Try setting this up as a washer integral (rotating about $y$-axis). You'd need $x = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{1-4y}}{2}$. The shell method was clearly preferable.

---

### Example 2 вҖ” Non-obvious Shell Radius

**Problem.** Rotate the region between $y = \sqrt{x}$ and $y = x$ (for $x \in [0,1]$) about the line $x = 2$.

**ASCII visualization:**

```

  0      1      2  (axis of rotation)
```

**Shell radius.** The axis is $x = 2$. A shell at position $x$ has radius $= 2 - x$ (distance from $x$ to the axis, which is to the _right_).

**Shell height.** $h(x) = \sqrt{x} - x$ (top minus bottom).

$$V = \int_0^1 2\pi(2-x)(\sqrt{x}-x),dx$$

Expand: $$= 2\pi \int_0^1 \left(2\sqrt{x} - 2x - x^{3/2} + x^2\right)dx$$

$$= 2\pi \left[\frac{4x^{3/2}}{3} - x^2 - \frac{2x^{5/2}}{5} + \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1$$

$$= 2\pi \left(\frac{4}{3} - 1 - \frac{2}{5} + \frac{1}{3}\right) = 2\pi \cdot \frac{8}{15} = \frac{16\pi}{15}$$

### Example 3 вҖ” Rotation about the $x$-axis (Shells Are Horizontal)

**Problem.** Rotate the region bounded by $x = y^2$ and $x = 1$ about the $x$-axis.

**When rotating about the $x$-axis**, the natural strips are **horizontal** (in $y$). The formula becomes:

$$V = \int_c^d 2\pi y \cdot g(y),dy$$

where $g(y)$ is the horizontal extent of the region at height $y$.

**Setup.** The region has $y \in [0,1]$ (by symmetry, consider the upper half and double, or integrate from 0 to 1 with the full horizontal extent).

At height $y$: the region extends from $x = y^2$ to $x = 1$, so $g(y) = 1 - y^2$.

$$V = \int_0^1 2\pi y(1 - y^2),dy = 2\pi \int_0^1 (y - y^3),dy$$

$$= 2\pi \left[\frac{y^2}{2} - \frac{y^4}{4}\right]_0^1 = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2}$$

## 5. General Formula вҖ” Any Axis

The key is always: **identify the shell radius and shell height as functions of the integration variable**.

|Axis of Rotation|Integration Variable|Shell Radius|Shell Height|
|---|---|---|---|
|$y$-axis|$x$|$x$|$f(x)$|
|$x = k$ (right of region)|$x$|$k - x$|$f(x)$|
|$x = k$ (left of region)|$x$|$x - k$|$f(x)$|
|$x$-axis|$y$|$y$|$g(y)$|
|$y = k$ (above region)|$y$|$k - y$|$g(y)$|
|$y = k$ (below region)|$y$|$y - k$|$g(y)$|

**The general template:**

$$V = 2\pi \int_{\text{start}}^{\text{end}} (\text{shell radius}) \cdot (\text{shell height}),d(\text{variable})$$

## 6. Shell vs. Washer вҖ” Decision Heuristic

```
                     Is the integrand expressible naturally 
                     in the variable parallel to the axis?
                              /            \
                            YES             NO
                             |               |
                      Use WASHER         Use SHELL
                      (perpendicular     (parallel
                        slices)           slices)
```

More concretely:

- **Use washers** when rotating about $y$-axis and $x = g(y)$ is easy; or rotating about $x$-axis and $y = f(x)$ is easy.
- **Use shells** when the function is hard to invert, or when the shell radius and height are naturally expressed in the integration variable you want.

> _Personal note: I almost always try the shell method first when rotating about a vertical axis with a function given as $y = f(x)$. The algebra is usually cleaner._

## 7. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

At first glance, this seems like pure geometric calculus. But the shell method вҖ” and integration in general вҖ” underlies several fundamental concepts in modern ML.

### 7.1 Probability Density Functions and Marginalization

In Bayesian ML, we constantly marginalize joint distributions:

$$p(x) = \int p(x, z),dz$$

The shell method is conceptually the **same decomposition**: we decompose 3D volume into infinitesimal shells (2D surfaces Г— thickness), just as we decompose a joint distribution into conditional slices. The change-of-variables in integration is everywhere in probabilistic modeling.

### 7.2 Gaussian Integrals and the Normal Distribution

The normalization constant of the Gaussian distribution requires computing:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2},dx = \sqrt{\pi}$$

The standard proof uses **rotation to polar coordinates** вҖ” which is precisely the spirit of the shell/disk decomposition:

$$\left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2},dx\right)^2 = \iint e^{-(x^2+y^2)},dx,dy = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-r^2} \cdot 2\pi r,dr$$

That last integrand $2\pi r \cdot e^{-r^2}$ is **exactly the shell method formula** in disguise ($2\pi r$ = circumference, $e^{-r^2}$ = height of the "function", $dr$ = thickness). This is why the Gaussian is so analytically tractable вҖ” it separates in polar coordinates.

### 7.3 Volume of the $n$-Ball вҖ” The Curse of Dimensionality

In high-dimensional ML (think: weight spaces of neural networks), a key geometric fact is:

> **Most of the volume of a high-dimensional ball lies near its surface (shell).**

This is made rigorous by computing the volume $V_n(R)$ of an $n$-dimensional ball of radius $R$:

$$V_n(R) = \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2 + 1)} R^n$$

The **shell** at radius $r$ has "volume" (surface measure Г— thickness):

$$dV_n = n \cdot \frac{\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2+1)} r^{n-1},dr$$

For large $n$, the factor $r^{n-1}$ concentrates near $r = R$. The fraction of the ball's volume within $\epsilon$ of the surface is:

$$1 - \left(1 - \frac{\epsilon}{R}\right)^n \to 1 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

This is the **curse of dimensionality** вҖ” random points in high dimensions are nearly always near the boundary shell. It explains why:

- Distance-based algorithms (KNN) degrade in high dimensions
- Sampling from high-dimensional distributions concentrates on shells
- Neural networks live in very high-dimensional parameter spaces where geometric intuition from 3D fails

### 7.4 Radial Basis Functions and Kernel Methods

Many kernel functions in SVMs and Gaussian Processes depend only on $|x - x'|$ вҖ” they are **radially symmetric**. Computing feature map integrals with such kernels uses exactly shell-method decompositions:

$$\int_{\mathbb{R}^d} K(|x|),dx = \int_0^\infty K(r) \cdot S_{d-1}(r),dr$$

where $S_{d-1}(r)$ is the surface area of the $(d-1)$-sphere of radius $r$. This is the $d$-dimensional generalization of our $2\pi r$ circumference factor.

### 7.5 Normalizing Flows

**Normalizing flows** are deep learning models that learn invertible transformations $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$ to transform a simple distribution into a complex one. The change-of-density formula uses the **Jacobian determinant**:

$$p_X(x) = p_Z(f^{-1}(x)) \cdot \left|\det \frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial x}\right|$$

For radially symmetric flows, this Jacobian computation reduces to a 1D integral вҖ” again, the shell decomposition. The shell method is the 1D instance of the general "co-area formula" from geometric measure theory.

## 8. A Rigorous Note on Validity

The formula $V = \int_a^b 2\pi x f(x),dx$ requires:

1. **$f$ continuous on $[a,b]$** вҖ” ensures Riemann integrability.
2. **$f(x) \geq 0$ on $[a,b]$** вҖ” shells have non-negative height; otherwise use $|f(x)|$ or split the integral.
3. **$0 \leq a$** вҖ” the axis of rotation ($y$-axis, $x = 0$) must not cut through the region. If $a < 0$, the shell radius isn't simply $x$; you need to be more careful about cancellation.

When $f$ can be negative or the region straddles the axis, write:

$$V = \int_a^b 2\pi x [f_{\text{top}}(x) - f_{\text{bottom}}(x)],dx$$

and ensure $f_{\text{top}} \geq f_{\text{bottom}}$ throughout.

## 10. Quick Reference вҖ” Setup Checklist

When you encounter a volume of revolution problem:

- [ ] Identify the region (sketch it!)
- [ ] Identify the axis of rotation
- [ ] Choose method: can I express everything naturally in one variable?
- [ ] If shells: write $dV = 2\pi \cdot r \cdot h \cdot d(\text{var})$
- [ ] Determine $r$ = distance from the strip to the axis
- [ ] Determine $h$ = length of the strip (top $-$ bottom or right $-$ left)
- [ ] Find limits of integration
- [ ] Integrate and simplify
