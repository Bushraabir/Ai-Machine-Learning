

> _"Volume is what happens when you take the area idea seriously in three dimensions. The transition from area to volume is conceptually one step — integrate cross-sectional area — but that one step opens a vast landscape of three-dimensional geometry."_

---

## § 0 — The Unifying Principle

Every volume formula in this note is a single idea dressed in different clothing:

$$\boxed{V = \int_a^b A(x),dx}$$

where $A(x)$ is the **cross-sectional area** of the solid at position $x$. Slice the solid into infinitely thin slabs of thickness $dx$, compute the area of each cross-section, and integrate. This is Cavalieri's Principle elevated to a theorem.

The only question in any volume problem is: **what is $A(x)$?**

---

## § 1 — Volumes by Cross-Sections (General)

### Theorem (Cavalieri's Principle)

If two solids have equal cross-sectional area $A(x)$ at every $x \in [a,b]$, then they have equal volume, regardless of their shape.

### The Volume Formula

Let $S$ be a solid bounded between $x = a$ and $x = b$. If the cross-section of $S$ perpendicular to the $x$-axis at position $x$ has area $A(x)$, and $A$ is integrable on $[a,b]$, then:

$$V = \int_a^b A(x),dx$$

**Derivation.** Partition $[a,b]$ with points $x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n$. The slab between $x_{i-1}$ and $x_i$ has approximate volume $A(x_i^*),\Delta x_i$. Summing:

$$V \approx \sum_{i=1}^n A(x_i^*),\Delta x_i$$

Taking $|P| \to 0$ gives the integral. $\square$

```
Solid S sliced perpendicular to the x-axis:

         A(x) ← cross-section at x
          ╭───╮
    ╭─────╯███╰──────╮
    │████████████████│
    │█████A(x)███████│  ← solid
    │████████████████│
    ╰────────────────╯
    a        x       b
    
    V = ∫ₐᵇ A(x) dx
    
    Infinitely many slabs of thickness dx,
    each contributing A(x)·dx to total volume.
```

## § 2 — The Disk Method

### Setup

Rotate the region under $y = f(x) \ge 0$ on $[a,b]$ about the $x$-axis. Each cross-section perpendicular to the $x$-axis is a **disk** with radius $f(x)$.

$$A(x) = \pi,[f(x)]^2$$

### Formula

$$\boxed{V = \pi\int_a^b [f(x)]^2,dx}$$

```
y = f(x) rotated about x-axis:

     y
     │    ╭──────╮
     │   ╱        ╲        Rotate
     │  ╱          ╲  ─────────→
     │ ╱            ╲
     │╱              ╲
     └────────────────── x
     a                b

     Each cross-section is a disk:
     
         ← r = f(x) →
         ╭───────────╮
        ╱             ╲
       │    ○  disk    │  radius = f(x)
        ╲             ╱  area = π[f(x)]²
         ╰───────────╯
```

### Rotation About the $y$-Axis

If the region $x = g(y)$ on $[c,d]$ is rotated about the $y$-axis:

$$V = \pi\int_c^d [g(y)]^2,dy$$

## § 3 — The Washer Method

### Setup

Rotate the region between $f(x) \ge g(x) \ge 0$ on $[a,b]$ about the $x$-axis. Each cross-section is an annulus (disk with a hole) — a **washer**.

- Outer radius: $R(x) = f(x)$
- Inner radius: $r(x) = g(x)$
- Cross-sectional area: $A(x) = \pi R^2 - \pi r^2 = \pi\bigl([f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2\bigr)$

### Formula

$$\boxed{V = \pi\int_a^b \bigl([f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2\bigr),dx}$$

```
Washer cross-section at position x:

     ╭───────────────╮  ← outer radius R = f(x)
    ╱  ╭───────────╮  ╲
   │  ╱    hole     ╲  │ ← inner radius r = g(x)
   │ │    (empty)    │ │
   │  ╲             ╱  │
    ╲  ╰───────────╯  ╱
     ╰───────────────╯

  Area = π(R² - r²) = π([f(x)]² - [g(x)]²)
```

> **Common error.** The washer formula is $\pi(R^2 - r^2)$, not $\pi(R - r)^2$. These are different. Always square each radius separately, then subtract.

## § 4 — The Shell Method

### Motivation

The disk/washer method slices perpendicular to the axis of rotation, giving circular cross-sections. The **shell method** slices parallel to the axis of rotation, giving cylindrical shells. For certain regions, shells give a single clean integral where washers require splitting.

### Setup

Rotate the region under $y = f(x) \ge 0$ on $[a,b]$ (with $a \ge 0$) about the $y$-axis. A thin vertical strip at position $x$ with width $dx$ sweeps out a **cylindrical shell**:

- Radius: $x$
- Height: $f(x)$
- Thickness: $dx$
- Volume of shell: $2\pi x \cdot f(x),dx$ (circumference $\times$ height $\times$ thickness)

### Formula

$$\boxed{V = 2\pi\int_a^b x,f(x),dx}$$

```
Shell method — rotating about y-axis:

     y
     │    f(x)
     │  ╭──────╮
     │  │██████│ ← thin strip at x
     │  │██████│   width dx, height f(x)
     │  │██████│
     └──┼──────┼──→ x
        x  x+dx

  This strip sweeps a cylindrical shell:
  
       ╭──────────────────────╮
      ╱│                      │╲
     │ │  ← shell radius x    │ │
      ╲│  height f(x)         │╱
       ╰──────────────────────╯
       
  Volume = 2πx · f(x) · dx
```

### General Shell Formula

For rotation about the $y$-axis with region between $f$ and $g$ ($f \ge g$):

$$V = 2\pi\int_a^b x,[f(x) - g(x)],dx$$

For rotation about the $x$-axis, integrate with respect to $y$:

$$V = 2\pi\int_c^d y,[f(y) - g(y)],dy$$

### Disk vs. Shell: Which to Use?

|Situation|Preferred Method|
|---|---|
|Rotating about $x$-axis, region given as $y = f(x)$|Disk/Washer in $x$|
|Rotating about $y$-axis, region given as $y = f(x)$|Shell in $x$ (avoids inverting $f$)|
|Rotating about $y$-axis, region given as $x = g(y)$|Disk/Washer in $y$|
|Rotating about $x$-axis, region given as $x = g(y)$|Shell in $y$|

The shell method is typically superior when inverting the function to change variables would be messy.

## § 5 — Rotation About Lines Other Than the Axes

When rotating about the line $y = k$ (instead of $y = 0$), the radius is the _distance_ from the curve to the axis.

- Region above $y = k$: radius $= f(x) - k$
- Region below $y = k$: radius $= k - f(x)$

**Example.** Rotating $y = x^2$ about $y = -1$: $$V = \pi\int_a^b [x^2 - (-1)]^2,dx = \pi\int_a^b [x^2 + 1]^2,dx$$

Similarly for rotation about $x = k$: replace $x$ with $|x - k|$ as the radius in the disk formula, or adjust the shell radius.

> **The key.** Radius always means the perpendicular distance from the curve to the axis of rotation. Draw a picture. Identify the distance formula. Then plug into the universal formula $V = \int A(x),dx$.

## § 6 — Volumes by Known Cross-Sections (Non-Circular)

Not all solids come from rotation. A solid may have cross-sections that are squares, triangles, semicircles, or any shape.

### Method

If the cross-sections perpendicular to the $x$-axis are known shapes, express $A(x)$ in terms of a characteristic length determined by the base curve, then integrate.

**Common cross-section areas:**

|Shape|If side/diameter $= s$|Area $A(x)$|
|---|---|---|
|Square|$s = f(x) - g(x)$|$[f(x)-g(x)]^2$|
|Equilateral triangle|$s = f(x) - g(x)$|$\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}[f(x)-g(x)]^2$|
|Semicircle|diameter $= f(x)-g(x)$|$\frac{\pi}{8}[f(x)-g(x)]^2$|
|Isosceles right triangle (leg $= s$)|$s = f(x)-g(x)$|$\frac{1}{2}[f(x)-g(x)]^2$|

**Example.** The base of a solid is the region between $y = \sqrt{x}$ and $y = 0$ on $[0,4]$. Cross-sections perpendicular to the $x$-axis are squares with side $= \sqrt{x}$:

$$V = \int_0^4 (\sqrt{x})^2,dx = \int_0^4 x,dx = \left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^4 = 8$$

## § 7 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Disk Method

Find the volume of the solid obtained by rotating $y = \sqrt{x}$ on $[0,4]$ about the $x$-axis.

$$V = \pi\int_0^4 (\sqrt{x})^2,dx = \pi\int_0^4 x,dx = \pi\left[\frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^4 = 8\pi$$

---

### Example 2 — Washer Method

Find the volume obtained by rotating the region between $y = x$ and $y = x^2$ on $[0,1]$ about the $x$-axis.

Intersections: $x = x^2 \Rightarrow x = 0, 1$. On $[0,1]$: $x \ge x^2$, so $R = x$, $r = x^2$.

$$V = \pi\int_0^1 (x^2 - x^4),dx = \pi\left[\frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^1 = \pi\left(\frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{5}\right) = \frac{2\pi}{15}$$

---

### Example 3 — Shell Method

Find the volume obtained by rotating $y = x - x^2$ (above $y=0$) on $[0,1]$ about the $y$-axis.

Inverting $y = x - x^2$ for $x$ in terms of $y$ is messy. Use shells.

$$V = 2\pi\int_0^1 x(x - x^2),dx = 2\pi\int_0^1 (x^2 - x^3),dx = 2\pi\left[\frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^4}{4}\right]_0^1 = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{12} = \frac{\pi}{6}$$

---

### Example 4 — Rotation About $y = 1$

Rotate the region between $y = x^2$ and $y = 0$ on $[0,1]$ about $y = 1$.

The axis $y=1$ is _above_ the region. Distances:

- Outer radius (from axis down to $y=0$): $R = 1 - 0 = 1$
- Inner radius (from axis down to $y = x^2$): $r = 1 - x^2$

$$V = \pi\int_0^1(R^2 - r^2),dx = \pi\int_0^1\bigl(1 - (1-x^2)^2\bigr),dx$$

$$= \pi\int_0^1(1 - 1 + 2x^2 - x^4),dx = \pi\int_0^1(2x^2 - x^4),dx$$

$$= \pi\left[\frac{2x^3}{3} - \frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^1 = \pi\left(\frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{5}\right) = \frac{7\pi}{15}$$

---

### Example 5 — Classic: Volume of a Sphere

The sphere of radius $r$ is the rotation of $y = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$ about the $x$-axis on $[-r, r]$. Cross-sections are disks of radius $\sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$:

$$V = \pi\int_{-r}^{r}(r^2 - x^2),dx = \pi\left[r^2 x - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-r}^{r} = \pi\left[\left(r^3 - \frac{r^3}{3}\right) - \left(-r^3 + \frac{r^3}{3}\right)\right] = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$$

The familiar formula, derived from first principles. $\square$

---

### Example 6 — Shell, Region Between Two Curves

Rotate the region between $y = x+2$ and $y = x^2$ about the $y$-axis.

Intersections: $x+2 = x^2 \Rightarrow x = -1, 2$.

For $x \ge 0$: shells work cleanly. But the region extends to $x = -1 < 0$.

For shells about the $y$-axis with region crossing $x = 0$, the shell radius is $|x|$. Split at $x = 0$:

$$V = 2\pi\int_{-1}^{0}|x|(x+2-x^2),dx + 2\pi\int_0^2 x(x+2-x^2),dx$$

In the first piece, $|x| = -x$ for $x < 0$:

$$V = 2\pi\int_{-1}^0 (-x)(x+2-x^2),dx + 2\pi\int_0^2 x(x+2-x^2),dx$$

Computing each (left as exercise): $V = \dfrac{7\pi}{6} + \dfrac{8\pi}{3} = \dfrac{7\pi + 16\pi}{6} = \dfrac{23\pi}{6}$.

> Or: use the washer method in $y$, where the region is described cleanly as $x = g(y)$ with a single outer-inner pair. This is often cleaner for such problems.

## § 9 — Connection to MIT 18.02

In 18.02, the volume formula $V = \int_a^b A(x),dx$ is recovered as a **iterated integral**:

$$V = \iiint_S dV = \int_a^b\left(\iint_{D(x)} dA\right)dx = \int_a^b A(x),dx$$

where $D(x)$ is the cross-sectional region at $x$. This is Fubini's theorem — the triple integral over a 3D solid factors into an outer integral over $x$ and an inner double integral over the cross-section.

**Solids of revolution in cylindrical coordinates.** The solid obtained by rotating $y = f(x)$ about the $x$-axis is described in cylindrical coordinates $(x, r, \theta)$ as ${(x,r,\theta) : a \le x \le b,\ 0 \le r \le f(x),\ 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi}$. The volume is:

$$V = \int_a^b\int_0^{f(x)}\int_0^{2\pi} r,d\theta,dr,dx = \int_a^b\int_0^{f(x)} 2\pi r,dr,dx = \int_a^b \pi[f(x)]^2,dx$$

The disk formula is exactly the triple integral in cylindrical coordinates with the $\theta$ and $r$ integrations carried out first.

**The shell formula from cylindrical coordinates.** Reordering integration — integrating over $x$ first, then $r$:

$$V = \int_0^R \int_0^{2\pi} \int_{x: r \le f(x)} r,dx,d\theta,dr = 2\pi\int_0^R r \cdot (\text{length at radius } r),dr$$

This is the shell formula. The two methods are the same triple integral evaluated in different orders — **Fubini's theorem**.

## § 10 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 10.1 — Volumetric Data and 3D Convolutions

Medical imaging (CT, MRI), LiDAR point clouds, and 3D object recognition all involve **volumetric data** — functions defined on $\mathbb{R}^3$. Computing the total mass, density integral, or activation volume of a 3D feature map is:

$$V = \int\int\int_S f(x, y, z),dV$$

which reduces to the Cavalieri principle when $f = 1$ (indicator function). Deep networks processing 3D data (3D CNNs, PointNet, NeRF) operate on discretizations of this integral. Understanding the continuous limit — the true volume integral — is essential for interpreting what these networks compute.

### 10.2 — Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF)

**NeRF** (Mildenhall et al., 2020) represents a 3D scene as a neural network that maps position $(x,y,z)$ and viewing direction to color and density. The rendered pixel color along a ray is:

$$C(\mathbf{r}) = \int_{t_n}^{t_f} T(t),\sigma(\mathbf{r}(t)),\mathbf{c}(\mathbf{r}(t), \mathbf{d}),dt$$

where $T(t) = \exp!\left(-\int_{t_n}^t \sigma(\mathbf{r}(s)),ds\right)$ is transmittance, and $\sigma$ is volume density. This is a **volume integral along a ray** — the continuous generalization of the cross-section formula. The density $\sigma$ plays the role of $A(x)$; integrating it along the ray gives the accumulated opacity.

**The discrete approximation** used in practice is:

$$C(\mathbf{r}) \approx \sum_{i=1}^N T_i(1 - e^{-\sigma_i \delta_i}),\mathbf{c}_i$$

This is a Riemann sum approximation of the volume integral — exactly the slab approximation from § 1.

```
NeRF volume rendering:

  camera → ray through scene → pixel color
  
  ╭──────────────────────────────────────╮
  │ 3D scene (density field σ(x,y,z))   │
  │                                      │
  │  •  •  ●  ●  ●  ●  •  •  •  •      │  ← sample points
  │                ↑ high density        │
  ╰──────────────────────────────────────╯
  
  C = ∫ T(t)·σ(t)·c(t) dt  ← volume integral
    ≈ Σ Tᵢ·(1−e^{−σᵢδᵢ})·cᵢ  ← Riemann sum
```

### 10.3 — Volumetric Loss Functions in 3D Generation

3D generative models (occupancy networks, signed distance functions, 3D diffusion models) define a loss over a volume:

$$\mathcal{L} = \int_\Omega |f_\theta(x) - f_*(x)|^2,dV$$

This is a continuous loss integral — the $L^2$ distance between two volumetric functions. In practice, it is approximated by Monte Carlo sampling (a Riemann sum), but the object being minimized is precisely the volume integral.

### 10.4 — Solid Angle and Attention

In graphics and physically-based rendering (which underlies diffusion models trained on rendered data), the **solid angle** subtended by a surface patch is:

$$\Omega = \iint_S \frac{\cos\theta}{r^2},dA$$

This is a surface integral (18.02), but its numerical approximation mirrors the cross-section method: sample directions, weight by the integrand, sum. Attention mechanisms in transformers compute a weighted sum over queries — a discrete analog of exactly this kind of integral over a "solid angle" of context.

### 10.5 — Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks and Univariate Integration

**KAN** (Liu et al., 2024) represents functions as compositions of learnable univariate functions placed on edges. Each edge function is implicitly a definite integral (or its inverse). The architecture is motivated by the Kolmogorov-Arnold representation theorem, which expresses multivariate functions in terms of univariate ones — a decomposition that, in the continuous limit, involves volume integrals factoring into products of one-dimensional integrals (when the function is separable). Understanding when $\iiint f(x)g(y)h(z),dx,dy,dz = (\int f)(\int g)(\int h)$ — which is the separability of volumes — directly informs when KAN representations are exact.

### 10.6 — Expectation as Volume Under a Surface

In probability, the expected value of $f(X, Y)$ under a joint distribution $p(x,y)$ is:

$$\mathbb{E}[f(X,Y)] = \iint f(x,y),p(x,y),dx,dy$$

This is the volume under the surface $z = f(x,y),p(x,y)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Every 2D expectation in machine learning — joint entropy, mutual information, 2D kernel density estimation, bivariate loss functions — is a volume computation. Monte Carlo integration approximates this volume with a Riemann sum over samples.

## § 11 — Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Squaring after subtracting instead of subtracting after squaring.** Washer: $\pi(R^2 - r^2) \neq \pi(R-r)^2$. Always square each radius separately, then subtract.

**Mistake 2: Using the wrong radius when rotating about a non-standard axis.** If rotating about $y = 2$ and the curve is $y = x^2$, the radius is $2 - x^2$ (not $x^2$). Always draw the axis and measure the perpendicular distance.

**Mistake 3: Forgetting limits when using the shell method with region crossing $x=0$.** The shell radius is $|x|$, so negative $x$ must be handled separately. When in doubt, switch to washers.

**Mistake 4: Applying the disk formula to a region that isn't touching the axis.** If the region between $y=1$ and $y=3$ is rotated about the $x$-axis, there is a hole — use the washer method, not the disk method.

**Mistake 5: Not identifying intersection points correctly.** The limits of integration must be the $x$-values where the bounding curves meet (or the stated boundary). Sketch the region and its rotation before writing any integral.

**Mistake 6: Confusing which method integrates in which variable.** Disk/Washer rotated about $x$-axis → integrate in $x$. Shell about $y$-axis with $y = f(x)$ → integrate in $x$. Getting this crossed produces an integral that is either wrong or much harder than necessary.
