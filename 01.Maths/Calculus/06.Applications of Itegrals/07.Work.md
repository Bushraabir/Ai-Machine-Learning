## 0. Motivation — Why Integration?

In elementary physics, work is defined as:

$$W = F \cdot d \quad \text{(constant force, straight-line displacement)}$$

But real problems almost never have constant force:

- A spring exerts more force the more you stretch it
- Gravity on a cable varies depending on how much cable remains to be lifted
- A pump must push water farther as the tank empties

When force varies with position, we need integration.

---

## 1. Definition — Work as an Integral

> **Definition.** Suppose an object moves along the $x$-axis from $x = a$ to $x = b$, subject to a force $F(x)$ acting in the direction of motion. The **work done** by the force is: $$W = \int_a^b F(x),dx$$

**Units.**

|System|Force|Distance|Work|
|---|---|---|---|
|SI|Newtons (N)|meters (m)|Joules (J = N·m)|
|Imperial|pounds (lb)|feet (ft)|foot-pounds (ft·lb)|

**Sign convention.** If $F(x) > 0$ and motion is in the positive $x$-direction, work is positive (force aids motion). If $F(x) < 0$, work is negative (force opposes motion).

---

## 2. Derivation from First Principles

Partition $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals of width $\Delta x_i$. On the $i$-th subinterval, the force is approximately constant at $F(x_i^_)$ for any sample point $x_i^_$.

Work done over the $i$-th piece: $$\Delta W_i \approx F(x_i^*),\Delta x_i$$

Total work: $$W \approx \sum_{i=1}^n F(x_i^*),\Delta x_i$$

As $|\Delta| \to 0$, this Riemann sum converges to: $$\boxed{W = \int_a^b F(x),dx}$$

**Validity.** $F$ must be continuous (or at least integrable) on $[a,b]$.

## 3. Hooke's Law — Springs

The canonical example. A spring exerts a **restoring force** proportional to its displacement from natural length:

$$F(x) = kx$$

where $x$ is the displacement from equilibrium and $k > 0$ is the **spring constant** (stiffness), measured in N/m.

```
Natural length:
  ├───────────────┤
  0              L₀

Stretched by x:
  ├───────────────────────┤
  0              L₀      L₀+x
                          ↑
                          F = kx  (restoring force, points left)
```

> **Theorem (Hooke's Law).** The work done in stretching (or compressing) a spring by a distance $d$ from its natural length is: $$W = \int_0^d kx,dx = \frac{1}{2}kd^2$$

**Proof.** $$W = \int_0^d kx,dx = k\cdot\frac{x^2}{2}\Bigg|_0^d = \frac{1}{2}kd^2 \qquad \square$$

---

### Example 1 — Spring: Finding $k$ and Then Work

**Problem.** A force of 40 N is required to hold a spring that has been stretched 10 cm beyond its natural length. How much work is done in stretching it from 10 cm to 35 cm beyond its natural length?

**Step 1: Find $k$.**

$$F(0.10) = k(0.10) = 40 \implies k = 400 \text{ N/m}$$

**Step 2: Compute work from $x = 0.10$ to $x = 0.35$ (in meters).**

$$W = \int_{0.10}^{0.35} 400x,dx = 400\cdot\frac{x^2}{2}\Bigg|_{0.10}^{0.35}$$

$$= 200\left[(0.35)^2 - (0.10)^2\right] = 200[0.1225 - 0.01] = 200 \times 0.1125 = 22.5 \text{ J}$$

## 4. Lifting Problems — Variable Weight

### 4.1 Lifting a Rope or Cable

A rope of length $L$, linear density $\rho$ (kg/m), hangs vertically. To lift it, you must raise each small segment — but the segments at the bottom travel farther.

```
        ┌── fixed point (top)
        │
        │  ← segment at height x from bottom
        │     must be lifted (L - x) meters
        │
        └── bottom of rope (x = 0)
```

Consider a small segment at height $x$ from the bottom, of length $dx$:

- Mass: $\rho,dx$
- Weight (force): $\rho g,dx$
- Distance it must be lifted: $L - x$
- Work on this segment: $\rho g(L - x),dx$

Total work: $$W = \int_0^L \rho g (L - x),dx = \rho g\left[Lx - \frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^L = \rho g \cdot \frac{L^2}{2}$$

> **Quick check.** The center of mass of the rope is at $L/2$ from the bottom. To lift the entire rope, you raise the center of mass by $L/2$. Total work $= (\rho L) g \cdot (L/2) = \rho g L^2/2$. ✓

---

### Example 2 — Cable with Object Attached

**Problem.** A 200 kg motor is lifted using a cable of mass 3 kg/m. If the motor starts 30 m below the target level, how much work is done?

**Work lifting the motor** (constant force, constant displacement): $$W_{\text{motor}} = (200)(9.8)(30) = 58{,}800 \text{ J}$$

**Work lifting the cable.** Segment at height $x$ from bottom travels $30 - x$: $$W_{\text{cable}} = \int_0^{30} (3)(9.8)(30 - x),dx = 29.4\left[30x - \frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^{30}$$ $$= 29.4\left[900 - 450\right] = 29.4 \times 450 = 13{,}230 \text{ J}$$

**Total:** $$W = 58{,}800 + 13{,}230 = 72{,}030 \text{ J}$$

## 5. Pumping Problems — Work Against Gravity

Pumping liquid out of a tank is perhaps the richest application because the **force** (weight of a water slice) and the **distance** it must travel both depend on position.

### 5.1 General Setup

```
        ┌─────────────────┐ ← discharge level (height H above some reference)
        │                 │
        │~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~~│ ← water surface
        │                 │
        │   thin slice    │ ← at height y, thickness dy
        │   width w(y)    │
        │                 │
        └─────────────────┘ ← bottom (y = 0)
```

For a slice of water at height $y$, thickness $dy$:

- Volume: $A(y),dy$ where $A(y)$ = cross-sectional area at height $y$
- Weight (force): $\rho_w \cdot g \cdot A(y),dy$ where $\rho_w \approx 1000$ kg/m³
- Distance to pump: $(H - y)$ where $H$ = height of discharge point
- Work on this slice: $\rho_w g A(y)(H - y),dy$

$$\boxed{W = \rho_w g \int_{y_1}^{y_2} A(y)(H - y),dy}$$

---

### Example 3 — Pumping from a Cylindrical Tank

**Problem.** A cylindrical tank of radius 3 m and height 5 m is full of water. Find the work required to pump all the water out over the top.

**Setup.** $A(y) = \pi(3)^2 = 9\pi$ m² (constant), $H = 5$ m (discharge at top), $y \in [0,5]$.

$$W = \rho_w g \int_0^5 9\pi(5 - y),dy = 9\pi \rho_w g \int_0^5 (5-y),dy$$

$$= 9\pi(1000)(9.8)\left[5y - \frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^5 = 9\pi(9800)\left[25 - \frac{25}{2}\right]$$

$$= 9\pi(9800)(12.5) = 1{,}102{,}500\pi \approx 3.46 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$$

---

### Example 4 — Pumping from a Conical Tank

**Problem.** A conical tank (vertex down) has height 10 m and top radius 4 m. It is filled to 8 m with water. Find the work to pump all water to a pipe 2 m above the top of the tank.

**Setup.** By similar triangles, radius at height $y$: $r(y) = \frac{4}{10}y = \frac{2y}{5}$.

Cross-sectional area: $A(y) = \pi\left(\frac{2y}{5}\right)^2 = \frac{4\pi y^2}{25}$

Discharge height: $H = 10 + 2 = 12$ m above vertex.

Water fills from $y = 0$ to $y = 8$.

$$W = \rho_w g \int_0^8 \frac{4\pi y^2}{25}(12 - y),dy$$

$$= \frac{4\pi \rho_w g}{25} \int_0^8 (12y^2 - y^3),dy$$

$$= \frac{4\pi(9800)}{25}\left[4y^3 - \frac{y^4}{4}\right]_0^8$$

$$= \frac{4\pi(9800)}{25}\left[4(512) - \frac{4096}{4}\right]$$

$$= \frac{4\pi(9800)}{25}\left[2048 - 1024\right] = \frac{4\pi(9800)(1024)}{25}$$

$$= \frac{40{,}140{,}800\pi}{25} \approx 5.048 \times 10^6 \text{ J}$$

## 6. Work Done by a Variable Force — General

In the most general 1D setting, the work-energy theorem connects work to kinetic energy:

> **Work-Energy Theorem.** If a net force $F(x)$ acts on an object of mass $m$ moving from $x = a$ to $x = b$, then: $$W_{\text{net}} = \Delta KE = \frac{1}{2}mv_b^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_a^2$$

**Proof.** By Newton's second law, $F = ma = m\frac{dv}{dt}$. Then:

$$W = \int_a^b F(x),dx = \int_a^b m\frac{dv}{dt},dx$$

Using the chain rule: $\frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{dv}{dx}\cdot\frac{dx}{dt} = v\frac{dv}{dx}$. So:

$$W = \int_a^b mv\frac{dv}{dx},dx = \int_{v_a}^{v_b} mv,dv = \frac{1}{2}mv_b^2 - \frac{1}{2}mv_a^2 \qquad \square$$

## 7. Escape Velocity — Gravitation

**Problem.** How much work is required to launch a rocket of mass $m$ from Earth's surface to infinity?

Newton's law of gravitation: force at distance $r$ from Earth's center:

$$F(r) = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$

where $G$ = gravitational constant, $M$ = Earth's mass, $R$ = Earth's radius.

$$W = \int_R^\infty \frac{GMm}{r^2},dr = GMm\left[-\frac{1}{r}\right]_R^\infty = \frac{GMm}{R}$$

Setting this equal to initial kinetic energy $\frac{1}{2}mv^2$:

$$\frac{1}{2}mv^2 = \frac{GMm}{R} \implies v_{\text{escape}} = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}} \approx 11.2 \text{ km/s}$$

> The integral converges because $1/r^2$ decays fast enough — gravity does finite total work even over infinite distance.

## 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 8.1 Gradient Descent — Work Along the Loss Landscape

In optimization, gradient descent moves parameters $\theta$ in the direction of steepest descent of the loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

The **work done by the gradient force** as parameters move from $\theta_0$ to $\theta^*$ along a path $\gamma$ is:

$$W = \int_\gamma \nabla \mathcal{L} \cdot d\theta = \mathcal{L}(\theta^*) - \mathcal{L}(\theta_0)$$

This is the **fundamental theorem of calculus for line integrals** (gradient theorem). The loss decrease IS the work done by the gradient field. Every step of gradient descent is computing an infinitesimal version of $\Delta W = F \cdot \Delta x$.

### 8.2 Energy-Based Models

**Energy-based models (EBMs)** define a probability distribution via an energy function $E(x)$:

$$p(x) = \frac{e^{-E(x)}}{Z}, \quad Z = \int e^{-E(x)},dx$$

The partition function $Z$ is exactly the analogue of total work: it is the integral of a force-like quantity ($e^{-E}$) over the configuration space. Training an EBM requires estimating $Z$ — which is generally intractable and approximated by MCMC, just as a complex work integral might require numerical integration.

### 8.3 Contrastive Learning — Work Done Between Distributions

In contrastive learning (SimCLR, CLIP), the model learns by pushing positive pairs together and negative pairs apart in embedding space. The loss involves computing distances (works) between points:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\log \frac{e^{-d(z_i, z_j)/\tau}}{\sum_k e^{-d(z_i, z_k)/\tau}}$$

Moving embeddings in representation space against this loss gradient is literally computing work done by a repulsive/attractive force field in high-dimensional space.

### 8.4 Optimal Transport — The Wasserstein Distance

The **Wasserstein distance** (used in GANs, domain adaptation) between two distributions $\mu$ and $\nu$ is:

$$W_1(\mu, \nu) = \inf_{\gamma \in \Pi(\mu,\nu)} \int |x - y|,d\gamma(x,y)$$

This is literally the **minimum work** (in the physics sense) required to transport the mass distribution $\mu$ into the distribution $\nu$ — the "earth mover's distance." The Wasserstein GAN minimizes this quantity, and its computation is a direct generalization of $W = \int F,dx$ to the space of probability distributions.

### 8.5 Hamiltonian Monte Carlo — Work and Energy Conservation

**Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC)** is a powerful MCMC method used in Bayesian deep learning (e.g., in probabilistic programming systems like PyMC, Stan). It introduces a fictional "momentum" $p$ and simulates Hamiltonian dynamics:

$$H(\theta, p) = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}(\theta)}_{\text{potential energy}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}p^T M^{-1} p}_{\text{kinetic energy}}$$

The system evolves so that $H$ is conserved (total energy = constant). The work done by the potential gradient as $\theta$ moves is exactly transferred to kinetic energy — the work-energy theorem of §6 in the loss landscape. HMC exploits this to make far more efficient proposals than random-walk Metropolis, and it is why Bayesian neural networks can be sampled efficiently.

### 8.6 Attention as a Soft Work Integral

In transformers, the output of attention can be written as:

$$\text{out}(q) = \int K(q, k) \cdot v(k),dk$$

(in the continuous limit over keys $k$). This is an **integral of a force-like weight $K(q,k)$ times a value $v(k)$** — structurally identical to $W = \int F(x),dx$. The softmax kernel $K$ determines how much "work" (influence) each key exerts on the query.

## 9. Numerical Methods for Work Integrals

When $F(x)$ is given as tabular data (common in ML applications with empirical force measurements):

**Trapezoidal Rule:** $$W \approx \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{F(x_{i-1}) + F(x_i)}{2},\Delta x_i$$

**Simpson's Rule** (requires even $n$, more accurate): $$W \approx \frac{\Delta x}{3}\left[F(x_0) + 4F(x_1) + 2F(x_2) + 4F(x_3) + \cdots + F(x_n)\right]$$

In deep learning, **numerical integration of gradients** appears in:

- Computing the path integral of the gradient (integrated gradients, a feature attribution method)
- Computing the KL divergence between distributions
- Normalizing flows (change of variables requires integrating the log-Jacobian)

## 10. Summary

|Problem Type|Force $F(x)$|Work Formula|
|---|---|---|
|Spring (Hooke's Law)|$kx$|$\frac{1}{2}kd^2$|
|Lifting rope (density $\rho$)|$\rho g(L-x)$ per element|$\int_0^L \rho g(L-x),dx$|
|Pumping liquid|$\rho_w g A(y)(H-y)$|$\int \rho_w g A(y)(H-y),dy$|
|Gravitation|$GMm/r^2$|$\int_R^\infty GMm/r^2,dr$|
|General variable force|$F(x)$ continuous|$\int_a^b F(x),dx$|

## 11. Setup Checklist

- [ ] Identify the object being moved and the direction of motion
- [ ] Write $F$ as a function of position — what changes as the object moves?
- [ ] For pumping: identify a thin slice, find its weight ($\rho g A(y),dy$) and distance to travel ($H - y$)
- [ ] For ropes/cables: identify a segment, find its weight and how far it must be lifted
- [ ] Set limits of integration
- [ ] Integrate; check units (must be Joules or ft·lb)
- [ ] Sanity check: could you estimate the answer using average force × distance?
