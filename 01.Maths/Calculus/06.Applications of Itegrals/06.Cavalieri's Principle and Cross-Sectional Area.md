## 0. Historical Note

**Bonaventura Cavalieri** (1598–1647), a student of Galileo, developed his _Method of Indivisibles_ around 1635 — a precursor to integral calculus. His core insight: you can compare volumes (or areas) without knowing their exact shapes, provided you know their cross-sections agree everywhere. Riemann would later make this rigorous, but the geometric intuition is entirely Cavalieri's.

---

## 1. The Principle — Two Versions

### 1.1 The 2D Version (Areas)

> **Cavalieri's Principle (2D).** Suppose two plane regions lie between the same two parallel lines $y = c$ and $y = d$. If every horizontal line $y = k$ (for $c \leq k \leq d$) intersects both regions in segments of equal length, then the two regions have equal area.

**ASCII illustration:**

```
y = d ─────────────────────────────────────
        │←── L(k) ──→│    │←── L(k) ──→│
y = k  ─┼────────────┼────┼────────────┼──
        │            │    │            │
y = c ─────────────────────────────────────

        Region 1           Region 2
        (different shape, same cross-section lengths)
        ⟹  Area(Region 1) = Area(Region 2)
```

### 1.2 The 3D Version (Volumes)

> **Cavalieri's Principle (3D).** Suppose two solids lie between the same two parallel planes. If every plane parallel to these two planes intersects both solids in cross-sections of equal area, then the two solids have equal volume.

This is the version we care about most in integral calculus.

---

## 2. The Rigorous Statement and Proof

> **Theorem.** Let $S_1$ and $S_2$ be two solids contained between the planes $x = a$ and $x = b$. Let $A_1(x)$ and $A_2(x)$ denote the areas of cross-sections of $S_1$ and $S_2$ cut by the plane at position $x$, for each $x \in [a,b]$. If $A_1(x) = A_2(x)$ for all $x \in [a,b]$, then: $$V(S_1) = V(S_2)$$

**Proof.** By the cross-sectional area method (see §3), the volume of any solid with integrable cross-sectional area function is: $$V = \int_a^b A(x),dx$$

Since $A_1(x) = A_2(x)$ for all $x \in [a,b]$, we have: $$V(S_1) = \int_a^b A_1(x),dx = \int_a^b A_2(x),dx = V(S_2) \qquad \square$$

> **Remark.** The real content of this theorem is in the volume formula $V = \int_a^b A(x),dx$, which we establish independently in §3. Cavalieri's Principle is then an immediate corollary — and a powerful one.

## 3. Volumes by Cross-Sectional Area — The General Method

This is the foundational method from which both the disk/washer method and Cavalieri's Principle flow as special cases.

### 3.1 Setup

Let $S$ be a solid that lies between the planes $x = a$ and $x = b$. For each $x \in [a,b]$, let $A(x)$ be the area of the cross-section of $S$ in the plane perpendicular to the $x$-axis at position $x$.

```
                          A(x) = cross-sectional area
                          at position x
            
        a ─────────────────────────────── b
        |         |         |             |
        |  A(a)   |  A(x)   |    A(b)     |
        |  ╔═══╗  |  ╔══╗   |    ╔════╗   |
        |  ║   ║  |  ║  ║   |    ║    ║   |
        |  ╚═══╝  |  ╚══╝   |    ╚════╝   |
        |         |         |             |
        
        Slice the solid into thin slabs of thickness Δx.
        Each slab has volume ≈ A(x) · Δx.
```

### 3.2 Derivation

**Partition** $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals of width $\Delta x_i$. In the $i$-th slab, pick a sample point $x_i^*$.

Each slab is approximately a **cylinder** (in the generalized sense: a shape with uniform cross-section): $$\Delta V_i \approx A(x_i^*),\Delta x_i$$

Total volume: $$V \approx \sum_{i=1}^n A(x_i^*),\Delta x_i$$

This is a Riemann sum. Taking $|\Delta| \to 0$:

$$\boxed{V = \int_a^b A(x),dx}$$

**Validity condition:** $A(x)$ must be continuous (or at least integrable) on $[a,b]$.

---

## 4. The Disk and Washer Methods as Special Cases

The cross-section method is the _general_ framework. The disk and washer methods arise when the solid is a **solid of revolution**.

### 4.1 Disk Method

Rotate $y = f(x) \geq 0$ on $[a,b]$ about the $x$-axis. The cross-section at $x$ is a **disk** of radius $f(x)$:

$$A(x) = \pi [f(x)]^2$$

$$V = \int_a^b \pi [f(x)]^2,dx$$

### 4.2 Washer Method

Rotate the region between $y = f(x)$ (outer) and $y = g(x)$ (inner) about the $x$-axis. The cross-section at $x$ is a **washer** (annulus) with outer radius $f(x)$ and inner radius $g(x)$:

$$A(x) = \pi[f(x)]^2 - \pi[g(x)]^2$$

$$V = \int_a^b \pi\bigl([f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2\bigr),dx$$

### 4.3 Non-Circular Cross-Sections

The method handles any cross-sectional shape — this is where Cavalieri's Principle becomes powerful in practice.

## 5. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Pyramid with Square Base (Non-Circular)

**Problem.** Find the volume of a pyramid with height $h$ and square base of side $a$.

**Setup.** Place the apex at the origin, base at $x = h$. By similar triangles, the cross-section at position $x$ is a square with side $s(x) = \frac{a}{h} x$.

$$A(x) = \left(\frac{a}{h}x\right)^2 = \frac{a^2}{h^2}x^2$$

$$V = \int_0^h \frac{a^2}{h^2}x^2,dx = \frac{a^2}{h^2} \cdot \frac{h^3}{3} = \frac{1}{3}a^2 h$$

> This is the standard formula $V = \frac{1}{3} \cdot \text{base} \cdot \text{height}$, derived rigorously. Note: **no solid of revolution was needed**.

---

### Example 2 — Cavalieri Applied: Sphere = Stack of Disks

**Problem.** Find the volume of a sphere of radius $R$.

**Setup.** Center the sphere at the origin. The cross-section at position $x \in [-R, R]$ is a disk of radius $r(x) = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2}$:

$$A(x) = \pi(R^2 - x^2)$$

$$V = \int_{-R}^R \pi(R^2 - x^2),dx = \pi\left[R^2 x - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-R}^R$$

$$= \pi\left[\left(R^3 - \frac{R^3}{3}\right) - \left(-R^3 + \frac{R^3}{3}\right)\right] = \pi \cdot \frac{4R^3}{3} = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 \checkmark$$

---

### Example 3 — Cavalieri's Principle in Action: Oblique Cylinder

**Problem.** Show that an oblique cylinder has the same volume as a right cylinder with the same base area $A$ and height $h$.

```
Right Cylinder:          Oblique Cylinder:
  ┌─────┐                 ╱─────╱
  │     │                ╱     ╱
  │  A  │  height h     ╱  A  ╱  height h (perpendicular)
  │     │              ╱     ╱
  └─────┘             ╱─────╱
```

**Proof via Cavalieri.** Every horizontal plane at height $y$ intersects both solids in a cross-section congruent to the base (by the definition of a cylinder — the cross-sections are translates of the base). Hence $A_1(y) = A_2(y) = A$ for all $y \in [0,h]$.

By Cavalieri's Principle: $$V_{\text{oblique}} = V_{\text{right}} = \int_0^h A,dy = Ah \qquad \square$$

> _This is why "base times height" works for any cylinder — the obliqueness is irrelevant._

---

### Example 4 — Solid with Triangular Cross-Sections

**Problem.** The base of a solid is the region in the $xy$-plane bounded by $y = 1 - x^2$ and $y = 0$. Every cross-section perpendicular to the $x$-axis is an **equilateral triangle** with one side in the base. Find the volume.

**Setup.** The base extends over $x \in [-1, 1]$. At position $x$, the base of the equilateral triangle has length $\ell(x) = 1 - x^2$.

Area of equilateral triangle with side $\ell$: $$A = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\ell^2$$

So: $$A(x) = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(1-x^2)^2$$

$$V = \int_{-1}^1 \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}(1-x^2)^2,dx = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\int_{-1}^1 (1 - 2x^2 + x^4),dx$$

$$= \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}\left[x - \frac{2x^3}{3} + \frac{x^5}{5}\right]_{-1}^1 = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4} \cdot \frac{16}{15} = \frac{4\sqrt{3}}{15}$$

> **Key insight:** the solid is not a surface of revolution at all. Cross-sections handle it cleanly.

---

### Example 5 — Comparing Two Solids via Cavalieri

**Problem.** A sphere of radius $R$ and a cylinder of radius $R$ and height $2R$ with a double cone removed — show they have equal volume.

This is **Archimedes' Hat-Box Theorem** (his proudest result, reportedly requested for his tombstone).

**Solid A:** Sphere of radius $R$, centered at origin. Cross-section at height $y$: $$A_{\text{sphere}}(y) = \pi(R^2 - y^2)$$

**Solid B:** Cylinder of radius $R$, height $2R$, centered at origin, minus two cones (apex at origin, base at $y = \pm R$). At height $y$, the cone has radius $|y|$, so the cross-section is a washer: $$A_{\text{B}}(y) = \pi R^2 - \pi y^2 = \pi(R^2 - y^2)$$

Since $A_{\text{sphere}}(y) = A_{\text{B}}(y)$ for all $y \in [-R, R]$:

$$V_{\text{sphere}} = V_{\text{cylinder}} - V_{\text{double cone}} = \pi R^2 \cdot 2R - 2 \cdot \frac{1}{3}\pi R^2 \cdot R = 2\pi R^3 - \frac{2}{3}\pi R^3 = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 \checkmark$$

> Archimedes proved this entirely without calculus, using balance arguments. Cavalieri's Principle makes the proof a single paragraph.

## 6. The Formula Hierarchy

```
                    ┌─────────────────────────────────┐
                    │     V = ∫ A(x) dx               │
                    │  (General Cross-Section Method)  │
                    └──────────────┬──────────────────┘
                                   │
                    ┌──────────────┼──────────────┐
                    │              │              │
             ┌──────┴─────┐ ┌─────┴──────┐ ┌────┴──────────────┐
             │ Disk Method │ │   Washer   │ │  Non-Circular     │
             │ A = π[f(x)]²│ │   Method   │ │  (pyramid, cone,  │
             │             │ │A=π[f²-g²]  │ │   wedge, etc.)    │
             └─────────────┘ └────────────┘ └───────────────────┘
                    
                    ↑
                    └── Cavalieri's Principle: if A₁(x) = A₂(x) ∀x, then V₁ = V₂
```

## 7. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 7.1 Computing Expectations — The Probabilistic Cross-Section

In probability and ML, computing the expectation of a function $g(X, Y)$ under a joint distribution is:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)] = \int\int g(x,y),p(x,y),dx,dy$$

By **Fubini's theorem** (the rigorous generalization of Cavalieri), we can integrate one variable at a time:

$$= \int \left[\int g(x,y),p(y|x),dy\right] p(x),dx = \int h(x),p(x),dx$$

The inner bracket $h(x) = \mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)|X=x]$ is exactly a **cross-section** of the integrand at fixed $x$. We are summing cross-sections — Cavalieri's method, probabilistically.

### 7.2 Sufficient Statistics and Cross-Sections of the Likelihood

In statistics, a **sufficient statistic** $T(X)$ captures all the information in data $X$ about a parameter $\theta$. The Fisher–Neyman factorization theorem says:

$$p(x|\theta) = h(x) \cdot g(T(x), \theta)$$

The "cross-section" of the likelihood at fixed $T(x) = t$ has the same shape regardless of $x$. This is a Cavalieri-like invariance: the data can vary (different $x$) while the informational cross-section (likelihood shape) stays the same.

### 7.3 Integral Approximations — Monte Carlo as Riemann Sums

Monte Carlo integration estimates $\int_\Omega f(x),dx$ by sampling:

$$\hat{I} = \frac{|\Omega|}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f(x_i), \quad x_i \sim \text{Uniform}(\Omega)$$

The cross-section formula $V = \int A(x),dx$ is the theoretical ground truth that Monte Carlo approximates. In **deep learning**, computing the partition function $Z = \int e^{-E(x)},dx$ of an energy-based model is intractable — we approximate it via Monte Carlo (MCMC). Cavalieri's framework — slice, measure, integrate — is the conceptual scaffold.

### 7.4 Attention Mechanisms — Weighted Cross-Sections

The self-attention operation in transformers computes:

$$\text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

Each output token is a **weighted sum** of value vectors — a weighted integral over the sequence "dimension." If you think of the value matrix as a function over sequence positions, attention is computing a **weighted cross-section** (projection) of the information content at each query position. The softmax weights play the role of a probability measure over cross-sections.

### 7.5 Neural Tangent Kernel and Volume in Function Space

The **Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)** framework analyzes infinite-width neural networks through the kernel:

$$K(x, x') = \mathbb{E}_\theta\left[\frac{\partial f(x;\theta)}{\partial \theta} \cdot \frac{\partial f(x';\theta)}{\partial \theta}\right]$$

This kernel defines an inner product on function space. The "volume" of the region of functions a neural network can represent is analyzed via the eigenspectrum of this kernel — which is computed by integrating cross-sections of the kernel over the data distribution. Cavalieri's geometric decomposition underlies the functional analysis.

### 7.6 The Curse of Dimensionality — Cross-Sections of the Hypercube

In a $d$-dimensional unit hypercube $[0,1]^d$, consider cross-sections perpendicular to the main diagonal. By Cavalieri, the volume is:

$$V = \int_0^1 A(t),dt = 1$$

But the cross-sectional area $A(t)$ is a polynomial of degree $d-1$ whose shape concentrates near $t = 1/2$ as $d \to \infty$ (by the CLT — the cross-sections are projections of uniform distributions). This concentration means that all the "probability mass" in high-dimensional spaces is in a thin shell around the equator — the same geometric phenomenon seen in [[Volumes by Cylindrical Shells]] §7.3.

## 8. Rigorous Subtleties

### 8.1 When Does the Formula Fail?

The formula $V = \int_a^b A(x),dx$ requires $A(x)$ to be **Riemann integrable** on $[a,b]$. By a theorem of Riemann, a bounded function is integrable if and only if its set of discontinuities has **measure zero**.

For most geometric solids encountered in calculus, $A(x)$ is continuous — so no issue. But for a solid with a fractal cross-section that jumps discontinuously, one must appeal to the **Lebesgue integral** instead.

### 8.2 The Measure-Theoretic Version of Cavalieri

In measure theory, Cavalieri's Principle is a consequence of **Fubini's Theorem**:

> Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$ be a measurable set. Then: $$\text{Vol}(S) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \text{Area}(S \cap {x = t}),dt$$

This holds for any Lebesgue-measurable set $S$ and is the rigorous foundation of Cavalieri's geometric intuition. The condition is that $S$ be measurable — which all "reasonable" geometric solids are.

### 8.3 Cavalieri Does NOT Imply Congruence

Two solids can satisfy Cavalieri's conditions (equal cross-sections at every level) and yet be non-congruent. The sphere and the Archimedean solid of Example 5 have the same volume but are obviously not congruent. Volume equality is weaker than congruence.

## 10. Setup Checklist

When using cross-sections to find volume:

- [ ] Sketch the solid — identify its extent in $x$ (or $y$, or $z$)
- [ ] Determine the shape of the cross-section at a generic position $x$
- [ ] Write $A(x)$ as a function of $x$ using geometry of that shape
- [ ] Set up $V = \int_a^b A(x),dx$ with correct limits
- [ ] Integrate
- [ ] Sanity-check: does the answer have units of length$^3$? Does it scale correctly with the parameters?
