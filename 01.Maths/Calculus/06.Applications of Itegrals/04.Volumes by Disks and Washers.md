## 0. Conceptual Foundation ŌĆö Cavalieri's Principle

Before any formula, here is the geometric principle underlying everything.

> **Cavalieri's Principle.** If two solids have the same cross-sectional area $A(x)$ at every $x \in [a,b]$, then they have the same volume.

This means: **volume is completely determined by the cross-sectional area function**. If we can write down $A(x)$, we can compute the volume by integrating it.

$$\boxed{V = \int_a^b A(x), dx}$$

This is the master formula. The disk and washer methods are simply the special cases where the cross-sections happen to be **circles** or **annuli** (rings).

---

## 1. The Disk Method

### 1.1 Setup

Take a region under $y = f(x)$ on $[a,b]$ and revolve it about the $x$-axis.

**What does a cross-section look like?** Slice the solid with a plane perpendicular to the $x$-axis at position $x$. The cross-section is a **disk** (filled circle) with:

- Radius $R = f(x)$ (the $y$-value at that $x$)
- Area $A(x) = \pi [f(x)]^2$

### 1.2 The Formula

Applying Cavalieri's Principle with $A(x) = \pi [f(x)]^2$:

$$\boxed{V = \pi \int_a^b [f(x)]^2, dx}$$

**Rotation about the $y$-axis.** If instead we revolve $x = g(y)$ on $[c,d]$ about the $y$-axis:

$$V = \pi \int_c^d [g(y)]^2, dy$$

The logic is identical; only the orientation changes.

---

### 1.3 Worked Example ŌĆö The Cone

**Problem.** Prove that the volume of a cone of radius $r$ and height $h$ is $\frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h$.

**Setup.** Place the apex at the origin, axis along the $x$-axis. The slant edge is the line $y = \frac{r}{h} x$, for $x \in [0, h]$.

**Disk radius at position $x$:** $R(x) = \frac{r}{h}x$.

$$V = \pi \int_0^h \left(\frac{r}{h}x\right)^2 dx = \frac{\pi r^2}{h^2} \int_0^h x^2, dx = \frac{\pi r^2}{h^2} \cdot \frac{h^3}{3} = \frac{1}{3}\pi r^2 h \qquad \square$$

> _This is deeply satisfying: the factor of $\frac{1}{3}$ emerges from $\int x^2,dx = \frac{x^3}{3}$, not from some geometric trick._

---

### 1.4 Worked Example ŌĆö The Sphere

**Problem.** Prove that the volume of a sphere of radius $r$ is $\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$.

**Setup.** The sphere is the solid obtained by revolving the semicircle $y = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$ on $[-r, r]$ about the $x$-axis.

**Disk radius at position $x$:** $R(x) = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$.

$$V = \pi \int_{-r}^r (r^2 - x^2), dx = \pi \left[r^2 x - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_{-r}^r$$

$$= \pi \left[\left(r^3 - \frac{r^3}{3}\right) - \left(-r^3 + \frac{r^3}{3}\right)\right] = \pi \cdot \frac{4r^3}{3} = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3 \qquad \square$$

## 2. The Washer Method

### 2.1 Setup

Now suppose the region is **between two curves**: $y = f(x)$ on top and $y = g(x)$ on the bottom, with $f(x) \geq g(x) \geq 0$ on $[a,b]$.

When revolved about the $x$-axis, the cross-section at $x$ is no longer a full disk ŌĆö it is an **annulus** (washer): a disk with a hole punched in it.

```
        Outer radius: R = f(x)
        Inner radius: r = g(x)

                 ŌĢŁŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌĢ«
               ŌĢŁŌöĆŌĢ»  ŌĢŁŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌĢ«    ŌĢ░ŌöĆŌĢ«
              ŌöĆŌĢ»    Ōöé  hole Ōöé     ŌĢ░ŌöĆ
                    ŌĢ░ŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌĢ»
               ŌĢ░ŌöĆŌĢ«              ŌĢŁŌöĆŌĢ»
                 ŌĢ░ŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌöĆŌĢ»

        Area = ŽĆ R┬▓ - ŽĆ r┬▓ = ŽĆ(R┬▓ - r┬▓)
```

**Washer area:** $$A(x) = \pi [f(x)]^2 - \pi [g(x)]^2 = \pi\left([f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2\right)$$

### 2.2 The Formula

$$\boxed{V = \pi \int_a^b \left([f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2\right) dx}$$

**Common mistake.** $[f(x)]^2 - [g(x)]^2 \neq [f(x) - g(x)]^2$. The subtraction is _outside_ the squaring. You square each radius separately, then subtract.

**Rotation about the $y$-axis:** $$V = \pi \int_c^d \left([f_{\text{right}}(y)]^2 - [f_{\text{left}}(y)]^2\right) dy$$

where $f_{\text{right}}$ and $f_{\text{left}}$ are the right and left boundaries of the region as functions of $y$.

---

### 2.3 Worked Example ŌĆö Classic Washer

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid obtained by rotating the region bounded by $y = x^2$ and $y = x$ (for $x \in [0,1]$) about the $x$-axis.

**Find intersections:** $x^2 = x \Rightarrow x = 0$ or $x = 1$.

On $[0,1]$: $x \geq x^2$, so outer radius $R = x$, inner radius $r = x^2$.

$$V = \pi \int_0^1 \left(x^2 - x^4\right) dx = \pi \left[\frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^1 = \pi\left(\frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{5}\right) = \frac{2\pi}{15}$$

---

### 2.4 Worked Example ŌĆö Rotation about a Non-Standard Axis

**Problem.** Rotate the region bounded by $y = x^2$ and $y = 4$ about the line $y = 4$.

**Key shift.** The axis is $y = 4$, not $y = 0$. The radius of each disk is the _distance_ from the curve to the axis.

The region is bounded above by $y = 4$ and below by $y = x^2$, for $x \in [-2, 2]$.

Since we rotate about $y = 4$ (which is the _top_ boundary), and $y = x^2$ is _below_ it:

$$R(x) = 4 - x^2 \quad (\text{distance from } y = x^2 \text{ to } y = 4)$$

No hole (the top boundary is the axis itself), so this is a pure disk method:

$$V = \pi \int_{-2}^{2} (4 - x^2)^2, dx = \pi \int_{-2}^{2} (16 - 8x^2 + x^4), dx$$

By symmetry, $= 2\pi \int_0^2 (16 - 8x^2 + x^4), dx$:

$$= 2\pi \left[16x - \frac{8x^3}{3} + \frac{x^5}{5}\right]_0^2 = 2\pi \left(32 - \frac{64}{3} + \frac{32}{5}\right) = 2\pi \cdot \frac{256}{15} = \frac{512\pi}{15}$$

---

### 2.5 Worked Example ŌĆö Revolving About a Vertical Axis (Washers in $y$)

**Problem.** Rotate the region bounded by $x = y^2$ and $x = 4$ about the $y$-axis.

The curves meet at $y = \pm 2$.

At height $y$: the region runs from $x = y^2$ (left boundary) to $x = 4$ (right boundary).

Revolving about $y$-axis: the **outer** radius is $R = 4$, **inner** radius is $r = y^2$.

$$V = \pi \int_{-2}^{2} \left(16 - y^4\right) dy = 2\pi \int_0^2 (16 - y^4), dy = 2\pi \left[16y - \frac{y^5}{5}\right]_0^2$$

$$= 2\pi \left(32 - \frac{32}{5}\right) = 2\pi \cdot \frac{128}{5} = \frac{256\pi}{5}$$

## 3. General Framework ŌĆö Any Axis of Rotation

Let $d(x)$ denote the distance from a point at coordinate $x$ to the axis of rotation.

|Configuration|Outer Radius $R$|Inner Radius $r$|
|---|---|---|
|About $x$-axis, $f \geq g \geq 0$|$f(x)$|$g(x)$|
|About $y = k$ (above region), $f \leq k$|$k - g(x)$|$k - f(x)$|
|About $y = k$ (below region), $f \geq k$|$f(x) - k$|$g(x) - k$|
|About $y$-axis, $f_R \geq f_L \geq 0$ (in $y$)|$f_R(y)$|$f_L(y)$|
|About $x = k$ (right of region)|$k - f_L(y)$|$k - f_R(y)$|
|About $x = k$ (left of region)|$f_R(y) - k$|$f_L(y) - k$|

**The rule:** $R$ is always the radius to the _far_ boundary, $r$ to the _near_ boundary (or zero for the disk case).

## 5. Relation to Shell Method ŌĆö The Complementary Pair

The disk/washer method and the [[Volumes by Cylindrical Shells|shell method]] are **dual** decompositions of the same solid. Given the same solid:

||Disk/Washer|Shell|
|---|---|---|
|Slices|Perpendicular to axis|Parallel to axis|
|Element|Thin circular slab|Thin cylindrical shell|
|Best when|Function easy to express in variable along axis|Function hard to invert|
|Example: rotate $y=f(x)$ about $x$-axis|Natural (integrate in $x$)|Need $f$ invertible as $x = f^{-1}(y)$|
|Example: rotate $y=f(x)$ about $y$-axis|Need inverse $x = f^{-1}(y)$|Natural (integrate in $x$)|

> **Personal heuristic.** For rotation about the $x$-axis with $y = f(x)$ given: use **disks/washers** (integrate in $x$, everything is natural). For rotation about the $y$-axis with $y = f(x)$ given: strongly prefer **shells** (avoid inverting $f$).

## 6. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 6.1 Cavalieri's Principle ŌåÆ Lebesgue Integration

The master formula $V = \int A(x),dx$ is an instance of **Fubini's theorem**: computing a higher-dimensional integral by iterated 1D integrals over cross-sections. In ML, we constantly use this:

$$\mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim p}[f(x,y)] = \int \left(\int f(x,y), p(y|x), dy\right) p(x), dx$$

The inner integral is a "cross-section" (conditional expectation at fixed $x$), and the outer integral averages over all $x$. Fubini is the theorem that justifies swapping integration order ŌĆö essential for almost every derivation in probabilistic ML.

### 6.2 The Gaussian ŌĆö Disk Slices of the Bell Curve

The normalization of the multivariate Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \mathbf{I})$ in $\mathbb{R}^d$ requires:

$$\int_{\mathbb{R}^d} e^{-|\mathbf{x}|^2/2}, d\mathbf{x} = (2\pi)^{d/2}$$

The proof proceeds by **disk slicing**: level sets of $e^{-|x|^2/2}$ are spheres. At radius $r$, the level set has surface area $S_{d-1} r^{d-1}$ (a generalized "disk"), so:

$$\int_{\mathbb{R}^d} e^{-|\mathbf{x}|^2/2}, d\mathbf{x} = \int_0^\infty e^{-r^2/2} \cdot S_{d-1} r^{d-1}, dr$$

This is the **disk method in $d$ dimensions** ŌĆö integrating spherical cross-sections. The entire theory of multivariate Gaussians rests on this.

### 6.3 Fisher Information and the Shape of the Loss Landscape

In optimization and information geometry (used in natural gradient descent, second-order optimization for deep networks), the **Fisher information matrix** is:

$$\mathcal{F}_{ij}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \log p(x|\theta)}{\partial \theta_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \log p(x|\theta)}{\partial \theta_j}\right]$$

For a 1D exponential family, $\mathcal{F}(\theta)$ has a clean geometric interpretation as the **curvature of the cross-section** of the statistical manifold. The disk/washer decomposition provides the geometric intuition: the Fisher metric measures how the "cross-sectional shape" of the probability simplex changes as you move along $\theta$.

### 6.4 Activation Functions and "Solid" Coverage

In neural networks, the **effective volume of the activation region** of a neuron (the set of inputs that activate it) can be analyzed using disk/washer cross-sections when the decision boundaries are spherical or ellipsoidal (e.g., RBF networks, Gaussian kernel SVMs). The expressivity of such neurons is directly related to the volume of these regions:

$$V_{\text{active}} = \int_{\text{region}} A(x),dx$$

Understanding this volume is crucial for capacity analysis.

### 6.5 KL Divergence ŌĆö Integration Over Cross-Sections of Probability Space

The **Kullback-Leibler divergence**:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(p | q) = \int p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)}, dx$$

is itself an integral of cross-sections. In variational inference (the backbone of VAEs), we minimize:

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}[\log p(x|z)] - D_{\text{KL}}(q(z|x) | p(z))$$

The second term has an exact closed form for Gaussians ŌĆö derived by disk-slicing (the cross-sectional areas of Gaussian ellipsoids integrate cleanly). This is why VAEs are analytically tractable.

### 6.6 Volume of Confidence Ellipsoids

In Bayesian linear regression and Gaussian processes, the posterior uncertainty is captured by an **ellipsoidal confidence region**:

$$\mathcal{E} = {\theta : (\theta - \hat\theta)^\top \Sigma^{-1} (\theta - \hat\theta) \leq \chi^2_d(\alpha)}$$

The volume of this ellipsoid is computed by the washer/disk method after an orthogonal change of variables (diagonalizing $\Sigma$):

$$V(\mathcal{E}) = \frac{\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2+1)} \prod_{i=1}^d \sqrt{\lambda_i} \cdot [\chi^2_d(\alpha)]^{d/2}$$

where $\lambda_i$ are eigenvalues of $\Sigma$. The $\Gamma$ function in the denominator comes directly from integrating the disk cross-sections via the gamma integral $\int_0^\infty r^{d-1} e^{-r},dr = \Gamma(d)$.

## 7. Rigorous Conditions for Validity

The formula $V = \pi \int_a^b [f(x)]^2,dx$ requires:

1. **$f$ continuous on $[a,b]$** ŌĆö guarantees $[f(x)]^2$ is Riemann integrable.
2. **$f(x) \geq 0$ on $[a,b]$** ŌĆö the radius must be non-negative. If $f$ takes negative values, the radius is $|f(x)|$ (the disk is the same regardless of sign), so replace $f(x)$ with $|f(x)|$.
3. **For washers: $f(x) \geq g(x) \geq 0$ on $[a,b]$** ŌĆö outer radius is genuinely larger, and both radii are non-negative. If $g < 0$, the formula breaks down; rewrite as distance to the axis.

**What if $f$ changes sign?** Split the integral at zeros and use $|f(x)|$ throughout. The disk's radius is always a positive length ŌĆö the sign of $f$ is geometrically irrelevant.

## 8. Setup Checklist

- [ ] Sketch the region carefully ŌĆö label curves, identify top/bottom or left/right
- [ ] Identify the axis of rotation
- [ ] Determine integration variable ($x$ or $y$) based on which way you slice
- [ ] Identify cross-section type: disk (one boundary) or washer (two boundaries)
- [ ] Write $R(x)$ and $r(x)$ as distances to the axis (not just function values!)
- [ ] Set limits from the intersection points or given bounds
- [ ] Write $V = \pi \int [R^2 - r^2],dx$ (or $dy$) and integrate
- [ ] Sanity check: units ($\text{length}^3$), sign (must be positive), rough magnitude

## 9. Quick Reference

$$\text{Disk: } V = \pi \int_a^b [R(x)]^2,dx \qquad \text{Washer: } V = \pi \int_a^b \left([R(x)]^2 - [r(x)]^2\right)dx$$

$$\text{In } y\text{: } V = \pi \int_c^d \left([R(y)]^2 - [r(y)]^2\right)dy$$

**Memory hook:** "Area of a washer = $\pi R^2 - \pi r^2$. Volume = integrate the washer area."
