

> _"The integral does not care whether you are weighing a lamina, locating a centroid, or training a neural network. It is always the same operation — accumulate, then normalize."_

## § 0 — The Unifying Idea

Every application in this note follows the same template:

$$\text{Global quantity} = \iint_D (\text{local density of that quantity}), dA$$

The "local density" changes — mass density, charge density, probability density, squared-distance density — but the machinery is identical. Master the pattern once; apply it everywhere.

---

## § 1 — Mass and Density

### Setup

A **lamina** is a thin flat plate occupying region $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$. Its **surface mass density** $\rho(x,y)$ (units: kg/m²) describes how mass is distributed: a small patch $dA$ near $(x,y)$ has mass $\approx \rho(x,y), dA$.

### Definition

$$M = \iint_D \rho(x,y), dA$$

If $\rho$ is constant (uniform lamina), then $M = \rho \cdot A(D)$ — consistent with the elementary formula.

### Example 1 — Variable density lamina

A triangular lamina occupies $D = {(x,y) : 0 \le x \le 1,, 0 \le y \le x}$ with density $\rho(x,y) = x + y$.

**Sketch of $D$:** the region below the line $y = x$ in the unit square; a right triangle with legs along the axes.

**Mass:**

$$M = \int_0^1 \int_0^x (x+y), dy, dx$$

Inner integral:

$$\int_0^x (x+y), dy = \left[xy + \frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^x = x^2 + \frac{x^2}{2} = \frac{3x^2}{2}$$

Outer integral:

$$M = \int_0^1 \frac{3x^2}{2}, dx = \frac{3}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{2}$$

## § 2 — Moments and Center of Mass

### Motivation

The **center of mass** $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ is the point at which the lamina would balance on a pin. It is the density-weighted average position.

### Definitions

The **first moments** about the coordinate axes:

$$M_y = \iint_D x, \rho(x,y), dA \qquad \text{(moment about the } y\text{-axis)}$$

$$M_x = \iint_D y, \rho(x,y), dA \qquad \text{(moment about the } x\text{-axis)}$$

The **center of mass**:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{M_y}{M} = \frac{\iint_D x, \rho, dA}{\iint_D \rho, dA}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{M_x}{M} = \frac{\iint_D y, \rho, dA}{\iint_D \rho, dA}$$

> **Why $M_y$ uses $x$?** The moment about the $y$-axis measures tendency to rotate _around_ the $y$-axis — driven by perpendicular distance, which is the $x$-coordinate.

### Special case: uniform density

When $\rho = $ const, it cancels:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{\iint_D x, dA}{A(D)}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{\iint_D y, dA}{A(D)}$$

This is the **centroid** — purely geometric, depending only on the shape of $D$.

### Example 2 — Center of mass of Example 1's lamina

Continuing with $D = {0 \le x \le 1, 0 \le y \le x}$, $\rho = x+y$, $M = 1/2$.

**Compute $M_y$:**

$$M_y = \int_0^1 \int_0^x x(x+y), dy, dx$$

Inner: $\int_0^x (x^2 + xy), dy = x^2 y + \frac{xy^2}{2}\Big|_0^x = x^3 + \frac{x^3}{2} = \frac{3x^3}{2}$

Outer: $\int_0^1 \frac{3x^3}{2}, dx = \frac{3}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{3}{8}$

**Compute $M_x$:**

$$M_x = \int_0^1 \int_0^x y(x+y), dy, dx$$

Inner: $\int_0^x (xy + y^2), dy = \frac{xy^2}{2} + \frac{y^3}{3}\Big|_0^x = \frac{x^3}{2} + \frac{x^3}{3} = \frac{5x^3}{6}$

Outer: $\int_0^1 \frac{5x^3}{6}, dx = \frac{5}{6} \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{5}{24}$

**Center of mass:**

$$\bar{x} = \frac{M_y}{M} = \frac{3/8}{1/2} = \frac{3}{4}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{M_x}{M} = \frac{5/24}{1/2} = \frac{5}{12}$$

> **Sanity check:** The lamina lives in $0 \le y \le x \le 1$. The density $\rho = x+y$ is heavier toward the corner $(1,1)$. So $\bar{x}$ should be above $1/2$, and $\bar{y}$ should be above the geometric centroid $y = 1/3$ of the region. Both are: $\bar{x} = 3/4$ ✓, $\bar{y} = 5/12 > 1/3$ ✓.

## § 3 — Moments of Inertia

### Motivation

While the center of mass governs translational balance, the **moment of inertia** governs **rotational inertia** — resistance to angular acceleration. It is the rotational analogue of mass.

For a point mass $m$ at distance $r$ from the axis: $I = mr^2$. For a distributed mass, integrate.

### Definitions

**Second moments (moments of inertia):**

$$I_x = \iint_D y^2, \rho(x,y), dA \qquad \text{(about the }x\text{-axis, distance = }|y|\text{)}$$

$$I_y = \iint_D x^2, \rho(x,y), dA \qquad \text{(about the }y\text{-axis, distance = }|x|\text{)}$$

$$I_0 = \iint_D (x^2 + y^2), \rho(x,y), dA \qquad \text{(about the origin/}z\text{-axis)}$$

Note: $I_0 = I_x + I_y$ (the **perpendicular axis theorem**).

**Radius of gyration** $\tilde{x}$ about the $y$-axis:

$$\tilde{x} = \sqrt{\frac{I_y}{M}}$$

$\tilde{x}$ is the distance from the axis at which all the mass, if concentrated, would give the same $I_y$.

### Example 3 — Disk with uniform density

Disk $D: x^2 + y^2 \le R^2$, uniform density $\rho$.

By symmetry $I_x = I_y$, so compute $I_0 = \iint_D r^2 \rho, dA$ in polar:

$$I_0 = \rho \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^R r^2 \cdot r, dr, d\theta = \rho \cdot 2\pi \cdot \frac{R^4}{4} = \frac{\pi \rho R^4}{2}$$

Since $M = \rho \pi R^2$:

$$I_0 = \frac{MR^2}{2}, \qquad I_x = I_y = \frac{MR^2}{4}$$

These are the standard formulas used throughout engineering mechanics.

### Example 4 — Rectangle, uniform density

Rectangle $[0,a] \times [0,b]$, uniform $\rho$. Mass $M = \rho ab$.

$$I_x = \rho \int_0^a \int_0^b y^2, dy, dx = \rho \cdot a \cdot \frac{b^3}{3} = \frac{\rho a b^3}{3} = \frac{M b^2}{3}$$

$$I_y = \frac{M a^2}{3}$$

> **Why engineers care:** $I$ appears in $\tau = I\alpha$ (torque = moment of inertia × angular acceleration) and in the deflection of beams under load. A wide, flat beam resists bending better — that is $I_x \propto b^3$: doubling height multiplies bending resistance by 8.

## § 4 — Probability

### Setup

A function $f(x,y) \ge 0$ is a **joint probability density function (PDF)** for continuous random variables $X, Y$ if:

$$\iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} f(x,y), dA = 1$$

The probability that $(X,Y)$ falls in a region $D$:

$$P\big((X,Y) \in D\big) = \iint_D f(x,y), dA$$

### Marginal Densities

Integrate out one variable to get the marginal:

$$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x,y), dy, \qquad f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x,y), dx$$

### Independence

$X$ and $Y$ are **independent** if and only if $f(x,y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$ — the joint PDF factors.

### Example 5 — Verifying a PDF and computing probability

Let $f(x,y) = 6xy^2$ on $D = {0 \le x \le 1,, 0 \le y \le 1}$, zero elsewhere.

**Verify:** $\int_0^1 \int_0^1 6xy^2, dy, dx = 6 \int_0^1 x, dx \int_0^1 y^2, dy = 6 \cdot \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} = 1$ ✓

**Compute** $P(X > Y)$: the region is ${0 \le y \le x \le 1}$.

$$P(X > Y) = \int_0^1 \int_0^x 6xy^2, dy, dx = \int_0^1 6x \cdot \frac{x^3}{3}, dx = \int_0^1 2x^4, dx = \frac{2}{5}$$

### Example 6 — Bivariate normal (the fundamental distribution in ML)

The bivariate normal with independent components $\sigma_X = \sigma_Y = \sigma$, means $\mu_X = \mu_Y = 0$:

$$f(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2} \exp!\left(-\frac{x^2+y^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

This factors as $f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$ — confirming independence. To verify $\iint f = 1$, switch to polar:

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2} e^{-r^2/2\sigma^2} r, dr, d\theta = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\int_0^\infty r, e^{-r^2/2\sigma^2}, dr$$

Substitute $u = r^2/2\sigma^2$: integral $= \sigma^2 \int_0^\infty e^{-u}, du = \sigma^2$. So $\frac{1}{\sigma^2} \cdot \sigma^2 = 1$ ✓

## § 5 — Expected Values

### Definitions

For random variables $(X,Y)$ with joint PDF $f$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} x, f(x,y), dA = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x, f_X(x), dx$$

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} y, f(x,y), dA$$

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)] = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} g(x,y), f(x,y), dA$$

**Variance:**

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])^2] = \iint (x - \mu_X)^2 f(x,y), dA$$

**Covariance:**

$$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)] = \iint (x-\mu_X)(y-\mu_Y), f(x,y), dA$$

> **Structural observation:** Compare with § 2.
> 
> |Physics|Probability|
> |---|---|
> |Mass density $\rho(x,y)$|Probability density $f(x,y)$|
> |Total mass $M = \iint \rho, dA$|Total probability $= 1$|
> |First moment $M_y = \iint x\rho, dA$|$\mathbb{E}[X] = \iint xf, dA$|
> |Moment of inertia $I_y = \iint x^2\rho, dA$|$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \iint x^2 f, dA$|
> |$I_y/M = \tilde{x}^2$|$\mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mu_X^2 = \text{Var}(X)$|
> 
> The physics and probability are the **same mathematical structure** with different interpretations of the weight function.

### Example 7 — Expected value and variance

Using $f(x,y) = 6xy^2$ from Example 5:

**$\mathbb{E}[X]$:** (use marginal $f_X(x) = \int_0^1 6xy^2, dy = 2x$)

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^1 x \cdot 2x, dx = 2\int_0^1 x^2, dx = \frac{2}{3}$$

**$\mathbb{E}[Y]$:** (marginal $f_Y(y) = \int_0^1 6xy^2, dx = 3y^2$)

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \int_0^1 y \cdot 3y^2, dy = 3\int_0^1 y^3, dy = \frac{3}{4}$$

**$\text{Var}(X)$:** $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \int_0^1 x^2 \cdot 2x, dx = \frac{1}{2}$, so $\text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{2} - \left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{4}{9} = \frac{1}{18}$

## § 7 — Applications in AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 7.1 — The Loss Function is a Moment Integral

The population risk (true loss) is:

$$\mathcal{R}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim p}[\ell(f_\theta(x), y)] = \iint \ell(f_\theta(x), y), p(x,y), dx, dy$$

This is the **first moment** of the loss function under the data distribution. Minimizing $\mathcal{R}(\theta)$ is the theoretical objective; empirical risk minimization (ERM) approximates this integral by a sample mean — a Monte Carlo estimate of the double integral.

### 7.2 — Variance and Bias-Variance Tradeoff

The bias-variance decomposition involves:

$$\mathbb{E}[(f_\theta(x) - y)^2] = \text{Bias}^2 + \text{Variance} + \sigma^2_{\text{noise}}$$

Each term is an integral of the form $\iint (\cdot)^2, p(x,y), dx, dy$ — a **second moment**, exactly as $I_y = \iint x^2 \rho, dA$. The parallel is not a metaphor; the mathematics is identical.

### 7.3 — Covariance and Feature Correlation

The **covariance matrix** $\Sigma$ of a feature vector $(X_1, \ldots, X_d)$ has entries:

$$\Sigma_{ij} = \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = \iint (x_i - \mu_i)(x_j - \mu_j), p(\mathbf{x}), d\mathbf{x}$$

In 2D: $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \iint (x-\mu_X)(y-\mu_Y), f(x,y), dA$.

PCA finds the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$, which requires computing these double (or higher-dimensional) integrals from data. The empirical covariance matrix $\hat{\Sigma} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_i (x_i - \bar{x})(x_i - \bar{x})^T$ is the Monte Carlo estimate of this integral.

### 7.4 — Gaussian Distributions are Everywhere

The multivariate Gaussian $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$ with density:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\mu)\right)$$

is defined by the condition $\iint \cdots \int f, d\mathbf{x} = 1$. Verifying this uses the polar-coordinates trick from Example 6, generalized to $d$ dimensions. Every forward pass through a Bayesian neural network, every Gaussian Process prediction, and every VAE latent space manipulation involves this integral implicitly.

### 7.5 — Center of Mass in Attention Mechanisms

The **attention mechanism** in Transformers computes:

$$\text{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$

The softmax produces a probability distribution (the weights), and the output is their weighted average of the values $V$. In the continuous limit (over a sequence modeled as a density), this is:

$$\text{output}(q) = \int \alpha(q, k), v(k), dk, \qquad \int \alpha(q,k), dk = 1$$

This is precisely a **center of mass computation**: the output is the $\alpha$-weighted average of the values $v$ — the continuous analogue of $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{M}\iint x\rho, dA$.

### 7.6 — Moment of Inertia and Regularization

**Weight regularization** (L2/ridge) adds $\lambda |\theta|^2$ to the loss:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) + \lambda \sum_i \theta_i^2$$

The sum $\sum \theta_i^2$ is a discrete second moment — the analogue of moment of inertia $I = \sum m_i r_i^2$. Regularization is the assertion: keep the parameter "mass" close to the origin. The continuous limit is $\lambda \int \theta^2, p(\theta), d\theta$ — a 1D (or higher) moment of inertia.

### 7.7 — Probability Density Estimation

**Kernel Density Estimation (KDE):**

$$\hat{f}(x,y) = \frac{1}{Nh^2} \sum_{i=1}^N K!\left(\frac{x-x_i}{h}, \frac{y-y_i}{h}\right)$$

estimates $f(x,y)$ from data. The kernel $K$ must satisfy $\iint K = 1$ — a double integral normalization condition. Evaluating probabilities under $\hat{f}$ over regions requires $\iint_D \hat{f}, dA$, switching the double integral and the sum via Fubini.

### 7.8 — Score-Based Diffusion Models

Modern diffusion models (DDPM, score matching) learn $\nabla_x \log p(x)$, the **score function** of the data density. The training objective involves:

$$\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p, \epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}}\left[|s_\theta(x + \sigma\epsilon) - \nabla_x \log p_\sigma(x)|^2\right]$$

Every expectation here is a double (or higher-dimensional) integral. The entire theoretical foundation — existence of scores, denoising score matching equivalence, probability flow ODEs — rests on properties of these integrals: their existence, Fubini-type interchanges, and moments of the Gaussian kernel.
