## § 0 — Why This Theorem Exists

Before we state anything, let us ask: **what problem does Fubini solve?**

Suppose you want to compute

$$\iint_R f(x,y), dA$$

over some rectangle $R = [a,b] \times [c,d]$. The double integral is defined as a limit of Riemann sums over 2D partitions — conceptually clean, computationally brutal. What we _want_ to do is reduce this to two ordinary 1D integrals, nested inside each other:

$$\int_a^b \left( \int_c^d f(x,y), dy \right) dx \quad \text{or} \quad \int_c^d \left( \int_a^b f(x,y), dx \right) dy$$

These are called **iterated integrals**. But why should they equal the double integral? And why should the two orderings give the same answer?

That is precisely what Fubini's Theorem guarantees — under the right conditions.

---

## § 1 — Setup and Notation

**Definition (Double Integral over a Rectangle).** Let $R = [a,b] \times [c,d] \subset \mathbb{R}^2$. Partition $[a,b]$ into $m$ subintervals and $[c,d]$ into $n$ subintervals, forming $mn$ subrectangles $R_{ij}$ each of area $\Delta A_{ij} = \Delta x_i \cdot \Delta y_j$. Pick sample points $(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_)$ in each. The double integral is:

$$\iint_R f(x,y), dA = \lim_{|P| \to 0} \sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{j=1}^{n} f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_), \Delta A_{ij}$$

where $|P|$ is the norm of the partition (diameter of the largest subrectangle).

**Definition (Iterated Integral).** For fixed $x$, define $g(x) = \int_c^d f(x,y), dy$ (integrate in $y$ first). The iterated integral integrating $y$ first is:

$$\int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y), dy, dx = \int_a^b g(x), dx$$

Similarly, integrating $x$ first:

$$\int_c^d \int_a^b f(x,y), dx, dy$$

---

## § 2 — The Theorem

### Theorem (Fubini, rectangle version)

> Let $f$ be **continuous** on the rectangle $R = [a,b] \times [c,d]$. Then:
> 
> $$\iint_R f(x,y), dA = \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y), dy, dx = \int_c^d \int_a^b f(x,y), dx, dy$$

Two things are being asserted simultaneously:

1. **The double integral equals each iterated integral** (reducing 2D to nested 1D)
2. **The two orderings are equal** (order of integration can be swapped freely)

> **Remark (continuity is sufficient, not necessary).** Continuity is the condition Stewart emphasizes, and it is the right condition for a first encounter. The theorem holds under weaker hypotheses (Fubini's full theorem in measure theory requires only that $f$ be integrable in the Lebesgue sense — see § 6 for the general statement). Continuity on a closed rectangle is more than enough.

---

## § 3 — Proof Sketch (the honest version)

A full measure-theoretic proof belongs to a real analysis course. But let us trace the argument to see _why_ it is true.

**Step 1: Continuity implies uniform continuity.** Since $f$ is continuous on the compact set $R$, it is uniformly continuous. This ensures that for any $\varepsilon > 0$, the iterated integrals are well-approximated by Riemann sums simultaneously in both variables.

**Step 2: The inner integral defines a continuous function.** Define

$$A(x) = \int_c^d f(x,y), dy$$

Since $f$ is continuous jointly in $(x,y)$, for fixed $x$ the function $y \mapsto f(x,y)$ is continuous (hence integrable), and $A(x)$ is well-defined. Moreover, uniform continuity of $f$ implies $A(x)$ is continuous in $x$.

**Step 3: Both equal the double integral.** We use the uniform continuity to show that the Riemann sum approximations for $\int_a^b A(x), dx$ and for $\iint_R f, dA$ can be made to agree within $\varepsilon$. The key identity is:

$$\sum_i \sum_j f(x_i^_, y_j^_), \Delta x_i, \Delta y_j \approx \sum_i \left( \int_c^d f(x_i^*, y), dy \right) \Delta x_i \approx \int_a^b A(x), dx$$

Both approximation steps use uniform continuity. Taking $|P| \to 0$ gives the result. $\blacksquare$

## § 4 — Examples

### Example 1 — Polynomial integrand (warm-up)

$$\iint_R (x^2 + 2xy), dA, \quad R = [0,1] \times [1,2]$$

**Integrating $y$ first:**

$$\int_0^1 \int_1^2 (x^2 + 2xy), dy, dx$$

Inner integral (treat $x$ as constant):

$$\int_1^2 (x^2 + 2xy), dy = \left[ x^2 y + xy^2 \right]_1^2 = (2x^2 + 4x) - (x^2 + x) = x^2 + 3x$$

Outer integral:

$$\int_0^1 (x^2 + 3x), dx = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{3x^2}{2} \right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{3}{2} = \frac{11}{6}$$

**Verify by switching order:**

$$\int_1^2 \int_0^1 (x^2 + 2xy), dx, dy$$

Inner integral:

$$\int_0^1 (x^2 + 2xy), dx = \left[ \frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 y \right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{3} + y$$

Outer integral:

$$\int_1^2 \left( \frac{1}{3} + y \right) dy = \left[ \frac{y}{3} + \frac{y^2}{2} \right]_1^2 = \left(\frac{2}{3} + 2\right) - \left(\frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{3}{2} = \frac{11}{6} \checkmark$$

---

### Example 2 — Separable functions (the cleanest case)

Suppose $f(x,y) = g(x)\cdot h(y)$ on $R = [a,b]\times[c,d]$. Then:

$$\iint_R g(x)h(y), dA = \left(\int_a^b g(x), dx\right)\left(\int_c^d h(y), dy\right)$$

_Proof:_

$$\int_a^b \int_c^d g(x)h(y), dy, dx = \int_a^b g(x) \left(\int_c^d h(y), dy\right) dx = \left(\int_c^d h(y), dy\right)\int_a^b g(x), dx$$

The inner integral is a constant with respect to $x$, so it factors out. This is an extremely useful pattern — memorize it.

**Concrete instance:** $\iint_{[0,\pi]\times[0,1]} \sin(x) \cdot e^y, dA = \int_0^\pi \sin x, dx \cdot \int_0^1 e^y, dy = 2 \cdot (e-1)$.

---

### Example 3 — Switching order rescues a computation

Sometimes one iterated order is tractable and the other is not.

$$\int_0^1 \int_x^1 e^{y^2}, dy, dx$$

Integrating $y$ first (as written) requires evaluating $\int e^{y^2}, dy$ — a non-elementary integral. Stuck.

**Switch the order.** The region of integration is ${(x,y) : 0 \le x \le 1,, x \le y \le 1}$, i.e., the triangle below the diagonal $y = x$ in $[0,1]^2$.

Describing this region with $y$ as the outer variable: $0 \le y \le 1$, $0 \le x \le y$.

$$\int_0^1 \int_0^y e^{y^2}, dx, dy = \int_0^1 e^{y^2} \cdot y, dy$$

Now substitute $u = y^2$, $du = 2y, dy$:

$$= \frac{1}{2}\int_0^1 e^u, du = \frac{1}{2}(e - 1)$$

> **Lesson:** Fubini does not just tell you that you _can_ switch order — it gives you the freedom to switch when one order makes the problem tractable.

## § 5 — Extension to General Regions

The theorem extends beyond rectangles. For a **Type I region** $D = {(x,y) : a \le x \le b,, g_1(x) \le y \le g_2(x)}$:

$$\iint_D f(x,y), dA = \int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y), dy, dx$$

For a **Type II region** $D = {(x,y) : c \le y \le d,, h_1(y) \le x \le h_2(y)}$:

$$\iint_D f(x,y), dA = \int_c^d \int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y), dx, dy$$

The strategy is always: describe the region, identify which variable has fixed limits (the outer integral), and determine how the other variable's bounds depend on the outer.

## § 6 — The Full Theorem (Measure-Theoretic Statement)

> **Fubini's Theorem (general).** Let $(X, \mathcal{M}, \mu)$ and $(Y, \mathcal{N}, \nu)$ be $\sigma$-finite measure spaces, and let $f : X \times Y \to \mathbb{R}$ be measurable. If
> 
> $$\int_{X \times Y} |f|, d(\mu \times \nu) < \infty$$
> 
> (i.e., $f \in L^1(\mu \times \nu)$), then:
> 
> $$\int_{X \times Y} f, d(\mu \times \nu) = \int_X \left(\int_Y f(x,y), d\nu(y)\right) d\mu(x) = \int_Y \left(\int_X f(x,y), d\mu(x)\right) d\nu(y)$$

The **Tonelli variant** (for non-negative measurable $f$, no integrability hypothesis needed — equality holds though both sides may be $+\infty$) is often used to _check_ the integrability hypothesis before invoking Fubini.

### The Classic Counterexample

Without the absolute integrability condition, Fubini fails. Consider $[0,1]^2$ with:

$$f(x,y) = \frac{x^2 - y^2}{(x^2 + y^2)^2}$$

One can verify: $\int_0^1 \int_0^1 f, dy, dx = \frac{\pi}{4}$ but $\int_0^1 \int_0^1 f, dx, dy = -\frac{\pi}{4}$.

The two iterated integrals _disagree_ because $\int \int |f|, dA = \infty$. Fubini's hypothesis is violated, and it shows.

## § 7 — Visualization

### 7.1 — The Geometric Interpretation

```
y
|
d |-----+-----+-----+
  |  f  |  f  |  f  |   ← fix y = y₀: integrate across x  →  A(y₀)
c |-----+-----+-----+
  a           b        x

∫_c^d A(y) dy  =  ∫_a^b B(x) dx  =  Volume under surface z = f(x,y)
```

The double integral computes the **volume** under the surface $z = f(x,y)$ above the region $R$. Fubini says: slice the volume with planes $x = \text{const}$ to get cross-sectional area $A(y)$ (or $B(x)$), then integrate those areas.

### 7.2 — Cross-Section Mental Model

Imagine the solid ${(x,y,z) : (x,y) \in R,, 0 \le z \le f(x,y)}$.

- Fix $x = x_0$. The cross-section is a 2D region in the $yz$-plane with area $\int_c^d f(x_0, y), dy$.
- Slide $x_0$ from $a$ to $b$. Integrating those cross-sectional areas gives the volume.

This is **Cavalieri's Principle** in disguise. Fubini is the analytic rigorous version of: _the volume is the integral of the cross-sectional areas_.

```
z                      z = f(x,y)
|    /~~~~~\
|   /       \
|  /  cross  \
| /  section  \
|/_______________\ y
x = x₀  (fixed)

Area of slice = ∫_c^d f(x₀, y) dy
```

## § 8 — Fubini in AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This is not a digression. Fubini is everywhere in modern ML.

### 8.1 — Expectation and Joint Distributions

The expected value of a function $\phi(X, Y)$ under a joint distribution $p(x,y)$ is:

$$\mathbb{E}[\phi(X,Y)] = \iint \phi(x,y), p(x,y), dx, dy$$

**Fubini lets us marginalize in any order:**

$$= \int \left(\int \phi(x,y), p(x|y), dx\right) p(y), dy = \int \left(\int \phi(x,y), p(y|x), dy\right) p(x), dx$$

Every time you condition on a variable and integrate out another, you are invoking Fubini.

### 8.2 — The ELBO in Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)

The Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) involves:

$$\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{q(z|x)}\left[\log p(x|z)\right] - \text{KL}(q(z|x) | p(z))$$

The KL divergence term requires switching integration and differentiation in a way that is only valid because the integrands are sufficiently well-behaved — Fubini-type arguments underlie the interchange.

### 8.3 — Gradient of Expectations (REINFORCE, Score Function Estimator)

In reinforcement learning and variational inference, one needs:

$$\nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta(x)}[f(x)] = \nabla_\theta \int f(x), p_\theta(x), dx = \int f(x), \nabla_\theta p_\theta(x), dx$$

Interchanging the gradient and the integral is justified by the **Leibniz integral rule** — a direct corollary of Fubini applied to a product measure (treating $\theta$ as one coordinate, $x$ as another). The score function trick $\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x) = \frac{\nabla_\theta p_\theta(x)}{p_\theta(x)}$ is the payoff.

### 8.4 — Kernel Methods and Integral Operators

In kernel machines and Gaussian Processes, the kernel matrix entry is:

$$K(x, x') = \int \phi(x, \omega), \phi(x', \omega), p(\omega), d\omega$$

(Random Fourier Features, Bochner's theorem). Manipulating such integrals — bounding generalization error, computing posterior means — routinely requires switching integration order.

### 8.5 — Convolutional Neural Networks

The convolution operation at layer $l$:

$$(f * g)(x) = \int f(x - y), g(y), dy$$

is itself an integral transform. Properties like associativity of convolution — essential to understanding deep network composition — follow from Fubini:

$$\int \int f(x - y), g(y - z), h(z), dy, dz = \int h(z) \left(\int f(x-y), g(y-z), dy\right) dz$$

The inner integral is a convolution; rearranging the order is Fubini.

### 8.6 — Normalizing Flows and Change of Variables

In normalizing flows, one models a complex distribution by a sequence of invertible transformations. The change of variables formula in multiple dimensions:

$$p_X(x) = p_Z(f^{-1}(x)) \left|\det J_{f^{-1}}(x)\right|$$

requires that the Jacobian determinant integrates correctly under the joint distribution — which is again a Fubini-type argument ensuring the integral of $p_X$ over $\mathbb{R}^n$ equals 1.

## § 9 — Summary and Working Rules

|Situation|What to do|
|---|---|
|Rectangle, $f$ continuous|Apply Fubini freely in either order|
|Non-rectangular region|Sketch the region; decide Type I or Type II; set up limits carefully|
|One order leads to non-elementary integral|Switch the order of integration|
|Verifying integrability (general $f$)|Check $\iint \|f\| < \infty$ first (Tonelli), then invoke Fubini|
|Counterexample risk|Remember: switching order without absolute integrability can give wrong answers|

**The working habit:** _Always sketch the region before setting up any iterated integral._ Getting the limits wrong is far more common than misapplying the theorem itself.
