

> _"Going from double to triple integrals is not a new idea — it is the same idea, promoted one dimension. The reward is access to the full physical world: volume, mass, gravity, charge, and probability in three dimensions."_

## § 0 — The Template, Extended to 3D

The same master pattern from double integrals now lives in $\mathbb{R}^3$:

$$\text{Global quantity} = \iiint_E \underbrace{(\text{density of that quantity})}_{\text{changes with application}}, dV$$

The region $E \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ replaces the planar region $D$. The volume element $dV = dx, dy, dz$ (Cartesian), $r, dr, d\theta, dz$ (cylindrical), or $\rho^2 \sin\phi, d\rho, d\phi, d\theta$ (spherical) replaces $dA$.

Everything else — setting up limits, applying Fubini, switching coordinate systems — is the same machinery.

---

## § 1 — Volume

The simplest application: when the "density" is identically $1$, the triple integral counts volume.

$$V(E) = \iiint_E dV$$

This is the definition of volume for regions too complex for elementary geometry.

### Example 1 — Volume of a tetrahedron

Let $E$ be the tetrahedron bounded by the coordinate planes and the plane $x + y + z = 1$, i.e.:

$$E = {(x,y,z) : x \ge 0,, y \ge 0,, z \ge 0,, x+y+z \le 1}$$

**Set up limits:** Fix $x \in [0,1]$. For fixed $x$, $y$ ranges $[0, 1-x]$. For fixed $x, y$: $z \in [0, 1-x-y]$.

$$V = \int_0^1 \int_0^{1-x} \int_0^{1-x-y} dz, dy, dx$$

Innermost: $\int_0^{1-x-y} dz = 1-x-y$

Middle: $\int_0^{1-x} (1-x-y), dy = \left[(1-x)y - \frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{1-x} = (1-x)^2 - \frac{(1-x)^2}{2} = \frac{(1-x)^2}{2}$

Outer: $\int_0^1 \frac{(1-x)^2}{2}, dx = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6}$

The known formula for a tetrahedron with legs $a=b=c=1$ is $V = \frac{abc}{6} = \frac{1}{6}$ ✓

### Example 2 — Volume between paraboloid and plane (cylindrical)

Find the volume of the solid $E$ bounded above by $z = 4$ and below by $z = x^2 + y^2$.

The paraboloid meets $z=4$ when $r^2 = 4$, i.e., $r = 2$. In cylindrical coordinates:

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^2 \int_{r^2}^{4} r, dz, dr, d\theta$$

Inner: $\int_{r^2}^4 dz = 4 - r^2$

Middle: $\int_0^2 r(4-r^2), dr = \int_0^2 (4r - r^3), dr = \left[2r^2 - \frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^2 = 8 - 4 = 4$

Outer: $\int_0^{2\pi} 4, d\theta = 8\pi$

## § 2 — Mass and Density

A solid $E$ with **volume mass density** $\rho(x,y,z)$ (kg/m³) has total mass:

$$M = \iiint_E \rho(x,y,z), dV$$

### Example 3 — Sphere with radially varying density

Solid ball $E: x^2+y^2+z^2 \le R^2$, density $\rho = k\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2} = k\rho_s$ (using $\rho_s$ for the radial coordinate to avoid clash with mass density — see note below).

> **Notation alert:** In spherical coordinates the radial variable is traditionally also called $\rho$. To avoid collision with mass density, I write the spherical radial coordinate as $r_s$ in this example.

In spherical coordinates ($r_s, \phi, \theta$), $dV = r_s^2 \sin\phi, dr_s, d\phi, d\theta$:

$$M = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\int_0^R k r_s \cdot r_s^2 \sin\phi, dr_s, d\phi, d\theta$$

$$= k \int_0^{2\pi} d\theta \int_0^{\pi} \sin\phi, d\phi \int_0^R r_s^3, dr_s = k \cdot 2\pi \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{R^4}{4} = k\pi R^4$$

**Interpretation:** Denser material toward the surface (like a hollow shell approximation) gives $M \propto R^4$ rather than the uniform $M \propto R^3$.

## § 3 — Moments and Center of Mass

### Definitions

**First moments** about the coordinate planes:

$$M_{yz} = \iiint_E x,\rho, dV \qquad M_{xz} = \iiint_E y,\rho, dV \qquad M_{xy} = \iiint_E z,\rho, dV$$

**Center of mass:**

$$\bar{x} = \frac{M_{yz}}{M}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{M_{xz}}{M}, \qquad \bar{z} = \frac{M_{xy}}{M}$$

For uniform density, these reduce to the **centroid** — a purely geometric property.

### Example 4 — Centroid of the tetrahedron from Example 1

Tetrahedron $E: x+y+z \le 1$, $x,y,z \ge 0$, uniform $\rho = 1$. $M = V = 1/6$.

By the complete symmetry of $E$ in $x$, $y$, $z$: $\bar{x} = \bar{y} = \bar{z}$.

Compute $M_{yz} = \iiint_E x, dV$:

$$\int_0^1 \int_0^{1-x} \int_0^{1-x-y} x, dz, dy, dx = \int_0^1 x \int_0^{1-x}(1-x-y), dy, dx$$

Inner over $y$: $\frac{(1-x)^2}{2}$ (from Example 1)

$$= \int_0^1 \frac{x(1-x)^2}{2}, dx = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^1 (x - 2x^2 + x^3), dx = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{2} - \frac{2}{3} + \frac{1}{4}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{12} = \frac{1}{24}$$

$$\bar{x} = \frac{M_{yz}}{M} = \frac{1/24}{1/6} = \frac{1}{4}$$

So the centroid of the tetrahedron is $\left(\frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{4}, \frac{1}{4}\right)$.

> **General result:** The centroid of a tetrahedron is always the average of its four vertices: $\frac{1}{4}\big((0,0,0)+(1,0,0)+(0,1,0)+(0,0,1)\big) = (1/4, 1/4, 1/4)$ ✓

## § 4 — Moments of Inertia

### Definitions

$$I_x = \iiint_E (y^2 + z^2),\rho, dV \qquad \text{(distance}^2 \text{ from } x\text{-axis)}$$

$$I_y = \iiint_E (x^2 + z^2),\rho, dV \qquad I_z = \iiint_E (x^2 + y^2),\rho, dV$$

The distance from the $z$-axis is $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$, so $I_z = \iiint (x^2+y^2)\rho, dV$ — which in cylindrical coordinates is $\iiint r^2 \rho, dV$.

**Radius of gyration** about axis $\ell$: $\tilde{r} = \sqrt{I_\ell / M}$

### Example 5 — Moment of inertia of a cylinder

Solid cylinder: $r \le a$, $0 \le z \le h$, uniform density $\rho_0$.

$$I_z = \rho_0 \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^a\int_0^h r^2 \cdot r, dz, dr, d\theta = \rho_0 \cdot 2\pi \cdot h \int_0^a r^3, dr = \rho_0 \cdot 2\pi h \cdot \frac{a^4}{4} = \frac{\pi\rho_0 h a^4}{2}$$

Since $M = \rho_0 \pi a^2 h$:

$$\boxed{I_z = \frac{1}{2}Ma^2}$$

This is the formula used in every introductory mechanics course. The radius of gyration is $\tilde{r} = a/\sqrt{2}$ — not $a/2$, because the $r^2$ weighting overrepresents the outer material.

### Example 6 — Moment of inertia of a sphere

Uniform solid sphere, radius $R$, mass $M$. Compute $I_z = \iiint (x^2+y^2)\rho, dV$.

In spherical: $x^2 + y^2 = r_s^2 \sin^2\phi$, $dV = r_s^2 \sin\phi, dr_s, d\phi, d\theta$:

$$I_z = \rho_0\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\int_0^{\pi}\sin^2\phi\cdot\sin\phi, d\phi\int_0^R r_s^4, dr_s$$

$$= \rho_0 \cdot 2\pi \cdot \frac{4}{3} \cdot \frac{R^5}{5} = \frac{8\pi\rho_0 R^5}{15}$$

Since $M = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3 \rho_0$:

$$\boxed{I_z = \frac{2}{5}MR^2}$$

> The integral $\int_0^\pi \sin^3\phi, d\phi = 4/3$ uses the substitution $u = \cos\phi$.

## § 5 — Probability in Three Dimensions

A function $f(x,y,z) \ge 0$ is a **joint PDF** for $(X,Y,Z)$ if:

$$\iiint_{\mathbb{R}^3} f(x,y,z), dV = 1$$

Probability of an event $E$:

$$P\big((X,Y,Z) \in E\big) = \iiint_E f(x,y,z), dV$$

**Marginal densities:**

$$f_{XY}(x,y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x,y,z), dz \qquad f_X(x) = \iint f(x,y,z), dy, dz$$

### The 3D Gaussian

The trivariate normal with independent components, $\sigma_X = \sigma_Y = \sigma_Z = \sigma$:

$$f(x,y,z) = \frac{1}{(2\pi\sigma^2)^{3/2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{x^2+y^2+z^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

Verification $\iiint f = 1$: the integral factors into three independent Gaussians, each integrating to $1$. In spherical coordinates, the same polar trick from § 4 of the double-integral note works, now with the 3D volume element.

## § 6 — Expected Values and Moments

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \iiint x, f(x,y,z), dV, \quad \mathbb{E}[g(X,Y,Z)] = \iiint g\cdot f, dV$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \iiint (x - \mu_X)^2 f, dV = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mu_X^2$$

### The Unified Dictionary (Extended)

|3D Physics|3D Probability|ML/DL Object|
|---|---|---|
|Volume density $\rho(x,y,z)$|Joint PDF $f(x,y,z)$|Data/latent distribution|
|Total mass $\iiint\rho,dV$|Total probability $=1$|Partition function|
|$M_{yz} = \iiint x\rho,dV$|$\mathbb{E}[X]$|Feature mean|
|$I_z = \iiint(x^2+y^2)\rho,dV$|$\mathbb{E}[X^2+Y^2]$|Second moment|
|Center of mass $(\bar{x},\bar{y},\bar{z})$|Mean $(\mu_X,\mu_Y,\mu_Z)$|Centroid of embedding|
|Radius of gyration $\tilde{r}$|RMS deviation|RMS activation|

### 7.1 — The Three Coordinate Systems

```
CARTESIAN          CYLINDRICAL              SPHERICAL
   z                    z                       z
   |                    |                       |
   |____y               |    r                  |   φ (polar angle)
  /                    θ\  /                    |  /
 x                       \/                    rs/
                          P = (r,θ,z)           P = (rs,φ,θ)

dV = dx dy dz    dV = r dr dθ dz      dV = rs² sinφ drs dφ dθ

Use Cartesian:      Use Cylindrical:       Use Spherical:
box, tetrahedra     cylinders, cones       balls, shells
flat boundaries     rotationally           full 3D symmetry
                    symmetric in z
```

### 7.2 — Setting Up Limits: The 3D Strategy

```
Step 1: Sketch E (or describe it in words)
Step 2: Choose the outermost variable (one with fixed, constant limits)
Step 3: For the middle variable, limits may depend on the outer
Step 4: For the innermost variable, limits depend on both outer variables

Example: E = {x²+y²+z² ≤ 1, z ≥ 0}  (upper hemisphere)

Cartesian: outer x ∈ [-1,1]
           middle y ∈ [-√(1-x²), √(1-x²)]
           inner  z ∈ [0, √(1-x²-y²)]
           → painful

Spherical: rs ∈ [0,1], φ ∈ [0,π/2], θ ∈ [0,2π]
           → all constant limits. Use spherical.
```

### 7.3 — Moment of Inertia: The $r^2$ Effect

## § 8 — Applications in AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 8.1 — Latent Space Geometry and Volume

In generative models (VAEs, GANs, diffusion models), the latent space is a region $E \subset \mathbb{R}^d$ (high-dimensional, but often locally approximated by $\mathbb{R}^3$ for theory). The **volume** of the high-probability region of the prior $p(z)$ controls:

- **Posterior collapse** in VAEs: if the posterior $q(z|x)$ occupies a tiny volume relative to the prior, gradients vanish.
- **Mode coverage**: the fraction of latent space volume occupied by each class $= \iiint_{\text{class region}} p(z), dV$.

The KL divergence $\text{KL}(q | p) = \iiint q(z)\log\frac{q(z)}{p(z)}, dz$ is a triple (or higher-dimensional) integral — its minimization is what the ELBO training objective achieves.

### 8.2 — 3D Deep Learning: Point Clouds and Voxels

In 3D computer vision (autonomous driving, robotics, medical imaging), objects are represented as:

**Voxel grids:** Discretize $E \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ into an $N^3$ grid. The **mass** of a voxel object:

$$M \approx \sum_{i,j,k} \rho_{ijk}, \Delta V$$

is the discretization of $\iiint_E \rho, dV$. Networks like 3D-UNet and VoxNet learn $\rho_{ijk}$ directly.

**Continuous density fields (NeRF — Neural Radiance Fields):**

$$\hat{C}(\mathbf{r}) = \int_{t_n}^{t_f} T(t), \sigma(\mathbf{r}(t)), \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{r}(t), \mathbf{d}), dt$$

where $\sigma$ is the **volume density** (how opaque the scene is at point $\mathbf{r}(t)$), $T(t) = \exp!\left(-\int_{t_n}^t \sigma, ds\right)$ is transmittance, and the color $\hat{C}$ is a **line integral through a 3D density field**. This is a triple integral (ray $\times$ volume density) — the generalization of $M = \iiint \rho, dV$ to rendering.

### 8.3 — Monte Carlo in High Dimensions

The population risk over a 3D input:

$$\mathcal{R}(\theta) = \iiint_{\mathbb{R}^3} \ell(f_\theta(x,y,z), t), p(x,y,z), dx, dy, dz$$

is approximated by mini-batch SGD:

$$\hat{\mathcal{R}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^B \ell(f_\theta(x_i, y_i, z_i), t_i)$$

This is Monte Carlo estimation of the triple integral — unbiased, with error $O(1/\sqrt{B})$ regardless of dimension. In 3D medical imaging (CT, MRI), each sample $(x_i, y_i, z_i)$ is a voxel.

### 8.4 — Center of Mass in 3D Feature Spaces

**Prototype-based classification** (e.g., ProtoNets in few-shot learning) represents each class by its **centroid** in embedding space:

$$\mathbf{c}_k = \frac{1}{|S_k|}\sum_{i \in S_k} \mathbf{e}_i \approx \frac{\iiint_{\text{class}_k} \mathbf{x}, p(\mathbf{x}), d\mathbf{x}}{\iiint_{\text{class}_k} p(\mathbf{x}), d\mathbf{x}}$$

The discrete sum is the empirical version of $\bar{\mathbf{x}} = M_{yz}/M$ in 3D. Classification assigns a new point to the nearest centroid — which is meaningful only if the centroid reliably represents the class distribution, i.e., if the embedding clusters are convex and well-separated.

### 8.5 — Moment of Inertia and Spectral Normalization

The **spectral norm** of a weight matrix $W$ is its largest singular value. In the continuous limit, the singular values of the integral operator $T_k f(x) = \iiint k(x,y)f(y), dy$ are related to the eigenvalues of the operator — which are moments of $k$ with respect to an orthogonal basis. **Spectral normalization** (used in GANs to stabilize training) constrains $|W|_2 \le 1$, which bounds the "rotational inertia" of the weight operator. The connection is direct: $I_z = \iiint r^2\rho, dV$ measures how spread-out the mass is; $|W|_2$ measures how spread-out the operator's action is.

### 8.6 — Occupancy Networks and Implicit Representations

**Occupancy Networks** (Mescheder et al., 2019) represent 3D shapes as:

$$f_\theta : \mathbb{R}^3 \to [0,1], \quad f_\theta(x,y,z) = P(\text{point }(x,y,z)\text{ is inside the shape})$$

The **volume** of the reconstructed shape:

$$V = \iiint_{\mathbb{R}^3} f_\theta(x,y,z), dx, dy, dz$$

is a triple integral over the learned density field. Training minimizes the binary cross-entropy between $f_\theta$ and ground-truth occupancy, which in the continuous limit is:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\iiint \left[o(x)\log f_\theta(x) + (1-o(x))\log(1-f_\theta(x))\right] p(x), dx, dy, dz$$

again a triple integral over $\mathbb{R}^3$.

## § 9 — Worked Problem: All Concepts Together

**Problem.** A radioactive solid occupies the upper hemisphere $E: x^2+y^2+z^2 \le a^2$, $z \ge 0$. Its density is $\rho(x,y,z) = kz$ (denser near the top).

Find: (a) total mass $M$, (b) center of mass $(\bar{x},\bar{y},\bar{z})$, (c) moment of inertia $I_z$.

**Use spherical:** $x = r_s\sin\phi\cos\theta$, $y = r_s\sin\phi\sin\theta$, $z = r_s\cos\phi$, $\rho = kr_s\cos\phi$, $dV = r_s^2\sin\phi, dr_s, d\phi, d\theta$. Limits: $r_s \in [0,a]$, $\phi \in [0, \pi/2]$, $\theta \in [0,2\pi]$.

**(a) Mass:**

$$M = \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^a kr_s\cos\phi \cdot r_s^2\sin\phi, dr_s, d\phi$$

$$= k\cdot 2\pi \cdot \frac{a^4}{4} \cdot \int_0^{\pi/2}\sin\phi\cos\phi, d\phi = k\cdot 2\pi\cdot\frac{a^4}{4}\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \frac{k\pi a^4}{4}$$

**(b) Center of mass:** By axial symmetry ($\rho$ depends only on $z$), $\bar{x} = \bar{y} = 0$.

$$M_{xy} = \iiint_E z\rho, dV = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^a (r_s\cos\phi)(kr_s\cos\phi) r_s^2\sin\phi, dr_s, d\phi, d\theta$$

$$= k\cdot 2\pi\cdot\frac{a^5}{5}\cdot\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^2\phi\sin\phi, d\phi = k\cdot 2\pi\cdot\frac{a^5}{5}\cdot\frac{1}{3} = \frac{2k\pi a^5}{15}$$

$$\bar{z} = \frac{M_{xy}}{M} = \frac{2k\pi a^5/15}{k\pi a^4/4} = \frac{8a}{15}$$

**(c) Moment of inertia about $z$-axis** ($x^2+y^2 = r_s^2\sin^2\phi$ in spherical):

$$I_z = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^a r_s^2\sin^2\phi \cdot kr_s\cos\phi \cdot r_s^2\sin\phi, dr_s, d\phi, d\theta$$

$$= k\cdot 2\pi\cdot\frac{a^6}{6}\cdot\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^3\phi\cos\phi, d\phi = k\cdot 2\pi\cdot\frac{a^6}{6}\cdot\frac{1}{4} = \frac{k\pi a^6}{12}$$

In terms of $M = k\pi a^4/4$: $I_z = \frac{a^2}{3}\cdot M = \frac{Ma^2}{3}$.

## § 10 — Summary

|Application|Formula|
|---|---|
|Volume|$V = \iiint_E dV$|
|Mass|$M = \iiint_E \rho, dV$|
|First moments|$M_{yz} = \iiint x\rho, dV$, $M_{xz} = \iiint y\rho, dV$, $M_{xy} = \iiint z\rho, dV$|
|Center of mass|$(\bar{x},\bar{y},\bar{z}) = (M_{yz}/M,, M_{xz}/M,, M_{xy}/M)$|
|Moment of inertia ($z$-axis)|$I_z = \iiint (x^2+y^2)\rho, dV$|
|Moment of inertia ($x$-axis)|$I_x = \iiint (y^2+z^2)\rho, dV$|
|Probability of event $E$|$P((X,Y,Z)\in E) = \iiint_E f, dV$|
|Expected value|$\mathbb{E}[g] = \iiint g(x,y,z), f(x,y,z), dV$|

**Coordinate system choice — quick rule:**

```
Flat/polyhedral boundaries    →  Cartesian
Cylindrical symmetry (axis)   →  Cylindrical
Full spherical symmetry       →  Spherical
```
