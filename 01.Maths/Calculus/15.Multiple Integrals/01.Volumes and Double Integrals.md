

> _"The single integral measures length swept into area. The double integral measures area swept into volume. The pattern continues — and so does the wonder."_

---

## 0. Preamble — Motivation

The definite integral $\int_a^b f(x),dx$ computes the signed area between $y = f(x)$ and the $x$-axis. Now we have a function $f(x,y)$ of two variables — its graph is a surface $z = f(x,y)$ floating above the $xy$-plane.

**The question:** What is the volume of the solid between this surface and the $xy$-plane, over a region $D$?

The answer is the **double integral** $\iint_D f(x,y),dA$, and building it from scratch — through the same limiting process as the single integral — is what this note is about.

---

## 1. The Riemann Sum Construction

### 1.1 Setting Up the Approximation

Let $f : D \to \mathbb{R}$ be defined on a closed rectangle $R = [a,b] \times [c,d]$. Divide:

- $[a,b]$ into $m$ subintervals of width $\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{m}$, with partition points $x_0 = a < x_1 < \cdots < x_m = b$
- $[c,d]$ into $n$ subintervals of width $\Delta y = \frac{d-c}{n}$, with partition points $y_0 = c < y_1 < \cdots < y_n = d$

This creates $mn$ sub-rectangles $R_{ij} = [x_{i-1}, x_i] \times [y_{j-1}, y_j]$, each with area: $$\Delta A = \Delta x \cdot \Delta y$$

### 1.2 The Sample Points

In each $R_{ij}$, choose a **sample point** $(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_)$. The value $f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_)$ is the height of the surface at that point. The volume of the thin box (column) over $R_{ij}$ is approximately: $$\text{Volume of box}_{ij} \approx f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_) \cdot \Delta A$$

### 1.3 The Double Riemann Sum

Summing over all sub-rectangles: $$V \approx \sum_{i=1}^{m} \sum_{j=1}^{n} f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_),\Delta A$$

This is the **double Riemann sum**. As $m, n \to \infty$ (boxes get thinner), the approximation becomes exact.

### 1.4 The Double Integral — Definition

**Definition.** If the limit exists and is the same regardless of choice of sample points, then $f$ is **integrable** over $R$ and: $$\iint_R f(x,y),dA = \lim_{m,n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{j=1}^{n} f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_),\Delta A$$

**Theorem.** If $f$ is continuous on $R$, then $f$ is integrable over $R$.

> **Geometric meaning when $f \geq 0$:** $\iint_R f(x,y),dA$ is the volume of the solid between the surface $z = f(x,y)$ and the rectangle $R$ in the $xy$-plane.
> 
> **When $f$ takes negative values:** Like the single integral, the double integral computes _signed_ volume — regions where $f < 0$ contribute negatively.

## 2. Fubini's Theorem — How to Actually Compute

The definition via limits is impractical. Fubini's theorem converts a double integral into two successive single integrals.

### 2.1 The Theorem

**Theorem (Fubini).** _If $f$ is continuous on the rectangle $R = [a,b] \times [c,d]$, then:_ $$\iint_R f(x,y),dA = \int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y),dy,dx = \int_c^d \int_a^b f(x,y),dx,dy$$

Both **iterated integrals** give the same answer. The order of integration can be swapped freely (when $f$ is continuous).

### 2.2 The Idea Behind Fubini — Cavalieri's Principle

Fix $x \in [a,b]$. The **cross-sectional area** of the solid at position $x$ is: $$A(x) = \int_c^d f(x,y),dy$$ (treat $x$ as a constant, integrate over $y$).

The volume is then: $$V = \int_a^b A(x),dx = \int_a^b \left[\int_c^d f(x,y),dy\right] dx$$

This is Cavalieri's principle: volume = integral of cross-sectional areas. Fubini's theorem is the rigorous version.

### 2.3 Notation

The iterated integral is written: $$\int_a^b \int_c^d f(x,y),dy,dx$$

Read from the _inside out_:

1. Inner integral: $\int_c^d f(x,y),dy$ — integrate with respect to $y$, treating $x$ as a constant. Result is a function of $x$.
2. Outer integral: $\int_a^b [\cdots],dx$ — integrate the result with respect to $x$.

### 2.4 Worked Example — Rectangle

Compute $\iint_R (x^2 + 2xy),dA$ where $R = [0,2] \times [1,3]$.

**Method (integrate $y$ first, then $x$):**

$$\int_0^2 \int_1^3 (x^2 + 2xy),dy,dx$$

Inner integral ($x$ fixed): $$\int_1^3 (x^2 + 2xy),dy = \left[x^2 y + xy^2\right]_1^3 = (3x^2 + 9x) - (x^2 + x) = 2x^2 + 8x$$

Outer integral: $$\int_0^2 (2x^2 + 8x),dx = \left[\frac{2x^3}{3} + 4x^2\right]_0^2 = \frac{16}{3} + 16 = \frac{64}{3}$$

**Verify by reversing order (integrate $x$ first, then $y$):**

$$\int_1^3 \int_0^2 (x^2 + 2xy),dx,dy$$

Inner integral ($y$ fixed): $$\int_0^2 (x^2 + 2xy),dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 y\right]_0^2 = \frac{8}{3} + 4y$$

Outer integral: $$\int_1^3 \left(\frac{8}{3} + 4y\right)dy = \left[\frac{8y}{3} + 2y^2\right]_1^3 = \left(8 + 18\right) - \left(\frac{8}{3} + 2\right) = 26 - \frac{14}{3} = \frac{64}{3} \checkmark$$

## 3. Double Integrals Over General Regions

Most domains of interest are not rectangles. We integrate over general regions by describing the boundary carefully.

### 3.1 Type I Regions (vertical slices)

**Definition.** $D$ is a **Type I** region if: $$D = {(x,y) : a \leq x \leq b,; g_1(x) \leq y \leq g_2(x)}$$

The $y$-limits depend on $x$ — the region lies between two curves $y = g_1(x)$ (bottom) and $y = g_2(x)$ (top).

$$\iint_D f(x,y),dA = \int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f(x,y),dy,dx$$

**Example.** The region bounded by $y = x^2$ and $y = 2x$ is Type I with $a = 0$, $b = 2$ (intersection points: $x^2 = 2x \implies x = 0, 2$), $g_1(x) = x^2$, $g_2(x) = 2x$.

$$\iint_D f,dA = \int_0^2 \int_{x^2}^{2x} f(x,y),dy,dx$$

### 3.2 Type II Regions (horizontal slices)

**Definition.** $D$ is a **Type II** region if: $$D = {(x,y) : c \leq y \leq d,; h_1(y) \leq x \leq h_2(y)}$$

The $x$-limits depend on $y$ — the region lies between $x = h_1(y)$ (left) and $x = h_2(y)$ (right).

$$\iint_D f(x,y),dA = \int_c^d \int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f(x,y),dx,dy$$

### 3.3 Choosing the Order of Integration

Some integrals are impossible (or very hard) in one order but elementary in the other. The strategy is:

> Sketch the region $D$. Identify which description (Type I or Type II) produces integrable limits. Try the other order if the inner integral cannot be evaluated in closed form.

**Example of switching order.** Consider $\int_0^1 \int_x^1 e^{y^2},dy,dx$.

$e^{y^2}$ has no elementary antiderivative in $y$ — we cannot evaluate the inner integral directly.

**Switch order.** The region is ${0 \leq x \leq y,; 0 \leq y \leq 1}$. As a Type II region: $0 \leq y \leq 1$, $0 \leq x \leq y$.

$$\int_0^1 \int_0^y e^{y^2},dx,dy = \int_0^1 \left[xe^{y^2}\right]_0^y dy = \int_0^1 y e^{y^2},dy = \left[\frac{e^{y^2}}{2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{e-1}{2}$$

## 4. Volume as a Double Integral

The volume of the solid bounded above by $z = f(x,y) \geq 0$ and below by the region $D$ in the $xy$-plane is:

$$V = \iint_D f(x,y),dA$$

This is the defining geometric interpretation.

### 4.1 Volume Between Two Surfaces

If $f(x,y) \geq g(x,y)$ on $D$, the volume between the two surfaces $z = f(x,y)$ (top) and $z = g(x,y)$ (bottom) is: $$V = \iint_D [f(x,y) - g(x,y)],dA$$

### 4.2 Full Worked Example — Volume Under a Paraboloid

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid bounded above by $z = 4 - x^2 - y^2$ and below by the $xy$-plane, over the region where $z \geq 0$.

The surface meets the $xy$-plane where $4 - x^2 - y^2 = 0$, i.e., on the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 4$. So $D = {x^2 + y^2 \leq 4}$.

$$V = \iint_D (4 - x^2 - y^2),dA$$

Describe $D$ as Type I: $-2 \leq x \leq 2$, $-\sqrt{4-x^2} \leq y \leq \sqrt{4-x^2}$.

$$V = \int_{-2}^{2} \int_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}^{\sqrt{4-x^2}} (4 - x^2 - y^2),dy,dx$$

Inner integral: $$\int_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}^{\sqrt{4-x^2}} (4 - x^2 - y^2),dy = \left[(4-x^2)y - \frac{y^3}{3}\right]_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}^{\sqrt{4-x^2}}$$

Let $r^2 = 4 - x^2$. By symmetry in $y$: $$= 2\left[(4-x^2)\sqrt{4-x^2} - \frac{(4-x^2)^{3/2}}{3}\right] = 2(4-x^2)^{3/2}\left[1 - \frac{1}{3}\right] = \frac{4}{3}(4-x^2)^{3/2}$$

Outer integral: $$V = \int_{-2}^{2} \frac{4}{3}(4-x^2)^{3/2},dx$$

Substitute $x = 2\sin\theta$, $dx = 2\cos\theta,d\theta$, $\sqrt{4-x^2} = 2\cos\theta$, limits $\theta \in [-\pi/2, \pi/2]$:

$$V = \int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} \frac{4}{3}(2\cos\theta)^3 \cdot 2\cos\theta,d\theta = \frac{4}{3} \cdot 16 \int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} \cos^4\theta,d\theta$$

Using $\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2}\cos^4\theta,d\theta = \frac{3\pi}{8}$:

$$V = \frac{64}{3} \cdot \frac{3\pi}{8} = 8\pi$$

_(This result is more elegantly obtained using polar coordinates — see §6.)_

## 5. Properties of the Double Integral

These mirror the single-variable properties exactly:

**Linearity:** $$\iint_D [f + g],dA = \iint_D f,dA + \iint_D g,dA, \qquad \iint_D cf,dA = c\iint_D f,dA$$

**Additivity over regions:** If $D = D_1 \cup D_2$ with $D_1 \cap D_2$ having zero area: $$\iint_D f,dA = \iint_{D_1} f,dA + \iint_{D_2} f,dA$$

**Monotonicity:** If $f \geq g$ on $D$, then $\iint_D f,dA \geq \iint_D g,dA$.

**Area formula:** If $f \equiv 1$: $$\iint_D 1,dA = \text{Area}(D)$$

**Average value:** $$\bar{f} = \frac{1}{\text{Area}(D)}\iint_D f(x,y),dA$$

## 6. Double Integrals in Polar Coordinates

When $D$ is a disk, annulus, or sector — or when $f$ involves $x^2 + y^2$ — polar coordinates simplify the integral dramatically.

**Transformation:** $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, with the crucial factor: $$dA = r,dr,d\theta$$

> ⚠️ The factor $r$ is not optional. It arises from the Jacobian of the transformation: $\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)} = r$. A polar area element is a thin wedge of area $r,dr,d\theta$, not $dr,d\theta$.

**Formula:** $$\iint_D f(x,y),dA = \int_\alpha^\beta \int_{r_1(\theta)}^{r_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)\cdot r,dr,d\theta$$

### 6.1 Common Polar Regions

|Region|Polar Description|
|---|---|
|Disk $x^2+y^2 \leq a^2$|$0 \leq r \leq a$, $0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi$|
|Upper half-disk|$0 \leq r \leq a$, $0 \leq \theta \leq \pi$|
|Annulus $a^2 \leq x^2+y^2 \leq b^2$|$a \leq r \leq b$, $0 \leq \theta \leq 2\pi$|
|Sector, angle $\alpha$ to $\beta$|$0 \leq r \leq a$, $\alpha \leq \theta \leq \beta$|

### 6.2 Revisiting the Paraboloid Volume

$$V = \iint_{x^2+y^2 \leq 4} (4 - x^2 - y^2),dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 (4 - r^2),r,dr,d\theta$$

$$= \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta \cdot \int_0^2 (4r - r^3),dr = 2\pi \left[2r^2 - \frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^2 = 2\pi\left(8 - 4\right) = 8\pi \checkmark$$

Far cleaner than Cartesian.

### 6.3 The Gaussian Integral — A Famous Application

Double integrals in polar coordinates give one of the most celebrated results in all of mathematics:

$$\left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2},dx\right)^2 = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} e^{-(x^2+y^2)},dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2} r,dr,d\theta = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \pi$$

Therefore: $$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2},dx = \sqrt{\pi}$$

This integral is the backbone of probability theory (it normalizes the Gaussian distribution) and appears throughout physics and ML.

## 8. Changing the Order of Integration — Strategy Guide

**Step 1.** Identify the region $D$ from the given limits:

- Inner limits of $y$: $y$ goes from $\phi_1(x)$ to $\phi_2(x)$
- Outer limits of $x$: $x$ goes from $a$ to $b$
- Sketch the region $D$.

**Step 2.** Re-describe $D$ with the roles of $x$ and $y$ swapped:

- New $y$ range (outer): read off the global $y$ extent from the sketch
- New $x$ range (inner): express $x$ bounds as functions of $y$

**Step 3.** Write the new iterated integral and evaluate.

**Example.** Reverse the order: $\int_0^1 \int_{y}^{1} \frac{\sin x}{x},dx,dy$.

Region: ${0 \leq y \leq x,; 0 \leq x \leq 1}$ (triangle below $y=x$).

As Type I: $0 \leq x \leq 1$, $0 \leq y \leq x$.

$$\int_0^1 \int_0^x \frac{\sin x}{x},dy,dx = \int_0^1 \frac{\sin x}{x} \cdot x,dx = \int_0^1 \sin x,dx = [-\cos x]_0^1 = 1 - \cos 1$$

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 9.1 Probability Distributions — The Core Application

In ML, every probabilistic model involves a probability density function (pdf) $p(\mathbf{x})$, which satisfies $p \geq 0$ and: $$\iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} p(x,y),dx,dy = 1$$

This normalization condition is a double integral. The Gaussian (normal) distribution in 2D is: $$p(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_1\sigma_2} \exp!\left(-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma_1^2} - \frac{y^2}{2\sigma_2^2}\right)$$

That this integrates to 1 is precisely a consequence of the Gaussian integral $\int e^{-x^2},dx = \sqrt{\pi}$ proved via polar coordinates in §6.3.

**Expected values.** If $X, Y$ are continuous random variables with joint density $p(x,y)$: $$\mathbb{E}[f(X,Y)] = \iint f(x,y),p(x,y),dx,dy$$

Every expectation in probabilistic ML — expected loss, expected reward in RL, variational lower bound in VAEs — is a (high-dimensional) integral of this form.

### 9.2 The Loss Function as an Expected Double Integral

In supervised learning, the **population risk** (expected loss over the true data distribution $p(x,y)$) is: $$\mathcal{R}(f) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim p},[\ell(f(x), y)] = \iint \ell(f(x), y),p(x,y),dx,dy$$

Training minimizes the _empirical_ version (a Riemann sum approximation!): $$\hat{\mathcal{R}}(f) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f(x_i), y_i) \approx \mathcal{R}(f)$$

The **law of large numbers** says the empirical risk converges to the true integral as $n \to \infty$. The double Riemann sum is not just an approximation scheme for volumes — it is the mathematical model for how a training set approximates the underlying data distribution.

### 9.3 Monte Carlo Integration — The ML Workhorse

In high dimensions ($n \gg 2$), iterated integrals are computationally infeasible — the cost grows as $N^n$ for a grid of $N$ points per dimension. ML uses **Monte Carlo integration** instead:

$$\iint f(x,y),p(x,y),dx,dy \approx \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f(x_i, y_i), \quad (x_i, y_i) \sim p$$

This is a stochastic Riemann sum: sample points from $p$, evaluate $f$, average. Error scales as $1/\sqrt{N}$ _regardless of dimension_ — this dimension-independence is why Monte Carlo is used universally in ML (MCMC, variational inference, policy gradient in RL).

The whole theory — why MC works, its variance, importance sampling — rests on the definition of the integral as a limit of Riemann sums.

### 9.4 Convolutional Neural Networks — Convolution as an Integral

The **convolution** operation (which gives CNNs their name) is, in continuous form: $$(f * g)(x) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x - t),g(t),dt$$

In 2D (image processing): $$(f * g)(x,y) = \iint f(x - s,, y - t),g(s,t),ds,dt$$

A convolutional layer computes a discrete approximation of this — a double Riemann sum. Every convolution in deep learning is a double integral in disguise.

### 9.5 The Marginal Distribution — Integrating Out Variables

Given a joint distribution $p(x,y)$, the **marginal** over $x$ is: $$p(x) = \int p(x,y),dy$$

This is the fundamental operation in latent variable models (VAEs, GMMs, hidden Markov models): to compute the probability of observed data $x$, integrate out the latent variables $y$. The integral is usually intractable in closed form — approximating it is the central challenge of **variational inference**, which replaces the integral with an optimization problem.

|Calculus Concept|ML Manifestation|

## 10. Summary of Techniques

|Situation|Method|
|---|---|
|Rectangular domain, continuous $f$|Fubini: either order, pick simpler|
|General domain, vertical slices|Type I: $\int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} f,dy,dx$|
|General domain, horizontal slices|Type II: $\int_c^d \int_{h_1(y)}^{h_2(y)} f,dx,dy$|
|Inner integral has no antiderivative|Switch order of integration|
|$D$ is a disk/annulus/sector, or $f = f(x^2+y^2)$|Polar: $\iint f,dA = \int!\int f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)\cdot r,dr,d\theta$|
|Volume between two surfaces|$V = \iint_D [f_\text{top} - f_\text{bottom}],dA$|
