

> _"Cartesian coordinates are honest but often unkind. Polar coordinates reward those who see the geometry."_

---

## §0 — Preamble

There is a class of double integrals that is technically legitimate in Cartesian form but computationally brutal — integrands involving $x^2 + y^2$, domains that are disks or sectors or annuli. Forcing these into the $dx,dy$ framework is like trying to describe a circle with a ruler: possible in principle, ugly in practice.

Polar coordinates exist precisely for this class of problems. The passage from $(x,y)$ to $(r,\theta)$ is not merely a substitution — it is a **change of measure**, and the appearance of the Jacobian factor $r$ is the theorem's most important output. This note proves the transformation rigorously, develops the geometric intuition, catalogues the standard domain types, supplies worked examples (including the famous Gaussian integral), and traces polar integration into deep learning and probability.

**References:** Stewart §15.3; MIT 18.02 Sessions 55–56.

---

## §1 — Motivation: When Cartesian Coordinates Fail

### 1.1 The Archetypal Hard Integral

$$ \iint_D e^{-(x^2+y^2)},dA, \quad D = {x^2 + y^2 \le R^2} $$

In Cartesian form:

$$ \int_{-R}^{R}\int_{-\sqrt{R^2-x^2}}^{\sqrt{R^2-x^2}} e^{-(x^2+y^2)},dy,dx $$

The inner integral $\int e^{-(x^2+y^2)},dy$ has no closed form in elementary functions. The limits involve square roots. This is a disaster.

In polar form, the same integral becomes $\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^R e^{-r^2},r,dr,d\theta$ — a product of two elementary integrals.

### 1.2 The Diagnostic Test

**Use polar coordinates when:**

- The integrand contains $x^2 + y^2$, $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$, $x^2 - y^2$, $\arctan(y/x)$, or $xy/(x^2+y^2)$
- The domain is a disk, annulus, sector, or wedge
- The domain boundary is described by $r = f(\theta)$

## §2 — The Polar Coordinate System

### 2.1 The Transformation

The polar coordinate map $\Phi: (r,\theta) \mapsto (x,y)$ is:

$$ x = r\cos\theta, \qquad y = r\sin\theta $$

with inverse (on $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {(0,0)}$):

$$ r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \qquad \theta = \arctan!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right) \quad \text{(with quadrant correction)} $$

**Convention.** We always take $r \ge 0$ and $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ (or $(-\pi, \pi]$ when convenient). Negative $r$ is defined by $(-r, \theta) = (r, \theta + \pi)$, but we never need it here.

### 2.2 Polar Grid Geometry

The two families of coordinate curves are:

- **Circles** $r = c$: centred at the origin
- **Rays** $\theta = \alpha$: emanating from the origin

```
θ = π/2
      │
      │
      │    r = 2
      │  ╭───╮
      │ ╭╯   ╰╮   r = 1
      │╭╯ ╭──╮╰╮
      ││  │  │  │
──────┼╯  │  │  ╰┼──── θ = 0
      │╰╮ ╰──╯ ╭╯│
      │ ╰╮   ╭╯  │
      │  ╰───╯   │
      │
θ = 3π/2
```

A "polar rectangle" $[r_1, r_2] \times [\theta_1, \theta_2]$ is a **curved wedge** — an annular sector.

## §3 — The Jacobian: The Central Theorem

This is the heart of the matter. The change-of-variables formula for double integrals says:

$$ \iint_D f(x,y),dA = \iint_{D^*} f(r\cos\theta,, r\sin\theta),|\det J_\Phi|,dr,d\theta $$

where $D^*$ is the pre-image of $D$ in the $(r,\theta)$-plane, and $J_\Phi$ is the **Jacobian matrix** of $\Phi$.

### 3.1 Computing the Jacobian

$$ J_\Phi = \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)} = \begin{pmatrix} \partial x/\partial r & \partial x/\partial\theta \ \partial y/\partial r & \partial y/\partial\theta \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix} $$

$$ \det J_\Phi = \cos\theta \cdot r\cos\theta - (-r\sin\theta)\cdot\sin\theta = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r $$

Since $r \ge 0$: $|\det J_\Phi| = r$.

### 3.2 The Main Formula

$$ \boxed{\iint_D f(x,y),dA = \iint_{D^*} f(r\cos\theta,, r\sin\theta);r;dr,d\theta} $$

**The $r$ is not optional.** It is the Jacobian determinant, encoding the geometric fact that a polar area element is not $dr,d\theta$ but $r,dr,d\theta$.

### 3.3 Geometric Proof of the $r$ Factor

Consider a small polar rectangle: $r \in [r, r+\Delta r]$, $\theta \in [\theta, \theta + \Delta\theta]$.

```
           ╭────╮  ← arc of radius r + Δr, length (r+Δr)Δθ
          ╱      ╲
    Δr   ╱  ΔA    ╲
        ╱    ≈ r   ╲
       ╰────╯        ← arc of radius r, length rΔθ
       ←  rΔθ  →
```

The area of this curved wedge is:

$$ \Delta A = \frac{1}{2}(r+\Delta r)^2\Delta\theta - \frac{1}{2}r^2\Delta\theta = r,\Delta r,\Delta\theta + \frac{1}{2}(\Delta r)^2\Delta\theta $$

In the limit $\Delta r, \Delta\theta \to 0$:

$$ dA = r,dr,d\theta $$

The $r$ factor is the **ratio of arc length to radius** — it compensates for the fact that arcs at larger $r$ subtend more area per unit angle than arcs near the origin.

## §4 — Standard Polar Domains

### 4.1 Type I Polar Region (Fixed $r$-bounds)

$$ D^* = {(\theta, r) : \alpha \le \theta \le \beta,; 0 \le r \le R} $$

**Examples:** Full disk ($\alpha=0, \beta=2\pi$), half-disk, sector.

$$ \iint_D f,dA = \int_\alpha^\beta \int_0^R f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta),r,dr,d\theta $$

### 4.2 Type II Polar Region ($r$-bounds depend on $\theta$)

$$ D^* = {(\theta, r) : \alpha \le \theta \le \beta,; r_1(\theta) \le r \le r_2(\theta)} $$

**Examples:** Regions bounded by polar curves like $r = 1 + \cos\theta$ (cardioid), $r = \cos(2\theta)$ (rose).

$$ \iint_D f,dA = \int_\alpha^\beta \int_{r_1(\theta)}^{r_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta),r,dr,d\theta $$

### 4.3 Common Polar Domains Illustrated

## §5 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Area of a Disk (Verification)

**Compute** $\text{Area}(D)$ where $D = {x^2 + y^2 \le R^2}$.

$$ \text{Area}(D) = \iint_D 1,dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^R r,dr,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}\frac{R^2}{2},d\theta = \pi R^2 \checkmark $$

---

### Example 2 — The Gaussian Integral (The Most Important Integral in Probability)

**Compute** $I = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2},dx$.

**The trick.** Consider $I^2$:

$$ I^2 = \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2},dx\right)!\left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-y^2},dy\right) = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} e^{-(x^2+y^2)},dA $$

The second equality uses separability (the domain is all of $\mathbb{R}^2$, a "rectangle" in the extended sense).

**Switch to polar.** Let $D_R = {r \le R}$. By exhaustion ($D_R \nearrow \mathbb{R}^2$ as $R \to \infty$):

$$ I^2 = \lim_{R\to\infty}\iint_{D_R} e^{-r^2},r,dr,d\theta = \lim_{R\to\infty}\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^R e^{-r^2},r,dr,d\theta $$

Inner integral: let $u = r^2$, $du = 2r,dr$:

$$ \int_0^R e^{-r^2},r,dr = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{R^2}e^{-u},du = \frac{1}{2}(1-e^{-R^2}) $$

Therefore:

$$ I^2 = \lim_{R\to\infty}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1}{2}(1-e^{-R^2}),d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\cdot 2\pi\cdot 1 = \pi $$

$$ \boxed{I = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2},dx = \sqrt{\pi}} $$

This is the **most elegant application** of polar integration. Every Gaussian in probability and deep learning traces back to this computation.

---

### Example 3 — Integrand with $x^2 + y^2$

**Compute** $\displaystyle\iint_D (x^2 + y^2)^{3/2},dA$ where $D = {1 \le x^2 + y^2 \le 4}$ (annulus).

In polar: $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$, so $(x^2+y^2)^{3/2} = r^3$. Domain: $r \in [1,2]$, $\theta \in [0,2\pi]$.

$$ = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_1^2 r^3 \cdot r,dr,d\theta = 2\pi\int_1^2 r^4,dr = 2\pi\cdot\frac{r^5}{5}\Bigg|_1^2 = 2\pi\cdot\frac{31}{5} = \frac{62\pi}{5} $$

---

### Example 4 — Sector Region

**Compute** $\displaystyle\iint_D xy,dA$ where $D$ is the region in the first quadrant between the circles $r=1$ and $r=3$.

$D$: $r \in [1,3]$, $\theta \in [0,\pi/2]$. In polar: $xy = r\cos\theta \cdot r\sin\theta = r^2\sin\theta\cos\theta = \frac{r^2}{2}\sin(2\theta)$.

$$ = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_1^3 \frac{r^2}{2}\sin(2\theta)\cdot r,dr,d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\left(\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin(2\theta),d\theta\right)!\left(\int_1^3 r^3,dr\right) $$

$$ = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\left[-\frac{\cos(2\theta)}{2}\right]_0^{\pi/2}\cdot\frac{r^4}{4}\Bigg|_1^3 = \frac{1}{2}\cdot 1 \cdot 20 = 10 $$

---

### Example 5 — Non-standard Limits: Region Described by a Polar Curve

**Compute** the area enclosed by one petal of the rose $r = \cos(2\theta)$.

One petal lies in $\theta \in [-\pi/4, \pi/4]$ (where $\cos(2\theta) \ge 0$).

$$ \text{Area} = \int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\int_0^{\cos(2\theta)} r,dr,d\theta = \int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{\cos^2(2\theta)}{2},d\theta $$

Using $\cos^2(u) = \frac{1+\cos(2u)}{2}$:

$$ = \frac{1}{2}\int_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4}\frac{1+\cos(4\theta)}{2},d\theta = \frac{1}{4}\left[\theta + \frac{\sin(4\theta)}{4}\right]_{-\pi/4}^{\pi/4} = \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{\pi}{8} $$

---

### Example 6 — Converting a Cartesian Integral to Polar

**Evaluate** $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} e^{x^2+y^2},dy,dx$.

**Identify the region.** This is the quarter-disk in the first quadrant: $x^2 + y^2 \le 1$, $x \ge 0$, $y \ge 0$.

**Polar translation:** $r \in [0,1]$, $\theta \in [0, \pi/2]$. Integrand: $e^{r^2}$.

$$ = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^1 e^{r^2},r,dr,d\theta = \frac{\pi}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2}\left[e^{r^2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{\pi}{4}(e-1) $$

## §6 — The Change of Variables Theorem (Full Generality)

The polar substitution is a special case of the general **Change of Variables** theorem. This section states the general theorem so that polar is properly contextualised.

**Theorem (Change of Variables).** Let $\Phi: D^* \to D$ be a $C^1$ bijection (except possibly on a set of measure zero), where $D^*, D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$. Then:

$$ \iint_D f(x,y),dA = \iint_{D^*} f(\Phi(u,v)),\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|,du,dv $$

where $\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right| = |\det J_\Phi|$ is the **absolute Jacobian determinant**.

**For polar coordinates:** $\Phi(r,\theta) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$, $|\det J_\Phi| = r$.

**Geometric meaning of the Jacobian.** $|\det J_\Phi|$ measures the **local area magnification** of $\Phi$: a small region near $(u,v)$ of area $\delta A$ in the $(u,v)$-plane maps to a region of area $\approx |\det J_\Phi|\cdot\delta A$ in the $(x,y)$-plane. The Jacobian is the correction factor ensuring areas are correctly accounted for.

```
(r,θ)-plane                    (x,y)-plane
                    Φ
  ┌──────┐   ─────────────→   ╭──────╮
  │ Δr Δθ│                   ╱  r·Δr·Δθ ╲
  └──────┘                  ╱            ╲
  area = ΔrΔθ               ╰────────────╯
                              area = r·ΔrΔθ

  magnification factor = r = |det J_Φ|
```

## §7 — Common Errors and Pathologies

### Error 1 — Forgetting the $r$ Factor

$$ \iint_D f,dA \ne \int_\alpha^\beta\int_{r_1}^{r_2} f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta),dr,d\theta $$

The correct formula **always** has $r,dr,d\theta$. Forgetting $r$ produces a systematically wrong answer with no warning sign.

**Mnemonic.** $dA = dx,dy = r,dr,d\theta$. The area element on the left is the same object expressed in two coordinate systems.

### Error 2 — Wrong $\theta$-Range for a Full Circle

A full circle requires $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$, not $[0, \pi]$. The interval $[0,\pi]$ gives a half-disk.

### Error 3 — Origin Included When $r_1 = 0$

When $r_1 = 0$, $\Phi$ is not injective at the origin (every $\theta$ maps to $(0,0)$). This is legal because ${r=0}$ has measure zero in the $(r,\theta)$-plane — the change-of-variables theorem allows failures on measure-zero sets.

### Error 4 — Applying Polar to a Non-Circular Domain

Polar coordinates are optimal for **circular** geometry. For ellipses $\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}\le 1$, use the **elliptic substitution** $x = ar\cos\theta$, $y = br\sin\theta$ (Jacobian: $abr$), not standard polar.

### Error 5 — Sign of $r$

Always $r \ge 0$ in our convention. If a polar curve formula gives $r < 0$ for some $\theta$, the curve traces a reflected point — handle by restricting $\theta$ or using $|r|$.

## §8 — Symmetry in Polar Coordinates

Symmetry is easier to exploit in polar form.

|Symmetry|Condition|Simplification|
|---|---|---|
|About $x$-axis|$f(r,-\theta) = f(r,\theta)$ and $D$ symmetric|$\int_0^{2\pi} \to 2\int_0^\pi$|
|About $y$-axis|$f(r,\pi-\theta) = f(r,\theta)$ and $D$ symmetric|$\int_0^{2\pi} \to 2\int_0^\pi$|
|Full rotational|$f$ depends only on $r$|$\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta = 2\pi$ factors out|
|$n$-fold rotational|$f(r,\theta+2\pi/n) = f(r,\theta)$|$\int_0^{2\pi} = n\int_0^{2\pi/n}$|

**Key example.** If $f(x,y) = g(r)$ depends only on $r$ (radially symmetric):

$$ \iint_D g(r),dA = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^R g(r),r,dr,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^R g(r),r,dr $$

## §9 — The Polar Coordinate Machine: Step-by-Step Protocol

```
GIVEN: ∫∫_D f(x,y) dA

STEP 1.  Does the domain D have circular/radial structure?
         └── No → stay Cartesian (or use other substitution)
         └── Yes → proceed

STEP 2.  Describe D in polar:
         Find α, β (θ-range) and r₁(θ), r₂(θ) (r-bounds at each θ).
         Draw the region. Label all boundary curves in polar form.

STEP 3.  Substitute:
         x = r cosθ,  y = r sinθ
         Replace all occurrences of x²+y² with r².

STEP 4.  Write the integral:
         ∫_α^β ∫_{r₁(θ)}^{r₂(θ)}  f(rcosθ, rsinθ) · r  dr dθ
                                                        ↑
                                               NEVER FORGET THIS

STEP 5.  Evaluate inner integral (in r), then outer (in θ).

STEP 6.  Check dimensional sanity: Area integrals should give [length]².
```

## §10 — Connection to Probability and Machine Learning

### 10.1 The Gaussian Distribution — Its Very Existence Depends on This

The univariate normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ has density $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}$. The normalisation constant $\sqrt{2\pi}$ comes from:

$$ \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2},dx = \sqrt{2\pi} $$

**Proof:** Substitute $x = t/\sqrt{2}$ to get $\sqrt{2}\int e^{-t^2/2},dt$... which reduces to our Gaussian integral. So the very fact that the standard normal density _integrates to 1_ is a consequence of polar coordinates applied to a double integral. Without this calculation, the Gaussian distribution is not well-defined.

### 10.2 Multivariate Gaussian

For $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^2$ with covariance $\Sigma = \sigma^2 I$:

$$ p(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}e^{-|\mathbf{x}|^2/(2\sigma^2)} $$

Normalisation check:

$$ \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} p(x,y),dA = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2/(2\sigma^2)},r,dr,d\theta = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma^2}\cdot 2\pi\cdot\sigma^2 = 1 $$

This is exactly the polar double integral computation, and it must equal 1 for $p$ to be a valid density.

### 10.3 Reparameterisation Trick in VAEs (Radial Decomposition)

In **Variational Autoencoders**, the latent space prior is $\mathcal{N}(0, I)$. Sampling from this prior and computing expectations uses the identity:

$$ \mathbb{E}_{\mathbf{z}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}[f(\mathbf{z})] = \iint f(x,y),\frac{e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}}{2\pi},dA = \int_0^\infty\int_0^{2\pi} f(r,\theta),\frac{e^{-r^2/2}}{2\pi},r,d\theta,dr $$

Decomposing into radial and angular components (polar coordinates) underlies the **radial basis function** interpretation of the latent space and the geometry of the latent manifold.

### 10.4 Attention Mechanisms — Radial Decay

In some **spatial attention** architectures (e.g., for point clouds or 2D feature maps), attention weights decay radially:

$$ \alpha(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}) = \exp!\left(-\frac{|\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{k}|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) $$

Computing the total attention mass over a spatial region $D$ is:

$$ \int_D \alpha(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}),dA = \iint_D e^{-r^2/(2\sigma^2)},r,dr,d\theta $$

(centring at $\mathbf{q}$). This is a polar integral, and its closed form informs how to set $\sigma$ for a desired **effective receptive field size**.

### 10.5 Score-Based Generative Models (Diffusion)

The **score function** $\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p(\mathbf{x})$ for a radially symmetric density $p(\mathbf{x}) \propto e^{-V(r)}$ is:

$$ \nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p = -V'(r),\hat{r} = -V'(r),\frac{\mathbf{x}}{r} $$

Computing normalisation constants and partition functions for such densities requires polar integration. Diffusion model theory (DDPM, Score SDE) extensively uses radially-symmetric reference distributions whose analysis is pure polar calculus.

## §11 — Extended Example: Normalising an Unnormalised Density

**Problem.** The function $f(x,y) = C e^{-(x^2+y^2)}(1 + x^2 + y^2)$ is a probability density on $\mathbb{R}^2$. Find $C$.

**We need** $\iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} f,dA = 1$, so $C^{-1} = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} e^{-(x^2+y^2)}(1+x^2+y^2),dA$.

**Polar form.** $r \in [0,\infty)$, $\theta \in [0,2\pi]$, integrand $= e^{-r^2}(1+r^2)$:

$$ C^{-1} = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2}(1+r^2),r,dr,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^\infty (re^{-r^2} + r^3 e^{-r^2}),dr $$

Compute each piece using $\int_0^\infty r^{2k+1}e^{-r^2},dr = \frac{k!}{2}$:

- $\int_0^\infty re^{-r^2},dr = \frac{0!}{2} = \frac{1}{2}$
- $\int_0^\infty r^3 e^{-r^2},dr = \frac{1!}{2} = \frac{1}{2}$

$$ C^{-1} = 2\pi\left(\frac{1}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\right) = 2\pi \implies \boxed{C = \frac{1}{2\pi}} $$

This is a non-trivial unnormalised density that arises in practice (it is related to the Chi-squared distribution), and polar coordinates make the normalisation tractable.

## §12 — Connections to Other Coordinate Systems

Polar coordinates in 2D are the first member of a family:

|Dimension|Coordinates|Jacobian|Area/Volume Element|
|---|---|---|---|
|2D|Polar $(r, \theta)$|$r$|$r,dr,d\theta$|
|3D (cylinder)|Cylindrical $(r,\theta,z)$|$r$|$r,dr,d\theta,dz$|
|3D (sphere)|Spherical $(\rho,\phi,\theta)$|$\rho^2\sin\phi$|$\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$|
|General|$(u_1,\ldots,u_n)$|$|\det J|

The Jacobian $r$ (polar) generalises to $r$ (cylindrical) and $\rho^2\sin\phi$ (spherical). In all cases, it is the determinant of the coordinate transformation's Jacobian matrix.

## §13 — Summary Theorem Sheet

**Main Formula:**

$$ \iint_D f(x,y),dA = \int_\alpha^\beta\int_{r_1(\theta)}^{r_2(\theta)} f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta);r;dr,d\theta $$

**Key substitutions:**

$$ x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta, \quad x^2+y^2 = r^2, \quad dA = r,dr,d\theta $$

**Jacobian:** $\det\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(r,\theta)} = r$

**Gaussian integral (consequence):** $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2},dx = \sqrt{\pi}$

**Radially symmetric integrand:** $\displaystyle\iint_D g(r),dA = 2\pi\int_0^R g(r),r,dr$ (for full disk of radius $R$)
