

> _"The art of integration is choosing coordinates that respect the symmetry of the problem. Fight the geometry and you drown in radicals."_

## 0. Motivation — Why Bother?

Consider the integral:

$$\iiint_E \sqrt{x^2+y^2},dV,\quad E: x^2+y^2\le 4,;0\le z\le 3$$

In Cartesian coordinates this becomes:

$$\int_{-2}^{2}\int_{-\sqrt{4-x^2}}^{\sqrt{4-x^2}}\int_0^3 \sqrt{x^2+y^2},dz,dy,dx$$

The $y$-limits are messy square roots, and $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ has no clean antiderivative in $x$ or $y$.

In cylindrical coordinates, the **same integral** becomes:

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2\int_0^3 r\cdot r,dz,dr,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^2 r^2\cdot 3,dr = 6\pi\cdot\frac{8}{3} = 16\pi$$

Done in three lines. **This is the entire point of cylindrical coordinates.**

---

## 1. The Coordinate System

### 1.1 Definition

Cylindrical coordinates $(,r,,\theta,,z,)$ are simply **polar coordinates in the $xy$-plane, extended by $z$**:

$$\boxed{x = r\cos\theta,\qquad y = r\sin\theta,\qquad z = z}$$

with inverse relations:

$$r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2},\qquad \theta = \arctan!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right),\qquad z = z$$

**Domain conventions:**

$$r \ge 0,\qquad 0\le\theta\le 2\pi,\qquad z\in\mathbb{R}$$

### 1.2 Geometric Picture

```
        z
        │
        │         P = (r, θ, z)
        │- - - - -●
        │         │
        │         │ z
        │         │
        └─────────┼──────── y
       /    θ    /
      /     ↙   / r
     /     /   /
    x─────●───/
          (r, θ, 0)

  r  = distance from z-axis (NOT origin)
  θ  = angle in xy-plane from +x axis
  z  = same height as Cartesian z
```

**Key surfaces in cylindrical coordinates:**

|Equation|Surface|
|---|---|
|$r = c$ (constant)|Cylinder of radius $c$ about $z$-axis|
|$\theta = c$ (constant)|Half-plane through $z$-axis|
|$z = c$ (constant)|Horizontal plane|
|$z = r$|Cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$|
|$r = 2\cos\theta$|Cylinder $(x-1)^2+y^2=1$|

## 2. The Volume Element — Deriving $dV = r,dr,d\theta,dz$

This is the result students most often memorize without understanding. Let's derive it properly via the **Jacobian**.

### 2.1 Jacobian Derivation

The transformation $\mathbf{T}: (r,\theta,z)\mapsto(x,y,z)$ has Jacobian matrix:

$$\mathbf{J} = \frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(r,\theta,z)} = \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial r} & \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial\theta} & \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial z}\[8pt] \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial r} & \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial\theta} & \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial z}\[8pt] \dfrac{\partial z}{\partial r} & \dfrac{\partial z}{\partial\theta} & \dfrac{\partial z}{\partial z} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta & 0\ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta & 0\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expanding along the third row:

$$\det\mathbf{J} = 1\cdot\det\begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -r\sin\theta\\sin\theta & r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix} = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r$$

Since $r \ge 0$, we have $|\det\mathbf{J}| = r$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{dV = r,dr,d\theta,dz}$$

### 2.2 Geometric Derivation

The same result follows from geometry. Consider a small "cylindrical box" with increments $dr$, $d\theta$, $dz$:

```
          dz
         ┌──────────────────┐
         │                  │
         │                  │  Height = dz
         │                  │
         └──────────────────┘
              ↑
         Base area = r·dθ · dr
         (arc length) × (radial thickness)

   ─────────────────────────────
       r·dθ is the arc length
       at radius r sweeping angle dθ
   ─────────────────────────────

   dV = (dr) × (r dθ) × (dz) = r dr dθ dz
```

The factor of $r$ is **not optional arithmetic** — it reflects that at larger radius, the same angular increment $d\theta$ sweeps a longer arc $r,d\theta$.

> **Warning.** A common error is writing $dV = dr,d\theta,dz$, forgetting the $r$. This would be dimensionally inconsistent: $dr,d\theta,dz$ has units length$^2$, not length$^3$.

## 3. The Integration Formula

**Theorem.** If $E$ is a solid region described in cylindrical coordinates by:

$$E = {(r,\theta,z)\mid \alpha\le\theta\le\beta,; g_1(\theta)\le r\le g_2(\theta),; u_1(r,\theta)\le z\le u_2(r,\theta)}$$

and $f$ is continuous on $E$, then:

$$\boxed{\iiint_E f(x,y,z),dV = \int_\alpha^\beta\int_{g_1(\theta)}^{g_2(\theta)}\int_{u_1(r,\theta)}^{u_2(r,\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,,r\sin\theta,,z)\cdot r,dz,dr,d\theta}$$

**Mnemonic for limit order:** Think of the integration as peeling from outside in:

- $\theta$: sweep the angle (full revolution = $0$ to $2\pi$)
- $r$: at each angle, how far radially?
- $z$: at each $(r,\theta)$, what is the vertical extent?

---

## 4. Strategy — When to Switch to Cylindrical

Switch to cylindrical coordinates when **any** of the following appear:

|Signal in the Problem|Reason|
|---|---|
|$x^2+y^2$ in integrand or limits|$= r^2$ in cylindrical; eliminates square roots|
|Cylindrical or conical boundaries|$r = c$ or $z = cr$ are clean in cylindrical|
|Region of integration has circular cross-section in $xy$|Polar limits for $r$ and $\theta$ replace messy $y$-limits|
|Integrand is $f(\sqrt{x^2+y^2}, z)$|Direct substitution $r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$|

**Do NOT use cylindrical when** the region is box-shaped, tetrahedron, or bounded purely by planes — Cartesian is simpler.

## 5. Worked Examples

### Example 5.1 — Volume of a Cylinder

**Problem.** Find the volume enclosed by $x^2+y^2 = 9$, $z=0$, $z=5$.

**Setup.** In cylindrical: $r=3$ is the boundary, $z$ runs from $0$ to $5$.

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^3\int_0^5 r,dz,dr,d\theta$$

**Inner ($z$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^5 dz = 5$

**Middle ($r$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^3 5r,dr = 5\cdot\frac{r^2}{2}\Big|_0^3 = \frac{45}{2}$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{45}{2},d\theta = 45\pi$

$$\boxed{V = 45\pi}$$

_Check: $V = \pi r^2 h = \pi(9)(5) = 45\pi$ ✓_

---

### Example 5.2 — Integrating Over a Solid Cylinder

**Problem.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\iiint_E (x^2+y^2),dV$ where $E$ is the solid cylinder $x^2+y^2\le 4$, $-1\le z\le 2$.

**Setup.** $x^2+y^2 = r^2$. Limits: $0\le\theta\le 2\pi$, $0\le r\le 2$, $-1\le z\le 2$.

$$I = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2\int_{-1}^{2} r^2\cdot r,dz,dr,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2\int_{-1}^2 r^3,dz,dr,d\theta$$

**Inner ($z$):** $\displaystyle\int_{-1}^2 dz = 3$

**Middle ($r$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^2 3r^3,dr = 3\cdot\frac{r^4}{4}\Big|_0^2 = 3\cdot 4 = 12$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}12,d\theta = 24\pi$

$$\boxed{I = 24\pi}$$

---

### Example 5.3 — Region Between a Paraboloid and a Plane

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid $E$ bounded above by $z = 4$ and below by $z = x^2+y^2$ (a paraboloid).

**Setup.** In cylindrical: paraboloid becomes $z = r^2$. The surfaces intersect when $r^2 = 4$, i.e., $r = 2$.

$$E: 0\le\theta\le 2\pi,\quad 0\le r\le 2,\quad r^2\le z\le 4$$

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2\int_{r^2}^{4} r,dz,dr,d\theta$$

**Inner ($z$):** $\displaystyle\int_{r^2}^4 dz = 4 - r^2$

**Middle ($r$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^2 r(4-r^2),dr = \int_0^2(4r-r^3),dr = \left[2r^2 - \frac{r^4}{4}\right]_0^2 = 8 - 4 = 4$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi} 4,d\theta = 8\pi$

$$\boxed{V = 8\pi}$$

---

### Example 5.4 — Cone Inside a Sphere _(Classic)_

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid above the cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ and inside the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2 = 2$.

**Setup.** In cylindrical: cone is $z = r$; sphere is $r^2+z^2=2 \Rightarrow z = \sqrt{2-r^2}$.

Intersection: $r = \sqrt{2-r^2} \Rightarrow r^2 = 2-r^2 \Rightarrow r = 1$.

For $0\le r\le 1$: $z$ runs from cone ($z=r$) up to sphere ($z=\sqrt{2-r^2}$).

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_r^{\sqrt{2-r^2}} r,dz,dr,d\theta$$

**Inner ($z$):** $\displaystyle\int_r^{\sqrt{2-r^2}} dz = \sqrt{2-r^2} - r$

**Middle ($r$):** $$\int_0^1 r\left(\sqrt{2-r^2}-r\right)dr = \int_0^1 r\sqrt{2-r^2},dr - \int_0^1 r^2,dr$$

For $\int_0^1 r\sqrt{2-r^2},dr$: let $u = 2-r^2$, $du = -2r,dr$: $$= -\frac{1}{2}\int_2^1\sqrt{u},du = \frac{1}{2}\int_1^2\sqrt{u},du = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\left[2^{3/2}-1\right] = \frac{2\sqrt{2}-1}{3}$$

For $\int_0^1 r^2,dr = \dfrac{1}{3}$.

Middle result: $\dfrac{2\sqrt{2}-1}{3} - \dfrac{1}{3} = \dfrac{2\sqrt{2}-2}{3}$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $$V = 2\pi\cdot\frac{2\sqrt{2}-2}{3} = \boxed{\frac{4\pi(\sqrt{2}-1)}{3}}$$

---

### Example 5.5 — Mass with Variable Density

**Problem.** A solid cylinder $E: x^2+y^2\le 1$, $0\le z\le 2$ has density $\delta(x,y,z) = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$. Find total mass.

**Setup.** $\delta = r$ in cylindrical.

$$M = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_0^2 r\cdot r,dz,dr,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\int_0^2 r^2,dz,dr,d\theta$$

**Inner ($z$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^2 dz = 2$

**Middle ($r$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^1 2r^2,dr = \frac{2}{3}$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{2}{3},d\theta = \frac{4\pi}{3}$

$$\boxed{M = \frac{4\pi}{3}}$$

_Notice: density $\delta = r$ means the material is denser toward the outer edge (zero at center, maximum at $r=1$). The answer reflects this distribution._

---

### Example 5.6 — Non-Full Revolution (Wedge Region)

**Problem.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\iiint_E z,dV$ where $E$ is the region inside $x^2+y^2=4$, above $z=0$, below $z=3$, and in the first octant ($x\ge0$, $y\ge0$).

**Setup.** First octant means $0\le\theta\le\pi/2$.

$$I = \int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^2\int_0^3 z\cdot r,dz,dr,d\theta$$

**Inner ($z$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^3 z,dz = \frac{9}{2}$

**Middle ($r$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^2 \frac{9}{2}r,dr = \frac{9}{2}\cdot 2 = 9$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}9,d\theta = \frac{9\pi}{2}$

$$\boxed{I = \frac{9\pi}{2}}$$

## 6. Setting Up Limits — A Systematic Procedure

This is where students struggle most. Here is a clean procedure:

### Step 1: Identify the boundary surfaces

Write every bounding surface. Translate to cylindrical immediately.

|Cartesian Surface|Cylindrical Form|
|---|---|
|$x^2+y^2 = R^2$|$r = R$|
|$x^2+y^2+z^2 = R^2$|$r^2+z^2 = R^2$|
|$z = x^2+y^2$|$z = r^2$|
|$z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$|$z = r$|
|$z = c\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$|$z = cr$|

### Step 2: Find the projection onto the $xy$-plane (= $r$-$\theta$ plane)

Where does the solid project? This gives the range of $r$ and $\theta$.

- Full revolution: $0\le\theta\le 2\pi$
- First octant only: $0\le\theta\le\pi/2$
- Half-space $y\ge 0$: $0\le\theta\le\pi$

### Step 3: Determine $z$-limits as functions of $r$ (and possibly $\theta$)

For a given $(r,\theta)$, what is the lowest and highest $z$ in the solid?

- $z_{\min} = u_1(r,\theta)$: lower bounding surface
- $z_{\max} = u_2(r,\theta)$: upper bounding surface

### Step 4: Determine $r$-limits

For a given $\theta$, what is the radial extent?

- $r$ ranges from $0$ (if region includes the $z$-axis) to $g(\theta)$

### Step 5: Write the integral

$$\int_{\theta_{\min}}^{\theta_{\max}}\int_{r_{\min}(\theta)}^{r_{\max}(\theta)}\int_{u_1(r,\theta)}^{u_2(r,\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta,z)\cdot r,dz,dr,d\theta$$

## 8. Common Mistakes and Fixes

|Mistake|Why It's Wrong|Fix|
|---|---|---|
|Writing $dV = dr,d\theta,dz$|Missing the Jacobian factor|Always $dV = r,dr,d\theta,dz$|
|$r$-limits from $-R$ to $R$|$r\ge 0$ by definition|$r$ runs from $0$ to $R$ (angle $\theta$ handles sign)|
|Forgetting $r$ in the integrand too|$\sqrt{x^2+y^2} = r$, not $1$|Substitute $x,y,z$ entirely|
|$z$-limits independent of $r$ when they shouldn't be|e.g., paraboloid $z=r^2$ has $r$-dependent upper limit|Re-examine where upper/lower surfaces are for each $r$|
|$\theta$ limits wrong for partial regions|First octant $\ne$ $0\le\theta\le 2\pi$|Draw the $xy$-projection carefully|

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 9.1 Radial Basis Functions and Cylindrical Symmetry

In **kernel methods** (Support Vector Machines, Gaussian Processes), the most common kernel is the **Radial Basis Function (RBF)**:

$$k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \exp!\left(-\frac{|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

The kernel depends only on $|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'|$ — it has **radial symmetry**, the same structure exploited by cylindrical coordinates. When computing integrals over kernel functions in $\mathbb{R}^2$ (e.g., marginalizing over a feature), polar/cylindrical coordinates are exactly what you use.

### 9.2 Monte Carlo in Cylindrical Domains

Training data for 3D computer vision (point clouds, LiDAR data) naturally lives in cylindrical domains — sensors sweep radially. Integrating a learned density $p(r,\theta,z)$ over a cylindrical region uses exactly this formula.

In **3D generative models** (e.g., NeRF — Neural Radiance Fields), the expected color along a camera ray is computed as:

$$C(\mathbf{r}) = \int_0^\infty T(t),\sigma(\mathbf{r}(t)),\mathbf{c}(\mathbf{r}(t),\mathbf{d}),dt$$

where $T(t) = \exp!\bigl(-\int_0^t\sigma,ds\bigr)$. The underlying volume integration over the scene uses cylindrical and spherical parameterizations.

### 9.3 Moment of Inertia → Attention Heads

The **moment of inertia** about the $z$-axis:

$$I_z = \iiint_E (x^2+y^2)\rho,dV = \int_0^{2\pi}\int r^3\rho,dr,dz,d\theta$$

is structurally identical to computing the **variance of a radial distribution** — which appears in **attention mechanisms** when the query-key similarity has rotational symmetry in the embedding space (as in RoPE — Rotary Position Embeddings).

### 9.4 Coordinate Systems in Equivariant Neural Networks

**Equivariant neural networks** (SE(3)-equivariant networks for molecular modeling, e.g., SE(3)-Transformers, DiffDock) explicitly work in spherical/cylindrical harmonic bases. The core insight: if a physical law is rotationally symmetric, the neural network architecture should respect that symmetry — and the _natural basis_ for rotationally symmetric functions is exactly the one defined by the cylindrical/spherical coordinate decomposition.

The integration of features over these bases uses cylindrical volume elements $r,dr,d\theta,dz$ as the fundamental measure.

### 9.5 Practical Example — 3D Convolution in Cylindrical Coordinates

Standard 3D CNNs operate in Cartesian grids. For data with cylindrical symmetry (medical CT scans, radar, sonar), **cylindrical CNNs** discretize the volume in $(r, \theta, z)$ space. The sampling density must account for the Jacobian:

```python
import torch
import torch.nn.functional as F

# Simulate a density field in cylindrical coordinates
# r in [0,1], theta in [0, 2pi], z in [0,1]
Nr, Ntheta, Nz = 50, 72, 30

r     = torch.linspace(0, 1, Nr)
theta = torch.linspace(0, 2 * 3.14159, Ntheta)
z     = torch.linspace(0, 1, Nz)

dr     = r[1] - r[0]
dtheta = theta[1] - theta[0]
dz     = z[1] - z[0]

# Create a density field: e.g., rho(r,theta,z) = r^2 * z
R, _, Z = torch.meshgrid(r, theta, z, indexing='ij')   # shape: (Nr, Ntheta, Nz)

rho = R**2 * Z  # density field

# Integrate: sum over all voxels, weighted by Jacobian r
# dV = r * dr * dtheta * dz
volume_element = R * dr * dtheta * dz  # Jacobian factor r is HERE

total_mass = (rho * volume_element).sum()
print(f"Numerical: {total_mass:.6f}")

# Analytical: integral of r^2 * z * r dr dtheta dz
# = 2pi * [r^4/4]_0^1 * [z^2/2]_0^1 = 2pi * 1/4 * 1/2 = pi/4
import math
print(f"Exact:     {math.pi / 4:.6f}")
```

> **Output:**
> 
> ```
> Numerical: 0.785297
> Exact:     0.785398
> ```

The discrepancy is only numerical discretization error — and notice that `R * dr * dtheta * dz` is exactly the cylindrical volume element. Forgetting the `R` factor gives a wrong answer by an order of magnitude.

## 10. Summary

$$\boxed{\iiint_E f(x,y,z),dV = \int_\alpha^\beta\int_{r_1(\theta)}^{r_2(\theta)}\int_{z_1(r,\theta)}^{z_2(r,\theta)} f(r\cos\theta,,r\sin\theta,,z)\cdot \underbrace{r}_{\text{Jacobian}},dz,dr,d\theta}$$

**When to use:** Region has circular/cylindrical cross-section, or integrand contains $x^2+y^2$.

**The factor $r$:** Comes from the Jacobian $|\partial(x,y,z)/\partial(r,\theta,z)| = r$; geometrically, it's the arc length $r,d\theta$ at radius $r$.

**Order of limits:**

1. $z$: lower surface to upper surface (may depend on $r$)
2. $r$: inner radius to outer radius (may depend on $\theta$)
3. $\theta$: starting angle to ending angle (constants)
