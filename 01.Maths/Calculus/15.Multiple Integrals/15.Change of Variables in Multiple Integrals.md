

> _"A good coordinate system is half the solution. The Jacobian is the price you pay for the privilege of working in comfortable coordinates."_

---

## 0. The Core Problem

Suppose you need to evaluate:

$$\iint_R e^{\frac{x-y}{x+y}},dA$$

where $R$ is the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(0,1)$.

In Cartesian coordinates this is a nightmare — the exponent is a rational function of $x$ and $y$, and the triangular region produces $y$-limits that depend on $x$. There is no clean antiderivative.

**But watch what happens** if we introduce $u = x-y$, $v = x+y$. Then:

$$e^{\frac{x-y}{x+y}} = e^{u/v}$$

The triangular region in $xy$ maps to a triangular region in $uv$ with simple, constant limits. The integral collapses.

This is the essence of change of variables: **trade a hard region/integrand pair for an easy one**, paying a precise price (the Jacobian) to keep the integral equal.

---

## 1. Review — The 1D Case

Before generalizing, recall the substitution rule from single-variable calculus:

$$\int_a^b f(x),dx = \int_c^d f(g(u)),g'(u),du$$

where $x = g(u)$, $g(c)=a$, $g(d)=b$.

The factor $g'(u) = dx/du$ accounts for how the transformation _stretches or compresses_ the interval. If $g'(u) > 1$, a small $du$ corresponds to a larger $dx$; the integral must be scaled accordingly.

The **absolute value** $|g'(u)|$ is what matters for area/volume — not the sign. The sign of $g'$ determines orientation, not magnitude.

**The Jacobian in higher dimensions is the exact generalization of $|g'(u)|$.**

---

## 2. The 2D Change of Variables Formula

### 2.1 Setup

Let $T: (u,v) \mapsto (x,y)$ be a transformation defined by:

$$x = x(u,v), \qquad y = y(u,v)$$

Suppose:

- $T$ is a $C^1$ (continuously differentiable) bijection from region $S$ in $uv$-space onto region $R$ in $xy$-space
- $T$ is one-to-one on the interior of $S$ (it may fail on the boundary)

### 2.2 The Jacobian

**Definition.** The **Jacobian determinant** of the transformation $T$ is:

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial x}{\partial v}\[10pt] \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \dfrac{\partial y}{\partial v} \end{vmatrix} = \frac{\partial x}{\partial u}\frac{\partial y}{\partial v} - \frac{\partial x}{\partial v}\frac{\partial y}{\partial u}$$

### 2.3 The Change of Variables Theorem

**Theorem.** Under the conditions above:

$$\boxed{\iint_R f(x,y),dA = \iint_S f(x(u,v),,y(u,v))\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|du,dv}$$

> **What the Jacobian does.** The area element $dA = dx,dy$ in $xy$-space corresponds to $\left|\dfrac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|du,dv$ in $uv$-space. The Jacobian measures the **local area magnification factor** of the transformation at each point.

---

## 3. Geometric Meaning of the Jacobian

### 3.1 Linear Approximation

Near a point $(u_0,v_0)$, the transformation $T$ is approximately linear:

$$\begin{pmatrix}x\y\end{pmatrix} \approx \begin{pmatrix}x_0\y_0\end{pmatrix} + \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix}\partial x/\partial u & \partial x/\partial v\\partial y/\partial u & \partial y/\partial v\end{pmatrix}}_{\mathbf{J}}\begin{pmatrix}u-u_0\v-v_0\end{pmatrix}$$

A small rectangle $[u_0, u_0+du]\times[v_0,v_0+dv]$ in $S$ maps (approximately) to a **parallelogram** in $R$ spanned by the columns of $\mathbf{J}$:

$$\mathbf{a} = \begin{pmatrix}\partial x/\partial u\\partial y/\partial u\end{pmatrix}du,\qquad \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix}\partial x/\partial v\\partial y/\partial v\end{pmatrix}dv$$

The area of this parallelogram is $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}| = |\det\mathbf{J}|,du,dv$.

### 3.2 Visualization

```
uv-space                           xy-space
                                   
  v                                y
  │                                │
  │  ┌──────┐                      │    ╱──────╲
  │  │      │  ──── T ────>        │   ╱        ╲
  │  │  S   │  du×dv               │  ╱    R     ╲
  │  └──────┘                      │ ╱             ╲
  │   du  dv                       │╱ |det J|·du·dv ╲
  └──────────── u                  └────────────────── x

  Rectangle of area du·dv   →   Parallelogram of area |det J|·du·dv

  If |det J| > 1: transformation EXPANDS area locally
  If |det J| < 1: transformation COMPRESSES area locally
  If |det J| = 0: transformation is DEGENERATE (collapses dimension)
```

### 3.3 Why the Absolute Value?

The determinant $\det\mathbf{J}$ can be negative — this means $T$ **reverses orientation** (like a reflection). For computing areas or integrals of positive quantities, orientation doesn't matter; only the magnitude of the area scaling does. Hence $|\det\mathbf{J}|$.

---

## 4. The Inverse Jacobian

A useful identity: if $T: (u,v)\mapsto(x,y)$ has Jacobian $\partial(x,y)/\partial(u,v)$, then the inverse transformation $T^{-1}: (x,y)\mapsto(u,v)$ satisfies:

$$\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} = \left(\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right)^{-1}$$

**Proof.** By the chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\cdot\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} = \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(x,y)} = \det(I) = 1$$

> **Practical use.** Sometimes it's easier to define $T^{-1}$ (i.e., express $u,v$ in terms of $x,y$) and compute $\partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)$, then take its reciprocal to get $\partial(x,y)/\partial(u,v)$.

---

## 5. The 3D Change of Variables Formula

The extension to three dimensions is direct. Let $T: (u,v,w)\mapsto(x,y,z)$.

**Jacobian:**

$$\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(u,v,w)} = \begin{vmatrix} \partial x/\partial u & \partial x/\partial v & \partial x/\partial w\ \partial y/\partial u & \partial y/\partial v & \partial y/\partial w\ \partial z/\partial u & \partial z/\partial v & \partial z/\partial w \end{vmatrix}$$

**Formula:**

$$\boxed{\iiint_E f(x,y,z),dV = \iiint_{E^*} f(T(u,v,w))\left|\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(u,v,w)}\right|du,dv,dw}$$

The Jacobian now measures the **local volume magnification factor** of the transformation.

## 6. Standard Coordinate Systems as Change of Variables

Every special coordinate system is a change of variables with a known Jacobian. Let's re-derive them.

### 6.1 Polar Coordinates ($\mathbb{R}^2$)

$$x = r\cos\theta,\qquad y = r\sin\theta$$

$$\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -r\sin\theta\\sin\theta & r\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det\mathbf{J} = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r$$

$$dA = r,dr,d\theta$$

### 6.2 Cylindrical Coordinates ($\mathbb{R}^3$)

$$x = r\cos\theta,\qquad y = r\sin\theta,\qquad z = z$$

$$\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta & -r\sin\theta & 0\\sin\theta & r\cos\theta & 0\0 & 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det\mathbf{J} = r\quad(\text{same as polar, z-row is trivial})$$

$$dV = r,dr,d\theta,dz$$

### 6.3 Spherical Coordinates ($\mathbb{R}^3$)

$$x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\qquad y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\qquad z = \rho\cos\phi$$

$$\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} \sin\phi\cos\theta & \rho\cos\phi\cos\theta & -\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\ \sin\phi\sin\theta & \rho\cos\phi\sin\theta & \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\ \cos\phi & -\rho\sin\phi & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expanding along the third row:

$$\det\mathbf{J} = \rho^2\sin\phi$$

$$dV = \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$$

> **Pattern.** All three standard coordinate Jacobians are non-negative (for $r\ge0$, $0\le\phi\le\pi$), so the absolute value is automatic.

## 7. Worked Examples

### Example 7.1 — Linear Transformation (Parallelogram Region)

**Problem.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_R (x+y),dA$ where $R$ is the parallelogram with vertices $(0,0)$, $(2,1)$, $(3,3)$, $(1,2)$.

**Step 1: Identify the transformation.** The sides of the parallelogram are parallel to $\mathbf{a} = (2,1)$ and $\mathbf{b} = (1,2)$. Set:

$$x = 2u + v,\qquad y = u + 2v,\qquad (u,v)\in[0,1]\times[0,1]$$

Verify corners: $(u,v) = (0,0)\to(0,0)$; $(1,0)\to(2,1)$; $(0,1)\to(1,2)$; $(1,1)\to(3,3)$. ✓

**Step 2: Jacobian.**

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix}2 & 1\1 & 2\end{vmatrix} = 4 - 1 = 3$$

**Step 3: Transform integrand.** $x + y = (2u+v) + (u+2v) = 3u + 3v$

**Step 4: Integrate.**

$$\iint_R(x+y),dA = \int_0^1\int_0^1(3u+3v)\cdot 3,du,dv = 9\int_0^1\int_0^1(u+v),du,dv$$

$$= 9\int_0^1\left[\frac{1}{2}+v\right]dv = 9\left[\frac{v}{2}+\frac{v^2}{2}\right]_0^1 = 9\cdot 1 = \boxed{9}$$

---

### Example 7.2 — The Opening Problem

**Problem.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_R e^{\frac{x-y}{x+y}},dA$ where $R$ is the triangle with vertices $(0,0)$, $(1,0)$, $(0,1)$.

**Step 1: Choose substitution.** Let $u = x - y$, $v = x + y$.

**Step 2: Find the inverse Jacobian** (easier to compute $\partial(u,v)/\partial(x,y)$ first):

$$\frac{\partial(u,v)}{\partial(x,y)} = \begin{vmatrix}1 & -1\1 & 1\end{vmatrix} = 1+1 = 2$$

Therefore:

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \frac{1}{2}$$

**Step 3: Map the region.** In terms of $x,y$:

- Edge $y=0$, $0\le x\le 1$: $u = x$, $v = x \Rightarrow u = v$
- Edge $x=0$, $0\le y\le 1$: $u = -y$, $v = y \Rightarrow u = -v$
- Edge $x+y=1$: $v = 1$ (constant!)

So $S$ in $uv$-space: bounded by $u=v$, $u=-v$, $v=1$, i.e., $0\le v\le 1$, $-v\le u\le v$.

**Step 4: Integrate.**

$$\iint_R e^{u/v}\cdot\frac{1}{2},du,dv = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^1\int_{-v}^v e^{u/v},du,dv$$

**Inner ($u$):**

$$\int_{-v}^v e^{u/v},du = v\cdot e^{u/v}\Big|_{-v}^v = v(e^1 - e^{-1}) = v(e-e^{-1})$$

**Outer ($v$):**

$$\frac{1}{2}\int_0^1 v(e-e^{-1}),dv = \frac{e-e^{-1}}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \boxed{\frac{e-e^{-1}}{4}}$$

---

### Example 7.3 — Elliptic Region

**Problem.** Find the area of the ellipse $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}\le 1$.

**Substitution.** $x = au$, $y = bv$. Then the ellipse maps to the unit disk $u^2+v^2\le 1$.

**Jacobian:**

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix}a & 0\0 & b\end{vmatrix} = ab$$

**Integrate:**

$$A = \iint_{\text{ellipse}}dA = \iint_{u^2+v^2\le 1} ab,du,dv = ab\cdot\pi(1)^2 = \boxed{\pi ab}$$

_This is a one-line derivation of the ellipse area formula. No elliptic integrals required._

---

### Example 7.4 — General Ellipsoid Volume (3D)

**Problem.** Find the volume of $\dfrac{x^2}{a^2}+\dfrac{y^2}{b^2}+\dfrac{z^2}{c^2}\le 1$.

**Substitution.** $x=au$, $y=bv$, $z=cw$. Maps ellipsoid to unit ball $u^2+v^2+w^2\le 1$.

**Jacobian:**

$$\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(u,v,w)} = \begin{vmatrix}a&0&0\0&b&0\0&0&c\end{vmatrix} = abc$$

**Integrate:**

$$V = \iiint_{\text{ellipsoid}}dV = abc\cdot\iiint_{u^2+v^2+w^2\le 1}du,dv,dw = abc\cdot\frac{4\pi}{3} = \boxed{\frac{4\pi abc}{3}}$$

---

### Example 7.5 — Non-Obvious Substitution

**Problem.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\iint_R (x^2-xy+y^2),dA$ where $R: 1\le x^2-xy+y^2\le 4$.

**Observation.** The region boundaries are level curves of the integrand. Let:

$$u = x^2 - xy + y^2 \quad\text{(this defines level curves)}$$

But we need a _two-variable_ substitution. Notice $x^2-xy+y^2$ is a quadratic form. Complete the square-style rotation: let $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$... actually, let's use the substitution:

$$x = \sqrt{u}\cdot f(\theta), \quad y = \sqrt{u}\cdot g(\theta)$$

for the right $f, g$. Instead, a cleaner approach: introduce $u,\theta$ via:

$$x = \sqrt{u},\frac{\cos\theta - \sin\theta/\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt[4]{3/4}}, \quad y = \sqrt{u},\frac{2\sin\theta/\sqrt{3}}{\sqrt[4]{3/4}}$$

_This is getting complex — let's use the clean version:_

The form $x^2-xy+y^2 = u$ with the substitution $x = u^{1/2}\cdot p$, $y = u^{1/2}\cdot q$ where $p^2-pq+q^2 = 1$ (the unit level curve). By the theory of quadratic forms, $x^2-xy+y^2$ can be diagonalized via rotation by $\theta = \pi/4$:

$$x = \frac{r\cos\theta - s\sin\theta}{\sqrt{\lambda_1}},\quad y = \frac{r\sin\theta + s\cos\theta}{\sqrt{\lambda_2}}$$

**Simpler direct approach:** use $x^2-xy+y^2 = u$ and $y/x = t$, then $x^2(1 - t + t^2) = u \Rightarrow x = \sqrt{u/(1-t+t^2)}$.

Jacobian computation is involved; the key result via polar-type rescaling gives $|\mathbf{J}| = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$ (constant!).

$$\iint_R u\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}},du,d\theta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\int_0^{2\pi}\int_1^4 u,du,d\theta = \frac{2\pi}{\sqrt{3}}\cdot\frac{15}{2} = \boxed{\frac{15\pi}{\sqrt{3}} = 5\pi\sqrt{3}}$$

---

### Example 7.6 — Spherical Coordinates Derived from Scratch

Rather than memorizing the spherical Jacobian, **derive it**:

$$x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\quad y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\quad z = \rho\cos\phi$$

$$\mathbf{J} = \begin{pmatrix} \sin\phi\cos\theta & \rho\cos\phi\cos\theta & -\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\ \sin\phi\sin\theta & \rho\cos\phi\sin\theta & \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\ \cos\phi & -\rho\sin\phi & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expand along row 3:

$$\det\mathbf{J} = \cos\phi\begin{vmatrix}\rho\cos\phi\cos\theta & -\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\\rho\cos\phi\sin\theta & \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\end{vmatrix} - (-\rho\sin\phi)\begin{vmatrix}\sin\phi\cos\theta & -\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\\sin\phi\sin\theta & \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\end{vmatrix}$$

First minor: $$= \rho^2\cos\phi\cos\theta\sin\phi\cos\theta + \rho^2\sin\phi\sin\theta\cos\phi\sin\theta = \rho^2\cos\phi\sin\phi(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) = \rho^2\cos\phi\sin\phi$$

Second minor: $$= \rho\sin^2\phi\cos^2\theta + \rho\sin^2\phi\sin^2\theta = \rho\sin^2\phi$$

Therefore: $$\det\mathbf{J} = \cos\phi\cdot\rho^2\cos\phi\sin\phi + \rho\sin\phi\cdot\rho\sin^2\phi = \rho^2\sin\phi\cos^2\phi + \rho^2\sin^3\phi = \rho^2\sin\phi$$

$$\boxed{dV = \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta}$$

## 8. How to Choose a Substitution

There is no algorithm, but there are heuristics:

### 8.1 The Region Is the Guide

|Shape of $R$|Try|
|---|---|
|Parallelogram|Linear map aligning sides with coordinate axes|
|Ellipse $\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}\le 1$|$x=au$, $y=bv$ (maps to disk)|
|Region bounded by $xy=c_1$, $xy=c_2$, $y/x=c_3$, $y/x=c_4$|$u=xy$, $v=y/x$|
|Region bounded by $x+y=c$|$u=x+y$, $v=x-y$ or $v=x$|
|Circular or radial symmetry|Polar or cylindrical|
|Spherical symmetry|Spherical coordinates|

### 8.2 The Integrand Is the Guide

If the integrand contains an expression like $g(x,y)$, setting $u = g(x,y)$ is often productive. Then choose $v$ to make the Jacobian nonzero.

### 8.3 Match Boundaries to Coordinate Lines

The ideal substitution makes each boundary of $R$ correspond to $u = \text{const}$ or $v = \text{const}$. This gives rectangular limits $[u_1,u_2]\times[v_1,v_2]$ in $uv$-space — the simplest possible.

## 9. The Jacobian in Full Generality — A Linear Algebra View

### 9.1 The Jacobian as the Derivative

For a differentiable map $T: \mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n$, the **total derivative** at a point $\mathbf{p}$ is the $n\times n$ matrix $DT(\mathbf{p})$ of all partial derivatives — precisely the Jacobian matrix $\mathbf{J}$.

**Locally**, $T$ acts like the linear map $\mathbf{J}$. A unit cube in the domain maps to a parallelepiped in the range with volume $|\det\mathbf{J}|$.

### 9.2 Singular Jacobians

If $\det\mathbf{J} = 0$ at some point, the transformation **collapses** a region of positive area/volume to zero. The change-of-variables formula requires $\det\mathbf{J} \ne 0$ on the interior of $S$ (one-to-one condition).

Example: polar coordinates have $\det\mathbf{J} = r = 0$ at the origin. This is a single point (measure zero), so the formula still holds.

### 9.3 The Chain Rule for Jacobians

If $T_1: (u,v)\mapsto(s,t)$ and $T_2: (s,t)\mapsto(x,y)$, then $T_2\circ T_1$ has:

$$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(s,t)}\cdot\frac{\partial(s,t)}{\partial(u,v)}$$

This is the **chain rule for Jacobians** — it mirrors $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$.

## 10. Proof Sketch of the Change of Variables Theorem

_For the rigorous mind — here's why the theorem is true._

**Step 1.** For a **linear** transformation $T(\mathbf{u}) = A\mathbf{u}$ (constant matrix $A$), a rectangle in $\mathbf{u}$-space maps to a parallelogram in $\mathbf{x}$-space. The area of the parallelogram equals $|\det A|$ times the area of the rectangle. This is the geometric content of the determinant.

**Step 2.** For a **nonlinear** $T$, partition $S$ into small rectangles $S_{ij}$. On each $S_{ij}$, approximate $T$ by its linearization $DT(\mathbf{p}_{ij})$ at the center point. By Step 1, each small piece maps to area $|\det DT(\mathbf{p}_{ij})|,\Delta u,\Delta v$.

**Step 3.** The Riemann sum:

$$\sum_{i,j} f(T(\mathbf{p}_{ij})),|\det DT(\mathbf{p}_{ij})|,\Delta u,\Delta v \to \iint_S f(T(\mathbf{u})),|\det DT(\mathbf{u})|,du,dv$$

as the partition is refined. This equals $\iint_R f(\mathbf{x}),dA$ by the definition of the integral (since $T$ maps $S$ to $R$ bijectively).

**Step 4.** The error in the linear approximation (Step 2) is $O(\Delta u^2 + \Delta v^2)$ per rectangle, which vanishes in the limit because there are $O(1/(\Delta u,\Delta v))$ rectangles — the error is $O(\Delta u + \Delta v) \to 0$. $\square$

## 11. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 11.1 Normalizing Flows — The Jacobian as the Core Operation

**Normalizing flows** are a class of deep generative models that learn to transform a simple distribution (e.g., Gaussian) into a complex data distribution using a sequence of invertible transformations.

If $\mathbf{z} \sim p_Z(\mathbf{z})$ (simple base distribution) and $\mathbf{x} = f(\mathbf{z})$ (invertible transformation), then the density of $\mathbf{x}$ is:

$$p_X(\mathbf{x}) = p_Z(f^{-1}(\mathbf{x}))\left|\det\frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\right| = p_Z(f^{-1}(\mathbf{x}))\left|\det\frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}}\right|^{-1}$$

**This is precisely the change of variables formula** applied to probability densities. The Jacobian determinant $\left|\det\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{z}}\right|$ accounts for how $f$ stretches or compresses probability mass.

**Design challenge:** Computing $\det\mathbf{J}$ for a general $n\times n$ matrix costs $O(n^3)$. For neural networks with millions of parameters, this is infeasible. The main research in normalizing flows is designing architectures where $\det\mathbf{J}$ is cheap to compute (e.g., triangular Jacobians → $O(n)$; coupling layers in RealNVP, Glow).

```
Training a normalizing flow:

  z ~ N(0,I)  →  [f₁]  →  [f₂]  →  ... →  [fₖ]  →  x ≈ data

  Log-likelihood: log p(x) = log p_Z(z) - Σᵢ log|det J_fᵢ|
  
  Each |det J_fᵢ| IS the Jacobian determinant from change of variables.
  
  Triangular architecture → det J = product of diagonal entries → O(n)
```

### 11.2 The Reparameterization Trick — Gradient Through a Stochastic Node

In **Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)**, the encoder outputs $\mu$ and $\sigma$, and we sample:

$$\mathbf{z} = \mu + \sigma\odot\boldsymbol{\epsilon},\qquad \boldsymbol{\epsilon}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)$$

This is a **change of variables**: $\mathbf{z} = T(\boldsymbol{\epsilon}; \mu, \sigma)$. The Jacobian of $T$ with respect to $\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$ is diagonal (since $z_i = \mu_i + \sigma_i\epsilon_i$):

$$\det\mathbf{J} = \prod_i \sigma_i$$

This allows gradients to flow through the sampling operation — which is why the reparameterization trick works mathematically: it replaces a stochastic node with a deterministic transformation of a fixed noise distribution.

### 11.3 Score Matching and Diffusion Models

**Diffusion models** (DDPM, score-based models) define a forward process that gradually adds noise:

$$\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{\bar\alpha_t},\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{1-\bar\alpha_t},\boldsymbol{\epsilon}$$

This is a change of variables (linear transformation) from $(\mathbf{x}_0, \boldsymbol{\epsilon})$ to $\mathbf{x}_t$. The Jacobian of this transformation is $\sqrt{\bar\alpha_t}^n$ (for $n$-dimensional data) — a scalar scaling of the volume element.

The **probability flow ODE** in continuous diffusion models is:

$$d\mathbf{x} = \left[\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x},t) - \frac{1}{2}g(t)^2\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p_t(\mathbf{x})\right]dt$$

The change in log-probability along trajectories involves the **instantaneous change of variables formula** (Liouville's equation):

$$\frac{d\log p(\mathbf{x}(t))}{dt} = -\nabla_\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{v}(\mathbf{x},t)$$

where $\nabla_\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{v}$ is the divergence — the infinitesimal Jacobian. This is the continuous-time generalization of the discrete change-of-variables theorem.

### 11.4 The Jacobian in Backpropagation

Every layer $l$ in a neural network computes $\mathbf{h}^{(l)} = f^{(l)}(\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)})$. The **backpropagation** algorithm computes:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}} = \mathbf{J}_{f^{(l)}}^T\cdot\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\mathbf{h}^{(l)}}$$

where $\mathbf{J}_{f^{(l)}} = \dfrac{\partial\mathbf{h}^{(l)}}{\partial\mathbf{h}^{(l-1)}}$ is the Jacobian matrix of the layer. For an $n$-neuron layer, this is an $n\times n$ matrix — the same object as our change-of-variables Jacobian, now used not for area scaling but for gradient propagation.

**Batch normalization**, **layer normalization**, and **spectral normalization** all control the singular values of this Jacobian matrix — keeping them near 1 prevents vanishing/exploding gradients.

### 11.5 Computing Jacobians in Code

```python
import torch

# Define a transformation T: R^2 -> R^2
# x = r*cos(theta), y = r*sin(theta)  (polar coords)
def polar_to_cartesian(r_theta):
    r, theta = r_theta[:, 0:1], r_theta[:, 1:2]
    x = r * torch.cos(theta)
    y = r * torch.sin(theta)
    return torch.cat([x, y], dim=1)

# Compute Jacobian at a point using torch.autograd.functional.jacobian
from torch.autograd.functional import jacobian

point = torch.tensor([[2.0, torch.pi / 3]], requires_grad=True)
J = jacobian(polar_to_cartesian, point)  # shape: (1,2,1,2)
J_matrix = J[0, :, 0, :]  # extract 2x2 Jacobian

print("Jacobian matrix:\n", J_matrix)
print("det(J) =", torch.det(J_matrix).item())
print("Expected r =", point[0, 0].item())  # should equal r = 2.0
```

Output:

```
Jacobian matrix:
 tensor([[ 0.5000, -1.7321],
         [ 0.8660,  1.0000]])
det(J) = 2.0000
Expected r = 2.0  ✓
```

The determinant equals $r = 2$ — exactly the polar coordinate Jacobian.
