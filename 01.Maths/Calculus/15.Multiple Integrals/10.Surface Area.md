

> _"Area is the shadow that curvature casts on the flat world."_

---

## §0 — Preamble

The length of a curve is computed by straightening infinitesimal arcs. The area of a surface is computed by flattening infinitesimal patches. Both operations involve a Jacobian-like correction factor — the arc length element $ds = \sqrt{1 + (f')^2},dx$ in 1D becomes the surface area element $dS = \sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2},dA$ in 2D. The derivation of this element is a theorem, not a definition, and its proof requires understanding how a tangent plane approximates a curved surface locally.

This note builds the theory from scratch: the geometric motivation, the rigorous derivation of $dS$, parametric surfaces and their general area formula, the role of the cross product, worked examples, and — because the machinery is identical — the appearance of surface area in neural rendering and implicit surface learning.

**References:** Stewart §15.5 (surface area), §16.6 (parametric surfaces); MIT 18.02 Sessions 70–72.

---

## §1 — Motivation: Why $dA$ Alone Is Wrong

Suppose $z = f(x,y)$ defines a surface $S$ over a domain $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$. A naive attempt at the area is $\iint_D dA$ — but this gives the area of the **shadow** (the projection of $S$ onto the $xy$-plane), not the area of $S$ itself. The two agree only when $S$ is flat (i.e., $f$ is constant).

```
z
│        ╱╲    ← surface S (tilted)
│       ╱  ╲
│      ╱    ╲
│     ╱  S   ╲
│    ╱──────────  ← shadow on xy-plane (area = ∫∫ dA, WRONG)
└─────────────── xy

Tilted patch has MORE area than its shadow.
Correction factor = sec(γ) where γ = angle between patch and horizontal.
```

A tilted planar patch of area $\Delta S$ projects to a horizontal patch of area $\Delta A = \Delta S \cos\gamma$, where $\gamma$ is the angle between the patch and the $xy$-plane. So $\Delta S = \Delta A / \cos\gamma = \Delta A \cdot \sec\gamma$.

The whole theory is about computing $\sec\gamma$ in terms of $f_x$ and $f_y$.

## §2 — The Surface Area Element: Rigorous Derivation

### 2.1 Setup

Let $S$ be the surface $z = f(x,y)$ over a domain $D$. Assume $f \in C^1(D)$.

Partition $D$ into small rectangles $R_{ij}$ of dimensions $\Delta x \times \Delta y$. Above each $R_{ij}$, the surface $S$ has a small curved patch $S_{ij}$. We approximate $S_{ij}$ by its **tangent plane** at a corner point $(x_i, y_j, f(x_i, y_j))$.

### 2.2 The Tangent Plane Patch

At $(x_0, y_0, z_0) = (x_0, y_0, f(x_0,y_0))$, the tangent plane to $z = f(x,y)$ is:

$$ z - z_0 = f_x(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0) + f_y(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0) $$

Two vectors lying in this tangent plane, corresponding to steps $\Delta x$ and $\Delta y$ in the domain, are:

$$ \mathbf{a} = \langle \Delta x,; 0,; f_x,\Delta x \rangle = \Delta x,\langle 1, 0, f_x \rangle $$

$$ \mathbf{b} = \langle 0,; \Delta y,; f_y,\Delta y \rangle = \Delta y,\langle 0, 1, f_y \rangle $$

### 2.3 Area of the Tangent Patch

The area of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ is $|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|$.

$$ \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b} = \Delta x,\Delta y;\begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & 0 & f_x \ 0 & 1 & f_y \end{vmatrix} = \Delta x,\Delta y;\langle -f_x,;-f_y,;1\rangle $$

$$ |\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}| = \Delta x,\Delta y;\sqrt{f_x^2 + f_y^2 + 1} $$

### 2.4 The Surface Area Formula

Summing over all patches and taking the limit:

$$ \boxed{A(S) = \iint_D \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)^2};dA} $$

The integrand $dS = \sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2},dA$ is the **surface area element**.

### 2.5 The $\sec\gamma$ Interpretation

The unit normal to the surface is $\hat{n} = \frac{\langle -f_x, -f_y, 1\rangle}{\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}}$.

The angle $\gamma$ between the surface and the $xy$-plane satisfies:

$$ \cos\gamma = \hat{n}\cdot\hat{k} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}} $$

So $\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2} = \sec\gamma$, and:

$$ A(S) = \iint_D \sec\gamma,dA $$

This confirms the geometric intuition: each infinitesimal area element $dA$ in the domain is inflated by the secant of the tilt angle to give the true surface area.

## §3 — Parametric Surfaces (The General Case)

### 3.1 Definition

A **parametric surface** is a map $\mathbf{r}: D^* \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$:

$$ \mathbf{r}(u,v) = \langle x(u,v),; y(u,v),; z(u,v) \rangle, \quad (u,v) \in D^* $$

The surface $z = f(x,y)$ is the special case $\mathbf{r}(x,y) = \langle x, y, f(x,y)\rangle$.

Other examples:

- **Sphere:** $\mathbf{r}(\phi,\theta) = \langle R\sin\phi\cos\theta,, R\sin\phi\sin\theta,, R\cos\phi\rangle$
- **Cylinder:** $\mathbf{r}(\theta, z) = \langle R\cos\theta,, R\sin\theta,, z\rangle$
- **Torus:** $\mathbf{r}(\theta,\phi) = \langle (R+r\cos\phi)\cos\theta,, (R+r\cos\phi)\sin\theta,, r\sin\phi\rangle$

### 3.2 The Tangent Vectors

The partial derivatives of $\mathbf{r}$ give tangent vectors to the surface:

$$ \mathbf{r}_u = \frac{\partial\mathbf{r}}{\partial u} = \left\langle \frac{\partial x}{\partial u},;\frac{\partial y}{\partial u},;\frac{\partial z}{\partial u}\right\rangle $$

$$ \mathbf{r}_v = \frac{\partial\mathbf{r}}{\partial v} = \left\langle \frac{\partial x}{\partial v},;\frac{\partial y}{\partial v},;\frac{\partial z}{\partial v}\right\rangle $$

$\mathbf{r}_u$ is tangent to the $u$-parameter curve (holding $v$ fixed); $\mathbf{r}_v$ similarly.

### 3.3 The Surface Area Element for Parametric Surfaces

A small patch $[u, u+du]\times[v, v+dv]$ maps to a parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{r}_u,du$ and $\mathbf{r}_v,dv$. Its area is:

$$ dS = |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv $$

**General Surface Area Formula:**

$$ \boxed{A(S) = \iint_{D^*} |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv} $$

### 3.4 Recovering the Graph Formula

For $\mathbf{r}(x,y) = \langle x, y, f(x,y)\rangle$:

$$ \mathbf{r}_x = \langle 1, 0, f_x\rangle, \qquad \mathbf{r}_y = \langle 0, 1, f_y\rangle $$

$$ \mathbf{r}_x\times\mathbf{r}_y = \langle -f_x,;-f_y,;1\rangle, \qquad |\mathbf{r}_x\times\mathbf{r}_y| = \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2} $$

Recovering $dS = \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2},dA$. $\square$

## §4 — The Cross Product $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$: What It Encodes

$$ \mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ \partial x/\partial u & \partial y/\partial u & \partial z/\partial u \ \partial x/\partial v & \partial y/\partial v & \partial z/\partial v \end{vmatrix} $$

Expanding:

$$ = \left\langle \frac{\partial(y,z)}{\partial(u,v)},; \frac{\partial(z,x)}{\partial(u,v)},; \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} \right\rangle $$

where $\frac{\partial(y,z)}{\partial(u,v)} = \det\begin{pmatrix}y_u & z_u \ y_v & z_v\end{pmatrix}$ is a $2\times 2$ Jacobian.

So:

$$ |\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|^2 = \left(\frac{\partial(y,z)}{\partial(u,v)}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial(z,x)}{\partial(u,v)}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right)^2 $$

This is the **2D analogue of the arc-length formula**: just as $ds = \sqrt{(dx/dt)^2+(dy/dt)^2+(dz/dt)^2},dt$ in 1D, here the "speed" of the surface parameterisation in 2D is $|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|$.

```
1D curve:   r(t)          →   ds = |r'(t)| dt       (speed × time)
2D surface: r(u,v)        →   dS = |r_u × r_v| du dv (area magnification)
```

The cross product magnitude is the **area magnification factor** of the parametrisation $\mathbf{r}$ — it is the surface analogue of the Jacobian determinant.

## §5 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Surface Area of a Sphere

**Claim:** $A(S^2_R) = 4\pi R^2$.

**Parametrisation.** Use spherical angles $\phi \in [0,\pi]$ (polar), $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$ (azimuthal):

$$ \mathbf{r}(\phi,\theta) = \langle R\sin\phi\cos\theta,; R\sin\phi\sin\theta,; R\cos\phi\rangle $$

**Compute tangent vectors:**

$$ \mathbf{r}_\phi = \langle R\cos\phi\cos\theta,; R\cos\phi\sin\theta,; -R\sin\phi\rangle $$

$$ \mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -R\sin\phi\sin\theta,; R\sin\phi\cos\theta,; 0\rangle $$

**Cross product:**

$$ \mathbf{r}_\phi\times\mathbf{r}_\theta = R^2\langle -\sin^2\phi\cos\theta,; -\sin^2\phi\sin\theta,; -\sin\phi\cos\phi\rangle $$

$$ |\mathbf{r}_\phi\times\mathbf{r}_\theta| = R^2\sin\phi\sqrt{\sin^2\phi\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\phi\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\phi} = R^2\sin\phi $$

(using $\sin^2\phi(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) + \cos^2\phi = \sin^2\phi + \cos^2\phi = 1$).

**Area:**

$$ A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi R^2\sin\phi,d\phi,d\theta = R^2\int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\int_0^\pi\sin\phi,d\phi = R^2\cdot 2\pi\cdot[-\cos\phi]_0^\pi = 4\pi R^2 \checkmark $$

---

### Example 2 — Surface Area of a Cone

**Find** the surface area of the cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ over the disk $D = {x^2+y^2\le R^2}$.

**Compute partials:**

$$ f_x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}, \qquad f_y = \frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}} $$

$$ f_x^2 + f_y^2 = \frac{x^2+y^2}{x^2+y^2} = 1 $$

$$ \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2} = \sqrt{2} $$

**Area:**

$$ A = \iint_D \sqrt{2},dA = \sqrt{2}\cdot\text{Area}(D) = \sqrt{2}\cdot\pi R^2 $$

**Verification.** The cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ has half-angle $45°$. The slant height is $R\sqrt{2}$. The lateral surface area is $\pi R \cdot (R\sqrt{2}) = \pi R^2\sqrt{2}$. $\checkmark$

---

### Example 3 — Surface Area of a Paraboloid

**Find** the area of $z = x^2 + y^2$ over $D = {x^2+y^2\le 1}$.

$$ f_x = 2x, \quad f_y = 2y, \quad \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2} = \sqrt{1+4(x^2+y^2)} $$

**Switch to polar** ($r^2 = x^2+y^2$):

$$ A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 \sqrt{1+4r^2},r,dr,d\theta = 2\pi\int_0^1 r\sqrt{1+4r^2},dr $$

Let $u = 1+4r^2$, $du = 8r,dr$:

$$ = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{8}\int_1^5\sqrt{u},du = \frac{\pi}{4}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\left[u^{3/2}\right]_1^5 = \frac{\pi}{6}(5\sqrt{5}-1) $$

---

### Example 4 — Cylinder (Parametric)

**Find** the area of the lateral surface of $x^2 + y^2 = R^2$, $0 \le z \le H$.

**Parametrisation:** $\mathbf{r}(\theta, z) = \langle R\cos\theta,; R\sin\theta,; z\rangle$, $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$, $z\in[0,H]$.

$$ \mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -R\sin\theta,; R\cos\theta,; 0\rangle, \qquad \mathbf{r}_z = \langle 0,; 0,; 1\rangle $$

$$ \mathbf{r}_\theta\times\mathbf{r}_z = \langle R\cos\theta,; R\sin\theta,; 0\rangle, \qquad |\mathbf{r}_\theta\times\mathbf{r}_z| = R $$

$$ A = \int_0^H\int_0^{2\pi} R,d\theta,dz = 2\pi R H \checkmark $$

---

### Example 5 — A Tricky Graph: $z = xy$ Over a Triangle

**Find** the area of $z = xy$ over $D = {0\le x\le 1,; 0\le y\le x}$.

$$ f_x = y,\quad f_y = x,\quad \sqrt{1+x^2+y^2} $$

$$ A = \int_0^1\int_0^x\sqrt{1+x^2+y^2},dy,dx $$

The inner integral does not have a pleasant closed form in general, but we can note:

$$ \sqrt{1+x^2+y^2} \ge 1 \implies A \ge \text{Area}(D) = \frac{1}{2} $$

$$ \text{On }D:; x^2+y^2\le 2x^2\le 2 \implies \sqrt{1+x^2+y^2}\le\sqrt{3} \implies A\le \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} $$

The Estimation property bounds $A \in \left[\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\right]$ without computing the integral. Numerically, $A\approx 0.603$.

## §6 — Special Cases and Short-Cuts

### 6.1 Flat Surfaces

$z = c$ (constant): $f_x = f_y = 0$, so $dS = dA$. Area $= \text{Area}(D)$.

### 6.2 Constant-Slope Planes

$z = ax + by + c$: $f_x = a$, $f_y = b$, so $dS = \sqrt{1+a^2+b^2},dA$.

$$ A = \sqrt{1+a^2+b^2}\cdot\text{Area}(D) $$

The magnification factor $\sqrt{1+a^2+b^2} = \sec\gamma$ is constant — the plane is uniformly tilted.

### 6.3 Surfaces of Revolution

Rotate $y = g(x) \ge 0$, $a\le x\le b$, about the $x$-axis. Parametrise:

$$ \mathbf{r}(x,\theta) = \langle x,; g(x)\cos\theta,; g(x)\sin\theta\rangle $$

$$ |\mathbf{r}_x\times\mathbf{r}_\theta| = g(x)\sqrt{1+[g'(x)]^2} $$

$$ \boxed{A = 2\pi\int_a^b g(x)\sqrt{1+[g'(x)]^2},dx} $$

This is the familiar **surface of revolution formula** from single-variable calculus — it is a special case of the parametric surface area formula.

**Example:** Sphere of radius $R$. Rotate $y = \sqrt{R^2-x^2}$, $x\in[-R,R]$:

$$ g'(x) = \frac{-x}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2}}, \quad 1+[g'(x)]^2 = \frac{R^2}{R^2-x^2}, \quad g(x)\sqrt{1+[g'(x)]^2} = R $$

$$ A = 2\pi\int_{-R}^R R,dx = 4\pi R^2 \checkmark $$

## §7 — The First Fundamental Form (Differential Geometry Preview)

For a parametric surface $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$, define the **metric coefficients**:

$$ E = \mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_u = |\mathbf{r}_u|^2, \qquad F = \mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_v, \qquad G = \mathbf{r}_v\cdot\mathbf{r}_v = |\mathbf{r}_v|^2 $$

The **First Fundamental Form** is the matrix $\begin{pmatrix}E & F \ F & G\end{pmatrix}$.

**Key identity:**

$$ |\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|^2 = |\mathbf{r}_u|^2|\mathbf{r}_v|^2 - (\mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_v)^2 = EG - F^2 $$

(by the Lagrange identity $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|^2 = |\mathbf{a}|^2|\mathbf{b}|^2 - (\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})^2$).

Therefore:

$$ dS = \sqrt{EG-F^2},du,dv $$

**Coordinate systems.** For **orthogonal parametrisations** ($F = \mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_v = 0$):

$$ dS = \sqrt{EG},du,dv = |\mathbf{r}_u||\mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv $$

|Surface|$E$|$F$|$G$|$dS$|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Sphere $(\phi,\theta)$|$R^2$|$0$|$R^2\sin^2\phi$|$R^2\sin\phi,d\phi,d\theta$|
|Cylinder $(\theta,z)$|$R^2$|$0$|$1$|$R,d\theta,dz$|
|Graph $z=f(x,y)$|$1+f_x^2$|$f_xf_y$|$1+f_y^2$|$\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2},dx,dy$|

The First Fundamental Form is the object that intrinsic geometry lives in — lengths, angles, and areas on the surface are all determined by $E, F, G$. It is the bridge between multivariable calculus and Riemannian geometry.

## §8 — Comparison: Arc Length vs Surface Area

It is worth pausing to see the exact structural parallel:

|Object|Parametrisation|Differential|Total measure|
|---|---|---|---|
|Curve in $\mathbb{R}^2$|$\mathbf{r}(t)$, $t\in[a,b]$|$ds =|\mathbf{r}'(t)|
|Graph $y=f(x)$|—|$ds = \sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx$|$\int_a^b\sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx$|
|Surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$|$\mathbf{r}(u,v)$, $(u,v)\in D^*$|$dS =|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|
|Graph $z=f(x,y)$|—|$dS = \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2},dA$|$\iint_D\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2},dA$|

In each case: **parametrise → differentiate → take magnitude → integrate**.

The 1D magnitude is a vector norm; the 2D magnitude is a cross-product norm. Both are Jacobian-type correction factors.

## §9 — Common Errors and Pitfalls

|Error|What Goes Wrong|Remedy|
|---|---|---|
|Writing $A = \iint_D dA$|Computes shadow area, not surface area|Always include $\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}$|
|Wrong order $\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v$ vs $\mathbf{r}_v\times\mathbf{r}_u$|Sign change in normal, but $|\cdot|
|Forgetting $r,dr,d\theta$ when converting to polar|See §3 of polar notes|The $\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}$ and the polar $r$ are two separate factors|
|Using $(u,v)$ with $\mathbf{r}_u\parallel\mathbf{r}_v$|Cross product $= 0$, parametrisation degenerate|Choose $(u,v)$ so tangent vectors are non-parallel|
|Forgetting $\sin\phi$ factor for sphere|$|\mathbf{r}_\phi\times\mathbf{r}_\theta|

## §10 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 10.1 Neural Radiance Fields (NeRF) and Surface Rendering

**NeRF** represents a scene as a continuous function $(x,y,z,\theta,\phi) \mapsto (\text{colour}, \sigma)$ where $\sigma$ is volume density. The rendered colour along a ray integrates:

$$ C(\mathbf{r}) = \int_0^\infty T(t),\sigma(\mathbf{r}(t)),\mathbf{c}(\mathbf{r}(t),\mathbf{d}),dt $$

The **surface** of an object is the level set ${\sigma = \sigma_{\text{threshold}}}$. Computing the _area_ of this implicit surface uses the coarea formula — a generalisation of the surface area integral. The training loss in some NeRF variants explicitly penalises surface area (to encourage thin, sharp surfaces), making the surface area integral a component of the optimisation objective.

### 10.2 Implicit Neural Representations (INR) and the Eikonal Equation

**Signed Distance Functions (SDFs)** represent surfaces as zero level sets of a function $\Phi: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$. The surface is $S = {\Phi(\mathbf{x}) = 0}$.

The **coarea formula** gives:

$$ \text{Area}(S) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^3} |\nabla\Phi(\mathbf{x})|,\delta(\Phi(\mathbf{x})),d\mathbf{x} $$

When $\Phi$ is a true SDF, $|\nabla\Phi| = 1$ everywhere (**eikonal condition**). The loss function in **IGR** (Implicit Geometric Regularisation) and **NeuS** includes:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{eikonal}} = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^3}(|\nabla\Phi(\mathbf{x})| - 1)^2,d\mathbf{x} $$

This is the $L^2$ error of the gradient magnitude — enforcing the surface to be a valid SDF. Its derivation uses exactly the parametric surface area machinery developed here.

### 10.3 Geometric Deep Learning and Surface Meshing

In **mesh-based** shape generation (e.g., Pixel2Mesh, DeepMesh), the model outputs a triangulated surface. The loss often includes a **surface area regulariser**:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{area}} = \sum_{\text{triangles}} \text{Area}(\triangle_i) $$

where $\text{Area}(\triangle_i) = \frac{1}{2}|(\mathbf{v}_2-\mathbf{v}_1)\times(\mathbf{v}_3-\mathbf{v}_1)|$ is exactly the cross-product formula for the area of the parallelogram spanned by two edge vectors — the discrete version of $|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|$.

The gradient of this loss with respect to vertex positions $\mathbf{v}_i$ drives shape towards smooth, minimal-area surfaces.

### 10.4 Riemannian Geometry and Transformer Attention

The attention mechanism in a Transformer computes scores between tokens living in a high-dimensional space. When this space is given a **Riemannian metric** $G = (g_{ij})$, the "volume" of attention neighbourhoods is:

$$ \text{Vol}(\mathcal{N}) = \int_{\mathcal{N}} \sqrt{\det G},du,dv $$

The factor $\sqrt{\det G}$ is exactly $\sqrt{EG-F^2}$ (for 2D manifolds) — the same quantity that appears in the First Fundamental Form. Recent work on **hyperbolic** and **spherical** transformers explicitly computes this volume element to normalise attention weights.

### 10.5 Diffusion on Surfaces (Graph Neural Networks)

**Geometric diffusion** — used in protein structure prediction (AlphaFold), molecular property prediction, and 3D shape analysis — involves the **Laplace–Beltrami operator** on a surface. The heat kernel and eigenfunctions of this operator are defined through integrals of the form:

$$ \langle f, g \rangle_S = \iint_S f,g,dS $$

where $dS$ is exactly the surface area element. The entire spectral theory of signals on surfaces — used by spectral GNNs — lives in the Hilbert space $L^2(S, dS)$, whose inner product is the surface integral.

## §11 — The Protocol: Setting Up Any Surface Area Integral

```
GIVEN: A surface S

STEP 1. How is S described?
        ├── As a graph z = f(x,y) over domain D
        │       → use A = ∫∫_D √(1 + f_x² + f_y²) dA
        │       → identify D, compute partials, simplify radical
        │
        └── Parametrically r(u,v) over D*
                → compute r_u and r_v
                → compute r_u × r_v  (3×3 determinant)
                → compute |r_u × r_v|  (simplify!)
                → integrate: A = ∫∫_{D*} |r_u × r_v| du dv

STEP 2. Is the domain circular?
        └── Yes → switch to polar before integrating
            (remember: dA = r dr dθ is SEPARATE from √(1+f_x²+f_y²))

STEP 3. Simplify the radical FIRST.
        Look for:
        - f_x² + f_y² = constant  (cone, plane)  → √ pulls out
        - f_x² + f_y² = g(r²)     (paraboloid)   → polar substitution

STEP 4. Check with dimension: area has units [length]².
```

## §12 — Summary Formula Sheet

**Graph $z = f(x,y)$:**

$$ A(S) = \iint_D\sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2},dA $$

$$ A(S) = \iint_{D^_}|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv = \iint_{D^_}\sqrt{EG-F^2},du,dv $$
