## §0 — Preamble

Single-variable integration has a clean list of properties: linearity, additivity, monotonicity, the triangle inequality. Double integrals inherit every one of these, and add a few of their own born from the two-dimensional domain. This note proves each property rigorously from the definition, supplies geometric intuition, catalogues the failure modes, and traces the properties into their machine learning incarnations — because every identity here gets used implicitly in probabilistic modelling and numerical integration.

**Convention throughout.** $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ is a bounded region whose boundary has measure zero. All functions are assumed bounded and continuous unless otherwise stated, ensuring Riemann integrability.

---

## §1 — The Definition We Are Working From

Recall: enclose $D$ in a rectangle $R$. Partition $R$ into sub-rectangles $R_{ij}$ of area $\Delta A_{ij} = \Delta x_i \Delta y_j$. Choose sample points $(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_)$. The double integral is the limit of the Riemann sum:

$$ \iint_D f,dA := \lim_{|P| \to 0} \sum_{i,j} f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_),\Delta A_{ij} $$

where the sum ranges only over sub-rectangles that _intersect_ $D$, and $|P|$ is the mesh (largest diagonal of any sub-rectangle).

Every property below follows from manipulating this sum and taking limits.

---

## §2 — Property I: Linearity

### 2.1 Homogeneity

$$ \boxed{\iint_D c,f(x,y),dA = c\iint_D f(x,y),dA, \quad c \in \mathbb{R}} $$

**Proof.** At the level of Riemann sums:

$$ \sum_{i,j} [c,f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_)],\Delta A_{ij} = c\sum_{i,j} f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_),\Delta A_{ij} $$

Taking $|P| \to 0$ and using the linearity of limits gives the result. $\square$

### 2.2 Additivity of Integrand

$$ \boxed{\iint_D [f(x,y) + g(x,y)],dA = \iint_D f(x,y),dA + \iint_D g(x,y),dA} $$

**Proof.** Identical: the Riemann sum of a sum is the sum of Riemann sums, and limits distribute over finite sums. $\square$

### 2.3 Combined Linearity

Combining 2.1 and 2.2: for $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$,

$$ \iint_D [\alpha f + \beta g],dA = \alpha\iint_D f,dA + \beta\iint_D g,dA $$

**Geometric reading.** $\iint_D f,dA$ is the signed volume under $z = f(x,y)$ over $D$. Scaling $f$ by $c$ scales the volume by $c$; stacking two surfaces $f$ and $g$ yields the sum of their volumes.

```
z
│       f+g
│      ╱╲
│     ╱  ╲   ← volume under f+g = vol(f) + vol(g)
│    ╱  f  ╲
│   ╱────────╲
│  ╱    g     ╲
└──────────────── xy-plane (D)
```

**ML connection.** The **expected value** operator $\mathbb{E}$ is a double integral (when the state space is $\mathbb{R}^2$):

$$ \mathbb{E}[\alpha f(X,Y) + \beta g(X,Y)] = \alpha,\mathbb{E}[f(X,Y)] + \beta,\mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)] $$

This is _exactly_ linearity of the double integral against the density $p(x,y)$. Every linearity argument in a derivation involving expected values — loss function decomposition, bias-variance tradeoff, ELBO manipulation — is an invocation of this property.

## §3 — Property II: Domain Additivity

$$ \boxed{\iint_D f,dA = \iint_{D_1} f,dA + \iint_{D_2} f,dA} $$

whenever $D = D_1 \cup D_2$ and $D_1 \cap D_2$ has **area zero** (e.g., they share only a boundary curve).

**Proof.** The extension-by-zero functions $F_1, F_2$ (of $f$ onto a common rectangle $R$) satisfy $F_1 + F_2 = F$ pointwise a.e. (they agree except on the shared boundary, which has measure zero). Linearity of the integral over $R$ gives the result. $\square$

**Geometric reading.**

```
         D₁        │        D₂
  ╭───────────╮    │    ╭───────────╮
  │           │    │    │           │
  │  ∫∫f dA  │ ───┼─── │  ∫∫f dA  │
  │   D₁      │    │    │   D₂      │
  ╰───────────╯    │    ╰───────────╯
                   ↑ shared boundary = measure zero
```

**Corollary.** For any finite decomposition $D = D_1 \cup \cdots \cup D_n$ (pairwise overlapping only on boundaries):

$$ \iint_D f,dA = \sum_{k=1}^n \iint_{D_k} f,dA $$

**ML connection.** In **mixture models**, the joint density is $p(x,y) = \sum_k \pi_k p_k(x,y)$ with $\sum_k \pi_k = 1$. Computing the marginal:

$$ p_X(x) = \int p(x,y),dy = \sum_k \pi_k \int p_k(x,y),dy = \sum_k \pi_k p_{k,X}(x) $$

The interchange of sum and integral is domain additivity (plus linearity) applied to the decomposition of the support into mixture components.

## §4 — Property III: Monotonicity

$$ \boxed{f(x,y) \le g(x,y) \text{ on } D \implies \iint_D f,dA \le \iint_D g,dA} $$

**Proof.** Since $f \le g$ on $D$, for every partition and every sample point:

$$ \sum_{i,j} f(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_),\Delta A_{ij} \le \sum_{i,j} g(x_{ij}^_, y_{ij}^_),\Delta A_{ij} $$

Taking limits preserves the inequality. $\square$

**Corollary (Positivity).** $f \ge 0$ on $D \implies \iint_D f,dA \ge 0$.

**Proof.** Apply monotonicity with $g \equiv 0$. $\square$

**Geometric reading.** If the surface $z = f$ lies entirely below $z = g$ over $D$, then the volume under $f$ cannot exceed the volume under $g$.

```
z
│    z = g(x,y)  ←  upper surface
│   ╭──────────╮
│  ╭╯          ╰╮  z = f(x,y)  ← lower surface
│  │     D      │
└──┴────────────┴── xy
    vol(f) ≤ vol(g)
```

## §5 — Property IV: Estimation (Bounding)

Let $m \le f(x,y) \le M$ for all $(x,y) \in D$. Then:

$$ \boxed{m \cdot \text{Area}(D) \le \iint_D f(x,y),dA \le M \cdot \text{Area}(D)} $$

**Proof.** Apply monotonicity to the chain $m \le f \le M$ and use:

$$ \iint_D m,dA = m\cdot\text{Area}(D), \qquad \iint_D M,dA = M\cdot\text{Area}(D) \qquad \square $$

**Geometric reading.**

```
z = M ─────────────────────  ← top slab, volume = M · Area(D)
         ╭──────╮
z = f ───╯  D   ╰────────    ← actual surface, volume in between
z = m ─────────────────────  ← bottom slab, volume = m · Area(D)
```

The integral is "sandwiched" between two box-volumes.

**ML connection.** In **Bayesian inference**, when the likelihood $\mathcal{L}(\theta \mid x,y) \le M$ is bounded, the normalising constant (evidence) satisfies:

$$ m \cdot \text{Vol}(\Theta) \le \int_\Theta \mathcal{L}(\theta),\pi(\theta),d\theta \le M \cdot \text{Vol}(\Theta) $$

These bounds are used to check the sanity of variational approximations and to set hyperparameters in variational inference without computing the evidence exactly.

---

## §6 — Property V: The Area Formula

$$ \boxed{\iint_D 1,dA = \text{Area}(D)} $$

**Proof.** The Riemann sum for $f \equiv 1$ is $\sum_{i,j} 1 \cdot \Delta A_{ij}$, which is the total area of all sub-rectangles intersecting $D$. In the limit $|P| \to 0$ this converges to $\text{Area}(D)$ (since $\partial D$ has measure zero). $\square$

**This is not a definition** — it is a theorem that the double integral recovers area, validating the geometric interpretation.

**Example.** Area of the unit disk $D = {x^2 + y^2 \le 1}$:

$$ \iint_D 1,dA = \pi(1)^2 = \pi $$

(verified directly via polar coordinates: $\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r,dr,d\theta = \pi$).

---

## §7 — Property VI: Absolute Value Inequality (Triangle Inequality)

$$ \boxed{\left|\iint_D f,dA\right| \le \iint_D |f|,dA} $$

**Proof.** Since $-|f| \le f \le |f|$, monotonicity gives:

$$ -\iint_D |f|,dA \le \iint_D f,dA \le \iint_D |f|,dA $$

which is the two-sided inequality. $\square$

**Why it matters.** This is the 2D analogue of $|\int f| \le \int |f|$. In **functional analysis** it is the key estimate for showing that integration defines a bounded linear functional on $L^1(D)$.

**ML connection.** In **generalisation bounds** (e.g., PAC learning theory), the difference in expected risk between two models $f$ and $g$ is bounded:

$$ \left|\mathbb{E}[\ell(f)] - \mathbb{E}[\ell(g)]\right| = \left|\iint [\ell(f) - \ell(g)],p,dA\right| \le \iint |\ell(f) - \ell(g)|,p,dA $$

This is precisely the triangle inequality for double integrals. Rademacher complexity bounds and uniform convergence arguments all rely on it.

## §8 — Property VII: Mean Value Theorem for Double Integrals

$$ \boxed{\iint_D f,dA = f(x_0, y_0)\cdot\text{Area}(D)} $$

for some $(x_0, y_0) \in D$, provided $f$ is continuous and $D$ is connected and compact.

**Proof.** Since $D$ is compact and $f$ continuous, $f$ attains its minimum $m$ and maximum $M$ on $D$. By the estimation property:

$$ m \le \frac{\iint_D f,dA}{\text{Area}(D)} \le M $$

By the Intermediate Value Theorem (since $D$ is connected and $f$ is continuous), $f$ takes every value between $m$ and $M$. In particular, there exists $(x_0, y_0) \in D$ such that:

$$ f(x_0, y_0) = \frac{\iint_D f,dA}{\text{Area}(D)} \qquad \square $$

**The point $(x_0, y_0)$** is called the **mean value point** — it is the spatial location at which the function exactly equals its average value over $D$.

**Geometric reading.** The volume under $z = f$ over $D$ equals the volume of a **flat box** of height $f(x_0, y_0)$ and base $D$.

```
z
│   ┌─────────────────┐  ← z = f(x₀,y₀)  (the average height)
│   │                 │
│   │  same volume!   │
│   │                 │
└───┴─────────────────┴── D
    Area(D) × f(x₀,y₀) = ∫∫f dA
```

**ML connection.** The **average activation** of a feature map in a CNN over a spatial region $D$ (Global Average Pooling) is:

$$ \bar{f} = \frac{1}{\text{Area}(D)}\iint_D f(x,y),dA = f(x_0, y_0) $$

The MVT guarantees that this average is _attained somewhere_ — it is not merely an abstract number but a realised value of the function.

## §9 — Property VIII: Comparison with Integral Over Subdomain

If $D_1 \subseteq D_2$ and $f \ge 0$ on $D_2$:

$$ \boxed{\iint_{D_1} f,dA \le \iint_{D_2} f,dA} $$

**Proof.** Write $D_2 = D_1 \cup (D_2 \setminus D_1)$ (essentially disjoint). By domain additivity and positivity:

$$ \iint_{D_2} f,dA = \iint_{D_1} f,dA + \underbrace{\iint_{D_2 \setminus D_1} f,dA}_{\ge 0} \ge \iint_{D_1} f,dA \qquad \square $$

**Remark.** The hypothesis $f \ge 0$ is essential. For signed $f$, enlarging the domain can decrease the integral.

## §10 — Property IX: Symmetry Reduction

These properties dramatically simplify computation when the domain has geometric symmetry.

### 10.1 Odd Function on Symmetric Domain

If $D$ is **symmetric about the $y$-axis** (i.e., $(x,y) \in D \Rightarrow (-x,y) \in D$) and $f$ is **odd in $x$** ($f(-x,y) = -f(x,y)$):

$$ \boxed{\iint_D f(x,y),dA = 0} $$

**Proof.** Split $D = D^+ \cup D^-$ where $D^+ = D \cap {x \ge 0}$. Map $D^+$ to $D^-$ via $(x,y) \mapsto (-x,y)$:

$$ \iint_{D^-} f,dA = \iint_{D^+} f(-x,y),dA = -\iint_{D^+} f(x,y),dA $$

So $\iint_D f,dA = \iint_{D^+} f,dA + \iint_{D^-} f,dA = 0$. $\square$

### 10.2 Even Function on Symmetric Domain

If $D$ is symmetric about the $y$-axis and $f$ is **even in $x$** ($f(-x,y) = f(x,y)$):

$$ \boxed{\iint_D f(x,y),dA = 2\iint_{D^+} f(x,y),dA} $$

**Analogues.** Replace "$y$-axis / odd in $x$" with "$x$-axis / odd in $y$", "$x=y$ / odd under swap", etc. Full symmetry (both axes) and odd function $\Rightarrow$ integral is zero.

**Example.** $\iint_D xy,dA$ where $D = {x^2 + y^2 \le 1}$.

$D$ is symmetric about both axes. $f(x,y) = xy$ is odd in $x$ (and in $y$). Immediate conclusion: $\iint_D xy,dA = 0$.

**ML connection.** In **neural network weight initialisation**, symmetry arguments like this explain why certain initialisations (e.g., all-zeros) cause **symmetry breaking failure**: the gradient $\nabla_W \mathcal{L}$ — an integral over the data distribution — vanishes by a symmetry argument analogous to the above, trapping all neurons in identical states.

## §11 — Property X: Separability

If $f(x,y) = g(x)\cdot h(y)$ and $D = [a,b]\times[c,d]$ is a rectangle:

$$ \boxed{\iint_D g(x),h(y),dA = \left(\int_a^b g(x),dx\right)!\left(\int_c^d h(y),dy\right)} $$

**Proof.** By Fubini:

$$ \iint_D g(x),h(y),dA = \int_a^b g(x)\left[\int_c^d h(y),dy\right]dx = \left(\int_c^d h(y),dy\right)\int_a^b g(x),dx \qquad \square $$

**Warning.** Separability requires $D$ to be a **rectangle**. On a general domain, $\iint_D g(x)h(y),dA$ does _not_ factor, because the limits of the inner integral depend on $x$.

**ML connection.** In **probabilistic graphical models**, if $X \perp Y$ (independence), then $p(x,y) = p_X(x),p_Y(y)$. The joint expectation factors:

$$ \mathbb{E}[g(X)h(Y)] = \iint g(x)h(y),p_X(x),p_Y(y),dA = \mathbb{E}[g(X)]\cdot\mathbb{E}[h(Y)] $$

This is separability of the double integral. The breakdown of separability when $X \not\perp Y$ is precisely the reason correlation matters.

## §12 — The Symmetry of the Properties: A Summary Table

|#|Property|Hypothesis|Statement|
|---|---|---|---|
|I|Linearity|$f, g$ integrable|$\iint_D (\alpha f + \beta g),dA = \alpha\iint f + \beta\iint g$|
|II|Domain Additivity|$D = D_1 \cup D_2$, overlap measure 0|$\iint_D f = \iint_{D_1} f + \iint_{D_2} f$|
|III|Monotonicity|$f \le g$ on $D$|$\iint_D f \le \iint_D g$|
|IV|Estimation|$m \le f \le M$ on $D$|$m\cdot A(D) \le \iint_D f \le M\cdot A(D)$|
|V|Area|$f \equiv 1$|$\iint_D 1,dA = \text{Area}(D)$|
|VI|Triangle Ineq.|$f$ integrable|$|
|VII|Mean Value Thm|$f$ continuous, $D$ compact connected|$\exists(x_0,y_0)\in D: \iint_D f = f(x_0,y_0)\cdot A(D)$|
|VIII|Subdomain|$D_1 \subseteq D_2$, $f \ge 0$|$\iint_{D_1} f \le \iint_{D_2} f$|
|IX|Symmetry|Symmetric $D$, odd/even $f$|$\iint_D f = 0$ or $= 2\iint_{D^+} f$|
|X|Separability|$f = g(x)h(y)$, $D$ rectangle|$\iint_D f = (\int g)(\int h)$|

## §13 — Worked Examples Using the Properties

### Example 1 — Symmetry Kills the Integral

**Compute** $\displaystyle\iint_D (x^3 + \sin y + 4),dA$ where $D = {(x,y): x^2 + y^2 \le 9}$.

**Strategy.** Use linearity to split, then symmetry.

$$ \iint_D (x^3 + \sin y + 4),dA = \underbrace{\iint_D x^3,dA}_{=0,\ \text{odd in }x} + \underbrace{\iint_D \sin y,dA}_{=0,\ \text{odd in }y} + \iint_D 4,dA $$

$D$ is a disk of radius $3$: $\text{Area}(D) = 9\pi$. Therefore:

$$ = 0 + 0 + 4\cdot 9\pi = 36\pi $$

### Example 2 — Estimation Without Computing

**Show** that $\displaystyle\iint_D e^{x^2 + y^2},dA \le e^2\pi$ where $D = {x^2 + y^2 \le 1}$.

On $D$: $0 \le x^2 + y^2 \le 1$, so $e^0 \le e^{x^2+y^2} \le e^1$.

By estimation:

$$ \iint_D e^{x^2+y^2},dA \le e\cdot\text{Area}(D) = e\cdot\pi \lt e^2\pi \qquad \checkmark $$

In fact the sharp upper bound is $e\pi$, but $e^2\pi$ follows immediately from the estimation property.

### Example 3 — Separability

**Compute** $\displaystyle\iint_{[0,1]^2} e^x \cos y,dA$.

$f(x,y) = e^x \cos y = g(x)h(y)$ and $D$ is a rectangle. By separability:

$$ = \left(\int_0^1 e^x,dx\right)!\left(\int_0^1 \cos y,dy\right) = (e - 1)(\sin 1) $$

### Example 4 — Mean Value Theorem Applied

**Find** a point $(x_0, y_0)$ in $D = [0,1]^2$ such that $\iint_D (x + y),dA = (x_0 + y_0)\cdot\text{Area}(D)$.

Compute:

$$ \iint_D (x+y),dA = \int_0^1\int_0^1(x+y),dy,dx = \int_0^1\left(x + \frac{1}{2}\right)dx = 1 $$

$\text{Area}(D) = 1$, so we need $x_0 + y_0 = 1$. Any point on the segment ${x + y = 1} \cap [0,1]^2$ works — e.g., $(x_0, y_0) = (1/2, 1/2)$. _(The MVT guarantees existence; it does not specify uniqueness.)_

## §14 — Failure Modes and Pathological Cases

### 14.1 Linearity Does Not Extend to Infinite Sums

$$ \iint_D \sum_{n=1}^\infty f_n(x,y),dA \ne \sum_{n=1}^\infty \iint_D f_n(x,y),dA \quad \text{in general} $$

Swapping infinite sums and integrals requires **uniform convergence** (or dominated convergence in the Lebesgue sense). This failure is the core obstruction in many analysis problems.

### 14.2 Domain Additivity Requires Measure-Zero Overlap

If $D_1 \cap D_2$ has positive area, the formula $\iint_D f = \iint_{D_1} f + \iint_{D_2} f$ **overcounts** the overlap region. The correct formula is inclusion-exclusion:

$$ \iint_{D_1 \cup D_2} f = \iint_{D_1} f + \iint_{D_2} f - \iint_{D_1 \cap D_2} f $$

### 14.3 Symmetry Requires the Domain to Be Symmetric

$\iint_D x,dA = 0$ _only if $D$ is symmetric about the $y$-axis._ If $D = [0,1]^2$ (not symmetric), then:

$$ \iint_D x,dA = \int_0^1\int_0^1 x,dy,dx = \frac{1}{2} \ne 0 $$

### 14.4 Separability Fails on Non-Rectangular Domains

On the triangle $D = {0 \le x \le 1, 0 \le y \le x}$:

$$ \iint_D xy,dA = \int_0^1 x\int_0^x y,dy,dx = \int_0^1 x\cdot\frac{x^2}{2},dx = \frac{1}{8} $$

But $\left(\int_0^1 x,dx\right)!\left(\int_0^1 y,dy\right) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} \ne \frac{1}{8}$.

## §15 — Measure-Theoretic Perspective

_(For the reader who wants to see behind the curtain.)_

In Lebesgue theory, the double integral is $\int_D f,d(m_1 \times m_2)$ where $m_1 \times m_2$ is the **product measure** on $\mathbb{R}^2$. The properties become:

|Riemann Property|Measure-Theoretic Basis|
|---|---|
|Linearity|Linearity of the Lebesgue integral|
|Domain Additivity|Countable additivity of measure|
|Monotonicity|Monotonicity of the Lebesgue integral|
|Estimation|$m \cdot \mu(D) \le \int_D f \le M \cdot \mu(D)$|
|Area formula|$m_1 \times m_2(D) = \text{Area}(D)$|
|Triangle inequality|$L^1$ norm satisfies $\|f\|_1 = \int|

The MVT is a theorem about continuous functions on compact connected sets — it is _not_ a purely measure-theoretic statement (it uses topology).

**Why this matters for ML.** Deep learning theory increasingly uses **measure theory directly**:

- The **Wasserstein distance** (used in Wasserstein GANs) is defined via an infimum of integrals over coupling measures.
- **Neural ODEs** and **continuous normalizing flows** work with push-forward measures.
- The **Neural Tangent Kernel** convergence proofs use dominated convergence (the measure-theoretic version of swapping limits and integrals).

All of these require the properties above, in their full Lebesgue generality.
