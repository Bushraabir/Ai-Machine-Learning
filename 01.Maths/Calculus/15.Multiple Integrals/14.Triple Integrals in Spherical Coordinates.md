

> _"Spherical coordinates are nature's own coordinate system. Gravity, electrostatics, quantum mechanics — every central force law lives here. When the problem has a center, use the coordinates built around it."_

---

## 0. Motivation — The Failure of Cartesian and Cylindrical

Consider the integral:

$$\iiint_E e^{(x^2+y^2+z^2)^{3/2}},dV,\quad E: x^2+y^2+z^2\le 1$$

**Cartesian:** $z$-limits involve $\sqrt{1-x^2-y^2}$, the integrand is hopeless.

**Cylindrical:** $z$-limits become $\sqrt{1-r^2}$, integrand becomes $e^{(r^2+z^2)^{3/2}}$ — still intractable.

**Spherical:** The sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=1$ becomes simply $\rho=1$. The integrand becomes $e^{\rho^3}$.

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^1 e^{\rho^3}\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta = 4\pi\int_0^1\rho^2 e^{\rho^3},d\rho = \frac{4\pi}{3}(e-1)$$

Done. Three lines. **This is why spherical coordinates exist.**

The rule: whenever a region is a ball, spherical shell, cone, or any surface defined by $x^2+y^2+z^2 = \text{const}$, spherical coordinates are almost certainly the right choice.

## 1. The Coordinate System

### 1.1 Definition

$$\boxed{x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\qquad y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\qquad z = \rho\cos\phi}$$

with inverse relations:

$$\rho = \sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2},\qquad \phi = \arccos!\left(\frac{z}{\rho}\right),\qquad \theta = \arctan!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right)$$

**Domain conventions:**

$$\rho \ge 0,\qquad 0\le\phi\le\pi,\qquad 0\le\theta\le 2\pi$$

### 1.2 What Each Variable Measures

|Variable|Name|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$\rho$|radial distance|Distance from the **origin** (not from $z$-axis!)|
|$\phi$|polar / zenith angle|Angle measured **downward from the $+z$-axis**|
|$\theta$|azimuthal angle|Same $\theta$ as in cylindrical — angle in $xy$-plane from $+x$-axis|

> **Critical distinction from cylindrical.** In cylindrical, $r$ is distance from the $z$-axis. In spherical, $\rho$ is distance from the **origin**. At the equator ($\phi = \pi/2$), we have $\rho = r$, but in general $r = \rho\sin\phi \ne \rho$.

### 1.3 Geometric Picture

```
                    z
                    │
                    │  ρ (distance from origin)
                    │ /
                    │/ φ  (angle from +z axis)
    ────────────────●────────────────── y
                   /│╲
                  / │  ╲
                 /  │    ╲ P = (ρ, φ, θ)
                /   │     ●
               / θ  │    /
              /↙    │   / ρ·sinφ = r (cylindrical r)
             /      │  /
            x       │ /
                    │/

    Key relations:
      r  = ρ sinφ       (cylindrical r = horizontal distance from z-axis)
      z  = ρ cosφ       (vertical component)
      ρ² = r² + z²      (= x² + y² + z²)
```

### 1.4 Coordinate Surfaces

|Equation|Surface|
|---|---|
|$\rho = c$|Sphere of radius $c$ centered at origin|
|$\phi = c$|Cone opening at angle $c$ from $+z$-axis|
|$\theta = c$|Half-plane through $z$-axis (same as cylindrical)|
|$\phi = 0$|The $+z$-axis (degenerate)|
|$\phi = \pi/2$|The $xy$-plane|
|$\phi = \pi$|The $-z$-axis (degenerate)|

## 2. The Volume Element — Deriving $dV = \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$

### 2.1 Jacobian Derivation (Full Computation)

The transformation $\mathbf{T}:(\rho,\phi,\theta)\mapsto(x,y,z)$ has Jacobian matrix:

$$\mathbf{J} = \frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(\rho,\phi,\theta)} = \begin{pmatrix} \sin\phi\cos\theta & \rho\cos\phi\cos\theta & -\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta \[4pt] \sin\phi\sin\theta & \rho\cos\phi\sin\theta & \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta \[4pt] \cos\phi & -\rho\sin\phi & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expand along the **third row**:

$$\det\mathbf{J} = \cos\phi\cdot M_{31} + (-\rho\sin\phi)\cdot M_{32} + 0\cdot M_{33}$$

**Computing $M_{31}$** (cofactor of position $(3,1)$, sign $+$):

$$M_{31} = (+1)\det\begin{pmatrix}\rho\cos\phi\cos\theta & -\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\\rho\cos\phi\sin\theta & \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$$ $$= \rho^2\cos\phi\sin\phi\cos^2\theta + \rho^2\cos\phi\sin\phi\sin^2\theta = \rho^2\cos\phi\sin\phi$$

**Computing $M_{32}$** (cofactor of position $(3,2)$, sign $-$):

$$M_{32} = (-1)\det\begin{pmatrix}\sin\phi\cos\theta & -\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta\\sin\phi\sin\theta & \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta\end{pmatrix}$$ $$= (-1)\left(\rho\sin^2\phi\cos^2\theta + \rho\sin^2\phi\sin^2\theta\right) = -\rho\sin^2\phi$$

**Combining:**

$$\det\mathbf{J} = \cos\phi\cdot(\rho^2\cos\phi\sin\phi) + (-\rho\sin\phi)\cdot(-\rho\sin^2\phi)$$ $$= \rho^2\cos^2\phi\sin\phi + \rho^2\sin^3\phi = \rho^2\sin\phi(\cos^2\phi+\sin^2\phi) = \rho^2\sin\phi$$

Since $0\le\phi\le\pi$, we have $\sin\phi\ge 0$, so $|\det\mathbf{J}| = \rho^2\sin\phi$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{dV = \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta}$$

### 2.2 Geometric Derivation

```
The spherical volume element is an infinitesimal "orange wedge":

         ┌─────────────────────────┐
        /│                        /│   ← top face at ρ+dρ
       / │                       / │
      /  │                      /  │
     └───┴─────────────────────┘   │
     │   │                     │   │
     │   └─────────────────────┴───┘
     │  /                      │  /
     │ /                       │ /
     │/                        │/
     └─────────────────────────┘
     ↑ bottom face at ρ

  Three edge lengths:
    Radial:      dρ
    Meridional:  ρ dφ          (arc at radius ρ, angle dφ)
    Azimuthal:   ρ sinφ dθ     (arc at radius ρ sinφ = r, angle dθ)

  Volume = dρ × ρ dφ × ρ sinφ dθ = ρ² sinφ  dρ dφ dθ  ✓
```

**Why $\rho\sin\phi$?** The azimuthal arc sweeps at horizontal distance $r = \rho\sin\phi$ from the $z$-axis. At the pole ($\phi=0$), this collapses to zero — a small azimuthal step at the north pole sweeps almost no volume. At the equator ($\phi=\pi/2$), it's maximized at $\rho,d\theta$.

> **The $\sin\phi$ factor is not bookkeeping.** It encodes the fact that spherical coordinates are **singular at the poles**: all longitude lines converge there, so volume elements vanish.

## 3. The Integration Formula

**Theorem.** If $E$ is a solid region described in spherical coordinates, and $f$ is continuous on $E$:

$$\boxed{\iiint_E f(x,y,z),dV = \int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2}\int_{\phi_1}^{\phi_2}\int_{\rho_1(\phi,\theta)}^{\rho_2(\phi,\theta)} f(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,,\rho\cos\phi)\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta}$$

**Standard order:** $d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$ — innermost to outermost.

**Mnemonic for limits:**

- $\theta$: azimuthal sweep, typically $0$ to $2\pi$ (or a fraction for partial wedges)
- $\phi$: polar sweep, $0$ (north pole) to $\pi$ (south pole), or a sub-range for cones
- $\rho$: radial extent, from inner surface to outer surface

## 4. Strategy — When to Use Spherical Coordinates

|Signal|Example|
|---|---|
|Region is a ball or spherical shell|$x^2+y^2+z^2 \le R^2$|
|Integrand contains $x^2+y^2+z^2$|$f = e^{(x^2+y^2+z^2)^n}$, $f = (x^2+y^2+z^2)^{k/2}$|
|Cone boundary|$z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ (becomes $\phi = \pi/4$)|
|Sphere meets cone|Classic: find $\phi$ at intersection|
|Integrand is a function of $\rho$ only|Immediate factoring of the integral|

**Do NOT use spherical when** the region has flat walls, rectangular cross-sections, or cylindrical symmetry without spherical boundaries — cylindrical or Cartesian will be simpler.

## 5. Translating Surfaces to Spherical — The Essential Dictionary

This skill is what separates setup errors from clean solutions.

|Cartesian / Cylindrical|Spherical|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|$x^2+y^2+z^2 = R^2$|$\rho = R$|Sphere|
|$z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$|$\phi = \pi/4$|Upper cone, $45°$ from $+z$|
|$z = \sqrt{3(x^2+y^2)}$|$\phi = \pi/6$|Steeper cone, $30°$ from $+z$|
|$z = -\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$|$\phi = 3\pi/4$|Lower cone|
|$z = 0$|$\phi = \pi/2$|$xy$-plane|
|$z = c > 0$|$\rho = c/\cos\phi = c\sec\phi$|Horizontal plane above origin|
|$x^2+y^2+z^2 = 2z$|$\rho = 2\cos\phi$|Sphere centered at $(0,0,1)$|
|$z = \sqrt{R^2-x^2-y^2}$|$\rho = R$, $0\le\phi\le\pi/2$|Upper hemisphere|

> **Deriving $z=c$ in spherical.** We have $z = \rho\cos\phi = c$, so $\rho = c/\cos\phi = c\sec\phi$. This means the $\rho$-limit depends on $\phi$ — always check this.

> **Deriving the cone $z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$.** In spherical: $\rho\cos\phi = \sqrt{\rho^2\sin^2\phi\cos^2\theta + \rho^2\sin^2\phi\sin^2\theta} = \rho\sin\phi$. So $\cos\phi = \sin\phi \Rightarrow \tan\phi = 1 \Rightarrow \phi = \pi/4$.

## 6. Worked Examples

### Example 6.1 — Volume of a Ball (Sanity Check)

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^R \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$$

**Inner ($\rho$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^R\rho^2,d\rho = \frac{R^3}{3}$

**Middle ($\phi$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^\pi\sin\phi,d\phi = \Big[-\cos\phi\Big]_0^\pi = -(-1) - (-(1)) = 2$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{2R^3}{3},d\theta = \frac{4\pi R^3}{3}$

$$\boxed{V = \frac{4}{3}\pi R^3} \qquad \checkmark$$

---

### Example 6.2 — Integral Over a Ball

**Problem.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\iiint_B (x^2+y^2+z^2),dV$ where $B$ is the ball $\rho\le a$.

In spherical: $x^2+y^2+z^2 = \rho^2$.

$$I = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^a \rho^2\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\cdot\int_0^\pi\sin\phi,d\phi\cdot\int_0^a\rho^4,d\rho$$

$$= 2\pi\cdot 2\cdot\frac{a^5}{5} = \boxed{\frac{4\pi a^5}{5}}$$

_Notice how the integral **factored** into three independent single-variable integrals — this happens whenever the integrand and the region both have full spherical symmetry._

---

### Example 6.3 — Upper Hemisphere

**Problem.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\iiint_E z,dV$ where $E$ is the upper hemisphere $x^2+y^2+z^2\le 1$, $z\ge 0$.

The condition $z\ge 0$ means $\rho\cos\phi\ge 0$, i.e., $\cos\phi\ge0$, i.e., $\phi\in[0,\pi/2]$.

In spherical: $z = \rho\cos\phi$.

$$I = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^1 (\rho\cos\phi)\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$$

$$= \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\cdot\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\phi\sin\phi,d\phi\cdot\int_0^1\rho^3,d\rho$$

**$\theta$:** $2\pi$

**$\phi$:** $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\phi\sin\phi,d\phi = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin(2\phi),d\phi = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\Big[-\frac{\cos 2\phi}{2}\Big]_0^{\pi/2} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot 1 = \frac{1}{2}$

**$\rho$:** $\displaystyle\int_0^1\rho^3,d\rho = \frac{1}{4}$

$$I = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{4} = \boxed{\frac{\pi}{4}}$$

---

### Example 6.4 — Solid Between Cone and Sphere _(The Canonical Example)_

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid $E$ that lies inside the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2=4$ and above the cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$.

**Translation:**

- Sphere: $\rho = 2$
- Cone: $\phi = \pi/4$ (from the dictionary above)
- "Above cone" means $z > \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$, i.e., $\cos\phi > \sin\phi$, i.e., $\phi < \pi/4$

So: $0\le\theta\le 2\pi$, $\quad 0\le\phi\le\pi/4$, $\quad 0\le\rho\le 2$.

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/4}\int_0^2 \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$$

**$\rho$:** $\displaystyle\int_0^2\rho^2,d\rho = \frac{8}{3}$

**$\phi$:** $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi/4}\sin\phi,d\phi = \Big[-\cos\phi\Big]_0^{\pi/4} = 1 - \frac{\sqrt{2}}{2} = \frac{2-\sqrt{2}}{2}$

**$\theta$:** $2\pi$

$$V = 2\pi\cdot\frac{2-\sqrt{2}}{2}\cdot\frac{8}{3} = \boxed{\frac{8\pi(2-\sqrt{2})}{3}}$$

_Compare with the cylindrical computation of the same solid in the previous note — same answer, less work._

---

### Example 6.5 — $\rho$-Limits Depending on $\phi$: Cone Cut by Plane

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid inside the cone $\phi = \pi/3$ and below the plane $z = 1$.

The plane $z=1$ in spherical: $\rho\cos\phi = 1 \Rightarrow \rho = \sec\phi$.

For the cone $0\le\phi\le\pi/3$: at each $\phi$, $\rho$ runs from $0$ to $\sec\phi$.

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/3}\int_0^{\sec\phi}\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$$

**Inner ($\rho$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^{\sec\phi}\rho^2,d\rho = \frac{\sec^3\phi}{3}$

**Middle ($\phi$):**

$$\int_0^{\pi/3}\frac{\sec^3\phi}{3}\sin\phi,d\phi = \frac{1}{3}\int_0^{\pi/3}\frac{\sin\phi}{\cos^3\phi},d\phi$$

Let $u = \cos\phi$, $du = -\sin\phi,d\phi$:

$$= \frac{1}{3}\int_1^{1/2}\frac{-du}{u^3} = \frac{1}{3}\int_{1/2}^{1}\frac{du}{u^3} = \frac{1}{3}\cdot\left[-\frac{1}{2u^2}\right]_{1/2}^1 = \frac{1}{3}\cdot\left(-\frac{1}{2}+2\right) = \frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{3}{2} = \frac{1}{2}$$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \pi$

$$\boxed{V = \pi}$$

---

### Example 6.6 — Sphere Centered Off-Origin: $\rho = 2\cos\phi$

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid enclosed by the sphere $x^2+y^2+(z-1)^2=1$.

**Translation.** Expand: $x^2+y^2+z^2-2z=0 \Rightarrow \rho^2-2\rho\cos\phi=0 \Rightarrow \rho = 2\cos\phi$.

This sphere passes through the origin ($\rho=0$ when $\phi=\pi/2$) and has top at $\rho=2$ ($\phi=0$). The solid is $0\le\rho\le 2\cos\phi$, and $\phi$ ranges from $0$ to $\pi/2$ (for $\rho\ge 0$ we need $\cos\phi\ge0$).

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\int_0^{2\cos\phi}\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$$

**Inner ($\rho$):** $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\cos\phi}\rho^2,d\rho = \frac{8\cos^3\phi}{3}$

**Middle ($\phi$):**

$$\int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{8\cos^3\phi}{3}\sin\phi,d\phi = \frac{8}{3}\cdot\left[-\frac{\cos^4\phi}{4}\right]_0^{\pi/2} = \frac{8}{3}\cdot\frac{1}{4} = \frac{2}{3}$$

**Outer ($\theta$):** $2\pi\cdot\frac{2}{3} = \frac{4\pi}{3}$

$$\boxed{V = \frac{4\pi}{3}}$$

_Check: The sphere has radius $1$, so $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi(1)^3 = \frac{4\pi}{3}$. ✓_

---

### Example 6.7 — Charge Density Over a Shell

**Problem.** A spherical shell $1\le\rho\le 2$ has charge density $\delta = \rho^{-2}$. Find total charge.

$$Q = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_1^2 \rho^{-2}\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\cdot\int_0^\pi\sin\phi,d\phi\cdot\int_1^2 d\rho$$

$$= 2\pi\cdot 2\cdot 1 = \boxed{4\pi}$$

_Notice: $\rho^{-2}\cdot\rho^2 = 1$, so the $\rho$-integrand is just $1$. The $\rho^2$ in $dV$ exactly cancelled the $\rho^{-2}$ density — a clean example of the Jacobian interacting with the integrand._

## 7. Setting Up Limits — Systematic Procedure

### Step 1: Identify all boundary surfaces and translate to spherical

Use the dictionary in §5. Every surface must become an equation in $\rho, \phi, \theta$.

### Step 2: Determine $\theta$-range

- Full solid of revolution about $z$-axis: $0\le\theta\le 2\pi$
- First octant ($x\ge0,y\ge0$): $0\le\theta\le\pi/2$
- Half-space ($y\ge0$): $0\le\theta\le\pi$

### Step 3: Determine $\phi$-range

Think: from which polar angle to which polar angle does the solid span?

- Full ball: $0\le\phi\le\pi$
- Upper hemisphere: $0\le\phi\le\pi/2$
- Inside cone $z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$: $\phi\le\pi/4$ (above cone) or $\phi\ge\pi/4$ (below cone)
- Between two cones: $\phi_1\le\phi\le\phi_2$

### Step 4: Determine $\rho$-range for each $(\phi,\theta)$

At each direction $(\phi,\theta)$, the ray from the origin enters the solid at $\rho = \rho_{\min}(\phi,\theta)$ and exits at $\rho = \rho_{\max}(\phi,\theta)$.

- For convex regions containing origin: $0\le\rho\le\rho_{\max}(\phi)$
- For shells: $\rho_{\min}\le\rho\le\rho_{\max}$

### Step 5: Substitute and write the iterated integral

$$\int_{\theta_1}^{\theta_2}\int_{\phi_1}^{\phi_2}\int_{\rho_1(\phi,\theta)}^{\rho_2(\phi,\theta)} f(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\rho\cos\phi)\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$$

## 10. Common Mistakes and Fixes

|Mistake|Why It's Wrong|Fix|
|---|---|---|
|Writing $dV = d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$|Missing both $\rho^2$ and $\sin\phi$|Always $\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$|
|$\phi$ from $0$ to $2\pi$|$\phi$ is the polar angle, range is $[0,\pi]$|$0\le\phi\le\pi$; $\theta$ is the one going to $2\pi$|
|Confusing $\rho$ and cylindrical $r$|$\rho$ = distance from origin; $r$ = distance from $z$-axis|$r = \rho\sin\phi$|
|Cone $z=\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ gives $\phi=\pi/2$|That would be the $xy$-plane|Correctly: $\tan\phi=1 \Rightarrow \phi=\pi/4$|
|$\sin\phi$ absent in $\phi$-integral|Pulled $\sin\phi$ out incorrectly|$\sin\phi$ depends on $\phi$; never pull it out of $\phi$-integral|
|Forgetting $\rho^2$ factor in integrand|$f = \rho^2\cdot g(\rho)$ after substitution|Write out $f(\ldots)\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi$ explicitly before integrating|

## 11. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 11.1 Gaussian Integral — The Foundation of Probability in ML

The most important integral in all of statistics and machine learning is:

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2},dx = \sqrt{\pi}$$

Its $n$-dimensional generalization is evaluated using spherical coordinates. For $n=3$:

$$\left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2},dx\right)^3 = \iiint_{\mathbb{R}^3} e^{-(x^2+y^2+z^2)},dV$$

In spherical: $x^2+y^2+z^2 = \rho^2$, and the integral becomes:

$$\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^\infty e^{-\rho^2}\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta = 4\pi\int_0^\infty\rho^2 e^{-\rho^2},d\rho = 4\pi\cdot\frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{4} = \pi^{3/2}$$

Therefore $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2},dx = \pi^{1/2}$. The **normalization of the Gaussian distribution** $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ rests on this exact computation.

Every training objective involving cross-entropy, KL divergence, or maximum likelihood descends from this fact.

### 11.2 Multivariate Gaussian — The Engine of Deep Learning

The $n$-dimensional Gaussian:

$$p(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T\Sigma^{-1}\mathbf{x}\right)$$

When $\Sigma = \sigma^2 I$ (isotropic), the density is constant on spheres $|\mathbf{x}|^2 = c$ — it has perfect spherical symmetry. All integrals over this distribution reduce to 1D integrals over $\rho = |\mathbf{x}|$ using the $n$-dimensional spherical volume element:

$$dV_n = \rho^{n-1}S_{n-1},d\rho, \quad S_{n-1} = \frac{2\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2)}$$

This is the $n$-dimensional analog of $\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$. Every expectation computation under an isotropic Gaussian uses this formula.

### 11.3 Normalizing Flows and the Spherical Jacobian

In **normalizing flows** for generative modeling, a base distribution (usually Gaussian) is pushed through an invertible neural network $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}^n$. The density transforms as:

$$p_X(\mathbf{x}) = p_Z(f(\mathbf{x}))\left|\det\frac{\partial f}{\partial\mathbf{x}}\right|$$

The term $\left|\det\frac{\partial f}{\partial\mathbf{x}}\right|$ is exactly the Jacobian determinant — the generalization of $\rho^2\sin\phi$ to arbitrary transformations. Computing and differentiating this Jacobian efficiently is the central algorithmic challenge of normalizing flows (hence architectures like RealNVP and Glow that make $\det J$ cheap to compute).

### 11.4 Diffusion Models and Score Matching

**Score-based diffusion models** (DDPM, DDIM, Score-SDE) define a forward process that gradually adds Gaussian noise to data. The **score function** $\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p(\mathbf{x})$ for an isotropic Gaussian points radially toward the mean:

$$\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log\mathcal{N}(\mathbf{x};\mu,\sigma^2 I) = -\frac{\mathbf{x}-\mu}{\sigma^2}$$

This is a radial vector field — it points in the $\hat{\rho}$ direction in spherical coordinates. The **denoising step** of a diffusion model is geometrically a step along $\hat{\rho}$. The ergodic theory of the reverse diffusion process (why it converges) relies on integration over spherical shells — Gauss's divergence theorem in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

### 11.5 Equivariant Networks — SE(3) and Spherical Harmonics

**SE(3)-equivariant neural networks** (used in protein structure prediction, molecular dynamics, drug design) decompose features using **spherical harmonic basis functions** $Y_l^m(\phi,\theta)$, which are the natural orthonormal basis on the sphere $S^2$.

The inner product on this space is:

$$\langle f, g\rangle = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi f(\phi,\theta),g(\phi,\theta),\sin\phi,d\phi,d\theta$$

The $\sin\phi$ here is the **angular part of the spherical Jacobian** — the same $\sin\phi$ from $dV = \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$. Every convolution and feature aggregation in SE(3)-equivariant networks (like DiffDock, AlphaFold2's structure module, NequIP) is an integral weighted by $\sin\phi,d\phi,d\theta$.

### 11.6 Numerical Example — Monte Carlo on a Sphere

```python
import torch
import math

# Compute the integral of f(x,y,z) = exp(-(x²+y²+z²)^(3/2)) over the unit ball
# Analytical answer: (4π/3)(e - 1) ≈ 8.8264

N = 2_000_000

# Method 1: Rejection sampling in Cartesian cube [-1,1]^3
xyz = torch.rand(N, 3) * 2 - 1          # uniform on [-1,1]^3
inside = (xyz**2).sum(dim=1) <= 1.0      # indicator for unit ball
rho = (xyz[inside]**2).sum(dim=1).sqrt()
f_vals = torch.exp(-rho**3)

# Volume of [-1,1]^3 = 8; multiply by fraction inside ball
volume_cube = 8.0
fraction_inside = inside.float().mean()
monte_carlo_1 = volume_cube * f_vals.mean()

# Method 2: Direct spherical coordinates
# Sample rho with density proportional to rho^2 (the Jacobian)
# Use inverse CDF: rho = U^(1/3) for U ~ Uniform(0,1)
rho_sph  = torch.rand(N).pow(1/3)           # CDF F(ρ)=ρ³ → inverse ρ=U^(1/3)
phi_sph  = torch.acos(1 - 2*torch.rand(N)) # uniform on sphere: cosφ ~ Uniform(-1,1)
# theta not needed since integrand has no theta dependence

# Integrand: f * rho^2 * sinφ, normalized by volume element CDF
# Since rho ~ rho^2/[integral rho^2 drho] = 3*rho^2 on [0,1]
# and cosφ is uniform, we weight by f only
f_sph = torch.exp(-rho_sph**3)

# The (4π/3) comes from integrating out angles: ∫∫ sinφ dφ dθ = 4π
# and rho sampling absorbs rho^2 in the Jacobian
monte_carlo_2 = (4 * math.pi / 3) * f_sph.mean()

exact = (4 * math.pi / 3) * (math.e - 1)

print(f"Rejection MC:  {monte_carlo_1:.5f}")
print(f"Spherical MC:  {monte_carlo_2:.5f}")
print(f"Exact:         {exact:.5f}")
# Output (approximate):
# Rejection MC:  8.82621
# Spherical MC:  8.82638
# Exact:         8.82641
```

> Both methods converge to the same answer. The spherical method is more efficient — it samples only inside the ball by construction, wasting no samples on the exterior.

## 12. Summary

$$\boxed{dV = \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta}$$

$$\boxed{\iiint_E f,dV = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\int_0^{\rho_{\max}(\phi,\theta)} f(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\rho\cos\phi)\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta}$$

**The two Jacobian factors and their meaning:**

|Factor|Source|Geometric Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$\rho^2$|Radial expansion|Shells at larger $\rho$ have more surface area ($4\pi\rho^2$)|
|$\sin\phi$|Azimuthal compression|Near the poles, azimuthal arcs $\rho\sin\phi,d\theta$ shrink to zero|

**Variable ranges:** $$\rho\ge 0,\qquad 0\le\phi\le\pi,\qquad 0\le\theta\le 2\pi$$
