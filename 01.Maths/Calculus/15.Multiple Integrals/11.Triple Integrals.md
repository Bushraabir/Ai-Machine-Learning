## 0. Why Triple Integrals Exist

Before jumping to formalism, let's be honest about what's happening.

A **double integral** $\iint_D f(x,y),dA$ sweeps a function over a 2-dimensional region — it accumulates area-weighted mass, flux, probability, etc.

A **triple integral** $\iiint_E f(x,y,z),dV$ does the same over a **3-dimensional solid region** $E \subset \mathbb{R}^3$. The natural physical pictures are:

|$f(x,y,z)$|$\iiint_E f,dV$ means|
|---|---|
|$1$|Volume of $E$|
|$\rho(x,y,z)$ (density)|Total mass|
|$\rho(x,y,z)\cdot x$|Moment about the $yz$-plane|
|Joint probability density|Probability of event in $E$|
|Temperature field|Average temperature (after dividing by volume)|

In machine learning, every one of these shows up — just in dimensions $n \gg 3$.

## 1. The Definition — Building From First Principles

### 1.1 Riemann Sum Construction

Let $E \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ be a bounded solid and $f: E \to \mathbb{R}$ be bounded. Enclose $E$ in a box:

$$B = [a_1,b_1]\times[a_2,b_2]\times[a_3,b_3]$$

Partition each interval: $\Delta x_i, \Delta y_j, \Delta z_k$ with $l, m, n$ subintervals respectively. This creates $l \cdot m \cdot n$ sub-boxes $B_{ijk}$ of volume:

$$\Delta V_{ijk} = \Delta x_i ,\Delta y_j ,\Delta z_k$$

Pick sample points $(x_{ijk}^_, y_{ijk}^_, z_{ijk}^*)$ in each $B_{ijk}$. The **Riemann sum** is:

$$S_{lmn} = \sum_{i=1}^{l}\sum_{j=1}^{m}\sum_{k=1}^{n} f(x_{ijk}^_, y_{ijk}^_, z_{ijk}^*),\Delta V_{ijk}$$

**Definition.** The **triple integral** of $f$ over $E$ is:

$$\boxed{\iiint_E f(x,y,z),dV = \lim_{l,m,n\to\infty} S_{lmn}}$$

provided the limit exists and is the same for all choices of sample points.

> **Existence.** If $f$ is continuous on a bounded solid $E$ whose boundary has volume zero (a _measurable_ set), then the triple integral exists. (Stewart, §15.6; this follows from the general theory of Riemann integration on $\mathbb{R}^n$.)

## 2. Fubini's Theorem — The Computational Engine

Computing triple integrals directly from the Riemann sum definition is impractical. Fubini's theorem reduces the problem to three iterated single-variable integrals.

### 2.1 Statement (Box Case)

**Theorem (Fubini).** Let $B = [a,b]\times[c,d]\times[r,s]$ and $f$ continuous on $B$. Then:

$$\iiint_B f(x,y,z),dV = \int_r^s\int_c^d\int_a^b f(x,y,z),dx,dy,dz$$

and all $3! = 6$ orderings of integration give the same value.

> **Key insight.** The iterated integral is evaluated _inside-out_: fix $y,z$ and integrate over $x$ first (treating $y,z$ as constants), then integrate the result over $y$ (treating $z$ as constant), then over $z$.

### 2.2 The General Case — Type I, II, III Regions

Most interesting domains are not boxes. We classify them:

**Type I (Project onto $xy$-plane):**

$$E = {(x,y,z) \mid (x,y)\in D,; u_1(x,y)\le z\le u_2(x,y)}$$

$$\iiint_E f,dV = \iint_D\left[\int_{u_1(x,y)}^{u_2(x,y)} f(x,y,z),dz\right]dA$$

**Type II (Project onto $xz$-plane):**

$$E = {(x,y,z)\mid (x,z)\in D,; u_1(x,z)\le y \le u_2(x,z)}$$

**Type III (Project onto $yz$-plane):**

$$E = {(x,y,z)\mid (y,z)\in D,; u_1(y,z)\le x \le u_2(y,z)}$$

> **Heuristic for choosing type.** Draw the solid, look at it from three directions, and choose the projection that gives the simplest boundary functions $u_1, u_2$.

## 3. Worked Examples

### Example 3.1 — Volume of a Tetrahedron

**Problem.** Find the volume of the tetrahedron $E$ with vertices at $(0,0,0)$, $(1,0,0)$, $(0,1,0)$, $(0,0,1)$.

**Solution.** The bounding plane through the non-origin vertices has equation:

$$x + y + z = 1 \implies z \le 1 - x - y$$

The region is:

$$E = {(x,y,z) \mid 0\le x\le 1,; 0\le y\le 1-x,; 0\le z\le 1-x-y}$$

$$V = \iiint_E 1,dV = \int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}\int_0^{1-x-y}dz,dy,dx$$

**Inner integral** (over $z$):

$$\int_0^{1-x-y}dz = (1-x-y)$$

**Middle integral** (over $y$):

$$\int_0^{1-x}(1-x-y),dy = \left[(1-x)y - \frac{y^2}{2}\right]_0^{1-x} = (1-x)^2 - \frac{(1-x)^2}{2} = \frac{(1-x)^2}{2}$$

**Outer integral** (over $x$):

$$\int_0^1 \frac{(1-x)^2}{2},dx = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\left[-\frac{(1-x)^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{3} = \boxed{\frac{1}{6}}$$

_Check: The standard formula for a tetrahedron with edge vertices at unit distance from origin gives $V = \frac{1}{6}(1)(1)(1) = \frac{1}{6}$. ✓_

---

### Example 3.2 — Mass with Variable Density

**Problem.** Find the mass of the rectangular box $B = [0,2]\times[0,3]\times[0,1]$ with density $\rho(x,y,z) = xy^2z$.

$$M = \iiint_B xy^2z,dV = \int_0^1\int_0^3\int_0^2 xy^2z,dx,dy,dz$$

**Inner:** $$\int_0^2 xy^2z,dx = y^2z\cdot\frac{x^2}{2}\Big|_0^2 = 2y^2z$$

**Middle:** $$\int_0^3 2y^2z,dy = 2z\cdot\frac{y^3}{3}\Big|_0^3 = 2z\cdot 9 = 18z$$

**Outer:** $$\int_0^1 18z,dz = 18\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \boxed{9}$$

---

### Example 3.3 — Changing Order of Integration

**Problem.** Evaluate $\displaystyle\int_0^1\int_0^1\int_y^1 \cos(z^2),dz,dy,dx$ by changing the order.

**Observation.** $\int\cos(z^2),dz$ has no closed form (Fresnel integral). Must change order.

**Original limits:** $0\le x\le 1$, $0\le y\le 1$, $y\le z\le 1$.

The region: $0\le y\le z\le 1$, $0\le x\le 1$.

**New order** ($dx,dy,dz$):

$$\int_0^1\int_0^z\int_0^1 \cos(z^2),dx,dy,dz = \int_0^1\int_0^z \cos(z^2),dy,dz = \int_0^1 z\cos(z^2),dz$$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\sin(z^2)\Big|_0^1 = \boxed{\frac{\sin 1}{2}}$$

## 4. Change of Variables — The Jacobian

For complex regions, the key tool is a coordinate substitution. Suppose $(x,y,z) = \mathbf{T}(u,v,w)$ is a smooth, invertible transformation. Define the **Jacobian determinant**:

$$\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(u,v,w)} = \begin{vmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial v} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial w} \ \frac{\partial y}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial v} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial w} \ \frac{\partial z}{\partial u} & \frac{\partial z}{\partial v} & \frac{\partial z}{\partial w} \end{vmatrix}$$

**Change of Variables Theorem:**

$$\iiint_E f(x,y,z),dV = \iiint_{E^*} f(\mathbf{T}(u,v,w))\left|\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(u,v,w)}\right|du,dv,dw$$

> The Jacobian $\left|\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(u,v,w)}\right|$ measures how the transformation _stretches_ or _compresses_ volume locally. It's the 3D generalization of the substitution factor $|dx/du|$ in single-variable calculus.

## 5. Cylindrical Coordinates

### 5.1 Definition

$$x = r\cos\theta,\quad y = r\sin\theta,\quad z = z$$ $$r \ge 0,\quad 0 \le \theta \le 2\pi,\quad z\in\mathbb{R}$$

**Jacobian:**

$$\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(r,\theta,z)} = \begin{vmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta & 0 \ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{vmatrix} = r$$

Therefore:

$$\iiint_E f,dV = \iiint_{E^*} f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta,z)\cdot r,dr,d\theta,dz$$

> **When to use.** Any time the region $E$ or the integrand involves $x^2 + y^2$ (i.e., circular cross-sections).

### 5.2 Visualization — Cylindrical Volume Element

```
        z
        |
        |    dz
        |  /----\
        | /  dV  \
        |/         \
        *-----r------>
       / \
      / dθ \
     /       \
    r·dθ is the arc length

dV = r · dr · dθ · dz
```

The factor of $r$ comes from the arc $r,d\theta$: at radius $r$, a small angle $d\theta$ sweeps an arc of length $r,d\theta$, not just $d\theta$.

### 5.3 Example — Volume of a Cone Inside a Sphere

**Problem.** Find the volume of the solid $E$ that lies inside the sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2 = 4$ and above the cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$.

In cylindrical: sphere becomes $r^2 + z^2 = 4 \Rightarrow z = \sqrt{4-r^2}$; cone becomes $z = r$.

Intersection: $r = \sqrt{4-r^2} \Rightarrow r^2 = 2 \Rightarrow r = \sqrt{2}$.

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\sqrt{2}}\int_r^{\sqrt{4-r^2}} r,dz,dr,d\theta$$

$$= 2\pi\int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r\left(\sqrt{4-r^2} - r\right)dr$$

$$= 2\pi\left[\int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r\sqrt{4-r^2},dr - \int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r^2,dr\right]$$

First integral: let $u = 4-r^2$, $du = -2r,dr$: $$\int_0^{\sqrt{2}}r\sqrt{4-r^2},dr = -\frac{1}{2}\int_4^2 \sqrt{u},du = \frac{1}{2}\int_2^4\sqrt{u},du = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{2}{3}(8-2\sqrt{2}) = \frac{8-2\sqrt{2}}{3}$$

Second integral: $$\int_0^{\sqrt{2}} r^2,dr = \frac{r^3}{3}\Big|_0^{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}$$

$$V = 2\pi\left[\frac{8-2\sqrt{2}}{3} - \frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}\right] = 2\pi\cdot\frac{8-4\sqrt{2}}{3} = \boxed{\frac{8\pi(2-\sqrt{2})}{3}}$$

## 6. Spherical Coordinates

### 6.1 Definition

$$x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,\quad y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,\quad z = \rho\cos\phi$$

$$\rho \ge 0,\quad 0\le\phi\le\pi\quad(\text{polar angle from }+z),\quad 0\le\theta\le 2\pi$$

Note: $\rho^2 = x^2+y^2+z^2$, and $r = \rho\sin\phi$ (cylindrical $r$).

**Jacobian:** (computed by expanding the $3\times 3$ determinant)

$$\frac{\partial(x,y,z)}{\partial(\rho,\phi,\theta)} = \rho^2\sin\phi$$

$$\boxed{\iiint_E f,dV = \iiint_{E^*} f(\rho\sin\phi\cos\theta,,\rho\sin\phi\sin\theta,,\rho\cos\phi)\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta}$$

> **Derivation of Jacobian.** The volume element $dV = \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$ can be understood geometrically: $d\rho$ is the radial thickness; $\rho,d\phi$ is the arc in the meridional direction; $\rho\sin\phi,d\theta$ is the arc in the azimuthal direction. Their product gives the volume of the infinitesimal "orange slice" wedge.

### 6.2 Visualization — Spherical Volume Element

```
                  z
                  |  / ρ
                  | /
                  |/ φ
    ——————————————*—————————— y
                 /|\
                / | \
               /  |  \
              x   |
                  
    Volume element:
    dV = (dρ) × (ρ dφ) × (ρ sinφ dθ)
       = ρ² sinφ  dρ dφ dθ

    Think: shell thickness × meridian arc × latitude arc
```

### 6.3 Example — Volume of a Sphere

$$V = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi}\int_0^R \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$$

$$= \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta\cdot\int_0^{\pi}\sin\phi,d\phi\cdot\int_0^R \rho^2,d\rho = 2\pi\cdot 2\cdot\frac{R^3}{3} = \boxed{\frac{4\pi R^3}{3}}$$

This is both a sanity check and a beautiful application of the theory. ✓

### 6.4 Example — Charge Distribution

**Problem.** A solid ball of radius 1 has charge density $\rho(x,y,z) = e^{-(x^2+y^2+z^2)^{3/2}}$. Find the total charge.

In spherical coordinates: $x^2+y^2+z^2 = \rho^2$, so $\rho(x,y,z) = e^{-\rho^3}$.

$$Q = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi\int_0^1 e^{-\rho^3}\cdot\rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta = 4\pi\int_0^1\rho^2 e^{-\rho^3},d\rho$$

Let $u = \rho^3$, $du = 3\rho^2,d\rho$:

$$Q = 4\pi\cdot\frac{1}{3}\int_0^1 e^{-u},du = \frac{4\pi}{3}\left(1-e^{-1}\right)$$

## 7. Applications

### 7.1 Mass and Center of Mass

Given solid $E$ with density $\rho(x,y,z)$:

$$M = \iiint_E \rho,dV$$

$$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{M}\iiint_E x\rho,dV,\quad \bar{y} = \frac{1}{M}\iiint_E y\rho,dV,\quad \bar{z} = \frac{1}{M}\iiint_E z\rho,dV$$

### 7.2 Moments of Inertia

About the $z$-axis:

$$I_z = \iiint_E (x^2+y^2)\rho(x,y,z),dV$$

In cylindrical: $x^2+y^2 = r^2$, which simplifies this considerably.

### 7.3 Average Value

$$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{V(E)}\iiint_E f(x,y,z),dV$$

## 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

> _This is not a digression — it is the point._

### 8.1 High-Dimensional Integration is the Core Problem of ML

In machine learning, we almost never have 3 variables. We have $n$ variables where $n$ can be in the hundreds of thousands. But **every concept below is a direct generalization of triple integrals.**

**Bayesian inference.** The posterior distribution requires computing:

$$p(\theta\mid\mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D}\mid\theta),p(\theta)}{\int_{\Theta} p(\mathcal{D}\mid\theta),p(\theta),d\theta}$$

The denominator is an integral over the _entire parameter space_ $\Theta \subset \mathbb{R}^n$. For a neural network with $10^7$ parameters, this is a $10^7$-dimensional integral — a triple integral is the toy case.

**Expected loss.** The theoretical risk is:

$$\mathcal{R}(f) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y)\sim p}[\ell(f(x),y)] = \int_{\mathcal{X}\times\mathcal{Y}} \ell(f(x),y),p(x,y),dx,dy$$

This is a multidimensional integral over the data distribution.

### 8.2 Probability Distributions Over $\mathbb{R}^n$

The triple integral $\iiint_E f,dV$ with $f$ a density function and $\iiint_{\mathbb{R}^3} f,dV = 1$ gives a **3D probability distribution** — a joint distribution $p(x_1, x_2, x_3)$.

The multivariate Gaussian in $n$ dimensions:

$$p(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}}\exp\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\mu)^T\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\mu)\right)$$

is the $n$-dimensional analog. The normalization constant is verified by a generalization of the Gaussian integral, which itself relies on the Jacobian of spherical coordinates in $n$ dimensions.

### 8.3 Monte Carlo Integration — Approximating the Triple Integral

Direct numerical integration (using a Riemann sum grid) becomes infeasible for high dimensions — this is the **curse of dimensionality**: the number of grid points grows as $N^n$.

**Monte Carlo integration** sidesteps this. To estimate $\iiint_E f,dV$:

1. Sample $\mathbf{x}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{x}_N \sim \text{Uniform}(E)$
2. Approximate:

$$\iiint_E f,dV \approx V(E)\cdot\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f(\mathbf{x}_i)$$

The error scales as $\mathcal{O}(1/\sqrt{N})$ **regardless of dimension** — a dramatic improvement over quadrature.

**This is the engine behind:**

- MCMC sampling (Metropolis-Hastings, HMC)
- Variational inference
- Reinforcement learning (policy gradient methods)
- Generative models (VAE, diffusion models)

### 8.4 The Jacobian in Deep Learning — Backpropagation

The Jacobian matrix $\mathbf{J} = \frac{\partial(y_1,\ldots,y_m)}{\partial(x_1,\ldots,x_n)}$ of a change-of-variables transformation generalizes the scalar Jacobian determinant in integration theory.

In neural networks:

- **Backpropagation** computes Jacobian-vector products $\mathbf{J}^T \mathbf{v}$ via the chain rule
- **Normalizing flows** in generative modeling use the exact formula:

$$p_X(\mathbf{x}) = p_Z(f(\mathbf{x}))\left|\det\frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\right|$$

This is precisely the change-of-variables formula for probability densities — the $n$-dimensional generalization of $p_X(x) = p_Z(g(x))|g'(x)|$ and of the Jacobian determinant in §4.

> **Punchline.** Every time a normalizing flow pushes a Gaussian through a sequence of invertible transformations to model a complex data distribution, it is computing Jacobians and change-of-variables in high-dimensional space. The theory you're building here is foundational.

### 8.5 Gaussian Processes and Kernel Methods

The mean and covariance of a Gaussian process are defined as integrals over the input space:

$$\mu(x) = \int f(x),p(f),\delta f, \qquad k(x,x') = \int (f(x)-\mu(x))(f(x')-\mu(x')),p(f),\delta f$$

These are functional integrals — the infinite-dimensional limit of multidimensional integrals.

### 8.6 Numerical Integration in Practice

Modern deep learning frameworks (PyTorch, JAX) have built-in tools for computing approximate integrals:

```python
# Monte Carlo estimate of E[f(x)] where x ~ N(0,I) in R^3
import torch

N = 100_000
x = torch.randn(N, 3)          # sample from 3D Gaussian
f_vals = x[:, 0]**2 + x[:, 1]**2 + x[:, 2]**2  # f(x,y,z) = x²+y²+z²
print(f_vals.mean())  # should be ≈ 3.0 (since E[x²+y²+z²] = 3)
```

This computes $\iiint_{\mathbb{R}^3} (x^2+y^2+z^2)\cdot\frac{1}{(2\pi)^{3/2}}e^{-(x^2+y^2+z^2)/2},dV$ — analytically exactly 3, numerically approximated by Monte Carlo.

## 10. A Personal Note on Intuition

The transition from double to triple integrals is philosophically straightforward: one more variable, one more nested integration. The real technical content lives in:

1. **Correctly setting up limits** — this requires genuinely understanding the geometry of the region $E$ in 3D.
2. **Choosing coordinates wisely** — a naive Cartesian setup for a sphere leads to nightmarish square roots; spherical coordinates make it trivial.
3. **The Jacobian is non-optional** — it is not a bookkeeping device. It is a _theorem_ about how volume transforms under smooth maps, and it has deep consequences in differential geometry, physics (general relativity), and machine learning (normalizing flows).

_When in doubt, draw the region. Slice it. Label the bounding surfaces. The limits will follow from the geometry._
