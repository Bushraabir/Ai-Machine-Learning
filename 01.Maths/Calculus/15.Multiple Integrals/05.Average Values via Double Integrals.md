

> _"The average is the single number that, spread uniformly over the region, produces the same total. Everything else is bookkeeping."_

---

## § 0 — Motivation: From 1D to 2D

Recall the average value of a function $f(x)$ on $[a,b]$ from single-variable calculus:

$$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{b-a} \int_a^b f(x), dx$$

The denominator $b-a$ is the **measure** (length) of the domain. The integral is the **total accumulation**. Their ratio is the average.

In two dimensions, nothing changes philosophically. The domain now has **area** instead of length, and we integrate over a **region** $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ instead of an interval. The formula becomes:

$$\boxed{f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{A(D)} \iint_D f(x,y), dA}$$

where $A(D) = \iint_D dA$ is the area of $D$.

> **Intuition:** Imagine pouring water whose depth at $(x,y)$ is $f(x,y)$ into a flat-bottomed container of the same footprint $D$. The water redistributes to a uniform depth $f_{\text{avg}}$. The total volume is conserved:
> 
> $$f_{\text{avg}} \cdot A(D) = \iint_D f(x,y), dA$$

---

## § 1 — Formal Definition

**Definition.** Let $f$ be integrable on a bounded region $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ with area $A(D) > 0$. The **average value** of $f$ over $D$ is:

$$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{A(D)} \iint_D f(x,y), dA$$

**Remarks:**

- $A(D)$ is itself a double integral: $A(D) = \iint_D 1, dA$.
- If $f$ is continuous and $D$ is connected, the **Mean Value Theorem for Double Integrals** guarantees existence of a point $(x_0, y_0) \in D$ with $f(x_0, y_0) = f_{\text{avg}}$ (see § 4).
- The definition extends immediately to $\mathbb{R}^n$: replace area with $n$-dimensional volume.

---

## § 2 — Mean Value Theorem for Double Integrals

### Theorem

> Let $f$ be **continuous** on a closed, bounded, **connected** region $D$. Then there exists at least one point $(x_0, y_0) \in D$ such that:
> 
> $$f(x_0, y_0) = \frac{1}{A(D)} \iint_D f(x,y), dA = f_{\text{avg}}$$

### Proof

Since $f$ is continuous on compact $D$, it attains its minimum $m$ and maximum $M$:

$$m \le f(x,y) \le M \quad \forall, (x,y) \in D$$

Integrating over $D$:

$$m \cdot A(D) \le \iint_D f, dA \le M \cdot A(D)$$

Dividing by $A(D)$:

$$m \le \underbrace{\frac{1}{A(D)} \iint_D f, dA}_{f_{\text{avg}}} \le M$$

So $f_{\text{avg}} \in [m, M]$. Since $f$ is continuous on connected $D$, by the **Intermediate Value Theorem**, $f$ takes every value between $m$ and $M$. In particular, there exists $(x_0, y_0) \in D$ with $f(x_0, y_0) = f_{\text{avg}}$. $\blacksquare$

> **Note:** The MVT guarantees _existence_ of such a point, but not uniqueness. There may be many such points, or (for non-connected $D$) none at all.

## § 3 — Computing Average Values: Full Examples

### Example 1 — Average of a polynomial over a rectangle

**Problem.** Find the average value of $f(x,y) = x^2 y + 3y^2$ over $R = [0,2] \times [0,1]$.

**Step 1: Area.**

$$A(R) = (2-0)(1-0) = 2$$

**Step 2: Double integral.**

$$\iint_R (x^2 y + 3y^2), dA = \int_0^2 \int_0^1 (x^2 y + 3y^2), dy, dx$$

Inner integral ($x$ fixed):

$$\int_0^1 (x^2 y + 3y^2), dy = \left[\frac{x^2 y^2}{2} + y^3\right]_0^1 = \frac{x^2}{2} + 1$$

Outer integral:

$$\int_0^2 \left(\frac{x^2}{2} + 1\right) dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{6} + x\right]_0^2 = \frac{8}{6} + 2 = \frac{4}{3} + 2 = \frac{10}{3}$$

**Step 3: Average.**

$$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{10}{3} = \frac{5}{3}$$

---

### Example 2 — Average temperature over a triangular region

**Problem.** The temperature at $(x,y)$ is $T(x,y) = 4x + 2y$ (in °C). Find the average temperature over the triangle $D$ with vertices $(0,0)$, $(2,0)$, $(0,4)$.

**Step 1: Describe $D$.**

The hypotenuse has equation $\frac{x}{2} + \frac{y}{4} = 1$, i.e., $y = 4 - 2x$. So:

$$D = {(x,y) : 0 \le x \le 2,; 0 \le y \le 4 - 2x}$$

**Step 2: Area.**

$$A(D) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \text{base} \cdot \text{height} = \frac{1}{2}(2)(4) = 4$$

(Or via integration: $\int_0^2 (4-2x), dx = [4x - x^2]_0^2 = 4$.)

**Step 3: Double integral.**

$$\iint_D (4x + 2y), dA = \int_0^2 \int_0^{4-2x} (4x + 2y), dy, dx$$

Inner integral:

$$\int_0^{4-2x} (4x + 2y), dy = \left[4xy + y^2\right]_0^{4-2x} = 4x(4-2x) + (4-2x)^2$$

$$= (4-2x)\left[4x + (4-2x)\right] = (4-2x)(4+2x) = 16 - 4x^2$$

Outer integral:

$$\int_0^2 (16 - 4x^2), dx = \left[16x - \frac{4x^3}{3}\right]_0^2 = 32 - \frac{32}{3} = \frac{64}{3}$$

**Step 4: Average.**

$$T_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{64}{3} = \frac{16}{3} \approx 5.33^\circ\text{C}$$

---

### Example 3 — Average over a disk (polar coordinates)

**Problem.** Find the average value of $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ over the disk $D: x^2 + y^2 \le R^2$.

**Step 1: Area.**

$$A(D) = \pi R^2$$

**Step 2: Switch to polar.** $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$, $dA = r, dr, d\theta$.

$$\iint_D (x^2 + y^2), dA = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^R r^2 \cdot r, dr, d\theta = \int_0^{2\pi} d\theta \int_0^R r^3, dr$$

$$= 2\pi \cdot \frac{R^4}{4} = \frac{\pi R^4}{2}$$

**Step 3: Average.**

$$f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{\pi R^2} \cdot \frac{\pi R^4}{2} = \frac{R^2}{2}$$

> **Sanity check:** $f = r^2$ ranges from $0$ (center) to $R^2$ (boundary). The average $R^2/2$ lies strictly between them. ✓
> 
> **Geometric insight:** The average is NOT $R^2/2$ because of a naive "midpoint" argument — it is $R^2/2$ because points near the boundary carry more area weight (circumference $2\pi r$ grows with $r$), pulling the average above the naive midpoint $R^2/2$... wait, here the naive midpoint _is_ $R^2/2$. But in general, the area-weighted average shifts the answer. For $f = r$ over the disk: $f_{\text{avg}} = 2R/3 \ne R/2$ — the boundary-heavy weighting pulls the average toward the rim.

### 4.2 — Locating the Mean Value Point

For Example 2 ($T = 4x + 2y$ over the triangle), the average was $16/3$. The MVT guarantees a point with $4x_0 + 2y_0 = 16/3$ inside $D$. This is a line segment; any point on it inside $D$ works. The theorem asserts existence — it does not pinpoint a unique location.

### 4.3 — Effect of Region Shape on the Average

For $f(x,y) = x$ (temperature increasing left-to-right):

|Region $D$|$A(D)$|$\iint x, dA$|$f_{\text{avg}}$|
|---|---|---|---|
|Square $[0,1]^2$|$1$|$1/2$|$1/2$|
|Triangle: vertices $(0,0),(1,0),(0,1)$|$1/2$|$1/6$|$1/3$|
|Triangle: vertices $(0,0),(1,0),(1,1)$|$1/2$|$5/12$|$5/6$|

The shape of $D$ shifts the average by changing how much area lies near large vs. small values of $f$ — the geometric analogue of a weighted average.

## § 5 — Weighted Average and Probability Density

When the region carries a **weight function** $w(x,y) \ge 0$ (a probability density, mass density, etc.), the weighted average is:

$$\langle f \rangle_w = \frac{\iint_D f(x,y), w(x,y), dA}{\iint_D w(x,y), dA}$$

The standard average is the special case $w \equiv 1$.

**Center of mass** is a weighted average of position:

$$\bar{x} = \frac{\iint_D x, \rho(x,y), dA}{\iint_D \rho(x,y), dA}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{\iint_D y, \rho(x,y), dA}{\iint_D \rho(x,y), dA}$$

where $\rho$ is the surface mass density.

## § 6 — Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 6.1 — Expected Value IS a Weighted Average Integral

The expected value of a function $\phi(x,y)$ under a joint density $p(x,y)$:

$$\mathbb{E}_{(X,Y) \sim p}[\phi(X,Y)] = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} \phi(x,y), p(x,y), dx, dy$$

is precisely a **weighted average** with weight $w = p$. The denominator $\iint p, dA = 1$ (probability axiom). Every time you compute an expectation in ML, you are computing the weighted average value of a function over its domain.

### 6.2 — Loss Function as Average Loss

The empirical risk (training loss) in ML is a discrete average:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{i=1}^N \ell(\hat{y}_i, y_i)$$

Its continuous population analogue is exactly the average value integral:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \iint \ell(f_\theta(x), y), p(x, y), dx, dy$$

The difference between training loss (sample average) and population loss (integral average) is **generalization error** — and bounding it requires understanding how well discrete sums approximate double integrals (uniform laws of large numbers, VC theory).

### 6.3 — Batch Normalization

Batch normalization computes the mean and variance of activations _over the spatial dimensions_ of a feature map. For a $H \times W$ feature map $F$:

$$\mu = \frac{1}{HW} \sum_{i=1}^H \sum_{j=1}^W F_{ij}$$

This is a **discrete double average** — the exact discretization of $\frac{1}{A} \iint F(x,y), dA$ over the spatial domain. The continuous formulation is used when deriving the theory of spatial statistics and analyzing convolutional architectures.

### 6.4 — Gaussian Process Posterior Mean

In a Gaussian Process, the posterior mean function is:

$$\mu^*(x) = \int k(x, x'), \alpha(x'), dx'$$

where $k$ is the kernel and $\alpha$ encodes the training data. Over a 2D input space $(x_1, x_2)$, this is a weighted average value of $\alpha$ with weight kernel $k$ — a double integral of exactly the form § 5 above.

### 6.5 — Spatial Pooling in CNNs

**Global Average Pooling (GAP)** — common in modern architectures (ResNet, GoogLeNet) — replaces the final fully-connected layer with:

$$\text{GAP}(F^{(c)}) = \frac{1}{H \cdot W} \sum_{i,j} F^{(c)}_{ij}$$

for channel $c$. This is _literally_ the discrete average value of the feature map over the spatial domain. The motivation: compute the average activation of each feature detector across the entire image. The continuous limit is $f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{A} \iint F^{(c)}(x,y), dA$.

GAP reduces a 3D tensor to a vector while being translation-invariant — a property that follows from the invariance of the average value under rearrangement of the domain (Fubini + change of variables).

### 6.6 — Monte Carlo Integration

The average value formula is the basis of **Monte Carlo integration**. To compute $\iint_D f, dA$:

1. Sample $N$ points $(x_i, y_i)$ uniformly from $D$.
2. Estimate: $\iint_D f, dA \approx A(D) \cdot \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N f(x_i, y_i)$

Because the sample mean $\frac{1}{N}\sum f(x_i, y_i)$ is an unbiased estimator of $f_{\text{avg}} = \frac{1}{A(D)}\iint f, dA$, by the Law of Large Numbers.

Monte Carlo integration is the workhorse of:

- **Bayesian posterior approximation** (MCMC sampling)
- **Policy gradient methods** in RL (estimating $\mathbb{E}_\pi[R]$)
- **Training diffusion models** (DDPM, score matching objectives)
- **Dropout** (averaging over random subnetworks at test time)

The error scales as $O(1/\sqrt{N})$ **regardless of dimension** — this dimension-independence is why MC dominates for high-dimensional integrals, while deterministic quadrature degrades exponentially (the _curse of dimensionality_).

## § 7 — Summary

|Concept|Formula|
|---|---|
|Average value over $D$|$f_{\text{avg}} = \dfrac{1}{A(D)} \iint_D f, dA$|
|Area of $D$|$A(D) = \iint_D 1, dA$|
|Reconstruction identity|$\iint_D f, dA = f_{\text{avg}} \cdot A(D)$|
|Weighted average|$\langle f \rangle_w = \dfrac{\iint f, w, dA}{\iint w, dA}$|
|MVT guarantee|$\exists,(x_0,y_0)\in D$ s.t. $f(x_0,y_0) = f_{\text{avg}}$ (f cts, D connected)|

**The workflow for every problem:**

```
1. Sketch D
2. Compute A(D)  [geometry or integration]
3. Set up ∬_D f dA  [choose order, set variable limits]
4. Evaluate the iterated integral
5. Divide by A(D)
```
