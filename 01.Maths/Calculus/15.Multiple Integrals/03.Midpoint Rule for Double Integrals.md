

> _"When the exact integral is out of reach, the midpoint rule gives you the best possible rectangular approximation вҖ” and tells you precisely how wrong you are."_

## 0. Preamble вҖ” Why Approximate?

The machinery of Fubini's theorem computes $\iint_D f(x,y),dA$ exactly вҖ” but only when $f$ has a closed-form antiderivative. In practice:

- $f(x,y)$ may be given only as **tabular data** (sensor readings, simulation outputs)
- The antiderivative of $f$ may **not exist in closed form** (e.g., $f = e^{-x^2 - y^2}$ over a square)
- The domain $D$ may be **geometrically irregular**

In all these cases, we need a **numerical method**. The midpoint rule is the simplest and most natural вҖ” it is the double Riemann sum with a specific, optimal choice of sample points.

---

## 1. Recall вҖ” The Single-Variable Midpoint Rule

For $\int_a^b f(x),dx$, divide $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals of width $\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}$, with midpoints $\bar{x}_i = a + \left(i - \frac{1}{2}\right)\Delta x$:

$$M_n = \sum_{i=1}^n f(\bar{x}_i),\Delta x \approx \int_a^b f(x),dx$$

The midpoint is the optimal sample point for a rectangle because it cancels the leading error term (the linear part of $f$ over the subinterval integrates to zero when sampled at the center).

## 2. The Midpoint Rule for Double Integrals вҖ” Setup

### 2.1 Partition the Rectangle

Let $R = [a,b] \times [c,d]$. Divide:

- $[a,b]$ into $m$ equal subintervals of width $\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{m}$
- $[c,d]$ into $n$ equal subintervals of width $\Delta y = \frac{d-c}{n}$

This produces $mn$ sub-rectangles $R_{ij} = [x_{i-1}, x_i] \times [y_{j-1}, y_j]$, each with area: $$\Delta A = \Delta x \cdot \Delta y$$

### 2.2 The Midpoints

The midpoint of the subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ is: $$\bar{x}_i = \frac{x_{i-1} + x_i}{2} = a + \left(i - \frac{1}{2}\right)\Delta x, \qquad i = 1, \ldots, m$$

The midpoint of the subinterval $[y_{j-1}, y_j]$ is: $$\bar{y}_j = \frac{y_{j-1} + y_j}{2} = c + \left(j - \frac{1}{2}\right)\Delta y, \qquad j = 1, \ldots, n$$

The **center** of the sub-rectangle $R_{ij}$ is $(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_j)$.

### 2.3 The Midpoint Rule

**Definition (Midpoint Rule for Double Integrals).** The midpoint rule approximation is:

$$\boxed{M_{mn} = \sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{j=1}^{n} f(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_j),\Delta A}$$

where $\Delta A = \Delta x \cdot \Delta y$, and $(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_j)$ is the center of the sub-rectangle $R_{ij}$.

**Geometric meaning.** Over each sub-rectangle $R_{ij}$, we build a rectangular column (box) whose height is the value of $f$ at the _center_ of the base. The midpoint rule sums the volumes of all these columns.

## 3. Why Midpoints? вҖ” The Error Analysis

### 3.1 Intuition from 1D

Over a single subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, expand $f$ in a Taylor series about the midpoint $\bar{x}_i$: $$f(x) = f(\bar{x}_i) + f'(\bar{x}_i)(x - \bar{x}_i) + \frac{f''(\bar{x}_i)}{2}(x - \bar{x}_i)^2 + \cdots$$

Integrating over $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$: $$\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i} f(x),dx = f(\bar{x}_i)\Delta x + f'(\bar{x}_i)\underbrace{\int_{x_{i-1}}^{x_i}(x - \bar{x}_i),dx}_{=,0\text{ (odd, symmetric)}} + O((\Delta x)^3)$$

The linear error term vanishes because $(x - \bar{x}_i)$ is an odd function on $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ centered at $\bar{x}_i$. This is why the midpoint is better than an endpoint: it kills the $O((\Delta x)^2)$ error per interval, leaving only $O((\Delta x)^3)$ per interval.

The same cancellation happens in 2D over each $R_{ij}$.

### 3.2 Error Bound for the Midpoint Rule

**Theorem.** If $|f_{xx}|, |f_{yy}| \leq K$ on $R = [a,b]\times[c,d]$, then the error in the midpoint rule satisfies: $$|E_M| = \left|\iint_R f,dA - M_{mn}\right| \leq \frac{K(b-a)^3(d-c)}{24m^2} + \frac{K(b-a)(d-c)^3}{24n^2}$$

**Key observations:**

- Error $\sim O!\left(\frac{1}{m^2}\right) + O!\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right)$ вҖ” doubling $m$ and $n$ reduces error by factor of $4$.
- Error $\to 0$ as $m, n \to \infty$ вҖ” the rule is **consistent**.
- The bound depends on second derivatives of $f$, not first вҖ” confirming the cancellation of linear error.

> Compare with the naive left/right endpoint rule, whose error is $O(1/m) + O(1/n)$ вҖ” the midpoint rule converges an order faster.

## 4. Worked Example I вҖ” Direct Computation

**Problem.** Use the midpoint rule with $m = 2$, $n = 2$ to approximate $\iint_R \sqrt{x + 2y},dA$ where $R = [0,2]\times[0,1]$.

### Step 1 вҖ” Partition

$$\Delta x = \frac{2-0}{2} = 1, \qquad \Delta y = \frac{1-0}{2} = 0.5, \qquad \Delta A = 1 \times 0.5 = 0.5$$

Sub-rectangles (2Г—2 = 4 total):

|$(i,j)$|$x$-interval|$y$-interval|
|---|---|---|
|$(1,1)$|$[0,1]$|$[0, 0.5]$|
|$(1,2)$|$[0,1]$|$[0.5, 1]$|
|$(2,1)$|$[1,2]$|$[0, 0.5]$|
|$(2,2)$|$[1,2]$|$[0.5, 1]$|

### Step 2 вҖ” Midpoints

$$\bar{x}_1 = 0.5, \quad \bar{x}_2 = 1.5$$ $$\bar{y}_1 = 0.25, \quad \bar{y}_2 = 0.75$$

### Step 3 вҖ” Evaluate $f$ at Centers

|$(i,j)$|$(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_j)$|$f = \sqrt{x + 2y}$|
|---|---|---|
|$(1,1)$|$(0.5,; 0.25)$|$\sqrt{0.5 + 0.5} = \sqrt{1} = 1$|
|$(1,2)$|$(0.5,; 0.75)$|$\sqrt{0.5 + 1.5} = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.4142$|
|$(2,1)$|$(1.5,; 0.25)$|$\sqrt{1.5 + 0.5} = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.4142$|
|$(2,2)$|$(1.5,; 0.75)$|$\sqrt{1.5 + 1.5} = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.7321$|

### Step 4 вҖ” Sum

$$M_{22} = (1 + \sqrt{2} + \sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}) \times 0.5$$ $$= (1 + 2\sqrt{2} + \sqrt{3}) \times 0.5$$ $$= (1 + 2(1.4142) + 1.7321) \times 0.5$$ $$= (1 + 2.8284 + 1.7321) \times 0.5 = 5.5605 \times 0.5 \approx 2.780$$

### Step 5 вҖ” Compare with Exact Value

$$\iint_R \sqrt{x+2y},dA = \int_0^2\int_0^1 \sqrt{x+2y},dy,dx$$

Inner: $\int_0^1 \sqrt{x+2y},dy = \left[\frac{(x+2y)^{3/2}}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{(x+2)^{3/2} - x^{3/2}}{3}$

Outer: $\int_0^2 \frac{(x+2)^{3/2} - x^{3/2}}{3},dx = \frac{1}{3}\left[\frac{2(x+2)^{5/2}}{5} - \frac{2x^{5/2}}{5}\right]_0^2$

$= \frac{2}{15}\left[(4^{5/2} - 2^{5/2}) - (2^{5/2} - 0)\right] = \frac{2}{15}\left[32 - 2\cdot 2^{5/2}\right] = \frac{2}{15}(32 - 8\sqrt{2})$

$= \frac{64 - 16\sqrt{2}}{15} \approx \frac{64 - 22.627}{15} \approx \frac{41.373}{15} \approx 2.758$

**Error:** $|2.780 - 2.758| = 0.022$ with only a $2\times 2$ grid. Already less than $1%$ error.

## 5. Worked Example II вҖ” Function Given as a Table

This is the scenario where the midpoint rule is _indispensable_: $f$ is known only at discrete points.

**Problem.** The temperature $T(x,y)$ (in В°C) at the center of each sub-rectangle in the grid $[0,4]\times[0,2]$ (with $m=2$, $n=2$, so $\Delta x = 2$, $\Delta y = 1$, $\Delta A = 2$) is measured as:

||$\bar{y}_1 = 0.5$|$\bar{y}_2 = 1.5$|
|---|---|---|
|$\bar{x}_1 = 1$|$T = 12$|$T = 18$|
|$\bar{x}_2 = 3$|$T = 20$|$T = 25$|

Estimate the total heat content $\iint_R T(x,y),dA$.

$$M_{22} = (12 + 18 + 20 + 25) \times 2 = 75 \times 2 = 150$$

The average temperature over $R$ is $\bar{T} = \frac{150}{8} = 18.75,^\circ\text{C}$ (area of $R$ is $4 \times 2 = 8$).

## 7. The Midpoint Rule on General Regions

If $D$ is not a rectangle, a standard approach is:

1. Enclose $D$ in a rectangle $R$.
2. Extend $f$ to $R$ by setting $f = 0$ outside $D$.
3. Apply the midpoint rule to $R$.

The sub-rectangles $R_{ij}$ that lie entirely outside $D$ contribute zero. Sub-rectangles that straddle the boundary contribute an approximation error, but this boundary error $\to 0$ as the grid refines (assuming $D$ has a reasonable boundary).

**Alternative:** Use the midpoint rule over each boundary-aligned sub-region after decomposing $D$ into Type I or Type II strips.

## 8. Comparison with Other Rules

|Rule|Sample point in $R_{ij}$|Error order (per dimension)|Notes|
|---|---|---|---|
|Left endpoint|$(x_{i-1}, y_{j-1})$|$O(1/m) + O(1/n)$|Simplest; systematic error|
|Right endpoint|$(x_i, y_j)$|$O(1/m) + O(1/n)$|Systematic error (opposite sign)|
|**Midpoint**|$(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_j)$|$O(1/m^2) + O(1/n^2)$|**Best among 1-point rules**|
|Trapezoidal (2D)|Average of 4 corners|$O(1/m^2) + O(1/n^2)$|Same order as midpoint|
|Simpson's (2D)|Weighted combo|$O(1/m^4) + O(1/n^4)$|Higher order; needs even subdivisions|

> **Key takeaway.** The midpoint rule achieves the same asymptotic error order as the trapezoidal rule (both $O(h^2)$), but with half the function evaluations вҖ” one per sub-rectangle instead of four corner evaluations.

## 9. Connection to Machine Learning and Deep Learning

### 9.1 Mini-Batch SGD as a Midpoint Rule

In machine learning, the true objective is the expected loss: $$\mathcal{R}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \iint \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}; x, y),p(x,y),dx,dy$$

This integral is approximated by sampling. When we use a **mini-batch** of $B$ samples drawn uniformly from the dataset: $$\hat{\mathcal{R}}_B(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{b=1}^B \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}; x_b, y_b)$$

If the data space is partitioned into $B$ equal cells and one sample is drawn from near the center of each cell, this is structurally identical to the midpoint rule. The "midpoint" philosophy вҖ” sample the center of each region вҖ” is why **stratified sampling** (a structured variant of this) reduces variance in Monte Carlo integration.

**The key analogy:**

|Midpoint Rule|Mini-batch SGD|
|---|---|
|Partition $R$ into $mn$ sub-rectangles|Partition dataset into $B$ mini-batches|
|Sample $f$ at center $(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_j)$|Evaluate loss at batch samples|
|$\frac{1}{mn}\sum_{ij} f(\bar{x}_i,\bar{y}_j)$|$\frac{1}{B}\sum_b \ell(\boldsymbol{\theta}; x_b, y_b)$|
|Error $\to 0$ as $mn \to \infty$|Gradient estimate improves as $B \to \infty$|

### 9.2 Numerical Integration in Bayesian ML

Bayesian inference requires computing integrals of the form: $$p(y \mid x) = \int p(y \mid x, \boldsymbol{\theta}),p(\boldsymbol{\theta} \mid \mathcal{D}),d\boldsymbol{\theta}$$

For low-dimensional $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, the midpoint rule (or more sophisticated quadrature) is applied directly. For high-dimensional $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, Monte Carlo methods (MCMC, variational inference) take over вҖ” but they are conceptually grid-free versions of the same Riemann sum philosophy.

### 9.3 Quadrature in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

Physics-Informed Neural Networks minimize residuals of PDEs over a domain: $$\mathcal{L} = \iint_D \left[\mathcal{N}[u](https://claude.ai/chat/x,y)\right]^2 dA$$

where $\mathcal{N}[u]$ is a PDE operator applied to the network output $u$. This integral is approximated by the midpoint rule (or Gaussian quadrature) over a set of **collocation points** вҖ” the midpoints of a partition of $D$. The quality of the approximation directly controls training quality.

### 9.4 Gaussian Quadrature вҖ” The "Optimal Midpoint" Generalization

The midpoint rule uses one evaluation at the center of each sub-rectangle. **Gaussian quadrature** asks: what are the _optimal_ evaluation points and weights for a given number of function evaluations? The answer (GaussвҖ“Legendre points) generalizes the midpoint idea вҖ” place sample points where they eliminate the most error. This is used in high-performance numerical integration throughout scientific ML and finite element methods.

## 10. Full Procedure Summary

Given $\iint_R f(x,y),dA$ over $R = [a,b]\times[c,d]$, approximate with $m \times n$ grid:

$$\Delta x = \frac{b-a}{m}, \quad \Delta y = \frac{d-c}{n}, \quad \Delta A = \Delta x \cdot \Delta y$$

$$\bar{x}_i = a + \left(i - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\Delta x \quad (i = 1,\ldots,m)$$

$$\bar{y}_j = c + \left(j - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\Delta y \quad (j = 1,\ldots,n)$$

$$M_{mn} = \Delta A \sum_{i=1}^{m}\sum_{j=1}^{n} f(\bar{x}_i, \bar{y}_j)$$

**To improve accuracy:** increase $m$ and $n$. Error decreases as $O(1/m^2) + O(1/n^2)$.
