

> _"The Fresnel integrals are the canonical example of a function that exists perfectly — continuous, differentiable, with known Taylor series and asymptotic behavior — yet resists every attempt to write it in closed form. They are what mathematics looks like when FTC Part 1 is the end of the road, not the beginning."_

## 0. The Setup: A Function Defined by Integration

From [[Fundamental Theorem of Calculus]], we know that if $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then the area function

$$g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$$

is guaranteed to exist and satisfy $g'(x) = f(x)$ — even if $f$ has no elementary antiderivative.

The **Fresnel sine function** is precisely such an object:

$$\boxed{S(x) = \int_0^x \sin(t^2), dt}$$

The integrand $\sin(t^2)$ is continuous everywhere. Its antiderivative exists (guaranteed by FTC Part 1). But that antiderivative **cannot be written** using polynomials, exponentials, logarithms, trigonometric functions, or any finite combination thereof.

$S(x)$ is not a formula. It is a function defined by the act of integration itself.

This is not a failure. It is the normal state of affairs — most functions do not have elementary antiderivatives. The Fresnel functions are simply the most famous members of this class, arising naturally in physics and engineering.

---

## 1. Definitions

There are two Fresnel functions, arising as a pair:

$$\boxed{S(x) = \int_0^x \sin(t^2), dt}$$

$$\boxed{C(x) = \int_0^x \cos(t^2), dt}$$

They appear together because $\sin$ and $\cos$ are always companions — they are the imaginary and real parts of $e^{it}$, and $S(x) + iC(x) = \int_0^x e^{it^2}, dt$ (up to conventions).

### Alternate Normalization (Physics / Engineering)

In many physics texts (optics, diffraction theory), the Fresnel integrals are normalized differently:

$$S_{\text{phys}}(u) = \int_0^u \sin!\left(\frac{\pi t^2}{2}\right) dt, \qquad C_{\text{phys}}(u) = \int_0^u \cos!\left(\frac{\pi t^2}{2}\right) dt$$

The substitution $t^2 = \pi s^2/2$ converts between conventions. We use the Stewart convention $S(x) = \int_0^x \sin(t^2), dt$ throughout.

---

## 2. Immediate Properties from FTC

By [[Fundamental Theorem of Calculus]] Part 1, since $\sin(t^2)$ and $\cos(t^2)$ are continuous everywhere:

$$S'(x) = \sin(x^2), \qquad C'(x) = \cos(x^2)$$

This is the **complete answer** to "what is the derivative?" — and it follows in one line from FTC, no computation required.

From these derivatives, we can read off everything about the shape of $S$ and $C$:

### Critical Points of $S(x)$

$S'(x) = \sin(x^2) = 0$ when $x^2 = n\pi$ for integer $n \geq 0$:

$$x = \sqrt{n\pi}, \quad n = 0, 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

$$x = 0,\ \sqrt{\pi},\ \sqrt{2\pi},\ \sqrt{3\pi},\ \sqrt{4\pi},\ \ldots$$

$$\approx 0,\ 1.772,\ 2.507,\ 3.070,\ 3.545,\ \ldots$$

At each $x = \sqrt{n\pi}$, $S$ has a local extremum:

- $n$ odd: $\sin(x^2) = \sin(n\pi) = 0$, and changes sign $+ \to -$: **local maximum**
- $n$ even (nonzero): changes sign $- \to +$: **local minimum**

### Concavity of $S(x)$

$$S''(x) = 2x\cos(x^2)$$

Inflection points where $S''(x) = 0$:

- $x = 0$
- $x^2 = \frac{\pi}{2} + n\pi$, i.e. $x = \sqrt{\frac{(2n+1)\pi}{2}}$ for $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$

The concavity oscillates with increasing frequency as $x$ grows.

## 3. Values and Initial Behavior

### At the origin

$$S(0) = \int_0^0 \sin(t^2), dt = 0, \qquad C(0) = 0$$

### Near the origin: $S(x) \approx x^3/3$

For small $x$, $\sin(t^2) \approx t^2$, so:

$$S(x) \approx \int_0^x t^2, dt = \frac{x^3}{3}$$

More precisely, using the Taylor series $\sin u = u - u^3/6 + \cdots$ with $u = t^2$:

$$\sin(t^2) = t^2 - \frac{t^6}{6} + \frac{t^{10}}{120} - \cdots$$

Integrating term by term (valid by [[Fundamental Theorem of Calculus]]):

$$S(x) = \frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^7}{7 \cdot 6} + \frac{x^{11}}{11 \cdot 120} - \cdots = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+3}}{(2n+1)!,(4n+3)}$$

This series converges for **all** $x \in \mathbb{R}$ (by ratio test — the factorial in the denominator dominates any power of $x$). So we can compute $S(x)$ to arbitrary precision for any $x$.

Similarly:

$$C(x) = x - \frac{x^5}{5 \cdot 2!} + \frac{x^9}{9 \cdot 4!} - \cdots = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+1}}{(2n)!,(4n+1)}$$

## 4. Behavior as $x \to \infty$: The Cornu Spiral Limit

The most remarkable property of the Fresnel functions is their limiting behavior:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} S(x) = \lim_{x \to \infty} C(x) = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{4} \cdot \sqrt{2} = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}$$

More precisely:

$$\int_0^\infty \sin(t^2), dt = \int_0^\infty \cos(t^2), dt = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}} \approx 0.6267$$

This is a **non-trivial result** — the integrals converge even though $\sin(t^2)$ does not $\to 0$ as $t \to \infty$ in the usual sense (it keeps oscillating). The convergence is conditional, via cancellation of the increasingly rapid oscillations.

**Proof sketch (for the curious).** Use the substitution $u = t^2$, $du = 2t, dt$, $dt = \frac{du}{2\sqrt{u}}$:

$$\int_0^\infty \sin(t^2), dt = \int_0^\infty \sin(u)\frac{du}{2\sqrt{u}} = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^\infty u^{-1/2}\sin(u), du$$

This is a **Mellin transform** evaluated at $s = 1/2$, and equals $\frac{1}{2}\cdot\Gamma(1/2)\cdot\sin(\pi/4) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\sqrt{\pi}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}}$. $\blacksquare$

The fact that the integral converges to a finite value is a consequence of the **Riemann-Lebesgue lemma** — rapidly oscillating functions average out.

### 5.2 The Cornu Spiral (Euler's Spiral)

Parametrize the curve $(C(x), S(x))$ in the plane as $x$ runs from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$:

```
S(x)

 0.6|          ·      ·          ← approaches (√(π/8), √(π/8))

  |     ·                ·

  |   ·                        ·
  |  ·                              ·  ·  ·
  |·_________________________ ___________> C(x)
-0.6  -0.4  -0.2   0   0.2   0.4   0.6

⊕ = (√(π/8), √(π/8)) ≈ (0.627, 0.627): right eye of spiral
⊗ = (-√(π/8), -√(π/8)): left eye of spiral
```

The curve spirals inward toward $(+\sqrt{\pi/8}, +\sqrt{\pi/8})$ as $x \to +\infty$ and toward $(-\sqrt{\pi/8}, -\sqrt{\pi/8})$ as $x \to -\infty$ (by oddness of $S$ and $C$).

**Key geometric property:** The **tangent angle** of the Cornu spiral at arc-length parameter $x$ is exactly $x^2$ radians. This is the defining property used in optics.

The **curvature** at point $x$ is $\kappa(x) = 2x$ — it increases linearly with arc length, making the Cornu spiral the unique curve of linearly increasing curvature.

## 6. Symmetry Properties

Since $\sin((-t)^2) = \sin(t^2)$ and $\cos((-t)^2) = \cos(t^2)$, the integrands are **even functions** of $t$. Therefore:

$$S(-x) = \int_0^{-x}\sin(t^2), dt = -\int_0^x \sin(t^2), dt = -S(x)$$

$$C(-x) = -C(x)$$

**Both $S$ and $C$ are odd functions.** The Cornu spiral is symmetric under $180°$ rotation about the origin.

## 7. The Stewart Example: Reading $S(x)$ from $S'(x) = \sin(x^2)$

Stewart uses $S(x)$ as a paradigmatic example of FTC Part 1 in action (§5.3). The exercise is: given only that $S'(x) = \sin(x^2)$ and $S(0) = 0$, determine the behavior of $S$.

**Where is $S$ increasing?**

$S'(x) = \sin(x^2) > 0$ when $x^2 \in (2k\pi, (2k+1)\pi)$ for integer $k \geq 0$, i.e.:

$$x \in \left(\sqrt{2k\pi},\ \sqrt{(2k+1)\pi}\right), \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

The first increasing interval is $(0, \sqrt{\pi}) \approx (0, 1.772)$.

**Where is $S$ concave up?**

$S''(x) = 2x\cos(x^2) > 0$ when $\cos(x^2) > 0$ (for $x > 0$), i.e., $x^2 \in [0, \pi/2) \cup (3\pi/2, 5\pi/2) \cup \ldots$

**The first local maximum:**

At $x = \sqrt{\pi}$: $S(\sqrt{\pi}) = \int_0^{\sqrt{\pi}}\sin(t^2), dt \approx 0.6247$.

This can be estimated with $M_n$ or $T_n$. Using the Taylor series with enough terms gives the value to any desired precision.

## 8. Numerical Computation of $S(x)$

Since there is no closed form, we compute $S(x)$ numerically.

### Method 1: Taylor Series (small $x$)

$$S(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{N} \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+3}}{(2n+1)!,(4n+3)}$$

For $x = 1$, taking 4 terms:

$$S(1) \approx \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{42} + \frac{1}{1320} - \frac{1}{75600} \approx 0.3333 - 0.0238 + 0.000758 - 0.0000132 \approx 0.3103$$

True value: $S(1) \approx 0.3103$. $\checkmark$

For large $x$, the Taylor series converges slowly (large terms that nearly cancel). Switch to asymptotic expansion or numerical quadrature.

### Method 2: Midpoint Rule (see [[Midpoint Rule]])

For $S(2)$ with $n = 8$, $\Delta x = 0.25$:

Midpoints: $0.125, 0.375, 0.625, 0.875, 1.125, 1.375, 1.625, 1.875$

|$t_i$|$t_i^2$|$\sin(t_i^2)$|
|---|---|---|
|0.125|0.01563|0.01563|
|0.375|0.14063|0.14032|
|0.625|0.39063|0.38092|
|0.875|0.76563|0.69392|
|1.125|1.26563|0.95373|
|1.375|1.89063|0.94709|
|1.625|2.64063|0.45312|
|1.875|3.51563|−0.35791|
|**Sum**||**3.22682**|

$$S(2) \approx 0.25 \times 3.22682 = 0.80671$$

True value: $S(2) \approx 0.8048$. Error $\approx 0.002$. Reasonable for $n=8$ on a rapidly oscillating integrand.

### Method 3: Asymptotic Expansion (large $x$)

For $x \gg 1$, integration by parts gives:

$$S(x) \approx \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}} - \frac{\cos(x^2)}{2x} - \frac{\sin(x^2)}{4x^3} + \cdots$$

This is an **asymptotic series** — it does not converge, but for large $x$, the first few terms give excellent approximations.

## 9. Why This Function Matters: Fresnel Diffraction

The Fresnel functions were introduced by Augustin-Jean Fresnel (~1818) in his wave theory of light.

When a plane wave passes through a narrow slit, the intensity at a point on the screen is:

$$I \propto \left[C(u_2) - C(u_1)\right]^2 + \left[S(u_2) - S(u_1)\right]^2$$

where $u_1, u_2$ are parameters determined by the geometry. The diffraction pattern — the alternating bright and dark bands — is encoded in how $C$ and $S$ vary.

The **Cornu spiral** is the direct visual representation of this: the chord length between two points on the spiral gives the amplitude, and the chord squared gives the intensity.

This is not a contrived application. The Fresnel functions are the exact, non-approximated solution to the diffraction problem for arbitrary screen geometry.

## 10. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 10.1 The Error Function: Fresnel's Cousin in ML

The Gaussian integral $\int_0^x e^{-t^2}, dt$ is the **error function** $\text{erf}(x)$ (up to scaling) — structurally identical to $S(x)$: a continuous integrand with no elementary antiderivative, defined as an area function via FTC.

In ML, $\text{erf}$ appears everywhere:

- **Normal CDF**: $\Phi(x) = \frac{1}{2}\left[1 + \text{erf}!\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\right]$
- **GELU activation**: $\text{GELU}(x) = x,\Phi(x) = \frac{x}{2}\left[1 + \text{erf}!\left(\frac{x}{\sqrt{2}}\right)\right]$
- **Probit models**: logistic regression with Gaussian noise uses $\Phi^{-1}$

The GELU (Gaussian Error Linear Unit) is the default activation in BERT, GPT-2, GPT-3, and most modern transformers. It is defined via the error function — which is defined via FTC Part 1. Every forward pass through a transformer computes an area function.

### 10.2 Fresnel-Type Integrals in Quantum ML

In **quantum machine learning** and path integral formulations:

$$Z = \int \mathcal{D}[\phi], e^{iS[\phi]}$$

where $S[\phi]$ is the action — a Fresnel-type oscillatory integral in infinite dimensions. The Fresnel integral $\int e^{it^2} dt$ is the 1D baby case of the Feynman path integral. Approximating $Z$ numerically is one of the central computational challenges, and all the issues we saw — rapid oscillation, conditional convergence, need for asymptotic methods — appear in full force.

### 10.3 Fourier Analysis: Chirp Signals and Fresnel Transforms

A **chirp signal** is $f(t) = e^{i\alpha t^2}$ — a sinusoid whose frequency increases linearly with time. In radar, sonar, and MRI:

$$\hat{f}(\omega) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{i\alpha t^2} e^{-i\omega t}, dt$$

This integral evaluates to a Gaussian times a phase factor — by completing the square and using the Fresnel integral $\int e^{it^2}, dt = \sqrt{\pi/2}, e^{i\pi/4}$. **Matched filtering** in radar signal processing — how a receiver recognizes a transmitted chirp — is built on this Fresnel integral identity.

In ML: **linear attention** and **random Fourier features** (Rahimi & Recht, 2007) approximate kernel functions using exactly these oscillatory integrals.

### 10.4 Numerical Integration of Oscillatory Integrands

The challenge of computing $S(x)$ for large $x$ is representative of a general problem in scientific ML:

$$\int_a^b f(t)\sin(\omega t^2), dt, \quad \omega \gg 1$$

Standard quadrature (Midpoint, Trapezoid, Simpson) fails badly because the integrand oscillates faster than the grid can resolve. The solutions are:

- **Levin's method**: reformulate as an ODE, avoid oscillation entirely
- **Filon quadrature**: exploit the oscillatory structure analytically
- **Numerical steepest descent**: deform the contour into the complex plane where the integrand decays

These are active research areas in scientific computing and appear in Monte Carlo simulation for neural network training with oscillatory loss landscapes.

### 10.5 Taylor Series as Feature Expansion

The Taylor expansion of $S(x)$:

$$S(x) = \frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{x^7}{42} + \frac{x^{11}}{1320} - \cdots$$

is a **polynomial feature expansion** — exactly what kernel methods and polynomial regression use. The fact that we can represent $S(x)$ to arbitrary precision with a polynomial of high enough degree is the mathematical foundation of the **universal approximation theorem**: continuous functions on compact sets can be approximated arbitrarily well by polynomials (Weierstrass), and by neural networks (Cybenko, Hornik).

$S(x)$ is the canonical example that functions can be perfectly well-defined and smooth while requiring infinite series — never a finite formula. Neural networks are doing the same thing: approximating a target function (which may have no closed form) with a finite parametric family.

## 11. Quick-Reference Cheatsheet

$$S(x) = \int_0^x \sin(t^2), dt, \qquad C(x) = \int_0^x \cos(t^2), dt$$

$$S'(x) = \sin(x^2), \qquad C'(x) = \cos(x^2) \quad \text{(FTC Part 1)}$$

$$S(-x) = -S(x), \qquad C(-x) = -C(x) \quad \text{(odd functions)}$$

$$S(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+3}}{(2n+1)!,(4n+3)}, \qquad \text{converges } \forall x \in \mathbb{R}$$

$$\lim_{x\to\infty} S(x) = \lim_{x\to\infty} C(x) = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{2}} \approx 0.6267$$

$$\text{Critical points of } S: \quad x = \sqrt{n\pi},\ n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

$$\text{Cornu spiral: } \kappa(x) = 2x \quad \text{(curvature} = 2 \times \text{arc length)}$$

$$\text{erf}(x) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\int_0^x e^{-t^2}, dt \quad \text{(Fresnel's Gaussian cousin; GELU in transformers)}$$
