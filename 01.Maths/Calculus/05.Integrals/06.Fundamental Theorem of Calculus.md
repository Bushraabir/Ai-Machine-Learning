
> _"The Fundamental Theorem is not a computational trick. It is a declaration that two operations — differentiation and integration — which were invented to solve completely different problems, are in fact inverse processes. This is one of the most surprising facts in all of mathematics."_

---

## 0. The Two Problems, and Why Their Connection is Miraculous

Calculus was born from two entirely separate questions:

**The Tangent Problem** (Fermat, Descartes, ~1630s):

> Given a curve $y = f(x)$, what is the slope of the tangent line at a point? This led to the **derivative** $f'(x)$.

**The Area Problem** (Archimedes, Cavalieri, ~250 BC – 1630s):

> Given a curve $y = f(x)$, what is the area under it between two points? This led to the **definite integral** $\int_a^b f(x), dx$.

These are geometrically opposite questions — one is **local** (what is $f$ doing right here?), the other is **global** (what has $f$ accumulated over an interval?). There is no obvious reason they should be related.

The **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (FTC)** says they are not merely related — they are **inverse operations**, as opposite as multiplication and division.

This is the theorem. Everything else is commentary.

---

## 1. Statement: Two Parts, One Idea

### FTC Part 1 — Differentiation Undoes Integration

**Theorem.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then the area function

$$g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$$

is differentiable on $(a,b)$ and:

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx} \int_a^x f(t), dt = f(x)}$$

_This says: if you integrate $f$ and then differentiate, you get $f$ back._

### FTC Part 2 — Integration Undoes Differentiation

**Theorem.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and $F$ is **any** antiderivative of $f$ (i.e., $F' = f$), then:

$$\boxed{\int_a^b f(x), dx = F(b) - F(a)}$$

_This says: if you differentiate $F$ to get $f$, then integrating $f$ recovers the net change in $F$._

### The Compact Picture

$$\text{Differentiation} \underset{\text{FTC 2}}{\overset{\text{FTC 1}}{\rightleftharpoons}} \text{Integration}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[\int_a^x f(t), dt\right] = f(x) \tag{FTC 1}$$

$$\int_a^b \left[\frac{d}{dx}F(x)\right] dx = F(b) - F(a) \tag{FTC 2}$$

## 2. Proof of FTC Part 1

**Setup.** Let $f$ be continuous on $[a,b]$, and define $g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$.

**Step 1: Compute $g(x+h) - g(x)$.**

By additivity of the integral:

$$g(x+h) - g(x) = \int_a^{x+h} f(t), dt - \int_a^x f(t), dt = \int_x^{x+h} f(t), dt$$

**Step 2: Apply the Mean Value Theorem for Integrals.**

Since $f$ is continuous on $[x, x+h]$ (for small $h > 0$), $\exists, c_h \in (x, x+h)$ such that:

$$\int_x^{x+h} f(t), dt = f(c_h) \cdot h$$

**Step 3: Take the limit.**

$$g'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(c_h) \cdot h}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} f(c_h)$$

Since $c_h \in (x, x+h)$, as $h \to 0$ we have $c_h \to x$. By **continuity of $f$**:

$$\lim_{h \to 0} f(c_h) = f(x)$$

Therefore $g'(x) = f(x)$. $\blacksquare$

### What Continuity Actually Does

The proof depends on continuity in a precise way. If $f$ has a jump discontinuity at $x_0$:

$$f(x_0^-) \neq f(x_0^+)$$

then as $h \to 0^+$, $f(c_h) \to f(x_0^+)$ and as $h \to 0^-$, $f(c_h) \to f(x_0^-) $. The left and right limits of the difference quotient disagree. So $g$ is **not differentiable** at $x_0$ — even though $g$ is still continuous there (integration smooths out jump discontinuities by one degree of regularity).

## 3. Proof of FTC Part 2

**Setup.** $f$ continuous on $[a,b]$, $F$ any antiderivative ($F' = f$).

**Step 1: Invoke FTC Part 1.**

We know $g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$ satisfies $g'(x) = f(x)$.

So both $F$ and $g$ are antiderivatives of $f$.

**Step 2: Antiderivatives differ by a constant.**

If $F'(x) = g'(x) = f(x)$ on $(a,b)$, then $(F - g)'(x) = 0$ on $(a,b)$.

By the **Mean Value Theorem**: if a function has zero derivative everywhere on an interval, it is constant. So:

$$F(x) - g(x) = C \quad \text{for some constant } C \in \mathbb{R}$$

**Step 3: Determine $C$ and evaluate.**

At $x = a$: $g(a) = \int_a^a f(t), dt = 0$. So:

$$F(a) - 0 = C \implies C = F(a)$$

Therefore $g(x) = F(x) - F(a)$ for all $x \in [a,b]$.

At $x = b$:

$$\int_a^b f(t), dt = g(b) = F(b) - F(a)$$

$\blacksquare$

### Notation

We write $F(b) - F(a)$ as:

$$\Big[F(x)\Big]_a^b \quad \text{or} \quad F(x)\Big|_a^b$$

### The MVT is Load-Bearing

The step "antiderivatives differ by a constant" is not obvious — it requires the MVT for derivatives. Specifically: if $h' = 0$ on $(a,b)$, pick any $x \in (a,b)$; by MVT applied to $[a,x]$, $h(x) - h(a) = h'(c)(x-a) = 0$. So $h(x) = h(a)$ for all $x$. Without MVT, we cannot conclude $F - g = \text{const}$.

## 4. The Net Change Theorem

FTC Part 2 has an important physical restatement:

$$\int_a^b F'(x), dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

The integral of a **rate of change** equals the **net change**.

|$F(t)$|$F'(t)$|$\int_a^b F', dt = F(b) - F(a)$|
|---|---|---|
|Position|Velocity|Net displacement|
|Velocity|Acceleration|Net change in velocity|
|Population $P(t)$|Growth rate $P'(t)$|Net population change|
|Mass $m(x)$|Linear density $\rho(x)$|Total mass|
|Charge $Q(t)$|Current $I(t)$|Net charge transferred|
|Loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$|$\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} \cdot \dot\theta$|Net change in loss|

The last row is from ML — we return to it in §8.

## 5. Computing with FTC Part 2: Worked Examples

### Example 1: Polynomial

$$\int_1^3 (x^2 - 4x + 3), dx = \left[\frac{x^3}{3} - 2x^2 + 3x\right]_1^3$$

At $x=3$: $9 - 18 + 9 = 0$. At $x=1$: $\frac{1}{3} - 2 + 3 = \frac{4}{3}$.

$$= 0 - \frac{4}{3} = -\frac{4}{3}$$

The negative value means the region below the $x$-axis dominates.

### Example 2: Trigonometric

$$\int_0^{\pi/2} (\sin x + \cos x), dx = \Big[-\cos x + \sin x\Big]_0^{\pi/2}$$

At $x = \pi/2$: $0 + 1 = 1$. At $x = 0$: $-1 + 0 = -1$.

$$= 1 - (-1) = 2$$

### Example 3: Exponential

$$\int_0^{\ln 3} e^x, dx = \Big[e^x\Big]_0^{\ln 3} = e^{\ln 3} - e^0 = 3 - 1 = 2$$

### Example 4: Rational (requires recognizing antiderivative)

$$\int_1^e \frac{1}{x}, dx = \Big[\ln|x|\Big]_1^e = \ln e - \ln 1 = 1 - 0 = 1$$

### Example 5: FTC Part 1 with Chain Rule

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_{x^2}^{x^3} \sqrt{1+t^4}, dt$$

Split: let $g(x) = \int_0^{x^3}\sqrt{1+t^4},dt - \int_0^{x^2}\sqrt{1+t^4},dt$.

$$= \sqrt{1+x^{12}} \cdot 3x^2 - \sqrt{1+x^8} \cdot 2x$$

### Example 6: FTC + L'Hôpital

Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\int_0^x (e^{t^2} - 1), dt}{x^2}$.

Form $0/0$. Apply L'Hôpital — differentiate numerator via FTC Part 1:

$$= \lim_{x\to 0} \frac{e^{x^2} - 1}{2x} \overset{0/0}{=} \lim_{x\to 0}\frac{2x e^{x^2}}{2} = 0$$

## 6. Why Any Antiderivative Works

FTC Part 2 says $\int_a^b f = F(b) - F(a)$ for **any** antiderivative $F$. Why doesn't the choice matter?

Suppose $F$ and $G$ are both antiderivatives of $f$: $F' = G' = f$.

Then $(F - G)' = 0$ so $F(x) = G(x) + C$ for some constant.

$$G(b) - G(a) = [F(b) - C] - [F(a) - C] = F(b) - F(a)$$

The constant cancels. This is why we can write $\int x^2, dx = \frac{x^3}{3} + C$ and use any particular $C$ when evaluating a definite integral.

## 7. Visualization: The Geometric Heart of FTC

### FTC Part 1 — Geometric Proof Sketch

Fix $x$. The difference $g(x+h) - g(x) = \int_x^{x+h} f(t), dt$ is the area of a thin vertical strip:

```
f(t)
  |
  |    *
  |   * *
f(x)|--*---*--
  |  *     *
  |         *
  +----|----|----> t
       x   x+h
       |←h→|
       thin strip ≈ f(x)·h
```

As $h \to 0$, the strip becomes a rectangle of height $f(x)$ and width $h$. So:

$$g'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\text{area of strip}}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x)\cdot h + O(h^2)}{h} = f(x)$$

### FTC Part 2 — Telescoping Visualization

Partition $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals. By MVT for derivatives, on each $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$:

$$F(x_i) - F(x_{i-1}) = F'(c_i),\Delta x = f(c_i),\Delta x$$

Sum over all subintervals — the differences **telescope**:

$$F(b) - F(a) = \sum_{i=1}^n [F(x_i) - F(x_{i-1})] = \sum_{i=1}^n f(c_i),\Delta x$$

The right side is a Riemann sum for $\int_a^b f$. As $n \to \infty$:

$$F(b) - F(a) = \int_a^b f(x), dx \quad \blacksquare$$

This is arguably the most transparent proof of FTC Part 2 — it shows the integral as a **telescoping sum** of infinitesimal changes in $F$.

```
F(b) - F(a) = [F(x₁)-F(x₀)] + [F(x₂)-F(x₁)] + ··· + [F(xₙ)-F(xₙ₋₁)]
                    ≈f(c₁)Δx       ≈f(c₂)Δx              ≈f(cₙ)Δx
            = Σ f(cᵢ)Δx
            → ∫ₐᵇ f(x)dx
```

## 8. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

The FTC is not background mathematics for ML — it is operationally present in the core algorithms.

### 8.1 Backpropagation is FTC Part 2

Consider a scalar loss $\mathcal{L}$ as a function of parameters $\theta(t)$ evolving along a path. By the **chain rule** (which is the infinitesimal form of FTC Part 2):

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta(T)) - \mathcal{L}(\theta(0)) = \int_0^T \frac{d\mathcal{L}}{dt}, dt = \int_0^T \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} \cdot \dot\theta, dt$$

In discrete gradient descent with steps $\theta_{k+1} = \theta_k - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_k)$:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta_K) - \mathcal{L}(\theta_0) \approx \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} \nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}(\theta_k) \cdot (\theta_{k+1} - \theta_k) = -\eta\sum_{k=0}^K |\nabla\mathcal{L}|^2$$

This is a Riemann sum for $\int_0^T \nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot\dot\theta,dt$ — FTC Part 2 in discrete form. **Backpropagation computes this derivative so we can integrate it via gradient descent.** The entire training loop is:

$$\underbrace{\text{forward pass}}_{\text{evaluate } \mathcal{L}} \to \underbrace{\text{backprop}}_{\text{compute } F' = \nabla\mathcal{L}} \to \underbrace{\text{gradient step}}_{\text{integrate: } \Delta\theta = -\eta F'}$$

FTC Part 2: integrate the derivative to get net change.

### 8.2 The Adjoint Method in Neural ODEs is FTC Part 1

In a **Neural ODE**, the forward pass computes:

$$z(T) = z(0) + \int_0^T f_\theta(z(t), t), dt$$

To train, we need $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta}$. The **adjoint state** $a(t) = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z(t)}$ satisfies:

$$\frac{da}{dt} = -a(t)^\top \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial z}$$

This ODE is derived by **differentiating the area function** $\mathcal{L}(z(T)) = \mathcal{L}!\left(z(0) + \int_0^T f_\theta, dt\right)$ with respect to $z(t)$ — applying FTC Part 1 inside the gradient computation. The entire backward pass of a Neural ODE is FTC Part 1 applied to a functional.

### 8.3 Score Functions and the Log-Derivative Trick

In policy gradient RL and variational inference, we need:

$$\nabla_\theta \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_\theta}[f(x)] = \nabla_\theta \int f(x), p_\theta(x), dx$$

Passing $\nabla_\theta$ under the integral (valid by dominated convergence):

$$= \int f(x), \nabla_\theta p_\theta(x), dx = \int f(x), p_\theta(x), \frac{\nabla_\theta p_\theta(x)}{p_\theta(x)}, dx = \mathbb{E}_{x\sim p_\theta}[f(x), \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x)]$$

This is **differentiating under the integral sign** — FTC Part 1 applied to a parameter-dependent integral. The result is the **REINFORCE / score function estimator**, the foundation of policy gradient methods in RL.

### 8.4 Normalizing Flows: FTC as Change of Variables

A **normalizing flow** transforms a simple density $p_z(z)$ to a complex density $p_x(x)$ via an invertible map $x = g(z)$. The change-of-variables formula:

$$p_x(x) = p_z(g^{-1}(x))\left|\det\frac{\partial g^{-1}}{\partial x}\right|$$

In 1D, this is just:

$$p_x(x) = p_z(z)\left|\frac{dz}{dx}\right| = \frac{p_z(g^{-1}(x))}{|g'(g^{-1}(x))|}$$

which is the 1D FTC: $\int_a^b p_z(g^{-1}(x))|dg^{-1}/dx|,dx = \int_{g^{-1}(a)}^{g^{-1}(b)} p_z(z),dz$. Normalizing flows are architecturally built on FTC as a change-of-variables identity.

### 8.5 Continuous-Time Bellman Equation

In continuous-time RL with value function $V(s,t)$ and reward $r(s,t)$:

$$V(s,t) = \int_t^T r(s(\tau), \tau), e^{-\gamma(\tau-t)}, d\tau + e^{-\gamma(T-t)}V(s(T), T)$$

$V$ is an area function of $t$ (with lower limit $t$). Differentiating via FTC:

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} + \mathcal{H}\left(s, \nabla_s V\right) = \gamma V$$

This is the **Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation** — the central equation of optimal control. It is obtained by applying FTC Part 1 to the value function integral. Every RL algorithm (Q-learning, actor-critic, PPO) is, at the continuous-time limit, solving this PDE — which is a consequence of the FTC.

## 9. Common Errors and How to Avoid Them

### Error 1: Forgetting the Chain Rule in FTC Part 1

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_1^{x^2} e^{t^3}, dt \neq e^{x^6}$$

**Correct:** $= e^{(x^2)^3} \cdot 2x = e^{x^6} \cdot 2x$.

The upper limit is $u(x) = x^2$, so multiply by $u'(x) = 2x$.

### Error 2: Mixing the Integration Variable with the Limit

$$\int_1^x x^2, dx \quad \leftarrow \text{ILLEGAL}$$

The integration variable and the limit cannot share a name. Write $\int_1^x t^2, dt = \frac{x^3}{3} - \frac{1}{3}$.

### Error 3: Using FTC Part 2 Across a Discontinuity

$$\int_{-1}^{1} \frac{1}{x^2}, dx \neq \left[-\frac{1}{x}\right]_{-1}^{1} = -1-1 = -2$$

$f(x) = 1/x^2$ has a **non-integrable singularity** at $x = 0$. The function is not continuous on $[-1,1]$, so FTC Part 2 does not apply. (The correct answer is $+\infty$ — the integral diverges.)

Always verify continuity on $[a,b]$ before applying FTC Part 2.

### Error 4: Sign Errors When Differentiating a Lower Limit

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_x^b f(t), dt = \frac{d}{dx}\left(-\int_b^x f(t), dt\right) = -f(x)$$

Not $+f(x)$. Flip the integral, pick up a minus sign.

## 10. The Bigger Picture: FTC in the Landscape of Mathematics

The FTC is not an isolated result. It is the **$n=1$ case** of a family of theorems connecting derivatives to integrals across dimensions:

|Theorem|Domain|Statement|
|---|---|---|
|**FTC**|$[a,b] \subset \mathbb{R}^1$|$\int_a^b f', dx = f(b) - f(a)$|
|**Green's Theorem**|Region $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$|$\iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) dA = \oint_{\partial D} P, dx + Q, dy$|
|**Stokes' Theorem**|Surface $S \subset \mathbb{R}^3$|$\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$|
|**Divergence Theorem**|Volume $V \subset \mathbb{R}^3$|$\iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}), dV = \oiint_{\partial V} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$|
|**Generalized Stokes**|$n$-manifold $M$|$\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M} \omega$|

All of these say the same thing: **the integral of a derivative over a region equals the integral of the original over the boundary**. The FTC is the 1D baby case. The generalized Stokes' theorem (differential geometry) is the full adult.

This is why the FTC matters beyond calculus — it is a special case of one of the most general theorems in mathematics.

## 11. Quick-Reference Cheatsheet

$$\textbf{FTC Part 1:} \quad \frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t), dt = f(x)$$

$$\textbf{Chain Rule Form:} \quad \frac{d}{dx}\int_a^{u(x)} f(t), dt = f(u(x))\cdot u'(x)$$

$$\textbf{Both Limits Variable:} \quad \frac{d}{dx}\int_{v(x)}^{u(x)} f(t), dt = f(u(x))u'(x) - f(v(x))v'(x)$$

$$\textbf{FTC Part 2:} \quad \int_a^b f(x), dx = F(b) - F(a), \quad F' = f$$

$$\textbf{Net Change:} \quad \int_a^b F'(x), dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

$$\textbf{Checklist before FTC 2:}$$ $$\quad 1.\ f \text{ continuous on } [a,b]? \checkmark$$ $$\quad 2.\ F \text{ is antiderivative of } f \text{ on all of } [a,b]? \checkmark$$ $$\quad 3.\ \text{No singularities hidden inside } (a,b)? \checkmark$$
