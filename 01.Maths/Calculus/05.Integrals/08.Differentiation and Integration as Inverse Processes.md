

> _"To say that differentiation and integration are inverses is to say something far deeper than 'they undo each other.' It is to say that the local and the global are not separate — that knowing how fast something changes at every point is the same as knowing how much it accumulates over every interval. This equivalence is not obvious. It is a theorem. And it is the reason calculus works."_

---

## 0. The Philosophical Starting Point

Two operations. Two completely different origins.

**Differentiation** asks: _what is $f$ doing right now?_ It is local — it examines $f$ in an infinitesimal neighborhood of a single point and extracts the instantaneous rate of change.

**Integration** asks: _what has $f$ done over an interval?_ It is global — it surveys $f$ across an entire region and accumulates all contributions.

There is no a priori reason these should be related. A geographer measuring the slope of a hillside at a single rock is doing something completely different from a hydrologist measuring the total rainfall over a watershed. The FTC says: these two people are, in a precise mathematical sense, doing inverse operations of each other.

This note asks: **what exactly does "inverse" mean here?** How complete is the inversion? Where does it fail? And what is the algebraic structure underneath?

---

## 1. Two Operators, Precisely Defined

Let us be precise about what we are inverting.

**The Differentiation Operator $D$.**

$$D: f \mapsto f', \qquad (Df)(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(x+h)-f(x)}{h}$$

$D$ acts on differentiable functions and produces functions (their derivatives). It is a **linear operator**:

$$D(\alpha f + \beta g) = \alpha Df + \beta Dg$$

**The Indefinite Integration Operator $J$.**

$$J: f \mapsto F + C, \qquad (Jf)(x) = \int f(x), dx = F(x) + C, \quad F' = f$$

$J$ is also linear, but its output is not unique — it produces a **family of functions** differing by a constant.

**The Definite Integration Operator $I_a$** (fix left endpoint $a$):

$$(I_a f)(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$$

$I_a f$ is a single, well-defined function (not a family).

The FTC says $D$ and $I_a$ are mutual inverses — in a sense we now make precise.

---

## 2. FTC Part 1: $D \circ I_a = \text{Id}$

**Theorem (FTC Part 1).** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$:

$$D(I_a f)(x) = \frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t), dt = f(x)$$

In operator notation: $D \circ I_a = \text{Id}$ on the space of continuous functions.

**What this says:** Integrate first, then differentiate — you get back to where you started. The area function $\int_a^x f(t), dt$ is an antiderivative of $f$, and differentiating it recovers $f$ exactly.

### The Proof in One Diagram

```
              integrate
   f(t)  ─────────────────►  g(x) = ∫ₐˣ f(t) dt
     ▲                              │
     │  D∘Iₐ = Id                  │  differentiate
     │                              │
     └──────────────────────────────┘
              g'(x) = f(x)  ✓
```

The round trip — integrate then differentiate — brings you back to $f$.

**Proof.**

$$g'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{1}{h}\int_x^{x+h}f(t),dt = \lim_{h\to 0}f(c_h) = f(x)$$

where $c_h \in (x, x+h)$ by the MVT for integrals, and $c_h \to x$ forces $f(c_h) \to f(x)$ by continuity. $\blacksquare$

---

## 3. FTC Part 2: $I_a \circ D = $ Net Change

**Theorem (FTC Part 2).** If $F$ is differentiable on $[a,b]$ with continuous derivative $F'$:

$$I_a(DF)(b) = \int_a^b F'(x), dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

In operator notation: $I_a \circ D \neq \text{Id}$, but rather:

$$(I_a \circ D \circ F)(b) = F(b) - F(a)$$

**What this says:** Differentiate first, then integrate — you do **not** get back to $F(x)$. You get the **net change** $F(b) - F(a)$.

The round trip — differentiate then integrate — loses the starting value $F(a)$.

### Why It Is Not a Perfect Inverse

```
              differentiate
   F(x)  ─────────────────►  f(x) = F'(x)
     ▲                              │
     │  I∘D ≠ Id                   │  integrate from a to b
     │                              │
     └──────────────────────────────┘
       F(b) - F(a), not F(x)  ✗ (loses F(a))
```

Differentiation destroys the constant. If $F(x) = x^2 + 7$ and $G(x) = x^2 + 13$, then $F' = G' = 2x$. Integration recovers $2x$ but cannot reconstruct whether the constant was $7$ or $13$.

The information lost by $D$ is exactly the **initial value** $F(a)$.

## 4. The Precise Algebraic Relationship

Let us organize what we know into a clean statement.

**Setting:** Let $\mathcal{C}[a,b]$ be the space of continuous functions on $[a,b]$, and $\mathcal{C}^1[a,b]$ the space of continuously differentiable functions.

$$D: \mathcal{C}^1[a,b] \to \mathcal{C}[a,b], \qquad I_a: \mathcal{C}[a,b] \to \mathcal{C}^1[a,b]$$

**Result 1 (FTC Part 1):** $D \circ I_a = \text{Id}_{\mathcal{C}[a,b]}$

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t), dt = f(x) \quad \text{for all } f \in \mathcal{C}[a,b]$$

**Result 2 (FTC Part 2):** $I_a \circ D \neq \text{Id}_{\mathcal{C}^1[a,b]}$, but:

$$\int_a^x F'(t), dt = F(x) - F(a) \quad \text{for all } F \in \mathcal{C}^1[a,b]$$

So $I_a \circ D$ recovers $F$ up to the lost constant $F(a)$:

$$(I_a \circ D)(F)(x) = F(x) - F(a)$$

**Recovery formula:** $F(x) = (I_a \circ D)(F)(x) + F(a)$.

### The Kernel of $D$

The failure of $I \circ D = \text{Id}$ is quantified by the **kernel** of $D$:

$$\ker(D) = {F \in \mathcal{C}^1[a,b] : DF = 0} = {\text{constant functions}}$$

Differentiation kills constants. Integration cannot reconstruct them. The information lost is exactly a real number — the value $F(a)$.

This is the algebraic content of "$+C$" in indefinite integration.

---

## 5. The Indefinite Integral Resolves the Asymmetry

The **indefinite integral** $\int f(x), dx = F(x) + C$ is designed to represent the entire family of antiderivatives — recovering all the information that $D$ could have destroyed.

If we define:

$$J: f \mapsto {F + C : C \in \mathbb{R}}$$

then $D \circ J = \text{Id}$ and $J \circ D(F) = F + C$ — the full family containing $F$, not the specific function. The constant $C$ encodes the "missing" information.

The definite integral $I_a$ makes a **choice**: fix $C$ by requiring $g(a) = 0$. That pins down exactly one antiderivative.

### 6.3 The Asymmetry in Pictures

**Differentiate then integrate:**

$$F(x) = x^2 + 5 \xrightarrow{D} 2x \xrightarrow{I_0} x^2$$

We get $x^2$, not $x^2 + 5$. The constant $5$ was annihilated by $D$.

**Integrate then differentiate:**

$$f(x) = 2x \xrightarrow{I_0} x^2 \xrightarrow{D} 2x$$

We get $2x$ back exactly. The round trip is perfect.

This asymmetry is fundamental. $D$ is like **erasing a digit**: you can erase $5$ to get $_$, and writing it back in (specifying $+C$) restores full information. But once you hand me $_$ without telling me what was erased, I cannot recover it.

## 7. The Telescope: Why FTC Part 2 Works

The cleanest proof of FTC Part 2 reveals the mechanism of partial inversion.

Partition $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals. On each $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, the **MVT for derivatives** gives $c_i$ with:

$$F(x_i) - F(x_{i-1}) = F'(c_i),\Delta x$$

Sum:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n [F(x_i) - F(x_{i-1})] = \sum_{i=1}^n F'(c_i),\Delta x$$

Left side **telescopes** to $F(b) - F(a)$. Right side is a Riemann sum for $\int_a^b F'$. As $n\to\infty$:

$$F(b) - F(a) = \int_a^b F'(x), dx$$

The telescope is the mechanism. Integration sums up all the **local increments** $F'(c_i)\Delta x$, and they perfectly reconstruct the **net global change** $F(b)-F(a)$ — but only the net change, not the starting point.

```
F(b) - F(a)
= [F(x₁)-F(x₀)] + [F(x₂)-F(x₁)] + ··· + [F(xₙ)-F(xₙ₋₁)]   ← telescopes
≈ F'(c₁)Δx    +  F'(c₂)Δx    + ··· +  F'(cₙ)Δx              ← MVT
→ ∫ₐᵇ F'(x) dx                                                ← n→∞
```

**The telescope metaphor:** Each term $F(x_i) - F(x_{i-1})$ is a piece of the story. Integration is the act of reading all the pieces together. The story it recovers is the total change — not the initial chapter.

## 8. The Full Inverse Relationship: A Precise Summary

$$\boxed{ \begin{aligned} &\textbf{FTC Part 1 (Perfect inversion):}\ &\quad \frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t), dt = f(x) \quad &&\text{if } f \text{ continuous}\[8pt] &\textbf{FTC Part 2 (Imperfect: loses initial value):}\ &\quad \int_a^b F'(x), dx = F(b) - F(a) \quad &&\text{if } F' \text{ continuous}\[8pt] &\textbf{Recovery (complete inversion using initial condition):}\ &\quad F(x) = F(a) + \int_a^x F'(t), dt \quad &&\text{(ODE initial value problem)} \end{aligned} }$$

The third line is the key insight: FTC Part 2 becomes a **perfect inverse** once you supply the initial value $F(a)$. This is the fundamental theorem of ODEs — knowing $F'$ everywhere and $F$ at one point completely determines $F$ everywhere.

## 9. Analogy: Summation and Differencing

The discrete analogs sharpen the intuition.

Let $(a_n)$ be a sequence. Define:

- **Forward difference operator:** $\Delta a_n = a_{n+1} - a_n$ (discrete derivative)
- **Summation operator:** $\Sigma_k^n a_i = \sum_{i=k}^n a_i$ (discrete integral)

Then:

$$\sum_{i=k}^{n} \Delta a_i = \sum_{i=k}^n (a_{i+1} - a_i) = a_{n+1} - a_k \quad \text{(telescoping = discrete FTC Part 2)}$$

$$\Delta \left(\sum_{i=k}^n a_i\right) = a_{n+1} \quad \text{(discrete FTC Part 1)}$$

The discrete FTC **is** the telescoping sum. The continuous FTC is the limiting form of this same fact.

|Discrete|Continuous|
|---|---|
|Sequence $a_n$|Function $F(x)$|
|Difference $\Delta a_n = a_{n+1}-a_n$|Derivative $F'(x)$|
|Telescoping sum $\sum \Delta a_i = a_n - a_0$|$\int_a^b F', dx = F(b)-F(a)$|
|Summation then difference = Id|Integration then differentiation = Id|
|Difference then summation = net change|Differentiation then integration = net change|

## 10. Where the Inversion Fails

The FTC requires hypotheses. Each hypothesis failure breaks the inversion in a specific way.

### Failure 1: Discontinuity of $f$

If $f$ has a jump at $x_0$, then $g(x) = \int_a^x f$ is continuous but **not differentiable** at $x_0$. FTC Part 1 fails at the discontinuity:

$$g'(x_0) \text{ does not exist}$$

$$\text{Example: } f(x) = \begin{cases}0 & x < 0 \ 1 & x \geq 0\end{cases}, \quad g(x) = \int_{-1}^x f(t),dt = \max(0, x)$$

$g$ is the ramp function — continuous but not differentiable at $0$.

### Failure 2: Non-integrable singularity

$$\int_{-1}^1 \frac{1}{x^2}, dx \neq \left[-\frac{1}{x}\right]_{-1}^1$$

$F(x) = -1/x$ is an antiderivative of $1/x^2$ on $(-\infty,0)$ and on $(0,\infty)$ separately, but not across the singularity at $0$. The integral **diverges**. FTC Part 2 requires $F'$ continuous on the **entire** closed interval — no exceptions.

### Failure 3: Volterra's function (pathological)

There exist functions $F$ that are differentiable everywhere with $F'$ bounded, yet $F'$ is **not Riemann integrable**. For such $F$, FTC Part 2 fails in the Riemann sense (though it holds for the Lebesgue integral). This is why Lebesgue integration is the right framework for a fully general FTC.

### Failure 4: Complex analysis — a happy failure

In complex analysis, $f(z) = 1/z$ has no antiderivative on any domain containing the origin. The integral $\oint_{|z|=1} \frac{1}{z}, dz = 2\pi i \neq 0$ — the round trip does not return to zero. This "failure" of the FTC is **Cauchy's theorem** in disguise, and is the foundation of complex analysis and residue theory.

## 11. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

The inversion relationship between $D$ and $I$ is operationally present throughout modern ML.

### 11.1 Forward Pass and Backward Pass

The entire training loop of a neural network is organized around this inversion:

|Direction|Operation|Mathematical Role|
|---|---|---|
|**Forward pass**|Compute $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$|Evaluate $F$|
|**Backward pass (backprop)**|Compute $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$|Apply $D$ to $F$|
|**Gradient step**|$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta\nabla\mathcal{L}$|Apply $I$ to $DF$|
|**Net result**|$\Delta\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{L}_{\text{new}} - \mathcal{L}_{\text{old}}$|$I \circ D$ = net change|

The forward-backward-update cycle is one instance of "differentiate, then integrate" — FTC Part 2 applied to the loss landscape. The gradient step never recovers the full loss function; it only moves along it by a net change.

### 11.2 Neural ODEs: The Inversion Made Explicit

A **Neural ODE** directly instantiates $I \circ D = $ net change:

$$z(T) = z(0) + \underbrace{\int_0^T f_\theta(z(t), t), dt}_{I \circ D \text{ applied to } z}$$

The forward pass is $I$. The defining ODE $\dot{z} = f_\theta$ is $D$. Training requires computing $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta}$ — differentiating through the integral — which is exactly $D \circ I$ applied to a loss functional.

The **adjoint method** exploits FTC Part 1 ($D \circ I = \text{Id}$) to avoid storing intermediate states: instead of backpropagating through every integration step, it solves a new ODE backward in time. The correctness of this trick rests entirely on FTC Part 1.

### 11.3 Score-Based Generative Models (Diffusion)

A **diffusion model** corrupts data via a forward SDE:

$$dx = -\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x, dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)}, dW$$

and learns to reverse it. The **score function** $s_\theta(x,t) \approx \nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ is the gradient (differentiation) of the log-density. The reverse SDE integrates this score:

$$dx = \left[-\frac{1}{2}\beta(t)x - \beta(t)s_\theta(x,t)\right]dt + \sqrt{\beta(t)}, d\bar{W}$$

The entire generative process is: **learn the derivative** (score) of the log-density, then **integrate** to generate samples. This is $I \circ D$ — imperfect inversion that recovers the distribution up to the initial noise (the "lost constant").

### 11.4 Continuous Normalizing Flows

An **CNF** models a density via the change of variables formula. If $z(t)$ evolves by $\dot{z} = f_\theta(z,t)$, the log-density evolves by:

$$\frac{d\log p(z(t))}{dt} = -\text{tr}!\left(\frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial z}\right)$$

This is $D$ applied to the log-density along the flow. The log-likelihood at $t=1$ is:

$$\log p(z(1)) = \log p(z(0)) - \int_0^1 \text{tr}!\left(\frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial z}\right) dt$$

This is FTC Part 2: integrate the derivative ($D$ applied to log $p$) to get the net change in log-density. The initial value $\log p(z(0))$ must be specified separately — exactly the "lost constant" of $I \circ D$.

### 11.5 The Fundamental Inversion in Optimization Theory

In convex optimization, if $f$ is convex and $x^* = \arg\min f$, then:

$$f(x) - f(x^_) = \int_0^1 \langle \nabla f(x^_ + t(x-x^_)),\ x - x^_\rangle, dt$$

This is FTC Part 2 — the excess loss $f(x) - f(x^_)$ equals the integral of the gradient along a line. Convergence proofs for gradient descent (showing $f(x_k) \to f(x^_)$) use this identity.

The **Polyak-Łojasiewicz inequality**, the **gradient dominance condition**, and **smoothness bounds** are all forms of controlling the relationship between $D$ (gradient) and $I$ (accumulated loss change).

### 11.6 Residual Networks as Discrete FTC

A ResNet computes:

$$x_{k+1} = x_k + f_\theta(x_k)$$

This is the **Euler method** for integrating $\dot{x} = f_\theta(x)$. The output $x_K$ satisfies:

$$x_K = x_0 + \sum_{k=0}^{K-1} f_\theta(x_k) \approx x_0 + \int_0^T f_\theta(x(t)), dt$$

This is the **discrete FTC**: sum of increments ($\approx$ derivatives) equals net change. A ResNet is a telescoping sum — literally the discrete version of $I \circ D$.

The skip connections are what make ResNets equivalent to numerical ODE integrators. Remove the skip connections and you lose the telescoping structure — this is part of why plain deep networks are harder to train.

### Summary Table

|Inversion Concept|ML/DL Analog|
|---|---|
|$D \circ I_a = \text{Id}$ (perfect)|Adjoint method: backprop through ODE avoids redundant computation|
|$I \circ D = $ net change (imperfect)|Training step: loss decreases by $\int \|\nabla\mathcal{L}\|^2, dt$|
|Lost constant = initial value $F(a)$|Initial hidden state $h_0$; initial noise $z_0$ in diffusion|
|Recovery: $F(x) = F(a) + \int_a^x F'$|ODE initial value problem; score-based generation|

|Failure at discontinuity|Non-smooth activations (ReLU): subgradient needed|

## 12. The Wider World: $D$ and $I$ in Other Settings

The inversion relationship extends far beyond single-variable calculus:

### Multivariable Calculus (MIT 18.02)

The gradient $\nabla F$ is the multivariable derivative. Line integration is the multivariable integral. Their relationship:

$$\int_C \nabla F \cdot d\mathbf{r} = F(\mathbf{b}) - F(\mathbf{a})$$

This is the **gradient theorem** — FTC for line integrals. Perfect analog: integrate the derivative along a path, get net change. Path-independence of conservative fields follows directly.

### Differential Equations

The ODE $F' = f$, $F(a) = F_0$ has unique solution:

$$F(x) = F_0 + \int_a^x f(t), dt$$

This is the **complete inversion**: supply both $f$ (the derivative) and $F_0$ (the lost constant), and FTC Part 2 reconstructs $F$ exactly. The initial condition restores the information $D$ destroyed.

### Functional Analysis

On $L^2[a,b]$, differentiation $D$ and integration $I_a$ are **adjoints** in a suitable sense. The theory of Sobolev spaces, weak derivatives, and PDEs is built on making this adjoint relationship precise for non-smooth functions.

## 13. Quick-Reference Cheatsheet

$$\boxed{D \circ I_a = \text{Id}}: \quad \frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t),dt = f(x)$$

$$\boxed{I_a \circ D = \text{net change}}: \quad \int_a^b F'(x),dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

$$\boxed{\text{Complete inversion}}: \quad F(x) = F(a) + \int_a^x F'(t),dt$$

$$\ker(D) = {\text{constants}} \implies \text{indefinite integral} = F + C$$

$$\text{Discrete analog}: \quad \sum_{i=k}^{n}\Delta a_i = a_{n+1} - a_k \quad \text{(telescoping)}$$

$$\text{Fails when}: \begin{cases} f \text{ has jump} & \Rightarrow g = I_a f \text{ not differentiable there} \ F' \text{ has singularity in } [a,b] & \Rightarrow \text{FTC Part 2 inapplicable} \end{cases}$$

$$\text{ResNet: } x_K = x_0 + \sum_{k=0}^{K-1}f_\theta(x_k) \approx x_0 + \int_0^T f_\theta,dt \quad \text{(discrete FTC)}$$
