

> _"Symmetry is one of the most powerful problem-solving tools in mathematics. When a function has it, the integral often collapses to something trivial — or doubles into something clean. Learning to see symmetry before computing is the mark of a mature analyst."_

---

## § 0 — The Core Idea

Before you touch an antiderivative, ask: **does this integrand have symmetry?**

If the interval of integration is symmetric about the origin, $[-a, a]$, and the function is either purely even or purely odd, the integral either vanishes immediately or halves. This is not an approximation. It is an exact theorem, and it saves enormous computation.

The result is elementary but its reach is surprisingly wide — from trigonometric integrals to Fourier analysis to the symmetry arguments underlying quantum mechanics and neural network weight initialization.

## § 1 — Even and Odd Functions: Definitions and Geometry

### Definitions

A function $f$ defined on a symmetric interval $[-a, a]$ (or all of $\mathbb{R}$) is:

- **Even** if $f(-x) = f(x)$ for all $x$ in the domain
- **Odd** if $f(-x) = -f(x)$ for all $x$ in the domain

### Geometric Meaning

### Standard Examples

|Even|Odd|
|---|---|
|$x^{2n}$ (even powers)|$x^{2n+1}$ (odd powers)|
|$\cos x$|$\sin x$|
|$\cosh x$|$\sinh x$|
|$\|x\|$|$x^3 - x$|
|$e^{x^2}$|$x e^{-x^2}$|
|$f(x) + f(-x)$ (symmetrization)|$f(x) - f(-x)$ (antisymmetrization)|

> **Remark.** Most functions are neither even nor odd. But _every_ function with a symmetric domain decomposes uniquely as a sum of an even and an odd part: $$f(x) = \underbrace{\frac{f(x)+f(-x)}{2}}_{\text{even part}} + \underbrace{\frac{f(x)-f(-x)}{2}}_{\text{odd part}}$$ This decomposition is used heavily in Fourier analysis.

## § 2 — The Symmetry Theorems

### Theorem (Integrals of Symmetric Functions)

Let $f$ be integrable on $[-a, a]$.

**Part (i) — Odd function:** $$f \text{ odd} \implies \int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = 0$$

**Part (ii) — Even function:** $$f \text{ even} \implies \int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = 2\int_0^a f(x),dx$$

### Proof of Both Parts

Split the integral at $0$:

$$\int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = \int_{-a}^0 f(x),dx + \int_0^a f(x),dx$$

In the first integral, substitute $x = -t$, so $dx = -dt$. When $x = -a$, $t = a$; when $x = 0$, $t = 0$:

$$\int_{-a}^0 f(x),dx = \int_a^0 f(-t)(-dt) = \int_0^a f(-t),dt$$

Therefore:

$$\int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = \int_0^a f(-t),dt + \int_0^a f(t),dt = \int_0^a \bigl[f(-t) + f(t)\bigr],dt$$

**Case (i): $f$ odd.** Then $f(-t) = -f(t)$, so $f(-t) + f(t) = 0$: $$\int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = \int_0^a 0,dt = 0 \qquad \square$$

**Case (ii): $f$ even.** Then $f(-t) = f(t)$, so $f(-t) + f(t) = 2f(t)$: $$\int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = \int_0^a 2f(t),dt = 2\int_0^a f(x),dx \qquad \square$$

> **Proof anatomy.** The entire argument rests on a single substitution $x = -t$ plus the definition of symmetry. This substitution "folds" the left half $[-a, 0]$ onto the right half $[0, a]$, and the symmetry condition tells you what happens when the two halves meet.

## § 4 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Vanishing by Oddness

$$\int_{-3}^{3} \left(x^5 - 4x^3 + \sin x\right)dx$$

Check each term: $x^5$ is odd, $4x^3$ is odd, $\sin x$ is odd. Their sum is odd. On $[-3, 3]$:

$$\int_{-3}^{3} \left(x^5 - 4x^3 + \sin x\right)dx = 0$$

No computation required.

---

### Example 2 — Doubling by Evenness

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos^2 x,dx$$

$\cos^2(-x) = \cos^2(x)$, so $\cos^2 x$ is even:

$$= 2\int_0^{\pi} \cos^2 x,dx = 2\int_0^{\pi} \frac{1 + \cos 2x}{2},dx = \int_0^{\pi}(1+\cos 2x),dx$$

$$= \left[x + \frac{\sin 2x}{2}\right]_0^{\pi} = \pi$$

---

### Example 3 — Splitting a Mixed Integrand

$$\int_{-2}^{2} \left(x^4 + x^3 + 3\right)dx$$

Split by symmetry type: $x^4 + 3$ is even, $x^3$ is odd.

$$= \int_{-2}^{2}(x^4 + 3),dx + \underbrace{\int_{-2}^{2} x^3,dx}_{=,0} = 2\int_0^2(x^4 + 3),dx$$

$$= 2\left[\frac{x^5}{5} + 3x\right]_0^2 = 2\left(\frac{32}{5} + 6\right) = 2 \cdot \frac{62}{5} = \frac{124}{5}$$

---

### Example 4 — A Non-Obvious Odd Function

$$\int_{-1}^{1} \frac{x^3 + x}{1 + x^2 + x^4},dx$$

Let $f(x) = \dfrac{x^3 + x}{1 + x^2 + x^4}$. Check:

$$f(-x) = \frac{-x^3 - x}{1 + x^2 + x^4} = -f(x)$$

$f$ is odd. Therefore:

$$\int_{-1}^{1} \frac{x^3 + x}{1 + x^2 + x^4},dx = 0$$

The antiderivative of this rational function would be genuinely painful. Symmetry gives the answer in two lines.

---

### Example 5 — Trigonometric Integral (Odd $\times$ Even)

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin^3 x \cos^2 x,dx$$

$\sin^3 x$ is odd, $\cos^2 x$ is even. The product of an odd and an even function is **odd**. Therefore:

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin^3 x \cos^2 x,dx = 0$$

> **Parity multiplication rules.** odd $\times$ even $=$ odd. odd $\times$ odd $=$ even. even $\times$ even $=$ even. This mirrors the sign algebra of ${+1, -1}$ under multiplication.

---

### Example 6 — The Sigmoid

$$\int_{-1}^{1} \frac{e^x}{1 + e^x},dx$$

Let $\sigma(x) = \dfrac{e^x}{1+e^x}$. Note that $\sigma(x) + \sigma(-x) = 1$, so:

$$\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{2} + \underbrace{\left(\sigma(x) - \frac{1}{2}\right)}_{\text{odd part}}$$

The odd part integrates to zero on $[-1,1]$; the constant $\frac{1}{2}$ integrates to $1$:

$$\int_{-1}^{1}\sigma(x),dx = 1$$

## § 5 — The Algebra of Even and Odd Functions

|Operation|Result|
|---|---|
|even $+$ even|even|
|odd $+$ odd|odd|
|even $+$ odd|neither (in general)|
|even $\times$ even|even|
|odd $\times$ odd|**even**|
|even $\times$ odd|**odd**|
|$\frac{d}{dx}$(even)|odd|
|$\frac{d}{dx}$(odd)|even|
|$\int_0^x$(even $dt$)|odd (with zero constant)|
|$\int_0^x$(odd $dt$)|even|

The derivative/integral rows follow from FTC. If $f$ is even and $g(x) = \int_0^x f(t),dt$, then substituting $t = -s$:

$$g(-x) = \int_0^{-x} f(t),dt = -\int_0^x f(-s),ds = -\int_0^x f(s),ds = -g(x)$$

So $g$ is odd. $\square$

> **Deep pattern.** Even functions $\leftrightarrow$ cosines; odd functions $\leftrightarrow$ sines. This is Fourier's insight: every periodic function decomposes into even (cosine) and odd (sine) components. The integral theorem above is the seed of the orthogonality relations in Fourier analysis.

## § 6 — Extension: Symmetry About $x = c$

The theorem requires $[-a, a]$ (symmetric about $0$). For an interval symmetric about $c$, shift first.

### Theorem (Symmetry About $x = c$)

Let the interval be $[c - a, c + a]$. Substitute $t = x - c$:

$$\int_{c-a}^{c+a} f(x),dx = \int_{-a}^{a} f(t+c),dt$$

Now apply the odd/even theorem to $\tilde{f}(t) = f(t+c)$.

**Example.** $\displaystyle\int_1^5 (x-3)^3,dx$. Let $t = x-3$, interval becomes $[-2, 2]$:

$$\int_{-2}^{2} t^3,dt = 0 \quad (\text{odd function on symmetric interval})$$

**Example.** $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi} \sin x,dx$. Let $t = x - \pi/2$:

$$\int_{-\pi/2}^{\pi/2} \cos t,dt = 2\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos t,dt = 2[\sin t]_0^{\pi/2} = 2 \checkmark$$

## § 7 — Connection to MIT 18.02

In multivariable calculus, symmetry arguments become even more powerful.

### Double Integrals over Symmetric Regions

If $D$ is symmetric about the $y$-axis and $f(-x, y) = -f(x, y)$ (odd in $x$):

$$\iint_D f(x, y),dA = 0$$

**Example.** $\iint_D xy,dA$ where $D$ is the unit disk. The integrand $xy$ is odd in $x$, and the disk is symmetric about the $y$-axis:

$$\iint_D xy,dA = 0$$

No Jacobian, no polar conversion, no computation.

### Trigonometric Orthogonality

The integrals $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\sin\theta,d\theta = 0$ and $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}\cos\theta,d\theta = 0$ appear constantly in polar and spherical coordinate calculations. More precisely, for integers $m \neq n$:

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin(mx)\cos(nx),dx = 0$$

because $\sin(mx)\cos(nx)$ is odd (odd $\times$ even). This is the symmetry theorem. Every such orthogonality relation in Fourier series for mixed sin-cos terms reduces to this one observation.

## § 8 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 8.1 — Weight Initialization and Symmetry Breaking

With all weights initialized to zero, neurons at the same layer compute identical outputs — the **symmetry problem**. With symmetric (zero-mean) weight distributions, for any odd activation $\phi$ (e.g., $\tanh$):

$$\mathbb{E}_{w \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)}[\phi(wx)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\phi(wx)\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}e^{-w^2/2\sigma^2},dw = 0$$

The integrand is odd in $w$ (odd $\times$ even), the domain is symmetric, so the integral vanishes. The expected output of a $\tanh$ neuron under symmetric random weights is exactly zero. **He and Xavier initialization** introduce careful scaling of variance, not to destroy this symmetry, but to ensure gradients neither explode nor vanish despite it.

### 8.2 — Fourier Features and Attention

Random Fourier feature methods (Rahimi & Recht) and positional encodings in transformers rely on the orthogonality of sinusoids. The identity:

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin(mx)\cos(nx),dx = 0 \quad \text{for all } m, n$$

holds because the integrand is odd. Positional encodings use alternating $\sin$ and $\cos$ features at different frequencies; the orthogonality (which is the symmetry theorem) guarantees these features carry independent information.

### 8.3 — Batch Normalization and Odd Activations

After batch normalization, pre-activations are approximately $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. For any odd activation $\phi$:

$$\mathbb{E}_{z\sim\mathcal{N}(0,1)}[\phi(z)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\phi(z)\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-z^2/2},dz = 0$$

Odd activations ($\tanh$, $\text{erf}$, $\sinh$) have zero mean output after batch norm — a statistically clean property. ReLU, which is neither even nor odd, has mean output $\approx 0.5$ under $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, introducing a positive bias that must be handled. This asymmetry is one reason batch norm is more critical for ReLU networks.

### 8.4 — The Sigmoid's Even-Odd Decomposition

From Example 6 in general: since $\sigma(x) - \frac{1}{2}$ is odd:

$$\int_{-a}^{a}\sigma(x),dx = a$$

This has a direct interpretation: under zero-mean inputs, the sigmoid neuron has expected output $\frac{1}{2}$ — a constant positive bias. This saturation at $\frac{1}{2}$ is one reason $\tanh$ (which is odd, with zero mean) is often preferred over sigmoid in symmetric architectures.

### 8.5 — Score Matching and Stein's Identity

In score-based generative models (DDPM, score matching), the **Stein identity** states:

$$\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p}\left[\nabla_x \log p(x) \cdot \phi(x)\right] = -\mathbb{E}_{x \sim p}[\nabla_x \phi(x)]$$

When $p$ is symmetric (e.g., Gaussian) and $\phi$ is odd, both sides vanish — one by the symmetry theorem, the other by FTC on an odd function. This gives a zero-value **sanity check** for score network implementations: certain test expectations should be exactly zero under symmetric data.

### 8.6 — $L^2$ and $L^1$ Regularization

The $L^2$ regularizer $R(\theta) = \sum_i \theta_i^2$ is even in each $\theta_i$. Its gradient $\nabla_\theta R = 2\theta$ is odd — it always pushes symmetrically toward zero, with no directional bias. The $L^1$ regularizer $\sum_i |\theta_i|$ is also even; its subgradient $\text{sgn}(\theta_i)$ is odd. The symmetry of both regularizers about $\theta = 0$ is what makes them geometrically unbiased: they penalize deviation from zero equally in both directions.

## § 9 — Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Non-symmetric interval.** $\int_0^3 x^3,dx \neq 0$. The interval $[0, 3]$ is not symmetric about $0$. Oddness only kills the integral on $[-a, a]$.

**Mistake 2: Not checking the condition.** Always verify $f(-x) = \pm f(x)$ explicitly. Many functions look symmetric but are not. $e^x$ is neither even nor odd.

**Mistake 3: odd $\times$ odd = even, not odd.** $\int_{-a}^a x \cdot \sin x,dx \neq 0$. The product $x \cdot \sin x$ is even (odd $\times$ odd), so the integral doubles: $= 2\int_0^a x\sin x,dx$.

**Mistake 4: Singularities on the interval.** $f(x) = 1/x$ is odd, but $\int_{-1}^{1} 1/x,dx$ does not exist in the Riemann sense. The theorem requires integrability. (The Cauchy principal value is zero, but this is a separate concept.)

**Mistake 5: Not decomposing a mixed function.** For $\int_{-2}^{2}(e^x + x^2),dx$: write $e^x = \cosh x + \sinh x$. The odd part $\sinh x$ integrates to zero; the even part $\cosh x$ doubles. Always decompose before integrating.

## § 10 — Summary

$$f \text{ odd on } [-a,a]: \quad \int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = 0$$

$$f \text{ even on } [-a,a]: \quad \int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = 2\int_0^a f(x),dx$$

|Concept|Content|
|---|---|
|**Proof key step**|Substitute $x = -t$, fold $[-a,0]$ onto $[0,a]$, use $f(-t) \pm f(t)$|
|**Parity of products**|odd$\times$even = odd; odd$\times$odd = even; even$\times$even = even|
|**Unique decomposition**|$f = \frac{f(x)+f(-x)}{2} + \frac{f(x)-f(-x)}{2}$|
|**Symmetry about $c$**|Shift $t = x-c$, then apply theorem to $f(t+c)$|
|**18.02 extension**|Odd integrand over symmetric domain → double integral vanishes; Fourier orthogonality|
|**ML applications**|Weight initialization, batch norm, sigmoid bias, score function testing, regularizer geometry|
