
> _"The area function is the moment calculus becomes self-aware. We stop asking 'what is the area under this curve?' and start asking 'what function does the process of accumulating area produce?' The answer — that accumulation undoes differentiation — is the deepest theorem in elementary mathematics."_

---

## 0. The Central Question

We know how to compute $\int_a^b f(t), dt$ when $a$ and $b$ are fixed numbers. It gives a single real number.

Now ask something different: **what if the upper limit is allowed to vary?**

Fix $a$. For each $x \in [a, b]$, define:

$$g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$$

This is no longer a number — it is a **function of $x$**. It measures the **accumulated signed area** under $f$ from the fixed left endpoint $a$ to the moving right endpoint $x$.

This function $g$ is called an **area function** (or **accumulation function**). The question is: what are its properties? Is it differentiable? If so, what is $g'(x)$?

The answer is the **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 1** — and it is not obvious.

---

## 1. Building Intuition: A Concrete Example

Let $f(t) = t$ (a simple ramp) and $a = 0$. Define:

$$g(x) = \int_0^x t, dt$$

For each $x$, $g(x)$ is the area of a right triangle with base $x$ and height $x$:

$$g(x) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot x \cdot x = \frac{x^2}{2}$$

Now observe:

$$g'(x) = \frac{d}{dx}\left(\frac{x^2}{2}\right) = x = f(x)$$

**The derivative of the area function equals the original function.** This is not a coincidence.

### Another Example

Let $f(t) = \cos t$, $a = 0$:

$$g(x) = \int_0^x \cos t, dt = [\sin t]_0^x = \sin x$$

Then $g'(x) = \cos x = f(x)$. Again.

### And One More

Let $f(t) = e^t$, $a = 0$:

$$g(x) = \int_0^x e^t, dt = [e^t]_0^x = e^x - 1$$

Then $g'(x) = e^x = f(x)$. Every time.

The pattern is undeniable. The derivative of $\int_a^x f(t), dt$ is $f(x)$.

---

## 2. Visualization: The Area Function as a Running Total

Think of $f(t)$ as a **rate** — say, water flowing into a tank at rate $f(t)$ liters/second.

$$g(x) = \int_0^x f(t), dt = \text{total water in tank at time } x$$

The **rate of change of the total** at time $x$ is... the current flow rate — which is $f(x)$.

This is obvious physically. The FTC says it is also mathematically rigorous.

```
f(t):  rate of accumulation
  |
  |   ****
  |  *    *
  | *      *
  |*        *
  +-----------> t
  0          x

g(x) = ∫₀ˣ f(t) dt: total accumulated so far
  |                    *
  |                  **
  |               ***
  |           ****
  |      *****
  |******
  +-----------> x
  0
```

At any point $x$: the **slope** of $g$ equals the **height** of $f$.

- Where $f > 0$: $g$ is increasing
- Where $f < 0$: $g$ is decreasing
- Where $f = 0$: $g$ has a horizontal tangent
- Where $f$ has a maximum: $g$ has an inflection point

## 3. The Theorem: FTC Part 1

**Theorem (Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, Part 1 — Stewart §5.3).**

Let $f$ be continuous on $[a, b]$. Define the area function:

$$g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt, \quad x \in [a, b]$$

Then $g$ is **continuous** on $[a,b]$, **differentiable** on $(a,b)$, and:

$$\boxed{g'(x) = f(x)}$$

In Leibniz notation:

$$\frac{d}{dx} \int_a^x f(t), dt = f(x)$$

### Proof

By the definition of the derivative:

$$g'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{g(x+h) - g(x)}{h}$$

Compute the numerator using additivity of the integral:

$$g(x+h) - g(x) = \int_a^{x+h} f(t), dt - \int_a^x f(t), dt = \int_x^{x+h} f(t), dt$$

So:

$$g'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \int_x^{x+h} f(t), dt$$

Since $f$ is continuous on $[x, x+h]$ (assuming $h > 0$ small), the **Mean Value Theorem for Integrals** guarantees $\exists, c_h \in [x, x+h]$ such that:

$$\int_x^{x+h} f(t), dt = f(c_h) \cdot h$$

Therefore:

$$g'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(c_h) \cdot h}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} f(c_h)$$

As $h \to 0$, $c_h \in [x, x+h]$ forces $c_h \to x$. By **continuity** of $f$:

$$\lim_{h \to 0} f(c_h) = f(x)$$

Therefore $g'(x) = f(x)$. The case $h < 0$ is analogous. $\blacksquare$

### What the Proof Reveals

The proof uses **two** major theorems:

1. **MVT for Integrals** — the average value of $f$ on $[x, x+h]$ is achieved somewhere inside
2. **Continuity of $f$** — as the interval shrinks to a point, the average value converges to $f(x)$

Continuity is **essential**. If $f$ has a jump discontinuity at $x_0$, then $g$ is still continuous at $x_0$ but **not differentiable** there. The area function is always at least one derivative smoother than the integrand.

## 4. The Area Function is an Antiderivative

**Corollary.** $g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$ is an antiderivative of $f$ on $[a, b]$.

This is a profound statement. It says:

> **Every continuous function has an antiderivative.**

Before FTC, there was no guarantee that $\int_0^x e^{-t^2}, dt$ was the derivative of anything. FTC Part 1 guarantees it is — it is the derivative of itself, in the sense that:

$$\frac{d}{dx} \int_0^x e^{-t^2}, dt = e^{-x^2}$$

The function $g(x) = \int_0^x e^{-t^2}, dt$ is called the **error function** (up to scaling), written $\text{erf}(x)$. It has no elementary closed form — but it exists, it is differentiable, and FTC tells us its derivative exactly.

---

## 5. Changing the Lower Limit

The lower limit $a$ affects $g$ only by a constant. If we change $a$ to $b$:

$$\int_b^x f(t), dt = \int_a^x f(t), dt - \int_a^b f(t), dt = g(x) - C$$

So $\frac{d}{dx}\int_b^x f(t), dt = g'(x) = f(x)$ regardless of the lower limit.

The lower limit shifts the area function vertically — it does not change its derivative.

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_c^x f(t), dt = f(x) \quad \text{for any constant } c \in [a,b]$$

## 6. The Chain Rule Extension

What if the upper limit is not $x$ but $u(x)$?

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^{u(x)} f(t), dt = f(u(x)) \cdot u'(x)$$

This follows immediately from the Chain Rule applied to $g(u(x))$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[g(u(x))] = g'(u(x)) \cdot u'(x) = f(u(x)) \cdot u'(x)$$

### Examples

**Example 1.**

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_1^{x^3} \sin(t^2), dt = \sin(x^6) \cdot 3x^2$$

**Example 2.**

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_x^{x^2} e^{t^3}, dt$$

Split at any convenient constant $c$ (say $c = 0$):

$$= \frac{d}{dx}\int_0^{x^2} e^{t^3}, dt - \frac{d}{dx}\int_0^{x} e^{t^3}, dt = e^{x^6} \cdot 2x - e^{x^3}$$

**Example 3.** (Both limits variable)

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_{\sin x}^{\cos x} \sqrt{1+t^4}, dt = \sqrt{1+\cos^4 x}\cdot(-\sin x) - \sqrt{1+\sin^4 x}\cdot\cos x$$

The general rule: differentiate the upper limit contribution minus the lower limit contribution, each evaluated at the limit and multiplied by its derivative.

## 7. Reading $g$ from $f$: A Graphical Analysis

Given the graph of $f$, we can sketch $g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$ without computing anything.

### Rules

|Property of $f$ on $(p, q)$|Consequence for $g$|
|---|---|
|$f(x) > 0$|$g$ is **increasing**|
|$f(x) < 0$|$g$ is **decreasing**|
|$f(x) = 0$ (and sign change)|$g$ has a **local extremum**|
|$f$ increasing ($f' > 0$)|$g$ is **concave up**|
|$f$ decreasing ($f' < 0$)|$g$ is **concave down**|
|$f$ has a local max/min|$g$ has an **inflection point**|
|$f$ crosses $x$-axis from $+$ to $-$|$g$ has a **local maximum**|
|$f$ crosses $x$-axis from $-$ to $+$|$g$ has a **local minimum**|

### Example: Sketch $g$ from $f$

Suppose $f$ looks like this on $[0, 6]$:

```
f(t)
  |

  | *      *
  |*        *
--+----------*--------*----> t
  0    1    2    3   4*  5  6

```

$f > 0$ on $(0, 3)$: $g$ increases. $f = 0$ at $t = 3$: $g$ has a local max. $f < 0$ on $(3, 6)$: $g$ decreases. $f$ increases on $(0,1)$: $g$ concave up. $f$ decreases on $(1,3)$: $g$ concave down. Inflection of $g$ at $t = 1$ (where $f$ has its max).


## 8. Worked Problems

### Problem 1

Find $\dfrac{d}{dx}\displaystyle\int_0^x \frac{\sin t}{t}, dt$.

The integrand $\frac{\sin t}{t}$ has a removable discontinuity at $t = 0$ (define it as $1$ there), so $f$ is continuous. By FTC Part 1:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_0^x \frac{\sin t}{t}, dt = \frac{\sin x}{x}$$

This function, $\text{Si}(x) = \int_0^x \frac{\sin t}{t}, dt$, is called the **sine integral** — important in signal processing and optics. Its existence and differentiability are guaranteed by FTC.

### Problem 2

Let $h(x) = \displaystyle\int_1^{e^x} \ln t, dt$. Find $h'(x)$.

Chain rule: $u(x) = e^x$, $f(t) = \ln t$.

$$h'(x) = \ln(e^x) \cdot \frac{d}{dx}(e^x) = x \cdot e^x$$

### Problem 3

Find the inflection points of $g(x) = \displaystyle\int_0^x (t^2 - t - 2), dt$.

$g'(x) = x^2 - x - 2 = (x-2)(x+1)$

$g''(x) = 2x - 1$

$g''(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 1/2$. Check sign change: $g'' < 0$ for $x < 1/2$, $g'' > 0$ for $x > 1/2$.

Inflection point at $x = 1/2$.

(Note: $g'(1/2) = (1/4) - (1/2) - 2 = -9/4 \neq 0$, so this is genuinely an inflection, not an extremum.)

Also: $g'(x) = 0$ at $x = 2$ and $x = -1$ — local extrema of $g$.

### Problem 4 (Tricky)

Find $\lim_{x \to 0} \dfrac{\displaystyle\int_0^x \sin(t^2), dt}{x^3}$.

Direct substitution gives $0/0$ — L'Hôpital:

$$= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x^2)}{3x^2} = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin(x^2)}{3x^2} = \frac{1}{3}$$

(using $\lim_{u\to 0} \frac{\sin u}{u} = 1$ with $u = x^2$.)

**Key**: FTC Part 1 let us differentiate the numerator immediately: $\frac{d}{dx}\int_0^x \sin(t^2), dt = \sin(x^2)$.

## 9. Why the Dummy Variable $t$ Matters

A subtle but important point. In $g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt$, the variable $t$ is a **dummy variable** — it disappears after integration. We could write $\int_a^x f(u), du$ or $\int_a^x f(s), ds$ and get the same $g(x)$.

**We cannot write** $\int_a^x f(x), dx$ — this is a category error. The upper limit $x$ and the integration variable $x$ cannot share a name, because inside the integral, the integration variable ranges over $[a, x]$; the $x$ in the upper limit is fixed relative to that sweep.

$$\int_a^x f(x), dx \quad \leftarrow \text{ILLEGAL NOTATION}$$

$$\int_a^x f(t), dt \quad \leftarrow \text{correct}$$

This is not pedantry. Confusing bound and free variables is the source of many errors in both calculus and programming.

## 10. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 10.1 Accumulation Functions in Recurrent Networks

A **recurrent neural network (RNN)** computes a hidden state:

$$h_t = \sigma(W_h h_{t-1} + W_x x_t + b)$$

In the continuous limit (as the time step $\Delta t \to 0$), this becomes:

$$\frac{dh}{dt} = -h(t) + \sigma(W_x x(t) + b)$$

The hidden state $h(T)$ is an **area function**:

$$h(T) = h(0) + \int_0^T \left[-h(t) + \sigma(W_x x(t) + b)\right] dt$$

The hidden state is literally accumulating area under the transformed input signal. FTC Part 1 tells us that $\frac{dh}{dt}$ at any moment equals the current integrand — the network's current "velocity" of state change. This is precisely the continuous-time RNN (CT-RNN) formulation.

### 10.2 Neural ODEs: Area Functions as Forward Passes

In a **Neural ODE** (Chen et al., 2018):

$$z(T) = z(0) + \int_0^T f_\theta(z(t), t), dt$$

The output $z(T)$ is an area function with integrand $f_\theta$ — a neural network. The forward pass is numerical integration; the backward pass uses the **adjoint method**, which is itself derived by differentiating an area function.

By FTC Part 1:

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = f_\theta(z(t), t)$$

The "derivative of the area function equals the integrand" — this is the ODE that the network implicitly defines. FTC is the theorem that makes Neural ODEs well-posed.

### 10.3 Cumulative Distribution Functions

In probabilistic ML, every probability density $p(x)$ defines a **cumulative distribution function**:

$$F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x p(t), dt$$

$F$ is exactly an area function. FTC Part 1 gives:

$$F'(x) = p(x)$$

This is the foundational identity of probability theory: the density is the derivative of the CDF. When we use the **reparameterization trick** in VAEs, or when we compute **quantile functions** in quantile regression, we are working directly with area functions and their derivatives.

### 10.4 Gradient of a Loss Integral Over Time

In **reinforcement learning**, the return is:

$$G_t = \int_t^\infty r(s), e^{-\gamma(s-t)}, ds$$

(continuous-time discounted return). This is an area function of $t$ (with a moving lower limit). Differentiating:

$$\frac{dG_t}{dt} = -r(t) + \gamma G_t$$

By FTC Part 1 (with lower limit $t$ instead of upper):

$$\frac{d}{dt}\int_t^\infty r(s)e^{-\gamma(s-t)}, ds = -r(t) + \gamma \int_t^\infty r(s)e^{-\gamma(s-t)}, ds$$

This is the **Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman equation** in disguise — the fundamental equation of optimal control and RL. It follows directly from differentiating an area function.

### 10.5 Activation Functions as Area Functions

The **softplus** activation:

$$\text{softplus}(x) = \log(1 + e^x) = \int_{-\infty}^x \sigma(t), dt$$

where $\sigma(t) = 1/(1+e^{-t})$ is the sigmoid. By FTC:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\text{softplus}(x) = \sigma(x)$$

The sigmoid is the derivative of softplus — and both are area functions of each other. The entire zoo of smooth activation functions is organized by this differentiation-integration relationship.

## 11. Quick-Reference Cheatsheet

$$g(x) = \int_a^x f(t), dt \implies g'(x) = f(x) \quad \text{(FTC Part 1)}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^{u(x)} f(t), dt = f(u(x))\cdot u'(x) \quad \text{(Chain Rule)}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}\int_{v(x)}^{u(x)} f(t), dt = f(u(x))\cdot u'(x) - f(v(x))\cdot v'(x)$$

$$g(a) = \int_a^a f(t), dt = 0 \quad \text{(area function starts at zero)}$$

$$g \text{ increasing} \iff f > 0; \quad g \text{ concave up} \iff f \text{ increasing}$$

$$F'(x) = p(x) \quad \text{(CDF is area function of density)}$$
