
> _"The substitution rule is the integral counterpart of the chain rule — and just as the chain rule is the most-used differentiation tool, substitution is the most-used integration tool."_

---

## § 0 — Motivation: Why Substitution Exists

Direct antidifferentiation works for $\int x^2,dx$. It fails immediately for $\int 2x\sqrt{1+x^2},dx$. The integrand is a _composition_ — a function of a function — and the chain rule is what created it. Substitution is how we _undo_ the chain rule under an integral sign.

The idea is conceptually simple: rename part of the integrand as a new variable $u$, transforming a hard integral into a recognizable one. The rigor lies in understanding why this renaming is valid — it is not a notational trick, it is a theorem.

---

## § 1 — The Substitution Rule for Indefinite Integrals

### Theorem (Substitution Rule — Indefinite)

Let $g$ be a differentiable function on an interval $I$, and let $f$ be continuous on the range of $g$. Then:

$$\boxed{\int f(g(x)),g'(x),dx = \int f(u),du}$$

where $u = g(x)$.

### Proof

Let $F$ be an antiderivative of $f$, so $F' = f$. By the **Chain Rule**:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\bigl[F(g(x))\bigr] = F'(g(x))\cdot g'(x) = f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$$

Therefore $F(g(x))$ is an antiderivative of $f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$, so:

$$\int f(g(x)),g'(x),dx = F(g(x)) + C$$

On the other hand:

$$\int f(u),du = F(u) + C = F(g(x)) + C$$

The two sides are equal. $\square$

> **Key insight.** The substitution rule is the Chain Rule read backwards. Differentiation creates $f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$ from $F(g(x))$; substitution recovers $F(g(x))$ from $f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$.

### Differential Notation

Write $u = g(x)$, then $du = g'(x),dx$. This is the mnemonic:

$$\int f(\underbrace{g(x)}_{u})\underbrace{g'(x),dx}_{du} = \int f(u),du$$

The $dx$ is _not_ being cancelled like a fraction — but the differential notation is designed so that the algebra _behaves as if it were_, a consequence of the proof above.

## § 2 — The Substitution Rule for Definite Integrals

### Theorem (Substitution Rule — Definite)

Let $g'$ be continuous on $[a,b]$, and let $f$ be continuous on the range of $g$. Then:

$$\boxed{\int_a^b f(g(x)),g'(x),dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u),du}$$

### Proof

Let $F' = f$. By FTC Part 2 applied to the left side:

$$\int_a^b f(g(x)),g'(x),dx = \Bigl[F(g(x))\Bigr]_a^b = F(g(b)) - F(g(a))$$

By FTC Part 2 applied to the right side:

$$\int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u),du = \Bigl[F(u)\Bigr]_{g(a)}^{g(b)} = F(g(b)) - F(g(a))$$

The two sides are identical. $\square$

> **Critical remark.** The limits of integration change: $x = a \Rightarrow u = g(a)$ and $x = b \Rightarrow u = g(b)$. This is not optional. Forgetting to transform the limits — and then substituting $u$ back to $x$ — are the two most common errors.

## § 3 — Worked Examples (From Elementary to Subtle)

### Example 1 — Direct Recognition

$$\int 2x\sqrt{1+x^2},dx$$

Let $u = 1 + x^2$, so $du = 2x,dx$:

$$\int \sqrt{u},du = \frac{2}{3}u^{3/2} + C = \frac{2}{3}(1+x^2)^{3/2} + C$$

**Check by differentiating:** $\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{2}{3}(1+x^2)^{3/2}\right] = \frac{2}{3} \cdot \frac{3}{2}(1+x^2)^{1/2} \cdot 2x = 2x\sqrt{1+x^2}$. ✓

---

### Example 2 — Adjusting for a Missing Constant

$$\int x^2 e^{x^3},dx$$

Let $u = x^3$, so $du = 3x^2,dx$, i.e., $x^2,dx = \frac{1}{3}du$:

$$\frac{1}{3}\int e^u,du = \frac{1}{3}e^u + C = \frac{1}{3}e^{x^3} + C$$

---

### Example 3 — Definite Integral (Transform Limits)

$$\int_0^1 \frac{x}{(1+x^2)^3},dx$$

Let $u = 1+x^2$, $du = 2x,dx$. Limits: $x=0 \Rightarrow u=1$; $x=1 \Rightarrow u=2$.

$$\frac{1}{2}\int_1^2 u^{-3},du = \frac{1}{2}\left[-\frac{1}{2u^2}\right]_1^2 = \frac{1}{2}\left(-\frac{1}{8}+\frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{3}{8} = \frac{3}{16}$$

---

### Example 4 — Trigonometric Substitution Setup

$$\int \cos^5 x \sin x,dx$$

Let $u = \cos x$, $du = -\sin x,dx$:

$$-\int u^5,du = -\frac{u^6}{6} + C = -\frac{\cos^6 x}{6} + C$$

---

### Example 5 — Subtle: The Substitution is Non-Obvious

$$\int \frac{\ln x}{x},dx$$

Let $u = \ln x$, $du = \frac{1}{x}dx$:

$$\int u,du = \frac{u^2}{2} + C = \frac{(\ln x)^2}{2} + C$$

---

### Example 6 — Definite Integral with Symmetry

$$\int_{-2}^{2} x^3 \cos(x^2),dx$$

The integrand $f(x) = x^3\cos(x^2)$: note $x^3$ is odd, $\cos(x^2)$ is even, so their product is **odd**. For any odd function $f$ on $[-a,a]$:

$$\int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = 0$$

Therefore the answer is $0$ — no computation needed. Substitution is sometimes best avoided.

## § 4 — Visualization

### The Geometry of Substitution

```
Original integral: area under f(g(x))·g'(x) from a to b
                   in the x-coordinate system

         f(g(x))·g'(x)
          |   ████
          |  ██████
          | █████████
          |___________→ x
          a           b

After substitution u = g(x):
Area is preserved, redrawn in u-coordinates

         f(u)
          |     ██
          |   ██████
          |  █████████
          |____________→ u
         g(a)         g(b)
```

> The substitution is a _change of coordinates_. If $g$ is increasing and convex, the $u$-axis is a "stretched" version of the $x$-axis. The factor $g'(x)$ in the integrand is precisely the **Jacobian** of the coordinate change — it corrects for the stretching so that area is preserved.

### The Chain Rule / Substitution Duality

```
DIFFERENTIATION (Chain Rule):
  F(g(x)) ——d/dx——→ f(g(x)) · g'(x)
  
INTEGRATION (Substitution):
  f(g(x)) · g'(x) ——∫dx——→ F(g(x)) + C

These are exact reverses of each other.
Substitution is the chain rule run in reverse.
```

### Substitution as a Reparametrization

```
x-space:   [a ————————————————————— b]
              curves according to g
u-space:   [g(a) ————————————————— g(b)]
              straightened out

The differential du = g'(x)dx is the
"local magnification factor" at each point.
```

## § 5 — Symmetry Theorems (Bonus Tools via Substitution)

These follow directly from substitution with $u = -x$:

### Theorem (Odd/Even Symmetry)

Let $f$ be integrable on $[-a, a]$.

1. If $f$ is **even** ($f(-x) = f(x)$): $\displaystyle\int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = 2\int_0^a f(x),dx$
    
2. If $f$ is **odd** ($f(-x) = -f(x)$): $\displaystyle\int_{-a}^a f(x),dx = 0$
    

**Proof of (2).** Split: $\int_{-a}^a = \int_{-a}^0 + \int_0^a$. In the first piece, let $u = -x$, $du = -dx$:

$$\int_{-a}^0 f(x),dx = \int_a^0 f(-u)(-du) = \int_0^a f(-u),du = \int_0^a -f(u),du = -\int_0^a f(x),dx$$

Adding: $\int_{-a}^a f,dx = -\int_0^a f,dx + \int_0^a f,dx = 0$. $\square$

## § 6 — Common Mistakes and How to Catch Them

**Mistake 1: Forgetting to transform $dx$.** If $u = x^2$, then $du = 2x,dx$, not $du = dx$. The $dx$ must be fully replaced.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting to transform limits in definite integrals.** If you use limits of $x$ with the $u$-integrand, you get a wrong answer. Either transform limits (preferred) or back-substitute before evaluating.

**Mistake 3: The $g'(x)$ factor is not present.** $\int \sqrt{1+x^2},dx$ cannot be done by the substitution $u = 1+x^2$ because $du = 2x,dx$ and there is no $x$ in the integrand to absorb. A different technique (trig substitution) is needed.

**Mistake 4: Losing the constant of proportionality.** $\int e^{3x},dx$: Let $u = 3x$, $du = 3,dx$, so $dx = \frac{1}{3}du$. Answer: $\frac{1}{3}e^{3x}+C$. Forgetting the $\frac{1}{3}$ is the most common arithmetic error.

**Mistake 5: Applying substitution where symmetry gives zero instantly.** Always check odd/even symmetry on symmetric intervals before computing.

## § 7 — Connection to MIT 18.02 (Multivariable Extension)

In 18.02, the substitution rule generalizes to **change of variables in multiple integrals**.

For a transformation $(x,y) = \mathbf{g}(u,v)$:

$$\iint_R f(x,y),dA = \iint_S f(\mathbf{g}(u,v)),\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|,du,dv$$

The scalar $g'(x)$ in 1D becomes the **Jacobian determinant** $\left|\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right|$ in 2D — it measures how the transformation stretches area. This is exactly the same correction factor, now in matrix form.

**Classic 18.02 examples:**

- Polar coordinates: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, Jacobian $= r$, giving $dA = r,dr,d\theta$
- Spherical coordinates: $dV = \rho^2\sin\phi,d\rho,d\phi,d\theta$

Every change of variables in multivariable calculus is a higher-dimensional substitution.

## § 8 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 8.1 — The Reparametrization Trick (VAEs)

This is perhaps the most direct and famous use of substitution in deep learning.

In a **Variational Autoencoder (VAE)**, we want to compute:

$$\nabla_\phi ,\mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_\phi(z|x)}[\mathcal{L}(z)]$$

The gradient is with respect to $\phi$ (the encoder parameters), but $z$ is sampled from a distribution that _depends on_ $\phi$. Naively differentiating through a sampling operation is undefined.

**The fix is substitution.** Write $z = \mu_\phi(x) + \sigma_\phi(x) \cdot \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ (independent of $\phi$). Then:

$$\mathbb{E}_{z \sim q_\phi}[\mathcal{L}(z)] = \mathbb{E}_{\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)}[\mathcal{L}(\mu_\phi + \sigma_\phi \epsilon)]$$

This is a substitution $z = g_\phi(\epsilon)$ that moves the $\phi$-dependence out of the distribution and into the integrand, where $\nabla_\phi$ can act. The Jacobian factor here is absorbed into the change of variable.

**In integral notation:**

$$\int \mathcal{L}(z),q_\phi(z),dz = \int \mathcal{L}(g_\phi(\epsilon)),p(\epsilon),d\epsilon$$

This is literally the substitution theorem.

### 8.2 — Normalizing Flows

A **normalizing flow** transforms a simple distribution $p_\epsilon(\epsilon)$ into a complex one $p_x(x)$ via an invertible map $x = f(\epsilon)$. The **change of variables formula for probability densities** is:

$$p_x(x) = p_\epsilon(f^{-1}(x)),\left|\det\frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial x}\right|$$

This is the substitution rule in $\mathbb{R}^n$: the Jacobian determinant corrects for how the map $f$ stretches probability mass. The entire architecture of normalizing flows is built on applying the substitution theorem iteratively through a sequence of invertible layers.

### 8.3 — Feature Map Integrals (Kernel Methods)

In kernel methods, the kernel function is:

$$k(x, x') = \int \phi(x, \omega),\phi(x', \omega),p(\omega),d\omega$$

Random Fourier Features (Rahimi & Recht, 2007) approximate this by Monte Carlo. The substitution $\omega = g(z)$ for some base distribution $p(z)$ allows sampling from complex spectral distributions by transforming samples from simple ones. Again: substitution theorem, now inside an expectation over a feature space.

### 8.4 — Change of Variables in Loss Functions

When training with custom noise schedules (e.g., in **diffusion models** like DDPM or score-based models), the forward process adds noise according to a schedule $\alpha(t)$. The loss involves:

$$\mathcal{L} = \int_0^T w(t),\mathbb{E}\left[|\epsilon_\theta(x_t, t) - \epsilon|^2\right]dt$$

Changing time variable $t \to \lambda = \log\frac{\alpha(t)}{1-\alpha(t)}$ (log-SNR) simplifies the geometry of the loss. This is substitution — the weight function $w(t)$ transforms into $w(g^{-1}(\lambda))\cdot |{(g^{-1})}'(\lambda)|$, i.e., exactly $f(g(x))\cdot g'(x)$ under a new name.

### 8.5 — Integration by Substitution in Numerical Quadrature

Neural networks are often evaluated by integrating over their input space (e.g., computing expectations, partition functions, or marginal likelihoods). Substitution is used to:

- Map unbounded domains $(-\infty, \infty)$ to $(-1, 1)$ for Gaussian quadrature
- Importance sampling: $\int f(x),p(x),dx = \int f(x)\frac{p(x)}{q(x)},q(x),dx$, which is substitution of the measure

Importance sampling, which underlies MCMC, policy gradient (REINFORCE), and off-policy RL, is the substitution theorem applied to probability measures.

## § 9 — The Substitution Rule in the Bigger Picture

```
DIFFERENTIATION RULES          INTEGRATION COUNTERPARTS
──────────────────────────────────────────────────────
Power Rule                  ←→  Power Rule for integrals
Chain Rule                  ←→  Substitution Rule        ← (this note)
Product Rule                ←→  Integration by Parts
Quotient Rule               ←→  Partial Fractions
```

Every differentiation rule has an integration counterpart. Substitution is the most fundamental because the Chain Rule governs all composite functions, and _almost every interesting function is a composition_.

## § 10 — Summary

$$\int f(g(x)),g'(x),dx = \int f(u),du, \qquad u = g(x)$$

$$\int_a^b f(g(x)),g'(x),dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u),du$$

|Aspect|Content|
|---|---|
|**Theorem basis**|Chain Rule run in reverse|
|**Proof mechanism**|$\frac{d}{dx}[F(g(x))] = f(g(x))g'(x)$ + FTC|
|**Key requirement**|The factor $g'(x)$ must be present (up to a constant)|
|**Definite integrals**|Transform limits: $x: [a,b] \to u: [g(a), g(b)]$|
|**Symmetry**|Odd function on $[-a,a]$ → 0; even function → double half-interval|
|**18.02 extension**|Jacobian determinant replaces $g'(x)$ in multiple integrals|
|**ML applications**|Reparametrization trick (VAE), normalizing flows, diffusion model noise schedules, importance sampling|
