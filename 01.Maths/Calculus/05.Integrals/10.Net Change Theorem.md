

> _"The integral of a rate of change is the net change."_ — Stewart, Calculus §5.4

---

## § 0 — Why This Theorem Deserves Its Own Page

Most students encounter the Net Change Theorem as a footnote to FTC Part 2, barely a sentence long. But pause here. This is the theorem that turns the abstract machinery of integration into a _physical interpreter_. It says: **if you integrate a derivative, you recover displacement, not distance.** That distinction will haunt you through ODEs, physics, and eventually backpropagation in neural networks.

The theorem is not new mathematics — it is FTC Part 2 read with the right emphasis.

---

## § 1 — Statement

Let $F$ be differentiable on $[a, b]$ with $F' = f$ integrable on $[a, b]$. Then:

$$\boxed{\int_a^b f(x),dx = F(b) - F(a)}$$

Equivalently, stated in the language of _rates_:

> **The integral of a rate of change of a quantity over $[a,b]$ equals the net change of that quantity over $[a,b]$.**

This is FTC Part 2 (Stewart §5.3) reinterpreted. The new content is entirely in the _reading_ of it.

---

## § 2 — Proof (Rigorous)

**Setup.** Let $f$ be integrable on $[a,b]$ and let $F$ be any antiderivative of $f$, i.e., $F'(x) = f(x)$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, continuous on $[a,b]$.

**Proof.** Partition $[a,b]$ with $a = x_0 < x_1 < \cdots < x_n = b$. By the **Mean Value Theorem**, on each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$ there exists $c_i \in (x_{i-1}, x_i)$ such that:

$$F(x_i) - F(x_{i-1}) = F'(c_i)(x_i - x_{i-1}) = f(c_i),\Delta x_i$$

Summing over all subintervals (a telescoping sum on the left):

$$F(b) - F(a) = \sum_{i=1}^n [F(x_i) - F(x_{i-1})] = \sum_{i=1}^n f(c_i),\Delta x_i$$

The right-hand side is a **Riemann sum** for $f$ on $[a,b]$. Since $f$ is integrable, as $|\Delta| \to 0$:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n f(c_i),\Delta x_i ;\longrightarrow; \int_a^b f(x),dx$$

Therefore:

$$\int_a^b f(x),dx = F(b) - F(a) \qquad \square$$

> **Remark.** The key move is MVT on each subinterval — this is what "connects" $F'$ to the Riemann sum of $f$. The telescoping collapse of $F(b) - F(a)$ from $n$ local increments is the geometric soul of the argument.

## § 3 — The Semantic Content: Net vs. Total

This is the philosophical core.

|Quantity|Formula|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|**Net displacement**|$\displaystyle\int_a^b v(t),dt$|Final position minus initial position|
|**Total distance**|$\displaystyle\int_a^b \|v(t)\|,dt$|Accumulated odometer reading|

These are equal **only when $v(t) \geq 0$ throughout** (motion in one direction).

**Example.** A particle moves with velocity $v(t) = t^2 - 4t + 3 = (t-1)(t-3)$ on $[0,4]$.

- Net displacement: $$\int_0^4 (t^2 - 4t + 3),dt = \left[\frac{t^3}{3} - 2t^2 + 3t\right]_0^4 = \frac{64}{3} - 32 + 12 - 0 = \frac{4}{3}$$
    
- Total distance: note $v(t) < 0$ on $(1,3)$, so split the integral: $$\int_0^1 v,dt + \left|\int_1^3 v,dt\right| + \int_3^4 v,dt$$
    

Compute each: $$\int_0^1 (t^2-4t+3),dt = \left[\frac{t^3}{3}-2t^2+3t\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{3}-2+3 = \frac{4}{3}$$ $$\int_1^3 (t^2-4t+3),dt = \left[\frac{27}{3}-18+9\right] - \left[\frac{1}{3}-2+3\right] = 0 - \frac{4}{3} = -\frac{4}{3}$$ $$\int_3^4 (t^2-4t+3),dt = \left[\frac{64}{3}-32+12\right] - [9-18+9] = \frac{4}{3} - 0 = \frac{4}{3}$$

Total distance $= \dfrac{4}{3} + \dfrac{4}{3} + \dfrac{4}{3} = 4$, but net displacement $= \dfrac{4}{3}$.

> The particle wandered backward and almost returned to where it started.

## § 5 — Physical Instances of the Theorem

The theorem is a template. Recognize it everywhere:

|$f(t) = F'(t)$|$F(t)$|Net Change $\int_a^b f,dt$|
|---|---|---|
|Velocity $v(t)$|Position $x(t)$|Displacement $\Delta x$|
|Acceleration $a(t)$|Velocity $v(t)$|Change in velocity $\Delta v$|
|Power $P(t)$ [watts]|Energy $E(t)$ [joules]|Work done $\Delta E$|
|Current $I(t)$ [amps]|Charge $Q(t)$ [coulombs]|Net charge flow $\Delta Q$|
|Birth rate $-$ Death rate|Population $N(t)$|Net population change|
|Marginal cost $C'(q)$|Total cost $C(q)$|Added cost from producing $q_1$ to $q_2$ units|

## § 6 — Connection to MIT 18.02 (Multivariable Extension)

In 18.02, the Net Change Theorem is the 1D ancestor of the **Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals** (also called the Gradient Theorem):

$$\int_C \nabla F \cdot d\mathbf{r} = F(\mathbf{b}) - F(\mathbf{a})$$

Here the "rate of change" is $\nabla F$, the path is a curve $C$ from $\mathbf{a}$ to $\mathbf{b}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and the net change is still just endpoint values. The analog of "net vs. total" becomes path-independence vs. path-dependence (conservative vs. non-conservative fields).

Further generalizations follow the same logic:

- **Stokes' Theorem**: $\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$
- **Divergence Theorem**: $\iiint_V (\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}),dV = \oiint_{\partial V} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$

All of these say: _integrate a derivative over a region = evaluate something on its boundary._ The Net Change Theorem is the simplest member of this family.

## § 7 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

This is where calculus stops being "pure" and starts paying rent.

### 7.1 — Gradient Descent as Accumulated Net Change

In gradient descent, parameter $\theta$ evolves by discrete steps:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

In the continuous (idealized) limit, this is the ODE:

$$\frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

By the Net Change Theorem, the total displacement of parameters over training time $[0, T]$ is:

$$\theta(T) - \theta(0) = \int_0^T \frac{d\theta}{dt},dt = -\int_0^T \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta(t)),dt$$

The **net change in $\theta$** is the integral of its _rate of change_ (the gradient). Understanding whether the optimizer has truly moved the parameters far from initialization requires thinking about _net_ movement (trajectory integral), not step-by-step snapshots.

### 7.2 — Backpropagation: The Chain Rule is a Net Change Machine

In a deep network, the output loss $\mathcal{L}$ depends on the input $x$ through a composition $f_n \circ \cdots \circ f_1(x)$. Backpropagation computes $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_{ij}}$ for every weight.

When activations are viewed continuously (as in neural ODEs), the gradient flowing back through the network satisfies an adjoint ODE. By the Net Change Theorem applied to this adjoint:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta} = \int_0^T \mathbf{a}(t)^\top \frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}(z(t), \theta),dt$$

where $\mathbf{a}(t)$ is the adjoint (costate) variable. This is the _continuous backpropagation_ formula used in **Neural ODEs** (Chen et al., 2018). It is literally the Net Change Theorem applied to the adjoint system.

### 7.3 — Cumulative Loss and Learning Curves

When you plot a training loss curve and compute the **area under the curve (AUC)**, you are computing:

$$\text{Cumulative Loss} = \int_0^T \mathcal{L}(t),dt$$

This is the net accumulated loss, not the instantaneous value. Practitioners use this to compare learning schedules: two optimizers with the same final loss but different AUC curves have very different sample efficiency.

### 7.4 — Neural ODEs — The Continuous Limit of ResNets

A ResNet layer performs: $$\mathbf{h}_{t+1} = \mathbf{h}_t + f(\mathbf{h}_t, \theta_t)$$

This is a discrete Euler step for the ODE: $$\frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = f(\mathbf{h}(t), \theta)$$

By the Net Change Theorem, the **output of the network** is: $$\mathbf{h}(T) = \mathbf{h}(0) + \int_0^T f(\mathbf{h}(t), \theta),dt$$

The network output is the **initial hidden state** plus the **net change** accumulated by integrating the learned vector field $f$. ResNets literally compute a net change integral. When depth → ∞, this is exact.

### 7.5 — Reward in Reinforcement Learning

In RL, the **return** (cumulative discounted reward) is:

$$G_t = \int_t^\infty e^{-\lambda(s-t)} r(s),ds \quad \text{(continuous time)}$$

or in discrete form $G_t = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k r_{t+k}$. This is the net change in "value" from time $t$ forward, accumulated through the reward rate $r(s)$. Policy gradient methods optimize precisely this net change, using its gradient with respect to policy parameters.

## § 8 — Common Mistakes and Edge Cases

**Mistake 1: Confusing net displacement with total distance.** $$\int_a^b v(t),dt \neq \int_a^b |v(t)|,dt \quad \text{(in general)}$$ Net displacement can be zero even if the particle traveled far.

**Mistake 2: Forgetting units.** If $v$ is in m/s and $t$ in seconds, then $\int v,dt$ is in meters. The integral inherits units: $[f] \cdot [x]$.

**Mistake 3: The theorem applies to net change, not absolute value.** $\int_a^b f,dx = F(b) - F(a)$ can be negative. This means $F$ _decreased_ over $[a,b]$.

**Mistake 4: Assuming the antiderivative is unique.** $F$ is unique only up to a constant. But $F(b) - F(a)$ is constant-independent, so the net change is well-defined regardless of which antiderivative you choose.

---

## § 9 — A Note on the "Net" Language

Why "net"? The word comes from accounting: _net profit = revenue − expenses._ In calculus, positive and negative contributions to the Riemann sum cancel each other. What remains after cancellation is the _net_ contribution. The theorem says: all that survives the cancellation is the difference in endpoint values of $F$. Everything in between washes out.

This is profound: no matter how wild the path $F$ takes between $a$ and $b$, the integral of its derivative sees only the endpoints. The path is invisible to the Net Change Theorem.

## § 10 — Summary

$$\int_a^b F'(x),dx = F(b) - F(a)$$

|Aspect|Content|
|---|---|
|**What it says**|Integral of rate = net change in quantity|
|**Proof key step**|MVT on each subinterval + telescoping + Riemann sum limit|
|**Net ≠ Total**|Sign matters; total distance requires $\int\|v\|$|
|**1D → nD**|Gradient Theorem in 18.02 is the multivariable version|
|**ML connection**|Gradient descent ODE, Neural ODEs, backprop adjoint, RL returns|
