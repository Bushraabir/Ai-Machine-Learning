## 1. Motivation — Why Does This Rule Exist?

Suppose I want to differentiate $h(x) = \sin(x^2)$. This is not a function I can differentiate by any rule I already know — it's a **function of a function**. The sine is acting not on $x$, but on the output of $x^2$.

The chain rule is the precise answer to:

> _"If I know how $u$ responds to $x$, and how $y$ responds to $u$, what is the total response of $y$ to $x$?"_

This is a question about composed change. And the answer turns out to be multiplicative.

## 2. Single-Variable Chain Rule

### Theorem (Chain Rule, Single Variable)

Let $u = g(x)$ be differentiable at $x$, and let $y = f(u)$ be differentiable at $u = g(x)$. Then the composite $h(x) = f(g(x))$ is differentiable at $x$, and

$$\boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}}$$

or equivalently, in Lagrange notation:

$$h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

### Proof Sketch (the honest version)

The naive attempt: write $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$. Multiply and divide by $\Delta u$:

$$\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta u} \cdot \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x}$$

This _almost_ works. The subtlety is that $\Delta u$ might equal zero even when $\Delta x \neq 0$, making $\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta u}$ undefined.

The clean fix uses the **increment formulation**: since $f$ is differentiable at $u_0$, we can write

$$\Delta y = f'(u_0),\Delta u + \varepsilon(\Delta u)\cdot \Delta u$$

where $\varepsilon(\Delta u) \to 0$ as $\Delta u \to 0$. Dividing by $\Delta x$ and taking the limit as $\Delta x \to 0$:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = f'(u_0)\cdot g'(x_0) + \lim_{\Delta x\to 0}\varepsilon(\Delta u)\cdot \frac{\Delta u}{\Delta x} = f'(g(x_0))\cdot g'(x_0)$$

since $\Delta u \to 0$ as $\Delta x \to 0$ (continuity of $g$), so $\varepsilon \to 0$. $\blacksquare$

> **Reference:** Stewart §3.4, Theorem 3; MIT 18.02 Notes, Differentiation

## 3. Examples — Single Variable

### Example 1 — Polynomial inside trigonometric

$$h(x) = \sin(x^2)$$ Let $u = x^2$, $y = \sin u$.

$$h'(x) = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x$$

### Example 2 — Nested exponential

$$h(x) = e^{-x^2/2} \quad \text{(the Gaussian kernel!)}$$

$$h'(x) = e^{-x^2/2} \cdot (-x) = -x,e^{-x^2/2}$$

Notice $h'(x) = 0$ at $x = 0$ — the peak of the Gaussian. This is the density function of the standard normal distribution.

### Example 3 — Triple composition

$$h(x) = \ln(\sin(e^x))$$

Let $w = e^x$, $v = \sin(w)$, $y = \ln(v)$:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sin(e^x)} \cdot \cos(e^x) \cdot e^x = e^x \cot(e^x)$$

The rule chains: each layer contributes a multiplicative factor.

## 4. The Multivariable Chain Rule

This is where it gets genuinely powerful — and where it directly connects to machine learning.

### Setup

Let $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ be variables, and let $\mathbf{u} = (u_1, \ldots, u_m)$ with each $u_i = g_i(\mathbf{x})$. Let $y = f(\mathbf{u})$.

### Theorem (Multivariable Chain Rule)

If all partial derivatives exist and are continuous, then:

$$\frac{\partial y}{\partial x_i} = \sum_{j=1}^{m} \frac{\partial y}{\partial u_j} \cdot \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i}$$

In matrix form, if we think of $y$ as a scalar:

$$\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} y = \left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\right)^{!\top} \nabla_{\mathbf{u}} y$$

where $\dfrac{\partial \mathbf{u}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}$ is the **Jacobian matrix** $J \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$:

$$J_{ji} = \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i}$$

So the chain rule in full generality is:

$$\boxed{\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} y = J^\top \cdot \nabla_{\mathbf{u}} y}$$

This is exactly what a **VJP (Vector-Jacobian Product)** computes in autodiff frameworks.

> **Reference:** Stewart §14.5; MIT 18.02 Lecture 11

## 5. Visualization — Computational Graph

The chain rule is most naturally understood as propagating information backward through a **computation graph**.

Consider $y = f(g(h(x)))$, with intermediate nodes:

```
x ──→ [h] ──→ u ──→ [g] ──→ v ──→ [f] ──→ y
```

**Forward pass** computes values left to right. **Backward pass** (backpropagation) multiplies derivatives right to left:

```
∂y/∂x = (∂y/∂v) · (∂v/∂u) · (∂u/∂x)
          ↑            ↑          ↑
       f'(v)        g'(u)      h'(x)
```

Each node "knows" only its local derivative. The chain rule says: **compose them multiplicatively**.

### Diagram — A Simple Two-Layer Network

$$\text{Input } x \xrightarrow{W_1,,b_1} z_1 = W_1 x + b_1 \xrightarrow{\sigma} a_1 \xrightarrow{W_2,,b_2} z_2 = W_2 a_1 + b_2 \xrightarrow{\text{loss}} \mathcal{L}$$

Backprop through this:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_1} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z_2} \cdot \frac{\partial z_2}{\partial a_1} \cdot \frac{\partial a_1}{\partial z_1} \cdot \frac{\partial z_1}{\partial W_1}$$

Every gradient computation in a neural network is the chain rule, applied recursively.

## 6. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

> _"Backpropagation is just the chain rule, applied to a very large composition of functions."_ — standard observation in every ML textbook

### 6.1 Backpropagation

Consider a neural network as a function $\mathcal{L} = \ell(f_L(\cdots f_1(\mathbf{x}; W_1) \cdots; W_L))$ — a massive composition of parameterized maps. To train it, we need:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_k} \quad \text{for all } k = 1, \ldots, L$$

The **backpropagation algorithm** is nothing more than the multivariable chain rule applied layer by layer, traveling backward through the computation graph:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_k} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_L} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{a}_L}{\partial \mathbf{a}_{L-1}} \cdots \frac{\partial \mathbf{a}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_k} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{a}_k}{\partial W_k}$$

Each factor is a Jacobian (or its transpose in the VJP sense).

### 6.2 Automatic Differentiation (Autodiff)

PyTorch, JAX, TensorFlow — all build a **computation graph** dynamically, then execute the chain rule backward. The `.backward()` call in PyTorch literally traverses this graph, multiplying local Jacobians.

```python
import torch

x = torch.tensor(2.0, requires_grad=True)
y = torch.sin(x**2)   # h(x) = sin(x^2)
y.backward()
print(x.grad)         # dy/dx = cos(x^2) * 2x = cos(4) * 4 ≈ -2.615
```

### 6.3 The Vanishing Gradient Problem

In a deep network, the chain rule gives:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_1} = \prod_{k=1}^{L} \frac{\partial \mathbf{a}_k}{\partial \mathbf{a}_{k-1}}$$

If each Jacobian has spectral norm $< 1$ (e.g., from saturated sigmoids), the **product of $L$ small numbers** vanishes exponentially. This is the **vanishing gradient problem** — a direct consequence of multiplying many terms in the chain rule.

**ReLU activations**, **residual connections** (ResNets), and **layer normalization** are all engineering solutions designed to keep these Jacobian products healthy.

$$\sigma'_{\text{ReLU}}(z) = \begin{cases} 1 & z > 0 \ 0 & z \leq 0 \end{cases}$$

When active, the ReLU contributes a factor of $1$ to the chain — it does not shrink the gradient.

### 6.4 Gradient of Loss w.r.t. Parameters — A Concrete Example

Suppose a single neuron: $\hat{y} = \sigma(wx + b)$, loss $\mathcal{L} = (\hat{y} - y)^2$.

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w} = \underbrace{2(\hat{y} - y)}_{\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \underbrace{\sigma'(wx+b)}_{\partial \hat{y}/\partial z} \cdot \underbrace{x}_{\partial z / \partial w}$$

Three factors. Each is a local derivative. The chain rule assembles them. This is **gradient descent** — and the chain rule makes it computable.

## 7. Implicit Differentiation via Chain Rule

If $F(x, y) = 0$ defines $y$ implicitly as a function of $x$, then differentiating both sides with respect to $x$:

$$\frac{\partial F}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$

Solving:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}$$

**Example:** $x^2 + y^2 = 1$ (unit circle). Here $F = x^2 + y^2 - 1$:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{2x}{2y} = -\frac{x}{y}$$

This is the chain rule applied to a function defined implicitly.

## 8. The Chain Rule in Differential Form

For a more coordinate-free view: if $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ and $g : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^k$, the chain rule says

$$D(g \circ f)(\mathbf{x}) = Dg(f(\mathbf{x})) \cdot Df(\mathbf{x})$$

where $D$ denotes the total derivative (Jacobian matrix). This is **matrix multiplication** of Jacobians.

This form is clean and generalizes to manifolds, differential geometry, and the general change-of-variables formula in integration.

## 10. Common Errors to Avoid

1. **Forgetting the outer derivative.** $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin(x^2) \neq \cos(x^2)$. The $2x$ factor from the inner derivative is mandatory.
    
2. **Applying the chain rule to sums.** The chain rule applies to compositions $f(g(x))$, not to $f(x) + g(x)$. That's just linearity of differentiation.
    
3. **In multivariable settings**, summing over all intermediate variables is essential — don't drop terms in the sum $\sum_j$.
    
4. **In backprop**, transposing Jacobians matters. The VJP uses $J^\top$, not $J$. Getting this wrong silently gives wrong gradients.
