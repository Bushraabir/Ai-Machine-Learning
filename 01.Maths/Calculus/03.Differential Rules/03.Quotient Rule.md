## The Statement

Let $f$ and $g$ be differentiable at $x$, with $g(x) \neq 0$. Then $h(x) = \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ is differentiable at $x$, and

$$\boxed{\left(\frac{f}{g}\right)'(x) = \frac{f'(x),g(x) - f(x),g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2}}$$

**Leibniz notation:**

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{u}{v}\right] = \frac{v,\dfrac{du}{dx} - u,\dfrac{dv}{dx}}{v^2}$$

**Mnemonic** (genuinely useful, not shameful to use):

> _"Low d-High minus High d-Low, over Low squared below."_

Where "High" $= u$ (numerator) and "Low" $= v$ (denominator).

---

## Two Proofs

### Proof I — Directly from the Definition

We compute the limit directly.

$$h'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\dfrac{f(x+\Delta x)}{g(x+\Delta x)} - \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}}{\Delta x}$$

Combine the numerator over a common denominator:

$$= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x+\Delta x),g(x) - f(x),g(x+\Delta x)}{\Delta x \cdot g(x+\Delta x),g(x)}$$

Add and subtract $f(x),g(x)$ in the numerator:

$$= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{\bigl[f(x+\Delta x)-f(x)\bigr]g(x) - f(x)\bigl[g(x+\Delta x)-g(x)\bigr]}{\Delta x \cdot g(x+\Delta x),g(x)}$$

Split and take limits. Since $g$ is differentiable at $x$, it is continuous there, so $g(x+\Delta x) \to g(x)$:

$$= \frac{f'(x),g(x) - f(x),g'(x)}{[g(x)]^2} \qquad \blacksquare$$

> **Why $g(x) \neq 0$ is essential:** the denominator $[g(x)]^2$ must be nonzero for the formula to be defined. More subtly, we also need $g(x+\Delta x) \neq 0$ for small enough $\Delta x$ — this is guaranteed by continuity of $g$ and the assumption $g(x) \neq 0$.

---

### Proof II — From the Product Rule (The Cleaner Derivation)

Write $h = f \cdot g^{-1}$ and apply the product rule:

$$h' = f' \cdot g^{-1} + f \cdot (g^{-1})'$$

We need $(g^{-1})'$. Apply the chain rule to $\phi(g) = g^{-1}$:

$$(g^{-1})' = -g^{-2} \cdot g' = -\frac{g'}{g^2}$$

Substitute back:

$$h' = \frac{f'}{g} + f \cdot \left(-\frac{g'}{g^2}\right) = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2} \qquad \blacksquare$$

> This proof reveals the quotient rule's true nature: it is **not** a new rule at all. It is the product rule composed with the power rule via the chain rule. In practice, experienced analysts often skip the quotient rule entirely and just write $f \cdot g^{-1}$ — it reduces sign errors.

---

## Geometric Intuition

Think of $h(x) = f(x)/g(x)$ as a ratio that is changing. When $x$ moves by $\Delta x$:

- The numerator changes by $\approx f',\Delta x$ — this _increases_ the ratio.
- The denominator changes by $\approx g',\Delta x$ — this _decreases_ the ratio (if $g' > 0$).

The competition between these two effects, normalized by $g^2$ (the "scale" of the denominator), produces the formula. The $g^2$ in the denominator is a **normalization factor**: the larger $g$ is, the less sensitive the ratio is to changes in $f$ or $g$.

## Computed Examples

### Example 1 — Rational Function

$$h(x) = \frac{x^2 + 1}{x^3 - 4}$$

$$h'(x) = \frac{2x(x^3-4) - (x^2+1)(3x^2)}{(x^3-4)^2}$$

Expand the numerator:

$$= \frac{2x^4 - 8x - 3x^4 - 3x^2}{(x^3-4)^2} = \frac{-x^4 - 3x^2 - 8x}{(x^3-4)^2}$$

_Domain note:_ undefined at $x = \sqrt[3]{4}$.

---

### Example 2 — Tangent Function (Classic Derivation)

$$\tan x = \frac{\sin x}{\cos x}$$

$$(\tan x)' = \frac{\cos x \cdot \cos x - \sin x \cdot (-\sin x)}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{\cos^2 x + \sin^2 x}{\cos^2 x} = \frac{1}{\cos^2 x} = \sec^2 x$$

$$\boxed{(\tan x)' = \sec^2 x}$$

This is one of the most important derivative formulas in analysis — and it falls out of the quotient rule cleanly.

---

### Example 3 — All Six Trigonometric Derivatives via Quotient Rule

|Function|Written as|Derivative|
|---|---|---|
|$\tan x$|$\sin x / \cos x$|$\sec^2 x$|
|$\cot x$|$\cos x / \sin x$|$-\csc^2 x$|
|$\sec x$|$1 / \cos x$|$\sec x \tan x$|
|$\csc x$|$1 / \sin x$|$-\csc x \cot x$|

Every one of these follows directly from the quotient rule. There is no need to memorize all six independently — derive them when needed.

---

### Example 4 — Softmax Jacobian (The ML Example)

The **softmax function** $\sigma: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is defined component-wise as:

$$\sigma_i(\mathbf{z}) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{n} e^{z_k}}$$

Let $S = \sum_k e^{z_k}$ (a function of all inputs). Then $\sigma_i = e^{z_i} / S$.

**Case $i = j$** (diagonal of the Jacobian):

$$\frac{\partial \sigma_i}{\partial z_i} = \frac{e^{z_i} \cdot S - e^{z_i} \cdot e^{z_i}}{S^2} = \frac{e^{z_i}}{S}\left(1 - \frac{e^{z_i}}{S}\right) = \sigma_i(1 - \sigma_i)$$

**Case $i \neq j$** (off-diagonal):

$$\frac{\partial \sigma_i}{\partial z_j} = \frac{0 \cdot S - e^{z_i} \cdot e^{z_j}}{S^2} = -\frac{e^{z_i}}{S}\cdot\frac{e^{z_j}}{S} = -\sigma_i \sigma_j$$

The full Jacobian is:

$$\frac{\partial \boldsymbol{\sigma}}{\partial \mathbf{z}} = \text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}) - \boldsymbol{\sigma}\boldsymbol{\sigma}^\top$$

> This is computed in every forward pass through a classification head and in every attention layer in a Transformer. **The quotient rule is what makes the softmax backpropagation formula exact.**

- $h'(x) = \dfrac{x\cos x - \sin x}{x^2}$
- $\lim_{x\to 0} h(x) = 1$ (L'Hôpital or Taylor), but the quotient rule gives the derivative everywhere $x \neq 0$.
- Critical points satisfy $x\cos x = \sin x$, i.e., $\tan x = x$.

This function appears in **signal processing** (as the sinc function in Fourier analysis) and in the **attention score normalization** intuition in NLP.

### Behavior Near a Zero of the Denominator

```
h(x) = 1/(x-1)

        h(x)
         |
    ↑    |
   +∞    |       |
         |       |
  -------|-------|--------→ x
         |   x=1 |
   -∞    |       |
    ↓    |
```

As $g(x) \to 0$ with $f(x) \not\to 0$: the function blows up. The quotient rule formula $h' = (f'g - fg')/g^2$ reflects this: if $g \approx 0$, then $h'$ is extremely large — the function is nearly vertical near the singularity.

## Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 1. Softmax Backpropagation (Revisited)

As shown in Example 4, the entire Jacobian of softmax is derived via the quotient rule. In cross-entropy loss training:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\sum_i y_i \log \sigma_i(\mathbf{z})$$

The gradient $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial \mathbf{z}$ requires $\partial \sigma_i / \partial z_j$ — which is pure quotient rule. PyTorch's `F.softmax` backward pass encodes exactly the matrix $\text{diag}(\boldsymbol{\sigma}) - \boldsymbol{\sigma}\boldsymbol{\sigma}^\top$.

---

### 2. Attention Score Normalization

Scaled dot-product attention computes:

$$\text{Attn}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$

The $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling in the denominator is a constant, but during training, computing gradients with respect to $Q$ and $K$ involves differentiating a ratio (softmax of a quotient). The quotient rule and its chain rule composition govern what gradient signal flows back into the query and key matrices.

---

### 3. Normalization Layers

**Layer Normalization** normalizes activations as:

$$\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu}{\sigma + \epsilon}$$

where $\mu$ and $\sigma$ both depend on $x_i$. Computing $\partial \hat{x}_i / \partial x_j$ for $i = j$ vs $i \neq j$ is an exercise in the quotient rule with a function-valued denominator:

$$\frac{\partial \hat{x}_i}{\partial x_i} = \frac{(\sigma + \epsilon) - (x_i - \mu)\cdot\partial\sigma/\partial x_i}{(\sigma+\epsilon)^2}$$

This is the quotient rule applied where both numerator and denominator are functions of $x_i$.

---

### 4. Rational Activation Functions

**Padé approximants** and rational neural networks use activation functions of the form:

$$\phi(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$$

where $P$ and $Q$ are polynomials (learnable). The derivative — needed for backprop — is given by the quotient rule:

$$\phi'(x) = \frac{P'(x)Q(x) - P(x)Q'(x)}{[Q(x)]^2}$$

These are used in some state-of-the-art networks as trainable, flexible alternatives to ReLU.

---

### 5. The Score Function in Variational Inference

In **variational autoencoders (VAEs)** and reinforcement learning policy gradients, we differentiate the log of a ratio:

$$\frac{d}{d\theta} \log \frac{p(x)}{q_\theta(x)} = \frac{d}{d\theta}\left[\log p(x) - \log q_\theta(x)\right] = -\frac{\partial_\theta q_\theta(x)}{q_\theta(x)}$$

The right-hand side is the **score function**. Deriving it cleanly requires the quotient rule (or equivalently, the chain rule on the log). It is the foundation of **REINFORCE** and policy gradient methods in deep RL.

## The Quotient Rule vs. Rewriting as Product

A practical note worth internalizing: for many expressions, it is _algebraically cleaner_ to avoid the quotient rule entirely.

**Example.** Instead of differentiating $\dfrac{x+1}{x^2}$ with the quotient rule:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{x+1}{x^2}\right] = \frac{x^2 - 2x(x+1)}{x^4} = \frac{-x^2-2x}{x^4} = \frac{-(x+2)}{x^3}$$

Rewrite first:

$$\frac{x+1}{x^2} = x^{-1} + x^{-2} \implies \frac{d}{dx} = -x^{-2} - 2x^{-3} = \frac{-(x+2)}{x^3} \checkmark$$

The second approach is faster and less prone to sign errors. The quotient rule is essential when the denominator cannot be cleanly separated — as in the softmax example.

## Common Mistakes

|Mistake|Wrong|Correct|
|---|---|---|
|Reversing the subtraction|$(f'g + fg')/g^2$|$(f'g - fg')/g^2$ — order matters|
|Forgetting to square the denominator|$(f'g - fg')/g$|Must be $g^2$|
|Applying product rule instead|$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$|The quotient rule has a **minus** sign|
|Not checking $g(x) \neq 0$|Blindly applying the formula|Always verify the domain first|
|Differentiating $(\tan x)'$ as $(\sin x)' / (\cos x)'$|$\cos x / (-\sin x)$|Use the quotient rule, not component-wise division|

## Logarithmic Differentiation (When the Quotient Rule Becomes Unwieldy)

For complicated ratios, take the log first:

$$h(x) = \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \implies \ln h = \ln f - \ln g$$

Differentiate both sides:

$$\frac{h'}{h} = \frac{f'}{f} - \frac{g'}{g}$$

$$h' = h\left(\frac{f'}{f} - \frac{g'}{g}\right) = \frac{f}{g}\left(\frac{f'}{f} - \frac{g'}{g}\right) = \frac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$$

Same result — but the intermediate steps are often cleaner when $f$ and $g$ are themselves products or powers. This is the technique used in **maximum likelihood estimation** derivations throughout statistics and ML.
