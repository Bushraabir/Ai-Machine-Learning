

> _The idea behind linearization is almost embarrassingly simple — and yet it is one of the most powerful tools in all of mathematics. Every time you train a neural network, you are, at some level, exploiting this idea._

---

## § 1. The Geometric Idea (Before Any Formulas)

Zoom in on the graph of a differentiable function $f$ at a point $a$. Zoom in far enough, and the curve _looks_ like a straight line. This is not a metaphor — it is the definition of differentiability, expressed geometrically.

The tangent line at $(a, f(a))$ has equation:

$$L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)$$

We call $L$ the **linearization** of $f$ at $a$. The claim is:

$$f(x) \approx L(x) \quad \text{for } x \text{ near } a$$

This is the **linear approximation** (also called the **tangent line approximation** or **first-order approximation**).

---

## § 2. The Formal Statement

**Definition.** Let $f$ be differentiable at $a$. The _linearization_ of $f$ at $a$ is the function

$$\boxed{L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)}$$

**Theorem (Stewart §3.10).** If $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then

$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - L(x)}{x - a} = 0$$

This says the _error_ $f(x) - L(x)$ vanishes faster than $x - a$ as $x \to a$. In other words, the tangent line is not just any line through $(a, f(a))$ — it is the unique line for which the relative error goes to zero.

**Proof.** Compute directly:

$$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - L(x)}{x - a} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a) - f'(a)(x-a)}{x - a}$$

$$= \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} - f'(a) = f'(a) - f'(a) = 0. \quad \blacksquare$$

## § 3. Standard Examples

### Example 1 — Square root

Approximate $\sqrt{4.02}$.

Take $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$, $a = 4$.

$$f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}, \quad f'(4) = \frac{1}{4}$$

$$L(x) = \sqrt{4} + \frac{1}{4}(x - 4) = 2 + \frac{x-4}{4}$$

$$\sqrt{4.02} \approx L(4.02) = 2 + \frac{0.02}{4} = 2.005$$

The true value is $2.00499\ldots$ — the error is about $5 \times 10^{-6}$.

---

### Example 2 — Trigonometric (the physicist's favorite)

Approximate $\sin(0.1)$.

Take $f(x) = \sin x$, $a = 0$.

$$f'(x) = \cos x, \quad f'(0) = 1$$

$$L(x) = \sin 0 + 1 \cdot (x - 0) = x$$

So $\sin x \approx x$ for small $x$. This is the **small-angle approximation**, used constantly in optics, mechanics, and signal processing.

$$\sin(0.1) \approx 0.1 \quad \text{(true value: } 0.09983\ldots\text{)}$$

---

### Example 3 — The natural logarithm

Approximate $\ln(1.05)$.

Take $f(x) = \ln x$, $a = 1$.

$$f'(x) = \frac{1}{x}, \quad f'(1) = 1$$

$$L(x) = 0 + 1 \cdot (x - 1) = x - 1$$

So $\ln(1 + u) \approx u$ for small $u$. This approximation appears in finance (continuous compounding), information theory (entropy expansions), and the derivation of the softmax gradient.

## § 5. Differentials

The language of **differentials** repackages linearization in a form suited for computation and for generalizing to multiple variables.

**Definition.** Let $y = f(x)$. The **differential** $dy$ is defined by

$$\boxed{dy = f'(x), dx}$$

where $dx$ is an independent variable (think of it as a small but arbitrary increment).

The key interpretive move: if we set $dx = \Delta x = x - a$, then

$$dy = f'(a),\Delta x \approx \Delta y = f(x) - f(a)$$

The differential $dy$ is the _linear part_ of the change $\Delta y$. Precisely:

$$\Delta y = dy + \varepsilon \cdot \Delta x, \quad \varepsilon \to 0 \text{ as } \Delta x \to 0$$

This decomposition is the rigorous content of the approximation $\Delta y \approx dy$.

---

### The Differential Triangle

```
              f(x)
               │
     f(a)+Δy  ●  ← true value of f(x)
               │\
               │  \  ← error = Δy − dy
        f(a)+dy●    \
               │     ●  ← L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)·Δx
               │    /
               │   / slope = f'(a)
               │  /
        f(a)   ● /
               │/
               └──────────────
              a        x=a+Δx

  dx = Δx (horizontal increment)
  dy = f'(a)·dx  (vertical increment along tangent)
  Δy = f(x) − f(a)  (actual vertical increment on curve)
```

---

### Example: Propagation of Error

A sphere has radius measured as $r = 10$ cm with a possible error of $\pm 0.1$ cm. Estimate the error in the computed volume.

$$V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3, \quad dV = 4\pi r^2, dr$$

$$dV \bigg|_{r=10,, dr=0.1} = 4\pi (100)(0.1) = 40\pi \approx 125.7 \text{ cm}^3$$

The **relative error** is:

$$\frac{dV}{V} = \frac{4\pi r^2, dr}{\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3} = \frac{3, dr}{r} = \frac{3(0.1)}{10} = 3%$$

_Observation: relative error in volume is exactly $3 \times$ relative error in radius. This is a general fact about power functions — the differential makes it transparent._

## § 6. Functions of Several Variables (MIT 18.02 Perspective)

For $f(x, y)$ differentiable at $(a, b)$, the linearization generalizes to:

$$L(x, y) = f(a, b) + f_x(a, b)(x - a) + f_y(a, b)(y - b)$$

and the total differential is:

$$df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x},dx + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y},dy$$

The approximation $\Delta f \approx df$ becomes:

$$\Delta f \approx f_x,\Delta x + f_y,\Delta y$$

This is a **first-order Taylor expansion** in multiple variables. The tangent line becomes a **tangent plane**.

**Example.** Let $f(x,y) = x^2 y + \sin(xy)$. At $(1, \pi)$:

$$f_x = 2xy + y\cos(xy) \bigg|_{(1,\pi)} = 2\pi + \pi\cos(\pi) = 2\pi - \pi = \pi$$

$$f_y = x^2 + x\cos(xy) \bigg|_{(1,\pi)} = 1 + \cos(\pi) = 0$$

$$L(x,y) = f(1,\pi) + \pi(x - 1) + 0 \cdot (y - \pi) = (π + 0) + \pi(x-1) = \pi x$$

_(The linearization at this point depends only on $x$.)_

## § 7. The Error Bound (Taylor's Theorem, Order 1)

**Theorem.** Suppose $f''$ is continuous on $[a, x]$. Then:

$$f(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) + \frac{f''(c)}{2}(x - a)^2$$

for some $c$ between $a$ and $x$. Therefore the error in the linear approximation satisfies:

$$|f(x) - L(x)| \leq \frac{M}{2}|x - a|^2$$

where $M = \max_{t \in [a,x]} |f''(t)|$.

**Implication.** The error is $O((x-a)^2)$ — it is _quadratic_ in the displacement. This is why linear approximations are so useful: near $a$, the error is negligibly small compared to the displacement itself.

## § 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _This is where things get interesting. Linear approximation is not just a calculus trick — it is the mathematical spine of how neural networks are trained._

### 8.1 Gradient Descent is Linear Approximation

Given a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the parameter vector, the update rule in gradient descent is:

$$\theta_{\text{new}} = \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

This is precisely the linearization step. We are approximating:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta + \Delta\theta) \approx \mathcal{L}(\theta) + \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta)^\top \Delta\theta$$

and choosing $\Delta\theta = -\eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ to decrease the (linearized) loss. The gradient is nothing but the multivariable differential: $d\mathcal{L} = \nabla\mathcal{L} \cdot d\theta$.

_Every gradient step is a move along a tangent hyperplane._

---

### 8.2 Backpropagation is the Chain Rule for Differentials

The backpropagation algorithm computes $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta}$ by repeatedly applying the chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial z^{(L)}} \cdot \frac{\partial z^{(L)}}{\partial z^{(L-1)}} \cdots \frac{\partial z^{(1)}}{\partial \theta}$$

Each factor is a Jacobian — the matrix of all first-order partial derivatives of one layer's output with respect to its input. The entire computation is a cascade of linear approximations, layer by layer.

---

### 8.3 Newton's Method and Second-Order Optimizers

Standard gradient descent uses only the first-order (linear) approximation. **Newton's method** uses the second-order (quadratic) approximation:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta + \Delta\theta) \approx \mathcal{L}(\theta) + \nabla\mathcal{L}^\top \Delta\theta + \frac{1}{2}\Delta\theta^\top H \Delta\theta$$

where $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$ is the **Hessian**. Setting the gradient of this quadratic to zero gives:

$$\Delta\theta = -H^{-1} \nabla\mathcal{L}$$

Methods like **Adam**, **RMSprop**, and **K-FAC** can be understood as practical approximations to this Newton step (the Hessian is too expensive to compute exactly in large models).

---

### 8.4 The Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)

A deep result in theoretical machine learning (Jacot, Gabriel & Hongler, 2018): in the limit of infinite width, a neural network trained by gradient descent behaves like a **kernel method** defined by the **Neural Tangent Kernel**:

$$K(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \nabla_\theta f(\mathbf{x}; \theta)^\top \nabla_\theta f(\mathbf{x}'; \theta)$$

The training dynamics linearize around the initial parameters: the network is approximated by its tangent at initialization. This is literally the linearization $f(\mathbf{x}; \theta_0 + \Delta\theta) \approx f(\mathbf{x}; \theta_0) + \nabla_\theta f \cdot \Delta\theta$.

The NTK is the multivariable differential evaluated in function space.

### 8.5 Summary Table

|Concept in Calculus|Analog in ML/DL|

## § 9. Things Worth Remembering

1. **Linearization is exact iff $f$ is affine.** For all other smooth functions, it is an approximation whose quality degrades as $|x - a|$ grows.
    
2. **Differentials are not infinitesimals in the historical sense** (they are not "infinitely small numbers"). They are defined as ordinary real-valued linear functions. The notation $dy = f'(x),dx$ is shorthand for a precise algebraic relation.
    
3. **The tangent line approximation fails at inflection points... in the following sense:** the error $f(x) - L(x)$ changes sign there. It's still $O(\Delta x^2)$, but the _direction_ of the error reverses.
    
4. **In higher dimensions,** differentiability is strictly stronger than the existence of all partial derivatives. A function can have all partials at a point without being differentiable there. The linearization requires the stronger condition.
    

---

## § 10. Summary

$$\underbrace{f(x)}_{\text{actual}} \approx \underbrace{f(a) + f'(a)(x-a)}_{\text{linear approximation}} + \underbrace{O((x-a)^2)}_{\text{error}}$$

$$dy = f'(x),dx \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \Delta y \approx dy \text{ for small } \Delta x$$

The philosophy: **replace a hard nonlinear problem locally by an easy linear one.** This is the fundamental strategy not only in calculus, but in optimization, differential equations, and the training of every large language model that exists today.
