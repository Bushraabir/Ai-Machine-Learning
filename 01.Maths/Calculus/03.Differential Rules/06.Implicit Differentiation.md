## 1. Motivation — When $y$ Cannot Be Solved For

Consider the unit circle: $x^2 + y^2 = 1$.

This is not a function — it fails the vertical line test. We _can_ split it into $y = \sqrt{1-x^2}$ and $y = -\sqrt{1-x^2}$, but this is ugly, loses global structure, and becomes completely intractable for more complex curves like:

$$x^5 + y^5 - 5xy = 0 \quad \text{(folium-like)}$$ $$e^y + \sin(xy) = x^2 \quad \text{(transcendental)}$$

In these cases, isolating $y$ is algebraically impossible. Yet the curve is smooth, and we expect tangent lines to exist. **Implicit differentiation** extracts $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ directly from the equation $F(x,y) = 0$, without ever solving for $y$.

The key insight: _even if we cannot write $y = f(x)$ explicitly, we can treat $y$ as a function of $x$ locally_ (by the Implicit Function Theorem, §7), and differentiate both sides of the equation accordingly.

---

## 2. The Method — Mechanics

### Procedure

Given $F(x, y) = 0$ where $y$ is implicitly a function of $x$:

1. Differentiate **both sides** with respect to $x$.
2. Every time $y$ appears, apply the **chain rule**: $\dfrac{d}{dx}[\text{expression in }y] = \dfrac{d}{dy}[\cdots]\cdot\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.
3. Collect all terms involving $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$ on one side.
4. Solve algebraically for $\dfrac{dy}{dx}$.

### The Formula

If $F(x,y) = 0$, differentiating:

$$F_x + F_y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$

$$\boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}}$$

where $F_x = \dfrac{\partial F}{\partial x}$ and $F_y = \dfrac{\partial F}{\partial y}$.

This is exact — no approximation, no loss of generality. It is a direct consequence of the chain rule applied to $F(x, y(x)) = 0$.

> **Reference:** Stewart §3.5; MIT 18.02 Lecture 12

## 3. Examples

### Example 1 — The Unit Circle

$$F(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 - 1 = 0$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}[x^2 + y^2] = 0 \implies 2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}$$

**Geometric check:** At $(1/\sqrt{2},, 1/\sqrt{2})$: $dy/dx = -1$. The tangent is a $-45°$ line — correct by symmetry. At $(1, 0)$ the formula gives $-1/0$, i.e., vertical tangent — again correct.

---

### Example 2 — Folium of Descartes

$$F(x,y) = x^3 + y^3 - 3xy = 0$$

Differentiate with respect to $x$:

$$3x^2 + 3y^2\frac{dy}{dx} - 3y - 3x\frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$

Factor:

$$\frac{dy}{dx}(3y^2 - 3x) = 3y - 3x^2$$

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{y - x^2}{y^2 - x}$$

The folium has a node at the origin and a loop passing through $(3/2, 3/2)$. At that point: $dy/dx = (3/2 - 9/4)/(9/4 - 3/2) = (-3/4)/(3/4) = -1$. The curve has slope $-1$ there — geometrically, it's crossing its own axis of symmetry $y = x$ at that point, which makes perfect sense.

---

### Example 3 — Transcendental Equation

$$e^{xy} = x + y$$

Differentiate both sides:

$$e^{xy}\left(y + x\frac{dy}{dx}\right) = 1 + \frac{dy}{dx}$$

Expand and collect:

$$ye^{xy} + xe^{xy}\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 + \frac{dy}{dx}$$

$$\frac{dy}{dx}(xe^{xy} - 1) = 1 - ye^{xy}$$

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1 - ye^{xy}}{xe^{xy} - 1}$$

No algebraic approach could have isolated $y$ here. The implicit method cuts through directly.

---

### Example 4 — Second Derivative

Find $\dfrac{d^2y}{dx^2}$ for $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$.

First derivative: $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{x}{y}$.

Differentiate again (quotient rule, remembering $y$ depends on $x$):

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -\frac{y \cdot 1 - x \cdot \dfrac{dy}{dx}}{y^2} = -\frac{y - x(-x/y)}{y^2} = -\frac{y + x^2/y}{y^2} = -\frac{y^2 + x^2}{y^3}$$

Since $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$:

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -\frac{r^2}{y^3}$$

For the upper semicircle ($y > 0$), $d^2y/dx^2 < 0$ — the circle is concave down. Exactly right.

## 4. Visualization — Tangent Lines on Implicit Curves

```
          y
          |         . ' ' .
          |      '           '         ← x² + y² = 1
          |    '               '
          |   '                 '
       ---+--'---+------+---'---+--- x
          |   '       (x₀,y₀)  '
          |    '     /         '       ← tangent line: slope = -x₀/y₀
          |      '   /        '
          |         ' . . . '
          |
```

At any point $(x_0, y_0)$ on the curve, the gradient $\nabla F = (F_x, F_y)$ is **perpendicular to the level curve** $F = 0$. The tangent line is therefore perpendicular to $\nabla F$.

This is not incidental — it is the geometric heart of implicit differentiation and gradient descent.

### The Gradient is Always Normal to the Level Set

$$F(x,y) = c \implies \nabla F \perp \text{tangent to curve}$$

**Proof:** Let $\mathbf{r}(t) = (x(t), y(t))$ be a curve lying on $F = c$. Then $F(\mathbf{r}(t)) = c$ for all $t$. Differentiating:

$$\frac{d}{dt}F(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \nabla F \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) = 0$$

So $\nabla F \perp \mathbf{r}'(t)$ — the gradient is orthogonal to every tangent vector. $\blacksquare$

This is the foundation of constrained optimization, Lagrange multipliers, and support vector machines.

## 5. Multivariable Implicit Differentiation

### Two Variables, One Constraint

Given $F(x, y, z) = 0$ with $z = z(x, y)$:

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{F_x}{F_z}, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -\frac{F_y}{F_z}$$

**Example:** $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 1$ (unit sphere).

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{2x}{2z} = -\frac{x}{z}, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -\frac{y}{z}$$

The tangent plane to the sphere at $(x_0, y_0, z_0)$ has slope $-x_0/z_0$ in the $x$-direction and $-y_0/z_0$ in the $y$-direction.

### The Cyclic Identity (Thermodynamics, Statistical Mechanics)

For $F(x,y,z) = 0$, the three partial derivatives satisfy:

$$\left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial y}\right)_z \cdot \left(\frac{\partial y}{\partial z}\right)_x \cdot \left(\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\right)_y = -1$$

This is **not** $+1$ (as a naive cancellation might suggest), and the minus sign is essential. It appears in the Maxwell relations in thermodynamics and the triple product rule in physical chemistry.

> **Reference:** MIT 18.02 Lecture 13 — Gradient and Level Curves

## 6. The Implicit Function Theorem

_This is the rigorous foundation for why implicit differentiation is valid._

### Theorem (Implicit Function Theorem — IFT)

Let $F : \mathbb{R}^{n+1} \to \mathbb{R}$ be $C^1$ near a point $(x_0, y_0) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}$ satisfying:

1. $F(x_0, y_0) = 0$
2. $\dfrac{\partial F}{\partial y}(x_0, y_0) \neq 0$

Then there exists an open set $U \ni x_0$ and a **unique $C^1$ function** $y : U \to \mathbb{R}$ such that:

- $y(x_0) = y_0$
- $F(x, y(x)) = 0$ for all $x \in U$
- $\dfrac{dy}{dx} = -\dfrac{F_x}{F_y}$

### What This Means

The IFT says: _locally, near any point where $F_y \neq 0$, the curve $F(x,y) = 0$ really is the graph of a function $y = f(x)$._ So implicit differentiation is not a trick — it is a theorem-backed operation.

The condition $F_y \neq 0$ is what fails at vertical tangents (like $(1, 0)$ on the unit circle), and precisely where the formula $dy/dx = -F_x/F_y$ breaks down.

### Generalization

The full IFT handles systems: given $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{0}$ with $\mathbf{F} : \mathbb{R}^{n+m} \to \mathbb{R}^m$, the condition for locally solving for $\mathbf{y}(\mathbf{x})$ is:

$$\det\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial \mathbf{y}}\right) \neq 0$$

i.e., the Jacobian of $\mathbf{F}$ with respect to $\mathbf{y}$ must be invertible. The derivative formula becomes:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = -\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial \mathbf{y}}\right)^{-1} \frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}$$

This is matrix-valued implicit differentiation.

## 7. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 7.1 Implicit Layers in Deep Learning

A **standard layer** computes $y = f(x; W)$ — an explicit map. But some modern architectures define layers **implicitly** via a fixed-point equation:

$$z = f(z, x; \theta) \quad \text{(find } z \text{ such that this holds)}$$

This is called an **implicit layer** or **deep equilibrium model (DEQ)**. Examples include:

- **Deep Equilibrium Models (Bai et al., 2019):** the output $z^\star$ satisfies $z^\star = f_\theta(z^\star, x)$.
- **Neural ODEs:** the output is defined by an ODE, not a finite sequence of operations.

To train these, we need $\dfrac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta}$. But we never "unrolled" the computation — there's no explicit computational graph.

**Solution:** The implicit function theorem. At the fixed point $z^\star$, define $F(z, \theta) = z - f_\theta(z, x) = 0$. Then:

$$\frac{dz^\star}{d\theta} = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial z}\right)^{-1}\frac{\partial F}{\partial \theta} = -\left(I - \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}\right)^{-1}\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}$$

The gradient propagates **through the fixed-point equation**, not through the iterations used to find it. This is the **implicit differentiation trick** — and it's more memory-efficient than backpropagating through thousands of unrolled steps.

### 7.2 Constraint Satisfaction and Lagrange Multipliers

Training under constraints (e.g., fairness, energy, physics) involves:

$$\min_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta) \quad \text{subject to } G(\theta) = 0$$

The optimality condition is $\nabla \mathcal{L} = \lambda \nabla G$ — the gradient of the loss must be normal to the constraint surface. This is the geometry of implicit curves in high dimensions.

The constraint surface $G(\theta) = 0$ is a level set of $G$, and its tangent hyperplane is characterized by $\nabla G \perp \text{tangent vectors}$ — exactly the implicit differentiation picture from §4.

### 7.3 Meta-Learning and Implicit Gradients

In **MAML (Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning)** and bilevel optimization, we differentiate through the solution of an inner optimization:

$$\theta^\star(\phi) = \arg\min_\theta \mathcal{L}_\text{inner}(\theta, \phi)$$

At a minimum, $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_\text{inner} = 0$. Define $F(\theta, \phi) = \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_\text{inner}(\theta, \phi) = \mathbf{0}$. Then by the IFT:

$$\frac{d\theta^\star}{d\phi} = -\left(\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}_\text{inner}}{\partial \theta^2}\right)^{-1}\frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}_\text{inner}}{\partial \theta , \partial \phi}$$

This requires the **Hessian inverse** — computationally expensive, but analytically exact. Approximations to this drive much of modern meta-learning research.

### 7.4 Energy-Based Models

In energy-based models (EBMs), the output is:

$$y^\star(x) = \arg\min_y E_\theta(x, y)$$

At the minimum, $\dfrac{\partial E}{\partial y} = 0$ — an implicit equation. Differentiating this with respect to $x$:

$$\frac{dy^\star}{dx} = -\left(\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial y^2}\right)^{-1}\frac{\partial^2 E}{\partial y , \partial x}$$

The gradient of the predicted output with respect to the input involves the **Hessian of the energy** — a second-order implicit derivative.

## 8. Implicit vs. Explicit Differentiation — When to Use What

|Situation|Use|
|---|---|
|$y$ can be isolated easily|Explicit — just solve and differentiate|
|$y$ cannot be isolated (most curves)|Implicit differentiation|
|Need $dy/dx$ at a specific point only|Implicit is faster even when $y$ can be isolated|
|Multivariable constraint $F(x,y,z)=0$|Always implicit|
|Neural network with implicit layers/DEQs|IFT-based implicit differentiation|
|Bilevel optimization / meta-learning|IFT applied to optimality conditions|

## 9. Common Errors

1. **Forgetting the chain rule on $y$.** Writing $\dfrac{d}{dx}[y^2] = 2y$ instead of $2y,\dfrac{dy}{dx}$. This is the single most common mistake.
    
2. **Treating $y$ as a constant.** When differentiating $xy$, the product rule gives $y + x,\dfrac{dy}{dx}$, not just $y$.
    
3. **Dividing by $F_y = 0$.** The formula $dy/dx = -F_x/F_y$ fails exactly where $F_y = 0$. Check for vertical tangents separately.
    
4. **In the multivariable case**, forgetting the cyclic minus sign: $\left(\dfrac{\partial x}{\partial y}\right)_z \neq \left(\dfrac{\partial y}{\partial x}\right)_z^{-1}$ in general for constrained variables.
    
5. **In ML contexts**, confusing the Jacobian of the constraint with the Jacobian of the output — when applying IFT to implicit layers, the matrix to invert is $(I - \partial f/\partial z)$, not $\partial f / \partial z$ itself.

## 10. Summary

$$F(x,y) = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}$$

The formula is simple. Its depth comes from:

- The chain rule is doing all the work invisibly.
- The Implicit Function Theorem guarantees the formula is valid wherever $F_y \neq 0$.
- In higher dimensions, this becomes $J_y^{-1} J_x$ — matrix implicit differentiation.
- In machine learning, this enables gradients through fixed points, constraints, equilibria, and bilevel programs — things standard backprop cannot reach.
