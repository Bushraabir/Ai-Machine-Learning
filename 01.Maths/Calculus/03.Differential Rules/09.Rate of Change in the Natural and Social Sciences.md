## 0. Prelude — What Is a Rate of Change?

Let $Q = f(t)$ be any quantity that changes with time (or with some other independent variable). The **instantaneous rate of change** of $Q$ at time $t$ is:

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{f(t + \Delta t) - f(t)}{\Delta t}$$

The **average rate of change** over $[t_1, t_2]$ is:

$$\frac{\Delta Q}{\Delta t} = \frac{f(t_2) - f(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1}$$

The instantaneous rate is the limit of the average rate. Every application below is a specific _interpretation_ of this single mathematical object.

> **Dimensional remark.** The units of $\frac{dQ}{dt}$ are always $[\text{units of } Q] / [\text{units of } t]$. This is the first sanity check in any applied problem.

## 1. Physics — Mechanics

### 1.1 Position, Velocity, and Acceleration

Let $s(t)$ denote the **position** of a particle along a straight line at time $t$ (in metres, say).

|Quantity|Definition|Units|
|---|---|---|
|Velocity|$v(t) = \dfrac{ds}{dt} = s'(t)$|m/s|
|Acceleration|$a(t) = \dfrac{dv}{dt} = v'(t) = s''(t)$|m/s²|
|Jerk|$j(t) = \dfrac{da}{dt} = s'''(t)$|m/s³|

**The sign convention matters:**

- $v(t) > 0$: particle moves in the positive direction.
- $v(t) < 0$: particle moves in the negative direction.
- $v(t) = 0$: particle is momentarily at rest (possible turning point).
- $a(t)$ and $v(t)$ have the **same sign**: particle is speeding up.
- $a(t)$ and $v(t)$ have **opposite signs**: particle is slowing down.

**Worked Example.**

A particle moves along a line with position $s(t) = t^3 - 6t^2 + 9t + 2$ (metres), $t \geq 0$ (seconds).

$$v(t) = s'(t) = 3t^2 - 12t + 9 = 3(t-1)(t-3)$$

$$a(t) = v'(t) = 6t - 12 = 6(t - 2)$$

- At $t = 1$: $v = 0$ (turns around), $a = -6 < 0$ (still decelerating into the turn).
- At $t = 2$: $a = 0$ (inflection in velocity — jerk changes sign).
- At $t = 3$: $v = 0$ (second rest), $a = 6 > 0$ (accelerating forward).
- For $t \in (1,3)$: $v < 0$, particle moving backward.

**Newton's Second Law.** This is the law that _is_ a rate of change:

$$F = ma = m\frac{d^2s}{dt^2} = \frac{d}{dt}(mv) = \frac{dp}{dt}$$

Force is the rate of change of **momentum** $p = mv$.

### 1.2 Free Fall and Projectile Motion

Near Earth's surface, ignoring air resistance:

$$s(t) = s_0 + v_0 t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2$$

$$v(t) = v_0 - gt, \qquad a(t) = -g \approx -9.8 \text{ m/s}^2$$

The object hits the ground when $s(t) = 0$; its speed on impact is $|v(t_{\text{impact}})|$.

**Example.** A stone is dropped from a 80 m cliff ($s_0 = 80$, $v_0 = 0$):

$$0 = 80 - \frac{1}{2}(9.8)t^2 \implies t = \sqrt{\frac{160}{9.8}} \approx 4.04 \text{ s}$$

$$v(4.04) = -9.8 \times 4.04 \approx -39.6 \text{ m/s}$$

Speed on impact: $39.6$ m/s $\approx 142$ km/h.

## 2. Physics — Electricity and Thermodynamics

### 2.1 Electric Current

If $Q(t)$ is the **electric charge** (in coulombs) flowing past a point in a circuit:

$$I(t) = \frac{dQ}{dt}$$

Current is the **rate of flow of charge**. Units: amperes = C/s.

**Example.** If $Q(t) = 3t^2 + 2t$ coulombs:

$$I(t) = 6t + 2 \text{ A}$$

At $t = 3$ s: $I = 20$ A.

### 2.2 Power

If $W(t)$ is energy (joules) delivered by a circuit:

$$P(t) = \frac{dW}{dt} \quad \text{(watts = J/s)}$$

### 2.3 Newton's Law of Cooling

The temperature $T(t)$ of an object cooling in an environment at temperature $T_{\text{env}}$ satisfies:

$$\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_{\text{env}}), \quad k > 0$$

This says: **the rate of cooling is proportional to the temperature excess**.

**Solution:**

$$T(t) = T_{\text{env}} + (T_0 - T_{\text{env}})e^{-kt}$$

**Forensic Example.** A body is discovered at 2:00 AM with temperature $30°C$ in a $20°C$ room. At 2:30 AM it is $25°C$. Normal body temperature is $37°C$. When did death occur?

From the model: $$30 = 20 + 17e^{0 \cdot (-k)} \Rightarrow e^{-k \cdot 0} = 1 \text{ (reset clock at first measurement)}$$

Actually: $30 = 20 + 17e^{-k \cdot t_1}$ and $25 = 20 + 17 e^{-k(t_1 + 0.5)}$.

$$\frac{10}{5} = e^{0.5k} \implies k = 2\ln 2 \approx 1.386 \text{ hr}^{-1}$$

$$30 = 20 + 17 e^{-1.386 t_1} \implies e^{-1.386 t_1} = \frac{10}{17} \implies t_1 = \frac{\ln(17/10)}{1.386} \approx 0.38 \text{ hr}$$

Death occurred approximately $0.38$ hours before 2:00 AM, i.e., around **1:37 AM**.

## 3. Chemistry — Reaction Rates

### 3.1 The Rate of a Chemical Reaction

Consider the reaction $A \to B$. If $[A](https://claude.ai/chat/t)$ denotes the concentration of reactant $A$ (in mol/L):

$$\text{Rate} = -\frac{d[A]}{dt} = \frac{d[B]}{dt}$$

The **negative sign** reflects that $A$ is being consumed.

For a **first-order reaction**: $\frac{d[A]}{dt} = -k[A]$

**Solution:** $[A](https://claude.ai/chat/t) = [A]_0 e^{-kt}$

**Half-life:** $t_{1/2} = \dfrac{\ln 2}{k}$

**Example — Radioactive Decay (Physics/Chemistry crossover).**

Carbon-14 has half-life $t_{1/2} = 5730$ years. A fossil contains $30%$ of the original ${}^{14}C$.

$$k = \frac{\ln 2}{5730} \approx 1.21 \times 10^{-4} \text{ yr}^{-1}$$

$$0.30 = e^{-kt} \implies t = \frac{\ln(1/0.30)}{k} = \frac{\ln(10/3)}{1.21 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 9940 \text{ years old}$$

### 3.2 Second-Order Reactions

If the rate depends on two reactants: $\frac{d[A]}{dt} = -k[A]^2$

$$\frac{1}{[A](https://claude.ai/chat/t)} = \frac{1}{[A]_0} + kt$$

The rate of change itself changes with concentration — a nonlinear ODE.

## 4. Biology — Population Dynamics and Epidemiology

### 4.1 Exponential Population Growth

Let $P(t)$ = population size at time $t$.

**Malthusian model:**

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP$$

$r$ = intrinsic growth rate (birth rate minus death rate).

**Solution:** $P(t) = P_0 e^{rt}$

**Example.** A bacterial culture starts with $P_0 = 100$ cells and doubles every 20 minutes. What is the population after 2 hours?

$$r = \frac{\ln 2}{20 \text{ min}} \implies P(120) = 100 \cdot e^{({\ln 2}/{20}) \cdot 120} = 100 \cdot 2^6 = 6400 \text{ cells}$$

### 4.2 Logistic Growth

Exponential growth is unrealistic long-term (limited resources). The **logistic model**:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)$$

where $K$ = **carrying capacity** of the environment.

- When $P \ll K$: $\frac{dP}{dt} \approx rP$ (exponential phase).
- When $P \approx K$: $\frac{dP}{dt} \approx 0$ (growth stalls).
- When $P > K$: $\frac{dP}{dt} < 0$ (population declines).

**Solution:**

$$P(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \left(\dfrac{K - P_0}{P_0}\right)e^{-rt}}$$

Maximum growth rate occurs at $P = K/2$ (the **inflection point**).

### 4.3 The SIR Model (Epidemiology)

A compartmental model for infectious disease. Let $S$, $I$, $R$ be the susceptible, infected, recovered fractions of the population.

$$\frac{dS}{dt} = -\beta SI$$

$$\frac{dI}{dt} = \beta SI - \gamma I$$

$$\frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I$$

$\beta$ = transmission rate, $\gamma$ = recovery rate.

The **basic reproduction number** $R_0 = \beta/\gamma$:

- $R_0 > 1$: epidemic grows ($dI/dt > 0$ when $S \approx 1$).
- $R_0 < 1$: disease dies out.

> The COVID-19 pandemic modelling was built on this framework. The rate $\frac{dI}{dt}$ was the headline number that drove policy decisions worldwide.

## 5. Medicine and Pharmacology

### 5.1 Drug Concentration (Pharmacokinetics)

After a drug is administered, its concentration in the bloodstream $C(t)$ typically satisfies:

$$\frac{dC}{dt} = -k_e C$$

where $k_e$ is the **elimination rate constant**.

**Solution:** $C(t) = C_0 e^{-k_e t}$

**Half-life:** $t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / k_e$

**Clinical Example.** Ibuprofen has a half-life of approximately 2 hours. If $C_0 = 400$ mg:

$$k_e = \frac{\ln 2}{2} \approx 0.347 \text{ hr}^{-1}$$

After 6 hours: $C(6) = 400 e^{-0.347 \times 6} = 400 \times (0.5)^3 = 50$ mg.

This tells a physician when re-dosing is necessary and helps determine safe dosing intervals.

### 5.2 Two-Compartment Model

In reality, drugs distribute between blood plasma and tissue. The rate of change in each compartment is:

$$\frac{dC_1}{dt} = -(k_{12} + k_{10})C_1 + k_{21}C_2$$

$$\frac{dC_2}{dt} = k_{12}C_1 - k_{21}C_2$$

A system of ODEs — still built on rates of change of concentration.

### 5.3 Tumour Growth

The **Gompertz model** for tumour growth:

$$\frac{dV}{dt} = -\alpha V \ln!\left(\frac{V}{V_{\max}}\right)$$

Here $V(t)$ is tumour volume. The $\ln$ factor causes growth to slow as $V \to V_{\max}$, matching clinical data better than logistic models for many cancers.

## 6. Economics and Social Sciences

### 6.1 Marginal Analysis

In economics, **marginal** means "derivative of".

|Concept|Definition|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|Marginal Cost|$MC(x) = C'(x)$|Cost of producing one more unit|
|Marginal Revenue|$MR(x) = R'(x)$|Revenue from selling one more unit|
|Marginal Profit|$MP(x) = P'(x) = MR - MC$|Profit from one more unit|

**Profit is maximized** when $MP(x) = 0$, i.e., $MR(x) = MC(x)$.

**Example.** A firm's cost function is $C(x) = 0.001x^3 - 0.3x^2 + 100x + 5000$ (dollars), where $x$ is units produced.

$$MC(x) = C'(x) = 0.003x^2 - 0.6x + 100$$

At $x = 100$: $MC = 0.003(10000) - 60 + 100 = 70$ $/unit.

At $x = 200$: $MC = 0.003(40000) - 120 + 100 = 100$ $/unit.

The cost of the 201st unit is approximately $100 — more than the 101st unit ($70). Diminishing returns.

### 6.2 Elasticity of Demand

Price elasticity of demand measures how responsive quantity demanded $Q$ is to a change in price $P$:

$$E = \frac{dQ/Q}{dP/P} = \frac{P}{Q} \cdot \frac{dQ}{dP}$$

- $|E| > 1$: elastic (e.g., luxury goods). A 1% price increase causes >1% drop in demand.
- $|E| < 1$: inelastic (e.g., insulin). Demand is insensitive to price.
- $|E| = 1$: unit elastic. Revenue is maximized here.

**Example.** Demand function $Q(P) = 1200 - 5P$.

$$\frac{dQ}{dP} = -5 \implies E = \frac{P}{Q} \cdot (-5) = \frac{-5P}{1200 - 5P}$$

At $P = 100$: $Q = 700$, $E = -500/700 \approx -0.71$ (inelastic).

At $P = 180$: $Q = 300$, $E = -900/300 = -3$ (elastic).

### 6.3 Compound Interest and Continuous Growth

If an investment $A(t)$ grows at continuous rate $r$:

$$\frac{dA}{dt} = rA \implies A(t) = A_0 e^{rt}$$

This is the same ODE as population growth and radioactive decay. The mathematics is universal.

**Example.** $10,000 invested at 5% continuously compounded for 20 years:

$$A(20) = 10000 \cdot e^{0.05 \times 20} = 10000 \cdot e \approx $27{,}183$$

### 6.4 Spread of Information / Social Contagion

The spread of a rumour, adoption of a technology, or viral social media content often follows:

$$\frac{dN}{dt} = kN(M - N)$$

where $N$ = number who have adopted, $M$ = total population. This is the logistic equation — identical to population biology. The mathematics of epidemic spread and viral marketing are formally the same.

## 7. Environmental Science

### 7.1 Atmospheric CO₂ and Climate

The **Keeling Curve** records $\text{CO}_2$ concentration $C(t)$ at Mauna Loa. The rate of change:

$$\frac{dC}{dt} \approx 2.5 \text{ ppm/year (current estimate)}$$

This rate itself is increasing — the **second derivative** $\frac{d^2C}{dt^2} > 0$ indicates accelerating emissions.

### 7.2 Fluid Flow / Streamflow

The **discharge** $Q$ (m³/s) of a river is the rate of change of cumulative volume $V$:

$$Q(t) = \frac{dV}{dt}$$

During a storm event, hydrologists model the **hydrograph** — the graph of $Q(t)$ — to predict flooding. The peak of $Q(t)$ corresponds to maximum flood risk.

### 7.3 Pollution Dilution

A lake of volume $V$ (m³) receives pollutant at rate $r_{in}$ and outflows at rate $r_{out}$. Concentration $C(t)$:

$$V \frac{dC}{dt} = r_{in}C_{in} - r_{out}C$$

A first-order linear ODE. The rate of change of concentration determines whether cleanup interventions are working.

## 8. Demography

### 8.1 Birth and Death Rates

Let $P(t)$ = population. The **vital rates** are:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = (b - d)P + I - E$$

$b$ = birth rate, $d$ = death rate, $I$ = immigration rate, $E$ = emigration rate.

The **net reproduction rate** $r = b - d$ determines long-run growth.

### 8.2 Age-Structured Populations (McKendrick-von Foerster)

A more refined model tracks the population density $n(a, t)$ by age $a$:

$$\frac{\partial n}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial n}{\partial a} = -\mu(a)n(a,t)$$

where $\mu(a)$ is the age-specific mortality rate. This **partial differential equation** is built from rates of change in both time and age. Used by actuaries, pension funds, and national statistics offices.

### 8.3 Urbanization Rate

The fraction $U(t)$ of a country's population living in cities:

$$\frac{dU}{dt}$$

is tracked by the UN to project infrastructure needs. Many developing nations currently have $dU/dt \approx 0.5$–$1%$/year.

## 9. Connection to AI and Machine Learning

The derivative is not just applied to model the world — it is the engine of _learning itself_.

### 9.1 Gradient Descent — The Universal Optimizer

Every parameter update in machine learning is a rate of change:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_t}$$

The model **learns** by moving parameters in the direction of steepest descent of the loss $\mathcal{L}$. This is $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta}$ — a rate of change of error with respect to parameters.

### 9.2 Backpropagation = Repeated Chain Rule

In a neural network with layers $f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_L$, the output is:

$$\hat{y} = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{x})$$

By the Chain Rule:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{W}_k} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial f_L} \cdots \frac{\partial f_{k+1}}{\partial f_k} \cdot \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial \mathbf{W}_k}$$

Backpropagation is the **recursive application of rates of change** through the layers. Every gradient in PyTorch/TensorFlow is a derivative.

### 9.3 Neural ODEs

A modern deep learning architecture (Chen et al., 2018) defines the forward pass as the solution to an ODE:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{h}(t)}{dt} = f(\mathbf{h}(t), t, \theta)$$

where $\mathbf{h}(t)$ is a hidden state. The "depth" of the network becomes a continuous variable $t$. Training means learning $f$ — i.e., learning a rate of change. Neural ODEs are used in time-series modelling and generative models (continuous normalizing flows).

### 9.4 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

Given a PDE from physics (e.g., heat equation $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \alpha \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$), a PINN trains a neural network to satisfy the equation by penalizing violations of the PDE in the loss function. The derivative terms in the PDE are computed via automatic differentiation — the same mechanism as backpropagation.

### 9.5 Dynamical Systems and Reinforcement Learning

In RL, the **value function** $V^\pi(s)$ satisfies the Bellman equation, which in continuous time becomes the **Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman PDE**:

$$-\frac{\partial V}{\partial t} = \max_a \left[r(s,a) + \nabla_s V \cdot f(s,a)\right]$$

$\nabla_s V$ is the gradient — a vector of rates of change of value with respect to state. Solving this is the foundation of optimal control and model-based RL.

## 10. The Unifying Framework — Differential Equations

Almost every application above reduces to one of a handful of canonical ODEs:

### Exponential (Malthusian) Model

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = kQ \implies Q(t) = Q_0 e^{kt}$$

**Appears in:** radioactive decay ($k < 0$), population growth, compound interest, drug elimination, cooling (near equilibrium).

### Logistic Model

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = kQ\left(1 - \frac{Q}{K}\right) \implies Q(t) = \frac{K}{1 + Ce^{-kt}}$$

**Appears in:** population biology, epidemic modelling, technology adoption, tumour growth.

### Newton's Cooling / First-Order Linear

$$\frac{dQ}{dt} = -k(Q - Q_{\infty}) \implies Q(t) = Q_\infty + (Q_0 - Q_\infty)e^{-kt}$$

**Appears in:** temperature, pollutant dilution, drug washout, RC circuits.

### Second-Order (Oscillator)

$$\frac{d^2x}{dt^2} + \omega^2 x = 0 \implies x(t) = A\cos(\omega t) + B\sin(\omega t)$$

**Appears in:** spring-mass systems, pendulums, electrical LC circuits, sound waves, orbital mechanics.

> **Key insight.** The same differential equation governs phenomena across wildly different disciplines. The rate of change is the deep structure; the physical interpretation is the surface. This is why mathematicians and physicists often find that solving one problem solves many.
