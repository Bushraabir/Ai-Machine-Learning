## Core Idea: The Fundamental ODE

All exponential phenomena trace back to a single differential equation:

$$\frac{dy}{dt} = ky$$

This equation says: **the rate of change of a quantity is proportional to the quantity itself.**

Solving by separation of variables:

$$\frac{dy}{y} = k , dt \implies \ln|y| = kt + C \implies y(t) = y_0 e^{kt}$$

where $y_0 = y(0)$ is the initial condition.

- If $k > 0$: exponential **growth**
- If $k < 0$: exponential **decay**

This is the master formula. Every application below is a specialization of it.

## 1. Population Growth

### Setup

Let $P(t)$ = population at time $t$.

Malthusian (unlimited) growth assumes birth rate minus death rate is constant $r$:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP$$

### Solution

$$P(t) = P_0 e^{rt}$$

where $P_0 = P(0)$ is the initial population.

### Doubling Time

Set $P(t) = 2P_0$:

$$2P_0 = P_0 e^{rt} \implies e^{rt} = 2 \implies t_{\text{double}} = \frac{\ln 2}{r}$$

### Worked Example

A bacterial colony starts at 1000 cells. The growth rate is $r = 0.3$ per hour.

1. Population after 5 hours: $P(5) = 1000 \cdot e^{0.3 \cdot 5} = 1000 \cdot e^{1.5} \approx 4482$
2. Doubling time: $t_d = \ln 2 / 0.3 \approx 2.31$ hours

### Limitation

This model breaks down when resources are finite. The **logistic model** extends it:

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)$$

where $K$ is the carrying capacity. For this note, we stay in the pure exponential regime (early-stage growth, or $P \ll K$).

## 2. Radioactive Decay

### Setup

Radioactive nuclei decay at a rate proportional to the number of undecayed nuclei present. Let $N(t)$ = number of nuclei.

$$\frac{dN}{dt} = -\lambda N$$

where $\lambda > 0$ is the **decay constant** (units: $\text{time}^{-1}$). The negative sign enforces decay.

### Solution

$$N(t) = N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$$

### Half-Life

The **half-life** $t_{1/2}$ is the time for half the nuclei to decay:

$$\frac{N_0}{2} = N_0 e^{-\lambda t_{1/2}} \implies t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda}$$

Equivalently: $\lambda = \dfrac{\ln 2}{t_{1/2}}$

### Relationship: $\lambda$ and $t_{1/2}$

|Quantity|Formula|
|---|---|
|Decay constant|$\lambda = \ln 2 / t_{1/2}$|
|Half-life|$t_{1/2} = \ln 2 / \lambda$|
|Activity (decays/sec)|$A(t) = \lambda N(t)$|

### Worked Example

Carbon-14 has a half-life of 5730 years. A fossil has 30% of original C-14 remaining. Find its age.

$$0.30 = e^{-\lambda t}, \quad \lambda = \frac{\ln 2}{5730}$$

$$t = \frac{-\ln(0.30)}{\lambda} = \frac{\ln(0.30) \cdot 5730}{-\ln 2} = \frac{1.2040 \cdot 5730}{0.6931} \approx 9953 \text{ years}$$

### Key Insight

The half-life is **independent of the initial quantity**. Whether you start with $10^6$ or $10^{12}$ nuclei, the same fraction remains after each half-life. This is a direct consequence of the linear ODE structure.

## 3. Newton's Law of Cooling

### Setup

The rate of heat loss of an object is proportional to the difference between the object's temperature and the surrounding ambient temperature $T_a$.

Let $T(t)$ = temperature of the object at time $t$:

$$\frac{dT}{dt} = -k(T - T_a), \quad k > 0$$

### Solving the ODE

Let $u(t) = T(t) - T_a$. Then $\frac{du}{dt} = \frac{dT}{dt} = -ku$.

This is the standard decay ODE:

$$u(t) = u_0 e^{-kt} \implies T(t) - T_a = (T_0 - T_a)e^{-kt}$$

$$\boxed{T(t) = T_a + (T_0 - T_a)e^{-kt}}$$

### Interpretation

- As $t \to \infty$: $T(t) \to T_a$ (object reaches ambient temperature)
- The temperature difference decays exponentially
- $k$ depends on the object's surface area, material, and fluid properties (from heat transfer physics)

### Worked Example

A cup of coffee at $90^\circ$C is left in a $20^\circ$C room. After 10 minutes it cools to $70^\circ$C. Find its temperature after 30 minutes.

Step 1: Find $k$.

$$70 = 20 + (90 - 20)e^{-10k} \implies 50 = 70e^{-10k} \implies e^{-10k} = \frac{5}{7}$$

$$-10k = \ln!\left(\frac{5}{7}\right) \implies k = \frac{\ln(7/5)}{10} \approx 0.03365$$

Step 2: Temperature at $t = 30$.

$$T(30) = 20 + 70 \cdot e^{-0.03365 \cdot 30} = 20 + 70 \cdot \left(\frac{5}{7}\right)^3 = 20 + 70 \cdot \frac{125}{343} \approx 45.5^\circ\text{C}$$

### Forensic Application

Newton's Law of Cooling is used in forensics to estimate the time of death. If the body temperature is measured at two times post-discovery, the system of equations can be solved for both $k$ and the time elapsed since death.

## 4. Continuously Compounded Interest

### Setup

In standard compound interest with $n$ compounding periods per year:

$$A = P\left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^{nt}$$

As $n \to \infty$ (continuous compounding), recall the definition of $e$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{r}{n}\right)^n = e^r$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{A(t) = Pe^{rt}}$$

This is exactly the exponential growth equation with the ODE:

$$\frac{dA}{dt} = rA$$

meaning: the instantaneous rate of growth of money equals the rate $r$ times the current amount.

### Comparison: Discrete vs Continuous

|Compounding|Formula|Effective Annual Rate|
|---|---|---|
|Annual ($n=1$)|$A = P(1+r)^t$|$r$|
|Monthly ($n=12$)|$A = P(1+r/12)^{12t}$|$(1+r/12)^{12} - 1$|
|Continuous|$A = Pe^{rt}$|$e^r - 1$|

### Worked Example

$$5000$ is invested at $6%$ annual interest continuously compounded. Find its value after 10 years, and the time to double.

Value after 10 years:

$$A(10) = 5000 \cdot e^{0.06 \cdot 10} = 5000 \cdot e^{0.6} \approx 5000 \cdot 1.8221 \approx $9110.59$$

Time to double:

$$2P = Pe^{rt} \implies t = \frac{\ln 2}{r} = \frac{0.6931}{0.06} \approx 11.55 \text{ years}$$

### Rule of 70

An approximation: at interest rate $r%$, doubling time $\approx 70/r$ years. This comes from $\ln 2 \approx 0.693 \approx 0.70$.

## Unified Structure: All Four Models

|Application|ODE|Solution|Key Parameter|
|---|---|---|---|
|Population growth|$dP/dt = rP$|$P_0 e^{rt}$|Growth rate $r > 0$|
|Radioactive decay|$dN/dt = -\lambda N$|$N_0 e^{-\lambda t}$|Decay constant $\lambda > 0$|
|Newton's cooling|$dT/dt = -k(T-T_a)$|$T_a + (T_0-T_a)e^{-kt}$|Cooling constant $k > 0$|
|Compound interest|$dA/dt = rA$|$Pe^{rt}$|Interest rate $r > 0$|

Newton's cooling is the only one where the quantity of interest ($T - T_a$, not $T$ itself) follows the basic ODE.

## Finding the Constant $k$: General Strategy

Given two data points $(t_1, y_1)$ and $(t_2, y_2)$:

$$\frac{y_2}{y_1} = \frac{y_0 e^{kt_2}}{y_0 e^{kt_1}} = e^{k(t_2 - t_1)}$$

$$k = \frac{\ln(y_2/y_1)}{t_2 - t_1}$$

This does not require knowing $y_0$. Once $k$ is found, plug back into $y_1 = y_0 e^{kt_1}$ to find $y_0$ if needed.

## Linearization: Why Semi-log Plots Matter

If $y(t) = y_0 e^{kt}$, take the natural log of both sides:

$$\ln y = \ln y_0 + kt$$

This is linear in $t$. On a **semi-log plot** (log scale on $y$-axis, linear on $x$-axis), exponential data becomes a straight line.

- Slope of the line = $k$
- $y$-intercept = $\ln y_0$

This is how scientists verify whether a process is truly exponential and extract $k$ from experimental data.

## Common Errors and Misconceptions

**Error 1: Confusing half-life and time constant.**

The **time constant** $\tau = 1/k$ (or $1/\lambda$) is the time for the quantity to fall to $1/e \approx 36.8%$ of its initial value. It is not the half-life. Half-life $= \tau \ln 2 \approx 0.693\tau$.

**Error 2: Applying growth/decay to the wrong quantity.**

In Newton's cooling, the exponential law applies to $T(t) - T_a$, not to $T(t)$ directly. Forgetting to subtract $T_a$ before modeling gives wrong answers.

**Error 3: Unit inconsistency.**

If $r$ is in percent per year and $t$ is in months, the formula breaks. Always ensure $r$ and $t$ have consistent units.

## Connections to the Broader Curriculum

- **Stat 110 / Probability**: The exponential distribution $f(t) = \lambda e^{-\lambda t}$ is the continuous analog of geometric distribution, and governs the waiting time between events in a Poisson process. The decay constant $\lambda$ here is precisely the same as radioactive decay.
- **MIT 18.02 / Calculus**: Separation of variables technique used here is foundational. The integrating factor method generalizes this to non-separable first-order ODEs.
- **CS229 / ML**: Exponential families, learning rate schedules ($\alpha_t = \alpha_0 e^{-\lambda t}$), and the softmax function all embed exponential structure.
- **Pattern Recognition and ML (Bishop)**: Gaussian distributions contain exponential terms; the log-likelihood linearizes them, exactly as the semi-log trick above.

## Summary

All four applications (population growth, radioactive decay, Newton's cooling, continuously compounded interest) are governed by the single ODE $dy/dt = ky$. The solution is always an exponential. The sign of $k$ determines growth versus decay. Newton's cooling differs only in that the relevant quantity is the temperature surplus above ambient, not the temperature itself. Mastery of this note reduces to: recognizing the ODE in disguise, solving for the constant from data, and applying the formula precisely.
