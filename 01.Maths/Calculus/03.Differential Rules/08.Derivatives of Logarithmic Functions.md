## 1. The Natural Logarithm â€” Setup and Motivation

Recall that the natural logarithm $\ln x$ is defined for $x > 0$ as the **inverse** of $e^x$:

$$e^{\ln x} = x \quad \text{for all } x > 0$$

Equivalently, using the integral definition (MIT 18.02 perspective):

$$\ln x := \int_1^x \frac{1}{t}, dt, \quad x > 0$$

This integral definition is sometimes taken as _the_ definition. From it, all properties of $\ln$ follow â€” including its derivative, which becomes almost tautological. But let us proceed from the inverse function approach, as Stewart does, since it illuminates the technique.

> **Remark.** The domain is $x > 0$. We will extend to $\ln|x|$ shortly, which gives domain $x \neq 0$.

---

## 2. The Core Theorem

> **Theorem (Derivative of $\ln x$).** The function $f(x) = \ln x$ is differentiable on $(0, \infty)$, and $$\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}$$

This is arguably the most beautiful derivative in all of calculus. A transcendental function whose derivative is a rational function.

---

## 3. Rigorous Proof via Implicit Differentiation

**Proof.**

Let $y = \ln x$. By definition of the natural logarithm as the inverse of $e^x$:

$$e^y = x$$

Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, using the Chain Rule on the left:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[e^y] = \frac{d}{dx}[x]$$

$$e^y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 1$$

Solve for $\frac{dy}{dx}$:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{e^y}$$

Substitute back $e^y = x$:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{x} \qquad \blacksquare$$

**What this proof requires:**

- The derivative of $e^x$ (established separately: $(e^x)' = e^x$).
- The Chain Rule.
- The fact that $e^y$ is differentiable and its inverse is defined on $(0,\infty)$.
- Invertibility is guaranteed because $e^x$ is strictly increasing.

---

## 4. Alternative Proof via the Definition of $e$

Here is a second proof, using the limit definition of the derivative directly. This proof is illuminating because it reveals _why_ $e$ is the right base.

**Proof.**

By definition:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\ln(x+h) - \ln x}{h}$$

Use the logarithm law $\ln A - \ln B = \ln(A/B)$:

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \ln!\left(\frac{x+h}{x}\right) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{h} \ln!\left(1 + \frac{h}{x}\right)$$

Let $u = h/x$, so $h = ux$ and $h \to 0 \Leftrightarrow u \to 0$:

$$= \lim_{u \to 0} \frac{1}{ux} \ln(1 + u) = \frac{1}{x} \lim_{u \to 0} \frac{\ln(1+u)}{u}$$

Now use the continuity of $\ln$ and the key limit $\displaystyle\lim_{u \to 0}(1+u)^{1/u} = e$:

$$\lim_{u \to 0} \frac{\ln(1+u)}{u} = \lim_{u \to 0} \ln(1+u)^{1/u} = \ln!\left(\lim_{u \to 0}(1+u)^{1/u}\right) = \ln e = 1$$

Therefore:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x} \cdot 1 = \frac{1}{x} \qquad \blacksquare$$

> **Note.** This is exactly _why_ $e$ is the "natural" base. Any other base $a$ would leave a lingering factor of $\ln a$ (see Â§5 below). Base $e$ makes that factor equal to 1, giving the cleanest possible derivative.

---

## 5. The General Logarithm $\log_a x$

**Theorem.** For $a > 0$, $a \neq 1$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[\log_a x] = \frac{1}{x \ln a}$$

**Proof.**

Use the change-of-base formula:

$$\log_a x = \frac{\ln x}{\ln a}$$

Since $\ln a$ is a constant (with respect to $x$):

$$\frac{d}{dx}[\log_a x] = \frac{1}{\ln a} \cdot \frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{\ln a} \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{x \ln a} \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Special cases:**

- $a = e$: $\frac{1}{x \ln e} = \frac{1}{x}$ âś“
- $a = 10$: $\frac{d}{dx}[\log_{10} x] = \frac{1}{x \ln 10} \approx \frac{0.4343}{x}$

> **Remark.** This factor $\frac{1}{\ln a}$ is why base-10 logarithms are "unnatural" for calculus â€” they carry dead weight. Engineers use $\log_{10}$; mathematicians and everything downstream (information theory, ML) prefer $\ln$.

## 6. Logarithmic Differentiation

Logarithmic differentiation is a _technique_, not just a formula. It is the tool of choice whenever:

1. The function is a product/quotient of many terms.
2. The exponent involves the variable (variable-base, variable-exponent).

**Procedure:**

1. Set $y = f(x)$.
2. Take $\ln$ of both sides: $\ln y = \ln f(x)$.
3. Differentiate implicitly with respect to $x$.
4. Solve for $y'$ and substitute back $y = f(x)$.

**Why it works:** $\ln$ converts multiplicative structure into additive structure, which is far easier to differentiate:

$$\ln(u \cdot v) = \ln u + \ln v \qquad \ln(u/v) = \ln u - \ln v \qquad \ln(u^n) = n \ln u$$

---

## 7. The General Power Rule (Finally, Complete)

Previously you knew $\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}$ for $n \in \mathbb{Z}$, and later for $n \in \mathbb{Q}$. Now, with logarithmic differentiation, we can prove it for **all real $n$**.

**Theorem (General Power Rule).** For $x > 0$ and $n \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[x^n] = nx^{n-1}$$

**Proof via logarithmic differentiation.**

Let $y = x^n$. Then:

$$\ln y = n \ln x$$

Differentiate:

$$\frac{y'}{y} = \frac{n}{x}$$

$$y' = y \cdot \frac{n}{x} = x^n \cdot \frac{n}{x} = n x^{n-1} \qquad \blacksquare$$

## 8. Worked Examples

### Example 1 â€” Chain Rule with $\ln$

Differentiate $f(x) = \ln(x^2 + 1)$.

$$f'(x) = \frac{1}{x^2+1} \cdot 2x = \frac{2x}{x^2+1}$$

_Pattern:_ $\frac{d}{dx}[\ln g(x)] = \frac{g'(x)}{g(x)}$ â€” the logarithmic derivative of $g$.

---

### Example 2 â€” Absolute Value Extension

Differentiate $f(x) = \ln|x|$ on $x \neq 0$.

**Case $x > 0$:** $f(x) = \ln x$, so $f'(x) = \frac{1}{x}$.

**Case $x < 0$:** $f(x) = \ln(-x)$, so $f'(x) = \frac{1}{-x} \cdot (-1) = \frac{1}{x}$.

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx}[\ln|x|] = \frac{1}{x}, \quad x \neq 0}$$

This is why in integration: $\int \frac{1}{x}, dx = \ln|x| + C$.

---

### Example 3 â€” Logarithmic Differentiation with a Product

Differentiate $y = \dfrac{x^{3/4}\sqrt{x^2+1}}{(3x+2)^5}$.

Taking $\ln$:

$$\ln y = \frac{3}{4}\ln x + \frac{1}{2}\ln(x^2+1) - 5\ln(3x+2)$$

Differentiating:

$$\frac{y'}{y} = \frac{3}{4x} + \frac{x}{x^2+1} - \frac{15}{3x+2}$$

Therefore:

$$y' = \frac{x^{3/4}\sqrt{x^2+1}}{(3x+2)^5}\left(\frac{3}{4x} + \frac{x}{x^2+1} - \frac{15}{3x+2}\right)$$

Try differentiating that directly without logarithms â€” you'll appreciate the technique.

---

### Example 4 â€” Variable-Base, Variable-Exponent

Differentiate $y = x^x$ for $x > 0$.

$$\ln y = x \ln x$$

$$\frac{y'}{y} = \ln x + x \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \ln x + 1$$

$$y' = x^x(\ln x + 1)$$

> This cannot be handled by the power rule (exponent is not constant) or the exponential rule (base is not constant). Logarithmic differentiation is the only clean path.

---

### Example 5 â€” $\ln$ inside trigonometry

Differentiate $f(x) = \ln(\sin x)$ on $(0, \pi)$.

$$f'(x) = \frac{\cos x}{\sin x} = \cot x$$

## 9. Visualization and Geometric Intuition

### 9.1 The graph of $\ln x$ and its derivative

```
y = ln(x)                        y' = 1/x

    |    /                            |
    |   /                             |\ 
    |  /                              | \
    | /                               |  \
----+---------> x               ------+-------> x
    |                                 |    \___
    (1, 0) is where ln x = 0          (1, 1) is the peak of 1/x
    slope there is exactly 1          then slope decreases
```

**Key observations:**

- At $x = 1$: $\ln(1) = 0$ and $(\ln x)' = 1/1 = 1$. The tangent line at $(1, 0)$ has slope 1.
- As $x \to \infty$: $\ln x \to \infty$ but _slowly_ (sublinear growth). Its derivative $1/x \to 0$.
- As $x \to 0^+$: $\ln x \to -\infty$ and $1/x \to +\infty$ â€” the function plunges but its instantaneous rate of change becomes very steep.

### 9.2 Concavity

$$\frac{d^2}{dx^2}[\ln x] = \frac{d}{dx}!\left[\frac{1}{x}\right] = -\frac{1}{x^2} < 0 \quad \text{for all } x > 0$$

$\ln x$ is **strictly concave** on its entire domain. This will be deeply significant in machine learning (Â§10).

### 9.3 Tangent Line at $x = 1$

At $x_0 = 1$: $f(1) = 0$, $f'(1) = 1$. The tangent line is $y = x - 1$.

This gives the fundamental inequality:

$$\ln x \leq x - 1 \quad \text{for all } x > 0, \text{ equality iff } x = 1$$

_Proof:_ $g(x) = x - 1 - \ln x$, $g'(x) = 1 - 1/x$, $g'(1) = 0$ (minimum), $g(1) = 0$. Since $g \geq 0$ everywhere, $\ln x \leq x - 1$. $\blacksquare$

This single inequality is used in dozens of proofs in information theory and ML.

## 10. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

Logarithms are not decoration in ML â€” they are structurally fundamental. Here is where $\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}$ appears throughout the field.

---

### 10.1 Cross-Entropy Loss

The workhorse loss function for classification is **cross-entropy**:

$$\mathcal{L} = -\sum_{i=1}^{C} y_i \ln \hat{p}_i$$

where $y_i \in {0,1}$ are true labels and $\hat{p}_i \in (0,1)$ are predicted probabilities.

For binary classification ($C = 2$):

$$\mathcal{L} = -[y \ln \hat{p} + (1-y)\ln(1-\hat{p})]$$

**Why the gradient is clean:**

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{p}} = -\frac{y}{\hat{p}} + \frac{1-y}{1-\hat{p}}$$

This is $\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x] = \frac{1}{x}$ directly. The gradient of cross-entropy with respect to the logit (before softmax) simplifies to $\hat{p} - y$ â€” a single subtraction â€” precisely because $\ln$ and $\exp$ are inverses and their derivatives interact perfectly.

---

### 10.2 Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

Given data ${x_1, \ldots, x_n}$ drawn i.i.d. from a distribution with parameter $\theta$, the likelihood is:

$$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^n p(x_i; \theta)$$

Maximizing $L$ is equivalent to maximizing the **log-likelihood**:

$$\ell(\theta) = \ln L(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \ln p(x_i; \theta)$$

Why take $\ln$? Because:

1. Products become sums (numerically stable, analytically tractable).
2. $\frac{d}{d\theta}[\ln p] = \frac{p'}{p}$ â€” the **score function** â€” has mean zero under the true distribution. This is the Fisher information identity.
3. The $\ln$ is monotone, so argmax is preserved.

Setting $\nabla_\theta \ell = 0$ uses $\frac{d}{dx}[\ln x]$ at every step.

---

### 10.3 KL Divergence and Information Theory

The **Kullback-Leibler divergence** measures how one distribution $Q$ differs from another $P$:

$$D_{\mathrm{KL}}(P | Q) = \sum_x P(x) \ln \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}$$

This appears in:

- Variational autoencoders (VAE): the regularization term in the ELBO.
- RL (policy gradient methods): trust region constraints (TRPO, PPO).
- Bayesian inference: as a measure of information gained.

The concavity of $\ln$ (proven in Â§9.2) directly implies $D_{\mathrm{KL}} \geq 0$ via **Jensen's inequality**:

$$\mathbb{E}[\ln X] \leq \ln \mathbb{E}[X]$$

(Jensen applies because $\ln$ is concave. Equality iff $X$ is a.s. constant.)

---

### 10.4 Softmax and Numerical Stability

The softmax function is:

$$\sigma_i(\mathbf{z}) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}$$

In practice, $e^{z_i}$ overflows for large $z_i$. The stable computation uses:

$$\ln \sigma_i(\mathbf{z}) = z_i - \ln!\left(\sum_j e^{z_j}\right)$$

This **log-softmax** trick, combined with cross-entropy, gives `log_softmax + NLLLoss` in PyTorch. The gradient through log-softmax uses $\frac{d}{dz}[\ln \sigma] = 1 - \sigma$ â€” again flowing from $(\ln x)' = 1/x$.

---

### 10.5 The Log-Sum-Exp Trick

$$\mathrm{LSE}(\mathbf{z}) = \ln!\sum_j e^{z_j} = z^* + \ln!\sum_j e^{z_j - z^_}, \quad z^_ = \max_j z_j$$

This is used everywhere numerical stability matters (HMMs, CRFs, attention mechanisms). Its gradient:

$$\frac{\partial ,\mathrm{LSE}}{\partial z_i} = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}} = \sigma_i(\mathbf{z})$$

The softmax _is_ the gradient of log-sum-exp. This geometric fact underlies the connection between energy-based models and attention.

---

### 10.6 Logarithmic Derivative in Natural Gradient Descent

In standard gradient descent, the update is $\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$.

**Natural gradient descent** (Amari, 1998) uses the **Fisher information matrix** $F(\theta)$ to account for the geometry of the parameter space:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta F(\theta)^{-1} \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$$

The Fisher matrix is:

$$F_{ij}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}!\left[\frac{\partial \ln p(x;\theta)}{\partial \theta_i} \cdot \frac{\partial \ln p(x;\theta)}{\partial \theta_j}\right]$$

The **score** $\frac{\partial \ln p}{\partial \theta}$ is the logarithmic derivative of the model density. Natural gradient methods (including K-FAC, used in large-scale training) are built entirely on this structure.
