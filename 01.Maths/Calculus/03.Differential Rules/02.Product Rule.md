## The Statement

Let $f$ and $g$ be differentiable at $x$. Then their product $h(x) = f(x),g(x)$ is also differentiable at $x$, and

$$\boxed{(fg)'(x) = f'(x),g(x) + f(x),g'(x)}$$

**Leibniz notation** (the form I reach for when doing actual computation):

$$\frac{d}{dx}[u,v] = \frac{du}{dx}\cdot v + u \cdot \frac{dv}{dx}$$

The rule extends to any finite product — but let's earn that generalization properly.

---

## Proof from First Principles

This is the proof I trust. No handwaving.

**Setup.** We want to compute:

$$h'(x) = \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x+\Delta x),g(x+\Delta x) - f(x),g(x)}{\Delta x}$$

**The algebraic trick.** Add and subtract $f(x),g(x+\Delta x)$ in the numerator — this is the key move, and it is completely non-obvious until you've seen it once:

$$= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \frac{f(x+\Delta x),g(x+\Delta x) - f(x),g(x+\Delta x) + f(x),g(x+\Delta x) - f(x),g(x)}{\Delta x}$$

**Factor and split the limit:**

$$= \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \left[\frac{f(x+\Delta x) - f(x)}{\Delta x} \cdot g(x+\Delta x)\right] + \lim_{\Delta x \to 0} \left[f(x) \cdot \frac{g(x+\Delta x) - g(x)}{\Delta x}\right]$$

**Take the limits.** Since $g$ is differentiable at $x$, it is continuous there, so $g(x + \Delta x) \to g(x)$ as $\Delta x \to 0$:

$$= f'(x)\cdot g(x) + f(x)\cdot g'(x) \qquad \blacksquare$$

> **Why continuity of $g$ matters here:** we are taking the limit of a product $A_{\Delta x} \cdot B_{\Delta x}$. We know $A_{\Delta x} \to f'(x)$ (a finite number) and we need $B_{\Delta x} \to g(x)$. Differentiability of $g$ guarantees this via continuity. If $g$ were merely bounded but not continuous, the argument would fail.

## Geometric Intuition

Think of $f(x)$ and $g(x)$ as the two side lengths of a rectangle. The area is $A = f \cdot g$.

When $x$ changes by $\Delta x$:

```
┌────────────────────┬──────────────┐
│                    │              │
│   f · Δg           │  Δf · Δg     │
│                    │  (negligible)│
├────────────────────┼──────────────┤
│                    │              │
│     f · g          │   Δf · g     │
│   (original)       │              │
│                    │              │
└────────────────────┴──────────────┘
```

The change in area is: $$\Delta A = f \cdot \Delta g + \Delta f \cdot g + \Delta f \cdot \Delta g$$

As $\Delta x \to 0$, the term $\Delta f \cdot \Delta g$ is second-order small (it goes to zero faster than $\Delta x$), so:

$$\frac{dA}{dx} = f \cdot \frac{dg}{dx} + \frac{df}{dx} \cdot g$$

This is not a proof — it is a _picture to keep the formula memorable_.

## Computed Examples

### Example 1 — Polynomial × Exponential

$$h(x) = x^2 e^x$$

Let $f = x^2$, $g = e^x$. Then $f' = 2x$, $g' = e^x$.

$$h'(x) = 2x \cdot e^x + x^2 \cdot e^x = e^x(x^2 + 2x) = xe^x(x+2)$$

_Reading the answer:_ $h'(x) = 0$ at $x = 0$ and $x = -2$. These are the critical points — the function has a local minimum near $x = -2$ (check the second derivative).

---

### Example 2 — Trigonometric Product

$$h(x) = x\sin x$$

$$h'(x) = \sin x + x\cos x$$

_Check at $x = 0$:_ $h'(0) = 0 + 0 = 0$ ✓ (the function $x\sin x$ is tangent to the $x$-axis at the origin, which is geometrically obvious).

---

### Example 3 — Logarithm × Polynomial (Common in Statistics)

$$h(x) = x^n \ln x, \quad x > 0$$

$$h'(x) = nx^{n-1}\ln x + x^n \cdot \frac{1}{x} = x^{n-1}(n\ln x + 1)$$

Setting $h'(x) = 0$: $\ln x = -\frac{1}{n}$, so $x = e^{-1/n}$. This exact form appears in **maximum likelihood estimation** — see connection below.

---

### Example 4 — Triple Product (Generalized Rule)

For three differentiable functions:

$$(fgh)' = f'gh + fg'h + fgh'$$

**Proof by induction:** apply the product rule to $(fg)\cdot h$:

$$[(fg)h]' = (fg)'\cdot h + (fg)\cdot h' = (f'g + fg')h + fgh' = f'gh + fg'h + fgh'$$

The pattern generalizes: differentiating a product of $n$ functions gives a sum of $n$ terms, each being the product with exactly one factor differentiated.

## Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

This is where the product rule stops being a calculus exercise and becomes _operationally essential_.

### 1. Backpropagation and the Chain/Product Rule

In a neural network, the loss $\mathcal{L}$ is a composed and multiplied function of millions of parameters. **Backpropagation** is precisely the systematic application of the chain rule — and wherever two quantities are multiplied, the product rule appears.

**Example — attention mechanism (Transformer):**

The attention output is:

$$\text{Attn}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$

The gradient of the loss with respect to $Q$ involves differentiating a product of the softmax output $A(Q)$ and $V$ — the product rule gives:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Q} = \frac{\partial A}{\partial Q} \cdot V^\top \cdot \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \text{output}}$$

---

### 2. Parameter Updates via Gradient Descent

When the learning rate $\eta$ or a regularization coefficient $\lambda$ depends on the weights $\theta$ (as in some adaptive methods), we differentiate expressions like:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}} = \lambda(\theta) \cdot R(\theta)$$

The gradient is:

$$\frac{d\mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}}}{d\theta} = \lambda'(\theta),R(\theta) + \lambda(\theta),R'(\theta)$$

A direct application of the product rule — both terms matter for the correct update direction.

---

### 3. Batch Normalization Gradient

In batch normalization, the normalized output is:

$$\hat{x}_i = \gamma \cdot \frac{x_i - \mu_B}{\sigma_B} + \beta$$

where $\gamma$ is a learnable parameter and $\sigma_B$ depends on the batch. During backprop, differentiating $\gamma \cdot \hat{x}_i$ with respect to $\gamma$ and with respect to $x_i$ both invoke the product rule — $\gamma$ and the normalized term are both functions of the data.

---

### 4. Probability and Log-Likelihood

Maximum likelihood estimation maximizes:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} p(x_i \mid \theta)$$

Directly differentiating a product of $n$ terms using the product rule gives:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \left(\prod_{j \neq i} p(x_j \mid \theta)\right) \frac{\partial p(x_i \mid \theta)}{\partial \theta}$$

This is computationally monstrous. The standard trick: take the **log**, turning the product into a sum. But the product rule is the _reason_ why the log-likelihood is preferred — it linearizes what the product rule makes expensive.

---

### 5. Automatic Differentiation (Autograd)

Libraries like PyTorch and JAX implement **reverse-mode automatic differentiation**. Every primitive operation (including multiplication) is tracked in a computation graph. When two tensors are multiplied:

```python
z = x * y   # forward pass
```

The backward pass stores the rule:

```
∂L/∂x = ∂L/∂z · y
∂L/∂y = ∂L/∂z · x
```

This _is_ the product rule, encoded as a backward function at each node. Understanding the product rule is understanding what autograd is doing under the hood.

## Common Mistakes

|Mistake|Wrong|Correct|
|---|---|---|
|Differentiating product as product of derivatives|$(fg)' = f'g'$|$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$|
|Forgetting both terms|$(x^2 \sin x)' = 2x \sin x$|$(x^2 \sin x)' = 2x\sin x + x^2\cos x$|
|Sign error in $(\sin x \cos x)'$|$= -\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x$... wait, that's right|$= \cos^2 x - \sin^2 x = \cos 2x$ ✓|
|Triple product: missing a term|$(fgh)' = f'gh + fg'h$|Must also include $+,fgh'$|

## The Leibniz Rule (Generalization to Higher Derivatives)

The $n$-th derivative of a product satisfies:

$$(fg)^{(n)} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} f^{(k)}, g^{(n-k)}$$

Compare this to the binomial theorem — the structural analogy is exact, and not a coincidence. This is Leibniz's rule, and it is the product rule for higher-order differentiation.

**Example:** $(xe^x)'' = ?$

$$= \binom{2}{0}(x)''(e^x) + \binom{2}{1}(x)'(e^x)' + \binom{2}{2}(x)(e^x)''$$ $$= 0 + 2 \cdot 1 \cdot e^x + 1 \cdot x \cdot e^x = e^x(x+2)$$

(Matches differentiating $xe^x + e^x = e^x(x+1)$ once more: $e^x(x+1) + e^x = e^x(x+2)$ ✓)
