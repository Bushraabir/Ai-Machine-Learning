## 1. Why Inverse Trig Functions Need Their Own Treatment

The function $\sin(x)$ is not injective on $\mathbb{R}$ — it repeats every $2\pi$. To define an inverse, we **restrict the domain**. This restriction is not a technicality; it determines the sign convention in every derivative formula below. Getting the domain wrong means getting the formula wrong.

|Function|Restricted Domain|Range|
|---|---|---|
|$\sin^{-1} x$ (arcsin)|$[-\pi/2,, \pi/2]$|$[-1,,1]$|
|$\cos^{-1} x$ (arccos)|$[0,, \pi]$|$[-1,,1]$|
|$\tan^{-1} x$ (arctan)|$(-\pi/2,, \pi/2)$|$\mathbb{R}$|
|$\cot^{-1} x$ (arccot)|$(0,, \pi)$|$\mathbb{R}$|
|$\sec^{-1} x$ (arcsec)|$[0,\pi]\setminus{\pi/2}$|$(-\infty,-1]\cup[1,\infty)$|
|$\csc^{-1} x$ (arccsc)|$[-\pi/2,\pi/2]\setminus{0}$|$(-\infty,-1]\cup[1,\infty)$|

The strategy for every derivative is the same:

1. Write $y = \sin^{-1}(x)$, i.e., $\sin(y) = x$.
2. Differentiate both sides implicitly.
3. Solve for $\frac{dy}{dx}$.
4. Express everything back in terms of $x$ using a Pythagorean identity.

## 2. The Master Strategy — Implicit Differentiation

Let me do the arcsin case in full detail so the template is clear.

### Deriving $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin^{-1}(x)$

**Step 1.** Let $y = \sin^{-1}(x)$. By definition, $\sin(y) = x$, with $y \in [-\pi/2, \pi/2]$.

**Step 2.** Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$:

$$\cos(y) \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 1$$

**Step 3.** Solve:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\cos(y)}$$

**Step 4.** We need $\cos(y)$ in terms of $x$. From $\sin^2(y) + \cos^2(y) = 1$:

$$\cos(y) = \pm\sqrt{1 - \sin^2(y)} = \pm\sqrt{1-x^2}$$

**Step 5.** Choose the sign. Since $y \in [-\pi/2, \pi/2]$, we have $\cos(y) \geq 0$. So we take the **positive** root:

$$\cos(y) = \sqrt{1-x^2}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\sin^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}, \quad x \in (-1,1)}$$

> **The sign choice in Step 5 is the entire content of the domain restriction.** If we had used the $\cos^{-1}$ branch instead, we would pick the negative root and get a minus sign. That's exactly how $\cos^{-1}$ works.

## 3. All Six Derivatives — Derived

### 3.1 $\dfrac{d}{dx}\cos^{-1}(x)$

Let $\sin(y) + \cos(y)$... more cleanly: let $y = \cos^{-1}(x)$, so $\cos(y) = x$, $y \in [0,\pi]$.

$$-\sin(y)\cdot\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{\sin(y)}$$

From $\sin^2(y) + \cos^2(y) = 1$: $\sin(y) = \pm\sqrt{1-x^2}$. Since $y \in [0,\pi]$, $\sin(y) \geq 0$, so positive root:

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\cos^{-1}(x) = -\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}, \quad x \in (-1,1)}$$

**Observe:** $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin^{-1}(x) + \dfrac{d}{dx}\cos^{-1}(x) = 0$. This is because $\sin^{-1}(x) + \cos^{-1}(x) = \dfrac{\pi}{2}$ is a constant — differentiating a constant gives zero.

---

### 3.2 $\dfrac{d}{dx}\tan^{-1}(x)$

Let $y = \tan^{-1}(x)$, so $\tan(y) = x$, $y \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2)$.

$$\sec^2(y)\cdot\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sec^2(y)} = \cos^2(y)$$

Use $\sec^2(y) = 1 + \tan^2(y) = 1 + x^2$:

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\tan^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{1+x^2}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}}$$

No square root — no sign issue. This is the cleanest of the six.

---

### 3.3 $\dfrac{d}{dx}\cot^{-1}(x)$

By the same logic (or by noting $\cot^{-1}(x) + \tan^{-1}(x) = \pi/2$):

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\cot^{-1}(x) = -\frac{1}{1+x^2}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}}$$

**Derivation:** $y = \cot^{-1}(x)$, $\cot(y) = x$, $y \in (0,\pi)$.

$$-\csc^2(y)\cdot\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{1}{\csc^2(y)} = -\frac{1}{1+\cot^2(y)} = -\frac{1}{1+x^2}$$

---

### 3.4 $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sec^{-1}(x)$

This one is subtle because of the two-piece domain.

Let $y = \sec^{-1}(x)$, so $\sec(y) = x$, $y \in [0,\pi]\setminus{\pi/2}$.

$$\sec(y)\tan(y)\cdot\frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sec(y)\tan(y)} = \frac{1}{x\tan(y)}$$

From $\tan^2(y) = \sec^2(y) - 1 = x^2 - 1$, so $\tan(y) = \pm\sqrt{x^2-1}$.

For the standard branch: $\sec(y) = x$, and on $[0,\pi/2)$, $\tan(y) \geq 0$; on $(\pi/2,\pi]$, $\tan(y) \leq 0$. Combined with the sign of $x$, the product $x\tan(y)$ is always positive (a careful case analysis confirms this). Therefore:

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\sec^{-1}(x) = \frac{1}{|x|\sqrt{x^2-1}}, \quad |x| > 1}$$

The $|x|$ is forced by the domain split. Stewart uses this form; some texts use $\dfrac{1}{x\sqrt{x^2-1}}$ for $x > 1$ only.

---

### 3.5 $\dfrac{d}{dx}\csc^{-1}(x)$

By symmetry (or $\csc^{-1}(x) + \sec^{-1}(x) = \pi/2$):

$$\boxed{\frac{d}{dx}\csc^{-1}(x) = -\frac{1}{|x|\sqrt{x^2-1}}, \quad |x| > 1}$$

## 4. The Complete Reference Table

$$\begin{array}{|c|c|c|} \hline \textbf{Function} & \textbf{Derivative} & \textbf{Domain} \ \hline \sin^{-1}(x) & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} & (-1,1) \[10pt] \cos^{-1}(x) & -\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} & (-1,1) \[10pt] \tan^{-1}(x) & \dfrac{1}{1+x^2} & \mathbb{R} \[10pt] \cot^{-1}(x) & -\dfrac{1}{1+x^2} & \mathbb{R} \[10pt] \sec^{-1}(x) & \dfrac{1}{|x|\sqrt{x^2-1}} & |x|>1 \[10pt] \csc^{-1}(x) & -\dfrac{1}{|x|\sqrt{x^2-1}} & |x|>1 \ \hline \end{array}$$

**Memory pattern:** The co-functions ($\cos^{-1}$, $\cot^{-1}$, $\csc^{-1}$) are always the **negative** of their counterpart. If you know three, you know all six.

## 5. Chain Rule Compositions — Working Examples

In practice, these never appear alone. The real skill is handling $\sin^{-1}(g(x))$.

### Example 1

$$\frac{d}{dx}\tan^{-1}(x^2)$$

Let $u = x^2$: $$= \frac{1}{1+(x^2)^2}\cdot 2x = \frac{2x}{1+x^4}$$

### Example 2

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin^{-1}(\sqrt{x})$$

Let $u = \sqrt{x} = x^{1/2}$: $$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x}}\cdot\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x(1-x)}}$$

### Example 3 — Product rule + chain rule

$$\frac{d}{dx}\left[x^2\tan^{-1}(x)\right]$$

$$= 2x\tan^{-1}(x) + x^2\cdot\frac{1}{1+x^2} = 2x\tan^{-1}(x) + \frac{x^2}{1+x^2}$$

### Example 4 — Logarithm composed with arctan

$$\frac{d}{dx}\ln(\tan^{-1}(x))$$

$$= \frac{1}{\tan^{-1}(x)}\cdot\frac{1}{1+x^2}$$

### Example 5 — Recognizing a hidden inverse trig

$$\int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} = \sin^{-1}(x) + C$$

This is why these derivatives matter for integration — each formula is an antiderivative.

## 6. Geometric Interpretation

### For $\sin^{-1}(x)$

Think of the right triangle: if $\sin(\theta) = x$, then the opposite side is $x$, hypotenuse is $1$, adjacent side is $\sqrt{1-x^2}$.

$$\cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{\sqrt{1-x^2}}{1}$$

So $\dfrac{d}{dx}\sin^{-1}(x) = \dfrac{1}{\cos(\theta)} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$ — exactly the ratio we derived algebraically.

The triangle encodes the Pythagorean identity geometrically:

```
        1 (hypotenuse)
       /|
      / |
     /  |  x  (opposite = sin θ)
    /   |
   /θ   |
  -------
  √(1-x²) (adjacent)
```

For $\tan^{-1}(x)$: if $\tan(\theta) = x = x/1$, then opposite $= x$, adjacent $= 1$, hypotenuse $= \sqrt{1+x^2}$. So $\cos^2(\theta) = \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$, which is exactly $\dfrac{d}{dx}\tan^{-1}(x)$.

## 7. The Inverse Function Theorem — The Deeper Why

There is a general principle underlying all six formulas. If $f$ is differentiable and injective on an interval with $f'(x) \neq 0$, then $f^{-1}$ is differentiable and:

$$\left(f^{-1}\right)'(y) = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(y))}$$

Applied to $f = \sin$, $f^{-1} = \sin^{-1}$, $f'(y) = \cos(y)$:

$$(f^{-1})'(x) = \frac{1}{\cos(\sin^{-1}(x))} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}$$

This is not a coincidence — it's the **Inverse Function Theorem**, which works in any dimension. The single-variable formula above is its one-dimensional specialization.

> **Reference:** Stewart §3.4 (implicit differentiation), §3.5 (inverse trig derivatives)

## 8. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 8.1 Activation Functions — arctan and softsign

The function $\sigma(x) = \tan^{-1}(x)$ was historically used as an activation function because:

- It is smooth everywhere on $\mathbb{R}$
- It is bounded: $\tan^{-1}(x) \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2)$
- Its derivative $\dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$ is always positive and never zero

Compare to sigmoid $\sigma(x) = \dfrac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$: both saturate at $\pm\infty$ and both have vanishing gradient issues in deep networks, but arctan's derivative is clean and algebraically simple.

The **softsign** activation $f(x) = \dfrac{x}{1+|x|}$ has a derivative structurally similar to $\dfrac{1}{(1+x^2)}$ near zero.

### 8.2 Directional Encodings — Angle Representations

In robotics and reinforcement learning, angles appear constantly (joint positions, orientations). Gradients of loss with respect to angle parameters involve $\tan^{-1}$ and its derivative, since $\theta = \tan^{-1}(y/x)$ converts Cartesian to polar coordinates.

The gradient of $\theta = \text{atan2}(y,x)$ with respect to $x$ and $y$:

$$\frac{\partial \theta}{\partial x} = -\frac{y}{x^2+y^2}, \quad \frac{\partial \theta}{\partial y} = \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}$$

Both formulas are disguised versions of $\dfrac{d}{dx}\tan^{-1}(x) = \dfrac{1}{1+x^2}$.

### 8.3 Normalizing Flows and Change of Variables

In generative modeling, **normalizing flows** require computing the log-determinant of a Jacobian. When the flow involves arcsin or arctan transformations (common in bounded-support flows), the derivatives in §4 are exactly what enters the log-det computation:

$$\log\left|\det J\right| = \sum_i \log\left|\frac{\partial T_i}{\partial x_i}\right|$$

For an element-wise arctan layer: $T_i(x_i) = \tan^{-1}(x_i)$, so $\dfrac{\partial T_i}{\partial x_i} = \dfrac{1}{1+x_i^2}$, and the log-det becomes $-\sum_i \log(1+x_i^2)$.

### 8.4 Differentiable Geometry in Neural Networks

Graph neural networks and point-cloud models (PointNet, 3D vision) must handle angular features. Backpropagating through angle computations — which involve $\sin^{-1}$, $\cos^{-1}$, $\tan^{-1}$ — requires exactly these derivatives. PyTorch and JAX implement `torch.asin`, `torch.acos`, `torch.atan` with correct autodiff rules precisely derived from the formulas above.

```python
import torch

x = torch.tensor(0.5, requires_grad=True)
y = torch.atan(x**2)   # tan⁻¹(x²), chain rule: 2x/(1+x⁴)
y.backward()
print(x.grad)          # 2*0.5 / (1 + 0.5^4) = 1.0 / 1.0625 ≈ 0.9412
```

## 9. Standard Integrals — The Payoff

Every derivative formula yields an integral formula by reading it backwards:

$$\int \frac{du}{\sqrt{1-u^2}} = \sin^{-1}(u) + C$$

$$\int \frac{du}{1+u^2} = \tan^{-1}(u) + C$$

$$\int \frac{du}{u\sqrt{u^2-1}} = \sec^{-1}(u) + C$$

More generally, by substitution:

$$\int \frac{du}{\sqrt{a^2-u^2}} = \sin^{-1}!\left(\frac{u}{a}\right) + C$$

$$\int \frac{du}{a^2+u^2} = \frac{1}{a}\tan^{-1}!\left(\frac{u}{a}\right) + C$$

These are essential in integral calculus and arise when completing the square in the denominator.

**Example:** Evaluate $\displaystyle\int \frac{dx}{x^2+4x+5}$.

Complete the square: $x^2+4x+5 = (x+2)^2+1$. Let $u = x+2$:

$$\int \frac{du}{u^2+1} = \tan^{-1}(u) + C = \tan^{-1}(x+2) + C$$

## 10. Summary — The Structural Picture

The six inverse trig derivatives fall into three pairs. Each pair sums to zero (derivative of a constant $\pi/2$):

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin^{-1}(x) + \frac{d}{dx}\cos^{-1}(x) = 0$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}\tan^{-1}(x) + \frac{d}{dx}\cot^{-1}(x) = 0$$

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sec^{-1}(x) + \frac{d}{dx}\csc^{-1}(x) = 0$$

All six derivations follow the same template:

> **Implicit differentiation → solve for $dy/dx$ → Pythagorean identity → domain-appropriate sign choice**

And at the bottom of every derivation, the same machinery appears: the chain rule, applied to an identity.
