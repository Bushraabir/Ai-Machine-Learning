

> _"The tetrahedron is the atom of geometry — the irreducible simplex from which all of three-dimensional space can be built."_

---

## §1. What Is a Tetrahedron?

A **tetrahedron** is a polyhedron with **4 triangular faces**, **4 vertices**, **6 edges**. It is the 3-dimensional **simplex** $\Delta^3$ — the simplest convex solid that fully encloses a volume in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

**Euler's formula check:** $$V - E + F = 4 - 6 + 4 = 2 \checkmark$$

The tetrahedron is **self-dual**: its dual polyhedron is another tetrahedron (swap vertices $\leftrightarrow$ faces).

## §2. Setting Up Coordinates

### 2.1 General Tetrahedron

Given four vertices $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}, \mathbf{d} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, all other geometric quantities derive from these positions.

### 2.2 The Regular Tetrahedron — Canonical Coordinates

The most natural embedding of a regular tetrahedron (all edges equal) is obtained by placing vertices at alternating corners of a unit cube:

$$\mathbf{v}_1 = (1,1,1), \quad \mathbf{v}_2 = (1,-1,-1), \quad \mathbf{v}_3 = (-1,1,-1), \quad \mathbf{v}_4 = (-1,-1,1)$$

**Verify edge length:** $$|\mathbf{v}_1 - \mathbf{v}_2| = |(0,2,2)| = \sqrt{0+4+4} = 2\sqrt{2}$$

All $\binom{4}{2} = 6$ pairs give the same length $2\sqrt{2}$. ✓

```
         v₁ (1,1,1)
         /|\
        / | \
       /  |  \
    v₂───────v₃
      \   |  /
       \  | /
        \ |/
         v₄ (-1,-1,1)
```

Centroid: $\mathbf{G} = \frac{1}{4}(\mathbf{v}_1 + \mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{v}_3 + \mathbf{v}_4) = \frac{1}{4}(0,0,0) = \mathbf{0}$ ✓ (centred at origin)

## §3. Volume

### 3.1 The Scalar Triple Product Formula

For a tetrahedron with one vertex at $\mathbf{a}$ and the other three at $\mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}, \mathbf{d}$, define edge vectors from $\mathbf{a}$:

$$\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}, \quad \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{c} - \mathbf{a}, \quad \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{d} - \mathbf{a}$$

Then:

$$\boxed{V = \frac{1}{6}\left|\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w})\right|}$$

**Why 1/6?** The parallelepiped spanned by $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}$ has volume $|\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w})|$. A pyramid is $\frac{1}{3}$ of the prism with the same base and height; the tetrahedron is $\frac{1}{2}$ of that pyramid — so overall $\frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{6}$.

### 3.2 The Determinant Form

Equivalently, since $\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}) = \det[\mathbf{u}\ \mathbf{v}\ \mathbf{w}]$:

$$V = \frac{1}{6}\left|\det\begin{pmatrix} u_1 & v_1 & w_1 \ u_2 & v_2 & w_2 \ u_3 & v_3 & w_3 \end{pmatrix}\right|$$

Or equivalently, using all four vertices directly:

$$V = \frac{1}{6}\left|\det\begin{pmatrix} b_1 - a_1 & c_1 - a_1 & d_1 - a_1 \ b_2 - a_2 & c_2 - a_2 & d_2 - a_2 \ b_3 - a_3 & c_3 - a_3 & d_3 - a_3 \end{pmatrix}\right|$$

**Geometric meaning of the determinant:** It is the signed volume of the parallelepiped — positive if $(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})$ form a right-handed system, negative otherwise.

### 3.3 Worked Example

Let $\mathbf{a} = (0,0,0)$, $\mathbf{b} = (1,0,0)$, $\mathbf{c} = (0,1,0)$, $\mathbf{d} = (0,0,1)$ (the **unit tetrahedron**).

$$\mathbf{u} = (1,0,0),\quad \mathbf{v} = (0,1,0),\quad \mathbf{w} = (0,0,1)$$

$$\det\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\0&1&0\0&0&1\end{pmatrix} = 1$$

$$V = \frac{1}{6} \cdot |1| = \frac{1}{6}$$

This is $\frac{1}{6}$ of the unit cube — exactly one sixth, since the cube decomposes into exactly 6 congruent tetrahedra.

```
         (0,0,1)
           |  \
           |    \
           |      \
         (0,0,0)──(0,1,0)
          / \    /
         /   \  /
       (1,0,0)
```

### 3.4 Volume of the Regular Tetrahedron

For edge length $a$:

$$V_{\text{reg}} = \frac{a^3}{6\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{12}a^3$$

_Derivation:_ Set $a = 2\sqrt{2}$ (our cube embedding). Then $V = \frac{1}{6}|\det[\mathbf{v}_2-\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_3-\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_4-\mathbf{v}_1]|$.

$$\mathbf{v}_2 - \mathbf{v}_1 = (0,-2,-2),\quad \mathbf{v}_3 - \mathbf{v}_1 = (-2,0,-2),\quad \mathbf{v}_4 - \mathbf{v}_1 = (-2,-2,0)$$

$$\det = 0(0\cdot0 - (-2)(-2)) - (-2)((-2)(0)-(-2)(-2)) + (-2)((-2)(-2)-(0)(-2))$$ $$= 0 - (-2)(0-4) + (-2)(4-0) = -8 - 8 = -16$$

$$V = \frac{|-16|}{6} = \frac{8}{3}$$

Check: $\frac{\sqrt{2}}{12}(2\sqrt{2})^3 = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{12}\cdot 16\sqrt{2} = \frac{32}{12} = \frac{8}{3}$ ✓

## §4. Surface Area and Face Normals

### 4.1 Area of One Triangular Face

For a face with vertices $\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}$:

$$\text{Area} = \frac{1}{2}|(\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}) \times (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{p})|$$

The cross product $(\mathbf{q}-\mathbf{p}) \times (\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{p})$ is the **outward normal** to that face (up to sign).

### 4.2 Outward Face Normals

For each face, the outward unit normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_i$ points away from the opposite vertex. This is used in surface integrals, flux computations, and mesh rendering.

**Convention:** For face opposite vertex $\mathbf{d}$, the normal should satisfy $\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot (\mathbf{a} - \mathbf{d}) < 0$ (points away from $\mathbf{d}$).

### 4.3 Total Surface Area — Regular Tetrahedron

Each face is an equilateral triangle with side $a$, area $\frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}a^2$. Four faces:

$$S = 4 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{3}}{4}a^2 = \sqrt{3},a^2$$

## §5. Key Metric Properties — Regular Tetrahedron

Let edge length be $a$. All proofs below use the canonical cube embedding scaled so edge $= a$.

### 5.1 Circumradius $R$ (circumscribed sphere)

The circumsphere passes through all 4 vertices. By symmetry its centre is the centroid $\mathbf{G}$.

$$R = |\mathbf{v}_1 - \mathbf{G}| = \frac{a\sqrt{6}}{4}$$

### 5.2 Inradius $r$ (inscribed sphere)

The insphere is tangent to all 4 faces from inside. It satisfies:

$$r = \frac{3V}{S} = \frac{3 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2}}{12}a^3}{\sqrt{3},a^2} = \frac{a}{2\sqrt{6}} = \frac{a\sqrt{6}}{12}$$

**Key ratio:** $\frac{R}{r} = \frac{a\sqrt{6}/4}{a\sqrt{6}/12} = 3$

So the circumradius is always exactly **3 times** the inradius for a regular tetrahedron. This is a characterisation of tetrahedra among the Platonic solids.

### 5.3 Height $h$ (apex to base)

$$h = a\sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}$$

Note: $R + r = h$ for the regular tetrahedron (centroid divides the altitude in ratio $3:1$).

## §6. The Centroid and Barycentric Coordinates

### 6.1 Centroid

$$\mathbf{G} = \frac{\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c} + \mathbf{d}}{4}$$

The centroid divides each **median** (vertex to centroid of opposite face) in ratio $3:1$ from the vertex.

### 6.2 Barycentric Coordinates

Any point $\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ can be written as:

$$\mathbf{p} = \lambda_1 \mathbf{a} + \lambda_2 \mathbf{b} + \lambda_3 \mathbf{c} + \lambda_4 \mathbf{d}, \qquad \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3 + \lambda_4 = 1$$

The $\lambda_i$ are the **barycentric coordinates** of $\mathbf{p}$.

**Geometric meaning of $\lambda_i$:** $\lambda_i = \frac{V_i}{V}$ where $V_i$ is the volume of the sub-tetrahedron formed by $\mathbf{p}$ and the face opposite vertex $i$.

**$\mathbf{p}$ is inside the tetrahedron $\iff$ all $\lambda_i > 0$.**

```
Interior point P:
                  a
                 /|\
                / | \
               /  P  \      P = λ₁a + λ₂b + λ₃c + λ₄d
              /  / \  \     all λᵢ > 0
             / /     \ \
            b──────────c
              \      /
               \    /
                \  /
                 d
```

To **solve for barycentric coordinates** of $\mathbf{p}$: set up the $4\times 4$ linear system

$$\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & b_1 & c_1 & d_1 \ a_2 & b_2 & c_2 & d_2 \ a_3 & b_3 & c_3 & d_3 \ 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1\\lambda_2\\lambda_3\\lambda_4\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}p_1\p_2\p_3\1\end{pmatrix}$$

## §7. Dihedral Angles

The **dihedral angle** along an edge is the angle between the two faces sharing that edge.

For the **regular tetrahedron**, all 6 edges have the same dihedral angle:

$$\theta_{\text{dihedral}} = \arccos!\left(\frac{1}{3}\right) \approx 70.53°$$

**Derivation:** Let the edge be $\mathbf{v}_1\mathbf{v}_2$. The normals to the two adjacent faces are:

- $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{123} \propto (\mathbf{v}_2-\mathbf{v}_1)\times(\mathbf{v}_3-\mathbf{v}_1)$
- $\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{124} \propto (\mathbf{v}_2-\mathbf{v}_1)\times(\mathbf{v}_4-\mathbf{v}_1)$

The dihedral angle satisfies $\cos\theta = \hat{\mathbf{n}}_{123}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}_{124}$. By the canonical coordinates this evaluates to $\frac{1}{3}$, so:

$$\theta = \arccos!\left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right)$$

Note: The supplement $\pi - \arccos(1/3) \approx 109.47°$ is the **tetrahedral bond angle** in chemistry (e.g., methane CH₄).

```
        Face 1
          /\
         /  \
        /    \    ← dihedral angle ≈ 70.53°
  ─────/──────\─────  edge
        \    /
         \  /
          \/
        Face 2
```

## §8. The Solid Angle at Each Vertex

The **solid angle** $\Omega$ subtended at each vertex of a regular tetrahedron (the amount of the unit sphere "seen" from that vertex):

$$\Omega = \arccos!\left(\frac{23}{27}\right) \approx 0.55\text{ sr}$$

The total solid angle over all 4 vertices is $4\Omega \approx 2.2$ sr (less than $4\pi \approx 12.57$ sr — the full sphere — reflecting the open space between faces).

## §9. Decomposition and the Simplex

### 9.1 The Tetrahedron as a Simplex

The tetrahedron is the **3-simplex** $\Delta^3$:

$$\Delta^3 = \left{(\lambda_1,\lambda_2,\lambda_3,\lambda_4) \in \mathbb{R}^4 ;\middle|; \sum_{i=1}^4 \lambda_i = 1,\ \lambda_i \geq 0\right}$$

This is a 3-dimensional object living in a 3-dimensional affine subspace of $\mathbb{R}^4$.

Its **faces** correspond to subsets where some $\lambda_i = 0$:

- 4 triangular faces: one $\lambda_i = 0$
- 6 edges: two $\lambda_i = 0$
- 4 vertices: three $\lambda_i = 0$

### 9.2 Decomposition of the Cube

The unit cube $[0,1]^3$ decomposes into exactly **5 tetrahedra** (non-overlapping, filling the cube):

```
Cube → 5 tetrahedra:
  1 central regular tetrahedron
+ 4 corner tetrahedra (right-angle type)
```

Alternatively, into **6 congruent tetrahedra** (all sharing the main diagonal).

This decomposition is exploited in **finite element methods** and mesh generation.

## §10. The Cayley–Menger Determinant

The volume of a tetrahedron with vertices at mutual distances $d_{ij} = |\mathbf{v}_i - \mathbf{v}_j|$ can be computed **without coordinates** via:

$$288,V^2 = \begin{vmatrix} 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 0 & d_{12}^2 & d_{13}^2 & d_{14}^2 \ 1 & d_{12}^2 & 0 & d_{23}^2 & d_{24}^2 \ 1 & d_{13}^2 & d_{23}^2 & 0 & d_{34}^2 \ 1 & d_{14}^2 & d_{24}^2 & d_{34}^2 & 0 \end{vmatrix}$$

This is the **Cayley–Menger determinant**. It is purely metric — only pairwise distances enter.

**Consequence:** The shape of a tetrahedron (up to rigid motion and reflection) is completely determined by its 6 edge lengths.

**Check for regular tetrahedron** (all $d_{ij} = a$): All off-diagonal entries $= a^2$, giving $288V^2 = 2a^6$, so $V = \frac{a^3}{12\sqrt{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{12}a^3$. ✓

## §11. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

The tetrahedron is not merely a topic in solid geometry — it appears, often unexpectedly, at the foundations of modern ML.

### 11.1 Simplices and Probability Simplices

The **probability simplex** $\Delta^{n-1} = {\mathbf{p} \in \mathbb{R}^n : p_i \geq 0, \sum p_i = 1}$ is the direct generalisation of the tetrahedron.

For $n=4$: the set of all probability distributions over 4 classes is a **3-simplex** — a tetrahedron. Each vertex is a **one-hot** distribution (total certainty about one class); interior points are mixed distributions.

```
        p₁=1                    "Class 1 certain"
         ▲
        /|\
       / | \
      /  |  \
     ▼───┼───▼
   p₂=1  |  p₃=1
         ▼
        p₄=1
   
   Interior = all mixed distributions
   over 4 classes
```

**Softmax** maps logits $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ to the interior of $\Delta^{n-1}$:

$$\text{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}} \in \Delta^{n-1}$$

Every classification head of a neural network outputs a point in a probability simplex.

### 11.2 Barycentric Interpolation in Neural Rendering and Graphics

**NeRF (Neural Radiance Fields)** and rasterization-based rendering pipelines use barycentric coordinates constantly — every fragment shader interpolates vertex attributes (colour, normal, UV) using the $\lambda_i$ of the pixel's position within the triangle/tetrahedron.

The barycentric coordinate formula from §6.2 is the engine behind every real-time 3D renderer and neural rendering pipeline.

### 11.3 Tetrahedral Meshes in Physics-Informed Neural Networks

**FEM (Finite Element Method)** discretises a 3D domain into tetrahedra. The volume formula (§3) and barycentric coordinates (§6) determine the element stiffness matrices. **PINNs** that operate on unstructured 3D grids inherit exactly this tetrahedral structure.

The quality of a tetrahedral mesh — measured by the **aspect ratio** $R/r = 3$ for a regular tetrahedron — directly controls numerical conditioning of the resulting linear systems that neural solvers aim to bypass or approximate.

### 11.4 Simplex Methods in Optimisation

The **Nelder-Mead simplex algorithm** (used in hyperparameter tuning before gradient-based methods dominated) maintains a tetrahedron (in 3D) or $n$-simplex (in $n$D) in parameter space, and iteratively reflects, expands, or contracts it toward a minimum.

The operations — reflection through the face centroid, expansion along the axis — are exactly the affine operations on barycentric coordinates.

### 11.5 Tetrahedral Geometry in Molecular ML

Graph neural networks for molecular property prediction (e.g., **SchNet**, **DimeNet++**, **GemNet**) must represent 3D molecular geometry. Tetrahedral carbon ($sp^3$) is the fundamental structural motif. The dihedral angle $\approx 109.47°$ (§7) is a hard geometric constraint that symmetry-aware GNNs must respect.

**SE(3)-equivariant networks** (e.g., **NequIP**, **SEGNN**) encode both edge vectors and face normals of local tetrahedral environments as features — directly using the cross product structure of §4.

### 11.6 Convex Hull and Support Vector Machines

The **minimum enclosing simplex** problem — find the smallest simplex containing a set of points — is equivalent to finding the **convex hull** and relates to the **maximum margin** problem in SVMs.

A hard-margin SVM in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with 4 support vectors defines a tetrahedral margin geometry. The decision boundary is the hyperplane equidistant from two tetrahedrally arranged point clouds.

### 11.7 Topology in Deep Learning — Simplicial Complexes

**Topological Data Analysis (TDA)** and **simplicial neural networks** (e.g., **SNN**, **SAT**) use simplicial complexes — collections of vertices, edges, triangles, tetrahedra — to model higher-order interactions in data.

The tetrahedron $\Delta^3$ is the basic 3-cell. **Simplicial homology** counts "holes" of different dimensions; in a simplicial complex built from data points, a 3-cycle involving tetrahedra detects 3D voids, e.g., protein pockets or topological features of latent spaces.

## §13. Summary of Key Formulas

$$V = \frac{1}{6}|\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w})| = \frac{1}{6}|\det[\mathbf{u}\ \mathbf{v}\ \mathbf{w}]|$$

$$V_{\text{reg}} = \frac{\sqrt{2}}{12}a^3, \quad S_{\text{reg}} = \sqrt{3},a^2$$

$$R = \frac{a\sqrt{6}}{4}, \quad r = \frac{a\sqrt{6}}{12}, \quad \frac{R}{r} = 3$$

$$\theta_{\text{dihedral}} = \arccos!\left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right) \approx 70.53°, \quad \theta_{\text{bond}} = \pi - \arccos!\left(\tfrac{1}{3}\right) \approx 109.47°$$

$$288,V^2 = \text{Cayley-Menger det}(d_{ij})$$

$$\text{Barycentric: } \mathbf{p} = \sum \lambda_i \mathbf{v}_i,\quad \sum \lambda_i = 1,\quad \lambda_i > 0 \iff \mathbf{p} \text{ interior}$$
