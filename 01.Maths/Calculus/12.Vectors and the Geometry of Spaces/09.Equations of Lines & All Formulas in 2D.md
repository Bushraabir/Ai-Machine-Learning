## 0. Why Lines Matter (A Personal Note)

Before we get formal: lines are _not_ a trivial topic dressed up for introductory students. They are:

- The local approximation of _every_ differentiable function (tangent line = first-order Taylor)
- The building block of linear regression — the workhorse of supervised learning
- The 1D slice of a hyperplane, which is the decision boundary in SVMs and logistic regression
- The directional derivative along a line encodes gradient information in multivariable calculus

So when you're computing the gradient of a loss function in PyTorch, you are — whether you know it or not — working with the same slope formula you'll prove below.

## 1. The Coordinate Plane: Setting the Stage

Let $\mathbb{R}^2$ denote the Euclidean plane with the standard orthonormal basis ${\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2}$. A point is an ordered pair $P = (x, y)$.

```
         y
         |
    Q=(2,3) •
         |    \
         |     \
─────────┼──────•──── x
         |    P=(4,1)
         |
```

_Every geometric object in the plane is ultimately a set of points satisfying some algebraic condition. A line is the simplest such object: a degree-1 algebraic condition._

## 2. Slope: The Central Character

### 2.1 Definition

**Definition.** Let $P_1 = (x_1, y_1)$ and $P_2 = (x_2, y_2)$ be two distinct points with $x_1 \neq x_2$. The **slope** of the line through $P_1$ and $P_2$ is:

$$m = \frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x} = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$$

**Geometric meaning:** $m$ measures the rise per unit run — how much $y$ changes for a unit change in $x$.

|Slope|Behavior|
|---|---|
|$m > 0$|Line rises left to right|
|$m < 0$|Line falls left to right|
|$m = 0$|Horizontal line|
|$m$ undefined|Vertical line ($x_1 = x_2$)|
|$|m|
|$|m|

**Example.** $P_1 = (1, 2)$, $P_2 = (4, 8)$: $$m = \frac{8-2}{4-1} = \frac{6}{3} = 2$$

```
    y
  8 •  ← P₂
    |  ↑ Δy = 6
    |  |
  2 •──────→ x
    1  4
       Δx = 3    so m = 6/3 = 2
```

### 2.2 Slope is Well-Defined (it doesn't depend on which two points you choose)

**Lemma.** If $P_1, P_2, P_3$ are distinct collinear points (no two with the same $x$-coordinate), then $$\frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1} = \frac{y_3 - y_2}{x_3 - x_2}$$

_Proof sketch._ Collinearity means there exist constants $a, b$ with $y_i = ax_i + b$ for each $i$. Then both ratios equal $a$. $\square$

This is the rigorous reason we can speak of _the_ slope of a line, not just the slope between two particular points.

## 3. The Five Forms of a Line

### 3.1 Slope-Intercept Form

$$\boxed{y = mx + b}$$

where $m$ = slope, $b$ = $y$-intercept (the value of $y$ when $x = 0$).

**When to use:** When you know the slope and $y$-intercept directly. Most natural for function perspective.

**Example.** Line with slope $3$ crossing $y$-axis at $-2$: $$y = 3x - 2$$

At $x = 0$: $y = -2$ ✓. At $x = 1$: $y = 1$. At $x = 2$: $y = 4$.

### 3.2 Point-Slope Form

$$\boxed{y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)}$$

where $(x_0, y_0)$ is a known point on the line and $m$ is the slope.

**When to use:** When you know one point and the slope. This is the form that generalizes directly to the tangent line formula in calculus.

_Derivation._ For any point $(x, y)$ on the line, the slope from $(x_0, y_0)$ to $(x, y)$ must equal $m$: $$\frac{y - y_0}{x - x_0} = m \implies y - y_0 = m(x - x_0)$$

**Example.** Line through $(2, 5)$ with slope $-1$: $$y - 5 = -1(x - 2) \implies y = -x + 7$$

### 3.3 Two-Point Form

$$\boxed{y - y_1 = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}(x - x_1)}$$

_Derivation._ Compute $m = \frac{y_2 - y_1}{x_2 - x_1}$, then plug into point-slope form with $(x_1, y_1)$.

**Example.** Line through $(1, 3)$ and $(5, 11)$: $$m = \frac{11-3}{5-1} = 2 \qquad \Rightarrow \qquad y - 3 = 2(x-1) \implies y = 2x + 1$$

### 3.4 Standard (General) Form

$$\boxed{Ax + By + C = 0}$$

where $A, B, C \in \mathbb{R}$ and $(A, B) \neq (0, 0)$.

**Remarks:**

- Every line in $\mathbb{R}^2$ — including vertical lines — has this representation.
- Slope-intercept form fails for vertical lines; standard form does not.
- If $B \neq 0$: slope is $m = -A/B$, $y$-intercept is $-C/B$.
- If $B = 0$: vertical line $x = -C/A$.

**The coefficient vector $(A, B)$ is the normal vector to the line** — this is the key insight for machine learning (see §7).

**Example.** $3x - 2y + 6 = 0$. Here $A=3, B=-2, C=6$.  
Slope: $m = -3/(-2) = 3/2$. $y$-intercept: $-6/(-2) = 3$.  
Equivalent: $y = \frac{3}{2}x + 3$.

### 3.5 Intercept Form

$$\boxed{\frac{x}{a} + \frac{y}{b} = 1}$$

where $a$ is the $x$-intercept and $b$ is the $y$-intercept, both nonzero.

_Derivation._ The line passes through $(a, 0)$ and $(0, b)$. Apply the two-point form and simplify.

**Example.** Line cutting $x$-axis at $4$ and $y$-axis at $3$: $$\frac{x}{4} + \frac{y}{3} = 1 \implies 3x + 4y = 12$$

## 4. Special Lines

### 4.1 Horizontal Lines

$$\boxed{y = k}$$

Slope $= 0$. Parallel to $x$-axis. Standard form: $0 \cdot x + 1 \cdot y - k = 0$.

### 4.2 Vertical Lines

$$\boxed{x = h}$$

Slope is **undefined**. Cannot be written in slope-intercept form. Standard form: $1 \cdot x + 0 \cdot y - h = 0$.

_Note._ In the vector/ML world, a vertical line $x = h$ in 2D is a degenerate hyperplane with normal $(1, 0)$. There's nothing exceptional about it once you adopt the standard form.

## 5. Relationships Between Lines

### 5.1 Parallel Lines

**Theorem.** Two distinct lines $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ are **parallel** if and only if they have equal slopes (or both are vertical).

$$\ell_1 \parallel \ell_2 \iff m_1 = m_2$$

_Proof._ Lines are parallel iff they have no intersection. Suppose $y = m_1 x + b_1$ and $y = m_2 x + b_2$. Setting equal: $(m_1 - m_2)x = b_2 - b_1$. If $m_1 = m_2$, this has no solution (since $b_1 \neq b_2$) or infinitely many (same line). If $m_1 \neq m_2$, unique solution $x = (b_2 - b_1)/(m_1 - m_2)$. $\square$

**Example.** $y = 2x + 1$ and $y = 2x - 5$ are parallel (both slope $2$, different intercepts).

### 5.2 Perpendicular Lines

**Theorem.** Two non-vertical lines $\ell_1, \ell_2$ with slopes $m_1, m_2$ are **perpendicular** if and only if:

$$\boxed{m_1 \cdot m_2 = -1}$$

Equivalently, $m_2 = -1/m_1$ (the negative reciprocal).

_Proof._ Translate so intersection is at origin. Direction vectors of $\ell_1$ and $\ell_2$ are $\mathbf{v}_1 = (1, m_1)$ and $\mathbf{v}_2 = (1, m_2)$. Perpendicularity means $\mathbf{v}_1 \cdot \mathbf{v}_2 = 0$: $$1 \cdot 1 + m_1 m_2 = 0 \implies m_1 m_2 = -1 \qquad \square$$

**Example.** $\ell_1: y = 3x + 1$ has slope $3$. A perpendicular line has slope $-1/3$, e.g., $y = -\frac{1}{3}x + 4$.

```
         y
         |   / ← ℓ₁ (slope 3)
         |  /
─────────┼──────── x
         | \
         |  \ ← ℓ₂ (slope -1/3)
```

## 6. Key Distance & Geometric Formulas

### 6.1 Distance Between Two Points

$$\boxed{d(P_1, P_2) = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2}}$$

_Proof._ Direct application of the Pythagorean theorem on the right triangle with legs $|x_2 - x_1|$ and $|y_2 - y_1|$.

### 6.2 Midpoint Formula

$$\boxed{M = \left(\frac{x_1 + x_2}{2},, \frac{y_1 + y_2}{2}\right)}$$

_Proof._ The midpoint is the average position: $M = \frac{1}{2}(P_1 + P_2)$ in vector notation.

### 6.3 Distance from a Point to a Line

**Theorem.** The perpendicular distance from point $P_0 = (x_0, y_0)$ to the line $Ax + By + C = 0$ is:

$$\boxed{d = \frac{|Ax_0 + By_0 + C|}{\sqrt{A^2 + B^2}}}$$

_Proof._ The line has unit normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{(A,B)}{\sqrt{A^2+B^2}}$. Pick any point $Q = (x_1, y_1)$ on the line (so $Ax_1 + By_1 + C = 0$). The signed distance from $P_0$ to the line is the projection of $\overrightarrow{QP_0}$ onto $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$:

$$d = \left|\overrightarrow{QP_0} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}\right| = \frac{|A(x_0 - x_1) + B(y_0 - y_1)|}{\sqrt{A^2+B^2}} = \frac{|Ax_0 + By_0 + C|}{\sqrt{A^2+B^2}}$$

(using $Ax_1 + By_1 = -C$). $\square$

**Example.** Distance from $(3, 4)$ to the line $3x - 4y + 5 = 0$: $$d = \frac{|3(3) - 4(4) + 5|}{\sqrt{9 + 16}} = \frac{|9 - 16 + 5|}{5} = \frac{|-2|}{5} = \frac{2}{5}$$

_This formula is the margin formula in Support Vector Machines_ (see §7.3).

### 6.4 Point of Intersection of Two Lines

Given $\ell_1: A_1 x + B_1 y + C_1 = 0$ and $\ell_2: A_2 x + B_2 y + C_2 = 0$, solve the $2 \times 2$ linear system:

$$\begin{pmatrix} A_1 & B_1 \ A_2 & B_2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -C_1 \ -C_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Unique solution exists iff the determinant $A_1 B_2 - A_2 B_1 \neq 0$, i.e., the lines are not parallel.

### 6.5 Angle Between Two Lines

The acute angle $\theta$ between lines with slopes $m_1$ and $m_2$:

$$\boxed{\tan\theta = \left|\frac{m_1 - m_2}{1 + m_1 m_2}\right|}$$

_Derivation._ If $\alpha_1, \alpha_2$ are the inclination angles ($m_i = \tan\alpha_i$), then $\theta = \alpha_1 - \alpha_2$ or its supplement. Apply the tangent subtraction formula.

**Special case:** $\theta = 90°$ iff $1 + m_1 m_2 = 0$, recovering the perpendicularity condition.

## 7. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

This section is the reason these formulas are worth internalizing deeply — not just as geometry, but as _computational infrastructure_.

### 7.1 Linear Regression: The Line as a Model

**Setup.** Given data points ${(x_i, y_i)}_{i=1}^n$, fit a line $\hat{y} = mx + b$ minimizing the **mean squared error (MSE)**:

$$\mathcal{L}(m, b) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - mx_i - b)^2$$

Setting $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial m = 0$ and $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial b = 0$ yields the **normal equations**:

$$m = \frac{n\sum x_i y_i - \sum x_i \sum y_i}{n \sum x_i^2 - (\sum x_i)^2}, \qquad b = \bar{y} - m\bar{x}$$

_Every gradient descent step is a move along a line in parameter space._

### 7.2 Gradient Descent Along a Line

In 1D: if the loss is $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ and we step along the direction $-\nabla \mathcal{L}$, the update is:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \cdot \frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta}\bigg|_{\theta_t}$$

This is literally: move from the current point $\theta_t$ along the line with slope $-d\mathcal{L}/d\theta$ by step size $\eta$. The local linear approximation is $\mathcal{L}(\theta) \approx \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) + \mathcal{L}'(\theta_t)(\theta - \theta_t)$ — the **tangent line**.

### 7.3 Support Vector Machines: The Margin is a Point-to-Line Distance

An SVM finds the hyperplane (line in 2D) $\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b = 0$ that separates two classes with **maximum margin**. In 2D this is $w_1 x + w_2 y + b = 0$. The margin is:

$$\text{margin} = \frac{2}{|\mathbf{w}|} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{w_1^2 + w_2^2}}$$

The distance from a support vector $(x_0, y_0)$ to the decision boundary is exactly the point-to-line formula §6.3:

$$d = \frac{|w_1 x_0 + w_2 y_0 + b|}{\sqrt{w_1^2 + w_2^2}} = 1 \cdot \frac{1}{|\mathbf{w}|}$$

Maximizing the margin = minimizing $|\mathbf{w}|$ subject to the classification constraints.

```
            y
            |     ●  ← class +1
            |   ●
────────────┼──────────── x
     ○      |  /← decision boundary: w·x + b = 0
       ○    | /
            |/
```

_The margin is twice the point-to-line distance from the nearest data point._

### 7.4 Neural Networks: Neurons Compute Linear Thresholds

A single neuron with weights $w_1, w_2$ and bias $b$ computes:

$$\text{output} = \sigma(w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + b)$$

where $\sigma$ is a nonlinearity. The **pre-activation** $w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + b = 0$ is a line in the input space — the neuron's **linear decision boundary** before the nonlinearity.

In a neural network, stacking these creates piecewise-linear decision boundaries, which are just compositions of shifted, rotated lines.

### 7.5 Tangent Lines and Backpropagation

For a scalar function $f(x)$, the tangent line at $x_0$ is:

$$T(x) = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x - x_0)$$

This is the **first-order Taylor expansion**. In backpropagation, the chain rule propagates these local linear approximations layer by layer. Every activation function is approximated by its tangent line at the current input during the backward pass.

## 9. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Full line problem

_Find the line through $(1, -3)$ perpendicular to $2x - 5y + 1 = 0$._

**Step 1.** Slope of given line: $A=2, B=-5$, so $m_1 = -A/B = -2/(-5) = 2/5$.

**Step 2.** Slope of perpendicular: $m_2 = -1/m_1 = -5/2$.

**Step 3.** Point-slope form through $(1, -3)$: $$y - (-3) = -\frac{5}{2}(x - 1) \implies y = -\frac{5}{2}x + \frac{5}{2} - 3 = -\frac{5}{2}x - \frac{1}{2}$$

**Step 4.** Standard form: multiply by 2: $5x + 2y + 1 = 0$.

_Check perpendicularity:_ normal vectors $(2, -5)$ and $(5, 2)$: dot product $= 10 - 10 = 0$ ✓

### Example 2: Distance to a line

_Find the distance from the origin to the line $5x + 12y - 26 = 0$._

$$d = \frac{|5(0) + 12(0) - 26|}{\sqrt{25 + 144}} = \frac{26}{\sqrt{169}} = \frac{26}{13} = 2$$

### Example 3: Tangent line (the calculus bridge)

_Find the tangent line to $f(x) = x^2$ at $x = 3$._

$f(3) = 9$, $f'(x) = 2x$, $f'(3) = 6$. Point-slope: $$y - 9 = 6(x - 3) \implies y = 6x - 9$$

_This is exactly the point-slope formula from §3.2 — calculus doesn't change the geometry, only how we find $m$._
