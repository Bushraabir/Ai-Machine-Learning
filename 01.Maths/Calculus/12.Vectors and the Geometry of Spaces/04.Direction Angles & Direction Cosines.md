

> _"The direction of a vector is the soul of its geometry. Angles make that soul legible."_ — personal note, §12.3 philosophy

---

## 0. Motivation — Why Should We Care?

When you write $\mathbf{v} = \langle 3, -1, 2 \rangle$, you know the _components_. But what is its **orientation** in space? How does it tilt relative to each axis?

Direction angles answer this precisely. Direction cosines encode orientation in a form that is **invariant under scaling**, deeply connected to dot products, and — as we will see — absolutely central to how neural networks and SVMs work.

---

## 1. Setup & Notation

Let $\mathbf{a} = \langle a_1, a_2, a_3 \rangle \in \mathbb{R}^3$ be a nonzero vector.

$$|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2}$$

Denote the standard basis vectors $\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}$ (i.e., $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2, \mathbf{e}_3$).

---

## 2. Definition: Direction Angles

> **Definition.** The **direction angles** $\alpha, \beta, \gamma$ of $\mathbf{a}$ are the angles it makes with the positive $x$-, $y$-, $z$-axes respectively:
> 
> $$\alpha = \angle(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{i}), \quad \beta = \angle(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{j}), \quad \gamma = \angle(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{k})$$
> 
> with each angle in $[0, \pi]$.

These are _not_ Euler angles — they are the three separate angles the vector makes with the coordinate axes, one at a time.

### 2.1 Derivation via the Dot Product

Recall the fundamental formula: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta$.

Applying this to $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{i} = \langle 1,0,0 \rangle$:

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{i} = a_1$$

$$|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{i}|\cos\alpha = |\mathbf{a}| \cdot 1 \cdot \cos\alpha$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\cos\alpha = \frac{a_1}{|\mathbf{a}|}, \quad \cos\beta = \frac{a_2}{|\mathbf{a}|}, \quad \cos\gamma = \frac{a_3}{|\mathbf{a}|}}$$

---

## 3. Definition: Direction Cosines

> **Definition.** The three quantities $\cos\alpha, \cos\beta, \cos\gamma$ are called the **direction cosines** of $\mathbf{a}$.

They are simply the components of the **unit vector** in the direction of $\mathbf{a}$:

$$\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \frac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|} = \langle \cos\alpha,, \cos\beta,, \cos\gamma \rangle$$

This is both conceptually clean and computationally useful.

---

## 4. The Fundamental Identity

**Theorem.** For any nonzero $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^3$:

$$\cos^2\alpha + \cos^2\beta + \cos^2\gamma = 1$$

**Proof.** Since $\hat{\mathbf{a}}$ is a unit vector:

$$|\hat{\mathbf{a}}|^2 = \cos^2\alpha + \cos^2\beta + \cos^2\gamma = 1 \qquad \square$$

> ⚠️ **Key insight.** Not every triple $(\cos\alpha, \cos\beta, \cos\gamma)$ is freely choosable — this identity is a _constraint surface_ (a sphere of radius 1 in direction-cosine space). This is the 2-sphere $S^2$.

---

## 5. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Explicit Computation

Let $\mathbf{a} = \langle 1, 2, 3 \rangle$.

**Step 1.** Compute the magnitude: $$|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{1 + 4 + 9} = \sqrt{14}$$

**Step 2.** Direction cosines: $$\cos\alpha = \frac{1}{\sqrt{14}}, \quad \cos\beta = \frac{2}{\sqrt{14}}, \quad \cos\gamma = \frac{3}{\sqrt{14}}$$

**Step 3.** Direction angles (in radians, approx): $$\alpha \approx 1.301, \quad \beta \approx 1.006, \quad \gamma \approx 0.641$$

**Verification:** $$\frac{1}{14} + \frac{4}{14} + \frac{9}{14} = \frac{14}{14} = 1 \checkmark$$

---

### Example 2 — Axis-aligned vector

Let $\mathbf{a} = \langle 0, 0, 5 \rangle$ (pointing straight up).

$$|\mathbf{a}| = 5, \quad \cos\alpha = 0, \quad \cos\beta = 0, \quad \cos\gamma = 1$$

So $\alpha = \beta = \frac{\pi}{2}$, $\gamma = 0$.

This makes perfect sense: the vector is perpendicular to $x$ and $y$, and perfectly aligned with $z$.

---

### Example 3 — Symmetric direction

Let $\mathbf{a} = \langle 1, 1, 1 \rangle$.

$$|\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{3}, \quad \cos\alpha = \cos\beta = \cos\gamma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$$

All direction angles are equal: $\alpha = \beta = \gamma = \arccos!\left(\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right) \approx 54.74°$.

The "body diagonal" of the unit cube — a beautifully symmetric case.

## 7. Extension to $\mathbb{R}^n$

The concept generalizes immediately. For $\mathbf{v} = \langle v_1, \ldots, v_n \rangle \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\cos\theta_i = \frac{v_i}{|\mathbf{v}|}, \quad \sum_{i=1}^{n} \cos^2\theta_i = 1$$

The direction cosines are still just the components of the unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{v}}$, now living on $S^{n-1}$.

This is the **natural habitat** of machine learning.

---

## 8. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

> This section is where the abstraction pays off. Direction cosines are not just geometry — they are the backbone of how modern ML models measure similarity, learn representations, and make decisions.

---

### 8.1 Cosine Similarity

The **cosine similarity** between two vectors $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is:

$$\text{sim}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|} = \hat{\mathbf{u}} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{v}} = \cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors.

This is precisely the dot product of their direction-cosine vectors. It measures **angular proximity**, ignoring magnitude.

**Why this matters in NLP:** Word embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe) represent words as high-dimensional vectors. "king" and "queen" have high cosine similarity. "cat" and "refrigerator" do not. The cosine of the direction angles between their respective unit vectors is what is being compared.

$$\text{king} - \text{man} + \text{woman} \approx \text{queen} \quad \Leftrightarrow \quad \text{direction angles tell the story}$$

---

### 8.2 Weight Vectors and Decision Boundaries in SVMs

A Support Vector Machine constructs a hyperplane

$$\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b = 0$$

The vector $\mathbf{w}$ is the normal to this hyperplane. The **direction cosines of $\mathbf{w}$** determine the _orientation_ of the decision boundary in feature space.

The signed distance from a point $\mathbf{x}_0$ to the hyperplane is:

$$d = \frac{\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_0 + b}{|\mathbf{w}|} = \hat{\mathbf{w}} \cdot \mathbf{x}_0 + \frac{b}{|\mathbf{w}|}$$

Here $\hat{\mathbf{w}} = \langle \cos\alpha_1, \ldots, \cos\alpha_n \rangle$ — the direction cosines of the weight vector — directly project each feature onto the axis of decision.

---

### 8.3 Attention Mechanisms (Transformers)

In the scaled dot-product attention:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

The raw score $QK^\top$ computes $q_i \cdot k_j = |q_i||k_j|\cos\theta_{ij}$.

The direction of the query and key vectors — encoded in their direction cosines — determines which values get attended to. The $\sqrt{d_k}$ normalization factor is precisely to control the magnitude effect, making the **angular alignment** (direction cosines) the dominant factor.

---

### 8.4 Normalized Embeddings and Contrastive Learning

In **contrastive learning** (e.g., SimCLR, CLIP), embeddings are typically $L^2$-normalized before computing loss:

$$\hat{\mathbf{z}}_i = \frac{\mathbf{z}_i}{|\mathbf{z}_i|}$$

After normalization, $\hat{\mathbf{z}}_i$ is exactly the direction-cosine vector of $\mathbf{z}_i$. The loss function then works entirely on the unit sphere $S^{n-1}$ — a space defined by the constraint we proved in §4.

The model learns to align _directions_, not magnitudes. The geometry is entirely in direction angles.

---

### 8.5 Gradient Descent and Direction Cosines

The gradient $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ points in the direction of steepest ascent. The **direction cosines of the gradient** tell you which parameters $\theta_i$ the loss is most sensitive to:

$$\cos\alpha_i = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial \theta_i}{|\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}|}$$

A large $|\cos\alpha_i|$ means $\theta_i$ is almost aligned with the gradient — a small nudge there produces the most change.

## 10. Exercises

1. **(Warm-up)** Find the direction angles of $\mathbf{a} = \langle 2, -1, 2 \rangle$. Verify the identity.
    
2. **(Theoretical)** Prove that there is no vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with all three direction angles equal to $\frac{\pi}{3}$. What is the minimum possible common value?
    
3. **(Converse)** Given that $\cos\alpha = \frac{1}{2}$ and $\cos\beta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$, find all possible values of $\cos\gamma$.
    
4. **(ML connection)** Two word embeddings have $\mathbf{u} = \langle 3, 1, -2, 0 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{v} = \langle -1, 2, 1, 4 \rangle$. Compute the cosine similarity. What does a value near $0$ mean geometrically?
    
5. **(Higher dimensions)** A vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ makes equal angles with all coordinate axes. Show that $\cos\alpha_i = \pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$ for all $i$.
