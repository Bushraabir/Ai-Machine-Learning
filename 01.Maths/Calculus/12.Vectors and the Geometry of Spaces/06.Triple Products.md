

> _"The scalar triple product is the determinant in geometric clothing. The vector triple product is the identity that separates the masters from the beginners."_ — personal note, §12.4–12.5 philosophy

## 0. Motivation

We have the dot product $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}$ (scalar) and the cross product $\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}$ (vector). What happens when we combine **three** vectors?

There are exactly two natural ways:

|Name|Expression|Output|
|---|---|---|
|Scalar triple product|$\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$|scalar|
|Vector triple product|$\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$|vector|

Both appear constantly in physics, geometry, and ML. Let's build them carefully.

---

## 1. The Scalar Triple Product

### 1.1 Definition

> **Definition.** For $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, the **scalar triple product** is
> 
> $$\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) \in \mathbb{R}$$

### 1.2 Determinant Formula

Write $\mathbf{a} = \langle a_1, a_2, a_3 \rangle$, $\mathbf{b} = \langle b_1, b_2, b_3 \rangle$, $\mathbf{c} = \langle c_1, c_2, c_3 \rangle$.

Recall $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c} = \langle b_2 c_3 - b_3 c_2,; b_3 c_1 - b_1 c_3,; b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1 \rangle$.

Then:

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = a_1(b_2 c_3 - b_3 c_2) - a_2(b_1 c_3 - b_3 c_1) + a_3(b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1)$$

This is exactly the expansion of a $3 \times 3$ determinant along the first row:

$$\boxed{\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \begin{vmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{vmatrix}}$$

The rows are $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}$ in that order. This is not a coincidence — it is the definition.

---

### 1.3 Geometric Meaning: Volume of a Parallelepiped

> **Theorem.** $|\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})|$ equals the **volume** of the parallelepiped spanned by $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}$.

**Proof.**

- $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}$ is perpendicular to both $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$, with magnitude $|\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}| = $ area of base parallelogram.
- Let $\theta$ be the angle between $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}$.
- The height of the parallelepiped is $h = |\mathbf{a}||\cos\theta|$.

Therefore:

$$V = \text{base area} \times h = |\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}| \cdot |\mathbf{a}||\cos\theta| = |\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})| \qquad \square$$

```
ASCII diagram of the parallelepiped:

        a·(b×c) > 0: a is on the same side as b×c (right-hand orientation)
        a·(b×c) < 0: a is on the opposite side (left-hand orientation)
        a·(b×c) = 0: a lies in the plane of b and c  →  V = 0

              *---------*
             /|  ^      /|
            / |  | b×c / |  ← height = |a|cosθ
           *---------*   |
           |  |      |   |
       a → |  *------|---*
           | /  c    |  /
           |/        | /  ← base = parallelogram spanned by b, c
           *---------*
                b
```

**Corollary.** $\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = 0$ if and only if $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}$ are **coplanar**.

---

### 1.4 Cyclic Symmetry

Using determinant row-swap properties (each swap flips sign):

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b} \cdot (\mathbf{c} \times \mathbf{a}) = \mathbf{c} \cdot (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b})$$

These are **cyclic permutations** — rotating $(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}) \to (\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c},\mathbf{a}) \to (\mathbf{c},\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b})$ preserves the determinant.

An **anticyclic** permutation flips the sign:

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = -\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{c} \times \mathbf{b})$$

Compact notation: $[\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}]$ or $(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c})$ for the scalar triple product.

---

### 1.5 Dot and Cross Are Interchangeable

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}) \cdot \mathbf{c}$$

**Proof.** Both equal the same determinant — this follows from the cyclic property above. Specifically, the right side is $\mathbf{c} \cdot (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b})$, which is a cyclic permutation of the left side. $\square$

This is why the dot and cross in the scalar triple product are sometimes written interchangeably: the result is the same either way.

---

### 1.6 Worked Example

Let $\mathbf{a} = \langle 1, 4, -7 \rangle$, $\mathbf{b} = \langle 2, -1, 4 \rangle$, $\mathbf{c} = \langle 0, -9, 18 \rangle$.

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \begin{vmatrix} 1 & 4 & -7 \ 2 & -1 & 4 \ 0 & -9 & 18 \end{vmatrix}$$

Expanding along the first row:

$$= 1\begin{vmatrix} -1 & 4 \ -9 & 18 \end{vmatrix} - 4\begin{vmatrix} 2 & 4 \ 0 & 18 \end{vmatrix} + (-7)\begin{vmatrix} 2 & -1 \ 0 & -9 \end{vmatrix}$$

$$= 1(-18 + 36) - 4(36 - 0) - 7(-18 - 0)$$

$$= 18 - 144 + 126 = 0$$

So the three vectors are **coplanar**. (Indeed, one can verify $\mathbf{c} = -\frac{9}{2}\mathbf{b} + \ldots$ — they lie in a common plane through the origin.)

## 2. The Vector Triple Product

### 2.1 Definition

> **Definition.** The **vector triple product** of $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is
> 
> $$\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) \in \mathbb{R}^3$$

Note the parentheses matter: $\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) \neq (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}) \times \mathbf{c}$ in general.

### 2.2 The BAC–CAB Identity

> **Theorem (BAC–CAB rule).**
> 
> $$\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$$

**Mnemonic:** $\mathbf{A} \times (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{C}) - \mathbf{C}(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B})$. Read: "**BAC minus CAB**."

**Proof (direct computation).**

Let $\mathbf{a} = \langle a_1, a_2, a_3 \rangle$, etc. Compute $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}$ first:

$$\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c} = \langle b_2 c_3 - b_3 c_2,; b_3 c_1 - b_1 c_3,; b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1 \rangle$$

Now compute $\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$ — take the first component as representative:

$$[\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})]_1 = a_2(b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1) - a_3(b_3 c_1 - b_1 c_3)$$

$$= a_2 b_1 c_2 - a_2 b_2 c_1 - a_3 b_3 c_1 + a_3 b_1 c_3$$

$$= b_1(a_2 c_2 + a_3 c_3) - c_1(a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3)$$

Add and subtract $b_1 a_1 c_1 - c_1 a_1 b_1 = 0$:

$$= b_1(a_1 c_1 + a_2 c_2 + a_3 c_3) - c_1(a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3)$$

$$= b_1(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c}) - c_1(\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b})$$

This is exactly the first component of $\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})$. The other two components follow identically. $\square$

---

### 2.3 Geometric Meaning

**Claim.** $\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})$ lies in the **plane spanned by $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$**.

**Proof.** By BAC–CAB, the result is $\mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})$, which is a linear combination of $\mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{c}$. $\square$

This makes sense: $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}$ is perpendicular to the $\mathbf{b}$-$\mathbf{c}$ plane; crossing anything with it brings you back into that plane.

```
        b×c  ↑  (perpendicular to the b-c plane)
              |
   ___________↑__________
  /           |          /   ← plane of b and c
 /  b         |   c     /
/__________________________/

  a × (b×c) lives inside this plane.
  It is a linear combination of b and c — the "projection" effect.
```

---

### 2.4 The Other Parenthesization

$$(\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}) \times \mathbf{c} = -\mathbf{c} \times (\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}) = -[\mathbf{a}(\mathbf{c}\cdot\mathbf{b}) - \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{c}\cdot\mathbf{a})]$$

$$= \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{a}(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{c})$$

Compare with $\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})$.

They differ unless $\mathbf{a}(\mathbf{b}\cdot\mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})$, confirming that the **cross product is not associative**.

---

### 2.5 Worked Example

Let $\mathbf{a} = \langle 1,2,3 \rangle$, $\mathbf{b} = \langle 0,1,0 \rangle$, $\mathbf{c} = \langle 1,0,1 \rangle$.

**Via BAC–CAB:**

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{c} = 1 + 0 + 3 = 4, \qquad \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 0 + 2 + 0 = 2$$

$$\mathbf{a} \times (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}) = 4\mathbf{b} - 2\mathbf{c} = 4\langle 0,1,0 \rangle - 2\langle 1,0,1 \rangle = \langle -2, 4, -2 \rangle$$

**Verify directly:**

$$\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 1\end{vmatrix} = \langle 1\cdot1 - 0\cdot0,; 0\cdot1 - 0\cdot1,; 0\cdot0 - 1\cdot1 \rangle = \langle 1, 0, -1 \rangle$$

$$\mathbf{a} \times \langle 1,0,-1 \rangle = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & 2 & 3 \ 1 & 0 & -1\end{vmatrix} = \langle (2)(-1)-(3)(0),; (3)(1)-(1)(-1),; (1)(0)-(2)(1) \rangle$$

$$= \langle -2, 4, -2 \rangle \checkmark$$

## 3. Summary of All Identities

|Identity|Expression|
|---|---|
|Scalar triple product = determinant|$\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = \det[\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}]$|
|Volume of parallelepiped|$V =|
|Cyclic symmetry|$\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b}\cdot(\mathbf{c}\times\mathbf{a}) = \mathbf{c}\cdot(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})$|
|Dot-cross swap|$\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = (\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})\cdot\mathbf{c}$|
|BAC–CAB|$\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = \mathbf{b}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{c}) - \mathbf{c}(\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})$|
|Non-associativity|$\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) \neq (\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})\times\mathbf{c}$ in general|
|Coplanarity test|$\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = 0 \iff \mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}$ coplanar|

## 4. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 4.1 Volume Forms and Change of Variables in Generative Models

The scalar triple product is the $3 \times 3$ determinant. In **normalizing flows** and **variational autoencoders**, transformations $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ preserve probability mass via the change-of-variables formula:

$$p_X(\mathbf{x}) = p_Z(f^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) \left|\det J_f(\mathbf{x})\right|^{-1}$$

The Jacobian determinant $\det J_f$ is the $n$-dimensional generalization of the scalar triple product. For $n = 3$, the Jacobian determinant of a linear map $f(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$ with rows $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}, \mathbf{c}$ is exactly $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})$.

Understanding the triple product = understanding the volume scaling of a linear transformation = understanding how generative models warp probability distributions.

---

### 4.2 The BAC–CAB Rule in Backpropagation Through Cross Products

Certain physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) and 3D geometry models (e.g., PointNet, mesh CNNs) compute cross products as part of the forward pass — to get surface normals, angular momentum, torque.

When backpropagating through $\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c}$, the gradient with respect to $\mathbf{b}$ involves:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial \mathbf{b}}[\mathbf{a} \cdot (\mathbf{b} \times \mathbf{c})] = \mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{c}$$

This uses exactly the dot-cross swap identity from §1.5. The BAC–CAB rule then appears in higher-order derivatives through cross product layers.

---

### 4.3 Coplanarity → Linear Dependence → Rank Deficiency

The condition $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) = 0$ is coplanarity, which is exactly $\det[\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b},\mathbf{c}] = 0$, i.e., **linear dependence**.

In ML:

- A **feature matrix** with linearly dependent columns has a singular (rank-deficient) Gram matrix $X^\top X$. This breaks ordinary least squares and causes the normal equations to have no unique solution.
- **Regularization** (Ridge, Lasso) is the practical fix — but the geometry is precisely the parallelepiped collapsing to zero volume.
- A neural network with redundant neurons has weight vectors that are coplanar → the corresponding rows of the weight matrix are dependent → the layer is not full rank → information is being wasted.

---

### 4.4 Quaternions and 3D Rotation in Deep Learning

Quaternion multiplication for $\mathbf{p} = (p_0, \mathbf{p})$ and $\mathbf{q} = (q_0, \mathbf{q})$:

$$\mathbf{p}\mathbf{q} = (p_0 q_0 - \mathbf{p}\cdot\mathbf{q},; p_0\mathbf{q} + q_0\mathbf{p} + \mathbf{p}\times\mathbf{q})$$

The vector part of the product involves $\mathbf{p} \times \mathbf{q}$. When composing rotations or computing the double cover $SO(3) \cong S^3/{\pm 1}$, the BAC–CAB identity underlies the Rodrigues' rotation formula:

$$R_{\hat{n},\theta}(\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{v}\cos\theta + (\hat{n}\times\mathbf{v})\sin\theta + \hat{n}(\hat{n}\cdot\mathbf{v})(1-\cos\theta)$$

Expanding $\hat{n} \times (\hat{n} \times \mathbf{v})$ via BAC–CAB gives $\hat{n}(\hat{n}\cdot\mathbf{v}) - \mathbf{v}$, which is exactly the third term.

This rotation formula is used in **3D pose estimation networks** (e.g., in robotics, human pose, and NeRF-based models where camera orientations must be parameterized).

---

### 4.5 Geometric Algebra and Attention Over 3D Structures

In **equivariant neural networks** (e.g., SE(3)-Transformers, Equiformer), layers must be equivariant to rotations and reflections. The scalar triple product $\mathbf{a}\cdot(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})$ is a **pseudoscalar** — it is invariant under rotations but flips sign under reflections.

Networks that operate on molecular geometry (AlphaFold2, DiffDock) need to decide whether to use true scalars or pseudoscalars as features, because this determines how the model transforms under orientation reversal. Getting this wrong breaks equivariance.

2. **(Coplanarity)** For which value of $k$ are $\mathbf{a} = \langle 1,2,k \rangle$, $\mathbf{b} = \langle 3,1,0 \rangle$, $\mathbf{c} = \langle 1,-1,2 \rangle$ coplanar?

4. **(Non-associativity)** With $\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{i}$, $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{j}$, $\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{k}$, compute both $\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c})$ and $(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b})\times\mathbf{c}$ explicitly. Confirm they differ.
    
5. **(Theoretical)** Prove that $\mathbf{a}\times(\mathbf{b}\times\mathbf{c}) + \mathbf{b}\times(\mathbf{c}\times\mathbf{a}) + \mathbf{c}\times(\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{0}$. (This is the **Jacobi identity** for the cross product.)
    
6. **(ML)** A $3 \times 3$ weight matrix $W$ in a neural network has rows $\mathbf{w}_1, \mathbf{w}_2, \mathbf{w}_3$. Show that $\det(W) = \mathbf{w}_1 \cdot (\mathbf{w}_2 \times \mathbf{w}_3)$, and explain what $\det(W) = 0$ means for the network's expressive power.
