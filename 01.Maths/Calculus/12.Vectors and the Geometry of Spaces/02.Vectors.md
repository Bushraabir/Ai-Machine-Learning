## 0. Why Vectors, Before Anything Else

In single-variable calculus we got comfortable with one number at a time: a position on a line, a temperature, a rate. But the real world is obstinately multi-dimensional. A weather system has pressure, temperature, humidity, and wind velocity at every point in space. A neural network maps an input image — hundreds of thousands of pixel intensities — to a probability distribution over classes. None of that fits in a single real number.

The **vector** is the minimal honest object for multidimensional data. It carries both _magnitude_ (how much?) and _direction_ (which way?), and it obeys a clean algebra that turns geometric intuition into machinery we can compute with.

---

## 1. The Definition — Two Complementary Views

### 1.1 Geometric View

A vector $\mathbf{v}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a directed line segment — an arrow — characterized entirely by its **length** (magnitude) and **direction**, not by where it starts. Two arrows that are parallel, point the same way, and have the same length are _the same vector_, regardless of their foot.

> **Personal note:** This "position independence" is non-trivial. It is exactly what lets us _add_ a displacement vector to a position point and get a new position — the vector lives in its own space, the point lives in affine space. MIT 18.02 is careful about this distinction when it introduces position vectors.

### 1.2 Algebraic / Component View

Fix an orthonormal basis ${\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_n}$ (the standard basis in $\mathbb{R}^n$). Every vector $\mathbf{v}$ decomposes uniquely:

$$ \mathbf{v} = v_1,\mathbf{e}_1 + v_2,\mathbf{e}_2 + \cdots + v_n,\mathbf{e}_n $$

We write $\mathbf{v} = \langle v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n \rangle$ or as a column matrix. The scalars $v_i$ are the **components** of $\mathbf{v}$ relative to that basis.

> **Warning:** Components depend on the chosen basis. The vector itself does not. This subtlety matters enormously in ML when we talk about changing representation spaces.

## 2. Basic Operations — Definitions and Geometry

### 2.1 Vector Addition

$$ \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \langle u_1 + v_1,; u_2 + v_2,; \ldots,; u_n + v_n \rangle $$

**Geometric interpretation (Parallelogram Law):** Place $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ tail-to-tail; their sum is the diagonal of the parallelogram they span.

```
        u+v
       /   |
      /    |
     /     v
    u -----
```

Equivalently, **Triangle Law:** Place $\mathbf{v}$'s tail at $\mathbf{u}$'s head; the sum goes from $\mathbf{u}$'s tail to $\mathbf{v}$'s head.

### 2.2 Scalar Multiplication

$$ c,\mathbf{v} = \langle c,v_1, c,v_2, \ldots, c,v_n \rangle $$

Stretches (or shrinks, or flips) the vector. $c > 1$ elongates, $0 < c < 1$ shortens, $c < 0$ reverses direction.

### 2.3 The Zero Vector and Negation

$\mathbf{0} = \langle 0, 0, \ldots, 0 \rangle$ is the additive identity. $-\mathbf{v} = (-1)\mathbf{v}$, so $\mathbf{v} + (-\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}$.

## 3. Magnitude (Norm)

$$ |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2} $$

This is just the Pythagorean theorem iterated. In $\mathbb{R}^2$ it is the familiar distance formula; in $\mathbb{R}^3$ Stewart derives it from two applications of Pythagoras (§12.2).

**Unit vector:** A vector of magnitude 1. Given any nonzero $\mathbf{v}$: $$ \hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|} $$

This is the operation of **normalization** — it preserves direction and discards magnitude. Standard basis vectors $\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ are unit vectors.

> **Example.** Let $\mathbf{v} = \langle 3, -4 \rangle$. Then $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{9+16} = 5$, and $\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \langle \tfrac{3}{5}, -\tfrac{4}{5} \rangle$.

## 4. The Dot Product — The Most Important Operation

### 4.1 Definition (Algebraic)

$$ \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i v_i = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots + u_n v_n $$

The result is a **scalar** (a single number), not a vector. Also called the _inner product_ or _scalar product_.

### 4.2 The Geometric Formula (The Key Theorem)

**Theorem.** For any $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$: $$ \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}|,|\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta $$ where $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ is the angle between the vectors.

**Proof sketch** (Stewart's approach via Law of Cosines): Consider the triangle formed by $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{v}$, and $\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}$. By the Law of Cosines: $$ |\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta $$ Expanding $|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|^2$ algebraically using components yields the identity. $\square$

### 4.3 Orthogonality

$$ \mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v} \iff \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0 $$

(Assuming neither is the zero vector.) This follows directly from $\cos(90°) = 0$.

### 4.4 Projection

The **scalar projection** of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ (how much of $\mathbf{u}$ points along $\hat{\mathbf{v}}$): $$ \text{comp}_{\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|} $$

The **vector projection** (the actual component vector along $\mathbf{v}$): $$ \text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2},\mathbf{v} $$

```
    u
   /|
  / |
 /  |  ← perpendicular component
/θ  |
----+--------> v
  proj_v u
```

> **Example.** Find the projection of $\mathbf{u} = \langle 2, 3 \rangle$ onto $\mathbf{v} = \langle 4, 0 \rangle$ (the $x$-axis): $$\text{proj}_\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u} = \frac{8}{16}\langle 4,0\rangle = \langle 2, 0\rangle$$ Geometrically obvious: the shadow of $\langle 2,3 \rangle$ on the $x$-axis is $\langle 2, 0 \rangle$.

## 5. The Cross Product — Exclusive to $\mathbb{R}^3$

### 5.1 Definition

Given $\mathbf{u} = \langle u_1, u_2, u_3 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{v} = \langle v_1, v_2, v_3 \rangle$: $$ \mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{vmatrix} = \langle u_2 v_3 - u_3 v_2,; u_3 v_1 - u_1 v_3,; u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1 \rangle $$

The result is a **vector** (perpendicular to both $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$).

### 5.2 Geometric Interpretation

$$ |\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{u}|,|\mathbf{v}|\sin\theta $$

- **Magnitude** equals the area of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.
- **Direction** is given by the right-hand rule: curl fingers from $\mathbf{u}$ toward $\mathbf{v}$; thumb points in the direction of $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$.

**Key property:** $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = -(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{u})$ — the cross product is **anti-commutative**.

### 5.3 Immediate Corollary

$\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \iff \mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are parallel (or one is zero).

> **Example.** $\mathbf{i} \times \mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k}$, $;\mathbf{j} \times \mathbf{k} = \mathbf{i}$, $;\mathbf{k} \times \mathbf{i} = \mathbf{j}$.

## 6. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Work Done by a Force

A force $\mathbf{F} = \langle 3, 4, 0 \rangle$ Newtons moves a particle along displacement $\mathbf{d} = \langle 2, 1, 5 \rangle$ meters. Work done: $$ W = \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{d} = 6 + 4 + 0 = 10 \text{ Joules} $$

Only the component of force _along_ the direction of motion does work — that's the dot product.

### Example 2 — Normal to a Plane

Find a normal vector to the plane containing $A = (1,0,0)$, $B = (0,1,0)$, $C = (0,0,1)$.

$$\mathbf{u} = \overrightarrow{AB} = \langle -1, 1, 0 \rangle, \quad \mathbf{v} = \overrightarrow{AC} = \langle -1, 0, 1 \rangle$$

$$\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \langle 1\cdot1 - 0\cdot0,; 0\cdot(-1) - (-1)\cdot1,; (-1)\cdot0 - 1\cdot(-1) \rangle = \langle 1, 1, 1 \rangle$$

Plane equation: $x + y + z = 1$. ✓

### Example 3 — Angle Between Vectors

Find the angle between $\mathbf{a} = \langle 1, 2, 2 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{b} = \langle 0, 1, -1 \rangle$:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|} = \frac{0+2-2}{3\cdot\sqrt{2}} = 0$$

So $\theta = 90°$. These vectors are orthogonal.


The dot product _measures how much two vectors agree in direction._

## 8. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

> _This is where the abstract machinery earns its keep._

### 8.1 Data as Vectors

In ML, **every data point is a vector**. An image of size $28 \times 28$ pixels is flattened to $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{784}$. A word in NLP is mapped to an **embedding vector** $\mathbf{e} \in \mathbb{R}^{300}$ or $\mathbb{R}^{768}$ (in BERT-style models). A patient's medical record with 50 features is $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{50}$.

The entire machinery of ML is vector algebra applied at scale.

### 8.2 Linear Layers Are Matrix-Vector Products

A **linear (fully-connected) layer** in a neural network computes: $$ \mathbf{z} = W\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b} $$ where $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the input vector, $W \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is the weight matrix, $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is the bias vector, and $\mathbf{z} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is the output vector. Each row of $W$ is a weight vector whose **dot product** with $\mathbf{x}$ gives one output neuron's pre-activation.

$$ z_i = \mathbf{w}_i \cdot \mathbf{x} + b_i $$

A neuron fires strongly when its weight vector $\mathbf{w}_i$ is **aligned** with the input $\mathbf{x}$ — i.e., when the dot product (angle) is small. This is the dot product's geometric meaning made computational.

### 8.3 Cosine Similarity — Measuring Semantic Closeness

The **cosine similarity** between two vectors: $$ \text{cosim}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|} = \cos\theta $$

This is _exactly_ the geometric formula from §4.2, normalized to $[-1, 1]$.

**In NLP:** word embedding vectors for "king" and "queen" have high cosine similarity. "king" and "bicycle" have near-zero similarity. Recommendation systems use cosine similarity to find "most similar" items in embedding space.

**In deep learning:** attention mechanisms (e.g., Transformers) compute pairwise dot products between query and key vectors to determine which tokens "attend" to which: $$ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V $$

The $QK^\top$ term is a matrix of dot products — measuring alignment of every query vector with every key vector.

### 8.4 Gradient Vectors — The Direction of Steepest Ascent

The **gradient** $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ of the loss function $\mathcal{L}$ with respect to parameters $\theta$ is a vector in parameter space $\mathbb{R}^p$ (where $p$ can be billions). It points in the direction of _steepest increase_ of the loss.

**Gradient descent** updates: $$ \theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta,\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} $$

This is vector subtraction — we move _against_ the gradient vector to reduce loss. The learning rate $\eta$ is scalar multiplication scaling the step size.

The entire training loop of a deep neural network is iterative vector subtraction in a very high-dimensional space. Understanding vectors is understanding why gradient descent works geometrically.

### 8.5 Normalization — Why Unit Vectors Matter in DL

In batch normalization and layer normalization, activations are normalized to have unit norm (or unit variance). This corresponds exactly to the unit vector operation $\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{v}/|\mathbf{v}|$. It stabilizes training by preventing activations from growing unboundedly across layers.

Similarly, **weight normalization** explicitly reparametrizes weights as $\mathbf{w} = g \cdot \hat{\mathbf{v}}$, separating magnitude $g$ from direction $\hat{\mathbf{v}}$.

### 8.6 Cross Product — Normals in 3D Vision and Physics Simulations

The cross product appears in 3D computer graphics (computing surface normals for lighting), physics engines (computing torques $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}$), and robotics (computing angular velocities and Jacobians).

In NeRFs (Neural Radiance Fields) and 3D-aware deep learning models, the geometry of 3D scenes is represented using vector fields and surface normals, all built on cross product intuition.

## 10. Things to Prove Yourself

1. **Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality:** $|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$. (Hint: Consider $|\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}|^2 \geq 0$ as a quadratic in $t$, use the discriminant condition.)
    
2. **Triangle Inequality:** $|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$. (Follows from Cauchy-Schwarz.)
    
3. **Lagrange's Identity:** $|\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 - (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2$. (Verify componentwise in $\mathbb{R}^3$.)
    
4. Show that the dot product distributes over addition: $\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{w}$.
    
5. **Parallelogram Identity:** $|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}|^2 + |\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}|^2 = 2(|\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2)$. What does this say geometrically?

## 11. Looking Forward

From here we build immediately:

- **Lines and planes in $\mathbb{R}^3$** — parametric and normal forms, all using vectors (Stewart §12.5)
- **Vector-valued functions and curves** — $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t), z(t) \rangle$, velocity, acceleration (Stewart §13)
- **Partial derivatives and the gradient** — the gradient $\nabla f$ is a vector field; the directional derivative is a dot product $D_\mathbf{u}f = \nabla f \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$ (MIT 18.02, Week 3)
- **Multiple integrals and flux** — integrating vector fields over surfaces requires normals from cross products (MIT 18.02, Week 8–9)
- **Linear algebra proper** — vector spaces, linear maps, eigenvalues: the full language of ML
