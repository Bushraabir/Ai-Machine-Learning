## 0. The Organizing Principle

Every application in this note traces back to one of three fundamental facts:

> **Fact A.** The level sets of any quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = c$ are quadric surfaces.  
> **Fact B.** Any smooth function looks quadratic near a critical point (Taylor expansion).  
> **Fact C.** Physical laws written in terms of energy, distance, or intensity naturally produce degree-2 equations.

Keep these in mind. When you spot a quadric in the wild, ask: _which fact brought it here?_

## 1. Physics

### 1.1 Moment of Inertia Ellipsoid (Rigid Body Dynamics)

For a rigid body rotating about an axis $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, the moment of inertia is:

$$I(\hat{\mathbf{n}}) = \hat{\mathbf{n}}^T \mathbf{I} \hat{\mathbf{n}}$$

where $\mathbf{I}$ is the **inertia tensor** — a real symmetric $3 \times 3$ matrix:

$$\mathbf{I} = \begin{pmatrix} I_{xx} & -I_{xy} & -I_{xz} \ -I_{xy} & I_{yy} & -I_{yz} \ -I_{xz} & -I_{yz} & I_{zz} \end{pmatrix}$$

The surface ${(\hat{\mathbf{n}}) : I(\hat{\mathbf{n}}) = 1}$ traces out an **ellipsoid** called the **Poinsot ellipsoid** (or inertia ellipsoid). Its principal axes are the **principal axes of inertia** — the directions for which products of inertia vanish and the body rotates "cleanly."

**Why it matters:** Euler's equations of rigid body motion are simplest in the principal axis frame. Satellites, gyroscopes, and spacecraft attitude control all exploit this geometry. The famous "tennis racket theorem" (intermediate axis theorem) — a rigid body tumbling unstably about its intermediate-inertia axis — is a consequence of the saddle-like structure of energy on the angular momentum sphere intersected with the Poinsot ellipsoid.

$$\boxed{\text{The ellipsoid encodes ALL rotational inertia of a body.}}$$

---

### 1.2 Index Ellipsoid in Optics

In an anisotropic crystal, the refractive index depends on polarization direction. The **optical indicatrix** (index ellipsoid) is:

$$\frac{x^2}{n_x^2} + \frac{y^2}{n_y^2} + \frac{z^2}{n_z^2} = 1$$

where $n_x, n_y, n_z$ are principal refractive indices. Given a wave propagating in direction $\hat{\mathbf{k}}$, the allowed polarizations and their speeds are found by intersecting the plane $\hat{\mathbf{k}} \cdot \mathbf{r} = 0$ with this ellipsoid — the two semi-axes of the resulting ellipse give the two refractive indices (birefringence).

**Special cases:**

- $n_x = n_y \neq n_z$: uniaxial crystal (e.g., calcite) — ellipsoid of revolution. One optic axis.
- $n_x = n_y = n_z$: isotropic (sphere). No birefringence.
- All different: biaxial crystal — general ellipsoid. Two optic axes.

This is the foundational geometry of wave plates, polarizers, and electro-optic modulators.

---

### 1.3 Equipotential Surfaces

For a point charge $q$ at the origin, the electric potential is $V = \frac{q}{4\pi\epsilon_0 r}$, and equipotentials are spheres $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = r^2$ — the simplest quadric.

More interesting: for a **quadrupole** or in an external field, potentials become:

$$V = \alpha x^2 + \beta y^2 + \gamma z^2 + \text{linear terms}$$

and equipotential surfaces are general quadrics. **Magnetic traps** (e.g., Penning traps for electrons/ions) use quadrupole fields whose equipotentials are hyperboloids — the geometry determines which particles stay confined.

---

### 1.4 Stress and Strain Quadrics (Continuum Mechanics)

At any point in a deformed solid, the stress state is described by a symmetric tensor $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$. The **stress quadric** $\mathbf{x}^T \boldsymbol{\sigma} \mathbf{x} = \pm 1$ encodes the normal stress on every plane through that point: the normal stress on a plane with unit normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is $\hat{\mathbf{n}}^T \boldsymbol{\sigma} \hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

Principal stresses = eigenvalues of $\boldsymbol{\sigma}$ = semi-axes of the quadric. Engineering design requires these to stay within yield criteria — Mohr's circle in 2D is a slice of this quadric.

## 2. Engineering & Architecture

### 2.1 Paraboloidal Reflectors — Satellite Dishes, Telescopes, Headlights

The **elliptic paraboloid** $z = \frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2}$ (circular for $a=b$) has a miraculous reflective property:

> **Theorem.** All rays parallel to the axis of a circular paraboloid $z = \frac{x^2+y^2}{4f}$ reflect through the single focus $F = (0,0,f)$.

**Proof.** A point on the paraboloid is $P = (x_0, y_0, \frac{x_0^2+y_0^2}{4f})$. The outward normal direction at $P$ is $\nabla(x^2+y^2-4fz) = (2x_0, 2y_0, -4f)$. An incoming ray $\mathbf{d} = (0,0,-1)$ (downward) reflects as $\mathbf{d} - 2(\mathbf{d}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})\hat{\mathbf{n}}$. After normalizing and computing, one verifies the reflected ray passes through $(0,0,f)$. $\blacksquare$

**Applications:**

- Radio telescopes (Arecibo, VLA): collect weak signals into a detector at the focus.
- Satellite dishes: same geometry.
- Automobile headlights: run backward — place a bulb at the focus, get a parallel beam.
- Solar concentrators: focus sunlight to heat a fluid.

The key insight is purely geometric and follows from the quadric's definition as the locus equidistant from a focus and a directrix plane.

---

### 2.2 Cooling Towers and Hyperboloidal Structures

The hyperboloid of one sheet $\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} - \frac{z^2}{c^2} = 1$ is a **ruled surface** — it can be generated by straight lines (two families of them). This means:

- It can be built from **straight steel rods or cables** despite having a curved shape.
- It is structurally efficient: the doubly-ruled structure distributes load well.

Nuclear cooling towers and many architectural landmarks (the Canton Tower in Guangzhou, the McDonnell Planetarium in St. Louis) use this geometry precisely because it combines structural strength, material efficiency, and aesthetic curvature — all from a single algebraic equation.

> _A contractor can build a hyperboloidal tower using only straight pieces of material. Every point on the surface lies on two straight lines entirely contained in the surface. That is a remarkable theorem._

---

### 2.3 Elliptic Reflectors in Acoustics and Medicine

The **ellipsoid** has two foci $F_1, F_2$ such that any ray from $F_1$ reflects to $F_2$. This is a direct consequence of the ellipse reflection property extended to 3D.

**Applications:**

- **Lithotripsy:** Shock waves generated at $F_1$ of an ellipsoidal reflector converge at $F_2$ inside the patient, shattering kidney stones without surgery.
- **Whispering galleries:** An elliptical (or ellipsoidal) room makes a whisper at one focus audible at the other — used in museum exhibits and architecturally in the Capitol building's Statuary Hall.
- **Acoustic horns:** Near-ellipsoidal designs optimize sound directivity.

---

### 2.4 Gear Teeth and Cam Profiles

Elliptic gears (cross-sections are ellipses) transmit variable angular velocity — useful in mechanical presses and looms where you need speed variation within a cycle. The 3D cams whose profiles produce prescribed motion profiles are often quadric sections. Not glamorous, but every time you use a variable-speed mechanism, there's a conic or quadric hiding in the design.

## 3. Computer Graphics and Computer Vision

### 3.1 Ray–Quadric Intersection

In ray tracing (the algorithm behind physically-based rendering in films and games), a ray $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{o} + t\mathbf{d}$ is intersected with a quadric surface defined by $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{x} + c = 0$.

Substituting:

$$(\mathbf{o} + t\mathbf{d})^T A (\mathbf{o} + t\mathbf{d}) + \mathbf{b}^T(\mathbf{o} + t\mathbf{d}) + c = 0$$

Expanding, this is a **quadratic in $t$**:

$$(\mathbf{d}^T A \mathbf{d}) t^2 + (2\mathbf{d}^T A \mathbf{o} + \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{d}) t + (\mathbf{o}^T A \mathbf{o} + \mathbf{b}^T \mathbf{o} + c) = 0$$

i.e., $\alpha t^2 + \beta t + \gamma = 0$. The discriminant $\Delta = \beta^2 - 4\alpha\gamma$ immediately tells you:

- $\Delta < 0$: ray misses the surface.
- $\Delta = 0$: tangent.
- $\Delta > 0$: two intersection points; take the smaller positive $t$.

This is why spheres, ellipsoids, cylinders, and cones are primitive shapes in almost every ray tracer — their intersection test reduces to solving a single quadratic equation, which is analytically exact and computationally cheap.

---

### 3.2 Ellipsoid Fitting and Shape Estimation

Given a point cloud ${p_1, \ldots, p_n} \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ (from a 3D scanner, LiDAR, or depth camera), fitting an ellipsoid means finding $A$, $\mathbf{b}$, $c$ minimizing:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n \left( p_i^T A p_i + \mathbf{b}^T p_i + c \right)^2$$

This is a least-squares problem on the coefficients of a quadric. Applications:

- **Medical imaging:** Fitting an ellipsoid to a segmented organ (heart, kidney) provides compact shape descriptors for disease tracking.
- **Robotics:** Fitting ellipsoids to object point clouds gives bounding volumes for collision detection.
- **Astronomy:** Fitting ellipsoidal models to galaxy images to extract morphological parameters.

---

### 3.3 Quadrics in Projective Space — The Foundation of Geometric Computer Vision

In projective geometry, a **quadric** is defined by $\mathbf{x}^T Q \mathbf{x} = 0$ for homogeneous coordinates $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{P}^3$. The **absolute conic** — a fundamental object in camera calibration — is the intersection of the quadric $x^2+y^2+z^2=0$ (complex, but projectively real) with the plane at infinity.

**Camera calibration** (recovering intrinsic parameters from images of known patterns) exploits the image of the absolute conic. Every time you scan a QR code or do AR on your phone, the camera calibration that makes it work has quadric geometry at its foundation.

## 4. Machine Learning — Deep Dive

### 4.1 The Loss Landscape Is Locally a Quadric

This deserves a careful statement.

**Setup.** Let $L: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}$ be a smooth loss function with a critical point at $\theta^_$ (i.e., $\nabla L(\theta^_) = 0$). Taylor expansion gives:

$$L(\theta) = L(\theta^_) + \frac{1}{2}(\theta - \theta^_)^T H (\theta - \theta^_) + O(|\theta - \theta^_|^3)$$

where $H = \nabla^2 L(\theta^*) \in \mathbb{R}^{p \times p}$ is the Hessian. The level sets of this local approximation are **quadric hypersurfaces** in $\mathbb{R}^p$.

**Classification of critical points:**

|Hessian signature|Quadric type|Critical point type|
|---|---|---|
|All $\lambda_i > 0$|Ellipsoid (bowl)|Local minimum|
|All $\lambda_i < 0$|Inverted ellipsoid|Local maximum|
|Mixed signs|Saddle (hyperbolic)|Saddle point|
|Some $\lambda_i = 0$|Degenerate quadric|Needs higher-order analysis|

**Key result (Dauphin et al., 2014):** For random matrix models of neural network loss surfaces, the fraction of critical points that are local minima (all $\lambda_i > 0$) decays exponentially with the number of negative eigenvalues. In high dimensions, almost all critical points are saddles. The loss value at a saddle is typically higher than at minima — but the very deepest saddles have loss close to the global minimum. This means **finding a saddle with low loss value is almost as good as finding a local minimum.**

---

### 4.2 Condition Number, Ellipsoids, and Gradient Descent Convergence

**The geometry.** For a quadratic loss $L(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\theta^T H \theta$ with $H \succ 0$ (positive definite), the level set $L(\theta) = c$ is the ellipsoid:

$$\theta^T H \theta = 2c$$

whose principal axes are eigenvectors of $H$ with lengths $\sqrt{2c/\lambda_i}$.

**Gradient descent with step size $\eta$:**

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla L(\theta_t) = \theta_t - \eta H \theta_t = (I - \eta H)\theta_t$$

In the eigenbasis of $H$, component $i$ evolves as $\theta_t^{(i)} = (1 - \eta \lambda_i)^t \theta_0^{(i)}$.

For convergence: $|1 - \eta \lambda_i| < 1$ for all $i$, requiring $\eta < 2/\lambda_{\max}$.

The convergence rate of component $i$ is $|1 - \eta \lambda_i|$. The worst-conditioned component dictates overall speed.

**Condition number** $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$:

- The optimal step size is $\eta^* = 2/(\lambda_{\max} + \lambda_{\min})$.
- At this step size, the convergence factor is $\frac{\kappa - 1}{\kappa + 1}$.
- For $\kappa = 1000$ (highly elongated ellipsoid): factor $\approx 0.998$ — need $\sim 3000$ iterations to reduce error by $e^{-6}$.
- For $\kappa = 1$ (sphere): factor $= 0$ — one step convergence.

$$\boxed{\text{Elongated ellipsoid} \Leftrightarrow \text{high condition number} \Leftrightarrow \text{slow gradient descent.}}$$

**What fixes this:**

- **Preconditioning / Newton's method:** Use $H^{-1}\nabla L$ instead of $\nabla L$ — transforms the ellipsoid back into a sphere in the new metric.
- **Batch normalization:** Implicitly reduces condition number of the Fisher information matrix (related to the Hessian).
- **Adam / RMSProp:** Maintain per-parameter step sizes, effectively approximating the diagonal of $H^{-1}$.

### 4.3 Gaussian Distributions and the Mahalanobis Ellipsoid

**The density.** For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$ in $\mathbb{R}^d$:

$$p(x) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{d/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(x-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)\right)$$

The exponent is $-\frac{1}{2} d_M^2(x, \mu)$ where $d_M(x,\mu) = \sqrt{(x-\mu)^T \Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu)}$ is the **Mahalanobis distance**.

Level sets ${x : d_M(x,\mu) = r}$ are **ellipsoids** with:

- Center: $\mu$.
- Axes: eigenvectors of $\Sigma$.
- Axis lengths: $r\sqrt{\lambda_i(\Sigma)}$.

**The 68-95-99.7 rule in $d$ dimensions.** The fraction of probability within the ellipsoid $d_M \leq r$ is:

$$P(\chi^2_d \leq r^2)$$

This depends on dimension $d$ — in high dimensions, most probability mass concentrates in a thin shell near $r = \sqrt{d}$. The ellipsoid geometry is essential for understanding this **concentration of measure** phenomenon.

**Applications:**

- **Anomaly detection:** Flag $x$ as anomalous if $d_M(x, \mu) > \tau$ for some threshold $\tau$. Geometrically: is $x$ outside the "normal" ellipsoid?
- **Linear Discriminant Analysis (LDA):** Classify by nearest class center in Mahalanobis distance — the decision boundary is a quadric surface (hyperplane when two classes have equal $\Sigma$, quadric in general).
- **Gaussian Mixture Models (GMM):** The EM algorithm fits a mixture of Gaussians; each component's "territory" is carved by quadric decision boundaries.
- **Kalman Filter:** State uncertainty is an ellipsoid in state space; Kalman updates are ellipsoid-to-ellipsoid operations.

### 4.4 PCA — Eigendecomposition of the Covariance Ellipsoid

**Setup.** Given centered data $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times d}$, the sample covariance is:

$$\hat{\Sigma} = \frac{1}{n} X^T X$$

The level set ${x : x^T \hat{\Sigma}^{-1} x = 1}$ is an ellipsoid — the **data ellipsoid** — oriented along the principal directions of variation.

**PCA finds the axes of this ellipsoid.** The $k$-th principal component is the $k$-th largest eigenvector of $\hat{\Sigma}$ — the direction of the $k$-th longest axis. Projecting onto the top $k$ axes retains maximum variance.

**Geometric interpretation:** PCA whitens the data (transforms the covariance ellipsoid into a sphere) via $z = \Lambda^{-1/2} P^T x$ where $\hat{\Sigma} = P \Lambda P^T$. After whitening, Euclidean distance equals Mahalanobis distance.

**Connection to SVD:** $\hat{\Sigma} = \frac{1}{n} X^T X$. If $X = U S V^T$ (SVD), then $\hat{\Sigma} = V \frac{S^2}{n} V^T$ — the eigenvectors of $\hat{\Sigma}$ are the right singular vectors of $X$.

---

### 4.5 Kernel Methods — Quadric Decision Boundaries

A **support vector machine** with a degree-2 polynomial kernel $K(x, x') = (x \cdot x' + 1)^2$ implicitly maps $\mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^{d(d+3)/2 + 1}$ via:

$$\phi(x) = (x_1^2, \ldots, x_d^2, \sqrt{2}x_1 x_2, \ldots, \sqrt{2}x_{d-1}x_d, \sqrt{2}x_1, \ldots, \sqrt{2}x_d, 1)$$

A linear hyperplane in this feature space corresponds to a **quadric surface** in the original $\mathbb{R}^d$.

**Concrete example.** In $\mathbb{R}^2$, the decision function:

$$f(x) = w_1 x_1^2 + w_2 x_2^2 + w_3 x_1 x_2 + w_4 x_1 + w_5 x_2 + w_6 = 0$$

is a **conic section** (ellipse, hyperbola, or parabola). The XOR problem (not linearly separable) is separable by an ellipse — which is exactly a quadric decision boundary.

---

### 4.6 Quadratic Approximations in Bayesian Inference — The Laplace Approximation

In Bayesian ML, the posterior $p(\theta | \mathcal{D}) \propto p(\mathcal{D}|\theta)p(\theta)$ is often intractable. The **Laplace approximation** approximates it as a Gaussian:

1. Find the MAP estimate $\theta^* = \arg\max_\theta \log p(\theta|\mathcal{D})$.
2. Compute $H = -\nabla^2 \log p(\theta|\mathcal{D})\big|_{\theta^*}$ (the Hessian of the negative log-posterior).
3. Approximate: $p(\theta|\mathcal{D}) \approx \mathcal{N}(\theta^*,\ H^{-1})$.

The uncertainty region ${\theta : (\theta-\theta^_)^T H (\theta-\theta^_) \leq c}$ is an **ellipsoid** in parameter space — the local quadric geometry of the log-posterior encoded into a Gaussian approximation. This is the foundation of methods like **KFAC**, **natural gradient**, and Bayesian neural networks with Laplace approximations.

### 4.7 Second-Order Methods and the Newton Step

**Newton's method** for minimizing $L(\theta)$:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - H_t^{-1} \nabla L(\theta_t)$$

**Geometric interpretation.** At each step, Newton locally approximates $L$ by the quadratic $\hat{L}(\theta) = L(\theta_t) + \nabla L^T(\theta-\theta_t) + \frac{1}{2}(\theta-\theta_t)^T H_t (\theta-\theta_t)$ and jumps to its minimum exactly. The level sets of $\hat{L}$ are ellipsoids; Newton corrects for the elongation by applying $H_t^{-1}$, effectively working in the metric defined by the quadric.

**Why not always use Newton?** Computing and inverting $H \in \mathbb{R}^{p\times p}$ costs $O(p^3)$ — infeasible for $p = 10^8$ (modern neural nets). Quasi-Newton methods (L-BFGS) maintain a low-rank approximation of $H^{-1}$ built from gradient differences.

## 5. Statistics

### 5.1 Confidence Ellipsoids

For a parameter estimate $\hat{\theta}$ with asymptotic covariance $\Sigma$, the $1-\alpha$ confidence region is:

$$\left{\theta : (\theta - \hat{\theta})^T \Sigma^{-1} (\theta - \hat{\theta}) \leq \chi^2_{p,1-\alpha}\right}$$

This is an **ellipsoid** in parameter space. The chi-squared quantile determines its scale; $\Sigma$ determines its shape and orientation. Visualizing this ellipsoid (or its 2D cross-sections) is standard practice in econometrics, biostatistics, and experimental physics.

---

### 5.2 Chi-Squared Distribution Geometry

If $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_d)$, then $|X|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^d X_i^2 \sim \chi^2_d$.

The "sphere" $|X|^2 = r^2$ is where most probability concentrates. In general (correlated) Gaussians, this sphere becomes an ellipsoid. The chi-squared distribution is literally describing probability in the quadric level sets of the Gaussian density. Every $p$-value and hypothesis test at its core is asking: "is this observation inside or outside the ellipsoid?"

## 6. Differential Geometry and General Relativity (A Glimpse)

### 6.1 Curvature and the Dupin Indicatrix

At a point $P$ on a smooth surface, the **Dupin indicatrix** is the conic section:

$$\kappa_1 u^2 + \kappa_2 v^2 = \pm 1$$

where $\kappa_1, \kappa_2$ are the principal curvatures (eigenvalues of the second fundamental form). Its type classifies $P$:

|Indicatrix|Gaussian curvature $K = \kappa_1\kappa_2$|Point type|
|---|---|---|
|Ellipse|$K > 0$|**Elliptic** — bowl-shaped (like a sphere)|
|Hyperbola|$K < 0$|**Hyperbolic** — saddle-shaped|
|Parallel lines|$K = 0$|**Parabolic** — flat in one direction|
|Degenerate|$\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = 0$|**Planar** (umbilic or flat)|

This connects quadric surfaces to the intrinsic geometry of general surfaces: every surface is locally classified by which quadric its curvature resembles.

### 6.2 Metric Tensors in General Relativity

The metric tensor $g_{\mu\nu}$ defines the spacetime interval:

$$ds^2 = g_{\mu\nu} dx^\mu dx^\nu$$

This is a quadratic form in $dx^\mu$. The light cone $ds^2 = 0$ is a quadric (a cone in 4D spacetime). Near a massive body, the "ellipsoid of simultaneity" and the null cone geometry are quadrics in spacetime — and their deformation by gravity is literally what general relativity describes.

## 7. Geodesy and Earth Modeling

The Earth is not a sphere. It is more accurately modeled as an **oblate spheroid** — an ellipsoid of revolution:

$$\frac{x^2 + y^2}{a^2} + \frac{z^2}{b^2} = 1, \quad a > b$$

with $a \approx 6{,}378{,}137$ m (equatorial radius) and $b \approx 6{,}356{,}752$ m (polar radius). The flattening $f = (a-b)/a \approx 1/298.257$ is small but critical for GPS.

**GPS and WGS84:** The World Geodetic System 1984 defines coordinates relative to this reference ellipsoid. Every GPS receiver on the planet computes your latitude, longitude, and altitude by finding your position relative to this specific quadric surface embedded in 3D space. Your phone's location is literally solving an ellipsoid problem billions of times per year.

**Gravity and the geoid:** The actual equipotential surface of Earth's gravity field (the **geoid**) deviates from the reference ellipsoid by up to $\pm 100$ m. Geodesy is the science of measuring and modeling this deviation.

## 9. A Unifying Perspective

Here is the thread connecting everything:

**A quadric surface is the geometry of a symmetric bilinear form.**

Every domain has a natural symmetric bilinear form:

- Physics: kinetic energy $T = \frac{1}{2}\omega^T \mathbf{I} \omega$, potential energy, stress.
- ML: loss curvature $\theta^T H \theta$, data covariance $x^T \Sigma^{-1} x$.
- Statistics: chi-squared distances.
- Geometry: the second fundamental form.
- Relativity: the metric tensor $g_{\mu\nu}$.

In every case, the level sets of that form are quadric surfaces, and their geometry — the eigenvalues, condition number, positive/negative definiteness — tells you the qualitative behavior of the system.

> _This is why linear algebra is the language of modern science. Symmetric matrices produce quadric surfaces. Quadric surfaces encode geometry. Geometry governs behavior._
