## §0. Why Bother? A Note Before We Begin

Before I write a single formula, let me say this plainly: the 3D coordinate system is not merely a bookkeeping device. It is the **lingua franca** of modern science and engineering. Every point in a neural network's weight space, every pixel in a rendered scene, every atom in a protein fold — all of them live in some version of what we're about to build. If you want to understand deep learning at a non-superficial level, you must be fluent here. Not acquainted. _Fluent._

## §1. The Construction: Building $\mathbb{R}^3$ from Scratch

### 1.1 The Three Mutually Perpendicular Axes

Fix a point $O$ in space, called the **origin**. Through $O$, draw three lines, each perpendicular to the other two. Label them the $x$-axis, $y$-axis, and $z$-axis.

**The right-hand rule** (non-negotiable convention): Point your right hand's fingers along the positive $x$-axis and curl them toward the positive $y$-axis. Your thumb points in the direction of the positive $z$-axis.

```
        z
        |
        |
        |_______ y
       /
      /
     x
```

This defines a **right-handed coordinate system**. Left-handed systems exist (and are used in some graphics APIs like DirectX), but in mathematics and physics, right-handed is the default. Confusing them is a classical source of sign errors.

### 1.2 The Three Coordinate Planes

The three axes determine three planes:

|Plane|Contains|Equation|
|---|---|---|
|$xy$-plane|$x$-axis and $y$-axis|$z = 0$|
|$yz$-plane|$y$-axis and $z$-axis|$x = 0$|
|$xz$-plane|$x$-axis and $z$-axis|$y = 0$|

These three planes divide all of $\mathbb{R}^3$ into **eight octants**. The first octant is where $x > 0$, $y > 0$, and $z > 0$. Unlike in 2D (where the four quadrants are labeled I–IV), the octants in 3D have no standard numbering — only the first octant is universally named.

### 1.3 Coordinates as an Isomorphism

Every point $P$ in 3D space corresponds to a unique ordered triple $(a, b, c) \in \mathbb{R}^3$, where:

- $a$ = signed distance from the $yz$-plane (along the $x$-axis)
- $b$ = signed distance from the $xz$-plane (along the $y$-axis)
- $c$ = signed distance from the $xy$-plane (along the $z$-axis)

This correspondence $P \leftrightarrow (a, b, c)$ is a **bijection**. It is the content of the Cartesian project: geometry $\leftrightarrow$ algebra.

> **Example 1.1.** The point $P = (2, -3, 5)$: start at the origin, move 2 units in the positive $x$-direction, 3 units in the negative $y$-direction, then 5 units in the positive $z$-direction. You have arrived at $P$.

> **Example 1.2.** Where is $Q = (0, 4, 0)$? On the $y$-axis, 4 units above the origin (in the $y$-direction). All points of the form $(0, b, 0)$ lie on the $y$-axis.

## §2. Distance in $\mathbb{R}^3$

### 2.1 The Distance Formula

**Theorem (Distance Formula).** The distance between $P_1 = (x_1, y_1, z_1)$ and $P_2 = (x_2, y_2, z_2)$ is:

$$|P_1 P_2| = \sqrt{(x_2 - x_1)^2 + (y_2 - y_1)^2 + (z_2 - z_1)^2}$$

**Proof.** Apply the Pythagorean theorem twice. The horizontal displacement (in the $xy$-plane) from $P_1$ to the point $(x_2, y_2, z_1)$ directly beneath $P_2$ has length $d_{xy} = \sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2}$. Then the distance from $P_1$ to $P_2$ is the hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs $d_{xy}$ and $|z_2 - z_1|$:

$$|P_1P_2|^2 = d_{xy}^2 + (z_2-z_1)^2 = (x_2-x_1)^2 + (y_2-y_1)^2 + (z_2-z_1)^2$$

Taking square roots gives the result. $\blacksquare$

> **Example 2.1.** Distance from $A = (1, 2, -1)$ to $B = (3, 0, 4)$: $$|AB| = \sqrt{(3-1)^2 + (0-2)^2 + (4-(-1))^2} = \sqrt{4 + 4 + 25} = \sqrt{33}$$

### 2.2 The Midpoint Formula

The midpoint $M$ of the segment $P_1P_2$ is:

$$M = \left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},, \frac{y_1+y_2}{2},, \frac{z_1+z_2}{2}\right)$$

This is immediate: midpoint coordinates are arithmetic means. The proof is just the 1D midpoint formula applied coordinate-by-coordinate.

## §3. The Equation of a Sphere

### 3.1 Derivation

A **sphere** with center $C = (h, k, l)$ and radius $r > 0$ is the set of all points equidistant from $C$:

$${(x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : \sqrt{(x-h)^2+(y-k)^2+(z-l)^2} = r}$$

Squaring both sides:

$$\boxed{(x-h)^2 + (y-k)^2 + (z-l)^2 = r^2}$$

This is the **standard form** of the sphere equation. Think of it as the 3D analogue of the circle equation in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

### 3.2 Completing the Square (The Inverse Problem)

Given a general second-degree equation, recognize when it describes a sphere by completing the square in all three variables.

> **Example 3.1.** Identify the surface: $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 + 4x - 6y + 2z + 6 = 0$
> 
> Group and complete the square: $$(x^2 + 4x + 4) + (y^2 - 6y + 9) + (z^2 + 2z + 1) = -6 + 4 + 9 + 1 = 8$$
> 
> $$(x+2)^2 + (y-3)^2 + (z+1)^2 = 8$$
> 
> This is a sphere with center $(-2, 3, -1)$ and radius $\sqrt{8} = 2\sqrt{2}$.

**What can go wrong?** If after completing the square the right-hand side is 0, you get a single point. If it's negative, there are no real solutions — the "sphere" is imaginary.

## §4. Vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$

### 4.1 What Is a Vector?

A **vector** $\mathbf{v}$ is an object with both magnitude and direction. In coordinate form, we represent it as:

$$\mathbf{v} = \langle v_1, v_2, v_3 \rangle$$

Distinguish carefully: the **position vector** of a point $P = (a, b, c)$ is $\overrightarrow{OP} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$. The point is a location; the vector is a displacement. This distinction matters.

### 4.2 Vector Arithmetic

If $\mathbf{u} = \langle u_1, u_2, u_3 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{v} = \langle v_1, v_2, v_3 \rangle$, and $c \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \langle u_1+v_1,, u_2+v_2,, u_3+v_3 \rangle$$

$$c\mathbf{v} = \langle cv_1,, cv_2,, cv_3 \rangle$$

$$|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2}$$

The **unit vector** in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$ (assuming $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$):

$$\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}$$

### 4.3 Standard Basis Vectors

The three vectors:

$$\mathbf{i} = \langle 1, 0, 0 \rangle, \quad \mathbf{j} = \langle 0, 1, 0 \rangle, \quad \mathbf{k} = \langle 0, 0, 1 \rangle$$

form the **standard basis** for $\mathbb{R}^3$. Every vector decomposes uniquely:

$$\mathbf{v} = v_1\mathbf{i} + v_2\mathbf{j} + v_3\mathbf{k}$$

This is not just notation — it's the statement that ${\mathbf{i}, \mathbf{j}, \mathbf{k}}$ spans $\mathbb{R}^3$ and is linearly independent. (You'll make this precise in linear algebra, if you haven't already.)

## §5. The Dot Product

### 5.1 Definition and Geometric Interpretation

**Definition.** The **dot product** (inner product) of $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ is:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3$$

**Theorem.** $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$, where $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ is the angle between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.

**Proof.** Apply the law of cosines to the triangle formed by $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{v}$, and $\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}$:

$$|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$$

Expanding the left side using the coordinate definition:

$$|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}|^2 = (u_1-v_1)^2 + (u_2-v_2)^2 + (u_3-v_3)^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 - 2(u_1v_1+u_2v_2+u_3v_3) + |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

Comparing: $u_1v_1 + u_2v_2 + u_3v_3 = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary (Orthogonality Test).** $\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v} \iff \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$.

### 5.2 Projection

The **scalar projection** of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$:

$$\text{comp}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|}$$

The **vector projection** of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}}\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|^2}\mathbf{a}$$

> **Example 5.1.** Find the angle between $\mathbf{u} = \langle 1, 2, 2 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{v} = \langle 0, 1, -1 \rangle$:
> 
> $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0 + 2 - 2 = 0$
> 
> Since $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$, these vectors are perpendicular: $\theta = \pi/2$.

## §6. The Cross Product

### 6.1 Definition

The **cross product** $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$ is defined only in $\mathbb{R}^3$ (and $\mathbb{R}^7$, but let's not go there). It is computed via the formal determinant:

$$\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{vmatrix}$$

$$= (u_2v_3 - u_3v_2)\mathbf{i} - (u_1v_3 - u_3v_1)\mathbf{j} + (u_1v_2 - u_2v_1)\mathbf{k}$$

### 6.2 Geometric Meaning

**Theorem.** $|\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\sin\theta$, and $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$ is orthogonal to both $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$, with direction given by the right-hand rule.

**Immediate consequences:**

- $|\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}|$ equals the **area of the parallelogram** spanned by $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.
- $\mathbf{u} \parallel \mathbf{v} \iff \mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$.

**Anti-commutativity:** $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = -(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{u})$. The cross product is **not** commutative. Order matters — reversing it flips the sign (i.e., flips the direction of the resulting vector by 180°).

> **Example 6.1.** $\mathbf{u} = \langle 1, 0, 0 \rangle$, $\mathbf{v} = \langle 0, 1, 0 \rangle$:
> 
> $$\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0\end{vmatrix} = (0-0)\mathbf{i} - (0-0)\mathbf{j} + (1-0)\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{k}$$
> 
> As expected: $\mathbf{i} \times \mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k}$. The right-hand rule confirms the direction.

## §7. Lines and Planes in $\mathbb{R}^3$

### 7.1 Lines

A line through point $P_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0)$ with direction vector $\mathbf{d} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$:

**Vector equation:** $\mathbf{r}(t) = \mathbf{r}_0 + t\mathbf{d} = \langle x_0 + at,, y_0 + bt,, z_0 + ct \rangle$, $t \in \mathbb{R}$

**Parametric equations:** $$x = x_0 + at, \quad y = y_0 + bt, \quad z = z_0 + ct$$

**Symmetric equations** (if $a, b, c \neq 0$): $$\frac{x - x_0}{a} = \frac{y - y_0}{b} = \frac{z - z_0}{c}$$

If, say, $c = 0$, then $z = z_0$ is a separate equation and the others form the symmetric form.

### 7.2 Planes

A plane through $P_0 = (x_0, y_0, z_0)$ with normal vector $\mathbf{n} = \langle a, b, c \rangle$:

$$\mathbf{n} \cdot (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) = 0$$

$$a(x - x_0) + b(y - y_0) + c(z - z_0) = 0$$

Or in the general (linear) form:

$$ax + by + cz + d = 0$$

where $d = -(ax_0 + by_0 + cz_0)$.

**Key insight:** The normal vector $\langle a, b, c \rangle$ is readable directly from the coefficients. This is one of the most useful facts in multivariable calculus — when you see a plane's equation, you immediately know its normal direction.

> **Example 7.1.** Find the equation of the plane through $P(1, 2, 3)$, $Q(2, 3, 1)$, $R(3, 1, 2)$.
> 
> Find two vectors in the plane: $\overrightarrow{PQ} = \langle 1, 1, -2 \rangle$, $\overrightarrow{PR} = \langle 2, -1, -1 \rangle$
> 
> Normal: $\mathbf{n} = \overrightarrow{PQ} \times \overrightarrow{PR} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & 1 & -2 \ 2 & -1 & -1\end{vmatrix}$
> 
> $= (-1-2)\mathbf{i} - (-1+4)\mathbf{j} + (-1-2)\mathbf{k} = \langle -3, -3, -3 \rangle$
> 
> Simplify: $\mathbf{n} = \langle 1, 1, 1 \rangle$. Plane: $(x-1) + (y-2) + (z-3) = 0$, i.e., $x + y + z = 6$.

### 7.3 Distance from a Point to a Plane

For the plane $ax + by + cz + d = 0$ and point $P_1 = (x_1, y_1, z_1)$:

$$D = \frac{|ax_1 + by_1 + cz_1 + d|}{\sqrt{a^2 + b^2 + c^2}}$$

This formula is the 3D analogue of the familiar 2D point-to-line distance formula.

## §8. Cylinders and Quadric Surfaces

### 8.1 Cylinders

A **cylinder** in 3D is a surface generated by lines (rulings) parallel to a given axis, passing through a curve (the directrix) in a coordinate plane. The key observation:

> An equation in $\mathbb{R}^3$ that **lacks one variable** defines a cylinder whose rulings are parallel to that variable's axis.

|Equation|Surface|Axis|
|---|---|---|
|$x^2 + y^2 = r^2$|circular cylinder|$z$-axis|
|$y = x^2$|parabolic cylinder|$z$-axis|
|$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{z^2}{c^2} = 1$|elliptic cylinder|$y$-axis|

> **Example 8.1.** $z = \sin(x)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$: a sinusoidal cylinder extending infinitely in the $y$-direction. No matter what $y$ is, the cross-section in any plane $y = k$ is the curve $z = \sin(x)$.

### 8.2 Quadric Surfaces

These are the 3D analogues of conic sections — defined by second-degree equations in $x, y, z$. The six canonical types:

|Surface|Standard Equation|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|**Ellipsoid**|$\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}+\frac{z^2}{c^2}=1$|All traces are ellipses; sphere if $a=b=c$|
|**Elliptic Paraboloid**|$z = \frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}$|Opens upward; vertex at origin|
|**Hyperbolic Paraboloid**|$z = \frac{y^2}{b^2}-\frac{x^2}{a^2}$|Saddle shape; critical point is a saddle|
|**Hyperboloid (1 sheet)**|$\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}-\frac{z^2}{c^2}=1$|Connected; "hourglass without the pinch"|
|**Hyperboloid (2 sheets)**|$-\frac{x^2}{a^2}-\frac{y^2}{b^2}+\frac{z^2}{c^2}=1$|Two disconnected pieces|
|**Elliptic Cone**|$\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=\frac{z^2}{c^2}$|Two nappes meeting at origin|

**How to identify:** Find the **traces** — cross-sections with coordinate planes and planes parallel to them. The pattern of traces (ellipse, parabola, hyperbola) identifies the surface type.

> **Example 8.2.** Identify $4x^2 + y^2 - z^2 = 4$.
> 
> Rewrite: $x^2 + \frac{y^2}{4} - \frac{z^2}{4} = 1$. This is a **hyperboloid of one sheet** with $a=1$, $b=2$, $c=2$.
> 
> Trace in $z=k$: $x^2 + \frac{y^2}{4} = 1 + \frac{k^2}{4}$ — ellipses for all $k$.
> 
> Trace in $x=0$: $\frac{y^2}{4} - \frac{z^2}{4} = 1$ — a hyperbola.

## §9. Coordinate Systems Beyond Cartesian

### 9.1 Cylindrical Coordinates $(r, \theta, z)$

Essentially polar coordinates in the $xy$-plane, plus $z$ unchanged.

**Conversion:** $$x = r\cos\theta, \quad y = r\sin\theta, \quad z = z$$ $$r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \quad \theta = \arctan!\left(\frac{y}{x}\right), \quad z = z$$

Useful when the geometry has **rotational symmetry about the $z$-axis**.

> **Example 9.1.** The cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = 9$ becomes $r = 3$ in cylindrical coordinates. Far simpler.

### 9.2 Spherical Coordinates $(\rho, \theta, \phi)$

- $\rho \geq 0$: distance from origin
- $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$: azimuthal angle in $xy$-plane (same as cylindrical)
- $\phi \in [0, \pi]$: polar angle from positive $z$-axis (colatitude)

**Conversion:** $$x = \rho\sin\phi\cos\theta, \quad y = \rho\sin\phi\sin\theta, \quad z = \rho\cos\phi$$ $$\rho = \sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$$

**Warning on notation:** Physics often swaps $\theta$ and $\phi$ relative to mathematics. Always check the convention in your source.

> **Example 9.2.** The sphere $x^2+y^2+z^2 = a^2$ becomes $\rho = a$. The upper hemisphere $z \geq 0$ is $\phi \in [0, \pi/2]$, $\rho = a$.

## §10. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

_This section deserves serious treatment, not a footnote. Let me be precise._

### 10.1 Weight Space as $\mathbb{R}^n$: The Fundamental Analogy

Every neural network's parameters — weights and biases — can be concatenated into a single vector $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, where $n$ can be in the billions for large language models. The 3D coordinate system is the kindergarten version of this.

When you understand that:

- A point in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is $(x, y, z)$
- Moving from $P_1$ to $P_2$ is a displacement vector $\mathbf{v} = P_2 - P_1$

...you have already internalized the idea that:

- A set of model parameters is a point in $\mathbb{R}^n$
- **Gradient descent** is a displacement $-\alpha \nabla_\mathbf{w} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})$ in that high-dimensional space

### 10.2 The Loss Surface as a Hypersurface

The **loss function** $\mathcal{L}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ defines a **hypersurface** in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ — the exact analogue of a surface $z = f(x, y)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Concepts from 3D geometry map directly:

|3D Geometry|Deep Learning|
|---|---|
|$z = f(x,y)$, a surface|$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})$, the loss landscape|
|Local minimum: $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$, $f_{xx}, f_{yy} > 0$|Converged model: $\nabla_\mathbf{w} \mathcal{L} = \mathbf{0}$|
|Saddle point (hyperbolic paraboloid shape)|Saddle point in loss landscape (common, problematic)|
|Gradient $\nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle$ points uphill|Gradient $\nabla_\mathbf{w}\mathcal{L}$ points toward higher loss|
|Normal to a surface|Gradient of constraint in constrained optimization|

**The hyperbolic paraboloid** ($z = y^2 - x^2$) is literally the shape of a saddle point in a 2D parameter space. In deep learning, saddle points (not local minima) are believed to be the dominant critical points in high-dimensional loss landscapes. Understanding their geometry is active research.

### 10.3 Dot Products: The Engine of Neural Networks

Every **neuron** in a feedforward layer computes:

$$a = \sigma(\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b) = \sigma(w_1x_1 + w_2x_2 + \cdots + w_nx_n + b)$$

This is a dot product. The geometric meaning: $\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} = |\mathbf{w}||\mathbf{x}|\cos\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the weight vector and input vector. The neuron activates strongly when the input is **aligned** with the weight direction. This is the 3D intuition, lifted to $n$ dimensions.

**Attention in Transformers:** The attention score between query $\mathbf{q}$ and key $\mathbf{k}$ is:

$$\text{score}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}) = \frac{\mathbf{q} \cdot \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{d_k}}$$

This is exactly a scaled dot product — measuring angular similarity in $\mathbb{R}^{d_k}$. The $\sqrt{d_k}$ normalization prevents dot products from becoming too large in high dimensions (where random vectors tend to have larger dot products).

### 10.4 Distance and Similarity in Feature Space

In many ML tasks, we embed data points into $\mathbb{R}^n$ (feature space / embedding space) and use **distance** to measure similarity. The Euclidean distance formula from §2 is the default:

$$d(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - y_i)^2}$$

Used in:

- **$k$-Nearest Neighbors (kNN):** classify by the $k$ closest training points
- **$k$-Means Clustering:** assign points to nearest centroid
- **Word Embeddings (Word2Vec, GloVe):** semantic similarity is geometric proximity. The famous example: $\vec{\text{King}} - \vec{\text{Man}} + \vec{\text{Woman}} \approx \vec{\text{Queen}}$ — vector arithmetic in a high-dimensional analogue of $\mathbb{R}^3$.

**Cosine similarity** (preferred when magnitude is irrelevant):

$$\text{sim}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|} = \cos\theta$$

This is exactly the geometric content of the dot product theorem from §5.1, applied at scale.

### 10.5 Cross Products and Normal Vectors in 3D ML

In **3D deep learning** (point cloud processing, 3D object detection, robotics), cross products are used directly:

- Computing **surface normals** for 3D mesh learning
- **PointNet** and its successors process point clouds in $\mathbb{R}^3$, requiring 3D geometry fluency
- **SLAM** (Simultaneous Localization and Mapping) relies on rigid body transformations encoded as rotation matrices and cross products

### 10.6 Planes as Decision Boundaries

A linear classifier in $\mathbb{R}^n$ defines a **hyperplane** — the $n$-dimensional analogue of a plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$:

$$\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x} + b = 0$$

Compare with §7.2: $ax + by + cz + d = 0$. Same structure. The vector $\mathbf{w}$ is the normal to this hyperplane; classification is determined by which side of the hyperplane $\mathbf{x}$ falls on.

**Support Vector Machines (SVM)** explicitly maximize the margin (minimum distance) from training points to this hyperplane — using precisely the distance formula from §7.3.

The distance from a support vector $\mathbf{x}_i$ to the decision boundary is:

$$D = \frac{|\mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{x}_i + b|}{|\mathbf{w}|}$$

Recognize this? It is the point-to-plane distance formula, in $n$ dimensions.

```
R^3 coordinate system
│
├── Points & Distance ─────────────────► kNN, k-Means, embeddings
│
├── Vectors & Dot Product ──────────────► Neurons, Attention, Cosine Similarity
│
├── Planes & Normal Vectors ────────────► Linear Classifiers, SVM, Decision Boundaries
│
├── Surfaces & Quadrics ────────────────► Loss Landscape, Saddle Points, Optimization
│
├── Cross Products ─────────────────────► 3D ML, PointNet, Surface Normals
│
└── Coordinate Transformations ─────────► Feature Engineering, Latent Spaces
```

Every abstraction in deep learning — loss surface, attention mechanism, embedding space — is a generalization of something you learned in 3D. The 3D coordinate system is not a stepping stone you leave behind. It is the intuitive foundation on which all of high-dimensional geometry rests.
