

> _"Projection is the art of seeing one vector through the eyes of another."_

---

## 0. Motivation

You have two vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$. A natural question: **how much of $\mathbf{b}$ points in the direction of $\mathbf{a}$?**

This is not a vague question — it has a precise, unique, geometric answer. The projection extracts the "shadow" of $\mathbf{b}$ cast along $\mathbf{a}$, and the remainder is the part of $\mathbf{b}$ that is purely perpendicular to $\mathbf{a}$.

Everything in applied mathematics — least squares, attention mechanisms, PCA, Gram-Schmidt — rests on this one idea.

---

## 1. Scalar Projection

> **Definition.** The **scalar projection** (or _component_) of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$ is:
> 
> $$\text{comp}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|}$$

**Derivation.** Drop a perpendicular from the tip of $\mathbf{b}$ onto the line through $\mathbf{a}$. The foot of that perpendicular is at signed distance $|\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta$ from the origin along $\hat{\mathbf{a}}$.

From the dot product formula $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = |\mathbf{a}||\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta$:

$$|\mathbf{b}|\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|}$$

Note this is a **signed** scalar — negative when $\theta > \frac{\pi}{2}$.

Equivalently, using the unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \frac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|}$:

$$\text{comp}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} = \hat{\mathbf{a}} \cdot \mathbf{b}$$

The scalar projection is the dot product of $\mathbf{b}$ with the unit vector in the target direction. Clean.

---

## 2. Vector Projection

> **Definition.** The **vector projection** of $\mathbf{b}$ onto $\mathbf{a}$ is:
> 
> $$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|^2},\mathbf{a} = \left(\hat{\mathbf{a}} \cdot \mathbf{b}\right)\hat{\mathbf{a}}$$

**Derivation.** We want a vector parallel to $\mathbf{a}$, with signed length equal to $\text{comp}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b}$:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} = (\text{comp}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b}),\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|}\cdot\frac{\mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{a}|} = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|^2},\mathbf{a}$$

Write this more compactly using the outer product notation:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{a}\mathbf{a}^\top}{|\mathbf{a}|^2},\mathbf{b} = \hat{\mathbf{a}}\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top,\mathbf{b}$$

The matrix $P = \hat{\mathbf{a}}\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top$ is the **projection matrix** onto $\text{span}{\mathbf{a}}$.

---

## 3. The Orthogonal Decomposition

> **Theorem.** Any vector $\mathbf{b}$ decomposes _uniquely_ as:
> 
> $$\mathbf{b} = \underbrace{\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b}}_{\parallel,\mathbf{a}} + \underbrace{(\mathbf{b} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b})}_{\perp,\mathbf{a}}$$

**Proof of orthogonality.** Let $\mathbf{b}_\perp = \mathbf{b} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b}$. Then:

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}_\perp = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a} \cdot \left(\frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|^2},\mathbf{a}\right) = \mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} - \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|^2}|\mathbf{a}|^2 = 0 \quad \square$$

**Proof of uniqueness.** Suppose $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{p} + \mathbf{q}$ with $\mathbf{p} \parallel \mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{q} \perp \mathbf{a}$. Then $\mathbf{p} = c\mathbf{a}$ for some scalar $c$, and $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{q} = 0$. Taking the dot product of $\mathbf{b} = c\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{q}$ with $\mathbf{a}$:

$$\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = c|\mathbf{a}|^2 \implies c = \frac{\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|^2}$$

uniquely determined. $\quad\square$

```
  θ = π/2: b ⊥ a  →  proj_a(b) = 0,   b_⊥ = b
  θ = π:   b anti-∥ a → proj = -|b|â  (negative shadow)
```

## 5. Projection as a Linear Map — The Projection Matrix

Let $\hat{\mathbf{a}} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a unit vector. Define:

$$P = \hat{\mathbf{a}}\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$$

Then $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} = P\mathbf{b}$ for all $\mathbf{b}$.

**Properties:**

|Property|Formula|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|**Idempotent**|$P^2 = P$|Projecting twice = projecting once|
|**Symmetric**|$P^\top = P$|Orthogonal projection|
|**Rank**|$\text{rank}(P) = 1$|Projects onto a line|
|**Eigenvalues**|${0, 1}$|Vectors parallel: eigenvalue 1; perpendicular: eigenvalue 0|

**Proof of idempotency:** $$P^2 = (\hat{\mathbf{a}}\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top)(\hat{\mathbf{a}}\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top) = \hat{\mathbf{a}}(\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top\hat{\mathbf{a}})\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top = \hat{\mathbf{a}} \cdot 1 \cdot \hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top = P \quad \square$$

The complementary projection (onto the orthogonal complement) is $I - P$.

---

## 6. Projection onto a Subspace

More generally, suppose $W = \text{span}{\mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_k}$ where $\mathbf{u}_i$ are **orthonormal**. Then:

$$\text{proj}_W \mathbf{b} = \sum_{i=1}^{k} (\mathbf{u}_i \cdot \mathbf{b}),\mathbf{u}_i = UU^\top \mathbf{b}$$

where $U = [\mathbf{u}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{u}_k]$.

If the columns of $A$ span $W$ but are _not_ orthonormal, the projection formula becomes:

$$\text{proj}_W \mathbf{b} = A(A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top \mathbf{b}$$

This is the **normal equations** formula — the exact solution to the least squares problem.

## 7. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Standard vector projection

Let $\mathbf{b} = \langle 1, 2, 3 \rangle$, $\mathbf{a} = \langle 1, 1, 0 \rangle$.

**Step 1.** Dot product: $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b} = 1 + 2 + 0 = 3$

**Step 2.** $|\mathbf{a}|^2 = 1 + 1 + 0 = 2$

**Step 3.** Vector projection: $$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} = \frac{3}{2}\langle 1,1,0 \rangle = \left\langle \frac{3}{2}, \frac{3}{2}, 0 \right\rangle$$

**Step 4.** Perpendicular component: $$\mathbf{b}_\perp = \langle 1,2,3 \rangle - \left\langle \frac{3}{2}, \frac{3}{2}, 0 \right\rangle = \left\langle -\frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{2}, 3 \right\rangle$$

**Verification:** $\mathbf{a} \cdot \mathbf{b}_\perp = 1\cdot(-\frac{1}{2}) + 1\cdot\frac{1}{2} + 0 = 0 \checkmark$

---

### Example 2 — Work done by a force

A force $\mathbf{F} = \langle 3, 4, 0 \rangle$ is applied to move an object along $\mathbf{d} = \langle 5, 0, 0 \rangle$.

The work done is the force _in the direction of motion_, times the distance:

$$W = \mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{d}} \cdot |\mathbf{d}| = \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{d} = 15$$

The scalar projection $\text{comp}_{\mathbf{d}},\mathbf{F} = \frac{15}{5} = 3$ is the effective component of force. The component $4\hat{\mathbf{j}}$ does zero work — it is perpendicular to displacement.

---

### Example 3 — Projection matrix explicitly

Let $\hat{\mathbf{a}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\langle 1, 1 \rangle$. Then:

$$P = \hat{\mathbf{a}}\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\1 & 1\end{pmatrix}$$

Apply to $\mathbf{b} = \langle 3, 1 \rangle$:

$$P\mathbf{b} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}4\4\end{pmatrix} = \langle 2, 2 \rangle$$

Check: $\langle 2,2 \rangle$ is indeed parallel to $\langle 1,1 \rangle$. And $P^2 = P$:

$$P^2 = \frac{1}{4}\begin{pmatrix}2&2\2&2\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\1&1\end{pmatrix} = P \checkmark$$

## 8. Connection to Direction Cosines

The scalar projection onto $\mathbf{e}_i$ (the $i$-th standard basis vector) is simply:

$$\text{comp}_{\mathbf{e}_i},\mathbf{a} = \frac{\mathbf{e}_i \cdot \mathbf{a}}{|\mathbf{e}_i|} = a_i$$

The direction cosine $\cos\alpha_i = \frac{a_i}{|\mathbf{a}|}$ is the scalar projection of $\hat{\mathbf{a}}$ onto $\mathbf{e}_i$.

In other words: **the direction cosines are the projections of the unit vector onto the coordinate axes.** The fundamental identity $\sum \cos^2\alpha_i = 1$ is just $|\hat{\mathbf{a}}|^2 = 1$ in disguise.

## 9. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 9.1 Least Squares — The Core of Linear Regression

Given data matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ and labels $\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, linear regression solves:

$$\min_{\boldsymbol{\beta}} |\mathbf{y} - X\boldsymbol{\beta}|^2$$

The solution is:

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top \mathbf{y}$$

and the fitted values are:

$$\hat{\mathbf{y}} = X\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}} = \underbrace{X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top}_{\text{hat matrix } H},\mathbf{y} = \text{proj}_{\text{col}(X)},\mathbf{y}$$

Linear regression is **exactly** the orthogonal projection of $\mathbf{y}$ onto the column space of $X$. The residual $\mathbf{y} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}$ is perpendicular to every column of $X$ — that is the normal equations.

$$X^\top(\mathbf{y} - X\hat{\boldsymbol{\beta}}) = \mathbf{0}$$

This is the orthogonality condition from §3, generalized.

---

### 9.2 Principal Component Analysis (PCA)

PCA finds directions $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$ (principal components) that maximize variance. Each data point $\mathbf{x}_i$ is **projected** onto these directions:

$$z_{ij} = \mathbf{v}_j \cdot \mathbf{x}_i = \text{scalar projection of } \mathbf{x}_i \text{ onto } \mathbf{v}_j$$

The matrix of projected coordinates $Z = XV$ is exactly the projection of the data onto the subspace spanned by the principal components. The reconstruction $\hat{X} = ZV^\top = XV V^\top$ uses the projection matrix $P = VV^\top$ (since columns of $V$ are orthonormal).

Dimensionality reduction _is_ projection. The information discarded is $\mathbf{x} - P\mathbf{x}$, the part perpendicular to the subspace.

---

### 9.3 Gram-Schmidt and Orthogonalization in Neural Networks

The **Gram-Schmidt** process builds an orthonormal basis by iteratively removing projections:

$$\mathbf{u}_k = \mathbf{v}_k - \sum_{j=1}^{k-1} \text{proj}_{\mathbf{u}_j},\mathbf{v}_k$$

This is used in:

- **QR decomposition** (backbone of many numerical solvers)
- **Orthogonal weight initialization** in RNNs and deep nets, to preserve gradient norms
- **Spectral normalization** layers, which project weight matrices to have unit spectral norm

---

### 9.4 Attention as Projection

In the Transformer, the query $\mathbf{q}$ and key $\mathbf{k}$ interact via:

$$\text{score}(\mathbf{q}, \mathbf{k}) = \frac{\mathbf{q} \cdot \mathbf{k}}{\sqrt{d_k}} = \frac{|\mathbf{q}||\mathbf{k}|}{\sqrt{d_k}}\cos\theta$$

The scalar projection of $\mathbf{k}$ onto $\mathbf{q}$ is $\text{comp}_{\mathbf{q}},\mathbf{k} = \frac{\mathbf{q}\cdot\mathbf{k}}{|\mathbf{q}|}$. The attention score is a rescaled version of this. The softmax then selects values proportional to how much each key "projects" onto the current query.

The learned weight matrices $W_Q, W_K, W_V$ are projection matrices into query/key/value subspaces.

---

### 9.5 Residual Connections and Orthogonal Decomposition

In ResNets, the residual block computes:

$$\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x} + \mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x})$$

Viewed through the lens of projection: $\mathcal{F}(\mathbf{x})$ learns the component of the desired output that is _not already captured_ by the identity map — the orthogonal complement of what $\mathbf{x}$ already represents. This is the same spirit as $\mathbf{b}_\perp = \mathbf{b} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b}$. The network learns the residual, not the full transformation.

## 10. Summary

$$\boxed{ \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} = \frac{\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}}{|\mathbf{a}|^2},\mathbf{a}, \qquad \mathbf{b} = \text{proj}_{\mathbf{a}},\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{b}_\perp, \qquad \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b}_\perp = 0 }$$

|Concept|Formula|Key Property|
|---|---|---|
|Scalar projection|$\hat{\mathbf{a}}\cdot\mathbf{b}$|Signed length|
|Vector projection|$(\hat{\mathbf{a}}\cdot\mathbf{b})\hat{\mathbf{a}}$|Parallel to $\mathbf{a}$|
|Projection matrix|$P = \hat{\mathbf{a}}\hat{\mathbf{a}}^\top$|$P^2 = P$, $P^\top = P$|
|Orthogonal complement|$\mathbf{b} - P\mathbf{b}$|Perpendicular to $\mathbf{a}$|
|Subspace projection|$A(A^\top A)^{-1}A^\top\mathbf{b}$|Normal equations|
