## 0. A Personal Note Before We Start

When you first see "cylinder" in a multivariable calculus course, the instinct is to think of a tin can. Resist that. The calculus definition of cylinder is vastly more general, and vastly more important.

In MIT 18.02 and Stewart ¬ß12.6, a **cylinder** is not a round tube ‚Äî it is any surface generated by moving a line (called a _ruling_) parallel to a fixed direction, sweeping out above a curve in a coordinate plane. This single idea:

- Produces parabolic cylinders, elliptic cylinders, hyperbolic cylinders, and yes, the circular cylinder
- Encodes the idea that **one variable is missing from the equation** ‚Äî a profound observation
- Is the 3D prototype of **translational symmetry**, which underlies convolutional neural networks

Let's be precise.

---

## 1. The Fundamental Definition

**Definition (Cylinder).** Let $C$ be a plane curve and $\mathbf{d}$ a direction vector not parallel to the plane containing $C$. The **cylinder with directrix $C$ and ruling direction $\mathbf{d}$** is the surface swept by moving a line through each point of $C$ parallel to $\mathbf{d}$:

$$S = {, P + t,\mathbf{d} ;\mid; P \in C,; t \in \mathbb{R} ,}$$

Each line ${P + t\mathbf{d} : t \in \mathbb{R}}$ is called a **ruling** or **generator** of the cylinder.

```
        z
        |   ___---___         ‚Üê top "copy" of C
        |  /         \
        | |     C'    |
        |  \___   ___/
        |      ---            ‚Üê rulings (lines parallel to z-axis)
        |   ___---___
        |  /         \
        | |     C     |       ‚Üê directrix C in the xy-plane
        |  \___   ___/
        ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ x,y
```

**The critical observation for computation:** When the ruling direction is parallel to a coordinate axis, the equation of the cylinder is _exactly the equation of the directrix curve, with the ruling variable absent_.

---

## 2. Cylinders with Rulings Parallel to a Coordinate Axis

This is the standard case in Stewart and MIT 18.02. The key rule:

> **If an equation in $x, y, z$ is missing one variable, its graph is a cylinder whose rulings are parallel to the axis of the missing variable.**

This is worth proving carefully.

**Theorem.** Let $f(x, y) = 0$ be the equation of a curve $C$ in the $xy$-plane. Then the set $$S = {(x, y, z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : f(x, y) = 0}$$ is a cylinder with directrix $C$ and rulings parallel to the $z$-axis.

_Proof._ If $(x_0, y_0, 0) \in C$, then $f(x_0, y_0) = 0$. For any $t \in \mathbb{R}$, the point $(x_0, y_0, t)$ also satisfies $f(x_0, y_0) = 0$ since $z$ does not appear. So the entire vertical line through $(x_0, y_0, 0)$ parallel to the $z$-axis lies in $S$. Conversely, every point in $S$ lies on such a line. Hence $S$ is swept by vertical lines over $C$. $\square$

The same argument applies by permutation to equations missing $x$ (rulings $\parallel$ $x$-axis) or missing $y$ (rulings $\parallel$ $y$-axis).

## 3. The Standard Cylinders and Their Equations

### 3.1 Circular Cylinder

$$\boxed{x^2 + y^2 = r^2}$$

**Missing variable:** $z$. **Rulings:** parallel to $z$-axis.

**Directrix:** circle of radius $r$ in the $xy$-plane. Every horizontal cross-section ($z = k$) is the same circle $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$. This is the tin-can intuition ‚Äî but now understood rigorously.

**Parametric form:** $$\mathbf{r}(u, v) = (r\cos u,; r\sin u,; v), \qquad u \in [0, 2\pi),; v \in \mathbb{R}$$

**Cross-sections:**

|Cut by plane|Shape|
|---|---|
|$z = k$ (horizontal)|Circle $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$|
|$y = 0$ (through axis)|Two vertical lines $x = \pm r$|
|$x = a$, $|a|

```
        z
        |   ___---___
        |  /    |    \
        | |     |     |   ‚Üê r = radius
        |  \____|____/
        |       ‚Üë
        |    z-axis (rulings go up/down)
        ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ y
       /
      x
```

### 3.2 Parabolic Cylinder

$$\boxed{z = x^2}$$

**Missing variable:** $y$. **Rulings:** parallel to $y$-axis.

**Directrix:** parabola $z = x^2$ in the $xz$-plane. The surface is a "trough" ‚Äî like a parabola extruded infinitely in the $y$-direction.

**Cross-sections:**

|Cut|Shape|
|---|---|
|$y = k$|Parabola $z = x^2$ (same for all $k$)|
|$z = k \geq 0$|Two lines $x = \pm\sqrt{k}$, parallel to $y$-axis|
|$x = k$|Horizontal line $z = k^2$|

```
        z
        |  \       /
        |   \     /
        |    \   /
        |     \_/   ‚Üê parabola z = x¬≤ in xz-plane
        |
        ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ x
       /
      y  ‚Üê rulings go in and out of page (y-direction)
```

**Think of it as:** the graph of $z = x^2$ pushed infinitely forward and backward in the $y$-direction.

### 3.3 Elliptic Cylinder

$$\boxed{\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1}$$

**Missing variable:** $z$. **Rulings:** parallel to $z$-axis.

**Directrix:** ellipse with semi-axes $a$ (along $x$) and $b$ (along $y$) in the $xy$-plane.

Every horizontal slice is the same ellipse. If $a = b = r$: circular cylinder.

**Example.** $\frac{x^2}{4} + \frac{y^2}{9} = 1$: ellipse with $a=2$, $b=3$ extruded vertically.

### 3.4 Hyperbolic Cylinder

$$\boxed{\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1}$$

**Missing variable:** $z$. **Rulings:** parallel to $z$-axis.

**Directrix:** hyperbola in the $xy$-plane. The surface consists of **two sheets** (one for $x > 0$, one for $x < 0$), each swept vertically.

**Cross-sections at $z = k$:** always the same hyperbola $\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$.

### 3.5 Summary Table

|Name|Equation|Missing variable|Directrix|Shape intuition|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Circular cylinder|$x^2 + y^2 = r^2$|$z$|Circle in $xy$-plane|Tin can, infinite|
|Parabolic cylinder|$z = x^2$|$y$|Parabola in $xz$-plane|Curved trough|
|Elliptic cylinder|$\frac{x^2}{a^2} + \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$|$z$|Ellipse in $xy$-plane|Oval tube|
|Hyperbolic cylinder|$\frac{x^2}{a^2} - \frac{y^2}{b^2} = 1$|$z$|Hyperbola in $xy$-plane|Two curved sheets|
|General cylinder|$f(x,y) = 0$|$z$|Curve in $xy$-plane|Extrusion of any curve|

## 4. General Equation Recognition: The One-Variable-Missing Rule

**The practical test:**

1. Look at the equation in $x, y, z$.
2. **Which variable is absent?** That axis is the ruling direction.
3. **What curve does the remaining equation describe in 2D?** That's the directrix.
4. The 3D surface is that curve extruded along the missing axis.

**Examples:**

|Equation|Missing variable|Directrix (2D)|Surface|
|---|---|---|---|
|$y^2 + z^2 = 4$|$x$|Circle in $yz$-plane|Circular cylinder, axis = $x$-axis|
|$x = y^2 - 1$|$z$|Parabola in $xy$-plane|Parabolic cylinder|
|$xy = 1$|$z$|Hyperbola $xy=1$ in $xy$-plane|Hyperbolic cylinder (rotated 45¬∞)|
|$z = \sin x$|$y$|Sine wave in $xz$-plane|Sinusoidal cylinder|
|$y = e^x$|$z$|Exponential curve in $xy$-plane|Exponential cylinder|

_That last one ‚Äî $z = \sin x$ ‚Äî is an infinite corrugated sheet, the cross-section of certain Fourier components._

## 5. Parametric Representations

For computation and rendering, parametric forms are essential.

**General cylinder** with directrix $C: \mathbf{r}(u) = (x(u), y(u), 0)$ and ruling direction $\mathbf{d} = (0, 0, 1)$: $$\mathbf{R}(u, v) = \mathbf{r}(u) + v,\mathbf{d} = (x(u),; y(u),; v)$$

**Specific cases:**

$$\text{Circular: } \mathbf{R}(u,v) = (r\cos u,; r\sin u,; v)$$

$$\text{Elliptic: } \mathbf{R}(u,v) = (a\cos u,; b\sin u,; v)$$

$$\text{Parabolic: } \mathbf{R}(u,v) = (u,; v,; u^2)$$

The **normal vector** to the surface at a point is: $$\mathbf{n} = \frac{\partial \mathbf{R}}{\partial u} \times \frac{\partial \mathbf{R}}{\partial v}$$

For the circular cylinder: $\partial_u \mathbf{R} = (-r\sin u, r\cos u, 0)$, $\partial_v \mathbf{R} = (0, 0, 1)$: $$\mathbf{n} = (-r\sin u, r\cos u, 0) \times (0,0,1) = (r\cos u,; r\sin u,; 0)$$

This points radially outward ‚Äî the normal to a circular cylinder is horizontal (perpendicular to the axis), as expected.

## 6. Volume and Surface Area of a Finite Circular Cylinder

The **finite right circular cylinder** of radius $r$ and height $h$ (the "tin can"):

$$\boxed{V = \pi r^2 h}$$

_Derivation._ By Cavalieri's principle: every horizontal cross-section at height $z \in [0, h]$ is a disk of area $\pi r^2$. Integrating: $$V = \int_0^h \pi r^2 , dz = \pi r^2 h \qquad \square$$

**Lateral surface area** (curved side only): $$\boxed{A_{\text{lateral}} = 2\pi r h}$$

_Derivation._ Unroll the cylinder: it becomes a rectangle of width $2\pi r$ (circumference) and height $h$.

**Total surface area** (including top and bottom disks): $$\boxed{A_{\text{total}} = 2\pi r h + 2\pi r^2 = 2\pi r(h + r)}$$

## 7. Level Curves and Cylinders: The Deep Connection

This is a key conceptual link between cylinders and multivariable calculus.

**Definition.** For $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, the **level curve** at height $c$ is ${(x,y) : f(x,y) = c}$.

Now lift to 3D: the set ${(x, y, z) : f(x, y) = c}$ is exactly a **cylinder** over the level curve, with rulings parallel to $z$.

**The graph** $z = f(x, y)$ intersects the horizontal plane $z = c$ in the curve $f(x,y) = c$ ‚Äî precisely the level curve.

_So: every level curve of $f$ is the directrix of a vertical cylinder. Visualizing $f$ by its level curves is visualizing a family of cylinders stacked up._

**Example.** $f(x, y) = x^2 + y^2$. Level curves: $x^2 + y^2 = c$ (circles). The graph is a paraboloid; its level sets are circular cylinders $x^2 + y^2 = c$.

This is why topographic maps (level curves) and 3D surfaces are interchangeable representations ‚Äî and why **gradient descent on a loss function** moves perpendicular to the level curves of the loss.

## 8. Intersection of a Cylinder with a Plane

**Problem.** Intersect the circular cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ with the plane $z = mx + c$.

Substitute $z = mx + c$ into the parametric form $(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta, z)$:

$$z = mr\cos\theta + c$$

So the intersection curve in 3D is: $$\mathbf{r}(\theta) = (r\cos\theta,; r\sin\theta,; mr\cos\theta + c)$$

This is an **ellipse** in the cutting plane. (This is how Dandelin proved properties of conic sections ‚Äî cutting a cone or cylinder with planes always gives conics.)

_Special case:_ $m = 0$ (horizontal cut): $z = c$ ‚Äî a circle.

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 9.1 The "Missing Variable" Principle = Translational Symmetry = Convolution

The defining property of a cylinder is: **the equation is independent of one variable**. In other words, the surface looks the same no matter where you are along the ruling direction.

This is **translational invariance** ‚Äî the single most important structural prior in convolutional neural networks (CNNs).

A 1D convolutional filter $w(u)$ applied to a signal $f(x)$: $$(\text{output})(x) = \int w(u), f(x - u), du$$

produces the **same transformation regardless of where in $x$ you are** ‚Äî the filter doesn't care about absolute position, only relative shape. This is exactly the cylinder principle: the defining equation (the filter) is independent of the "ruling" direction (absolute position $x$).

In a 2D CNN layer, a filter $W(u, v)$ slides over an image $I(x, y)$ producing feature maps ‚Äî a 2D version of cylindrical translational symmetry.

_The cylinder is the geometric axiom underlying all convolutional architectures._

### 9.2 Decision Boundaries as Cylinders in High Dimensions

In a binary classifier operating on $n$-dimensional input, a **cylindrical decision boundary** in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is a surface that depends on only $k < n$ features. For example, a classifier using only $x_1$ and $x_2$ (ignoring $x_3, \ldots, x_n$) has a decision boundary that is a cylinder in $\mathbb{R}^n$ ‚Äî the equation is $f(x_1, x_2) = 0$ regardless of the other coordinates.

This connects to **feature selection**: identifying which variables the boundary depends on is equivalent to identifying the "ruling directions" of the boundary cylinder.

### 9.3 Loss Function Geometry: Cylindrical Valleys

If a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta_1, \theta_2)$ satisfies $\mathcal{L}(\theta_1, \theta_2) = g(\theta_1)$ (independent of $\theta_2$), then its level sets in parameter space are **parabolic or circular cylinders** ‚Äî infinitely long troughs. Gradient descent in this case cannot determine the optimal $\theta_2$ ‚Äî it slides freely along the ruling direction.

This is the geometric picture behind:

- **Underdetermined systems** in linear regression (infinitely many solutions)
- **Flat directions** in neural network loss landscapes
- The need for **regularization** (adding curvature in the cylinder's ruling direction)

```
  Loss
    ‚Üë         ___
    |       /     \          ‚Üê parabolic cylinder: L(Œ∏‚ÇÅ,Œ∏‚ÇÇ) = Œ∏‚ÇÅ¬≤
    |      |       |            flat in the Œ∏‚ÇÇ direction
    |       \___/
    ‚îî‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ‚îÄ Œ∏‚ÇÅ
   /
  Œ∏‚ÇÇ   ‚Üê gradient = 0 in this direction (flat valley)
```

### 9.4 Gaussian Kernels and Cylindrical Symmetry

The Gaussian kernel in 2D: $$K(x, y) = e^{-(x^2 + y^2)/2\sigma^2}$$

has **circular symmetry** ‚Äî it depends only on $r = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$, i.e., is constant on circular cylinders $x^2 + y^2 = c$. This is why isotropic Gaussian kernels blur equally in all directions.

An **anisotropic Gaussian** $K(x, y) = e^{-(x^2/\sigma_x^2 + y^2/\sigma_y^2)/2}$ has **elliptic cylindrical** level sets ‚Äî blurring more in one direction. This is used in edge-detection filters and attention mechanisms.

### 9.5 Volumetric Data in 3D CNNs

In 3D convolutional networks (used in medical imaging, protein structure prediction like AlphaFold), filters slide along three axes. When a 3D CNN learns a filter that activates on a tubular structure (e.g., a blood vessel), it is literally learning to detect a **cylindrical pattern** ‚Äî a feature that is approximately invariant along the vessel's axis.

## 10. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Identify and sketch

_What surface is $y^2 + z^2 = 9$?_

Missing variable: $x$. Directrix: circle $y^2 + z^2 = 9$ in the $yz$-plane (radius $3$). Rulings: parallel to $x$-axis.

**Surface:** Circular cylinder of radius $3$ with axis along the $x$-axis.

Any point $(t, 3\cos\theta, 3\sin\theta)$ for $t \in \mathbb{R}, \theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ lies on the surface.

### Example 2: Equation from description

_Write the equation of the parabolic cylinder whose directrix is the parabola $z = y^2 - 2y$ in the $yz$-plane, with rulings parallel to $x$._

Since rulings are parallel to $x$ (so $x$ is absent): $\boxed{z = y^2 - 2y}$.

Complete the square: $z = (y-1)^2 - 1$, i.e., $z + 1 = (y-1)^2$. Vertex of the parabola at $(y, z) = (1, -1)$ in the $yz$-plane.

### Example 3: Intersection with a plane

_Describe the curve of intersection of $x^2 + y^2 = 4$ and $z = x + 3$._

Parametrize the cylinder: $(2\cos t, 2\sin t, z)$. Substitute into the plane: $z = 2\cos t + 3$.

Intersection: $(2\cos t,; 2\sin t,; 2\cos t + 3)$ ‚Äî an ellipse tilted in 3D.

### Example 4: Volume of a solid

_Find the volume of the solid bounded by the parabolic cylinder $x = y^2$ and the planes $x = 0$, $z = 0$, $z = 4$._

For fixed $z \in [0,4]$, the cross-section in the $xy$-plane is $0 \leq x \leq y^2$... wait, reverse: the region is $0 \leq x \leq$ ... actually the solid is ${0 \leq x \leq ... }$. Let me set up correctly:

The cylinder is $x = y^2$ (rulings $\parallel z$). The solid: $y^2 \leq x$? No ‚Äî bounded by $x = y^2$, $x = 0$ (the plane), and $z \in [0,4]$:

Region in $xy$: between $x = 0$ and $x = y^2$, but we need bounds on $y$. Add $y \in [-1, 1]$ for a finite example. Then:

$$V = \int_0^4 \int_{-1}^1 \int_0^{y^2} dx; dy; dz = \int_0^4 dz \int_{-1}^1 y^2, dy = 4 \cdot \left[\frac{y^3}{3}\right]_{-1}^1 = 4 \cdot \frac{2}{3} = \frac{8}{3}$$
