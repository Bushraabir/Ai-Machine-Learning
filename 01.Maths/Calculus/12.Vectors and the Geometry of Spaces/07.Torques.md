

> _"Torque is the rotational analogue of force — but the geometry hidden inside the cross product is where the real story lives."_

---

## §1. Motivation — Why Torque?

Consider trying to open a heavy door. You instinctively push at the **edge**, not near the hinge. You push **perpendicular** to the door, not parallel to it. Both of these intuitions are encoded, simultaneously and elegantly, in a single mathematical object: the **torque vector**.

Torque answers the question: _given a force applied at a position in space, how much does it tend to rotate a body about some axis?_

---

## §2. Definition

Let $\mathbf{r}$ be the **position vector** from the pivot point (origin of rotation) to the point where force is applied, and let $\mathbf{F}$ be the applied force. Then the **torque** is:

$$\boxed{\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}}$$

This is a **cross product** — so torque is a **vector**, not a scalar.

### 2.1 Unpacking the Cross Product

Recall that for $\mathbf{r} = \langle r_1, r_2, r_3 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{F} = \langle F_1, F_2, F_3 \rangle$:

$$\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ r_1 & r_2 & r_3 \ F_1 & F_2 & F_3 \end{vmatrix}$$

$$= \mathbf{i}(r_2 F_3 - r_3 F_2) - \mathbf{j}(r_1 F_3 - r_3 F_1) + \mathbf{k}(r_1 F_2 - r_2 F_1)$$

### 2.2 Magnitude and Geometric Interpretation

$$|\boldsymbol{\tau}| = |\mathbf{r}||\mathbf{F}|\sin\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{F}$.

**Key insight:** Torque is maximised when $\mathbf{r} \perp \mathbf{F}$ (i.e., $\theta = 90°$), and **zero** when $\mathbf{r} \parallel \mathbf{F}$ (force points directly toward or away from the pivot — no rotation).

### 2.3 Direction — The Right-Hand Rule

The direction of $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}$ follows the **right-hand rule**:

- Point fingers of your right hand along $\mathbf{r}$
- Curl them toward $\mathbf{F}$
- Your thumb points in the direction of $\boldsymbol{\tau}$

```
         τ (out of page ⊙)
         ↑
         |
    r    |        F
  ───────●─────────→
       pivot
```

In 2D (rotation in the $xy$-plane), torque is purely in the $\hat{k}$ direction:

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = (r_1 F_2 - r_2 F_1),\hat{k}$$

A **positive** $\tau_z$ means counterclockwise rotation; **negative** means clockwise.

## §3. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Wrench on a Bolt (2D)

A force $\mathbf{F} = \langle 0, 10, 0 \rangle$ N is applied at position $\mathbf{r} = \langle 0.3, 0, 0 \rangle$ m (a wrench of length 0.3 m pointing along the $x$-axis).

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 0.3 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 10 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{k}(0.3 \cdot 10 - 0 \cdot 0) = 3,\hat{k} \text{ N·m}$$

$\boldsymbol{\tau} = 3,\hat{k}$ N·m — rotation is **counterclockwise** about the $z$-axis.

**Visualisation:**

```
    F = 10ĵ
    ↑
    |
    ●────────────→  r = 0.3î
  pivot
  
  Rotation: counterclockwise ↺
  τ = 3k̂ (pointing out of the page ⊙)
```

---

### Example 2 — Force at an Angle

Force $\mathbf{F} = \langle 5, 5, 0 \rangle$ N applied at $\mathbf{r} = \langle 2, 0, 0 \rangle$ m.

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 2 & 0 & 0 \ 5 & 5 & 0 \end{vmatrix} = \hat{k}(2 \cdot 5 - 0 \cdot 5) = 10,\hat{k} \text{ N·m}$$

Notice: only the **perpendicular component** of $\mathbf{F}$ (the $\hat{j}$ part) contributes to torque! The radial component (along $\hat{i}$) is wasted — it pushes toward the pivot and creates no rotation.

---

### Example 3 — Full 3D Torque

$\mathbf{r} = \langle 1, 2, 0 \rangle$, $\mathbf{F} = \langle 0, 3, -1 \rangle$:

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & 2 & 0 \ 0 & 3 & -1 \end{vmatrix} = \mathbf{i}(2 \cdot(-1) - 0 \cdot 3) - \mathbf{j}(1 \cdot(-1) - 0 \cdot 0) + \mathbf{k}(1 \cdot 3 - 2 \cdot 0)$$

$$= \mathbf{i}(-2) - \mathbf{j}(-1) + \mathbf{k}(3) = \langle -2, 1, 3 \rangle \text{ N·m}$$

## §4. Torque as a Signed Scalar (2D Special Case)

In planar problems (forces and positions in the $xy$-plane), it's common to work with the **scalar torque**:

$$\tau = r_x F_y - r_y F_x = |\mathbf{r}||\mathbf{F}|\sin\theta$$

This is exactly the $z$-component of $\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}$.

This is also the **signed area** of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{F}$ — a lovely geometric fact.

```
          F
         ↗ 
        /  |  ← area = |τ|
       /   |
      /    |
     ●─────┘
     r
```

## §5. Newton's Second Law for Rotation

Just as $\mathbf{F} = m\mathbf{a}$ governs linear dynamics, torque governs **rotational dynamics**:

$$\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} = I,\boldsymbol{\alpha}$$

where:

- $I$ = **moment of inertia** (rotational analogue of mass, depends on mass distribution)
- $\boldsymbol{\alpha} = \dot{\boldsymbol{\omega}}$ = **angular acceleration**

And the **angular momentum** $\mathbf{L} = I\boldsymbol{\omega}$ satisfies:

$$\boldsymbol{\tau} = \frac{d\mathbf{L}}{dt}$$

This is the rotational analogue of $\mathbf{F} = \frac{d\mathbf{p}}{dt}$.

---

## §6. Equilibrium and Static Torque

A rigid body is in **rotational equilibrium** iff:

$$\sum_i \boldsymbol{\tau}_i = \mathbf{0}$$

That is, all torques about any chosen pivot must sum to zero.

**Example — Seesaw:**

```
       m₁g↓        m₂g↓
         |            |
   ──────●────────────●──── plank
         |←   r₁  →|←  r₂  →|
                  △
                pivot
```

Equilibrium: $m_1 g r_1 = m_2 g r_2$ (torques balance about the pivot).

## §7. Connection to the Cross Product — A Deeper Look

The torque $\boldsymbol{\tau} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}$ is fundamentally an **antisymmetric** operation:

$$\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} = -\mathbf{F} \times \mathbf{r}$$

This reflects the physical fact that the _order matters_: the same force applied at the same point creates torques in opposite senses depending on the reference direction.

### Decomposition

Any force $\mathbf{F}$ can be split into components:

$$\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{F}_\perp + \mathbf{F}_\parallel$$

where $\mathbf{F}_\parallel$ is along $\mathbf{r}$ and $\mathbf{F}_\perp$ is perpendicular to $\mathbf{r}$.

Then: $$\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}_\perp + \underbrace{\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}_\parallel}_{= \mathbf{0}} = \mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}_\perp$$

**Only the perpendicular component produces torque.** The parallel component is rotational dead weight.

---

## §8. Moment Arm — An Equivalent Formulation

The **moment arm** $d$ is the perpendicular distance from the pivot to the **line of action** of $\mathbf{F}$.

$$|\boldsymbol{\tau}| = |\mathbf{F}| \cdot d$$

This is equivalent to $|\mathbf{r}||\mathbf{F}|\sin\theta$ since $d = |\mathbf{r}|\sin\theta$.

```
           ←────── line of action of F ────→  →F
                          ↕ d (moment arm)
           ●─────────────────────────────────────
         pivot        r
```

This formulation is often cleaner for problems where the line of action is easy to identify geometrically.

---

## §9. Torques and Differential Forms (Preview)

For the advanced reader: torque is naturally expressed using the **exterior product** $\wedge$. In 3D, $\mathbf{r} \times \mathbf{F}$ is dual (via the Hodge star) to the **2-form** $r \wedge F$, which represents an oriented area element.

This is why torque is sometimes called a **pseudovector** (or axial vector) — it behaves differently from a polar vector under reflections. In even dimensions, the cross product doesn't exist, but the exterior product always does.

## §10. Torque in AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

This is where classical mechanics and modern computation collide in surprising ways.

### 10.1 Gradient Descent as a "Torque" Analogy

In gradient descent, the **gradient** $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ plays the role of a force that "pushes" parameters. The **learning rate** $\eta$ scales the step — analogous to the moment arm scaling the torque. The update:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta,\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$$

is structurally similar to angular impulse: a force applied at a lever arm produces a rotation. The further the parameters are from a minimum (longer lever arm), the larger the induced rotation in parameter space.

### 10.2 Rigid Body Simulation in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

**Physics-Informed Neural Networks** often embed torque equations directly as constraints in the loss function. When simulating articulated bodies (robot arms, molecular dynamics):

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{physics}} = \left|\boldsymbol{\tau}_{\text{net}} - I\boldsymbol{\alpha}\right|^2$$

The network is penalised for predicting motions that violate $\boldsymbol{\tau} = I\boldsymbol{\alpha}$.

### 10.3 Robotics and Reinforcement Learning

In **robot manipulation** (a central problem in deep RL), the policy $\pi_\theta(a | s)$ outputs **joint torques** directly as actions. The agent must learn:

- Which joint to torque
- By how much
- In which direction

Algorithms like **PPO** and **SAC** are routinely benchmarked on torque-controlled MuJoCo environments (e.g., `HalfCheetah-v4`, `Ant-v4`).

```
State s_t ──→ π_θ ──→ [τ₁, τ₂, ..., τₙ] (joint torques)
                              ↓
                     Physics Simulator
                              ↓
                    Next state s_{t+1}, reward r_t
```

### 10.4 Attention Mechanism — A Structural Analogy

In **transformer self-attention**, the output is a weighted combination:

$$\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V}) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{\mathbf{Q}\mathbf{K}^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)\mathbf{V}$$

The $\frac{1}{\sqrt{d_k}}$ scaling factor plays the role of a **moment arm** — it controls how strongly the "rotational effect" (attention) of each query-key pair is expressed. Without this scaling, large $d_k$ causes dot products to grow large, driving softmax into saturation (tiny gradients, no effective learning) — analogous to applying force directly through the pivot.

### 10.5 Angular Momentum in Optimisers

The **momentum** term in SGD with momentum:

$$\mathbf{v}_{t+1} = \beta,\mathbf{v}_t + \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$$ $$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta,\mathbf{v}_{t+1}$$

is precisely **angular momentum** carried forward in time. Just as $\mathbf{L} = I\boldsymbol{\omega}$ persists unless acted on by torque, the velocity $\mathbf{v}_t$ persists and accumulates — enabling the optimiser to "coast" through flat loss landscapes.

**Adam** extends this further by normalising by the second moment of gradients — adaptive torque control, in effect.

### 10.6 Graph Neural Networks and Equivariance

**SE(3)-equivariant GNNs** (e.g., **SchNet**, **DimeNet**, **NequIP**) are designed for molecular property prediction. They must handle torques and angular forces correctly — the network output must transform as a **pseudovector** under rotations and reflections of the input molecule. Torque's status as an axial vector is not a curiosity; it is an architectural constraint.
