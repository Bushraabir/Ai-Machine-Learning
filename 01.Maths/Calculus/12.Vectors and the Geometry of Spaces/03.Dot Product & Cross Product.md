## 0. Orientation — Two Products, Two Philosophies

The dot product and cross product are both ways to "multiply" vectors, but they answer fundamentally different geometric questions and produce fundamentally different outputs:

||Dot Product $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}$|Cross Product $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$|
|---|---|---|
|**Output**|Scalar|Vector|
|**Lives in**|$\mathbb{R}^n$, any dimension|$\mathbb{R}^3$ only|
|**Measures**|Alignment / projection|Area & perpendicularity|
|**Zero when**|Vectors are orthogonal|Vectors are parallel|
|**Max when**|Vectors are parallel|Vectors are orthogonal|
|**Commutative?**|Yes: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}$|No: $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = -\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{u}$|

Think of the dot product as measuring the _shadow_ one vector casts on another. The cross product creates a _new_ vector perpendicular to both, whose length encodes the area they sweep out.

# Part I: The Dot Product

## 1. Definitions — Algebraic and Geometric

### 1.1 Algebraic Definition

Let $\mathbf{u} = \langle u_1, u_2, \ldots, u_n \rangle$ and $\mathbf{v} = \langle v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_n \rangle$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Then:

$$ \boxed{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i v_i = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + \cdots + u_n v_n} $$

The output is a single real number — a **scalar**.

> **Note on notation.** Some texts write $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle$ for the inner product, especially in functional analysis or quantum mechanics. In $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the standard basis, these coincide. The dot product $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$ is also written as the matrix product $\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v}$ when vectors are column matrices.

### 1.2 The Geometric Formula — The Central Theorem

**Theorem (Stewart §12.3).** For any $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$: $$ \boxed{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}|,|\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta} $$ where $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ is the angle between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.

**Proof.** Place both vectors with their tails at the origin. The vector $\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}$ forms the third side of the triangle. By the **Law of Cosines**:

$$ |\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta $$

Now expand $|\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|^2$ algebraically: $$ |\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (u_i - v_i)^2 = \sum u_i^2 - 2\sum u_i v_i + \sum v_i^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 - 2(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + |\mathbf{v}|^2 $$

Equating both expressions and canceling $|\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2$ from both sides: $$ -2(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) = -2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta \implies \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta \qquad \square $$

> **This proof is beautiful.** It is the bridge between pure computation (component-wise sum) and pure geometry (angle). Memorize the strategy: expand $|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}|^2$ two ways and match.

---

## 2. Properties of the Dot Product

Let $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$.

1. **Commutativity:** $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{u}$ _(clear from definition)_
2. **Distributivity:** $\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{w}$
3. **Scalar pullout:** $(c,\mathbf{u})\cdot\mathbf{v} = c(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{u}\cdot(c,\mathbf{v})$
4. **Self-dot = squared norm:** $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{v}|^2$
5. **Zero vector:** $\mathbf{0}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$
6. **Positive definiteness:** $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v} \geq 0$, with equality iff $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$

Properties 1–3 make the dot product a **bilinear form**. Properties 4–6, combined with 1, make it an **inner product** (on real vector spaces). These axioms generalize to infinite-dimensional spaces — the foundation of Hilbert spaces in functional analysis.

---

## 3. Reading $\cos\theta$ — What the Dot Product Detects

From $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$, the sign of the dot product tells you the qualitative relationship between the vectors:

```
                  u
                 /
                / θ < 90°
               /____________ v
    u·v > 0: acute angle, vectors "lean toward each other"


       u __________ v
    u·v = 0: θ = 90°, orthogonal — no alignment at all


    u ____________
                  \
                   \ θ > 90°
                    v
    u·v < 0: obtuse angle, vectors "pull away from each other"
```

The extreme cases:

- $\mathbf{u} \parallel \mathbf{v}$, same direction ($\theta = 0$): $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ — maximum
- $\mathbf{u} \parallel \mathbf{v}$, opposite direction ($\theta = \pi$): $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = -|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ — minimum
- $\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$ ($\theta = \pi/2$): $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$ — no projection

## 4. Projection — The Workhorse Application

### 4.1 Scalar Projection

The **scalar projection** (or component) of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ is the signed length of the shadow of $\mathbf{u}$ along $\hat{\mathbf{v}}$:

$$ \text{comp}_{\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|} = |\mathbf{u}|\cos\theta $$

### 4.2 Vector Projection

The **vector projection** is the actual shadow vector along $\mathbf{v}$:

$$ \text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u} = \left(\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}\right)\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{v} $$

### 4.3 Orthogonal Decomposition

**Every vector** $\mathbf{u}$ decomposes uniquely into a part _along_ $\mathbf{v}$ and a part _perpendicular_ to $\mathbf{v}$:

$$ \mathbf{u} = \underbrace{\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u}}_{\text{parallel to } \mathbf{v}} + \underbrace{(\mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u})}_{\perp, \text{to } \mathbf{v}} $$

**Verify** the perpendicular part is indeed orthogonal to $\mathbf{v}$: $$ (\mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u})\cdot\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} - \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0 \qquad \checkmark $$

```
         u
        /|
       / |
      /  |  ← perpendicular part (u - proj_v u)
     / θ |
    /    |
   +-----+-----------> v
         proj_v u
```

> **This decomposition is the root of the Gram-Schmidt process**, which orthogonalizes a basis, which underlies QR decomposition, PCA, and essentially all of numerical linear algebra.

## 5. The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

**Theorem.** $$ |\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}|,|\mathbf{v}|} $$

**Proof** (elegant algebraic method). Consider $f(t) = |\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}|^2 \geq 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

Expanding: $$ f(t) = |\mathbf{u}|^2 - 2t(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + t^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 \geq 0 $$

This is a quadratic in $t$ that is always non-negative, so its discriminant must be $\leq 0$: $$ \Delta = 4(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 - 4|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 \leq 0 $$ $$ \implies (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 \implies |\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}| \qquad \square $$

**Corollary.** The geometric formula $\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|}$ is valid: Cauchy-Schwarz guarantees the right side lives in $[-1,1]$, so $\theta = \arccos(\cdots)$ is well-defined.

**Corollary.** The **Triangle Inequality**: $|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$.

> Cauchy-Schwarz is one of the most used inequalities in all of mathematics. It appears in probability (correlation coefficients are bounded by 1), quantum mechanics (uncertainty principle), and optimization.

## 6. Worked Examples — Dot Product

### Example 1 — Computing the Angle

Find the angle between $\mathbf{a} = \langle 1, -2, 3 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{b} = \langle 2, 1, 1 \rangle$:

$$ \mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b} = 2 - 2 + 3 = 3 $$ $$ |\mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{1+4+9} = \sqrt{14}, \quad |\mathbf{b}| = \sqrt{4+1+1} = \sqrt{6} $$ $$ \cos\theta = \frac{3}{\sqrt{14}\cdot\sqrt{6}} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{84}} = \frac{3}{2\sqrt{21}} $$ $$ \theta = \arccos!\left(\frac{3}{2\sqrt{21}}\right) \approx 70.9° $$

### Example 2 — Projection and Decomposition

Project $\mathbf{u} = \langle 4, 2, 0 \rangle$ onto $\mathbf{v} = \langle 1, 1, 0 \rangle$:

$$ \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 4+2+0 = 6, \quad |\mathbf{v}|^2 = 2 $$ $$ \text{proj}_\mathbf{v},\mathbf{u} = \frac{6}{2}\langle 1,1,0\rangle = \langle 3,3,0\rangle $$ $$ \text{perpendicular part} = \langle 4,2,0\rangle - \langle 3,3,0\rangle = \langle 1,-1,0\rangle $$

Check: $\langle 1,-1,0\rangle \cdot \langle 1,1,0\rangle = 1-1 = 0$ ✓

### Example 3 — Physical Work

A force $\mathbf{F} = \langle 5, 3, -2 \rangle$ N displaces an object by $\mathbf{d} = \langle 1, 0, 4 \rangle$ m. Work done:

$$ W = \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{d} = 5 + 0 - 8 = -3 \text{ J} $$

Negative work means the force _opposed_ the displacement (like friction doing negative work on a sliding object).

# Part II: The Cross Product

## 7. Definition — Three Equivalent Forms

The cross product is defined **only in $\mathbb{R}^3$**. (A generalization exists in $\mathbb{R}^7$, but that's exotic mathematics.)

### 7.1 Determinant Form (Most Practical)

$$ \boxed{\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{vmatrix}} $$

Expanding along the first row:

$$ \mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{i}(u_2 v_3 - u_3 v_2) - \mathbf{j}(u_1 v_3 - u_3 v_1) + \mathbf{k}(u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1) $$

$$ = \langle u_2 v_3 - u_3 v_2,; u_3 v_1 - u_1 v_3,; u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1 \rangle $$

> **Memory device.** For the $i$-component, "cover" the first column and take the $2\times 2$ determinant of what remains. Alternate signs: $+, -, +$.

### 7.2 Geometric Definition

$$ \boxed{\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = (|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\sin\theta),\hat{\mathbf{n}}} $$

where $\theta \in [0,\pi]$ is the angle between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$, and $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is the unit vector perpendicular to both, determined by the **right-hand rule**.

**Right-hand rule:** Point fingers of right hand along $\mathbf{u}$, curl them toward $\mathbf{v}$. Thumb points in direction of $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$.

```
        ^ n = u × v (thumb)
        |
        |
   u ---+---> (fingers curl from u toward v)
         \
          v
```

### 7.3 Standard Basis Cross Products

$$ \mathbf{i}\times\mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k}, \quad \mathbf{j}\times\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{i}, \quad \mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{i} = \mathbf{j} $$ $$ \mathbf{j}\times\mathbf{i} = -\mathbf{k}, \quad \mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{j} = -\mathbf{i}, \quad \mathbf{i}\times\mathbf{k} = -\mathbf{j} $$ $$ \mathbf{i}\times\mathbf{i} = \mathbf{j}\times\mathbf{j} = \mathbf{k}\times\mathbf{k} = \mathbf{0} $$

**Mnemonic (cyclic):**

```
    i → j → k → i → j → k → ...
    Going forward: positive
    Going backward: negative
```

---

## 8. Properties of the Cross Product

Let $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$.

1. **Anti-commutativity:** $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = -(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{u})$
2. **Distributivity over addition:** $\mathbf{u}\times(\mathbf{v}+\mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{w}$
3. **Scalar pullout:** $(c,\mathbf{u})\times\mathbf{v} = c(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{u}\times(c,\mathbf{v})$
4. **Self-cross = zero:** $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{0}$ _(since $\sin 0 = 0$)_
5. **Perpendicularity:** $(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$ and $(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$
6. **NOT associative:** $(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})\times\mathbf{w} \neq \mathbf{u}\times(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w})$ in general

> **Property 1 is the critical one.** The cross product is _anti-commutative_, not commutative. Reversing the order flips the direction of the result. This is why order matters in 3D geometry — and in physics (torque, angular momentum).

**Proof of Property 5** (perpendicularity to $\mathbf{u}$): $$ (\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{u} = (u_2 v_3 - u_3 v_2)u_1 + (u_3 v_1 - u_1 v_3)u_2 + (u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1)u_3 $$ Expanding: $u_1 u_2 v_3 - u_1 u_3 v_2 + u_2 u_3 v_1 - u_1 u_2 v_3 + u_1 u_3 v_2 - u_2 u_3 v_1 = 0$ $\square$

---

## 9. Geometric Content — Area and Volume

### 9.1 Area of a Parallelogram

$$ \text{Area} = |\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\sin\theta $$

This is the area of the parallelogram with sides $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.

```
         v
        /----/
       /    /   Area = ||u × v||
      /----/
           u
```

**Area of the triangle** with sides $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$: $\frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}|$.

### 9.2 Scalar Triple Product and Volume of a Parallelepiped

The **scalar triple product** (or box product):

$$ [\mathbf{u},\mathbf{v},\mathbf{w}] = \mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w}) = \begin{vmatrix} u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \ w_1 & w_2 & w_3 \end{vmatrix} $$

This gives the _signed_ volume of the parallelepiped spanned by $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{w}$.

$$ V = |\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w})| $$

**Key property:** $\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{v}\cdot(\mathbf{w}\times\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{w}\cdot(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})$ — cyclic permutations preserve the value.

**Coplanarity test:** $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w}$ are coplanar $\iff$ $\mathbf{u}\cdot(\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{w}) = 0$.

---

## 10. Lagrange's Identity

**Theorem.** $$ |\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 - (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 $$

**Proof** (component-wise): Denote $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \langle a,b,c\rangle$ where $a = u_2v_3-u_3v_2$, etc. Compute $a^2+b^2+c^2$ and expand. Meanwhile: $$ |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 - (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 = (u_1^2+u_2^2+u_3^2)(v_1^2+v_2^2+v_3^2) - (u_1v_1+u_2v_2+u_3v_3)^2 $$ Expanding both and matching terms confirms equality. $\square$

**Geometric meaning:** Using $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$:

$$ |\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2(1 - \cos^2\theta) = |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2\sin^2\theta $$

So $|\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\sin\theta$. The geometric formula falls out of Lagrange's identity — the two definitions (determinant and geometric) are equivalent.

---

## 11. The BAC-CAB Rule

A crucial identity for the **vector triple product**:

$$ \boxed{\mathbf{A}\times(\mathbf{B}\times\mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{C}) - \mathbf{C}(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B})} $$

Mnemonic: **BAC minus CAB**. The result is a _linear combination_ of $\mathbf{B}$ and $\mathbf{C}$ — which makes sense, since $\mathbf{B}\times\mathbf{C}$ is perpendicular to both $\mathbf{B}$ and $\mathbf{C}$, so $\mathbf{A}\times(\mathbf{B}\times\mathbf{C})$ lies in the plane spanned by $\mathbf{B}$ and $\mathbf{C}$.

> This identity appears constantly in electromagnetism ($\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E})$) and fluid mechanics.

## 12. Worked Examples — Cross Product

### Example 1 — Computing a Cross Product

Let $\mathbf{u} = \langle 1, 2, 3 \rangle$ and $\mathbf{v} = \langle -1, 1, 0 \rangle$:

$$ \mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & 2 & 3 \ -1 & 1 & 0 \end{vmatrix} $$

$$ = \mathbf{i}(2\cdot0 - 3\cdot1) - \mathbf{j}(1\cdot0 - 3\cdot(-1)) + \mathbf{k}(1\cdot1 - 2\cdot(-1)) $$

$$ = \mathbf{i}(-3) - \mathbf{j}(3) + \mathbf{k}(3) = \langle -3, -3, 3 \rangle $$

**Verify perpendicularity:** $\langle -3,-3,3\rangle \cdot \langle 1,2,3\rangle = -3-6+9 = 0$ ✓ $\langle -3,-3,3\rangle \cdot \langle -1,1,0\rangle = 3-3+0 = 0$ ✓

### Example 2 — Area of a Triangle

Find the area of the triangle with vertices $A = (1,0,0)$, $B = (0,2,0)$, $C = (0,0,3)$:

$$ \overrightarrow{AB} = \langle -1,2,0\rangle, \quad \overrightarrow{AC} = \langle -1,0,3\rangle $$

$$ \overrightarrow{AB}\times\overrightarrow{AC} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\-1&2&0\-1&0&3\end{vmatrix} = \langle 6, 3, 2\rangle $$

$$ \text{Area} = \tfrac{1}{2}|\langle 6,3,2\rangle| = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{36+9+4} = \tfrac{1}{2}\sqrt{49} = \frac{7}{2} $$

### Example 3 — Volume of a Parallelepiped

$\mathbf{u} = \langle 1,0,0\rangle$, $\mathbf{v} = \langle 1,1,0\rangle$, $\mathbf{w} = \langle 1,1,1\rangle$:

$$ V = \left|,\det\begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\1&1&0\1&1&1\end{pmatrix}\right| = |1\cdot(1\cdot1-0\cdot1)| = 1 $$

## 13. Dot vs. Cross — Side-by-Side Comparison

```
SAME INPUT: u = <1, 0, 0>,  v = <0, 1, 0>  (perpendicular unit vectors)

DOT PRODUCT:                    CROSS PRODUCT:
u · v = 0                       u × v = k = <0, 0, 1>
→ Zero because θ = 90°          → Maximum magnitude (sin 90° = 1)
→ Captures: no alignment        → Captures: maximal area swept

SAME INPUT: u = <1, 0, 0>,  v = <2, 0, 0>  (parallel vectors)

DOT PRODUCT:                    CROSS PRODUCT:
u · v = 2                       u × v = <0, 0, 0>
→ Maximum (cos 0° = 1)          → Zero (sin 0° = 0)
→ Captures: perfect alignment   → Captures: no area, degenerate
```

**The duality:** the dot product maximizes when vectors agree, the cross product maximizes when they disagree. They are "complementary sensors."

## 14. Connections to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 14.1 The Dot Product is Everywhere in Neural Networks

Every forward pass in a neural network is fundamentally repeated dot products. A single neuron computes: $$ z = \mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x} + b = \sum_{i} w_i x_i + b $$

The dot product measures how much the input vector $\mathbf{x}$ aligns with the learned weight vector $\mathbf{w}$. A neuron **activates strongly** precisely when its weight vector points in nearly the same direction as the input — the geometric picture from §3.

**Efficiency:** Modern hardware (GPUs/TPUs) is physically optimized to compute dot products in parallel (matrix multiplication = batched dot products). The dot product's simplicity is what makes deep learning computationally feasible at scale.

### 14.2 Cosine Similarity — Semantic Search and Embeddings

From the geometric formula, **cosine similarity** isolates direction and discards magnitude: $$ \text{cosim}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|} $$

This is the dot product of the **normalized** vectors, i.e., unit vectors. It measures purely _directional_ agreement.

**Applications:**

- **Word2Vec / GloVe:** The famous `king - man + woman ≈ queen` result is vector addition/subtraction; nearness is measured by cosine similarity.
- **BERT embeddings:** Sentences mapped to $\mathbb{R}^{768}$ vectors; semantic similarity is cosine similarity.
- **Vector databases (Pinecone, Weaviate, Chroma):** Store embeddings of documents; a user query is embedded and the top-$k$ results are the $k$ vectors with highest cosine similarity. The entire retrieval step in Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) pipelines is a batched dot product search.

### 14.3 Self-Attention — Dot Products Scaled Up

The Transformer's attention mechanism: $$ \text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V $$

$QK^\top$ is a matrix of **pairwise dot products**: entry $(i,j)$ is $\mathbf{q}_i \cdot \mathbf{k}_j$ — how much query token $i$ attends to key token $j$. This is exactly cosine-like: the higher the dot product, the more attention is paid.

The $\sqrt{d_k}$ scaling prevents dot products from becoming too large in high dimensions (where even near-orthogonal vectors accumulate non-negligible dot products), which would saturate softmax and kill gradients. This is the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality being respected in practice.

### 14.4 Gradient as a Dot Product — Directional Derivatives

The directional derivative of a scalar field $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ in direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$: $$ D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f = \nabla f \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}} $$

This is a dot product. The gradient $\nabla f$ (a vector in parameter space) is the direction of _steepest ascent_. The rate of change in any other direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is the projection of $\nabla f$ onto $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ — exactly $|\nabla f|\cos\theta$.

**Gradient descent** works because moving _opposite_ to $\nabla f$ (i.e., choosing $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = -\hat{\nabla f}$) maximizes the _decrease_ in $f$: $$ D_{-\hat{\nabla f}} f = \nabla f \cdot (-\hat{\nabla f}) = -|\nabla f| $$ This is the minimum possible directional derivative (by Cauchy-Schwarz).

> The entire theory of gradient descent is the statement: **the dot product is minimized when the angle is $\pi$**.

### 14.5 The Cross Product in 3D Deep Learning

Cross products appear when learning from or generating 3D geometric data:

- **Surface normals in 3D vision:** Given two tangent vectors on a surface mesh, their cross product gives the normal — used in lighting models, physics simulations, and 3D generative models.
- **NeRF (Neural Radiance Fields):** Ray marching through 3D scenes; computing intersections and normals uses cross product geometry.
- **Rotation representations:** Angular velocity $\boldsymbol{\omega}$ of a rotating body gives instantaneous linear velocity $\mathbf{v} = \boldsymbol{\omega} \times \mathbf{r}$. Robot learning agents modeling 3D kinematics use this constantly.
- **Graph Neural Networks on point clouds:** Local coordinate frames at each point are defined using cross products of estimated tangent vectors.

### 14.6 PCA — Projection via Eigenvectors and Dot Products

**Principal Component Analysis** (the most fundamental dimensionality reduction technique):

1. Center the data: subtract the mean from every data vector.
2. Compute the covariance matrix $\Sigma = \frac{1}{n} X^\top X$ — every entry is a dot product.
3. Find eigenvectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots$ of $\Sigma$.
4. **Project** data onto the top eigenvectors: $z_i = \mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{v}_i$ — a dot product.

PCA is Gram-Schmidt (orthogonal decomposition, §4.3) applied to data, driven by dot products.

## 15. Summary

### Dot Product

$$ \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \sum u_i v_i = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta $$

- Scalar output. Works in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
- Zero $\iff$ orthogonal. Measures alignment.
- Powers: projections, angles, work, attention mechanisms, gradient descent, similarity search.

### Cross Product

$$ \mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = \det\begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\u_1&u_2&u_3\v_1&v_2&v_3\end{pmatrix} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\sin\theta;\hat{\mathbf{n}} $$

- Vector output. Only in $\mathbb{R}^3$.
- Zero $\iff$ parallel. Measures area, perpendicular axis.
- Powers: surface normals, torques, plane equations, 3D vision.

### The Deep Connection

$$ |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 = (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 + |\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}|^2 $$

(Lagrange's identity.) In words: the "total interaction" between two vectors splits into a _scalar_ part (dot product — alignment) and a _vector_ part (cross product — perpendicularity). They are complementary, and together they capture the complete relationship between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.

2. **Prove** that $(\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v})\cdot(\mathbf{w}\times\mathbf{z}) = (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{w})(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{z}) - (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{z})(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{w})$.
    
3. Show that the distance from point $P$ to the line through $A$ with direction $\hat{\mathbf{d}}$ is $|\overrightarrow{AP} - (\overrightarrow{AP}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{d}})\hat{\mathbf{d}}|$, or equivalently $\frac{|\overrightarrow{AP}\times\mathbf{d}|}{|\mathbf{d}|}$.
    
4. **ML connection:** If $\mathbf{w}$ and $\mathbf{x}$ are both unit vectors (normalized), show that $\mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{x} = \cos\theta$. What is the maximum value of the dot product, and when is it achieved? What does this mean for a neuron with normalized weight and input vectors?
    
5. Prove the polarization identity: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{4}(|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}|^2 - |\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}|^2)$. (The dot product is determined by the norm — this is why knowing all distances in a vector space is equivalent to knowing the inner product.)

## 17. Looking Forward

- **Lines and Planes (Stewart §12.5):** Normal vectors to planes are found via the cross product. The plane through $P_0$ with normal $\mathbf{n}$ is $\mathbf{n}\cdot(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0) = 0$ — a dot product equation.
- **Calculus of Vector Fields (MIT 18.02, Week 8–9):** Flux integrals $\iint \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$ are dot products integrated over surfaces. Curl uses the cross product: $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$.
- **Linear Algebra:** The dot product is the standard inner product; generalizing it yields Hilbert spaces, used in functional analysis and quantum mechanics.
- **Deep Learning Theory:** Kernel methods generalize the dot product to nonlinear similarity. The kernel trick embeds data into a high-dimensional feature space and computes dot products there implicitly.
