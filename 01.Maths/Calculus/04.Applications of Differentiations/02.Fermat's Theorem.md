

> _"To find the maxima and minima of a quantity, one takes the ordinary derivative and sets it equal to zero."_ _— Pierre de Fermat, c. 1638 (paraphrase)_
> 
> _Personal note: Fermat wrote this before calculus existed — before Newton, before Leibniz. He was computing what we now call derivatives using an ad-hoc "method of adequality." The theorem bearing his name is the rigorous formalization of his geometric intuition: at a peak or valley, the tangent line must be horizontal._

---

## §1. Motivation — What Does Extremum Mean Geometrically?

Stand on a smooth hill. At the very top, the ground beneath your feet is **flat** — the slope is zero in every direction. If you're in a valley, same thing. The tangent plane is horizontal.

In one dimension: if $f$ has a local max at $c$, then just to the left of $c$ the function is _increasing_ ($f' > 0$), and just to the right it is _decreasing_ ($f' < 0$). At the transition point itself, $f'(c) = 0$.

```
        f(c) ← local max
       /     \
      /        \       f' changes + → −
─────              ─────
    f' > 0   f'=0   f' < 0
```

Fermat's Theorem makes this intuition a **theorem** — rigorous, with clear hypotheses.

---

## §2. Statement of the Theorem

> **Theorem (Fermat's Theorem).** Let $f$ be defined on an open interval containing $c$. Suppose:
> 
> 1. $f$ has a **local extremum** at $c$ (either a local max or local min), and
> 2. $f$ is **differentiable** at $c$.
> 
> Then $f'(c) = 0$.

**Logically:** $$\bigl(f \text{ has local extremum at } c\bigr) ;\wedge; \bigl(f'(c) \text{ exists}\bigr) ;\implies; f'(c) = 0$$

---

## §3. Complete Proof

We prove the local maximum case. The local minimum case is symmetric (or apply the result to $-f$).

**Setup.** Suppose $f$ has a local maximum at $c$. By definition, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that: $$f(x) \leq f(c) \quad \text{for all } x \in (c - \delta,; c + \delta). \tag{$*$}$$

We are given that $f'(c)$ exists, so the limit $$f'(c) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(c+h) - f(c)}{h}$$ exists (as a finite real number). Since the two-sided limit exists, the one-sided limits both exist and equal $f'(c)$.

---

**Case 1: Right-hand limit** ($h \to 0^+$, i.e., $h > 0$ small).

For $0 < h < \delta$, we have $c + h \in (c - \delta, c + \delta)$, so by $(*)$: $$f(c + h) - f(c) \leq 0.$$

Since $h > 0$: $$\frac{f(c+h) - f(c)}{h} \leq 0.$$

Taking the limit as $h \to 0^+$ (the inequality is preserved under limits): $$f'(c) = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{h} \leq 0. \tag{1}$$

---

**Case 2: Left-hand limit** ($h \to 0^-$, i.e., $h < 0$ small).

For $-\delta < h < 0$, we have $c + h \in (c - \delta, c + \delta)$, so by $(*)$: $$f(c+h) - f(c) \leq 0.$$

But now $h < 0$, so dividing reverses the inequality: $$\frac{f(c+h) - f(c)}{h} \geq 0.$$

Taking the limit as $h \to 0^-$: $$f'(c) = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{f(c+h)-f(c)}{h} \geq 0. \tag{2}$$

---

**Conclusion.** From (1) and (2): $$f'(c) \leq 0 \quad \text{and} \quad f'(c) \geq 0 \implies \boxed{f'(c) = 0.}$$

For the local minimum case: the inequality $(*)$ reverses to $f(x) \geq f(c)$, which flips the signs in Cases 1 and 2, and we again conclude $f'(c) = 0$. $\blacksquare$

---

## §4. The Converse is FALSE — This is Critical

> **$f'(c) = 0$ does NOT imply $c$ is an extremum.**

This is one of the most common errors. Fermat's theorem is a **one-way implication only**.

### Counterexample: $f(x) = x^3$ at $c = 0$

$$f'(x) = 3x^2 \implies f'(0) = 0.$$

Yet $f$ is _strictly increasing_ on all of $\mathbb{R}$: for $x > 0$, $f(x) > 0 = f(0)$, and for $x < 0$, $f(x) < 0 = f(0)$. So $x = 0$ is neither a local max nor a local min — it is an **inflection point**.

```
  f(x) = x³

       │      /
       │     /
       │    /
  ─────┼───────── x
       │  /
       │ /
       │/
         ↑
    f'(0) = 0, but NOT an extremum
    (function strictly increasing through 0)
```

### Summary of Logical Structure

|Statement|True?|
|---|---|
|Local extremum at $c$ + differentiable $\Rightarrow$ $f'(c)=0$|✓ **Fermat's Theorem**|
|$f'(c) = 0$ $\Rightarrow$ local extremum at $c$|✗ **FALSE** ($x^3$ counterexample)|
|$f'(c) = 0$ $\Rightarrow$ $c$ is a critical number|✓ (by definition)|
|Local extremum at $c$ $\Rightarrow$ $c$ is a critical number|✓ (Fermat + non-diff. case)|

The correct chain is: $$\text{local extremum} \implies \text{critical number} \centernot\impliedby \text{critical number}$$

---

## §5. Why the Hypothesis "Differentiable at $c$" is Essential

Fermat's theorem **requires** $f'(c)$ to exist. Drop this and the conclusion fails.

### Example: $f(x) = |x|$ at $c = 0$

$f$ has an absolute (and local) minimum at $c = 0$. But: $$f'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|h| - 0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|h|}{h}$$

The right limit is $+1$, the left limit is $-1$ — they disagree. So $f'(0)$ **does not exist**.

The minimum occurs at a **corner**, not a smooth point. There is no horizontal tangent — there is no tangent line at all.

```
  f(x) = |x|

   \   /
    \ /
     *  ← global min, f'(0) DNE
     |
─────┼───── x
```

This is why the definition of **critical number** includes _both_ $f'(c) = 0$ _and_ $f'(c)$ DNE — to capture all possible extrema locations.

### The Full Picture: Where Can Extrema Occur?

$$\text{local extremum at } c \implies c \text{ is a critical number}$$

$$c \text{ is a critical number} \iff f'(c) = 0 ;\text{ or }; f'(c) \text{ does not exist}$$

Fermat's theorem handles the differentiable case. The non-differentiable case is separate (but the conclusion — that $c$ is a critical number — holds trivially).

---

## §6. Why the Hypothesis "Open Interval" is Essential

The theorem requires $f$ to be defined on an **open interval containing $c$** (not just at $c$, and not on a closed interval where $c$ is an endpoint).

**Why?** The proof uses the limit from _both sides_ of $c$. If $c = a$ is the left endpoint of $[a,b]$, only the right-hand limit is available:

$$f'(a^+) \leq 0$$

But we can't conclude $f'(a) \geq 0$ from the left (there is no left side). So we can't squeeze to $f'(a) = 0$.

**Example.** $f(x) = x$ on $[0, 1]$. The absolute minimum is at $x = 0$ and the absolute maximum at $x = 1$. But $f'(x) = 1 \neq 0$ everywhere — including at the endpoints. Fermat's theorem simply doesn't apply here, because $0$ and $1$ are not interior to the domain.

**Consequence.** This is precisely why the **Closed Interval Method** must check endpoints separately — Fermat doesn't cover them.

## §7. Visualization: The Full Taxonomy

```
All points c in domain of f
│
├── f'(c) exists
│   ├── f'(c) = 0  ──────────────────→ CRITICAL NUMBER
│   │   ├── sign of f' changes + → −  → local MAX    ← Fermat applies
│   │   ├── sign of f' changes − → +  → local MIN    ← Fermat applies
│   │   └── sign of f' doesn't change → INFLECTION (e.g. x³ at 0)
│   │
│   └── f'(c) ≠ 0  → NOT a critical number
│                     (cannot be an interior extremum by Fermat)
│
├── f'(c) does not exist
│   ├── corner (e.g. |x| at 0)  ──→ CRITICAL NUMBER (may be extremum)
│   ├── cusp (e.g. x^{2/3} at 0) → CRITICAL NUMBER (may be extremum)
│   └── vertical tangent         → CRITICAL NUMBER (may be extremum)
│
└── c is an ENDPOINT of [a,b]
    └── Fermat does NOT apply → check separately
```

## §8. Examples

### Example 8.1 — Polynomial, All Cases

$$f(x) = x^4 - 4x^3$$

$$f'(x) = 4x^3 - 12x^2 = 4x^2(x - 3)$$

Critical numbers: $f'(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$ or $x = 3$.

**Classify $x = 0$:**

Sign of $f'(x) = 4x^2(x-3)$ near $x=0$:

- For $x$ slightly $< 0$: $4x^2 > 0$, $(x-3) < 0$ → $f'(x) < 0$
- For $x$ slightly $> 0$: $4x^2 > 0$, $(x-3) < 0$ → $f'(x) < 0$

Sign does **not** change → $x = 0$ is **not an extremum** (inflection point). $f'(0) = 0$, Fermat's conclusion holds, but the converse is false.

**Classify $x = 3$:**

- For $x < 3$: $4x^2 > 0$, $(x-3) < 0$ → $f'(x) < 0$
- For $x > 3$: $4x^2 > 0$, $(x-3) > 0$ → $f'(x) > 0$

Sign changes $- \to +$ → $x = 3$ is a **local minimum**.

```
f'(x) sign chart:
      − − − 0 − − − 0 + + +
      ──────┼───────┼──────
            0       3
         inflec.  loc min
```

### Example 8.2 — Non-differentiable Extremum

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} -x + 1 & x < 0 \ x^2 + 1 & x \geq 0 \end{cases}$$

At $c = 0$: $$\lim_{h\to 0^-} \frac{f(h)-f(0)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^-} \frac{(-h+1)-1}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^-} \frac{-h}{h} = -1$$ $$\lim_{h\to 0^+} \frac{f(h)-f(0)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^+} \frac{h^2+1-1}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0^+} h = 0$$

One-sided limits disagree: $f'(0)$ **DNE**. Fermat's theorem does not apply. Yet $f(0) = 1$ is a local minimum (approaching from left, $f \to 1^+$; from right, $f \to 1^+$ through $x^2+1 \geq 1$).

### Example 8.3 — Verifying Fermat Directly

$$f(x) = \sin x, \quad c = \frac{\pi}{2}$$

$f'(x) = \cos x$, so $f'(\pi/2) = \cos(\pi/2) = 0$. ✓

$f(\pi/2) = 1$ is a local (and global) maximum on any interval containing $\pi/2$. Fermat's theorem confirms $f'(\pi/2) = 0$, consistent.

## §9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, Deep Learning

> _Fermat's Theorem is the theoretical certificate that gradient descent is doing the right thing — and also explains why it can get stuck._

### 9.1 The Training Objective

In machine learning, we minimize a loss: $$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{m}\sum_{i=1}^m \ell(f_\theta(x^{(i)}), y^{(i)}), \quad \theta \in \mathbb{R}^n$$

At any interior local minimizer $\theta^_$ where $\mathcal{L}$ is differentiable, Fermat's theorem (in $\mathbb{R}^n$) gives: $$\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta^_) = \mathbf{0}$$

This is the necessary condition gradient descent tries to satisfy. Every weight update moves toward making this gradient zero.

### 9.2 The Converse Failure = The Saddle Point Problem

The converse of Fermat's theorem fails in 1D with $x^3$. In high-dimensional ML it fails catastrophically at **saddle points**.

A saddle point $\theta^_$ satisfies $\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta^_) = \mathbf{0}$ but is neither a max nor a min — it's a max in some directions and a min in others. In $\mathbb{R}^n$ for large $n$, saddle points are **exponentially more common** than local minima.

```
  Saddle point in 2D:
  
    θ₂
    │    ╲   ╱   ← increasing in θ₂ direction (local max)
    │     ╲ ╱
    │      ×  ← ∇L = 0 here, but saddle
    │     ╱ ╲
    │    ╱   ╲   ← decreasing in θ₁ direction (local min)
    └──────────── θ₁
```

**Consequence for training:** Gradient descent near a saddle point has gradient $\approx \mathbf{0}$, so updates become tiny — training _stalls_. This is a major practical problem in deep learning.

**Solutions used in practice:**

- **Momentum** (SGD with momentum): carries velocity through saddle regions
- **Adam**: uses second moment estimates to escape flat regions
- **Noise** in SGD (from random minibatches): perturbs away from saddle points

All of these are engineering responses to the failure of the converse of Fermat's theorem.

### 9.3 ReLU and Non-differentiable Points in Neural Networks

The **ReLU activation** $\sigma(x) = \max(0, x)$ is not differentiable at $x = 0$: $$\sigma'(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x > 0 \ 0 & x < 0 \ \text{undefined} & x = 0 \end{cases}$$

This is the neural network version of $|x|$ — a corner. In practice, we use the **subgradient** (set $\sigma'(0) = 0$ or $0.5$ by convention). Fermat's theorem doesn't directly apply at these points, but the critical number concept extends to the non-smooth setting via **subdifferential calculus**.

The entire field of **non-smooth optimization** — used when training networks with $\ell_1$ regularization, ReLU activations, max-pooling — is a generalization of the observation that extrema can occur where $f'$ fails to exist.

### 9.4 Fermat's Theorem in Convex Optimization

For **convex** loss functions (e.g., logistic regression, ridge regression), Fermat's theorem becomes **if and only if**:

> $\theta^_$ is a global minimum $\iff$ $\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta^_) = \mathbf{0}$

This is because for convex differentiable functions, every critical point is a global minimum (there are no saddle points, no local-but-not-global minima). This is the reason logistic regression and linear SVM have _convergence guarantees_ — gradient descent provably reaches the global minimum.

For non-convex deep networks, only the $\Rightarrow$ direction holds (Fermat), not $\Leftarrow$.

|$f'(c)=0 \centernot\Rightarrow$ extremum|Zero gradient ≠ trained model (saddle points!)|

## §10. The Contrapositive — A Useful Form

Fermat's theorem by contrapositive:

$$f'(c) \neq 0 \implies c \text{ is NOT a local extremum (for interior points).}$$

**This is algorithmically useful.** If you're doing gradient descent and $|\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta)| > \varepsilon$, you can certify: $\theta$ is not (approximately) a local minimum. Training should continue.

Conversely, $|\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta)| \approx 0$ is a _necessary_ condition for convergence — but not sufficient (saddle point!). This is why modern optimizers also monitor the **loss value** and **validation metrics**, not just the gradient norm.

## §11. Connections Within Calculus

```
Fermat's Theorem
        │
        ├──→ Definition of Critical Numbers
        │           │
        │           ├──→ Closed Interval Method (absolute extrema)
        │           └──→ First Derivative Test (local extrema)
        │
        ├──→ Second Derivative Test
        │     (f'(c)=0 + f''(c)>0 → local min; classifies critical pts)
        │
        ├──→ Rolle's Theorem
        │     (f(a)=f(b) → ∃c: f'(c)=0; uses Fermat in proof)
        │
        └──→ Mean Value Theorem
              (follows from Rolle's; one of the deepest theorems in calculus)
```

**Rolle's Theorem proof sketch using Fermat.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $f(a) = f(b)$: by EVT, $f$ attains its max and min on $[a,b]$. If both occur at endpoints, $f$ is constant and $f' \equiv 0$ (any $c$ works). Otherwise, an extremum occurs at some interior $c \in (a,b)$, and by Fermat, $f'(c) = 0$. $\blacksquare$

Fermat's theorem is thus the **seed** from which Rolle's and the MVT grow.

## §12. Key Points — Professor's Summary

> 1. **The theorem:** differentiable local extremum $\Rightarrow$ zero derivative. The tangent is horizontal.
>     
> 2. **The proof idea:** squeeze the derivative between $\leq 0$ (from the right) and $\geq 0$ (from the left). Both sides of the derivative limit must agree, forcing $f'(c)=0$.
>     
> 3. **The converse is false.** $f'(c)=0$ only gives a _candidate_. Test the sign of $f'$ around $c$, or use the second derivative test.
>     
> 4. **Two hypotheses matter:** differentiability at $c$ (corners can be extrema without $f'=0$), and $c$ being interior (endpoints escape Fermat).
>     
> 5. **In ML:** this theorem is why $\nabla \mathcal{L} = \mathbf{0}$ is the training convergence condition — and why convergence to zero gradient is necessary but not sufficient for a good model.
>
