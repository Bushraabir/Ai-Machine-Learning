## 0. Why This Matters (Personal Note)

Every time a neural network trains, every time a robot finds the shortest path, every time an engineer minimises drag on a wing вЂ” _optimisation_ is happening. This note builds the full story: from single-variable calculus up through Lagrange multipliers and into the gradient-descent world of machine learning.

## 1. The Setup: What Is an Optimisation Problem?

We want to find the **maximum** or **minimum** value of a function $f$ subject to some domain or constraint. Formally:

$$\text{Find } x^* \in D \text{ such that } f(x^*) \leq f(x) ; \forall x \in D \quad \text{(minimiser)}$$

**Three types of optima:**

|Type|Notation|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|Global (absolute) max|$f(c) \geq f(x)$ for all $x \in D$|The tallest peak|
|Global (absolute) min|$f(c) \leq f(x)$ for all $x \in D$|The deepest valley|
|Local (relative) max/min|Only within some open interval around $c$|A local hill/pit|

> **Remark (Professor's margin note):** Every global extremum is also a local one, but not vice versa. The distinction matters enormously in ML вЂ” gradient descent finds _local_ minima, not necessarily global ones.

---

## 2. The Extreme Value Theorem (EVT)

**Theorem (Stewart В§4.1):** If $f$ is _continuous_ on a _closed_ interval $[a,b]$, then $f$ attains both an absolute maximum and minimum on $[a,b]$.

**Why the hypotheses are non-negotiable:**

- $f(x) = 1/x$ on $(0,1)$ вЂ” open interval, no minimum (approaches $+\infty$).
- $f(x) = \begin{cases} x & x \neq 0 \ 5 & x = 0 \end{cases}$ on $[-1,1]$ вЂ” discontinuous at $0$, the theorem fails.

> The EVT guarantees _existence_. It says nothing about _where_ or _how many_ extrema there are.

## 3. Critical Points: The Heart of the Matter

**Definition:** $c$ is a **critical number** of $f$ if either:

1. $f'(c) = 0$, or
2. $f'(c)$ does not exist.

**Fermat's Theorem:** If $f$ has a local extremum at $c$, then $c$ is a critical number of $f$.

> **The converse is false.** $f(x) = x^3$ has $f'(0) = 0$ but $x=0$ is an inflection point, not an extremum.

### 3.1 Closed Interval Method

To find absolute extrema of $f$ on $[a,b]$:

1. Find all critical numbers $c_i \in (a,b)$.
2. Evaluate $f(c_i)$, $f(a)$, $f(b)$.
3. The largest value is the absolute max, the smallest is the absolute min.

**Example:** Find the absolute extrema of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 + 1$ on $[-\tfrac{1}{2}, 4]$.

$$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x = 3x(x-2) = 0 \implies x = 0, ; x = 2$$

|$x$|$f(x)$|
|---|---|
|$-1/2$|$-1/8 - 3/4 + 1 = 1/8$|
|$0$|$1$|
|$2$|$8 - 12 + 1 = -3$|
|$4$|$64 - 48 + 1 = 17$|

**Absolute max:** $f(4) = 17$. **Absolute min:** $f(2) = -3$.

## 4. The First and Second Derivative Tests

### 4.1 First Derivative Test

Suppose $c$ is a critical number of a _continuous_ $f$:

- $f' $ changes $+ \to -$ at $c$ $\Rightarrow$ **local max** at $c$.
- $f'$ changes $- \to +$ at $c$ $\Rightarrow$ **local min** at $c$.
- $f'$ does not change sign $\Rightarrow$ **neither**.

### 4.2 Second Derivative Test

Suppose $f''$ is continuous near $c$ and $f'(c) = 0$:

- $f''(c) > 0 \Rightarrow$ **local min** (concave up вЂ” like $\cup$).
- $f''(c) < 0 \Rightarrow$ **local max** (concave down вЂ” like $\cap$).
- $f''(c) = 0 \Rightarrow$ **inconclusive**. Revert to first derivative test.

**Why it works:** Near a critical point, $f(x) \approx f(c) + \frac{1}{2}f''(c)(x-c)^2$ (Taylor). The sign of $f''(c)$ determines whether this parabolic approximation opens up or down.

> **ML Connection:** In neural network loss landscapes, the second derivative (Hessian) tells us the local curvature. Training is harder in directions of high curvature вЂ” this is why **adaptive optimisers** like Adam scale learning rates per parameter.

## 6. Applied Optimisation: The Method

When faced with a word problem:

1. **Draw and label** a diagram.
2. **Express the objective function** $Q$ in terms of one variable (use constraint equations to eliminate extras).
3. **Determine the domain** вЂ” is it a closed interval? Open?
4. **Differentiate** and find critical numbers.
5. **Classify** using the second derivative test or endpoint comparison.
6. **Answer the question** in context.

### 6.1 Worked Example: The Box Problem

_"A box with a square base and open top must hold $32000 , \text{cm}^3$. Find dimensions that minimise surface area."_

Let side $= x$, height $= h$. Constraint: $x^2 h = 32000$, so $h = 32000/x^2$.

$$S = x^2 + 4xh = x^2 + \frac{128000}{x}$$

$$S'(x) = 2x - \frac{128000}{x^2} = 0 \implies x^3 = 64000 \implies x = 40 , \text{cm}$$

$$S''(x) = 2 + \frac{256000}{x^3} > 0 \quad \forall x > 0 \implies \text{local (and global) min}$$

So $x = 40, \text{cm}$, $h = 32000/1600 = 20, \text{cm}$.

> **Insight:** The optimal box has height exactly half the base side. This "ratio result" comes up repeatedly in geometric optimisation.

## 7. Multivariable Optimisation (MIT 18.02)

Now $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$. The philosophy is the same, but the machinery is richer.

### 7.1 The Gradient and Critical Points

**Definition:** The **gradient** of $f(x,y)$ is: $$\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x},, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)$$

**Critical points:** $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$, i.e., all partial derivatives vanish simultaneously.

### 7.2 The Second Derivative Test for $f(x,y)$

At a critical point $(a,b)$, define: $$D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2 \qquad \text{(the discriminant / Hessian determinant)}$$

|Condition|Conclusion|
|---|---|
|$D > 0$ and $f_{xx} > 0$|Local minimum|
|$D > 0$ and $f_{xx} < 0$|Local maximum|
|$D < 0$|Saddle point|
|$D = 0$|Inconclusive|

**Example:** $f(x,y) = x^4 + y^4 - 4xy$.

$$f_x = 4x^3 - 4y = 0 \implies y = x^3$$ $$f_y = 4y^3 - 4x = 0 \implies x = y^3$$

Substituting: $x = (x^3)^3 = x^9 \implies x^9 - x = 0 \implies x(x^8 - 1) = 0$.

Critical points: $(0,0)$, $(1,1)$, $(-1,-1)$.

At $(1,1)$: $f_{xx} = 12$, $f_{yy} = 12$, $f_{xy} = -4$, so $D = 144 - 16 = 128 > 0$ and $f_{xx} > 0$ в†’ **local min**.

At $(0,0)$: $D = 0 \cdot 0 - 16 = -16 < 0$ в†’ **saddle point**.

## 8. Constrained Optimisation: Lagrange Multipliers

**Problem:** Optimise $f(x,y)$ subject to the constraint $g(x,y) = k$.

**The Lagrange Condition (MIT 18.02):** At a constrained extremum,

$$\boxed{\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g}$$

for some scalar $\lambda$ called the **Lagrange multiplier**. Together with $g(x,y) = k$, this gives a system of equations.

**Geometric intuition:** The level curves of $f$ and $g$ must be _tangent_ at the optimum вЂ” otherwise, you could move along $g = k$ to increase/decrease $f$. Tangency means the gradients are parallel, i.e., $\nabla f \parallel \nabla g$.

```
          Level curves of f (dashed)
          Constraint g = k (solid curve)

          ------- f = 3 -------
         /                      \
        /   --- f = 5 ---         \
       /   /             \         \
      |   |    f = 7 *<-------- tangency point: в€‡f в€Ґ в€‡g
       \   \             /         /
        \   --- f = 5 ---         /
         \                      /
          ------- f = 3 -------
             \___ g = k ___/
```

### 8.1 Worked Example: Lagrange

_"Maximise $f(x,y) = xy$ subject to $x + y = 10$."_

$g(x,y) = x + y$, $k = 10$.

$$\nabla f = (y, x), \quad \nabla g = (1,1)$$

Lagrange conditions: $y = \lambda$, $x = \lambda$, so $x = y$. With $x + y = 10$: $x = y = 5$.

$$f(5,5) = 25 \quad \leftarrow \text{maximum}$$

> **Note:** This is the AM-GM inequality in disguise: $xy \leq \left(\frac{x+y}{2}\right)^2$, with equality iff $x=y$.

### 8.2 Three Variables

Optimise $f(x,y,z)$ subject to $g(x,y,z) = k$:

$$\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \begin{cases} f_x = \lambda g_x \ f_y = \lambda g_y \ f_z = \lambda g_z \ g = k \end{cases}$$

Four equations, four unknowns: $x, y, z, \lambda$.

**Interpretation of $\lambda$:** $\lambda = \dfrac{df^*}{dk}$, the _rate of change of the optimal value_ as the constraint relaxes. In economics, $\lambda$ is the **shadow price**.

## 9. Convexity: When Local = Global

**Definition:** $f$ is **convex** if for all $x, y$ and $t \in [0,1]$: $$f(tx + (1-t)y) \leq tf(x) + (1-t)f(y)$$

The chord between any two points lies _above_ the graph.

**Key theorem:** For a convex $f$ on a convex domain, every local minimum is a global minimum.

**For differentiable $f$:** $f$ is convex iff $f''(x) \geq 0$ (single variable) or the Hessian $H_f \succeq 0$ (positive semidefinite, multivariable).

> **This is why convex optimisation is so powerful.** Any algorithm that finds a critical point is guaranteed to find the global optimum. Loss functions like mean squared error (MSE) in linear regression are convex вЂ” that's why normal equations give the exact global minimum.

## 10. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

This is where the pure mathematics becomes breathtakingly applied.

### 10.1 The Loss Landscape

In supervised learning, we have a **loss function**: $$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f_\theta(x_i), y_i)$$

where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^p$ are the model parameters ($p$ can be _billions_ in modern LLMs). Training = **minimising** $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$.

This is a high-dimensional optimisation problem. The landscape is:

- **Non-convex** (many local minima, saddle points, flat regions).
- **Extremely high-dimensional** (millions to billions of parameters).
- **Expensive to evaluate exactly** (billions of data points).

### 10.2 Gradient Descent

The fundamental algorithm: $$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the **learning rate**. This is the continuous analogue of "walk downhill in the direction of steepest descent."

**Why $-\nabla f$?** Because $\nabla f$ points in the direction of _greatest increase_ of $f$ (it is orthogonal to level surfaces and points outward). To minimise, we move _against_ the gradient.

**Variants:**

|Algorithm|Update Rule|Key Idea|
|---|---|---|
|Vanilla GD|$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}$|Exact gradient, slow|
|SGD|$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}_{\text{mini-batch}}$|Noisy but fast|
|Momentum|$v \leftarrow \beta v - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}$; $\theta \leftarrow \theta + v$|Escapes shallow minima|
|Adam|Adaptive per-parameter learning rates using first & second moments|State of the art|

### 10.3 The Hessian in Deep Learning

The **Hessian** $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$ (matrix of second partial derivatives) determines the local curvature. At a critical point:

- All eigenvalues of $H > 0$ в†’ local minimum.
- Some eigenvalues $< 0$ в†’ saddle point.
- Some eigenvalues $= 0$ в†’ flat directions (degenerate case).

In deep learning, most critical points found are **saddle points**, not local minima вЂ” but this turns out to be fine in practice, as saddle points in high dimensions are often surrounded by flat regions with similarly low loss.

> **Deep Result (Dauphin et al., 2014):** In high-dimensional non-convex landscapes, local minima are rare. Most critical points are saddle points. This is why neural networks train successfully despite non-convexity.

### 10.4 Lagrange Multipliers in ML: Constrained Learning

Lagrange multipliers appear directly in:

**Support Vector Machines (SVM):** Maximise the margin $2/|\mathbf{w}|$ subject to $y_i(\mathbf{w} \cdot x_i + b) \geq 1$. Via Lagrange duality, the dual problem involves multipliers $\alpha_i$ (one per training point), and only support vectors have $\alpha_i > 0$.

**Constrained policy optimisation (RL):** In reinforcement learning, we maximise expected reward subject to safety constraints вЂ” precisely a Lagrangian optimisation problem.

**Normalisation:** BatchNorm, LayerNorm force activations to have zero mean and unit variance вЂ” a constraint. The learnable scale/shift parameters are the degrees of freedom within this constraint.

### 10.5 Convexity in ML

|Convex|Non-convex|
|---|---|
|Linear regression (MSE)|Neural networks|
|Logistic regression|Autoencoders|
|SVMs|GANs|
|Lasso / Ridge|Transformers|

For convex problems, gradient descent converges to the _global_ minimum. For non-convex problems (deep learning), we hope and empirically verify that stochastic gradient descent finds "good enough" minima вЂ” and in practice, it does remarkably well.

> **The punchline:** Calculus-based optimisation theory _is_ the foundation of modern AI. The gradient $\nabla \mathcal{L}$, the Hessian $H$, the critical point conditions, Lagrange multipliers вЂ” every concept from this note appears in the machinery of machine learning.

## 12. Proof Sketch: Why $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$ at Constrained Extrema

Suppose $(x_0, y_0)$ is a constrained extremum of $f$ on $g = k$, and the constraint is locally a smooth curve parametrised by $\mathbf{r}(t) = (x(t), y(t))$ with $\mathbf{r}(t_0) = (x_0, y_0)$.

Since $g(\mathbf{r}(t)) = k$ for all $t$, differentiating: $$\nabla g \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) = 0$$

So $\nabla g$ is perpendicular to the constraint curve.

Since $(x_0, y_0)$ is an extremum of $h(t) = f(\mathbf{r}(t))$: $$h'(t_0) = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t_0) = 0$$

So $\nabla f$ is also perpendicular to $\mathbf{r}'(t_0)$.

In 2D, both $\nabla f$ and $\nabla g$ are perpendicular to the same tangent vector, hence they are **parallel**: $$\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g \qquad \blacksquare$$

## 14. Key Theorems at a Glance

> **Extreme Value Theorem:** Continuous $f$ on $[a,b]$ $\Rightarrow$ absolute extrema exist.

> **Fermat's Theorem:** Local extremum at $c$ $\Rightarrow$ $c$ is critical.

> **Lagrange:** Constrained extremum $\Rightarrow$ $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$.

> **Convex Optimisation:** $f$ convex, domain convex $\Rightarrow$ local min = global min.
