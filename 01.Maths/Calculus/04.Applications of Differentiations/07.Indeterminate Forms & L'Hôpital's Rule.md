

> _"An indeterminate form is not a number — it is a competition. Two tendencies fight each other, and the limit is the outcome of that fight. L'Hôpital's rule is the referee."_ — Personal note, drawn from Stewart §4.4 and MIT 18.02

## 0. Motivation: Why "0/0" Is Not Zero

Consider the innocent-looking limit: $$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x}$$

If you naively substitute $x = 0$: you get $\frac{0}{0}$. This is **not** the number zero. It is not $1$. It is not undefined in the sense of "no limit exists." It is **indeterminate** — the symbol $\frac{0}{0}$ carries no information about the actual limiting value. The answer could be anything: $1$, $5$, $\infty$, or the limit may not exist.

Indeed, $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$, while $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{5x}{x} = 5$, and $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x^2}{x} = 0$ — all of the form $\frac{0}{0}$.

The form itself tells you nothing. That is the point.

## 1. The Complete Taxonomy of Indeterminate Forms

There are **seven** indeterminate forms. All others reduce to one of these.

|Form|Symbol|Intuition|
|---|---|---|
|Quotient|$\dfrac{0}{0}$|Small over small — race to zero|
|Quotient|$\dfrac{\infty}{\infty}$|Large over large — race to infinity|
|Product|$0 \cdot \infty$|Tiny times huge — who wins?|
|Difference|$\infty - \infty$|Two infinities cancel — how much?|
|Power|$0^0$|Zero base, zero exponent|
|Power|$1^\infty$|Base approaching 1, exponent exploding|
|Power|$\infty^0$|Infinite base, vanishing exponent|

**Non-indeterminate forms** (these have definite values):

|Form|Value|
|---|---|
|$\frac{0}{\infty}$|$0$ (tiny over huge = zero)|
|$\frac{\infty}{0}$|$\infty$ (huge over tiny = blow up)|
|$0^\infty$|$0$|
|$\infty^\infty$|$\infty$|
|$\infty + \infty$|$\infty$|

Memorize this distinction. The non-indeterminate forms require no tools — just logic.

## 2. L'Hôpital's Rule: Statement and Proof Sketch

### Theorem 2.1 (L'Hôpital's Rule) _(Stewart §4.4)_

> Suppose $f$ and $g$ are differentiable on an open interval containing $a$ (except possibly at $a$ itself), and $g'(x) \neq 0$ near $a$. If $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x \to a} g(x) = 0$$ **or** $$\lim_{x \to a} |f(x)| = \infty \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x \to a} |g(x)| = \infty$$ then: $$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$ provided the right-hand limit exists (or is $\pm\infty$). The theorem holds equally for one-sided limits and for $a = \pm\infty$.

**The rule applies only to $\frac{0}{0}$ and $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ directly.** All other indeterminate forms must first be algebraically converted to one of these two.

### Proof Sketch (for $\frac{0}{0}$, $a$ finite)

Extend $f$ and $g$ by setting $f(a) = g(a) = 0$, making them continuous at $a$. Apply the **Cauchy Mean Value Theorem**: for any $x \neq a$, there exists $c$ between $x$ and $a$ such that:

$$\frac{f(x) - f(a)}{g(x) - g(a)} = \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}$$

Since $f(a) = g(a) = 0$, this gives $\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \dfrac{f'(c)}{g'(c)}$.

As $x \to a$, $c \to a$ (it is squeezed between $x$ and $a$), so: $$\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{c \to a} \frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)} = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)} \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Key insight**: L'Hôpital's rule is not "differentiate the fraction." It is "differentiate numerator and denominator _separately_ and form a new fraction." This is not the quotient rule.

## 3. Applying the Rule: The Two Direct Forms

### 3.1 The $\frac{0}{0}$ Form

**Example 3.1.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x}$

Check: $\sin 0 = 0$, $0 = 0$. Form is $\frac{0}{0}$. ✓

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} \overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x}{1} = \cos 0 = \boxed{1}$$

**Example 3.2.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 1} \frac{x^3 - 1}{x - 1}$

Form $\frac{0}{0}$: $$\overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to 1} \frac{3x^2}{1} = 3$$

_(Cross-check: factor as $(x-1)(x^2+x+1)/(x-1) = x^2+x+1 \to 3$. Consistent.)_

**Example 3.3 (Apply twice).** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x - 1 - x}{x^2}$

Form $\frac{0}{0}$: $$\overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x - 1}{2x} \quad \left(\frac{0}{0}\text{ again}\right) \overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{e^x}{2} = \frac{1}{2}$$

This is deeply related to the Taylor expansion: $e^x = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} + \cdots$, so $\frac{e^x - 1 - x}{x^2} \to \frac{1}{2}$.

### 3.2 The $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ Form

**Example 3.4.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x}$

Form $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$: $$\overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x}{1} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x} = 0$$

**Conclusion**: logarithms grow slower than any positive power of $x$. This is a fundamental fact in analysis and algorithm theory.

**Example 3.5.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{e^x}{x^n}$ for any fixed $n \in \mathbb{N}$

Apply L'Hôpital $n$ times: $$\frac{e^x}{x^n} \to \frac{e^x}{nx^{n-1}} \to \frac{e^x}{n(n-1)x^{n-2}} \to \cdots \to \frac{e^x}{n!} \to \infty$$

**Conclusion**: $e^x$ grows faster than any polynomial. Every time you differentiate, you get another $e^x$ on top — the exponential is inexhaustible.

### Hierarchy of Growth Rates (from these examples)

$$\ln x \ll x^a \ll e^x \ll e^{x^2} \qquad \text{as } x \to \infty$$

for any $a > 0$. This ordering is provable entirely via L'Hôpital applied to $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ forms.

## 4. Converting the Other Five Forms

The remaining five indeterminate forms must be **rewritten** into $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ before applying the rule.

### 4.1 Form $0 \cdot \infty$: Rewrite as a Fraction

If $\lim f = 0$ and $\lim g = \infty$: $$f \cdot g = \frac{f}{1/g} \quad \left(\frac{0}{0}\right) \qquad \text{or} \qquad f \cdot g = \frac{g}{1/f} \quad \left(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\right)$$

Choose whichever gives a cleaner derivative.

**Example 4.1.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x$

Form $0 \cdot (-\infty)$. Write $x \ln x = \dfrac{\ln x}{1/x}$, form $\frac{-\infty}{\infty}$:

$$\overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1/x}{-1/x^2} = \lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1/x \cdot x^2}{-1} = \lim_{x \to 0^+} (-x) = 0$$

So $x \ln x \to 0$ as $x \to 0^+$. The zero "wins" over the logarithm.

This means $f(x) = x \ln x$ extends continuously to $f(0) = 0$ — a useful fact in entropy calculations.

### 4.2 Form $\infty - \infty$: Combine into a Single Fraction

**Example 4.2.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \left(\frac{1}{\sin x} - \frac{1}{x}\right)$

Form $\infty - \infty$. Combine: $$= \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{x - \sin x}{x \sin x} \quad \left(\frac{0}{0}\right)$$

$$\overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{\sin x + x \cos x} \quad \left(\frac{0}{0}\right)$$

$$\overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{2\cos x - x \sin x} = \frac{0}{2} = 0$$

### 4.3 Power Forms $0^0$, $1^\infty$, $\infty^0$: Take the Logarithm

**Strategy**: If $L = \lim f(x)^{g(x)}$, then $\ln L = \lim g(x) \ln f(x)$, which is a $0 \cdot \infty$ form — handled by §4.1.

**Example 4.3 ($1^\infty$).** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} (1 + x)^{1/x}$

Let $L = \lim_{x \to 0}(1+x)^{1/x}$. Then: $$\ln L = \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\ln(1+x)}{x} \quad \left(\frac{0}{0}\right) \overset{H}{=} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1/(1+x)}{1} = 1$$

So $\ln L = 1$, hence $L = e^1 = e$. This is the **definition of $e$** as a limit.

**Example 4.4 ($0^0$).** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0^+} x^x$

$$\ln L = \lim_{x \to 0^+} x \ln x = 0 \quad \text{(from Example 4.1)}$$

So $L = e^0 = 1$.

**Example 4.5 ($\infty^0$).** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} x^{1/x}$

$$\ln L = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x} = 0 \quad \text{(from Example 3.4)}$$

So $L = e^0 = 1$.

## 5. Critical Warnings and Common Errors

### Warning 5.1: Check the Form First

L'Hôpital's rule applies **only** when the limit is genuinely indeterminate. Never apply it otherwise.

**Wrong:** $$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x + x}{x + 2} \overset{???}{=} \lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\cos x + 1}{1} = 2$$

The original limit: substitute $x = 0$: $\frac{0 + 0}{0 + 2} = \frac{0}{2} = 0$. Not indeterminate. The correct answer is $0$, not $2$. **L'Hôpital gave the wrong answer here.**

### Warning 5.2: Circular Reasoning for $\frac{\sin x}{x}$

The proof that $\frac{d}{dx}\sin x = \cos x$ uses $\lim_{x\to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$. Using L'Hôpital to prove that same limit is **circular**. The limit $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$ must be established geometrically first (squeeze theorem), then used to derive the derivative of $\sin x$.

### Warning 5.3: The New Limit Must Exist

The theorem states: apply L'Hôpital _if_ $\lim \frac{f'}{g'}$ exists. If it does not exist, this does **not** mean the original limit fails to exist — the original might still converge.

**Example 5.3.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x + \sin x}{x}$

Form $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$. Attempting L'Hôpital: $\frac{1 + \cos x}{1}$, which oscillates — no limit. Yet the original: $\frac{x + \sin x}{x} = 1 + \frac{\sin x}{x} \to 1$. The original limit **exists** even though L'Hôpital fails. The rule's hypothesis was not satisfied (the derivative ratio has no limit), so the rule simply does not apply — this is not a contradiction.

### Warning 5.4: Don't Loop Forever

Sometimes repeated application of L'Hôpital cycles back to the original form. Recognize this and use algebra instead.

**Example 5.4.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{e^x + e^{-x}}{e^x - e^{-x}}$

Applying L'Hôpital: $\frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}$ — the numerator and denominator have swapped. The rule will cycle indefinitely. Instead, divide numerator and denominator by $e^x$: $$\frac{1 + e^{-2x}}{1 - e^{-2x}} \to \frac{1 + 0}{1 - 0} = 1$$

## 6. Visualization: The $\frac{0}{0}$ Race

Think of $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ with $f(0) = g(0) = 0$ as asking: **which function reaches zero faster?**

```
Both start at 0 when x = 0.

f(x) = x²:    ●━━━━━━━━━━━━━━○   (reaches 0 quadratically — fast)
g(x) = x:     ●━━━━━━━━━━━━━━○   (reaches 0 linearly — slow)

Ratio x²/x = x → 0:   f reaches 0 much faster, so the ratio → 0.

f(x) = x:     ●━━━━━━━━━━━━━━○   (linear)
g(x) = x:     ●━━━━━━━━━━━━━━○   (same speed)

Ratio x/x = 1 → 1:    Dead heat.

f(x) = x:     ●━━━━━━━━━━━━━━○   (linear — slow)
g(x) = x³:   ●━━━━━━━━━━━━━━○   (cubic — fast)

Ratio x/x³ = 1/x² → ∞:  g reaches 0 far faster, ratio blows up.
```

L'Hôpital measures this "speed comparison" via the ratio of **instantaneous rates** at the collision point.

## 7. Connection to Taylor Series: The Deep Reason L'Hôpital Works

L'Hôpital's rule is, at its core, a corollary of **first-order Taylor approximation**.

Near $x = a$: $$f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a), \qquad g(x) \approx g(a) + g'(a)(x-a)$$

If $f(a) = g(a) = 0$: $$\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} \approx \frac{f'(a)(x-a)}{g'(a)(x-a)} = \frac{f'(a)}{g'(a)}$$

This is exactly L'Hôpital evaluated at $a$. The $(x - a)$ factors cancel, which is the algebraic reason the rule works.

**When $f'(a) = g'(a) = 0$ too**: you need second-order terms — apply L'Hôpital again. Each application peels off one order of the Taylor expansion.

**Example 7.1.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos x}{x^2}$

Taylor: $1 - \cos x = \frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^4}{24} + \cdots$

$$\frac{1 - \cos x}{x^2} \approx \frac{x^2/2}{x^2} = \frac{1}{2}$$

Via L'Hôpital twice: $\frac{\sin x}{2x} \to \frac{\cos x}{2} \to \frac{1}{2}$. ✓

## 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

Indeterminate forms appear throughout the mathematics of ML — often invisibly, inside definitions and derivations that practitioners take for granted.

### 8.1 Entropy and the $0 \cdot \ln 0 = 0$ Convention

In information theory, the **entropy** of a discrete distribution $p = (p_1, \ldots, p_n)$ is: $$H(p) = -\sum_{i=1}^n p_i \ln p_i$$

When $p_i = 0$, the term $0 \cdot \ln 0$ is the indeterminate form $0 \cdot (-\infty)$.

Using Example 4.1: $\lim_{p \to 0^+} p \ln p = 0$. So the convention $0 \ln 0 := 0$ is not arbitrary — it is the **exact limit**, ensuring entropy is continuous at zero probabilities.

This matters in:

- **Cross-entropy loss**: $\mathcal{L} = -\sum_i y_i \log \hat{p}_i$ — if the model outputs $\hat{p}_i = 0$ for a true class ($y_i = 1$), the loss is $\infty$. This is why softmax outputs are numerically clipped or log-sum-exp stabilized.
- **KL divergence**: $D_{KL}(P | Q) = \sum_i P_i \ln \frac{P_i}{Q_i)}$. The form $0/0$ under the log requires the convention $0 \ln(0/0) = 0$.

### 8.2 Softmax and the $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ Form

The softmax function is: $$\sigma_i(\mathbf{z}) = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}$$

When logits $z_i \to \infty$, this is the $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ form. L'Hôpital's intuition (the dominant term "wins") justifies the fact that softmax converges to a one-hot distribution as logits become extreme. More rigorously, after dividing numerator and denominator by $e^{\max_j z_j}$, the form resolves — exactly the algebraic technique of §5.4.

**Numerical overflow** in softmax is a practical manifestation of $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$: computing $e^{700}$ overflows float32. The standard fix — subtracting $\max z_j$ — is precisely the algebraic manipulation that resolves the indeterminate form before evaluating numerically.

### 8.3 The $\frac{0}{0}$ Form in Gradient Derivations

Many activation function gradients are defined as limits that hide $\frac{0}{0}$ forms:

**Sinc activation**: $\text{sinc}(x) = \frac{\sin x}{x}$, defined as $1$ at $x = 0$ by taking the limit (Example 3.1).

**Derivative of ReLU at 0**: $\text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0, x)$ is not differentiable at $0$ — the left and right derivatives disagree ($0$ vs $1$). In practice, subgradient methods assign $0$ or $1$ arbitrarily. The _reason_ we cannot resolve this via L'Hôpital is that the limit $\lim_{h\to 0} \frac{\text{ReLU}(h) - \text{ReLU}(0)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{h}{h} = 1 \neq \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{0}{h} = 0$ — the one-sided limits disagree. Not an indeterminate form problem; a genuine discontinuity in the derivative.

### 8.4 L'Hôpital-Type Reasoning in Neural Scaling Laws

The empirical **neural scaling laws** (Kaplan et al., OpenAI) describe how loss $\mathcal{L}$ behaves as compute $C \to \infty$: $$\mathcal{L}(C) \sim C^{-\alpha}$$

Comparing growth rates of different architectures involves limits of the form $\frac{C^{-\alpha}}{C^{-\beta}}$ as $C \to \infty$ — $\frac{0}{0}$ forms. L'Hôpital (equivalently, the growth hierarchy from §3) resolves these: the architecture with larger $\alpha$ dominates (loses slower = better). The mathematical structure is identical to Example 3.4.

### 8.5 The Gumbel-Softmax and $0^0$

The **Gumbel-Softmax trick** (for differentiable discrete sampling) involves: $$y_i = \frac{e^{(\log p_i + g_i)/\tau}}{\sum_j e^{(\log p_j + g_j)/\tau}}$$

As temperature $\tau \to 0$, this approaches a one-hot sample. The limiting form involves $e^{z/\tau}$ as $\tau \to 0^+$ — an $e^\infty = \infty^1$ competition — and the analysis reduces to comparing exponential growth rates, exactly the $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$ framework.

## 9. Summary

|Form|Strategy|Key example|
|---|---|---|
|$\frac{0}{0}$|Direct L'Hôpital|$\frac{\sin x}{x} \to 1$|
|$\frac{\infty}{\infty}$|Direct L'Hôpital|$\frac{\ln x}{x} \to 0$|
|$0 \cdot \infty$|Write as $\frac{f}{1/g}$|$x \ln x \to 0$|
|$\infty - \infty$|Combine into fraction|$\frac{1}{\sin x} - \frac{1}{x} \to 0$|
|$0^0, 1^\infty, \infty^0$|Take $\ln$, reduce to $0 \cdot \infty$|$(1+x)^{1/x} \to e$|

**The golden rule**: L'Hôpital applies only when the form is genuinely $\frac{0}{0}$ or $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$. Everything else is algebra first, limit second.

**The deep reason it works**: Taylor expansion. Each application of L'Hôpital peels one order off the Taylor series, revealing the dominant term.
