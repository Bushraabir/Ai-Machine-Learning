

> _"The Mean Value Theorem is the midwife of calculus — it delivers the deep connection between a function's local behavior and its global change."_ — Personal marginal note

---

## §1. Motivation — Why Should We Care?

Before the theorem, a question:

> If you drive from Dhaka to Chattogram — a distance of roughly 250 km — in 3 hours, **must** there have been some instant when your speedometer read exactly $\frac{250}{3} \approx 83.3 \text{ km/h}$?

Intuitively: **yes**. You can't average 83.3 km/h without _ever_ hitting that speed.

The Mean Value Theorem is precisely the theorem that makes this rigorous. It is, at its core, a theorem about the relationship between **average rate of change** (slope of a secant) and **instantaneous rate of change** (slope of a tangent).

---

## §2. Prerequisites

Before the MVT, we need two foundational results. I'll state them and prove the first in full.

### 2.1 Rolle's Theorem

> **Theorem (Rolle, 1691).** Let $f$ be a function satisfying:
> 
> 1. $f$ is **continuous** on $[a, b]$
> 2. $f$ is **differentiable** on $(a, b)$
> 3. $f(a) = f(b)$
> 
> Then there exists at least one $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f'(c) = 0$.

**Proof.**

Since $f$ is continuous on the closed bounded interval $[a,b]$, by the **Extreme Value Theorem** (Stewart §4.1), $f$ attains both an absolute maximum $M$ and an absolute minimum $m$ on $[a,b]$.

**Case 1:** $M = m$. Then $f$ is constant on $[a,b]$, so $f'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$. Any $c \in (a,b)$ works. $\checkmark$

**Case 2:** $M \neq m$. Since $f(a) = f(b)$, at least one of $M$ or $m$ must be attained at an interior point $c \in (a,b)$ (not just at the endpoints).

At such a $c$, $f$ has a local extremum. Since $f$ is differentiable at $c$, by **Fermat's Theorem** (Stewart §4.1): $$f'(c) = 0$$

$\square$

**Geometric picture:**

```
    f(a) = f(b)
      *-----------*
     /  horizontal \
    /   tangent     \
   /        *        \
  /        (c, f(c))  \
 a                    b
```

_Somewhere between $a$ and $b$, the curve must "turn around," and at that turning point the tangent is horizontal._

---

### 2.2 The Mean Value Theorem

> **Theorem (Lagrange's MVT).** Let $f$ be a function satisfying:
> 
> 1. $f$ is **continuous** on $[a, b]$
> 2. $f$ is **differentiable** on $(a, b)$
> 
> Then there exists at least one $c \in (a,b)$ such that: $$\boxed{f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}}$$

This is the theorem. Elegant. The right-hand side is the slope of the secant line through $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$. The theorem asserts: **the tangent line at $c$ is parallel to this secant**.

---

## §3. Proof of the MVT

The proof is a beautiful reduction to Rolle's Theorem. The key idea: **subtract the secant line** from $f$ to create a new function that satisfies Rolle's hypotheses.

**Construction.** Define the secant line through $(a, f(a))$ and $(b, f(b))$: $$L(x) = f(a) + \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x - a)$$

Now define: $$g(x) = f(x) - L(x) = f(x) - f(a) - \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(x-a)$$

**Verify Rolle's conditions:**

1. $g$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ — yes, $f$ is continuous and $L$ is a polynomial (hence continuous). ✓
2. $g$ is differentiable on $(a,b)$ — yes, $f$ is differentiable and $L'(x) = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$ exists. ✓
3. Check endpoints: $$g(a) = f(a) - f(a) - 0 = 0$$ $$g(b) = f(b) - f(a) - \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}(b-a) = f(b) - f(a) - (f(b)-f(a)) = 0$$

So $g(a) = g(b) = 0$. ✓

**Apply Rolle's Theorem:** $\exists, c \in (a,b)$ such that $g'(c) = 0$.

**Compute $g'$:** $$g'(x) = f'(x) - \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$

Setting $g'(c) = 0$: $$f'(c) - \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a} = 0 \implies f'(c) = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}$$

$\square$

**The geometric story of the proof:**

```
         f(b)
          *
         /|  ← secant line L(x)
        / |
   f(c)/  |
  ....*.  |   ← tangent parallel to secant
      /   |
     /    |
f(a)*     |
    a  c  b
```

$g(x)$ measures the _vertical gap_ between $f$ and the secant. At both endpoints this gap is 0. So somewhere in between, the gap must reach a maximum or minimum — and Rolle tells us $g'(c) = 0$ there, which is exactly $f'(c) =$ slope of secant.

## §4. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — The Driving Analogy Made Rigorous

Let $s(t)$ = position (km) at time $t$ (hours), with $s(0) = 0$, $s(3) = 250$.

Assume $s$ is continuous on $[0,3]$ and differentiable on $(0,3)$ (reasonable physical assumption).

By MVT: $\exists, c \in (0,3)$ such that: $$s'(c) = \frac{s(3)-s(0)}{3-0} = \frac{250}{3} \approx 83.3 \text{ km/h}$$

Since $s'(t)$ is velocity, **the speedometer must have read exactly 83.3 km/h at some moment $t = c$.**

This is not just intuition anymore — it's a theorem.

---

### Example 2 — A Polynomial

Let $f(x) = x^3 - x$ on $[0, 2]$.

Secant slope: $\dfrac{f(2)-f(0)}{2-0} = \dfrac{(8-2)-0}{2} = \dfrac{6}{2} = 3$

MVT guarantees $\exists, c \in (0,2)$ with $f'(c) = 3$.

$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 1$. Solve: $$3c^2 - 1 = 3 \implies 3c^2 = 4 \implies c = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} = \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3} \approx 1.155$$

Indeed $c \approx 1.155 \in (0,2)$. ✓

_Note: MVT guarantees existence — it doesn't say $c$ is unique. Here it happens to be unique in $(0,2)$, but for other functions there could be multiple such $c$._

---

### Example 3 — Using MVT to Prove an Inequality

> **Claim:** $|\sin b - \sin a| \leq |b - a|$ for all $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$.

**Proof.** If $a = b$ this is trivial. Assume WLOG $a < b$.

$f(x) = \sin x$ is differentiable everywhere. By MVT, $\exists, c \in (a,b)$: $$\sin b - \sin a = f'(c)(b-a) = \cos(c) \cdot (b-a)$$

Taking absolute values: $$|\sin b - \sin a| = |\cos c| \cdot |b-a| \leq 1 \cdot |b-a| = |b-a|$$

since $|\cos c| \leq 1$ always. $\square$

_This is the power of MVT for inequalities: you convert a statement about a function's values into a statement about its derivative, which is often easier to bound._

---

### Example 4 — Uniqueness of Solutions

> **Claim:** The equation $x^3 + x = 2$ has exactly one real solution.

**Existence:** $f(x) = x^3 + x - 2$ has $f(0) = -2 < 0$ and $f(1) = 0$. So $x = 1$ is a solution.

**Uniqueness (by MVT / Rolle):** Suppose there were two solutions $a < b$ with $f(a) = f(b) = 0$. By Rolle's Theorem, $\exists, c \in (a,b)$ with $f'(c) = 0$.

But $f'(x) = 3x^2 + 1 \geq 1 > 0$ for all $x$. Contradiction. ✗

Therefore $x = 1$ is the **unique** solution. $\square$

## §5. Important Corollaries

These follow quickly from MVT and are used constantly.

### Corollary 1 — Zero Derivative Implies Constant

> If $f'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is **constant** on $(a,b)$.

**Proof.** Take any $x_1, x_2 \in (a,b)$ with $x_1 < x_2$. By MVT: $$f(x_2) - f(x_1) = f'(c)(x_2 - x_1) = 0$$

So $f(x_1) = f(x_2)$. Since $x_1, x_2$ were arbitrary, $f$ is constant. $\square$

### Corollary 2 — Equal Derivatives Imply Functions Differ by a Constant

> If $f'(x) = g'(x)$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f(x) = g(x) + C$ for some constant $C$.

**Proof.** Apply Corollary 1 to $h(x) = f(x) - g(x)$. $\square$

_This is the reason antiderivatives are defined up to a constant — a fact that underlies all of integral calculus._

### Corollary 3 — Monotonicity

> - If $f'(x) > 0$ on $(a,b)$, then $f$ is **strictly increasing** on $[a,b]$.
> - If $f'(x) < 0$ on $(a,b)$, then $f$ is **strictly decreasing** on $[a,b]$.

**Proof (increasing case).** Take $x_1 < x_2$ in $[a,b]$. By MVT, $\exists, c$ with: $$f(x_2) - f(x_1) = f'(c)(x_2 - x_1) > 0$$

since $f'(c) > 0$ and $x_2 - x_1 > 0$. So $f(x_2) > f(x_1)$. $\square$

## §6. The Generalized (Cauchy) MVT

> **Theorem (Cauchy's MVT).** Let $f, g$ be continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $g'(x) \neq 0$ on $(a,b)$. Then $\exists, c \in (a,b)$: $$\frac{f'(c)}{g'(c)} = \frac{f(b)-f(a)}{g(b)-g(a)}$$

_Taking $g(x) = x$ recovers Lagrange's MVT._

This is the key lemma behind **L'Hôpital's Rule**. When computing $\lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)}$ of the form $\frac{0}{0}$, Cauchy's MVT provides the justification for replacing the ratio of functions with the ratio of derivatives.

## §7. MVT in Higher Dimensions — A Glimpse (MIT 18.02)

In multivariable calculus, the MVT generalizes but requires care. For a function $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$:

> **MVT for scalar fields.** If $f$ is differentiable, then for points $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n$: $$f(\mathbf{b}) - f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{c}) \cdot (\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a})$$ for some $\mathbf{c}$ on the segment joining $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$.

This is a direct generalization: the **gradient** $\nabla f$ replaces $f'$, and the **dot product** replaces ordinary multiplication.

For **vector-valued** $\mathbf{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$, the exact MVT _fails_ in general (there need not exist a single $c$). Instead, one uses the **MVT inequality**: $$|\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{b}) - \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{a})| \leq \sup_{\mathbf{c} \in \overline{ab}} |D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{c})| \cdot |\mathbf{b} - \mathbf{a}|$$

where $D\mathbf{f}$ is the Jacobian matrix. This inequality is fundamental in numerical analysis and functional analysis.

## §8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _This is where the theorem stops being "pure math" and starts running your GPU._

### 8.1 Gradient Descent — The MVT Beneath Every Update

The backbone of training neural networks is **gradient descent**. Given a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^p$ are the parameters:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

**Why does this work?** MVT tells us exactly:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta_{t+1}) - \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) = \nabla \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{c}) \cdot (\theta_{t+1} - \theta_t)$$

for some $\mathbf{c}$ between $\theta_t$ and $\theta_{t+1}$.

When the step is small, $\nabla \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{c}) \approx \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$, so: $$\mathcal{L}(\theta_{t+1}) - \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) \approx \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) \cdot (-\eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)) = -\eta |\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)|^2 \leq 0$$

**The loss decreases.** The MVT is the mathematical reason gradient descent works — it licenses the approximation that a small step in the negative gradient direction reduces the objective.

---

### 8.2 Learning Rate and the Lipschitz Condition

The MVT motivates one of the most important practical choices in deep learning: **choosing the learning rate $\eta$**.

If $\nabla \mathcal{L}$ is **Lipschitz continuous** with constant $L$: $$|\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta) - \nabla \mathcal{L}(\phi)| \leq L|\theta - \phi|$$

Then by MVT-type reasoning, one can show: $$\mathcal{L}(\theta_{t+1}) \leq \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) - \left(\eta - \frac{L\eta^2}{2}\right)|\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)|^2$$

The right-hand side is decreasing only when $\eta < \frac{2}{L}$.

> **Practical implication:** If your learning rate is too large (bigger than $\frac{2}{L}$), gradient descent is theoretically not guaranteed to converge. This is why learning rate schedules and adaptive methods (Adam, RMSProp) exist — they are heuristic attempts to stay in the MVT-safe zone.

---

### 8.3 Backpropagation and the Chain Rule

The **chain rule** — the engine of backpropagation — is proved using the MVT. Recall:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[f(g(x))] = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$$

One rigorous proof of this fact proceeds by writing: $$\frac{f(g(x+h)) - f(g(x))}{h} = \frac{f(g(x+h)) - f(g(x))}{g(x+h)-g(x)} \cdot \frac{g(x+h)-g(x)}{h}$$

and applying MVT to control the first factor as $h \to 0$.

In deep learning, every layer's gradient computation is an application of this chain rule. **The entire backpropagation algorithm is the chain rule applied recursively — and the chain rule's proof leans on MVT.**

---

### 8.4 Loss Landscape Analysis

Modern deep learning theory studies the **loss landscape** $\mathcal{L}: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}$ of neural networks. Questions like:

- Are there saddle points? Local minima? (Answered using critical point theory — $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 0$, directly from MVT corollaries.)
- How "flat" are the minima? (Studied via the Hessian $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$, which is the second-order MVT — Taylor's theorem.)

The **second-order MVT** (Taylor with remainder): $$\mathcal{L}(\theta + \delta) = \mathcal{L}(\theta) + \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta)^\top \delta + \frac{1}{2}\delta^\top H(\mathbf{c})\delta$$

is used in the analysis of **Newton's method**, **natural gradient descent**, and **second-order optimizers** like K-FAC.

---

### 8.5 Generalization and Lipschitz Neural Networks

In learning theory, the **generalization gap** (difference between training and test error) can be bounded using the Lipschitz constant of the network. By MVT:

$$|f_\theta(x) - f_\theta(x')| \leq \text{Lip}(f_\theta) \cdot |x - x'|$$

Networks with small Lipschitz constants generalize better — this is the theoretical motivation behind **spectral normalization** (used in GANs) and **gradient penalty** methods. All of these bound or control the Lipschitz constant, which is defined via MVT.

## §10. Hypotheses Are Not Optional

> _A common exam trap — and a deep conceptual point._

The MVT **requires** both continuity on $[a,b]$ and differentiability on $(a,b)$.

**Counterexample 1 — Continuity fails:** $$f(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x \in [0,1) \ 1 & x = 1 \end{cases}$$ $\frac{f(1)-f(0)}{1-0} = 1$, but $f'(x) = 0$ wherever it exists. No $c$ works.

**Counterexample 2 — Differentiability fails:** $$f(x) = |x| \text{ on } [-1, 1]$$ $\frac{f(1)-f(-1)}{2} = 0$, but $f'(x) = \pm 1 \neq 0$ everywhere $f'$ is defined; $f'(0)$ doesn't exist.

_The hypotheses in theorems are not bureaucratic formalities — they are load-bearing walls._
