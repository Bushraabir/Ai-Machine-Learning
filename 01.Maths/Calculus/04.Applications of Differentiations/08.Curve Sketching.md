## Preface: Why Bother Sketching?

Before numerical solvers and plotting libraries existed, mathematicians had to _understand_ a function before they could draw it. Curve sketching is the discipline of extracting the complete qualitative story of $f(x)$ from its formula alone — without evaluating hundreds of points. It is, at its heart, a systematic interrogation of a function.

This note follows the classical treatment of **Stewart §4.5** and the spirit of **MIT 18.02**, building up the full algorithmic pipeline from domain analysis to asymptotic behavior to the precise shape of local extrema.

---

## The Seven-Step Protocol

Given $f: D \subseteq \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, a complete sketch requires answering these questions in order:

|Step|Question|Tool|
|---|---|---|
|1|Where is $f$ defined?|Domain analysis|
|2|Does $f$ have symmetry?|Even/odd test|
|3|Where does $f$ cross the axes?|Intercepts|
|4|What happens at the boundary of $D$?|Asymptotes|
|5|Where is $f$ increasing/decreasing?|Sign of $f'$|
|6|Where are the peaks and valleys?|Critical points + 1st/2nd derivative test|
|7|Where does $f$ bend?|Sign of $f''$, inflection points|

Work through all seven before committing a single curve to paper.

---

## Step 1 — Domain

The **domain** $D$ is the maximal subset of $\mathbb{R}$ on which $f(x)$ is defined. Standard obstructions:

- Division by zero: $f(x) = \frac{1}{x-2}$ excludes $x = 2$.
- Even roots of negatives: $f(x) = \sqrt{x^2-1}$ requires $|x| \geq 1$.
- Logarithms: $f(x) = \ln(x)$ requires $x > 0$.

> **Principle.** Determine the domain _first_. Every subsequent step is restricted to $D$.

---

## Step 2 — Symmetry

**Definition.** A function $f$ is:

- **Even** if $f(-x) = f(x)$ for all $x \in D$ (symmetric about the $y$-axis).
- **Odd** if $f(-x) = -f(x)$ for all $x \in D$ (symmetric about the origin).

Exploiting symmetry reduces the domain you need to analyze by half — sketch on $[0, \infty)$, then reflect.

**Examples:**

- $f(x) = x^4 - 3x^2 + 1$ is **even** (all even powers).
- $f(x) = x^3 - x$ is **odd** (all odd powers).
- $f(x) = x^3 + x^2$ is **neither**.

---

## Step 3 — Intercepts

**$y$-intercept:** Set $x = 0$, compute $f(0)$ (only if $0 \in D$).

**$x$-intercepts (zeros):** Solve $f(x) = 0$. This can be hard in general; use what algebra allows.

> _Remark._ Do not spend excessive time hunting zeros unless they are obvious. The shape of the curve matters more than precise root locations for a sketch.

---

## Step 4 — Asymptotes

### 4.1 Vertical Asymptotes

$x = a$ is a **vertical asymptote** if $\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = \pm\infty$ or $\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = \pm\infty$.

Candidates: points where the denominator vanishes and the numerator does not.

**Example.** $f(x) = \frac{x}{x^2-1}$. The denominator vanishes at $x = \pm 1$.

$$\lim_{x \to 1^+} \frac{x}{x^2-1} = +\infty, \qquad \lim_{x \to 1^-} \frac{x}{x^2-1} = -\infty$$

So $x = 1$ is a vertical asymptote (similarly $x = -1$).

### 4.2 Horizontal Asymptotes

$y = L$ is a **horizontal asymptote** if $\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} f(x) = L$.

**Rational function rule:** $$f(x) = \frac{a_n x^n + \cdots}{b_m x^m + \cdots} \implies \begin{cases} y = 0 & n < m \ y = a_n/b_m & n = m \ \text{no H.A.} & n > m \end{cases}$$

### 4.3 Oblique (Slant) Asymptotes

When the degree of the numerator exceeds the denominator by exactly 1, divide:

$$f(x) = \frac{x^2 + 1}{x - 1} = x + 1 + \frac{2}{x-1}$$

The line $y = x + 1$ is an oblique asymptote as $x \to \pm\infty$.

---

## Step 5 — Intervals of Increase and Decrease

**Theorem (Monotonicity Test).** Let $f$ be differentiable on $(a,b)$.

- If $f'(x) > 0$ on $(a,b)$, then $f$ is strictly increasing on $[a,b]$.
- If $f'(x) < 0$ on $(a,b)$, then $f$ is strictly decreasing on $[a,b]$.

**Procedure:**

1. Compute $f'(x)$.
2. Find all **critical numbers** $c$ where $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ does not exist.
3. Use these to partition $D$ into intervals.
4. Test the sign of $f'$ on each interval (pick any sample point).

---

## Step 6 — Local Extrema

**Definition.** $f$ has a **local maximum** at $c$ if $f(c) \geq f(x)$ for all $x$ near $c$, and a **local minimum** if $f(c) \leq f(x)$ for all $x$ near $c$.

### First Derivative Test

Suppose $c$ is a critical number of $f$.

- If $f'$ changes from $+$ to $-$ at $c$: **local max**.
- If $f'$ changes from $-$ to $+$ at $c$: **local min**.
- If $f'$ does not change sign: **neither** (saddle).

### Second Derivative Test

Suppose $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c)$ exists.

- $f''(c) > 0 \Rightarrow$ local minimum (curve is concave up, so $c$ is the bottom of a bowl).
- $f''(c) < 0 \Rightarrow$ local maximum (curve is concave down, so $c$ is the top of a hill).
- $f''(c) = 0 \Rightarrow$ **inconclusive** (must revert to first derivative test).

> **Mnemonic.** A smiley face $\cup$ is concave up ($f'' > 0$); a frowny face $\cap$ is concave down ($f'' < 0$).

---

## Step 7 — Concavity and Inflection Points

**Definition.** $f$ is **concave up** on $(a,b)$ if $f''(x) > 0$ there; **concave down** if $f''(x) < 0$ there.

**Definition.** $c$ is an **inflection point** if $f$ is continuous at $c$ and the concavity changes at $c$.

> **Warning.** $f''(c) = 0$ is necessary but not sufficient for an inflection point. Check that concavity _actually changes_.

**Example.** $f(x) = x^4$: $f''(x) = 12x^2$, so $f''(0) = 0$, but concavity is up on both sides — $x = 0$ is **not** an inflection point.

## Complete Worked Example

$$f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x^2 - 4}$$

### Step 1: Domain

Denominator $= 0$ when $x = \pm 2$. So $D = \mathbb{R} \setminus {-2, 2}$.

### Step 2: Symmetry

$f(-x) = \frac{(-x)^2-1}{(-x)^2-4} = f(x)$. **Even function** — sketch for $x \geq 0$ and reflect.

### Step 3: Intercepts

- $y$-intercept: $f(0) = \frac{-1}{-4} = \frac{1}{4}$.
- $x$-intercepts: $x^2 - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1$.

### Step 4: Asymptotes

**Vertical:** $x = \pm 2$.

Check signs near $x = 2$: $$\lim_{x \to 2^+} \frac{x^2-1}{x^2-4}: \quad \text{numerator} \to 3 > 0, \quad \text{denominator} \to 0^+ \Rightarrow +\infty$$ $$\lim_{x \to 2^-} \frac{x^2-1}{x^2-4}: \quad \text{denominator} \to 0^- \Rightarrow -\infty$$

**Horizontal:** $$\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} \frac{x^2-1}{x^2-4} = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{1 - 1/x^2}{1 - 4/x^2} = 1$$ So $y = 1$ is a horizontal asymptote.

### Step 5: Monotonicity

$$f'(x) = \frac{2x(x^2-4) - (x^2-1)(2x)}{(x^2-4)^2} = \frac{2x[(x^2-4)-(x^2-1)]}{(x^2-4)^2} = \frac{-6x}{(x^2-4)^2}$$

Critical point: $f'(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$.

Sign analysis of $f'(x) = \frac{-6x}{(x^2-4)^2}$ (denominator always $\geq 0$, denominator $=0$ excluded):

|Interval|Sign of $-6x$|$f'$|Behavior|
|---|---|---|---|
|$(-\infty,-2)$|$+$|$+$|increasing|
|$(-2,0)$|$+$|$+$|increasing|
|$(0,2)$|$-$|$-$|decreasing|
|$(2,\infty)$|$-$|$-$|decreasing|

### Step 6: Extrema

$f'$ changes from $+$ to $-$ at $x = 0$: **local maximum** at $(0, 1/4)$.

Confirm: $f''(0)$ — or just note the sign change.

### Step 7: Concavity

$$f''(x) = \frac{-6(x^2-4)^2 - (-6x) \cdot 2(x^2-4)(2x)}{(x^2-4)^4} = \frac{-6(x^2-4) + 24x^2}{(x^2-4)^3} = \frac{18x^2 + 24}{(x^2-4)^3}$$

The numerator $18x^2 + 24 > 0$ always. So the sign of $f''$ is the sign of $(x^2-4)^3 = (x-2)^3(x+2)^3$:

|Region|$(x^2-4)^3$|Concavity|
|---|---|---|
|$|x|> 2$|
|$|x|< 2$|

No inflection points (concavity doesn't change within a continuous piece of the domain).

### Sketch Summary

```
    y
    |       *         *
  --|--  *  |  *   *  |  *  -- y=1 (asymptote)
    |  *    |    *    |    *
  1/4 -----+--------- --------
    | *     |         |     *
    |*      |         |      *
 ---+-------+---------+--------> x
   -2  -1   0    1    2
    
Vertical asymptotes at x=±2
Horizontal asymptote y=1
Local max at (0, 1/4)
Crosses x-axis at x=±1
```

## Visualization: Reading the Derivative Portrait

One of the most powerful mental models in curve sketching is the **derivative portrait** — aligning $f$, $f'$, and $f''$ visually.

```
f(x):  rises /    peaks ⌒    falls \    valley ⌣    rises /
f'(x): positive    zero      negative     zero      positive
f''(x): ?         negative    ?          positive      ?
```

Wherever $f''$ switches sign, that's an inflection point — the graph transitions from bending left to bending right (or vice versa). In practice, when sketching by hand, draw the $f'$ sign chart and $f''$ sign chart on the same number line as $f$ to keep track.

## Classic Examples Gallery

### Example 1: Polynomial — $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$

|Feature|Value|
|---|---|
|Domain|$\mathbb{R}$|
|Symmetry|Odd|
|Zeros|$x = 0, \pm\sqrt{3}$|
|$f'(x)$|$3x^2 - 3 = 3(x-1)(x+1)$|
|Critical pts|$x = \pm 1$|
|Local max|$(-1, 2)$|
|Local min|$(1, -2)$|
|$f''(x)$|$6x$|
|Inflection|$(0, 0)$|

The graph is an S-curve through the origin, rising steeply on both ends, with a local max on the left and local min on the right.

---

### Example 2: Rational with Hole — $f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$

$$f(x) = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2, \quad x \neq 2$$

This is a line with a **hole** (removable discontinuity) at $(2, 4)$. No asymptote — merely a gap. The sketch is a straight line with an open circle at $x = 2$.

> _This example is a reminder: factor before jumping to conclusions about asymptotes._

---

### Example 3: $f(x) = xe^{-x}$

$$f'(x) = e^{-x} - xe^{-x} = e^{-x}(1-x)$$

Critical point: $x = 1$ (local max since $f'$ changes $+ \to -$). $f(1) = e^{-1} \approx 0.368$.

$$f''(x) = e^{-x}(x-2)$$

Inflection at $x = 2$: $f(2) = 2e^{-2} \approx 0.271$.

As $x \to +\infty$: $f(x) \to 0$ (exponential beats polynomial). As $x \to -\infty$: $f(x) \to -\infty$.

This curve starts from $-\infty$ on the left, rises through the origin, peaks at $(1, e^{-1})$, inflects at $(2, 2e^{-2})$, then decays to $0$ — a beautiful unimodal shape that appears constantly in probability (it's related to the Gamma distribution) and in ML.

## Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _This is not a detour — it is the whole point of why any of this matters._

### 1. Loss Landscapes and Gradient Descent

The central problem in training a neural network is minimizing a **loss function** $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ over a high-dimensional parameter space $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^n$. In 1D, this is exactly curve sketching.

Gradient descent updates: $$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \cdot \mathcal{L}'(\theta_t)$$

This is navigating the curve $\mathcal{L}$ by following $-f'$. The algorithm:

- **follows $f'$** to find descent directions (Step 5 above)
- **gets stuck at $f'(c)=0$** — which can be a minimum, maximum, or saddle point (Step 6 above)
- **uses $f''$ (curvature)** in second-order methods like Newton's method and natural gradient descent

The entire theory of optimization in ML is the multivariable generalization of Steps 5–7.

### 2. The Sigmoid and Its Derivative

The **sigmoid function**: $$\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-x}}$$

is one of the most important activation functions. Its curve-sketching analysis:

- Domain: $\mathbb{R}$
- Range: $(0,1)$
- Horizontal asymptotes: $y = 0$ as $x \to -\infty$, $y = 1$ as $x \to +\infty$
- Inflection point at $x = 0$ (where $\sigma(0) = 1/2$)
- $\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x))$, which peaks at $x=0$ with value $1/4$

The **vanishing gradient problem** in deep networks is visible directly from the sketch: away from $x = 0$, $\sigma'(x) \to 0$. When gradients are multiplied across many layers, this near-zero derivative causes the gradient signal to vanish. This is why **ReLU** ($f(x) = \max(0,x)$, with $f'(x) = 1$ for $x > 0$) replaced sigmoid in deep networks — its derivative does not decay.

### 3. Convexity and Optimization Guarantees

**Definition.** $f$ is **convex** if for all $x, y$ in the domain and $\lambda \in [0,1]$: $$f(\lambda x + (1-\lambda)y) \leq \lambda f(x) + (1-\lambda)f(y)$$

Equivalently (for twice-differentiable $f$): $f'' \geq 0$ everywhere.

**Why it matters in ML:** If $\mathcal{L}$ is convex, every local minimum is a global minimum, and gradient descent is guaranteed to converge. Logistic regression and SVMs have convex losses. Deep neural networks do not — their loss landscapes are highly non-convex, which is why training requires careful initialization, momentum, and learning rate scheduling.

Understanding convexity _is_ Step 7 of curve sketching applied to loss functions.

### 4. Newton's Method — The Second-Derivative Optimizer

Newton's method for finding zeros of $f'$ (i.e., critical points of $f$): $$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \frac{f'(\theta_t)}{f''(\theta_t)}$$

In $n$ dimensions, this becomes: $$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - H^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}$$

where $H$ is the **Hessian** (matrix of second derivatives). This is the basis of **quasi-Newton methods** (L-BFGS), **natural gradient** methods, and **Fisher information** in deep learning — all of which use curvature information ($f''$) to take smarter steps than vanilla gradient descent.

### 5. Activation Function Design

Every nonlinear activation function in deep learning is designed with curve-sketching principles:

|Activation|$f(x)$|Key feature|Curve Property|
|---|---|---|---|
|Sigmoid|$1/(1+e^{-x})$|Bounded, smooth|Inflection at 0|
|Tanh|$(e^x - e^{-x})/(e^x + e^{-x})$|Bounded, zero-mean|Inflection at 0, odd|
|ReLU|$\max(0,x)$|No vanishing gradient|Non-differentiable at 0|
|GELU|$x\Phi(x)$|Smooth ReLU-like|Slight dip near $x = -0.17$|
|Softplus|$\ln(1+e^x)$|Smooth approximation to ReLU|$f'' > 0$ everywhere (convex)|

Each design decision traces back to understanding what the curve looks like — its range, its derivative behavior, its inflection structure.

### 6. Fourier Analysis and Neural Networks (a bridge)

MIT 18.02 motivates much of its analysis through the idea that complex curves can be decomposed into simpler components. Modern deep networks approximate arbitrary functions by composing simple nonlinearities — a continuous analog of the same idea. Universal approximation theorems guarantee that sufficiently wide networks with non-polynomial activations can approximate any continuous function on a compact set — but the _quality_ of that approximation depends on the smoothness structure of the target function, which is precisely the language of curve sketching.

## The Derivative Sign Chart: A Practical Tool

For any function, maintain a sign chart as you work:

```
x:         -∞    -2    -1     0     1     2    +∞
f'(x):           +++   +++   ---   ---  
f''(x):          ---   +++   +++   ---   
f:          ↗     ↗     ↘     ↘
concavity:   ∩     ∪     ∪     ∩
```

This single diagram encodes the entire qualitative behavior of the function before you draw a single point.

## Summary: The Checklist

- [ ] **Domain** — where is $f$ defined?
- [ ] **Symmetry** — even, odd, or neither?
- [ ] **Intercepts** — $f(0)$ and zeros of $f$
- [ ] **Vertical asymptotes** — zeros of denominator
- [ ] **Horizontal/oblique asymptotes** — limits at $\pm\infty$
- [ ] **$f'(x)$** — compute, find critical numbers, sign chart
- [ ] **Local extrema** — first or second derivative test
- [ ] **$f''(x)$** — compute, find inflection candidates, sign chart
- [ ] **Inflection points** — confirm sign change of $f''$
- [ ] **Sketch** — place all features, connect with smooth curve
