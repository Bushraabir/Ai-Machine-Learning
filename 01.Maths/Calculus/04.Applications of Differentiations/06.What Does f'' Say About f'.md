

> _"The second derivative is the derivative of the derivative — but it speaks a deeper language: it tells you how fast the slope itself is changing."_ — Personal note, drawn from Stewart §4.3 and MIT 18.02

---

## 0. Setup & Notation

Let $f : I \to \mathbb{R}$ be twice differentiable on an open interval $I$. Define:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}, \qquad f''(x) = (f')'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f'(x+h) - f'(x)}{h}$$

The question is: **what does knowing $f''$ tell us about $f'$?**

The answer operates on two levels:

1. **Analytical**: $f''$ is the rate of change of $f'$ — just as $f'$ is the rate of change of $f$.
2. **Geometric**: $f''$ controls the _concavity_ of $f$, i.e., whether the slope $f'$ is increasing or decreasing.

---

## 1. The Fundamental Relationship: $f''$ as the Rate of Change of $f'$

### Theorem 1.1 (Sign of $f''$ and Monotonicity of $f'$)

> Let $f$ be twice differentiable on $(a, b)$.
> 
> - If $f''(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (a, b)$, then $f'$ is **strictly increasing** on $(a, b)$.
> - If $f''(x) < 0$ for all $x \in (a, b)$, then $f'$ is **strictly decreasing** on $(a, b)$.
> - If $f''(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a, b)$, then $f'$ is **constant** on $(a, b)$ (i.e., $f$ is linear).

**Proof.** Apply the First Derivative Test to $f'$: since $(f')' = f''$, the sign of $f''$ determines whether $f'$ is monotone, by the standard theorem that a positive derivative implies a strictly increasing function. $\blacksquare$

This is the core insight. **Everything else follows from this.**

---

## 2. Concavity: The Geometric Translation

### Definition 2.1 (Concavity)

$f$ is **concave up** on $(a, b)$ if for all $x, y \in (a,b)$ and $t \in (0,1)$: $$f(tx + (1-t)y) \leq t f(x) + (1-t)f(y)$$

Geometrically: the chord lies **above** the curve. The tangent lines lie **below** the curve.

$f$ is **concave down** if the reverse inequality holds.

### Theorem 2.2 (Second Derivative Test for Concavity) _(Stewart §4.3)_

> - $f''(x) > 0$ on $(a,b)$ $\Rightarrow$ $f$ is concave up on $(a,b)$.
> - $f''(x) < 0$ on $(a,b)$ $\Rightarrow$ $f$ is concave down on $(a,b)$.

**Proof sketch.** Fix $c \in (a,b)$. The tangent line at $c$ is $L(x) = f(c) + f'(c)(x-c)$. Define $g(x) = f(x) - L(x)$. Then $g(c) = 0$, $g'(c) = 0$, and $g''(x) = f''(x) > 0$. By Theorem 1.1, $g'$ is increasing near $c$. Since $g'(c) = 0$: $g'(x) < 0$ for $x < c$ and $g'(x) > 0$ for $x > c$ near $c$. So $g$ has a local minimum at $c$, hence $g(x) \geq g(c) = 0$. This means $f(x) \geq L(x)$: the tangent line lies below $f$. $\blacksquare$

### Visualization

```
Concave Up (f'' > 0):          Concave Down (f'' < 0):
f' is INCREASING                f' is DECREASING

     *                           *       *
   *   *                           *   *
  *     *                            *
 *       *
 
slope gets steeper ↗            slope gets flatter ↘
tangent lines rotate CCW        tangent lines rotate CW
```

**ASCII slope arrows** along $f(x) = x^2$ (concave up everywhere):

```
x = -2: slope = -4  ↘ steep
x = -1: slope = -2  ↘ mild
x =  0: slope =  0  →
x =  1: slope =  2  ↗ mild
x =  2: slope =  4  ↗ steep
```

The slopes are **increasing** $(-4, -2, 0, 2, 4)$ — exactly what $f'' = 2 > 0$ predicts.

## 3. Inflection Points: Where $f''$ Changes Sign

### Definition 3.1 (Inflection Point)

A point $c$ is an **inflection point** of $f$ if:

1. $f$ is continuous at $c$, and
2. $f''$ changes sign at $c$ (concavity reverses).

**Critical nuance**: $f''(c) = 0$ is _necessary_ but **not sufficient** for an inflection point.

### Example 3.2 (The Classic Failure)

$$f(x) = x^4, \quad f''(x) = 12x^2$$

$f''(0) = 0$, yet $f''$ does **not** change sign at $0$ — it remains $\geq 0$ everywhere. So $x = 0$ is **not** an inflection point. The curve is concave up on all of $\mathbb{R}$.

### Example 3.3 (Genuine Inflection)

$$f(x) = x^3, \quad f'(x) = 3x^2, \quad f''(x) = 6x$$

- $f''(x) < 0$ for $x < 0$: concave down, $f'$ is decreasing.
- $f''(0) = 0$: inflection point.
- $f''(x) > 0$ for $x > 0$: concave up, $f'$ is increasing.

The slope $f'(x) = 3x^2$ reaches its **minimum** at $x = 0$ (it decreases then increases), consistent with $f''$ changing from $-$ to $+$.

## 4. The Second Derivative Test for Local Extrema

This is perhaps the most used consequence. It exploits $f''$'s information about $f'$ near a critical point.

### Theorem 4.1 (Second Derivative Test) _(Stewart §4.3)_

> Suppose $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c)$ exists.
> 
> - If $f''(c) > 0$: $f$ has a **local minimum** at $c$.
> - If $f''(c) < 0$: $f$ has a **local maximum** at $c$.
> - If $f''(c) = 0$: the test is **inconclusive**.

**Proof.** If $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c) > 0$: by continuity of $f''$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $f''(x) > 0$ on $(c - \delta, c + \delta)$. By Theorem 1.1, $f'$ is increasing on this interval. Since $f'(c) = 0$:

- For $x \in (c-\delta, c)$: $f'(x) < f'(c) = 0$, so $f$ is decreasing.
- For $x \in (c, c+\delta)$: $f'(x) > f'(c) = 0$, so $f$ is increasing.

Hence $f(c)$ is a local minimum. $\blacksquare$

**Why the test fails when $f''(c) = 0$:** Consider $f(x) = x^4$ (local min at 0), $g(x) = -x^4$ (local max at 0), $h(x) = x^3$ (saddle/inflection at 0). All have $f''(0) = 0$.

## 6. Worked Example: Complete Analysis of $f(x) = x^3 - 3x$

_(This is the standard example from Stewart §4.3 and MIT 18.02)_

$$f(x) = x^3 - 3x$$ $$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 3(x-1)(x+1)$$ $$f''(x) = 6x$$

**Step 1. Critical points:** $f'(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1$.

**Step 2. Apply Second Derivative Test:**

- $f''(-1) = -6 < 0$: local **maximum** at $x = -1$, value $f(-1) = 2$.
- $f''(1) = 6 > 0$: local **minimum** at $x = 1$, value $f(1) = -2$.

**Step 3. Concavity and inflection:**

- $f''(x) < 0$ for $x < 0$: concave down (slope decreasing).
- $f''(0) = 0$ and sign change: **inflection point** at $x = 0$.
- $f''(x) > 0$ for $x > 0$: concave up (slope increasing).

**Sketch of $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3$ alongside $f''(x) = 6x$:**

```
f'(x):                     f''(x):
      *           *                   /
       *         *              -----/-----
        *       *              /
         *     *          (f'' = 0 at origin)
          *   *
           * *
            *   ← minimum of f' at x=0
                  (where f'' changes sign)
```

The **minimum of $f'$** occurs at $x = 0$, which is precisely where $f'' = 0$ and changes sign. This is the inflection point of $f$. Perfect consistency.

## 7. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This is not a detour — this is where the second derivative becomes genuinely powerful in modern computational mathematics.

### 7.1 Gradient Descent and the Role of $f''$

In machine learning, we minimize a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ over parameters $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^n$. The standard update rule is:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

This uses only $\nabla \mathcal{L}$ (the first derivative). But the **curvature** — governed by $f''$ — critically affects convergence:

- If $f'' \gg 0$ (high curvature): a fixed learning rate $\eta$ causes **oscillation** or divergence.
- If $f'' \approx 0$ (flat region): gradient descent moves painfully slowly.

The **ideal** step size at each point is $\eta \approx \frac{1}{f''(x)}$ — larger curvature demands a smaller step.

### 7.2 Newton's Method: Explicitly Using $f''$

Newton's method for optimization is:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \frac{f'(\theta_t)}{f''(\theta_t)}$$

In higher dimensions, this becomes: $$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - H^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

where $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$ is the **Hessian matrix** — the multivariable analogue of $f''$. Each entry $H_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}$ describes how the gradient in direction $i$ changes as you move in direction $j$.

Newton's method **converges quadratically** (vs. linearly for gradient descent) because it accounts for curvature. The cost: computing and inverting $H$ for a network with $n$ parameters is $O(n^2)$ storage and $O(n^3)$ inversion — infeasible for modern deep networks with $n \sim 10^9$.

### 7.3 Saddle Points in Deep Learning

A critical point where $f'(c) = 0$ but $f''(c) = 0$ is called a **saddle point** in higher dimensions (or inflection-type point in 1D). In the loss landscapes of deep neural networks:

- **Local minima** are relatively rare in high dimensions.
- **Saddle points** are ubiquitous — the gradient vanishes, the Hessian has both positive and negative eigenvalues.

Standard gradient descent can get **stuck near saddle points** because the gradient becomes tiny. This is one reason why **momentum** methods (Adam, RMSProp) were developed — they use accumulated gradient history to escape flat regions.

The sign of $f''$ (or eigenvalues of the Hessian) determines whether a critical point is:

- A minimum: all eigenvalues $> 0$ (positive definite Hessian — $f''$-analogue)
- A maximum: all eigenvalues $< 0$
- A saddle: mixed signs

### 7.4 Convexity and Loss Landscapes

A function $\mathcal{L}$ is **convex** if and only if $f''(\theta) \geq 0$ everywhere (in 1D). Convexity guarantees:

- Every local minimum is a global minimum.
- Gradient descent converges to the global optimum.

Deep neural network loss functions are **highly non-convex** — $f''$ changes sign constantly, creating a rugged landscape of hills, valleys, and saddle points. Understanding where $f'' > 0$ vs. $f'' < 0$ in parameter space is an active research problem (loss landscape geometry).

### 7.5 Second-Order Optimization Methods in Practice

|Method|Uses $f'$?|Uses $f''$ (Hessian)?|Notes|
|---|---|---|---|
|Gradient Descent|✓|✗|Slow, simple|
|Momentum / Adam|✓|Approximate|Most common in deep learning|
|Newton's Method|✓|✓ (exact)|Fast convergence, expensive|
|L-BFGS|✓|✓ (approximate)|Used in small-scale ML, scipy|
|Natural Gradient|✓|✓ (Fisher info)|Theoretically principled|

The tension between **using $f''$ for faster convergence** vs. **the computational cost of computing it** is a central theme of modern optimization.

## 8. Rigorous Note: The Converse and Edge Cases

**Remark 8.1.** The converse of Theorem 2.2 is _false_: a function can be concave up at a point where $f''(c) = 0$. Example: $f(x) = x^4$ is concave up on all of $\mathbb{R}$, yet $f''(0) = 0$.

**Remark 8.2.** A function can have an inflection point where $f''$ fails to exist. Example: $f(x) = x^{1/3}$ has an inflection at $x = 0$ but $f''(0)$ does not exist.

**Remark 8.3.** The condition "$f''$ changes sign" is the correct definition of inflection — not merely "$f''(c) = 0$". Always verify sign change.
