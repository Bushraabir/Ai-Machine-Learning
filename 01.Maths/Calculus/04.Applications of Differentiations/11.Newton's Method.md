## 0. Motivation: Why Do We Need This?

Most equations $f(x) = 0$ have no closed-form solution. Consider:

$$x^5 - x - 1 = 0 \qquad e^x = 3x \qquad \cos x = x$$

No algebraic manipulation will isolate $x$. We need a **numerical method** — an algorithm that produces increasingly accurate approximations.

Newton's method (also called the **Newton–Raphson method**) is arguably the most elegant and powerful such algorithm. It converges _quadratically_ — each iteration roughly _doubles_ the number of correct decimal places.

---

## 1. The Geometric Idea

Suppose we want to solve $f(x) = 0$, and we have an initial guess $x_1$ near a root $r$.

**Key insight:** Approximate the curve $y = f(x)$ near $x_1$ by its **tangent line**. The tangent line is simple — it's linear. Find where the tangent line crosses the $x$-axis. Call that $x_2$. Repeat.

```
  y = f(x)
  |
  |          * (x₁, f(x₁))
  |         /|
  |        / |
  |       /  |
  |      /   |
  |     /    |
  |____/_____|_________*___________ x
  |         x₂        r  x₁
  |        (new      (true
  |        guess)     root)
  |
  Tangent line at x₁ crosses x-axis at x₂ — closer to r.
```

The tangent line at $(x_1, f(x_1))$ has equation: $$y - f(x_1) = f'(x_1)(x - x_1)$$

Setting $y = 0$: $$x_2 = x_1 - \frac{f(x_1)}{f'(x_1)}$$

---

## 2. The Iteration Formula

Repeating this process defines the sequence:

$$\boxed{x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}}$$

This is **Newton's Method**. Under suitable conditions, $x_n \to r$ where $f(r) = 0$.

**Reading the formula:**

- $f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$ is the **Newton step** — how far to move.
- The denominator $f'(x_n)$ is slope: steep slope → small step; shallow slope → large step.
- If $f'(x_n) = 0$, the method breaks down (tangent line is horizontal, never crosses $x$-axis).

---

## 3. Worked Example: Computing $\sqrt{2}$

We want $\sqrt{2}$, i.e., a root of $f(x) = x^2 - 2$.

$$f'(x) = 2x \implies x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{x_n^2 - 2}{2x_n} = \frac{x_n}{2} + \frac{1}{x_n} = \frac{x_n^2 + 2}{2x_n}$$

Starting with $x_1 = 1$:

| $n$ | $x_n$ | $f(x_n) = x_n^2 - 2$ | Error $|x_n - \sqrt{2}|$ | |-----|--------|----------------------|--------------------------| | 1 | 1.000000 | −1.000000 | 0.41421 | | 2 | 1.500000 | 0.250000 | 0.08579 | | 3 | 1.416667 | 0.006944 | 0.00245 | | 4 | 1.414216 | 0.000006 | 0.0000021 | | 5 | 1.414214 | $< 10^{-12}$ | $< 10^{-12}$ |

**Observation:** The error roughly _squares_ at each step. $0.086 \to 0.0024 \to 2 \times 10^{-6} \to 10^{-12}$. This is **quadratic convergence**.

> **Historical note:** This algorithm for $\sqrt{2}$ was known to the Babylonians around 1700 BCE as the "Babylonian method." Newton's contribution was the general framework for arbitrary $f$.

## 4. Convergence Analysis: Why Is It So Fast?

**Theorem (Quadratic Convergence):** Let $r$ be a simple root of $f$ (i.e., $f(r) = 0$, $f'(r) \neq 0$). If $f''$ is continuous near $r$ and $x_1$ is sufficiently close to $r$, then:

$$|x_{n+1} - r| \leq C \cdot |x_n - r|^2 \qquad \text{where } C = \frac{|f''(r)|}{2|f'(r)|}$$

### Proof

Let $e_n = x_n - r$ (the error at step $n$). By Taylor's theorem about $r$:

$$f(x_n) = f(r) + f'(r)(x_n - r) + \frac{1}{2}f''(\xi_n)(x_n - r)^2$$

Since $f(r) = 0$: $$f(x_n) = f'(r)e_n + \frac{1}{2}f''(\xi_n)e_n^2$$

Similarly, $f'(x_n) \approx f'(r) + f''(r)e_n$ (Taylor for $f'$). Now:

$$e_{n+1} = x_{n+1} - r = x_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)} - r = e_n - \frac{f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)}$$

$$= \frac{e_n f'(x_n) - f(x_n)}{f'(x_n)} = \frac{e_n[f'(r) + O(e_n)] - [f'(r)e_n + \frac{1}{2}f''(\xi_n)e_n^2]}{f'(x_n)}$$

$$= \frac{-\frac{1}{2}f''(\xi_n)e_n^2 + O(e_n^2)}{f'(x_n)}$$

As $x_n \to r$, $f'(x_n) \to f'(r) \neq 0$, so:

$$\boxed{e_{n+1} \approx -\frac{f''(r)}{2f'(r)} \cdot e_n^2}$$

The error at step $n+1$ is **proportional to the square of the error at step $n$**. $\blacksquare$

> **Implication:** If you are accurate to 5 decimal places, one more Newton step gives you ~10 decimal places. Another step gives ~20. This is _exponentially faster_ than most numerical methods.

### Comparison of Convergence Rates

|Method|Order|Error reduction per step|
|---|---|---|
|Bisection|1 (linear)|Halves each step|
|Secant method|~1.618 (superlinear)|Golden-ratio improvement|
|**Newton's**|**2 (quadratic)**|**Error squared**|
|Halley's method|3 (cubic)|Error cubed|

## 5. Failure Modes: When Newton's Method Goes Wrong

Newton's method is powerful but not foolproof. It can fail in several ways.

### 5.1 Bad Initial Guess

```
  y
  |
  |  f(x)                 *
  |                      / \
  |                     /   \
  |________*___________/     \______ x
  |      root       x₁
  |
  Tangent at x₁ shoots far left — new guess may be worse.
```

The tangent line at $x_1$ can shoot to a completely wrong region if $x_1$ is far from the root.

**Remedy:** Use bisection first to get into the "basin of attraction," then switch to Newton.

### 5.2 Cycle (Oscillation)

$f(x) = x^3 - 2x + 2$, $x_1 = 0$: $$x_2 = 0 - \frac{f(0)}{f'(0)} = 0 - \frac{2}{-2} = 1$$ $$x_3 = 1 - \frac{f(1)}{f'(1)} = 1 - \frac{1}{1} = 0$$

The method **cycles** between 0 and 1, never converging.

### 5.3 Derivative Is Zero or Near Zero

If $f'(x_n) \approx 0$, the step $f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$ explodes. The tangent line is nearly horizontal.

### 5.4 Multiple Root (Slow Convergence)

If $r$ is a **multiple root** — $f(r) = 0$ and $f'(r) = 0$ — convergence degrades to **linear** (order 1), not quadratic. The constant $C = f''(r)/(2f'(r))$ blows up as $f'(r) \to 0$.

**Fix:** Use the modified iteration $x_{n+1} = x_n - m \cdot f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$ where $m$ is the multiplicity.

## 6. Newton's Method for Optimisation

This is where it connects directly to **optimisation** and then to ML.

To find a minimum (or maximum) of $f(x)$, we solve $f'(x) = 0$. Apply Newton's method to $g(x) = f'(x)$:

$$x_{n+1} = x_n - \frac{g(x_n)}{g'(x_n)} = x_n - \frac{f'(x_n)}{f''(x_n)}$$

**The Newton step for optimisation** divides the gradient by the second derivative (curvature).

**Intuition:** If the function is steep ($|f'|$ large) and curved ($f''$ large), take a smaller step because the minimum is nearby. If the function is flat but has low curvature, take a larger step.

This is _qualitatively different_ from gradient descent, which uses: $$x_{n+1} = x_n - \eta f'(x_n)$$

Newton's method chooses the step size _automatically_ using local curvature. Gradient descent requires you to choose $\eta$ by hand.

---

## 7. Multivariable Newton's Method (MIT 18.02)

For $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$, solve $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}$.

The scalar formula $x_{n+1} = x_n - f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$ generalises to:

$$\mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n - [J_\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_n)]^{-1} \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_n)$$

where $J_\mathbf{F}$ is the **Jacobian matrix**:

$$J_\mathbf{F} = \begin{pmatrix} \partial F_1/\partial x_1 & \cdots & \partial F_1/\partial x_n \ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ \partial F_n/\partial x_1 & \cdots & \partial F_n/\partial x_n \end{pmatrix}$$

In practice, we never explicitly invert $J$ — we solve the **linear system**: $$J_\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_n) \cdot \Delta\mathbf{x} = -\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_n), \qquad \mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n + \Delta\mathbf{x}$$

This is Newton's method as a **linear algebra problem** at each step.

## 8. Newton's Method for Multivariable Optimisation: Newton-Raphson in ML

To minimise $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^p$, we solve $\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbf{0}$. Applying the multivariable Newton step:

$$\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n - [H_\mathcal{L}(\theta_n)]^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_n)$$

where $H_\mathcal{L}$ is the **Hessian matrix** of $\mathcal{L}$:

$$[H_\mathcal{L}]_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}$$

This is the **full Newton step** for optimisation.

```
  Gradient descent:            Newton's method (optimisation):
  
  θ_{n+1} = θ_n - η∇ℒ         θ_{n+1} = θ_n - H⁻¹∇ℒ
  
  ✓ Cheap per step             ✓ Quadratic convergence
  ✗ Needs tuned η              ✓ Automatic step size
  ✗ Slow (linear convergence)  ✗ H is p×p — HUGE in deep learning
  ✗ Ignores curvature          ✗ Inverting H costs O(p³)
```

For a network with $p = 10^8$ parameters, the Hessian is a $10^8 \times 10^8$ matrix — completely infeasible to store, let alone invert. This is why deep learning uses **first-order methods** (gradient descent variants) rather than full Newton.

---

## 9. Quasi-Newton Methods: The Compromise

Quasi-Newton methods approximate $H^{-1}$ iteratively using only gradient information — never computing the full Hessian.

### 9.1 BFGS

The **Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno (BFGS)** algorithm maintains a matrix $B_n \approx H^{-1}$ and updates it cheaply after each step using the **secant condition**:

$$B_{n+1}(y_n) = s_n \qquad \text{where } s_n = \theta_{n+1} - \theta_n, \quad y_n = \nabla\mathcal{L}_{n+1} - \nabla\mathcal{L}_n$$

The update is rank-2, preserving positive-definiteness. The step becomes: $$\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n - B_n \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_n)$$

BFGS achieves **superlinear convergence** — faster than gradient descent, cheaper than full Newton. It is the workhorse of optimisation in classical ML (SVMs, logistic regression, etc.).

### 9.2 L-BFGS

For large $p$, even storing $B_n$ (a $p \times p$ matrix) is expensive. **Limited-memory BFGS (L-BFGS)** stores only the last $m$ gradient difference vectors (typically $m = 10$–$20$) and reconstructs the implicit $B_n$ on-the-fly.

**Used in:** PyTorch's `torch.optim.LBFGS`, classical logistic regression, deep neural networks with small parameter counts, and fine-tuning.

## 10. Connection to Adam Optimiser

Adam (Adaptive Moment Estimation) is, loosely, a diagonal approximation of Newton's method.

The update rule: $$\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n - \frac{\eta}{\sqrt{\hat{v}_n} + \epsilon} \cdot \hat{m}_n$$

- $\hat{m}_n$: first moment (exponential moving average of gradients) → approximates $\nabla \mathcal{L}$.
- $\hat{v}_n$: second moment (moving average of squared gradients) → approximates diagonal of $H^2$.
- Dividing by $\sqrt{\hat{v}_n}$ → **rescales by curvature**, mimicking $H^{-1}$.

Adam is _not_ Newton's method, but the second-moment rescaling is philosophically Newtonian: parameters with high curvature (large $\hat{v}$) get small steps; parameters with low curvature get large steps.

## 12. Worked Example: Newton's Method in Optimisation

_"Find the minimum of $f(x) = x^4 - 4x^2 + x + 1$ using Newton's optimisation iteration, starting at $x_1 = 2$."_

$$f'(x) = 4x^3 - 8x + 1 \qquad f''(x) = 12x^2 - 8$$

Newton step: $x_{n+1} = x_n - \dfrac{f'(x_n)}{f''(x_n)}$

|$n$|$x_n$|$f'(x_n)$|$f''(x_n)$|step|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|1|2.0000|17.000|40.00|−0.4250|
|2|1.5750|3.8657|21.715|−0.1780|
|3|1.3970|0.3785|15.361|−0.0246|
|4|1.3724|0.0047|14.607|−0.0003|
|5|1.3721|$\approx 0$|—|converged|

At $x^* \approx 1.3721$: $f''(x^*) > 0$ → **local minimum** confirmed.

## 14. Key Results at a Glance

> **Newton iteration:** $x_{n+1} = x_n - f(x_n)/f'(x_n)$

> **Convergence:** Quadratic near a simple root. $|e_{n+1}| \leq C|e_n|^2$.

> **For optimisation:** Replace $f$ with $f'$, denominator becomes $f''$ (or Hessian).

> **Multivariable:** $\mathbf{x}_{n+1} = \mathbf{x}_n - J^{-1}\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}_n)$.

> **In ML:** Full Newton infeasible for large networks → quasi-Newton (L-BFGS) or diagonal Newton (Adam).
