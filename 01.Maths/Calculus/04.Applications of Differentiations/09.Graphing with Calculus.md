## Preface: Beyond Plotting Points

Point-by-point plotting is computationally brute force and geometrically blind. You might miss a narrow local maximum, misread an asymptote, or connect two branches that belong to different sheets of a discontinuous function.

Calculus gives you a _structural_ understanding: the derivative is the curve's **velocity** (how fast it's rising or falling), the second derivative is its **steering** (which way it bends). Together they give you a complete qualitative portrait — the exact shape, not just a cloud of dots.

This note is the natural continuation of the [[Curve Sketching]] note. Where that note gave you the **protocol**, this note goes deeper into the **geometric meaning** of each step, the **pathological cases** that break naive intuition, and the **rigorous theorems** that justify every claim.

## Foundational Theorems

These are the theorems that make graphing with calculus _rigorous_, not just heuristic. Every claim in a sketch ultimately rests on one of these.

### Extreme Value Theorem (EVT)

> **Theorem (Stewart §4.1).** If $f$ is continuous on a closed interval $[a,b]$, then $f$ attains both an absolute maximum and an absolute minimum on $[a,b]$.

This guarantees that extrema _exist_ before we go hunting for them. Note the hypotheses: _continuous_ and _closed_. Both matter.

- $f(x) = x$ on $(0,1)$: no maximum (open interval fails).
- $f(x) = 1/x$ on $[0,1]$: no maximum (not continuous at 0).

### Fermat's Theorem

> **Theorem.** If $f$ has a local extremum at $c$ and $f'(c)$ exists, then $f'(c) = 0$.

**Proof sketch.** Suppose $f$ has a local maximum at $c$. For small $h > 0$: $$\frac{f(c+h) - f(c)}{h} \leq 0 \implies f'(c) \leq 0 \quad (\text{right-side limit})$$ $$\frac{f(c+h) - f(c)}{h} \geq 0 \text{ for } h < 0 \implies f'(c) \geq 0 \quad (\text{left-side limit})$$ Since both limits must equal $f'(c)$, we get $f'(c) = 0$. $\square$

> **Warning.** The converse is FALSE. $f'(c) = 0$ does not imply a local extremum. Example: $f(x) = x^3$ at $x = 0$.

### Rolle's Theorem

> **Theorem.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, differentiable on $(a,b)$, and $f(a) = f(b)$, then there exists $c \in (a,b)$ such that $f'(c) = 0$.

Geometrically: if a smooth curve starts and ends at the same height, it must have at least one horizontal tangent somewhere in between.

### Mean Value Theorem (MVT)

> **Theorem.** If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then there exists $c \in (a,b)$ such that: $$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$

The MVT is the engine behind the monotonicity test. If $f' > 0$ on $(a,b)$, then for any $x_1 < x_2$ in $(a,b)$: $$f(x_2) - f(x_1) = f'(c)(x_2 - x_1) > 0$$ so $f$ is strictly increasing. This is the rigorous proof of what the derivative sign tells us.

## The Geometry of the Derivative

### Tangent Lines and Local Linearity

The derivative $f'(a)$ is the slope of the tangent line to $f$ at $x = a$: $$L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)$$

Near $x = a$, the function _looks like_ this line — this is **local linearity**, the central geometric idea of differential calculus. The curve and its tangent agree to first order.

This has a profound consequence for sketching: wherever $f'(a) > 0$, the curve is locally going uphill to the right. The sign of $f'$ is the direction of travel.

### The Derivative as a New Function

When sketching $f$, it is often illuminating to also sketch $f'$ (and $f''$) directly. The relationships:

|Property of $f$|Property of $f'$|
|---|---|
|Local maximum at $c$|Zero crossing: $+ \to -$|
|Local minimum at $c$|Zero crossing: $- \to +$|
|Inflection point at $c$|Local extremum of $f'$|
|Concave up|$f'$ is increasing|
|Concave down|$f'$ is decreasing|
|$f$ linear|$f'$ constant|
|$f$ quadratic|$f'$ linear|

If you can sketch $f'$ from $f$, you understand the curve.

## Concavity: The Geometry of $f''$

### Definition and Characterization

**Definition.** $f$ is **concave up** on $(a,b)$ if $f'$ is increasing on $(a,b)$, equivalently if $f''(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$.

$f$ is **concave down** if $f'$ is decreasing, equivalently $f''(x) < 0$.

**Geometric meaning:** A concave-up function lies _above_ all its tangent lines. A concave-down function lies _below_ all its tangent lines.

_Proof of the tangent-line characterization (concave up case):_ By the MVT applied on $[a, x]$, for any $x > a$: $$f(x) - f(a) = f'(c)(x-a) \quad \text{for some } c \in (a,x)$$ Since $f'$ is increasing and $c > a$, $f'(c) > f'(a)$, so: $$f(x) > f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) = L(x)$$ The curve lies strictly above the tangent. $\square$

### Second Derivative Test — Full Statement and Proof

> **Theorem.** Let $f'(c) = 0$.
> 
> - If $f''(c) > 0$: $f$ has a **local minimum** at $c$.
> - If $f''(c) < 0$: $f$ has a **local maximum** at $c$.
> - If $f''(c) = 0$: **inconclusive**.

**Proof (minimum case).** Since $f''(c) > 0$ and $f''$ is the derivative of $f'$, and $f'(c) = 0$, by the definition of the derivative: $$\lim_{x \to c} \frac{f'(x) - f'(c)}{x - c} = f''(c) > 0$$ This means for $x$ near $c$: $\frac{f'(x)}{x-c} > 0$, so $f'(x)$ has the same sign as $x - c$.

- For $x < c$: $f'(x) < 0$ (decreasing to the left).
- For $x > c$: $f'(x) > 0$ (increasing to the right). By the First Derivative Test, $c$ is a local minimum. $\square$

### Why $f''(c) = 0$ is Inconclusive

|Function|$f''(0)$|Behavior at 0|
|---|---|---|
|$f(x) = x^4$|$0$|Local min|
|$f(x) = -x^4$|$0$|Local max|
|$f(x) = x^3$|$0$|Neither (saddle)|
|$f(x) = x^5$|$0$|Neither|

When $f''(c) = 0$, look at the sign of $f'$ on both sides, or examine higher derivatives ($f'''(c) \neq 0$ implies inflection; $f^{(4)}(c) > 0$ with $f'(c) = f''(c) = f'''(c) = 0$ implies local min).

## Inflection Points: Rigorous Treatment

**Definition.** A point $(c, f(c))$ is an **inflection point** if $f$ is continuous at $c$ and there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $f$ is concave up on $(c-\delta, c)$ and concave down on $(c, c+\delta)$, or vice versa.

### Necessary Condition

If $(c, f(c))$ is an inflection point and $f''(c)$ exists, then $f''(c) = 0$.

> _This is the analog of Fermat's theorem, one derivative up._

### Sufficient Conditions for Inflection

**Condition 1.** If $f''(c) = 0$ and $f'''(c) \neq 0$, then $c$ is an inflection point.

_Why:_ $f'''(c) \neq 0$ means $f''$ is not flat at $c$ — it is genuinely crossing zero, so $f''$ changes sign.

**Condition 2.** If $f''$ changes sign at $c$ (regardless of whether $f''(c)$ exists), then $c$ is an inflection point.

**Example.** $f(x) = x^{1/3}$: $f''(x) = -\frac{2}{9} x^{-5/3}$, which does not exist at $x = 0$. Yet concavity changes (from concave up on $(-\infty,0)$ to concave down on $(0,\infty)$... actually let's check: $f''(x) < 0$ for $x > 0$ and $f''(x) > 0$ for $x < 0$). So $x=0$ is an inflection point even though $f''(0)$ doesn't exist.

## Asymptotes: Detailed Analysis

### Vertical Asymptotes — Four Cases

At $x = a$ where $f$ is undefined, there are four possible one-sided behaviors:

$$\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = +\infty, \quad \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = -\infty$$ $$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = +\infty, \quad \lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = -\infty$$

These can occur in any combination independently. Sketch all four near the asymptote.

**Example.** $f(x) = \frac{1}{x(x-1)}$.

Near $x = 0$: numerator $\to 1 > 0$, $(x-1) \to -1 < 0$, so sign depends on $x$:

- $x \to 0^+$: denominator $\to 0^+(-1) = 0^-$, so $f \to -\infty$.
- $x \to 0^-$: denominator $\to 0^-(-1) = 0^+$, so $f \to +\infty$.

Near $x = 1$: numerator $\to 1$, $x \to 1$, so sign depends on $(x-1)$:

- $x \to 1^+$: $f \to +\infty$.
- $x \to 1^-$: $f \to -\infty$.

### Oblique Asymptotes — Why They Occur

Oblique asymptotes arise when a rational function has the numerator degree exceeding the denominator by exactly 1. The quotient in polynomial long division gives the asymptote.

**General formula.** If $f(x) = Q(x) + R(x)/D(x)$ where $\deg R < \deg D$, then $y = Q(x)$ is an asymptote as $|x| \to \infty$ (it need not be linear if $Q$ is quadratic, etc. — but those are called **curvilinear asymptotes**).

**Example.** $f(x) = \frac{x^3}{x^2 + 1}$.

$$f(x) = x - \frac{x}{x^2+1}$$

Asymptote: $y = x$ (oblique). The correction term $-x/(x^2+1) \to 0$ as $x \to \pm\infty$.

As $x \to +\infty$: $-x/(x^2+1) < 0$, so $f$ approaches $y = x$ **from below**. As $x \to -\infty$: $-x/(x^2+1) > 0$, so $f$ approaches $y = x$ **from above**.

> _Note._ A curve can cross a horizontal or oblique asymptote — the asymptote describes behavior at $\infty$, not a barrier.

## Graphing Rational Functions: Complete Algorithm

For $f(x) = P(x)/Q(x)$ with $\gcd(P,Q) = 1$:

### 1. Factor completely

$$f(x) = \frac{a(x-r_1)^{m_1}\cdots(x-r_k)^{m_k}}{b(x-s_1)^{n_1}\cdots(x-s_j)^{n_j}}$$

### 2. Identify zeros and poles

- **Zeros** of $f$: $x = r_i$ (with multiplicity $m_i$)
- **Poles** (vertical asymptotes): $x = s_i$ (with multiplicity $n_i$)

### 3. Behavior at zeros

At a zero $x = r$ of multiplicity $m$:

- $m$ odd: graph **crosses** the $x$-axis (sign change)
- $m$ even: graph **touches** the $x$-axis (no sign change, bounces back)

### 4. Behavior at poles

At a pole $x = s$ of multiplicity $n$:

- $n$ odd: graph goes to $+\infty$ on one side, $-\infty$ on the other (opposite signs)
- $n$ even: graph goes to $+\infty$ (or $-\infty$) on **both** sides (same sign)

### 5. Determine end behavior

Compare degrees of $P$ and $Q$ as in the asymptote section above.

### 6. Sign chart of $f(x)$

The sign of $f$ changes at each zero of odd multiplicity and at each pole of odd multiplicity. It does not change at even-multiplicity zeros or poles.

Pick a test point in each interval and determine $+/-$.

## Complete Worked Example: $f(x) = \frac{x^2(x+2)}{(x-1)^2}$

### Domain

$D = \mathbb{R} \setminus {1}$

### Symmetry

Neither even nor odd (mixed powers, asymmetric denominator).

### Intercepts

- $y$-intercept: $f(0) = 0$
- $x$-intercepts: $x = 0$ (multiplicity 2, **bounce**) and $x = -2$ (multiplicity 1, **cross**)

### Asymptotes

**Vertical:** $x = 1$ (pole of order 2 → same sign on both sides).

Near $x = 1$: numerator $\to 1^2 \cdot 3 = 3 > 0$, denominator $\to 0^+$ on both sides. $$\lim_{x \to 1^\pm} f(x) = +\infty$$

**Horizontal/Oblique:** $\deg P = 3$, $\deg Q = 2$, degree difference is 1 → oblique asymptote.

$$\frac{x^2(x+2)}{(x-1)^2} = \frac{x^3 + 2x^2}{x^2 - 2x + 1}$$

Long division: $$= x + 4 + \frac{7x - 4}{(x-1)^2}$$

Oblique asymptote: $y = x + 4$.

As $x \to +\infty$: remainder $\frac{7x-4}{(x-1)^2} \to 0^+$, so $f$ approaches from **above**. As $x \to -\infty$: remainder $\to 0^-$, so $f$ approaches from **below**.

### Derivative

$$f'(x) = \frac{[2x(x+2) + x^2](https://claude.ai/chat/x-1)^2 - x^2(x+2) \cdot 2(x-1)}{(x-1)^4}$$

Factor $(x-1)$ from numerator:

$$= \frac{(x-1)[x(3x+4)(x-1) - 2x^2(x+2)]}{(x-1)^4} = \frac{x(x^2 - x - 4)}{(x-1)^3}$$

Wait — let's redo this cleanly.

$$f'(x) = \frac{(3x^2+4x)(x-1)^2 - x^2(x+2)\cdot 2(x-1)}{(x-1)^4} = \frac{(x-1)[(3x^2+4x)(x-1) - 2x^2(x+2)]}{(x-1)^4}$$

$$= \frac{(3x^3+x^2-4x) - (2x^3+4x^2)}{(x-1)^3} = \frac{x^3 - 3x^2 - 4x}{(x-1)^3} = \frac{x(x^2-3x-4)}{(x-1)^3} = \frac{x(x-4)(x+1)}{(x-1)^3}$$

Critical numbers: $x = 0, -1, 4$ (where $f'=0$); $x=1$ (where $f'$ is undefined).

Sign chart of $f'(x) = \frac{x(x-4)(x+1)}{(x-1)^3}$:

|Interval|$x$|$(x-4)$|$(x+1)$|$(x-1)^3$|$f'$|$f$|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|$(-\infty,-1)$|$-$|$-$|$-$|$-$|$-$|↘|
|$(-1, 0)$|$-$|$-$|$+$|$-$|$+$|↗|
|$(0, 1)$|$+$|$-$|$+$|$-$|$-$|↘|
|$(1, 4)$|$+$|$-$|$+$|$+$|$-$|↘|
|$(4, \infty)$|$+$|$+$|$+$|$+$|$+$|↗|

Local min at $x=-1$: $f(-1) = \frac{1 \cdot 1}{4} = \frac{1}{4}$ Local max at $x=0$: $f(0) = 0$ Local min at $x=4$: $f(4) = \frac{16 \cdot 6}{9} = \frac{32}{3} \approx 10.67$

### Sketch Summary

```
        y
        |          /  (oblique asymptote y=x+4)
        |        /
     32/3+     *           ← local min at (4, 32/3)
        |    /    .....
        |   / ....
        | /...
    ----|--+-----|------+----------> x
```

## Graphing Transcendental Functions

### Exponential and Logarithmic

Key features of $f(x) = e^x$:

- $f'(x) = e^x > 0$ always → strictly increasing
- $f''(x) = e^x > 0$ always → always concave up
- No zeros, no extrema, no inflection points
- Horizontal asymptote $y = 0$ as $x \to -\infty$

Key features of $f(x) = \ln x$:

- Domain $(0,\infty)$
- $f'(x) = 1/x > 0$ → strictly increasing
- $f''(x) = -1/x^2 < 0$ → always concave down
- Zero at $x = 1$
- Vertical asymptote $x = 0^+$ ($f \to -\infty$)

### Trigonometric Functions

$f(x) = \sin x$:

- $f' = \cos x$: zero at $x = \pi/2 + n\pi$
- $f'' = -\sin x$: zero at $x = n\pi$ (inflection points at the zeros of $\sin$)
- Local maxima at $x = \pi/2 + 2n\pi$, local minima at $x = -\pi/2 + 2n\pi$

$f(x) = \tan x$:

- Domain: $\mathbb{R} \setminus {\pi/2 + n\pi}$
- $f' = \sec^2 x > 0$ always → strictly increasing on each branch
- $f'' = 2\sec^2 x \tan x$: changes sign at $x = n\pi$ (inflection)

### Mixed Functions: $f(x) = x^n e^{-x}$, $n \geq 1$

This family is fundamental in statistics (Gamma distributions) and ML.

$$f'(x) = x^{n-1}e^{-x}(n - x)$$

- Critical point at $x = n$ (local/global max on $(0,\infty)$, since $f \to 0$ at both ends)
- $f(n) = n^n e^{-n}$
- Inflection points at $x = n \pm \sqrt{n}$ (from $f'' = 0$, approximately)

As $n$ increases, the peak moves right and sharpens — the function becomes more concentrated. This is exactly how Gamma distributions behave.

## Graphing in Parametric Form

When a curve is given as $x = x(t)$, $y = y(t)$, the calculus of sketching generalizes naturally.

**Slope of tangent:** $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy/dt}{dx/dt}$$

**Concavity:** $$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = \frac{d(dy/dx)/dt}{dx/dt}$$

**Critical points:** $dy/dt = 0$ with $dx/dt \neq 0$ (horizontal tangent); $dx/dt = 0$ with $dy/dt \neq 0$ (vertical tangent).

**Example.** The cycloid $x = t - \sin t$, $y = 1 - \cos t$:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\sin t}{1 - \cos t}$$

Cusp at $t = 0$ (both derivatives $= 0$). As $t \to 0$:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{\sin t}{1-\cos t} = \frac{2\sin(t/2)\cos(t/2)}{2\sin^2(t/2)} = \cot(t/2) \to \infty$$

Vertical tangent at each cusp.

## Graphing in Polar Form

For $r = f(\theta)$ (MIT 18.02 emphasis):

The Cartesian coordinates are $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$.

**Slope:** $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{r'(\theta)\sin\theta + r(\theta)\cos\theta}{r'(\theta)\cos\theta - r(\theta)\sin\theta}$$

**Key features to identify:**

- **Loops:** values of $\theta$ where $r = 0$
- **Maximum radius:** maximize $r(\theta)$
- **Symmetry:** $f(-\theta) = f(\theta)$ (symmetric about polar axis); $f(\pi - \theta) = f(\theta)$ (symmetric about $\theta = \pi/2$)

**Example.** Cardioid $r = 1 + \cos\theta$:

- $r = 0$ when $\theta = \pi$
- Maximum $r = 2$ at $\theta = 0$
- Symmetric about polar axis
- Cusp at origin (since $r = 0$ and $r'(\pi) = \sin\pi = 0$)

**Example.** Rose curve $r = \cos(n\theta)$:

- $n$ petals if $n$ is odd; $2n$ petals if $n$ is even
- Each petal is traced as $\theta$ sweeps an interval of length $\pi/n$

## Pathological Cases and What They Teach

### Case 1: Cusps

$f(x) = x^{2/3}$: $f'(x) = \frac{2}{3}x^{-1/3}$, which $\to \pm\infty$ as $x \to 0^\pm$.

The graph has a **cusp** at the origin — both tangent slopes blow up but from opposite signs. Not a corner (which has two finite different slopes), not a smooth point. The EVT still applies on $[-1,1]$: the minimum $f(0) = 0$ is achieved even though $f'(0)$ doesn't exist.

### Case 2: Corners

$f(x) = |x|$: $f'(0^+) = 1$, $f'(0^-) = -1$. A **corner** — both one-sided derivatives exist and are finite, but unequal. Not differentiable, but still a local minimum.

### Case 3: Vertical Tangent Without Asymptote

$f(x) = x^{1/3}$: $f'(0) = +\infty$ (in the limit sense). The graph passes _through_ the origin with a vertical tangent. This is distinct from a vertical asymptote (where the graph does not pass through).

### Case 4: Oscillation

$f(x) = x \sin(1/x)$ for $x \neq 0$, $f(0) = 0$. The function is continuous at 0 (by squeeze theorem: $|f(x)| \leq |x| \to 0$), but oscillates infinitely often near 0. The derivative does not exist at 0. Classic example showing continuity $\not\Rightarrow$ differentiability (especially dramatically near a point of accumulation of oscillations).

## Optimization: Graphing in Service of Extrema

### Closed Interval Method (Absolute Extrema)

To find the **absolute** maximum and minimum of a continuous $f$ on $[a,b]$:

1. Find all critical numbers $c$ in $(a,b)$.
2. Evaluate $f$ at each $c$ and at the endpoints $a$, $b$.
3. The largest value is the absolute max; the smallest is the absolute min.

**Example.** $f(x) = x^3 - 3x + 1$ on $[-2, 2]$.

$f'(x) = 3x^2 - 3 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1$.

|$x$|$f(x)$|
|---|---|
|$-2$|$-8+6+1 = -1$|
|$-1$|$-1+3+1 = 3$|
|$1$|$1-3+1 = -1$|
|$2$|$8-6+1 = 3$|

Absolute maximum: $3$ at $x = \pm 1$ and... wait: $f(-1) = 3$ and $f(2) = 3$. Two absolute maxima. Absolute minimum: $-1$ at $x = -2$ and $x = 1$.

### Unbounded Domains

On $(-\infty, \infty)$ or half-lines, check:

- All critical points
- Behavior as $x \to \pm\infty$

If $f(x) \to \infty$ at both ends and has a unique critical point, that point is the absolute minimum (no EVT guarantee needed — analyze directly).

## The Derivative Portrait: Reading Graphs of $f'$

Given a graph of $f'$ (not $f$), recover the graph of $f$:

|Feature of $f'$ graph|Implication for $f$|
|---|---|
|$f'(x) > 0$|$f$ increasing|
|$f'(x) < 0$|$f$ decreasing|
|$f'(x) = 0$ (zero crossing $+ \to -$)|local max of $f$|
|$f'(x) = 0$ (zero crossing $- \to +$)|local min of $f$|
|$f'$ has local max or min at $c$|inflection point of $f$ at $c$|
|$f'$ is increasing|$f$ concave up|
|$f'$ is decreasing|$f$ concave down|

This skill is tested heavily in both Stewart and MIT 18.02 problem sets, and is exactly the skill that appears in understanding loss landscapes in ML.

## Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 1. Activation Functions as Transcendental Curve Sketching

Every activation function is a curve sketching problem. The design of activation functions in deep learning is guided by exactly the features studied in this note:

**ReLU:** $f(x) = \max(0,x)$

Derivative: $f'(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < 0 \ 1 & x > 0 \end{cases}$ (undefined at 0)

- Corner at origin (Step 3 above: pathological case)
- Concavity: $f''(x) = 0$ everywhere except $x = 0$ (linear on both sides)
- Problem: $f'(x) = 0$ for $x < 0$ → **dead neurons**: gradient is zero, no learning signal flows back through inactive neurons

**Leaky ReLU:** $f(x) = \max(\alpha x, x)$, $0 < \alpha < 1$

Fixes dying neurons by giving a small nonzero slope on the negative side.

**GELU (Gaussian Error Linear Unit):** $f(x) = x \Phi(x)$ where $\Phi$ is the CDF of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

- Smooth approximation to ReLU
- Has a local minimum near $x \approx -0.17$ (a slight dip)
- $f'(x) = \Phi(x) + x\phi(x)$ where $\phi$ is the standard normal PDF
- Used in BERT, GPT, and most modern transformers

The choice between activations is exactly the question: which curve has the right derivative behavior (no vanishing, no dying, smooth, bounded)?

### 2. Convex vs. Non-Convex Loss Landscapes

A function is convex iff $f'' \geq 0$ everywhere. In higher dimensions, the analog is: the Hessian matrix $H$ is positive semidefinite everywhere.

**Convex losses (e.g., logistic regression, SVM):**

- Every local minimum is a global minimum (consequence of concavity analysis)
- Gradient descent converges
- The loss curve looks like a parabola (or higher-dimensional bowl)

**Non-convex losses (deep neural networks):**

- $f''$ changes sign in many places → many inflection points in the loss landscape
- Multiple local minima, saddle points, and flat regions
- Saddle points are critical: $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 0$ but the Hessian has both positive and negative eigenvalues (indefinite)

The geometry of saddle points is: $f''(c) = 0$, $f'''(c) \neq 0$ (or in higher dimensions, the Hessian is indefinite). Gradient descent near a saddle point slows to a crawl — the gradient is small but the point is not a minimum. This is why momentum-based optimizers (Adam, RMSProp) are essential for deep learning.

### 3. Second-Order Methods and the Hessian

The **Newton step** for optimization is:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - H^{-1} \nabla f$$

where $H = \nabla^2 f$ is the Hessian. In 1D, this is $\theta \leftarrow \theta - f'(\theta)/f''(\theta)$.

Geometrically: Newton's method uses the local quadratic approximation (Taylor expansion to second order) to jump directly to the minimum of the parabola. This works perfectly for convex quadratics and very well near the minimum of any smooth strongly convex function.

The problem: inverting $H$ costs $O(n^3)$ for $n$ parameters. A GPT-3 has $n \sim 175 \times 10^9$. Inverting that matrix is impossible. Hence:

- **L-BFGS:** approximates $H^{-1}$ using finite-difference estimates
- **Adam:** uses diagonal approximation to $H$ (i.e., only tracks $\partial^2 f / \partial \theta_i^2$)
- **Shampoo, K-FAC:** use structured approximations to the Hessian

All of these are curve-sketching (using $f''$) in disguise.

### 4. The Bias-Variance Tradeoff as Overfitting Geometry

A polynomial of degree $n$ fitted to data is a curve. The curve-sketching question: how many oscillations does it have?

A degree-$n$ polynomial has at most $n-1$ local extrema. Fitting high-degree polynomials to a small dataset produces a wildly oscillating curve — **overfitting**. Regularization (L2 = weight decay) penalizes large coefficients, pulling the curve back toward a smoother, flatter shape.

This is concavity control applied to the model, not the loss.

### 5. Taylor Expansion and Approximation Theory

The Taylor polynomial of $f$ at $a$:

$$f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a) + \frac{f''(a)}{2}(x-a)^2 + \cdots$$

This is the _local curve_ of $f$ near $a$ expressed in terms of derivatives. Neural networks can be understood as building _global_ approximations using local (layer-by-layer) Taylor-like approximations. The expressiveness of a network with ReLU activations comes precisely from its ability to produce piecewise-linear approximations to curved functions — with more layers, the approximation is finer, the effective number of linear pieces grows exponentially with depth.
