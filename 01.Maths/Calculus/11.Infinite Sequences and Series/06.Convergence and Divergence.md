## 1. The Central Question

Given a series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$, we ask:

$$\text{Does } S_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k \text{ approach a finite limit as } n \to \infty?$$

If yes: **convergent**. If no: **divergent**.

This is deceptively simple to state and genuinely difficult to settle in general. The tests we develop are not arbitrary tricks — each one probes a specific structural reason why partial sums may or may not stabilise.

**The fundamental hierarchy of difficulty:**

```
Level 0:  a_n → 0?             (Divergence Test — fastest check)
Level 1:  Recognise the form   (Geometric, p-series, telescoping)
Level 2:  Compare to known     (Comparison, Limit Comparison)
Level 3:  Self-referential      (Ratio Test, Root Test)
Level 4:  Signed series         (AST, Dirichlet, Abel)
Level 5:  Functions of x        (Uniform convergence, Weierstrass M)
```

---

## 2. Definitions — Precise and Complete

**Definition (Convergence).** The series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n$ **converges** to $L \in \mathbb{R}$ if:

$$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N} \text{ such that } n > N \implies \left|S_n - L\right| < \varepsilon$$

where $S_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_k$. We write $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n = L$.

**Definition (Divergence).** The series **diverges** if no such $L$ exists. This includes:

- $S_n \to +\infty$
- $S_n \to -\infty$
- $S_n$ oscillates with no limit

**Definition (Absolute Convergence).** $\sum a_n$ is **absolutely convergent** if $\sum |a_n|$ converges.

**Definition (Conditional Convergence).** $\sum a_n$ is **conditionally convergent** if $\sum a_n$ converges but $\sum |a_n|$ diverges.

**Theorem.** Absolutely convergent $\Rightarrow$ convergent. (Proof: see [[#5. Absolute vs. Conditional Convergence]])

**The converse is false:** $\sum \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n} = \ln 2$ converges, but $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges.

## 3. Geometric Intuition for Convergence vs. Divergence

### 3.1 The Race Between Terms and Growth

A series $\sum a_n$ converges when the terms $a_n$ shrink _fast enough_ to prevent $S_n$ from escaping to infinity.

The boundary cases reveal the sharpness required:

|Series|$a_n$|Verdict|Why|
|---|---|---|---|
|$\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$|$\sim \frac{1}{n^2}$|**Converges**|Shrinks fast enough|
|$\sum \frac{1}{n}$|$\sim \frac{1}{n}$|**Diverges**|Just barely too slow|
|$\sum \frac{1}{n \ln n}$|slower than $\frac{1}{n}$|**Diverges**|Still too slow|
|$\sum \frac{1}{n(\ln n)^2}$||**Converges**|Fast enough|

The boundary between convergence and divergence is not a cliff — it is an infinitely fine line. The $p$-series $\sum \frac{1}{n^p}$ converges iff $p > 1$: the critical exponent is exactly 1.

### 3.2 Visualisation: Phase Diagram of the p-Series

```
p
│
│  2 ─── Converges: Σ1/n² = π²/6
│        ↑ faster decay
│  1.5 ─ Converges: Σ1/n^(3/2)
│
│  1.0 ──────────────── BOUNDARY (harmonic, diverges)
│        ↓ slower decay
│  0.5 ─ Diverges: Σ1/√n
│
│  0 ─── Diverges: Σ1 (terms don't vanish)
│
└──────────────────────────────────→ Convergent/Divergent
```

### 3.3 Partial Sum Geometry

```
Converging series (terms shrink fast):       Diverging series (terms shrink too slowly):

S_n                                          S_n
│                    ─────── L               │
│            ────────                        │              ╱
│      ──────                                │           ╱
│   ───                                      │        ╱
│───                                         │     ╱
│                                            │  ╱
└──────────────────→ n                       └──────────────────→ n

Partial sums plateau.                        Partial sums grow without bound.
```

## 4. The Divergence Test — First Line of Defence

**Theorem (Divergence Test / $n$-th Term Test).**

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \text{ converges} \implies \lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = 0$$

**Contrapositive (operationally useful):**

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n \neq 0 \text{ (or DNE)} \implies \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \text{ diverges}$$

**Proof.** If $S_n \to L$, then: $$\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = \lim_{n \to \infty}(S_n - S_{n-1}) = L - L = 0 \qquad \square$$

**The test only works one way.** $a_n \to 0$ is necessary but not sufficient for convergence.

**Always apply this test first.** It costs almost nothing and eliminates many cases immediately.

### Examples

|Series|$\lim a_n$|Conclusion|
|---|---|---|
|$\sum \frac{n}{n+1}$|$1 \neq 0$|**Diverges** (DT)|
|$\sum \cos n$|DNE|**Diverges** (DT)|
|$\sum \frac{1}{n}$|$0$|DT inconclusive — use other tests|
|$\sum \frac{(-1)^n n}{2n+1}$|$\pm \frac{1}{2} \neq 0$|**Diverges** (DT)|

## 5. Absolute vs. Conditional Convergence

### The Implication Chain

$$\text{Absolutely convergent} \implies \text{Convergent}$$ $$\text{Conditionally convergent} \centernot\implies \text{Absolutely convergent}$$

**Proof that absolute convergence implies convergence.**

Observe: $0 \leq a_n + |a_n| \leq 2|a_n|$.

If $\sum |a_n|$ converges, then by Comparison, $\sum(a_n + |a_n|)$ converges.

Then: $$\sum a_n = \sum\bigl(a_n + |a_n|\bigr) - \sum |a_n|$$

is the difference of two convergent series, hence convergent. $\square$

### The Conditional Convergence Phenomenon

Conditional convergence is _fragile_. It works because positive and negative terms cancel in just the right way. Disturbing this balance — by rearranging terms — can destroy it entirely.

**The canonical example:** $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n} = \ln 2 \approx 0.6931$

This converges conditionally. The absolute series $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges.

### Why Absolute Convergence is "Safer"

For an absolutely convergent series:

- Any rearrangement converges to the same sum (Dirichlet's Theorem)
- Products of absolutely convergent series converge (Cauchy product)
- Term-by-term operations are safe

For conditionally convergent series: **none of these hold in general.**

## 6. The Integral Test and p-Series

### The Integral Test

**Setup.** Let $f: [1, \infty) \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous, positive, and _decreasing_, with $f(n) = a_n$.

**Theorem.** $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n \text{ converges} \iff \int_1^{\infty} f(x),dx \text{ converges}$$

**Proof idea.** Compare rectangles of width 1 and height $f(n)$ to the area under $f$:

$$\int_1^{n+1} f(x),dx \leq \sum_{k=1}^{n} f(k) \leq f(1) + \int_1^{n} f(x),dx$$

```
f(x)
│ █
│ █ █
│ █ █ █
│ █ █ █ █
│ █ █ █ █ █
│─────────────────→ x
  1 2 3 4 5

Left rectangles overestimate integral.
Right rectangles underestimate integral.
Both have the same convergence behaviour.
```

If $\int_1^\infty f,dx$ is finite, the partial sums are squeezed to be bounded; if infinite, they grow without bound. $\square$

**Important:** The test tells us _whether_ the series converges — not _what_ it converges to. The sum $\neq$ the integral.

### The p-Series Theorem

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^p} \begin{cases} \text{converges} & p > 1 \ \text{diverges} & p \leq 1 \end{cases}$$

**Proof.** Apply the Integral Test: $\displaystyle\int_1^\infty x^{-p},dx = \left[\frac{x^{1-p}}{1-p}\right]_1^\infty$.

- If $p > 1$: $x^{1-p} \to 0$ as $x \to \infty$. Integral $= \frac{1}{p-1}$. Converges.
- If $p < 1$: $x^{1-p} \to \infty$. Integral diverges.
- If $p = 1$: $\int_1^\infty \frac{dx}{x} = \ln x \big|_1^\infty = \infty$. Diverges. $\square$

**Memorise the $p$-series table:**

|$p$|Series|Value (if convergent)|
|---|---|---|
|$p = 2$|$\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$|$\frac{\pi^2}{6}$ (Basel)|
|$p = 3$|$\sum \frac{1}{n^3}$|Apéry's constant $\approx 1.202$|
|$p = 4$|$\sum \frac{1}{n^4}$|$\frac{\pi^4}{90}$|
|$p = 1$|$\sum \frac{1}{n}$|$\infty$ (harmonic)|
|$p = 1/2$|$\sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$|$\infty$|

### Error Estimation via Integral Test

If $\sum a_n$ converges by the Integral Test, the tail is bounded:

$$\int_{n+1}^{\infty} f(x),dx \leq R_n \leq \int_n^{\infty} f(x),dx$$

This gives a _computable_ error bound from a convergent improper integral.

**Example.** For $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$, after $N$ terms: $R_N \leq \int_N^\infty x^{-2},dx = \frac{1}{N}$. For error $< 0.001$: need $N \geq 1000$.

## 7. The Comparison Test

**Theorem (Direct Comparison Test).** Let $0 \leq a_n \leq b_n$ for all $n \geq N_0$.

$$\sum b_n \text{ converges} \implies \sum a_n \text{ converges}$$ $$\sum a_n \text{ diverges} \implies \sum b_n \text{ diverges}$$

**Proof.** If $\sum b_n$ converges, the partial sums $S_n^{(b)}$ are bounded above by some $M$. Then $S_n^{(a)} \leq S_n^{(b)} \leq M$, so ${S_n^{(a)}}$ is increasing and bounded, hence convergent. $\square$

**The logical structure:**

```
Convergent universe          Divergent universe
(dominated by known good)    (dominates known bad)

  CONVERGENT                   DIVERGENT
   ┌──────────┐                 ┌──────────┐
   │  Σb_n   │                 │  Σa_n   │
   │  ↑       │                 │  ↓       │
   │  Σa_n  │                 │  Σb_n   │
   └──────────┘                 └──────────┘
   If Σb_n converges,           If Σa_n diverges,
   then Σa_n converges.         then Σb_n diverges.
```

**Strategy:** Find a benchmark series (usually $p$-series or geometric) that sandwiches $a_n$.

### Examples

**1.** $\displaystyle\sum \frac{1}{n^2 + \sin^2 n}$: Since $n^2 + \sin^2 n \geq n^2$, we have $\frac{1}{n^2 + \sin^2 n} \leq \frac{1}{n^2}$. Since $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges, so does the original. ✓

**2.** $\displaystyle\sum \frac{\ln n}{n}$: For $n \geq 3$, $\ln n > 1$, so $\frac{\ln n}{n} > \frac{1}{n}$. Since $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges, so does the original. ✓

## 8. The Limit Comparison Test

**Motivation.** Direct comparison requires a clean inequality. When terms are complicated, we compare _asymptotically_.

**Theorem (Limit Comparison Test).** Let $a_n, b_n > 0$ and:

$$L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n}$$

|Value of $L$|Conclusion|
|---|---|
|$0 < L < \infty$|$\sum a_n$ and $\sum b_n$ **both converge or both diverge**|
|$L = 0$|If $\sum b_n$ converges, then $\sum a_n$ converges|
|$L = \infty$|If $\sum b_n$ diverges, then $\sum a_n$ diverges|

**Proof (for $0 < L < \infty$).** Since $\frac{a_n}{b_n} \to L$, for large $n$: $\frac{L}{2} < \frac{a_n}{b_n} < \frac{3L}{2}$, i.e.:

$$\frac{L}{2} b_n < a_n < \frac{3L}{2} b_n$$

Apply the Direct Comparison Test to both inequalities. $\square$

**Practical rule:** Identify the dominant term of $a_n$ for large $n$. Use that as $b_n$.

### Examples

**1.** $\displaystyle\sum \frac{3n^2 - 2n + 1}{5n^4 + n - 7}$: dominant terms give $a_n \sim \frac{3n^2}{5n^4} = \frac{3}{5n^2}$.

LCT with $b_n = \frac{1}{n^2}$: $\frac{a_n}{b_n} \to \frac{3}{5} \in (0,\infty)$. Since $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges, so does the original. ✓

**2.** $\displaystyle\sum \frac{1}{\sqrt{n^2 - 1}}$ (for $n \geq 2$): $a_n \sim \frac{1}{n}$.

LCT with $b_n = \frac{1}{n}$: $\frac{a_n}{b_n} = \frac{n}{\sqrt{n^2-1}} \to 1$. Since $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges, the original diverges. ✓

## 9. The Ratio Test

**Ideal for:** factorials, exponentials, products involving $n$.

**Theorem.** Let $L = \displaystyle\lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right|$ (or $\limsup$ if the limit doesn't exist).

|$L$|Conclusion|
|---|---|
|$L < 1$|Converges **absolutely**|
|$L > 1$|Diverges|
|$L = 1$|**Inconclusive**|

**Proof (convergence case, $L < 1$).** Pick $r$ with $L < r < 1$. For large $n$: $\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| < r$, so $|a_n| < |a_{N}| \cdot r^{n-N}$ for $n > N$. This is dominated by the geometric series $|a_N| \sum r^{n-N}$, which converges since $r < 1$. $\square$

**Why $L=1$ is inconclusive:**

|Series|$L$|Verdict|
|---|---|---|
|$\sum \frac{1}{n}$|1|Diverges|
|$\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$|1|Converges|

Both give $L=1$ under the Ratio Test. The test is blind at the boundary.

### The Ratio Test Procedure

```
Compute |a_{n+1}/a_n|, simplify, take limit.

Common simplifications:
  (n+1)! / n! = n+1
  (n+1)^n / n^n = (1 + 1/n)^n → e
  r^{n+1} / r^n = r
```

### Example

$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{n^2}{2^n}$:

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{(n+1)^2}{2^{n+1}} \cdot \frac{2^n}{n^2} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{n+1}{n}\right)^2 \to \frac{1}{2} < 1$$

**Converges absolutely.** ✓

## 10. The Root Test

**Ideal for:** terms of the form $(f(n))^n$.

**Theorem.** Let $L = \displaystyle\lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|}$ (or $\limsup$).

Same decision table as the Ratio Test.

**Proof.** If $L < 1$, pick $r \in (L, 1)$. For large $n$: $\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} < r$, so $|a_n| < r^n$. Since $\sum r^n$ converges ($r < 1$, geometric), by Comparison $\sum |a_n|$ converges. $\square$

**Connection to Ratio Test.** If $\lim |a_{n+1}/a_n| = L$, then $\lim \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = L$ as well. The Root Test is strictly stronger — it works in cases where the Ratio Test limit doesn't exist (using $\limsup$).

### Example

$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{2n+3}{5n-1}\right)^n$:

$$\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = \frac{2n+3}{5n-1} \to \frac{2}{5} < 1$$

**Converges absolutely.** ✓

## 11. The Alternating Series Test (Leibniz)

**Setup.** A series of the form $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} (-1)^{n-1} b_n$ with $b_n > 0$.

**Theorem (AST).** If:

1. $b_{n+1} \leq b_n$ for all $n$ (decreasing)
2. $\lim_{n \to \infty} b_n = 0$

Then the alternating series **converges**.

**Proof.** Consider even and odd partial sums separately.

$S_{2m} = (b_1 - b_2) + (b_3 - b_4) + \cdots + (b_{2m-1} - b_{2m})$

Each bracket is $\geq 0$ (since $b_n$ decreasing), so ${S_{2m}}$ is increasing.

Also: $S_{2m} = b_1 - (b_2 - b_3) - \cdots - (b_{2m-2} - b_{2m-1}) - b_{2m} \leq b_1$, so ${S_{2m}}$ is bounded above.

By the Monotone Convergence Theorem, $S_{2m} \to L$ for some $L$.

Similarly, $S_{2m+1} = S_{2m} + b_{2m+1} \to L + 0 = L$ (since $b_n \to 0$).

Both subsequences converge to $L$, so $S_n \to L$. $\square$

### The AST Error Bound

$$|L - S_n| \leq b_{n+1}$$

The error of stopping at the $n$-th partial sum is at most the _next term_. This is an exceptionally clean and useful estimate.

**Example.** $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n} = \ln 2$. After 100 terms: error $\leq \frac{1}{101} \approx 0.0099$.

### Convergence Geometry of Alternating Series

```
The partial sums straddle and close in on the limit:

b₁
●───────────────────────────────────────────
  S₁ = b₁

  S₂ = b₁ - b₂
  ●────────────────────────────
                                   L ←──── limit

        S₃ = S₂ + b₃
        ●────────────────────────────────
                ← b_{n+1} bound →
                S₄ = S₃ - b₄
                ●─────────────────
                        S₅
                        ●──────────────
                        ...→ L

Each partial sum overshoots, then corrects — oscillating toward L.
```

## 12. Dirichlet's Test and Abel's Test

These are the more powerful generalisations behind the AST. Rarely proved in introductory courses but worth knowing.

### Dirichlet's Test

**Theorem.** If ${A_n}$ (partial sums of ${a_n}$) is **bounded**, and $b_n \searrow 0$ monotonically, then $\sum a_n b_n$ converges.

**Proof sketch.** Apply Abel's Summation by Parts (see [[Partial Sums#9. Partial Summation]]). The boundary term $A_n b_{n+1} \to 0$ (bounded times zero). The sum $\sum A_n(b_{n+1}-b_n)$ converges absolutely by Comparison since $|A_n| \leq M$ and $\sum |b_{n+1}-b_n| = b_1 - \lim b_n = b_1$ (telescoping). $\square$

**AST as a special case:** Take $a_n = (-1)^n$; then $|A_n| \leq 1$ (bounded). ✓

**Application:** $\sum \frac{\sin n}{n}$ converges. Here $a_n = \sin n$ has bounded partial sums (geometric series of complex exponentials), $b_n = \frac{1}{n} \searrow 0$.

### Abel's Test

**Theorem.** If $\sum a_n$ **converges** and ${b_n}$ is **monotone and bounded**, then $\sum a_n b_n$ converges.

This is a companion to Dirichlet's: convergence of $\sum a_n$ is stronger than bounded partial sums, so the condition on $b_n$ is weaker.

## 13. Rearrangements — When Convergence Becomes Fragile

### Absolutely Convergent Series

**Theorem (Dirichlet).** If $\sum a_n$ converges absolutely and $\sigma: \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{N}$ is any bijection (rearrangement), then $\sum a_{\sigma(n)}$ converges to the same sum.

**Proof idea.** The tail of any rearrangement is controlled by the tail of $\sum |a_n|$, which goes to zero. $\square$

### The Riemann Rearrangement Theorem

**Theorem (Riemann).** If $\sum a_n$ converges **conditionally**, then for any $M \in \mathbb{R} \cup {+\infty, -\infty}$, there exists a rearrangement $\sum a_{\sigma(n)} = M$.

**Proof construction (for $M \in \mathbb{R}$).** Let $p_1, p_2, \ldots$ be the positive terms and $q_1, q_2, \ldots$ the absolute values of negative terms. Both $\sum p_n = \infty$ and $\sum q_n = \infty$ (otherwise the series would be absolutely convergent or divergent outright).

Strategy: take just enough positive terms to exceed $M$, then just enough negative terms to go below $M$, then repeat. The partial sums oscillate around $M$ with decreasing amplitude (since $a_n \to 0$), converging to $M$. $\square$

**What this means:** The sum of a conditionally convergent series is not a property of the _set_ of terms — it depends on their _order_. Addition is not commutative for conditionally convergent series.

> _This is one of the most disturbing theorems in analysis. It says that the value $\ln 2 = 1 - \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{3} - \cdots$ is not a fact about the numbers ${1, \frac{1}{2}, \frac{1}{3}, \ldots}$ — it is a fact about how they are arranged._

## 14. Uniform vs. Pointwise Convergence of Series of Functions

When a series involves a parameter $x$, the notion of convergence splits into two.

**Definition (Pointwise Convergence).** $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} f_n(x) = f(x)$ pointwise on $E$ if for each fixed $x \in E$:

$$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists N(x) \text{ such that } n > N(x) \implies |S_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon$$

Here $N$ may depend on $x$.

**Definition (Uniform Convergence).** $\sum f_n(x) \to f(x)$ uniformly on $E$ if:

$$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists N \text{ (independent of } x) \text{ such that } n > N \implies |S_n(x) - f(x)| < \varepsilon \text{ for all } x \in E$$

**The difference:** In uniform convergence, the same $N$ works for every $x$ simultaneously.

### Why Uniform Convergence Matters

|Property|Pointwise|Uniform|
|---|---|---|
|$\lim S_n$ continuous if each $f_n$ continuous?|**No**|**Yes**|
|$\int \lim S_n = \lim \int S_n$?|**No**|**Yes**|
|$\frac{d}{dx} \lim S_n = \lim \frac{d}{dx} S_n$?|**No**|**Yes** (with extra condition)|

### The Weierstrass M-Test

**Theorem.** If $|f_n(x)| \leq M_n$ for all $x \in E$, and $\sum M_n$ converges, then $\sum f_n(x)$ converges **uniformly and absolutely** on $E$.

**Proof.** For any $x$: $|R_n(x)| = \left|\sum_{k=n+1}^{\infty} f_k(x)\right| \leq \sum_{k=n+1}^{\infty} |f_k(x)| \leq \sum_{k=n+1}^{\infty} M_k \to 0$. Since the bound is independent of $x$, convergence is uniform. $\square$

**Power series application.** On $|x - a| \leq r < R$: $|c_n(x-a)^n| \leq |c_n| r^n =: M_n$. The Ratio Test shows $\sum M_n$ converges. By M-Test, the power series converges uniformly on $|x-a| \leq r$ — justifying term-by-term differentiation and integration.

## 15. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

Convergence and divergence are not abstract concerns confined to analysis courses. They govern whether training algorithms work, whether models generalise, and whether computations are numerically stable.

---

### 15.1 Convergence of Training: Loss as a Series

During gradient descent, the sequence of losses $\mathcal{L}_0, \mathcal{L}_1, \mathcal{L}_2, \ldots$ is tracked. The _change_ in loss at each step is $\Delta_t = \mathcal{L}_t - \mathcal{L}_{t+1} \geq 0$ (ideally).

The total improvement over all steps is the series:

$$\sum_{t=0}^{\infty} \Delta_t = \mathcal{L}_0 - \lim_{t\to\infty} \mathcal{L}_t$$

For this to have a finite limit, we need $\sum \Delta_t < \infty$, which requires $\Delta_t \to 0$. This is the Divergence Test in action: **if the loss doesn't eventually stop decreasing, training hasn't converged.**

For **convex losses** with $L$-smooth gradients, one proves:

$$\sum_{t=0}^{T} |\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)|^2 \leq \frac{2(\mathcal{L}_0 - \mathcal{L}^*)}{\eta}$$

This is a finite upper bound on the _series_ of squared gradient norms. The left side is a partial sum — and its boundedness (independent of $T$) implies $|\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)|^2 \to 0$: the Divergence Test guarantees eventual convergence to a stationary point.

---

### 15.2 Absolute Convergence and Numerical Stability

In floating-point arithmetic, the order of summation _matters_ — not because of Riemann rearrangement (that's infinite series), but because floating-point addition is not exactly associative.

However, for absolutely convergent series (with terms decaying fast), the reordering error is bounded. For conditionally convergent-like sums (many large positive and negative terms cancelling), catastrophic cancellation can occur.

**Practical consequence:** The **log-sum-exp trick** used in softmax computation:

$$\log \sum_i e^{x_i} = c + \log \sum_i e^{x_i - c}, \quad c = \max_i x_i$$

is precisely a stabilisation of a sum that would otherwise involve catastrophic cancellation when some $x_i$ are very large (the series of $e^{x_i}$ without normalisation would be dominated by the largest term, with the others contributing negligible but numerically unstable fractions).

---

### 15.3 Learning Rate and the Robbins-Monro Conditions

For stochastic gradient descent (SGD) to converge, the learning rate schedule ${\eta_t}$ must satisfy:

$$\sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \eta_t = \infty \qquad \text{(series diverges)}$$ $$\sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \eta_t^2 < \infty \qquad \text{(series converges)}$$

The first condition ensures the algorithm can _reach_ any point in parameter space — the steps must be large enough in total. The second ensures the steps shrink fast enough that the noise in stochastic gradients averages out.

**The standard choice:** $\eta_t = \frac{c}{t}$ satisfies both:

- $\sum \frac{1}{t} = \infty$ ✓ (harmonic series diverges)
- $\sum \frac{1}{t^2} < \infty$ ✓ ($p$-series with $p=2$ converges)

The classic choice $\eta_t = \frac{c}{\sqrt{t}}$ does _not_ satisfy the second condition ($\sum \frac{1}{t}$ diverges), and so SGD with this schedule has persistent noise — which is sometimes desirable (implicit regularisation) but prevents exact convergence.

This is a direct application of $p$-series convergence to algorithm design.

---

### 15.4 Spectral Analysis and Convergence of Neural Networks

The **singular values** $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots$ of a weight matrix $W$ determine the stability of forward and backward propagation. The **spectral norm** $|W| = \sigma_1$ controls how much the layer amplifies its input.

For a deep network with $L$ layers, the output norm is bounded by:

$$|f(x)| \leq |x| \cdot \prod_{l=1}^{L} |W_l|$$

The product $\prod |W_l|$ can be written as $\exp!\left(\sum_{l=1}^{L} \log |W_l|\right)$. This exponent is a partial sum. If $\log |W_l| > 0$ for all $l$ (expanding layers), the series diverges and gradients **explode**. If $\log |W_l| < 0$ (contracting layers), the series becomes very negative and gradients **vanish**.

The vanishing/exploding gradient problem is, fundamentally, a convergence/divergence problem for the series $\sum \log |W_l|$.

**Mitigations:**

- **Batch normalisation:** forces $|W_l| \approx 1$, making $\log |W_l| \approx 0$ — the series stays bounded.
- **Residual connections (ResNets):** $x_{l+1} = x_l + F(x_l)$ changes the product of norms into a sum, which is more controllable.
- **Spectral normalisation:** explicitly constrains $|W_l| = 1$, guaranteeing the product is 1.

---

### 15.5 Convergence of Infinite-Width Neural Networks

In the limit of infinite width, neural networks converge (in distribution) to **Gaussian Processes** — a result known as the NNGP correspondence. The proof involves showing that the pre-activations at each layer converge in distribution to a Gaussian as width $\to \infty$.

The key step uses the **Central Limit Theorem**, which is itself a statement about convergence: the normalised sum $\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{k=1}^n X_k$ converges in distribution to $\mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$.

The series of variance contributions from each weight must converge to a finite limit — this is precisely the condition that the kernel of the Gaussian Process is well-defined.

---

### 15.6 Convergence of MCMC and Variational Inference

**Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)** produces a sequence of samples $\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots$ from a target distribution $\pi$. The empirical average:

$$\hat{\mu}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{t=1}^{n} f(\theta_t)$$

is a partial sum (normalised). The **Ergodic Theorem** guarantees $\hat{\mu}_n \to \mathbb{E}_\pi[f]$ — this is a convergence theorem for a specific class of partial sums.

The **rate** of convergence (how large $n$ must be for $|\hat{\mu}_n - \mathbb{E}[f]| < \varepsilon$) is determined by the **mixing time** of the Markov chain — which is itself controlled by the spectral gap (eigenvalue analysis), connecting back to series convergence.

## 16. Worked Examples — Full Solutions

### Example 1: Full Convergence Analysis

**Determine whether $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n \cdot 3^n}{n! + 2^n}$ converges.**

**Step 1 — Divergence Test:** $a_n = \frac{n \cdot 3^n}{n! + 2^n}$. For large $n$, $n! \gg 2^n$, so $a_n \approx \frac{n \cdot 3^n}{n!} \to 0$ (factorials dominate exponentials). DT inconclusive.

**Step 2 — Ratio Test:**

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \frac{(n+1) \cdot 3^{n+1}}{(n+1)! + 2^{n+1}} \cdot \frac{n! + 2^n}{n \cdot 3^n}$$

For large $n$: numerator $\sim \frac{(n+1) \cdot 3^{n+1}}{(n+1)!}$, denominator $\sim \frac{n \cdot 3^n}{n!}$.

$$\sim \frac{(n+1) \cdot 3^{n+1}}{(n+1)!} \cdot \frac{n!}{n \cdot 3^n} = \frac{3(n+1)}{(n+1) \cdot n} \cdot \frac{n!}{n!/(n)} = \frac{3}{n} \to 0 < 1$$

**Converges absolutely.** ✓

---

### Example 2: The Subtlety of $L = 1$ in the Ratio Test

**Compare $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ and $\sum \frac{1}{n^2}$.**

For $a_n = \frac{1}{n}$: $\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} = \frac{n}{n+1} \to 1$. Ratio Test: inconclusive. (Diverges.)

For $b_n = \frac{1}{n^2}$: $\frac{b_{n+1}}{b_n} = \frac{n^2}{(n+1)^2} \to 1$. Ratio Test: inconclusive. (Converges.)

**Moral:** When the Ratio Test gives $L = 1$, the test has no opinion. The two series represent _opposite_ verdicts, both invisible to the Ratio Test. Switch to the Integral Test or $p$-series comparison.

---

### Example 3: Conditional Convergence — Full Verification

**Classify $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{\ln n}$.**

_Absolute convergence:_ $\sum \frac{1}{\ln n}$. Since $\frac{1}{\ln n} > \frac{1}{n}$ for $n \geq 3$ (as $\ln n < n$), and $\sum \frac{1}{n}$ diverges, by Comparison: $\sum \frac{1}{\ln n}$ diverges. Not absolutely convergent.

_Conditional convergence via AST:_

- $b_n = \frac{1}{\ln n} > 0$ ✓
- $b_n$ decreasing ($\ln n$ is increasing) ✓
- $b_n \to 0$ ✓

**Conditionally convergent.** ✓

---

### Example 4: Power Series — Radius and Boundary Analysis

**Determine the convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(x-2)^n}{n \cdot 4^n}$.**

**Radius of convergence (Ratio Test):**

$$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{(x-2)^{n+1}}{(n+1)4^{n+1}} \cdot \frac{n \cdot 4^n}{(x-2)^n}\right| = \frac{n}{4(n+1)} |x-2| \to \frac{|x-2|}{4}$$

Converges when $\frac{|x-2|}{4} < 1$, i.e. $|x-2| < 4$. So $R = 4$, centre at $x = 2$.

**Endpoint $x = 6$ (i.e. $x - 2 = 4$):**

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{4^n}{n \cdot 4^n} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \quad \textbf{Diverges (harmonic)}$$

**Endpoint $x = -2$ (i.e. $x - 2 = -4$):**

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-4)^n}{n \cdot 4^n} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n} \quad \textbf{Converges (AST)}$$

**Interval of convergence:** $[-2, 6)$.
