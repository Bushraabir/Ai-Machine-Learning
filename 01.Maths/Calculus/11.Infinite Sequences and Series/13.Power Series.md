

> _"A power series is an infinite polynomial — and infinite polynomials are how analysis secretly does everything."_

---

## 0. Motivation: Why Should You Care?

Before the formalism, a confession: most functions you care about — $\sin x$, $e^x$, $\ln(1+x)$, the sigmoid, the softmax's denominator — are **not** polynomials. But polynomials are the only things a computer can truly evaluate (finite additions and multiplications).

The game is: **approximate every reasonable function by an infinite polynomial**, and then figure out when and how that approximation is exact. This is precisely what power series accomplish.

In machine learning, this idea is everywhere — activation functions, Taylor expansions of loss surfaces, kernel methods, and the internal mechanics of transformers all rest, quietly, on power series logic.

---

## 1. Definition

> **Definition.** A **power series centered at** $a$ is a series of the form $$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (x - a)^n = c_0 + c_1(x-a) + c_2(x-a)^2 + c_3(x-a)^3 + \cdots$$ where $c_n \in \mathbb{R}$ are the **coefficients** and $a \in \mathbb{R}$ is the **center**.

When $a = 0$ (centered at the origin), this simplifies to: $$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n x^n = c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + \cdots$$

Think of it as a polynomial with infinitely many terms. The central question is: **for which $x$ does this series converge?**

---

## 2. Radius and Interval of Convergence

### 2.1 The Radius of Convergence

> **Theorem (Cauchy–Hadamard).** For every power series $\sum c_n(x-a)^n$, there exists $R \in [0, +\infty]$ called the **radius of convergence**, such that:
> 
> - The series **converges absolutely** for $|x - a| < R$
> - The series **diverges** for $|x - a| > R$
> - At $|x - a| = R$ (the boundary), anything can happen — must check separately.

The radius is given by: $$\frac{1}{R} = \limsup_{n \to \infty} |c_n|^{1/n}$$

In practice, the **Ratio Test** is usually more convenient:

$$R = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{c_n}{c_{n+1}}\right|$$

(when this limit exists).

### 2.2 The Interval of Convergence

The **interval of convergence** is the set of all $x$ for which the series converges. It always has the form of one of: $${a}, \quad (a-R,, a+R), \quad [a-R,, a+R), \quad (a-R,, a+R], \quad [a-R,, a+R]$$

The open interval $(a-R, a+R)$ is guaranteed. The endpoints require separate investigation (e.g., comparison test, alternating series test).

### 2.3 Example: Geometric Series as a Power Series

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n = 1 + x + x^2 + x^3 + \cdots = \frac{1}{1-x}, \quad |x| < 1$$

Here $c_n = 1$ for all $n$, $a = 0$, and: $$R = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{c_n}{c_{n+1}}\right| = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{1} = 1$$

At $x = 1$: series is $1 + 1 + 1 + \cdots$ — **diverges**.  
At $x = -1$: series is $1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + \cdots$ — **diverges**.  
Interval of convergence: $(-1, 1)$.

### 2.4 Example: Exponential Series

$$e^x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2!} + \frac{x^3}{3!} + \cdots$$

$$R = \lim_{n \to \infty} \left|\frac{1/n!}{1/(n+1)!}\right| = \lim_{n \to \infty} (n+1) = +\infty$$

Converges for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$. This is the "nicest" possible case.

## 4. Power Series as Functions

On its interval of convergence, a power series **defines a function**: $$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n(x-a)^n$$

This function has remarkable properties:

> **Theorem.** If $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n$ has radius of convergence $R > 0$, then:
> 
> 1. $f$ is **continuous** on $(a-R, a+R)$
> 2. $f$ is **differentiable** on $(a-R, a+R)$, and differentiation is term-by-term: $$f'(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n, c_n (x-a)^{n-1}$$
> 3. $f$ is **integrable** on $(a-R, a+R)$, and integration is term-by-term: $$\int f(x),dx = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{c_n}{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1} + C$$
> 4. Both the derivative and integral series have the **same radius of convergence** $R$.

This is not trivial — for arbitrary series of functions, you cannot swap $\sum$ and $\frac{d}{dx}$ freely. Power series are special because of **uniform convergence** on compact subsets of the interval of convergence.

## 5. Taylor and Maclaurin Series

### 5.1 Recovering Coefficients

Suppose $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n(x-a)^n$. Differentiating repeatedly at $x = a$:

$$f(a) = c_0$$ $$f'(a) = 1 \cdot c_1 \implies c_1 = f'(a)$$ $$f''(a) = 2 \cdot 1 \cdot c_2 \implies c_2 = \frac{f''(a)}{2!}$$ $$f^{(k)}(a) = k!, c_k \implies c_k = \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}$$

This gives us the **Taylor series**:

> **Definition.** If $f$ has derivatives of all orders at $a$, its **Taylor series** is: $$f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{f^{(n)}(a)}{n!}(x-a)^n$$ When $a = 0$, this is the **Maclaurin series**.

**Warning:** Not every infinitely-differentiable function equals its Taylor series everywhere. A function is called **analytic** at $a$ if it equals its Taylor series in some neighborhood of $a$. All the standard functions below are analytic.

### 5.2 The Standard Maclaurin Series (Memorize These)

|Function|Series|Radius|
|---|---|---|
|$\dfrac{1}{1-x}$|$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} x^n$|$R = 1$|
|$e^x$|$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{x^n}{n!}$|$R = \infty$|
|$\sin x$|$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$|$R = \infty$|
|$\cos x$|$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}$|$R = \infty$|
|$\ln(1+x)$|$\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \dfrac{(-1)^{n-1} x^n}{n}$|$R = 1$|
|$(1+x)^\alpha$|$\displaystyle\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \binom{\alpha}{n} x^n$|$R = 1$|

where $\binom{\alpha}{n} = \dfrac{\alpha(\alpha-1)\cdots(\alpha-n+1)}{n!}$ is the generalized binomial coefficient.

### 5.3 Partial Sums as Polynomial Approximations

The $N$-th partial sum $T_N(x) = \sum_{n=0}^N c_n(x-a)^n$ is the **degree-$N$ Taylor polynomial** — the best degree-$N$ polynomial approximation to $f$ near $a$.

**Example: approximating $e^x$ near 0**

$$T_0(x) = 1$$ $$T_1(x) = 1 + x$$ $$T_2(x) = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2}$$ $$T_3(x) = 1 + x + \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^3}{6}$$

```
e^x and its Taylor approximations near x = 0:

    y
    |          e^x (true)
  8 |        ,'
    |       /   T3 (cubic)
  4 |      /  ,'
    |     / ,'  T2 (quadratic)
  2 |    |/ ,'
  1 |   ,|,'  T1 (linear)
    |  ' |  T0
    +----+--------→ x
        0   1  2

As N increases, TN hugs e^x on a wider range.
```

## 6. Taylor's Theorem with Remainder

The full story requires controlling the error:

> **Taylor's Theorem.** If $f$ has $n+1$ derivatives on an interval containing $a$ and $x$, then: $$f(x) = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{f^{(k)}(a)}{k!}(x-a)^k + R_n(x)$$ where the **Lagrange remainder** is: $$R_n(x) = \frac{f^{(n+1)}(c)}{(n+1)!}(x-a)^{n+1}$$ for some $c$ between $a$ and $x$.

**Bounding the error:** If $\left|f^{(n+1)}(t)\right| \leq M$ for all $t$ in the interval, then: $$|R_n(x)| \leq \frac{M}{(n+1)!}|x-a|^{n+1}$$

For $e^x$ on $[0,1]$: since $|e^t| \leq e \leq 3$ for $t \in [0,1]$, $$|R_n(x)| \leq \frac{3}{(n+1)!}$$ which $\to 0$ as $n \to \infty$. This proves $e^x$ equals its Maclaurin series on $[0,1]$.

## 7. Operations on Power Series

### 7.1 Substitution

Replace $x$ by an expression. Example: derive $e^{-x^2}$ series:

$$e^u = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{u^n}{n!}, \quad \text{substitute } u = -x^2:$$ $$e^{-x^2} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{n!}, \quad R = \infty$$

This is heavily used in probability (Gaussian integrals) and ML (RBF kernels).

### 7.2 Integration of Non-Elementary Functions

$$\int_0^x e^{-t^2},dt = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{n!(2n+1)}$$

This is the error function $\text{erf}(x)$ (up to a constant) — no closed form exists otherwise.

### 7.3 Multiplication (Cauchy Product)

$$\left(\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n\right)\left(\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n x^n\right) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\sum_{k=0}^n a_k b_{n-k}\right) x^n$$

This is the **discrete convolution** of the coefficient sequences — a fundamental operation in signal processing and sequence modeling.

## 8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _This is where the abstract becomes viscerally relevant._

### 8.1 Activation Functions and Their Taylor Expansions

Neural networks apply nonlinear activation functions element-wise. Understanding their Taylor series reveals what transformations they implement:

**Sigmoid** $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$:

Using $\frac{1}{1+u} = \sum(-u)^n$ and $e^{-x} = \sum \frac{(-x)^n}{n!}$: $$\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{x}{4} - \frac{x^3}{48} + \cdots$$

Near $x = 0$, sigmoid is approximately **linear** with slope $\frac{1}{4}$. This explains the **vanishing gradient problem**: for large $|x|$, higher-order terms flatten the function, and gradients shrink.

**GELU** (used in GPT, BERT): defined via $\text{erf}$, directly connected to the Gaussian power series above.

**Softplus** $\ln(1 + e^x)$: its Taylor expansion around 0 shares leading terms with $\text{ReLU}$ — the series perspective clarifies why softplus is a smooth ReLU approximation.

### 8.2 Optimization and Loss Landscapes

Gradient descent navigates a loss surface $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$. The Taylor expansion of $\mathcal{L}$ at current parameters $\theta_0$ is: $$\mathcal{L}(\theta_0 + \Delta\theta) = \mathcal{L}(\theta_0) + \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_0)^\top \Delta\theta + \frac{1}{2}\Delta\theta^\top H \Delta\theta + O(|\Delta\theta|^3)$$

- **Gradient descent** uses only the **linear term** (first-order Taylor): $\Delta\theta = -\eta \nabla\mathcal{L}$.
- **Newton's method** / **natural gradient** uses the **quadratic term** (second-order Taylor), incorporating the Hessian $H$.
- **Learning rate scheduling** is a heuristic acknowledgment that the Taylor approximation breaks down far from $\theta_0$.

The **radius of convergence** of this expansion (around the current point) determines how large a step is safe — this is the intuition behind trust-region methods.

### 8.3 Kernel Methods and Infinite Feature Maps

The **RBF (radial basis function) kernel**: $$k(x, z) = e^{-|x-z|^2 / 2\sigma^2}$$

Expanding via the exponential power series: $$e^{x \cdot z / \sigma^2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(x \cdot z)^n}{\sigma^{2n} n!}$$

This shows the RBF kernel corresponds to an **infinite-dimensional polynomial feature map** — a countably infinite power series of features. Support vector machines with RBF kernels are doing regression/classification in this infinite feature space.

### 8.4 Transformers and Attention as Polynomial Operations

The softmax in attention is: $$\text{softmax}(x)_i = \frac{e^{x_i}}{\sum_j e^{x_j}}$$

The numerator and denominator involve $e^x = \sum \frac{x^n}{n!}$. Research into **polynomial attention** (approximating softmax by truncating the exponential Taylor series) uses exactly the power series perspective to build computationally cheaper attention mechanisms (e.g., Performer, linear attention).

### 8.5 Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)

In the infinite-width limit, a neural network's training dynamics are governed by the **Neural Tangent Kernel**, which can be expressed as a power series in the network parameters. The convergence radius of this series determines when the NTK approximation is valid — connecting functional analysis directly to training stability.

### 8.6 Automatic Differentiation

Backpropagation computes exact derivatives. But power series provide **dual number differentiation** — representing $f(x + \epsilon) = f(x) + f'(x)\epsilon$ (where $\epsilon^2 = 0$) as a truncated power series. Forward-mode autodiff is literally first-order Taylor expansion evaluation.

## 9. Worked Problems

### Problem 1: Find the interval of convergence of $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^n}{n}$

**Solution.**  
Apply the ratio test: $$\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \left|\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} \cdot \frac{n}{x^n}\right| = |x| \cdot \frac{n}{n+1} \to |x|$$

Converges absolutely when $|x| < 1$, diverges when $|x| > 1$. So $R = 1$.

**Endpoints:**

- $x = 1$: $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{n} = -\ln 2$ (alternating harmonic series — **converges** by AST).
- $x = -1$: $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n(-1)^n}{n} = \sum \frac{1}{n}$ — **diverges** (harmonic series).

**Interval of convergence:** $(-1, 1]$.

Note: this series represents $\ln(1+x)$ (the Maclaurin series for $\ln(1+x)$ with $c_n = \frac{(-1)^{n-1}}{n}$, up to sign re-indexing), consistent with $\ln(1+1) = \ln 2$.

---

### Problem 2: Compute $\displaystyle\int_0^1 \frac{\sin x}{x},dx$ to 4 decimal places

**Solution.** The integrand $\frac{\sin x}{x}$ has a removable singularity at 0 (the limit is 1). Use the series: $$\frac{\sin x}{x} = \frac{1}{x}\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n+1)!}$$

Integrate term by term: $$\int_0^1 \frac{\sin x}{x},dx = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{(2n+1)!\cdot(2n+1)} = 1 - \frac{1}{3\cdot 3!} + \frac{1}{5 \cdot 5!} - \frac{1}{7 \cdot 7!} + \cdots$$

$$= 1 - \frac{1}{18} + \frac{1}{600} - \frac{1}{35280} + \cdots$$

$$\approx 1 - 0.05556 + 0.001\overline{6} - 0.0000283 + \cdots \approx 0.9461$$

(True value: $\text{Si}(1) \approx 0.94608$.) The series converges fast since factorials dominate.

---

### Problem 3: Derive the series for $\arctan x$ and use it to approximate $\pi$

**Solution.** Recall $\frac{d}{dx}\arctan x = \frac{1}{1+x^2}$.

Start from the geometric series: $$\frac{1}{1+u} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-u)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-1)^n u^n, \quad |u| < 1$$

Substitute $u = x^2$: $$\frac{1}{1+x^2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-1)^n x^{2n}, \quad |x| < 1$$

Integrate from 0 to $x$: $$\arctan x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{2n+1} = x - \frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{x^5}{5} - \cdots$$

At $x = 1$ (boundary — check: converges by AST): $$\arctan 1 = \frac{\pi}{4} = 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{7} + \cdots$$

This is the **Leibniz formula for $\pi$**. It converges, but _slowly_ (alternating series with terms decaying like $1/n$). In practice, use Machin's formula: $$\frac{\pi}{4} = 4\arctan\frac{1}{5} - \arctan\frac{1}{239}$$ Both arguments are small, so the series converges rapidly.

## 10. Uniform Convergence: The Fine Print

Why can we differentiate/integrate term by term? Because on any closed subinterval $[a-r, a+r]$ with $r < R$, the power series converges **uniformly** — meaning the partial sums converge at the same rate everywhere on that interval.

> **Theorem.** A power series converges uniformly on every compact subset of its interval of convergence.

This is stronger than pointwise convergence and is what allows: $$\frac{d}{dx}\sum c_n(x-a)^n = \sum \frac{d}{dx} c_n(x-a)^n$$

Without uniform convergence, swapping $\sum$ and $\frac{d}{dx}$ is generally illegal.

---

## 11. Uniqueness of Power Series

> **Theorem.** If $\sum a_n(x-a)^n = \sum b_n(x-a)^n$ for all $x$ in some open interval containing $a$, then $a_n = b_n$ for all $n$.

**Proof sketch.** Set $x = a$: $a_0 = b_0$. Differentiate both sides and set $x = a$: $a_1 = b_1$. Continue by induction. $\square$

**Consequence:** Every analytic function has a unique power series representation. There's no ambiguity — the coefficients are pinned by the derivatives.

## 12. Summary: The Core Picture

```
A power series Σ cₙ(x-a)ⁿ:

  DEFINES a function f on (a-R, a+R)
        ↓
  f is C∞ (infinitely differentiable) there
        ↓
  Differentiate/integrate TERM BY TERM
        ↓
  Coefficients determined by: cₙ = f⁽ⁿ⁾(a)/n!
        ↓
  = Taylor series of f
        ↓
  Error controlled by Taylor's Remainder Theorem

In ML:
  Activations → Taylor expansion → understand saturation, gradients
  Loss surface → Taylor expansion → SGD, Newton, trust regions
  Kernels → infinite series of features (RBF = infinite polynomial)
  Attention → softmax via exp series → efficient approximations
```
