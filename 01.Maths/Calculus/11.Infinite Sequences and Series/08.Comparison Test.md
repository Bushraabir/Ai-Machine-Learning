## 1. Motivation — Why Compare?

Most series we meet in the wild resist closed-form evaluation. We cannot sum them explicitly, so we instead ask the binary question: _does this series converge or diverge?_

The **Comparison Test** operationalises a beautifully simple idea:

> If every term of a series is smaller than the corresponding term of a **known convergent** series, it must converge.  
> If every term is larger than a **known divergent** series, it must diverge.

The hard work is finding the right **benchmark series**. The standard toolkit consists of:

|Benchmark|Form|Behaviour|
|---|---|---|
|Geometric|$\sum ar^n$|Converges iff $\lvert r \rvert < 1$|
|$p$-series|$\sum \frac{1}{n^p}$|Converges iff $p > 1$|
|Harmonic|$\sum \frac{1}{n}$|Diverges ($p=1$ case)|

## 2. The Comparison Test (Direct Form)

### Theorem

Let $\sum a_n$ and $\sum b_n$ be series with **non-negative terms**. Suppose that

$$0 ;\leq; a_n ;\leq; b_n \quad \text{for all } n \geq N$$

(the inequality need only hold eventually — from some index $N$ onward).

**Then:**

$$\sum b_n \text{ converges} \implies \sum a_n \text{ converges}$$

$$\sum a_n \text{ diverges} \implies \sum b_n \text{ diverges}$$

### Proof

**Case 1 — Convergence.** Let $S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k$ and $T_n = \sum_{k=1}^n b_k$. Since $a_k \leq b_k$, the partial sums satisfy $S_n \leq T_n$. Because $\sum b_n$ converges, the sequence ${T_n}$ is bounded above by some $M$. Thus $S_n \leq T_n \leq M$ for all $n$. Since ${S_n}$ is monotone increasing (all terms non-negative) and bounded above, the **Monotone Convergence Theorem** guarantees ${S_n}$ converges. $\checkmark$

**Case 2 — Divergence.** This is the contrapositive of Case 1. If $\sum a_n$ diverges, then ${S_n} \to \infty$. Since $T_n \geq S_n$, we get ${T_n} \to \infty$, so $\sum b_n$ diverges. $\checkmark$

> **Crucial caveat:** The test is _one-directional_ in each case. If $a_n \leq b_n$ and $\sum a_n$ diverges, we learn _nothing_ about $\sum b_n$ from this test alone.

## 3. The Limit Comparison Test

The direct comparison test requires a term-by-term inequality, which is often algebraically awkward to establish. A more flexible tool:

### Theorem

Let $a_n, b_n > 0$ for all $n$. Suppose the limit

$$L = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n}$$

exists (finite) and $L > 0$. Then:

$$\sum a_n \text{ and } \sum b_n \text{ either both converge or both diverge.}$$

### Proof

Since $L > 0$ and $L < \infty$, choose $\varepsilon = L/2 > 0$. By definition of the limit, there exists $N$ such that for all $n \geq N$:

$$\left|\frac{a_n}{b_n} - L\right| < \frac{L}{2}$$

This gives us the two-sided inequality:

$$\frac{L}{2} < \frac{a_n}{b_n} < \frac{3L}{2}$$

Multiplying through by $b_n > 0$:

$$\frac{L}{2} \cdot b_n < a_n < \frac{3L}{2} \cdot b_n$$

**Right inequality:** If $\sum b_n$ converges, then $\sum \frac{3L}{2} b_n$ converges, and by the Direct Comparison Test, $\sum a_n$ converges.

**Left inequality:** If $\sum b_n$ diverges, then $\sum \frac{L}{2} b_n$ diverges, and by comparison, $\sum a_n$ diverges.

Since the convergence of $\sum b_n$ and $\sum a_n$ are equivalent in both directions, they share the same fate. $\square$

> **Degenerate cases:**
> 
> - $L = 0$: If $\sum b_n$ converges $\Rightarrow$ $\sum a_n$ converges (but not vice versa).
> - $L = \infty$: If $\sum b_n$ diverges $\Rightarrow$ $\sum a_n$ diverges (but not vice versa).

## 4. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Direct Comparison (Convergence)

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2 + n}$$

**Strategy:** The dominant term in the denominator is $n^2$, so compare with $\sum 1/n^2$.

**Bound:** $n^2 + n > n^2$, so $\dfrac{1}{n^2+n} < \dfrac{1}{n^2}$.

Since $\sum 1/n^2$ is a $p$-series with $p = 2 > 1$, it converges. By the Direct Comparison Test, $\sum \dfrac{1}{n^2+n}$ **converges**. $\checkmark$

_(Sanity check: we can also verify by partial fractions that the sum equals exactly $1$.)_

---

### Example 2 — Direct Comparison (Divergence)

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n^2 - 1}} \quad (n \geq 2)$$

**Strategy:** For large $n$, $\sqrt{n^2-1} \approx n$, so compare with $\sum 1/n$.

**Bound:** $n^2 - 1 < n^2$, so $\sqrt{n^2-1} < n$, giving:

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{n^2-1}} > \frac{1}{n}$$

Since $\sum 1/n$ (the harmonic series) diverges, by Direct Comparison, $\sum \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n^2-1}}$ **diverges**. $\checkmark$

---

### Example 3 — Limit Comparison

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{3n^2 + 2}{5n^4 + n + 7}$$

**Strategy:** As $n \to \infty$, the series behaves like $\dfrac{3n^2}{5n^4} = \dfrac{3}{5n^2}$. Benchmark: $b_n = \dfrac{1}{n^2}$.

$$L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{a_n}{b_n} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{(3n^2+2) \cdot n^2}{5n^4 + n + 7} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{3n^4 + 2n^2}{5n^4 + n + 7} = \frac{3}{5}$$

$L = 3/5 \in (0, \infty)$. Since $\sum 1/n^2$ converges ($p=2$), by the Limit Comparison Test, the original series **converges**. $\checkmark$

---

### Example 4 — A Tricky Case (Where Inequalities Mislead)

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\sin^2(n)}{n^2}$$

**Problem:** $\sin^2(n)$ is bounded and non-negative. Specifically, $0 \leq \sin^2(n) \leq 1$.

**Bound:** $\dfrac{\sin^2(n)}{n^2} \leq \dfrac{1}{n^2}$.

Since $\sum 1/n^2$ converges, by Direct Comparison, $\sum \dfrac{\sin^2(n)}{n^2}$ **converges**. $\checkmark$

> **Note:** This appears in ML as the analysis of certain Fourier-based regularisation penalties.

## 5. Visual Intuition

### The "Domination" Picture

Consider plotting the terms $a_n$ and $b_n$ as bar charts:

```
b_n  ████████████████████████████  ← known convergent series
a_n  ████████ ██████ █████ ████    ← our series (fits UNDER b_n)
         n=1  n=2   n=3   n=4 ...
```

If the total area of the top bars is finite, the total area of the bottom bars is _a fortiori_ finite.

### The Limit Comparison Picture

In the limit comparison test, we're asking: do $a_n$ and $b_n$ have the same _asymptotic shape_? If their ratio converges to a positive constant $L$, then their partial sums grow at the same rate — they sink or swim together.

```
a_n / b_n

 L + ε  ─────────────────────────  upper bound
   L    ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  ·  · ← ratio converges here
 L - ε  ─────────────────────────  lower bound
                               n →∞
```

For large $n$, the ratio is sandwiched: $\frac{L}{2} b_n < a_n < \frac{3L}{2} b_n$.

## 6. Common Pitfalls

|Mistake|Why it fails|
|---|---|
|Applying the test to series with negative terms|The monotonicity argument in the proof breaks down|
|Concluding divergence from $a_n \leq b_n$ and $\sum b_n$ diverges|The inequality goes the wrong direction|
|Choosing a benchmark where $L = 0$ or $L = \infty$ and claiming both converge/diverge|The full equivalence only holds for $0 < L < \infty$|
|Only verifying the inequality at a few values of $n$|Must hold for _all_ $n \geq N$, or the bound is invalid|

## 7. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This is where analysis and applied mathematics become surprisingly intimate.

### 7.1 Convergence of Infinite-Width Neural Networks

In the **Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)** literature, one studies the convergence of the kernel function as the network width $m \to \infty$. The kernel entries can be expressed as series, and showing they converge uniformly requires exactly comparison-test arguments: bounding each term of an unknown series against a known $p$-series.

### 7.2 Convergence of Gradient Descent and Learning Rate Schedules

Standard optimisation theory (see Robbins–Monro, 1951) requires the learning rate sequence ${\eta_t}$ to satisfy the **two-sum conditions**:

$$\sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \eta_t = \infty \qquad \text{and} \qquad \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \eta_t^2 < \infty$$

- The first condition ensures the iterates can reach any part of the parameter space.
- The second ensures cumulative noise (variance) remains bounded.

**Example:** $\eta_t = 1/t$ satisfies both:

- $\sum 1/t$ diverges (harmonic series — good, first condition met).
- $\sum 1/t^2$ converges ($p$-series with $p = 2$ — good, second condition met).

**Example:** $\eta_t = 1/\sqrt{t}$ satisfies only the first:

- $\sum 1/\sqrt{t}$ diverges ($p = 1/2 < 1$ — first condition met).
- $\sum 1/t$ diverges — second condition **not met**. This schedule is used in Adam and AdaGrad precisely because those optimisers adaptively rescale the gradient, compensating for the noise.

The comparison test is used constantly here: to verify that a proposed schedule $\eta_t$ is above or below these benchmark $p$-series.

### 7.3 Kernel Methods and Mercer's Theorem

Many kernel machines (SVMs, Gaussian Processes) rely on **Mercer's theorem**: a kernel $K(x,x')$ admits a convergent eigenfunction expansion

$$K(x,x') = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \lambda_n \phi_n(x) \phi_n(x')$$

The convergence of this series (in $L^2$ and uniformly) is established by showing the eigenvalues $\lambda_n$ decay fast enough — typically by comparing them to a known convergent series. The smoother the kernel, the faster the eigenvalue decay (exponential for Gaussian kernels, polynomial for Matérn kernels), and comparison tests provide the formal verification.

### 7.4 Fourier Analysis and Feature Maps

Random Fourier Features (Rahimi & Recht, 2007) approximate shift-invariant kernels by:

$$K(x-y) \approx \mathbb{E}_\omega[z_\omega(x) z_\omega(y)]$$

The approximation error analysis involves bounding the tails of a series expansion. Comparison against a geometric or $p$-series gives the required rate of convergence and determines how many features are needed for a given tolerance.

### 7.5 Numerical Stability in Deep Learning

Exploding/vanishing gradient analysis in RNNs involves products $\prod_{t=1}^{T} W_t$. The logarithm converts this to a sum $\sum_{t=1}^T \log |W_t|$. The comparison test tells us: if $\log|W_t| \leq C/t^p$ with $p > 1$, the sum converges (stability). This is exactly the theoretical underpinning of why gradient clipping and normalisation schemes work.

> **Takeaway for ML practitioners:** The comparison test is not a curiosity. It is the backbone of convergence arguments in optimisation theory, learning theory, and approximation theory — all of which are foundational to modern deep learning.

## 8. Decision Flowchart — Which Test to Use?

```
Is the series telescoping or geometric?
         │
       YES → Sum directly
         │
        NO
         │
Does the Divergence Test (lim aₙ ≠ 0) settle it?
         │
       YES → Diverges
         │
        NO
         │
Does the series involve factorials or exponentials?
         │
       YES → Try Ratio Test or Root Test
         │
        NO
         │
Does the series "look like" a p-series or geometric?
         │
       YES → Try Comparison / Limit Comparison ← YOU ARE HERE
         │
        NO
         │

         │
       YES → Try Alternating Series Test
```

## 9. Summary Card

$$\boxed{\text{Direct CT}: \quad 0 \leq a_n \leq b_n \implies \begin{cases} \sum b_n < \infty \Rightarrow \sum a_n < \infty \ \sum a_n = \infty \Rightarrow \sum b_n = \infty \end{cases}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Limit CT}: \quad \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{a_n}{b_n} = L \in (0,\infty) \implies \sum a_n \text{ and } \sum b_n \text{ have the same fate}}$$

**The three steps every time:**

1. Identify the _dominant behaviour_ of $a_n$ as $n \to \infty$.
2. Choose a _benchmark_ $b_n$ from the standard toolkit.
3. Verify the inequality (Direct CT) or compute the ratio limit (Limit CT).
