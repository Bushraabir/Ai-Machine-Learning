## Motivation

Consider the sequence defined recursively:

$$a_1 = 1, \qquad a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}\left(a_n + \frac{2}{a_n}\right)$$

Does this converge? _To what?_ You might suspect $\sqrt{2}$ ŌĆö and you'd be right ŌĆö but _proving_ it converges requires more than intuition. The **Monotonic Sequence Theorem (MST)** gives you a two-step proof structure:

1. Show the sequence is monotone (increasing or decreasing).
2. Show it is bounded (above or below, as appropriate).

Conclusion: it converges. _Then_ you find the limit by setting $L = \lim_{n\to\infty} a_n$ and solving the resulting equation.

> **Personal note:** The MST is one of the workhorses of analysis. It proves the existence of limits without ever exhibiting the limit explicitly. This "existence before computation" pattern recurs constantly ŌĆö in fixed-point theorems, in the intermediate value theorem, in the convergence of optimization algorithms.

## Definitions

> **Definition.** A sequence ${a_n}$ is:
> 
> - **Increasing** if $a_{n+1} > a_n$ for all $n \geq 1$
> - **Nondecreasing** if $a_{n+1} \geq a_n$ for all $n \geq 1$
> - **Decreasing** if $a_{n+1} < a_n$ for all $n \geq 1$
> - **Nonincreasing** if $a_{n+1} \leq a_n$ for all $n \geq 1$
> - **Monotone** if it is nondecreasing or nonincreasing

> **Definition.** A sequence ${a_n}$ is:
> 
> - **Bounded above** if $\exists M \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $a_n \leq M$ for all $n$
> - **Bounded below** if $\exists m \in \mathbb{R}$ such that $a_n \geq m$ for all $n$
> - **Bounded** if it is both bounded above and below

> **Definition (Supremum).** The **least upper bound** (supremum) of a set $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, written $\sup S$, is the smallest $M$ such that $s \leq M$ for all $s \in S$. It need not be an element of $S$.

## The Theorem ŌĆö Statement and Proof

> **Theorem (Monotonic Sequence Theorem).** Every bounded monotone sequence of real numbers converges.
> 
> More precisely:
> 
> - If ${a_n}$ is nondecreasing and bounded above, then $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = \sup{a_n : n \geq 1}$.
> - If ${a_n}$ is nonincreasing and bounded below, then $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = \inf{a_n : n \geq 1}$.

**Proof** (nondecreasing case).

Let $S = {a_n : n \geq 1}$. Since ${a_n}$ is bounded above, $S$ is a nonempty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ bounded above, so by the **Completeness Axiom** of $\mathbb{R}$, the supremum $L = \sup S$ exists.

We claim $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = L$.

Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be arbitrary. Since $L = \sup S$, $L - \varepsilon$ is _not_ an upper bound for $S$. Therefore there exists $N$ such that:

$$a_N > L - \varepsilon$$

Since ${a_n}$ is nondecreasing, for all $n \geq N$:

$$L - \varepsilon < a_N \leq a_n \leq L < L + \varepsilon$$

Therefore $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$ for all $n \geq N$.

Since $\varepsilon > 0$ was arbitrary, $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = L$. $\square$

**Proof** (nonincreasing case).

Let $b_n = -a_n$. Then ${b_n}$ is nondecreasing and bounded above (by $-\inf{a_n}$). By the first case, $b_n \to L'$, so $a_n = -b_n \to -L' = \inf{a_n}$. $\square$

> **Remark on necessity.** Both conditions are necessary:
> 
> - $a_n = n$ is monotone but not bounded ŌåÆ diverges to $\infty$.
> - $a_n = (-1)^n$ is bounded but not monotone ŌåÆ diverges (oscillates).

## The Completeness Axiom ŌĆö What's Really Going On

The proof invokes the **Completeness Axiom** (also called the _Least Upper Bound Property_):

> **Axiom.** Every nonempty subset of $\mathbb{R}$ that is bounded above has a supremum in $\mathbb{R}$.

This is what distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$. Consider:

$$S = {q \in \mathbb{Q} : q^2 < 2}$$

$S$ is a nonempty subset of $\mathbb{Q}$, bounded above (by 2). But $\sup S = \sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$. The rationals have a "hole" at $\sqrt{2}$.

The Monotonic Sequence Theorem would **fail over $\mathbb{Q}$**: the sequence of rational approximations to $\sqrt{2}$,

$$1,\ 1.4,\ 1.41,\ 1.414,\ 1.4142,\ \ldots$$

is nondecreasing and bounded above (by 2) in $\mathbb{Q}$, but converges to $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$ ŌĆö i.e., it _does not converge in $\mathbb{Q}$_.

> **The MST is logically equivalent to the Completeness Axiom.** You can take either one as the fundamental axiom of $\mathbb{R}$ and derive the other. Other equivalent formulations: the Cauchy completeness criterion, the Nested Interval Theorem, the Bolzano-Weierstrass theorem.

## Corollaries and Variants

> **Corollary 1.** A nondecreasing sequence either converges or diverges to $+\infty$.

_Proof._ If bounded above, MST gives convergence. If not bounded above, for every $M$ there exists $N$ with $a_N > M$, and since the sequence is nondecreasing, $a_n \geq a_N > M$ for all $n \geq N$. So $a_n \to +\infty$. $\square$

> **Corollary 2.** A sequence defined by $a_{n+1} = f(a_n)$ converges if: (i) $f$ is continuous, (ii) the sequence is monotone, (iii) the sequence is bounded. Moreover, the limit $L$ satisfies $L = f(L)$ (it is a **fixed point** of $f$).

_Proof._ MST gives $a_n \to L$. By continuity of $f$: $L = \lim a_{n+1} = \lim f(a_n) = f(\lim a_n) = f(L)$. $\square$

> **Corollary 3 (Ratio test for sequences).** If $a_n > 0$ and $a_{n+1}/a_n \leq r < 1$ for all large $n$, then $a_n \to 0$.

_Proof._ The sequence is eventually decreasing (geometric comparison). It is bounded below by 0. MST gives convergence; the fixed-point equation forces the limit to be 0. $\square$

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 ŌĆö The Sequence $a_n = \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n$

This defines $e$. We show it is increasing and bounded above by 3.

**Monotone:** By the AM-GM inequality applied to $(n+1)$ numbers ŌĆö one copy of $1$ and $n$ copies of $(1 + 1/n)$:

$$\left(\frac{1 + n(1 + 1/n)}{n+1}\right)^{n+1} \geq 1 \cdot \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n$$

$$\left(\frac{2n+1}{n+1}\right)^{n+1} \geq \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n$$

$$\left(1 + \frac{1}{n+1}\right)^{n+1} \geq \left(1 + \frac{1}{n}\right)^n$$

So $a_{n+1} \geq a_n$: the sequence is nondecreasing. Ō£ō

**Bounded:** By the binomial theorem:

$$a_n = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} \frac{1}{n^k} = \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{1}{k!}\cdot\frac{n(n-1)\cdots(n-k+1)}{n^k} \leq \sum_{k=0}^{n} \frac{1}{k!} \leq \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{k!}$$

Now $k! \geq 2^{k-1}$ for $k \geq 1$, so:

$$a_n \leq 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2^{k-1}} = 1 + 2 = 3$$

By MST, $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n$ exists. We call it $e \approx 2.71828$. Ō£ō

---

### Example 2 ŌĆö Newton's Method for $\sqrt{c}$

$$a_1 = c > 1, \qquad a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}\left(a_n + \frac{c}{a_n}\right)$$

**Bounded below by $\sqrt{c}$:** By AM-GM, $a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}(a_n + c/a_n) \geq \sqrt{a_n \cdot c/a_n} = \sqrt{c}$. So $a_n \geq \sqrt{c}$ for all $n \geq 2$. Ō£ō

**Decreasing (for $n \geq 2$):**

$$a_{n+1} - a_n = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{c}{a_n} - a_n\right) = \frac{c - a_n^2}{2a_n} \leq 0$$

since $a_n \geq \sqrt{c} \Rightarrow a_n^2 \geq c$. Ō£ō

By MST, $L = \lim a_n$ exists. Setting $L = \frac{1}{2}(L + c/L)$ gives $L^2 = c$, so $L = \sqrt{c}$.

> This is Newton's method in disguise ŌĆö and the proof of convergence _is_ the MST applied to a recursively defined sequence.

---

### Example 3 ŌĆö A Decreasing Bounded Sequence

$$a_n = \frac{n+1}{n} = 1 + \frac{1}{n}$$

**Decreasing:** $a_{n+1} = 1 + 1/(n+1) < 1 + 1/n = a_n$. Ō£ō

**Bounded below by 1:** $a_n = 1 + 1/n > 1$ for all $n$. Ō£ō

By MST, $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = \inf{1 + 1/n} = 1$.

---

### Example 4 ŌĆö Fibonacci Ratios

Let $F_n$ be the Fibonacci sequence: $F_1 = F_2 = 1$, $F_{n+1} = F_n + F_{n-1}$. Define $r_n = F_{n+1}/F_n$.

**Claim:** $r_n$ converges to the golden ratio $\phi = (1+\sqrt5)/2$.

One approach: show the even-indexed terms are decreasing and bounded below, odd-indexed terms are increasing and bounded above, and both bounds squeeze to the same value. Alternatively:

Assume $r_n \to L$. Then dividing $F_{n+2} = F_{n+1} + F_n$ by $F_{n+1}$: $$r_{n+1} = 1 + \frac{1}{r_n} \to 1 + \frac{1}{L}$$

So $L = 1 + 1/L$, giving $L^2 - L - 1 = 0$, hence $L = \phi$. (MST supplies the existence; then we solve for $L$.)

## The Theorem in Action ŌĆö Finding Limits

The standard proof pattern using MST:

**Step 1 ŌĆö Show monotone.** Use induction. For $a_{n+1} = f(a_n)$, show $a_2 \geq a_1$, then assume $a_{k+1} \geq a_k$ and prove $a_{k+2} \geq a_{k+1}$ (by applying $f$ and using monotonicity of $f$).

**Step 2 ŌĆö Show bounded.** Use induction again. Show $a_1 \leq M$; assume $a_k \leq M$; show $f(a_k) \leq M$.

**Step 3 ŌĆö Invoke MST.** Conclude $L = \lim a_n$ exists.

**Step 4 ŌĆö Find $L$.** Take limits on both sides of $a_{n+1} = f(a_n)$: $L = f(L)$. Solve.

**Example.** $a_1 = 2$, $a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{4}(a_n^2 + 3)$.

_Step 1 (decreasing):_ $a_{n+1} - a_n = \frac{1}{4}(a_n^2 + 3) - a_n = \frac{1}{4}(a_n - 1)^2 - 0$... Actually let's check: $a_2 = (4+3)/4 = 7/4 < 2 = a_1$. Assume $1 < a_n \leq 2$; then $a_{n+1} = (a_n^2 + 3)/4 \leq (4+3)/4 = 7/4 < 2$. And $a_{n+1} - 1 = (a_n^2 - 1)/4 > 0$. So $a_n$ is decreasing and bounded below by 1.

_Step 3:_ MST gives $L = \lim a_n$ exists.

_Step 4:_ $L = (L^2 + 3)/4 \Rightarrow 4L = L^2 + 3 \Rightarrow L^2 - 4L + 3 = 0 \Rightarrow (L-1)(L-3) = 0$.

Since $L \geq 1$ and $L \leq 2$, we have $L = 1$.

## Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

The Monotonic Sequence Theorem is not merely background mathematics ŌĆö it is the conceptual ancestor of convergence proofs throughout optimization and machine learning.

---

### 1. Gradient Descent ŌĆö Monotone Decrease of Loss

In standard gradient descent:

$$w_{n+1} = w_n - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(w_n)$$

For a convex loss $\mathcal{L}$ with $L$-Lipschitz gradient, choosing step size $\eta \leq 1/L$ gives:

$$\mathcal{L}(w_{n+1}) \leq \mathcal{L}(w_n) - \frac{\eta}{2}|\nabla \mathcal{L}(w_n)|^2$$

So the **loss sequence ${\mathcal{L}(w_n)}$ is nonincreasing**. It is also **bounded below by** $\mathcal{L}^* = \min_w \mathcal{L}(w) \geq 0$. By the MST, $\mathcal{L}(w_n)$ **converges**.

This is literally the MST applied to the loss sequence. The convergence proof for gradient descent is the MST in disguise.

> The bound $\eta \leq 1/L$ is the Lipschitz condition ensuring the loss sequence is actually monotone. Violating it (learning rate too large) breaks monotonicity ŌåÆ oscillation ŌåÆ divergence. This is why learning rate tuning matters.

---

### 2. EM Algorithm ŌĆö Monotone Likelihood

The **Expectation-Maximization (EM) algorithm** for fitting latent variable models (Gaussian mixtures, HMMs, etc.) produces iterates $\theta^{(n)}$ satisfying:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta^{(n+1)}) \geq \mathcal{L}(\theta^{(n)})$$

where $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \log p(\text{data} \mid \theta)$ is the log-likelihood. The log-likelihood is bounded above (by 0 for a proper probability model).

By the MST: $\mathcal{L}(\theta^{(n)})$ converges.

The proof of the EM inequality uses Jensen's inequality on the E-step and optimality on the M-step ŌĆö but the conclusion of convergence _is_ the MST.

> **Caveat:** The MST gives convergence of the _values_ $\mathcal{L}(\theta^{(n)})$. Convergence of the parameter sequence $\theta^{(n)}$ itself is a separate (harder) question requiring additional assumptions on the likelihood surface.

---

### 3. Value Iteration in Reinforcement Learning

In dynamic programming / reinforcement learning, the **value iteration** algorithm updates:

$$V_{n+1}(s) = \max_a \left[R(s,a) + \gamma \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a) V_n(s')\right]$$

This is the application of the **Bellman optimality operator** $\mathcal{T}$: $V_{n+1} = \mathcal{T}V_n$.

**Convergence proof:**

1. $\mathcal{T}$ is a **contraction mapping** with constant $\gamma < 1$ (discount factor) in the $\ell^\infty$ norm.
2. Therefore ${|\mathcal{T}^n V_0 - V^*|}$ is a _geometrically decreasing_ sequence.
3. A geometrically decreasing positive sequence is monotone and bounded below by 0 ŌåÆ MST gives convergence to 0.
4. Hence $V_n \to V^*$ (optimal value function).

The Banach Fixed-Point Theorem (contraction mapping theorem) is a generalization of the MST to complete metric spaces ŌĆö it says "a sequence defined by a contraction converges to a unique fixed point." The proof is the MST in a metric space.

---

### 4. Convergence of Neural Network Training ŌĆö ELBO Monotonicity

In **Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)**, the training objective is the **ELBO** (Evidence Lower BOund):

$$\text{ELBO}(\phi, \theta) = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)] - \text{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) | p(z))$$

Coordinate ascent variational inference (CAVI), used in mean-field VI, updates each variational parameter to maximize the ELBO holding others fixed. Each update monotonically increases the ELBO:

$$\text{ELBO}^{(n+1)} \geq \text{ELBO}^{(n)}$$

The ELBO is bounded above by $\log p_\theta(x)$. By the MST: **ELBO converges**.

This is the same structure as the EM algorithm (and EM is a special case of VI).

---

### 5. AdaGrad / RMSProp ŌĆö Accumulated Squared Gradients

In **AdaGrad**, the accumulated sum of squared gradients:

$$G_n = \sum_{k=1}^{n} g_k^2$$

is nondecreasing (each term $g_k^2 \geq 0$). In AdaGrad this is unbounded ŌĆö which causes the effective learning rate $\eta/\sqrt{G_n}$ to decay to zero (can be too aggressive). **RMSProp** replaces this with an exponential moving average:

$$G_n = \beta G_{n-1} + (1-\beta)g_n^2, \qquad \beta \in (0,1)$$

This is bounded above (by $\max g_k^2$) and nondecreasing when $g_n^2 \geq G_{n-1}$... actually it oscillates. The MST perspective explains _why_ AdaGrad can be ill-behaved (the monotone unbounded sequence $G_n \to \infty$) and why RMSProp fixes it (introducing a forgetting factor).

---

### 6. Convergence of MCMC ŌĆö Monotone Coupling

In **Markov Chain Monte Carlo** methods (Gibbs sampling, Metropolis-Hastings), convergence to the stationary distribution $\pi$ is sometimes proved via **monotone coupling**:

Given two chains $X_n$ and $Y_n$ started at different initial states, construct a coupling such that once they meet, they stay together. The **probability of not yet having met**, $P(\text{not coupled by time } n)$, is a _nonincreasing_ sequence bounded below by 0. By the MST, it converges ŌĆö and one shows the limit must be 0.

This is a probabilistic MST, but the structure is identical.

## Summary

|Condition|Conclusion|
|---|---|
|Nondecreasing + bounded above|Converges to $\sup{a_n}$|
|Nonincreasing + bounded below|Converges to $\inf{a_n}$|
|Monotone + unbounded|Diverges to $\pm\infty$|
|Bounded + not monotone|No conclusion (may or may not converge)|

**The proof recipe for recursive sequences:** $$a_{n+1} = f(a_n) \xrightarrow{\text{(1) monotone, (2) bounded}} \xrightarrow{\text{MST}} L \text{ exists} \xrightarrow{L = f(L)} \text{find } L$$

**The logical backbone:**

$$\text{Completeness of } \mathbb{R} \iff \text{MST} \iff \text{Cauchy criterion} \iff \text{Bolzano-Weierstrass} \iff \text{Nested Interval Theorem}$$

All four are equivalent ŌĆö different faces of the same fundamental property that distinguishes $\mathbb{R}$ from $\mathbb{Q}$.

> 2. Let $a_1 = 1$ and $a_{n+1} = \sqrt{2 + a_n}$. Prove by induction that ${a_n}$ is increasing and bounded above by 2. Find the limit.

> 3. Let $a_n = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{k^2}$. Show this sequence converges by applying the MST. (Bound it above using a telescoping comparison.)

> 4. Give an example of a bounded sequence that does not converge. Why doesn't the MST apply?

> 5. Give an example of an unbounded monotone sequence. What does Corollary 1 say about it?

> 6. (**Completeness**) Exhibit a nondecreasing sequence in $\mathbb{Q}$ that is bounded above in $\mathbb{Q}$ but whose supremum is irrational. What does this say about applying the MST over $\mathbb{Q}$?

> 7. (**ML connection**) Suppose $\mathcal{L}(w_n)$ is a nonincreasing sequence of loss values bounded below by 0. By the MST, $\mathcal{L}(w_n) \to L^*$. Does this imply $w_n$ converges? Construct a counterexample or give conditions under which it does.
