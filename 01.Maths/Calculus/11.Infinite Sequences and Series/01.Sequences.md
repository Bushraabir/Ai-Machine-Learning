## Definition & Notation

> **Definition.** A **sequence** is a function $a : \mathbb{N} \to \mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$, or a metric space). We write its values as $a_1, a_2, a_3, \ldots$ and denote the sequence ${a_n}_{n=1}^{\infty}$, or simply ${a_n}$.

The domain is the natural numbers вЂ” the sequence is **ordered** and **countably infinite** by definition. This is the essential difference from a set.

**Examples of sequences:**

|Name|Formula|First few terms|
|---|---|---|
|Reciprocals|$a_n = 1/n$|$1, 1/2, 1/3, 1/4, \ldots$|
|Powers of $r$|$a_n = r^n$|$r, r^2, r^3, \ldots$|
|Alternating|$a_n = (-1)^n/n$|$-1, 1/2, -1/3, 1/4, \ldots$|
|Factorial growth|$a_n = n!$|$1, 2, 6, 24, 120, \ldots$|
|Fibonacci|$F_1=1, F_2=1, F_n = F_{n-1}+F_{n-2}$|$1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, \ldots$|
|Recursion|$a_1 = 2,\ a_{n+1} = \frac{1}{2}!\left(a_n + \frac{2}{a_n}\right)$|$2, 1.5, 1.4167, \ldots \to \sqrt{2}$|

> **Note.** The last example is Newton's method for $\sqrt{2}$. We will return to this вЂ” it is the prototype of iterative algorithms in optimization, which is the heartbeat of modern ML.

## Limit of a Sequence

> **Definition (Informal).** We say $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = L$ if $a_n$ gets arbitrarily close to $L$ as $n$ gets large.

This is fine for intuition. But "arbitrarily close" and "gets large" need precision.

---

## Rigorous Definition: The Оµ-N Criterion

> **Definition (Formal).** We say $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = L$ if:
> 
> $$\forall \varepsilon > 0,\ \exists N \in \mathbb{N} \text{ such that } n > N \implies |a_n - L| < \varepsilon$$

**Unpacking the logic:**

- You give me any tolerance $\varepsilon > 0$ (no matter how small).
- I must produce a threshold $N$ (depending on $\varepsilon$).
- For all $n$ beyond $N$, the sequence is within $\varepsilon$ of $L$.

The **order of quantifiers** is everything: $\forall \varepsilon\ \exists N$, not $\exists N\ \forall \varepsilon$.

> **Personal note.** This definition is the foundation of all of analysis. Once you truly internalize it вЂ” not just "know" it вЂ” limits of functions, derivatives, integrals, and infinite series all fall into place. Every subsequent $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ argument is this definition in costume.

**Terminology.** If such $L$ exists, we say ${a_n}$ **converges** to $L$. Otherwise it **diverges**.

---

### Proof Template: $a_n = 1/n \to 0$

> **Claim.** $\lim_{n\to\infty} \dfrac{1}{n} = 0$.

**Proof.** Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given. Choose $N \in \mathbb{N}$ with $N > 1/\varepsilon$ (such $N$ exists by the Archimedean property of $\mathbb{R}$). Then for all $n > N$:

$$\left|\frac{1}{n} - 0\right| = \frac{1}{n} < \frac{1}{N} < \varepsilon$$

$\square$

> **Note.** The key move: we need $1/n < \varepsilon$, i.e., $n > 1/\varepsilon$. So set $N = \lceil 1/\varepsilon \rceil$. The work in every $\varepsilon$-$N$ proof is: _rearrange the target inequality to find what $N$ must be_.

---

### Proof Template: $a_n = r^n \to 0$ for $|r| < 1$

> **Claim.** If $|r| < 1$, then $\lim_{n\to\infty} r^n = 0$.

**Proof.** Let $\varepsilon > 0$. We need $|r^n - 0| = |r|^n < \varepsilon$. Taking logarithms: $$n \ln|r| < \ln\varepsilon \implies n > \frac{\ln\varepsilon}{\ln|r|}$$

(Note: $\ln|r| < 0$ since $|r| < 1$, so the inequality flips.) Choose $N > \dfrac{\ln\varepsilon}{\ln|r|}$. Then for all $n > N$: $|r|^n < \varepsilon$. $\square$

---

### Proof Template: Geometric Sequence Limit

> **Claim.** $\lim_{n\to\infty} \dfrac{n}{n+1} = 1$.

**Proof.** $\left|\dfrac{n}{n+1} - 1\right| = \dfrac{1}{n+1} < \dfrac{1}{n}$. Given $\varepsilon > 0$, choose $N > 1/\varepsilon$. Then $n > N \Rightarrow \dfrac{1}{n+1} < \dfrac{1}{n} < \varepsilon$. $\square$

## Limit Laws for Sequences

> **Theorem (Limit Laws).** Suppose $\lim a_n = L$ and $\lim b_n = M$. Then:
> 
> 1. $\lim(a_n \pm b_n) = L \pm M$
> 2. $\lim(a_n \cdot b_n) = LM$
> 3. $\lim(a_n / b_n) = L/M$ provided $M \neq 0$
> 4. $\lim c \cdot a_n = cL$ for constant $c$
> 5. $\lim a_n^p = L^p$ for $p > 0$ and $L > 0$

These are proved by $\varepsilon$-$N$ arguments using the triangle inequality.

**Key tool in proofs:**

$$|a_n b_n - LM| = |a_n b_n - a_n M + a_n M - LM| \leq |a_n||b_n - M| + |M||a_n - L|$$

Since ${a_n}$ converges it is bounded ($|a_n| \leq B$ for all $n$), giving:

$$\leq B|b_n - M| + |M||a_n - L| < B \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2B} + |M| \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2|M|} = \varepsilon$$

with the right choice of $N$.

## Important Theorems

### Squeeze Theorem

> **Theorem (Squeeze / Sandwich).** Suppose $a_n \leq b_n \leq c_n$ for all $n \geq N_0$, and $\lim a_n = \lim c_n = L$. Then $\lim b_n = L$.

**Proof.** Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Since $a_n \to L$, $\exists N_1$ s.t. $n > N_1 \Rightarrow |a_n - L| < \varepsilon \Rightarrow L - \varepsilon < a_n$. Since $c_n \to L$, $\exists N_2$ s.t. $n > N_2 \Rightarrow c_n < L + \varepsilon$. For $n > \max(N_0, N_1, N_2)$:

$$L - \varepsilon < a_n \leq b_n \leq c_n < L + \varepsilon \implies |b_n - L| < \varepsilon \qquad \square$$

**Classic application.** $\lim_{n\to\infty} \dfrac{\cos n}{n} = 0$, since $-\dfrac{1}{n} \leq \dfrac{\cos n}{n} \leq \dfrac{1}{n} \to 0$.

---

### Monotone Convergence Theorem

> **Theorem (MCT).** Every bounded monotone sequence converges.
> 
> - If ${a_n}$ is **increasing** ($a_n \leq a_{n+1}$) and **bounded above**, it converges to $\sup_n a_n$.
> - If ${a_n}$ is **decreasing** ($a_n \geq a_{n+1}$) and **bounded below**, it converges to $\inf_n a_n$.

**Proof (increasing case).** Let $L = \sup{a_n : n \in \mathbb{N}}$ (exists since the sequence is bounded above; this uses the **Completeness Axiom** of $\mathbb{R}$). By definition of supremum, for any $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists some $a_N > L - \varepsilon$. Since the sequence is increasing, for all $n > N$:

$$L - \varepsilon < a_N \leq a_n \leq L < L + \varepsilon$$

Hence $|a_n - L| < \varepsilon$. $\square$

> **This theorem is deep.** It is equivalent to the Completeness Axiom of $\mathbb{R}$ вЂ” you cannot prove it in $\mathbb{Q}$. The sequence $a_n = \lfloor n\sqrt{2} \rfloor / n$ is increasing and bounded above by $\sqrt{2}$, but $\sqrt{2} \notin \mathbb{Q}$.

---

### BolzanoвЂ“Weierstrass Theorem

> **Theorem (BolzanoвЂ“Weierstrass).** Every bounded sequence in $\mathbb{R}$ has a convergent subsequence.

**Proof sketch.** Let ${a_n} \subset [a,b]$. Bisect $[a,b]$: at least one half contains infinitely many terms; call it $I_1$. Bisect $I_1$: at least one half has infinitely many terms; call it $I_2$. Continue. By the Nested Interval Property, $\bigcap I_k = {L}$ for some $L$. The subsequence of selected terms converges to $L$. $\square$

**Significance.** This theorem is used to prove the existence of extreme values (Extreme Value Theorem), convergence of optimization algorithms, and is the beating heart of compactness arguments in analysis.

## Subsequences

> **Definition.** A **subsequence** of ${a_n}$ is ${a_{n_k}}$ where $n_1 < n_2 < n_3 < \cdots$.

> **Theorem.** If $\lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = L$, then every subsequence also converges to $L$.

> **Theorem (Contrapositive).** If two subsequences converge to different limits, the original sequence diverges.

**Classic example.** $a_n = (-1)^n$. The subsequence $a_{2k} = 1 \to 1$ and $a_{2k-1} = -1 \to -1$. Since the limits differ, ${(-1)^n}$ diverges.

## Special Sequences & Their Limits

These should be memorized. They recur constantly.

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n^p} = 0 \quad (p > 0)$$

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} r^n = 0 \quad (|r| < 1), \quad = 1 \quad (r = 1), \quad \text{diverges} \quad (|r| > 1)$$

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} n^{1/n} = 1$$

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n = e^x$$

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{x^n}{n!} = 0 \quad \text{for any fixed } x$$

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\ln n}{n} = 0$$

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n^k}{a^n} = 0 \quad (a > 1, k \text{ fixed}) \quad \text{[exponential beats polynomial]}$$

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{a^n}{n!} = 0 \quad \text{[factorial beats exponential]}$$

> **Hierarchy of growth rates (slowest to fastest):** $$\ln n \ll n^p \ll a^n \ll n! \ll n^n$$

---

### Proof: $\lim n^{1/n} = 1$

Let $a_n = n^{1/n} - 1 \geq 0$. By Bernoulli's inequality, for integer $n \geq 1$:

$$n = (1 + a_n)^n \geq 1 + na_n + \frac{n(n-1)}{2}a_n^2 \geq \frac{n(n-1)}{2}a_n^2$$

So $a_n^2 \leq \dfrac{2}{n-1}$, giving $0 \leq a_n \leq \sqrt{\dfrac{2}{n-1}} \to 0$. By Squeeze, $a_n \to 0$, hence $n^{1/n} \to 1$. $\square$

---

### Proof: $\lim (1 + x/n)^n = e^x$

Let $a_n = n \ln(1 + x/n)$. As $n \to \infty$, set $h = x/n \to 0$:

$$a_n = \frac{\ln(1+h)}{h} \cdot x \to 1 \cdot x = x$$

using $\lim_{h\to 0} \ln(1+h)/h = 1$ (L'HГөpital or Taylor). Since $\exp$ is continuous:

$$\left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n = e^{a_n} \to e^x \qquad \square$$

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 вЂ” Limit Laws

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{3n^2 - 2n + 1}{5n^2 + n - 4}$$

Divide numerator and denominator by $n^2$ (the leading power):

$$= \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{3 - 2/n + 1/n^2}{5 + 1/n - 4/n^2} = \frac{3 - 0 + 0}{5 + 0 - 0} = \frac{3}{5}$$

---

### Example 2 вЂ” Exponential vs Polynomial

$$\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{2^n}{n^{100}}$$

Use the fact that $\lim x^n/n! = 0$ to show $\lim r^n/n^k = \infty$ for $r > 1$:

By AM-GM or by writing $2^n = e^{n\ln 2}$ and comparing growth rates: $n\ln 2 \gg 100\ln n$. The limit is $+\infty$.

---

### Example 3 вЂ” Recursive Sequence via MCT

Define $a_1 = 1$, $a_{n+1} = \sqrt{2 + a_n}$. Does it converge?

**Step 1 вЂ” Bounded above.** Claim $a_n \leq 2$ for all $n$.

- Base: $a_1 = 1 \leq 2$. вњ“
- Inductive step: $a_{n+1} = \sqrt{2 + a_n} \leq \sqrt{2+2} = 2$. вњ“

**Step 2 вЂ” Increasing.** $a_2 = \sqrt{3} > 1 = a_1$. Suppose $a_n > a_{n-1}$. Then $a_{n+1} = \sqrt{2+a_n} > \sqrt{2+a_{n-1}} = a_n$.

**Step 3 вЂ” Conclude.** By MCT, $L = \lim a_n$ exists. Taking limits on both sides: $$L = \sqrt{2 + L} \implies L^2 = 2 + L \implies L^2 - L - 2 = 0 \implies (L-2)(L+1) = 0$$

Since $a_n > 0$ for all $n$, $L = 2$.

---

### Example 4 вЂ” Classic $\varepsilon$-$N$ Proof

> **Claim.** $\lim_{n\to\infty} \dfrac{2n+3}{n+1} = 2$.

**Scratch work.** $\left|\dfrac{2n+3}{n+1} - 2\right| = \left|\dfrac{2n+3 - 2n-2}{n+1}\right| = \dfrac{1}{n+1} < \dfrac{1}{n}$. We need this $< \varepsilon$: take $N > 1/\varepsilon$.

**Proof.** Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Choose $N \in \mathbb{N}$ with $N > 1/\varepsilon$. For all $n > N$: $$\left|\frac{2n+3}{n+1} - 2\right| = \frac{1}{n+1} < \frac{1}{n} < \frac{1}{N} < \varepsilon \qquad \square$$

---

### Example 5 вЂ” The Number $e$

The sequence $a_n = \left(1 + \dfrac{1}{n}\right)^n$ is:

- Increasing (can be shown via AM-GM)
- Bounded above by 3 (by Binomial Theorem: $a_n < \sum_{k=0}^n \frac{1}{k!} < \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{1}{k!} < 3$)

By MCT, it converges. Its limit is defined as $e \approx 2.71828\ldots$

This is one of the most important sequences in mathematics.

## Connection to Series

Every infinite series $\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k$ is defined through sequences вЂ” specifically, the sequence of **partial sums**:

$$S_n = \sum_{k=1}^n a_k$$

We say the series **converges** iff ${S_n}$ converges:

$$\sum_{k=1}^\infty a_k = \lim_{n\to\infty} S_n$$

So the entire theory of infinite series вЂ” geometric series, Taylor series, Fourier series вЂ” is secretly the theory of sequences. See: [[Series]].

> **Key necessary condition.** If $\sum a_k$ converges, then $a_k \to 0$. Proof: $a_n = S_n - S_{n-1} \to L - L = 0$. The converse fails: $\sum 1/n$ diverges despite $1/n \to 0$.

## Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

This is where the abstraction pays off. Nearly everything in modern ML is a sequence вЂ” and understanding convergence is understanding _whether and why algorithms work_.

---

### 1. Gradient Descent IS a Sequence

Define the parameter sequence:

$$\theta_{n+1} = \theta_n - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_n)$$

This is a **recursively defined sequence** in $\mathbb{R}^d$ (or millions of dimensions). The central question of optimization theory is:

$$\text{Does } {\theta_n} \text{ converge, and to what?}$$

The tools are exactly the ones in this note:

- **MCT analog:** If the loss is bounded below and decreasing along the sequence, we need a monotone convergence argument (in $\mathbb{R}^d$, this requires convexity).
- **Cauchy criterion:** Convergence without knowing the limit вЂ” useful when the true minimum is unknown.
- **Subsequences:** Stochastic gradient descent produces a _random_ sequence; convergence is shown for subsequences with probability 1 (almost sure convergence).

For strongly convex loss with Lipschitz gradient and step size $\eta < 2/L$:

$$|\theta_n - \theta^_| \leq \left(1 - \eta\mu\right)^n |\theta_0 - \theta^_|$$

This is **geometric convergence** вЂ” the sequence $r^n$ with $r = 1 - \eta\mu < 1$.

---

### 2. Learning Rate Schedules вЂ” Convergence Conditions

The Robbins-Monro conditions for SGD convergence require the learning rate sequence ${\eta_n}$ to satisfy:

$$\sum_{n=1}^\infty \eta_n = \infty \qquad \text{and} \qquad \sum_{n=1}^\infty \eta_n^2 < \infty$$

These are **series convergence conditions on sequences**. A common choice: $\eta_n = c/n$, for which $\sum 1/n = \infty$ (harmonic series diverges) and $\sum 1/n^2 < \infty$ (p-series, $p = 2 > 1$). Both conditions satisfied.

The first condition ensures the algorithm can "travel far enough" to reach the minimum; the second ensures the noise eventually dies out. This is rigorous sequence analysis applied directly to algorithm design.

---

### 3. Recurrent Neural Networks вЂ” Sequences as Input

RNNs process sequences ${x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T}$ by maintaining a hidden state:

$$h_t = f(W_h h_{t-1} + W_x x_t + b)$$

This is a **time-varying recursion** вЂ” a sequence of hidden states. The question of whether information at time $t=1$ affects $h_T$ as $T \to \infty$ is a question about the convergence/divergence of this recursion.

**Vanishing gradients** correspond to the sequence ${|\partial h_T/\partial h_1|}$ converging to 0 (like $r^n \to 0$ for $|r| < 1$). **Exploding gradients** correspond to divergence (like $r^n \to \infty$ for $|r| > 1$). LSTMs and GRUs are architectural fixes for this sequence convergence problem.

---

### 4. Attention & Positional Encoding

The transformer's positional encoding is defined as a **sequence of vectors**:

$$\text{PE}(n, 2i) = \sin!\left(\frac{n}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \quad \text{PE}(n, 2i+1) = \cos!\left(\frac{n}{10000^{2i/d}}\right)$$

This is a family of sequences in $n$ (position), parameterized by frequency $1/10000^{2i/d}$. The design choice ensures that **relative positions** can be expressed as linear transformations вЂ” a property that depends on the identities $\sin(n+k) = \sin n \cos k + \cos n \sin k$, i.e., the algebraic properties of these sequences.

---

### 5. Convergence of Neural Network Training вЂ” Loss Sequences

The loss sequence ${\mathcal{L}(\theta_n)}$ is a sequence of non-negative real numbers. We want it to converge. Tools from sequence analysis:

- **Bounded below** (loss $\geq 0$) + **decreasing** (with correct step size) в†’ by MCT, $\mathcal{L}(\theta_n)$ converges to some $L^*$.
- But does $\theta_n$ converge? Not necessarily вЂ” the loss can flatten while parameters wander (flat minima, manifold of solutions).
- **Cauchy sequences in parameter space:** If ${\theta_n}$ is Cauchy, then $\theta_n \to \theta^*$. This is what "converging weights" actually means.

The theory of convergence for Adam, RMSProp, etc. is active research, and it is precisely: proving that the parameter sequence ${\theta_n}$ is Cauchy under appropriate conditions.

---

### 6. Fixed-Point Iterations вЂ” Banach Contraction

Many algorithms are **fixed-point iterations**: $x_{n+1} = f(x_n)$, seeking $x^* = f(x^*)$.

> **Banach Fixed-Point Theorem.** If $f: X \to X$ is a **contraction** ($|f(x) - f(y)| \leq c|x-y|$, $c < 1$) on a complete metric space, then the sequence $x_{n+1} = f(x_n)$ converges to the unique fixed point $x^_$, and: $$|x_n - x^_| \leq \frac{c^n}{1-c}|x_1 - x_0|$$

This is a **geometric sequence bound** $\propto c^n$. Applications in ML:

- **Value iteration** in reinforcement learning (Bellman operator is a $\gamma$-contraction)
- **Newton's method** for training (quadratic convergence near solution)
- **Normalizing flows** вЂ” invertible neural networks as fixed-point iterations

> **RL note.** The Bellman optimality operator $\mathcal{T}$: $$(\mathcal{T}V)(s) = \max_a \left[R(s,a) + \gamma \sum_{s'} P(s'|s,a)V(s')\right]$$ is a $\gamma$-contraction in the $\ell^\infty$ norm (where $\gamma < 1$ is the discount factor). Therefore the sequence $V_{n+1} = \mathcal{T}V_n$ converges to $V^*$ by the Banach theorem. This is the theoretical guarantee behind value iteration.

---

### 7. Cauchy Sequences in Deep Learning Numerics

In floating-point arithmetic, exact limits don't exist вЂ” we work with **Cauchy-like stopping criteria**:

$$|\theta_{n+1} - \theta_n| < \delta \quad \text{(parameter change small)}$$ $$|\mathcal{L}_{n+1} - \mathcal{L}_n| < \delta \quad \text{(loss change small)}$$

These are practical implementations of the **Cauchy criterion**: the sequence has "essentially stopped moving." The relationship between these two criteria (loss converging в‰  parameters converging in non-convex landscapes) is a deep practical issue in training large models.

> 1. **$\varepsilon$-$N$ proof.** Prove from the definition that $\lim_{n\to\infty} \dfrac{n^2 + 1}{2n^2 - 3} = \dfrac{1}{2}$.

> 2. **Squeeze.** Prove $\lim_{n\to\infty} \dfrac{\sin(n^2)}{n} = 0$.

> 3. **MCT.** Let $a_1 = \sqrt{2}$ and $a_{n+1} = \sqrt{2 a_n}$. Prove the sequence converges and find its limit.

> 4. **Subsequence.** Show that $a_n = \sin(n\pi/2)$ diverges by finding two subsequences with different limits.

> 5. **Growth rates.** Order the following by rate of growth: $\ln n,\ n^{0.01},\ 1.001^n,\ n!,\ n^n / n!$.

> 6. **Recursive limit.** Define $a_1 = 0$, $a_{n+1} = \frac{a_n + 2}{a_n + 3}$. Assuming the limit $L$ exists, find it. Then prove the sequence converges using MCT.

> 7. **ML connection.** The gradient descent sequence satisfies $|\theta_n - \theta^_| \leq (1 - \eta\mu)^n |\theta_0 - \theta^_|$. Given $\mu = 0.1$, $\eta = 5$, $|\theta_0 - \theta^_| = 100$, find the smallest $n$ such that $|\theta_n - \theta^_| < 0.001$.
