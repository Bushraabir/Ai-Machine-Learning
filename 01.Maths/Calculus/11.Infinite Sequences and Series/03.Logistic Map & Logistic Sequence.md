## The Equation

$$\boxed{x_{n+1} = r, x_n (1 - x_n), \qquad x_0 \in (0,1),\quad r \in (0,4]}$$

That is the entire object of study. One quadratic map on $[0,1]$. The parameter $r$ controls the behaviour, and the range of behaviour as $r$ varies is extraordinary.

> **Personal note:** Pierre François Verhulst introduced the logistic _differential_ equation in 1838 as a model of population growth with a carrying capacity. The discrete analogue — the logistic _map_ — was popularised by Robert May in his landmark 1976 _Nature_ paper, which introduced it to biologists and arguably birthed the modern study of chaos. Read that paper. It is three pages and changed everything.

## Motivation: Discrete Population Growth

### The Malthusian (Unconstrained) Model

Start simple. If a population $x_n$ grows by a factor $r$ each generation:

$$x_{n+1} = r x_n$$

Solution: $x_n = r^n x_0$. Exponential growth — unbounded. Unrealistic.

### The Logistic Correction

Introduce a carrying capacity. Normalize so the maximum population is 1. The growth rate is penalized as $x_n \to 1$:

$$x_{n+1} = r x_n \underbrace{(1 - x_n)}_{\text{penalty term}}$$

- When $x_n \approx 0$: nearly unconstrained, $x_{n+1} \approx r x_n$.
- When $x_n \approx 1$: population at carrying capacity, $x_{n+1} \approx 0$.
- The factor $(1 - x_n)$ is the fraction of unused capacity.

This is the **logistic map**. The full behaviour as $r$ varies from 0 to 4 spans fixed points, cycles, and chaos.

## Fixed Points & Stability — The Full Analysis

### Finding Fixed Points

A fixed point $x^_$ satisfies $f(x^_) = x^*$ where $f(x) = rx(1-x)$:

$$rx^_(1 - x^_) = x^*$$

$$x^_[r(1-x^_) - 1] = 0$$

Two fixed points:

$$\boxed{x^* = 0} \qquad \text{and} \qquad \boxed{x^* = \frac{r-1}{r}} \quad (r > 1)$$

The second fixed point only lies in $(0,1)$ when $r > 1$.

### Stability Analysis

A fixed point $x^_$ is **stable** (attracting) if $|f'(x^_)| < 1$, and **unstable** (repelling) if $|f'(x^*)| > 1$.

Compute the derivative:

$$f'(x) = r(1 - 2x)$$

__At $x^_ = 0$:_*

$$f'(0) = r$$

- Stable when $r < 1$.
- Unstable when $r > 1$.

__At $x^_ = (r-1)/r$:_*

$$f'!\left(\frac{r-1}{r}\right) = r\left(1 - \frac{2(r-1)}{r}\right) = 2 - r$$

- Stable when $|2 - r| < 1$, i.e., $1 < r < 3$.
- At $r = 3$: $|f'(x^*)| = 1$ — **bifurcation point**.
- Unstable when $r > 3$.

### Stability Summary

|$r$ range|Attracting fixed point|Long-term behaviour|
|---|---|---|
|$0 < r < 1$|$x^* = 0$|Population dies out|
|$1 < r < 3$|$x^* = (r-1)/r$|Convergence to equilibrium|
|$r = 3$|Bifurcation|Marginal — period-2 born|
|$3 < r < 1+\sqrt{6} \approx 3.449$|None (2-cycle)|Period-2 oscillation|
|$r \approx 3.449$|—|Period-4 bifurcation|
|$r \approx 3.570$|—|Onset of chaos|
|$3.570 < r \leq 4$|—|Chaos (with periodic windows)|

## Bifurcations and the Period-Doubling Cascade

### What is a Bifurcation?

At $r = 3$, the fixed point $x^* = (r-1)/r$ loses stability — it transitions from attracting to repelling. Simultaneously, a **2-cycle** is born: two points $x_a, x_b$ with $f(x_a) = x_b$ and $f(x_b) = x_a$.

### Finding the 2-Cycle

The 2-cycle points satisfy $f(f(x)) = x$ but $f(x) \neq x$. Factoring out the fixed points from $f^2(x) - x = 0$, the 2-cycle points are:

$$x_{a,b} = \frac{(r+1) \pm \sqrt{(r+1)(r-3)}}{2r}$$

These are real only when $r \geq 3$ — the 2-cycle is born exactly at the bifurcation $r = 3$.

### Stability of the 2-Cycle

By the chain rule: $$\frac{d}{dx} f^2(x)\bigg|_{x=x_a} = f'(x_b) \cdot f'(x_a) = (2 - r)^2 + \text{(correction)}$$

Working through the algebra: the 2-cycle is stable for $3 < r < 1 + \sqrt{6} \approx 3.4495$.

### The Period-Doubling Cascade

At each subsequent $r_k$, the $2^{k-1}$-cycle loses stability and a $2^k$-cycle is born:

|Bifurcation $k$|$r_k$ (approx)|New period|
|---|---|---|
|1|$3.000000$|2|
|2|$3.449490$|4|
|3|$3.544090$|8|
|4|$3.564407$|16|
|5|$3.568759$|32|
|$\infty$|$3.569946\ldots$|$\infty$ (onset of chaos)|

## Chaos: Sensitive Dependence on Initial Conditions

### Definition (Devaney)

The logistic map at $r > r_\infty$ is **chaotic** in the sense of:

1. **Sensitive dependence:** $\exists, \delta > 0$ such that for any $x_0$ and any $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $y_0$ with $|x_0 - y_0| < \varepsilon$ yet $|x_n - y_n| > \delta$ for some $n$.
2. **Topological transitivity:** There exists an orbit dense in $[0,1]$.
3. **Dense periodic orbits:** Periodic points are dense.

### Conjugacy at $r = 4$

At $r = 4$ the logistic map is **topologically conjugate** to the tent/doubling map via $x_n = \sin^2(\pi\theta_n)$:

$$\theta_{n+1} = 2\theta_n \pmod{1}$$

This is the **doubling map**: it shifts binary digits of $\theta_0$. Any uncertainty in the last bit of $\theta_0$ propagates to the first bit after $n$ steps. The Lyapunov exponent at $r = 4$ is exactly $\lambda = \ln 2$.

## Feigenbaum's Universality Constant

In 1975, Mitchell Feigenbaum computed ratios of successive bifurcation intervals:

$$\delta = \lim_{k\to\infty} \frac{r_k - r_{k-1}}{r_{k+1} - r_k}$$

|$k$|$\delta_k$|
|---|---|
|1|$\approx 4.751$|
|2|$\approx 4.656$|
|3|$\approx 4.668$|
|$\infty$|$\mathbf{4.669201609\ldots}$|

$$\boxed{\delta = 4.669201609\ldots}$$

**This constant is universal:** it appears in the period-doubling cascade of _any_ unimodal map with a smooth quadratic maximum — sine map, Hénon map restricted to 1D, and many others. The specific equation doesn't matter; only the local shape of the maximum matters.

Feigenbaum proved this using **renormalization group** ideas borrowed from quantum field theory — the same mathematical structure that governs phase transitions in statistical mechanics.

> The Feigenbaum constant is transcendental and has no known closed form in terms of $\pi$, $e$, or other classical constants. It is a genuinely new mathematical constant.

## Lyapunov Exponents

### Definition

$$\lambda = \lim_{N \to \infty} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \ln |f'(x_n)| = \lim_{N\to\infty} \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \ln|r(1 - 2x_n)|$$

**Interpretation:**

- $\lambda < 0$: attracting periodic orbit
- $\lambda = 0$: bifurcation point (marginal stability)
- $\lambda > 0$: chaos — exponential separation of nearby orbits

### Exact Value at $r = 4$

Using the invariant measure $\rho(x) = 1/(\pi\sqrt{x(1-x)})$ (arcsine distribution):

$$\lambda = \int_0^1 \ln|f'(x)|, \rho(x), dx = \ln 2$$

### Lyapunov Exponent vs $r$ (schematic)

```

  │

  │
  └──────────────────────────────────── r
  0        1       2       3    3.57   4

  λ < 0 throughout stable regime; λ > 0 in chaos;
  periodic windows inside chaos give dips back below 0.
```

## The Connection to the Logistic Function (Sigmoid)

The **logistic function** (sigmoid):

$$\sigma(t) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-t}}$$

is the solution to the **continuous logistic ODE** $dp/dt = rp(1-p)$:

$$p(t) = \sigma!\left(rt + \ln\frac{p_0}{1-p_0}\right)$$

The logistic _map_ is the **Euler discretization** of this ODE with step size 1:

$$p_{n+1} \approx p_n + r p_n(1-p_n)$$

which is a logistic map with parameter $1 + r$. The sigmoid is the equilibrium of the continuous flow; the logistic map iterates the discrete approximation and discovers the rich bifurcation structure that the smooth ODE solution conceals.

**Key algebraic link.** The derivative of $\sigma$:

$$\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)(1 - \sigma(z))$$

is exactly the logistic map expression $x(1-x)$ evaluated at $x = \sigma(z)$. This is not a coincidence — it is why the logistic function is the canonical activation for binary classification, and why backpropagation through sigmoid layers involves the logistic map's own derivative structure.

## Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

---

### 1. The Sigmoid Neuron and the Vanishing Gradient Problem

The activation $\sigma(z) = 1/(1+e^{-z})$ maps $\mathbb{R} \to (0,1)$. Its gradient $\sigma'(z) = \sigma(z)(1-\sigma(z)) \leq 1/4$ — saturating to 0 when $|z|$ is large.

In a deep network of $L$ sigmoid layers, backpropagation multiplies gradients:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_1} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x_L} \cdot \prod_{k=1}^{L} \sigma'(z_k) \cdot W_k$$

Each $\sigma'(z_k) \leq 1/4$, so $\prod_{k=1}^{L}\sigma'(z_k) \leq (1/4)^L \to 0$ exponentially. **This is the vanishing gradient problem** — directly caused by the logistic map's derivative being bounded away from 1 at its fixed points. ReLU ($f'(z) = 1$ in the active region) was introduced precisely to escape this.

---

### 2. Bifurcations in RNNs — Exploding and Vanishing Gradients

A vanilla RNN updates:

$$h_{n+1} = \tanh(Wh_n + Ux_n + b)$$

The Jacobian of this map is $J_n = W \cdot \text{diag}(1 - h_n^2)$. Backpropagating through time multiplies these Jacobians:

$$\frac{\partial h_T}{\partial h_0} = \prod_{n=0}^{T-1} J_n$$

If $\rho(W) > 1$: eigenvalues of the product grow — **exploding gradients** (chaos regime of logistic map). If $\rho(W) < 1$: eigenvalues shrink — **vanishing gradients** (stable fixed point regime).

The LSTM's **gating mechanism** is a learned bifurcation controller: the forget gate $f_t = \sigma(W_f h_{t-1} + U_f x_t)$ adaptively scales the recurrent eigenvalues to stay near 1 — precisely the $r \approx 3$ boundary between stability and chaos.

---

### 3. Echo State Networks — Edge of Chaos

**Reservoir Computing** (Echo State Networks) designs a fixed random RNN and trains only the readout. The design principle is the **edge of chaos**: spectral radius $\rho(W) \approx 1$.

This is _exactly_ the logistic map at $r \approx 3$:

- $\rho < 1$ (stable): reservoir forgets inputs, poor memory
- $\rho > 1$ (chaotic): sensitive dependence, unreliable computation
- $\rho \approx 1$ (edge): maximum memory capacity and computational richness

This is a theorem in reservoir computing (Jaeger 2001), and it is Feigenbaum's story rewritten in $N \times N$ matrix language.

---

### 4. Neural ODEs — Discretization Chaos

A Neural ODE learns $dz/dt = f_\theta(z, t)$ and integrates it numerically. The Euler method with step $h$:

$$z_{n+1} = z_n + h f_\theta(z_n)$$

applied to the logistic ODE $dp/dt = p(1-p)$ gives:

$$p_{n+1} = p_n(1 + h - h p_n)$$

a logistic map with effective $r = 1 + h$. For $h$ small: stable fixed point (correct). For $h$ large (say $h = 2.5$): $r = 3.5$ — **period-4 oscillations** from a smooth ODE whose exact solution has no oscillation at all.

**Lesson:** naive large-step discretization of physics can produce spurious chaos. This motivates adaptive solvers (Dormand-Prince, Runge-Kutta 4/5) inside Neural ODE training, where step size is controlled to keep the effective $r$ safely below the bifurcation threshold.

---

### 5. SGD at the Edge of Stability

For learning rate $\eta$ on a loss with sharpness $\lambda_{\max}(\nabla^2 \mathcal{L})$, the linearized gradient descent update is:

$$w_{n+1} = w_n - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(w_n)$$

Stability requires $\eta \lambda_{\max} < 2$ — directly analogous to requiring $r < 3$ in the logistic map. Recent work (Cohen et al., ICLR 2021) shows that Adam and SGD with large $\eta$ operate in the **Edge of Stability** regime where $\eta \lambda_{\max} \approx 2$ throughout training — the network self-organises to the bifurcation boundary, similar to how some dynamical systems are attracted to the edge of chaos.

---

### 6. The Logistic Loss — Why It Cancels the Sigmoid Derivative

For binary classification with sigmoid output $\hat y = \sigma(z)$, the squared error loss is:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{sq}} = \frac{1}{2}(\sigma(z) - y)^2 \implies \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\text{sq}}}{\partial z} = (\sigma(z) - y) \cdot \sigma'(z)$$

When $\sigma(z) \approx 0$ or $1$ (confident but wrong), $\sigma'(z) \approx 0$ — the gradient vanishes. The **cross-entropy (logistic) loss**:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}} = -y\log\sigma(z) - (1-y)\log(1-\sigma(z)) \implies \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_{\text{CE}}}{\partial z} = \sigma(z) - y$$

The sigmoid derivative _cancels_ because $\partial(-\log\sigma)/\partial z = 1 - \sigma = (1-\sigma)$, and combined they give $\sigma - y$ directly. The logistic loss is the unique loss that cancels the saturation of the logistic activation — a deep compatibility between the loss and activation rooted in the exponential family structure of the Bernoulli distribution.

---

### 7. Chaos and Generalization — Lyapunov Exponents of Training

Recent theory links **generalization** to the **Lyapunov exponent** of the SGD trajectory in parameter space. Models that generalise well tend to reside near broad, flat minima (negative Lyapunov exponent — attracting fixed points of the loss landscape dynamics). Models that overfit tend to converge to sharp, narrow minima (borderline unstable, analogous to $r \approx 3$). This is an active research area (2022–present) directly inspired by dynamical systems theory of the logistic map.

## Practice Problems

> 1. Show $x^* = (r-1)/r$ is stable for $1 < r < 3$ by showing $|f'(x^*)| = |2 - r| < 1$ directly.

> 2. For $r = 2$, make the substitution $x_n = \frac{1}{2}(1 - y_n)$ and find the recurrence for $y_n$. Solve it in closed form.

> 3. Verify that the 2-cycle points $(x_a, x_b)$ found in Example 2 satisfy $f(x_a) = x_b$ and $f(x_b) = x_a$ for $r = 3.2$.

> 4. Prove that at $r = 4$, the substitution $x_n = \sin^2(\pi\theta_n)$ transforms the logistic map into the doubling map $\theta_{n+1} = 2\theta_n \pmod 1$.

> 5. Using $r_1 = 3$, $r_2 = 3.4495$, $r_3 = 3.5441$, $r_4 = 3.5644$, compute $\delta_1$, $\delta_2$, $\delta_3$ and compare to $\delta = 4.669$.

> 6. (**ML connection**) Show that the logistic loss $\mathcal{L} = -y\log\sigma(z) - (1-y)\log(1-\sigma(z))$ has gradient $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial z = \sigma(z) - y$. Explain why this is superior to squared error for sigmoid-activated neurons from a gradient-flow perspective.

> 7. (**Hard**) Prove that the period-3 window implies chaos: if $f$ has a period-3 orbit, then by Sharkovskii's Theorem, it has orbits of all periods. What does this imply about the logistic map for $r$ in the period-3 window near $r \approx 3.83$?
