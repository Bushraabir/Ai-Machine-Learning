
## §1. Motivation — The Question Nobody Thinks to Ask

When you differentiate $f(x,y)$ first with respect to $x$, then with respect to $y$, do you get the same thing as differentiating first in $y$, then in $x$?

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right) \stackrel{?}{=} \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)$$

In shorthand: $f_{xy} \stackrel{?}{=} f_{yx}$.

Intuitively, differentiation feels like a linear operation — order shouldn't matter. But that intuition is **not a proof**, and it's not always true. Clairaut's theorem gives the precise sufficient conditions under which order genuinely doesn't matter, and the failure cases reveal something deep about regularity.

---

## §2. Notation and Setup

Let $f: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ where $U$ is open. The **mixed partial derivatives** are:

$$f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y , \partial x} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)$$

$$f_{yx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x , \partial y} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)$$

> **Warning on notation.** The Leibniz notation $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y , \partial x}$ means: differentiate $x$ **first**, then $y$. The denominator is read **right to left** — the innermost operation is on the right. The subscript notation $f_{xy}$ is read **left to right** — $x$ first, then $y$. These two notations **agree** in their convention, but double-check in whatever textbook you're using.

For $f: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, higher-order mixed partials are defined analogously: $$f_{x_i x_j} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_j , \partial x_i}$$

---

## §3. The Theorem

> **Theorem (Clairaut, also attributed to Schwarz / Young).** Let $f: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$. Suppose that $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ both exist on $U$, and that they are both **continuous** at a point $(a,b) \in U$. Then: $$\boxed{f_{xy}(a,b) = f_{yx}(a,b)}$$

More practically — and the form you'll use in 99% of computations:

> **Corollary.** If $f \in C^2(U)$ (i.e., all second-order partial derivatives exist and are continuous on $U$), then all mixed second-order partials are equal throughout $U$.

---

## §4. Proof

This is a genuine $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ argument. It's worth doing once carefully.

**Setup.** Fix $(a,b) \in U$. For small $h, k \neq 0$ (chosen so the rectangle stays in $U$), define the **double increment**:

$$\Delta(h,k) = f(a+h, b+k) - f(a+h, b) - f(a, b+k) + f(a,b)$$

The key observation: $\Delta(h,k)$ can be factored in **two ways** using the single-variable Mean Value Theorem.

---

**Factoring via $x$ first.** Define $\varphi(x) = f(x, b+k) - f(x, b)$. Then: $$\Delta(h,k) = \varphi(a+h) - \varphi(a)$$

By MVT applied to $\varphi$ on $[a, a+h]$: $\exists, \theta_1 \in (0,1)$ such that: $$\Delta(h,k) = h,\varphi'(a + \theta_1 h) = h\bigl[f_x(a+\theta_1 h,, b+k) - f_x(a+\theta_1 h,, b)\bigr]$$

Apply MVT again to $f_x(a+\theta_1 h, \cdot)$ on $[b, b+k]$: $\exists, \theta_2 \in (0,1)$ such that: $$\Delta(h,k) = hk, f_{xy}(a + \theta_1 h,, b + \theta_2 k)$$

---

**Factoring via $y$ first.** Define $\psi(y) = f(a+h, y) - f(a, y)$. By the same MVT argument: $$\Delta(h,k) = hk, f_{yx}(a + \theta_3 h,, b + \theta_4 k)$$

for some $\theta_3, \theta_4 \in (0,1)$.

---

**Conclusion.** Since both expressions equal $\Delta(h,k)$: $$hk, f_{xy}(a + \theta_1 h,, b + \theta_2 k) = hk, f_{yx}(a + \theta_3 h,, b + \theta_4 k)$$

Dividing by $hk \neq 0$: $$f_{xy}(a + \theta_1 h,, b + \theta_2 k) = f_{yx}(a + \theta_3 h,, b + \theta_4 k)$$

Now take $h, k \to 0$. Since $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are **continuous at $(a,b)$**, both sides converge to their values at $(a,b)$: $$f_{xy}(a,b) = f_{yx}(a,b) \qquad \square$$

---

## §5. The Counterexample — When It Fails

The continuity hypothesis is **not** superfluous. The following classic example shows Clairaut can fail without it.

**Define:** $$f(x,y) = \begin{cases} xy,\dfrac{x^2 - y^2}{x^2 + y^2} & (x,y) \neq (0,0) \ 0 & (x,y) = (0,0) \end{cases}$$

**Compute $f_x(0,y)$ for $y \neq 0$** via the limit definition: $$f_x(0,y) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(h,y)-f(0,y)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{hy \cdot \frac{h^2-y^2}{h^2+y^2}}{h} = y \cdot \frac{-y^2}{y^2} = -y$$

So $f_x(0,y) = -y$ for $y \neq 0$, and $f_x(0,0) = 0$.

**Then:** $f_{xy}(0,0) = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}[f_x(0,y)]\big|_{y=0} = \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(-y)\big|_{y=0} = -1$

**By symmetry** (swap $x \leftrightarrow y$, note sign change): $f_{yx}(0,0) = +1$

$$f_{xy}(0,0) = -1 \neq 1 = f_{yx}(0,0)$$

**Why did it fail?** You can verify that $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ exist everywhere, but are **not continuous at the origin**. Clairaut's hypothesis is violated, and the conclusion fails. $\blacksquare$

## §6. Worked Examples

### Example A — Standard Verification

Let $f(x,y) = x^3 y^2 - \sin(xy) + e^{x+y}$.

$f \in C^\infty(\mathbb{R}^2)$, so Clairaut applies everywhere. Let's verify explicitly.

$$f_x = 3x^2 y^2 - y\cos(xy) + e^{x+y}$$ $$f_{xy} = 6x^2 y - \cos(xy) + xy\sin(xy) + e^{x+y}$$

$$f_y = 2x^3 y - x\cos(xy) + e^{x+y}$$ $$f_{yx} = 6x^2 y - \cos(xy) + xy\sin(xy) + e^{x+y}$$

$f_{xy} = f_{yx}$. ✓

---

### Example B — Checking the Curl Condition (FTLI Connection)

$\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q\rangle = \langle 2xy + y^3,; x^2 + 3xy^2\rangle$.

For $\mathbf{F}$ to be conservative, we need $P_y = Q_x$ (this is exactly Clairaut applied to the potential $f$):

$$P_y = 2x + 3y^2, \qquad Q_x = 2x + 3y^2$$

$P_y = Q_x$ ✓. So $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative.

_Find $f$:_ $f_x = 2xy + y^3 \Rightarrow f = x^2y + xy^3 + g(y)$. $f_y = x^2 + 3xy^2 + g'(y) = x^2 + 3xy^2 \Rightarrow g'(y) = 0 \Rightarrow f = x^2y + xy^3 + C$.

The whole machinery of conservative fields **depends on Clairaut**: that $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$ is what makes $P_y = Q_x$ a valid test.

---

### Example C — Higher Order Partials ($\mathbb{R}^3$, $f \in C^3$)

For $f \in C^3(U)$, Clairaut extends to third-order: any ordering of three variables gives the same result. For example:

$$f_{xyz} = f_{xzy} = f_{yxz} = f_{yzx} = f_{zxy} = f_{zyx}$$

There are $3! = 6$ orderings of ${x,y,z}$ — all equal for $C^3$ functions.

**Check:** $f(x,y,z) = x^2 y z^3$

$$f_{xyz} = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(2xyz^3) = \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(2xz^3) = 6xz^2$$

$$f_{zyx} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}\frac{\partial}{\partial y}(3x^2yz^2) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(3x^2z^2) = 6xz^2$$ ✓

## §7. Visualization

### 7.1 What Do Mixed Partials Measure?

Single partials: rate of change in one direction.

Mixed partials: **how the rate of change in one direction varies as you move in another direction**.

$f_{xy}$: "How does the slope in the $x$-direction change as you move in the $y$-direction?"

Clairaut says: this is the same as asking "How does the slope in the $y$-direction change as you move in the $x$-direction?" — at least when these quantities are continuous.

### 7.2 The Rectangle Argument — Geometric Core of the Proof

The double increment $\Delta(h,k)$ is exactly the **signed sum of $f$ at the four corners of a rectangle**:

```
(a, b+k) ───────────── (a+h, b+k)
    |                       |
    |    Δ(h,k) = f(a+h,b+k)
    |           - f(a+h, b)
    |           - f(a,  b+k)
    |           + f(a,  b)
    |                       |
 (a, b)  ───────────── (a+h, b)
```

This quantity $\Delta(h,k)$ measures the **"twist"** of $f$ across the rectangle. Both mixed partials try to measure the same twist — from different directions — and Clairaut says they agree in the limit when continuity holds.

### 7.3 Saddle Surface — Where Mixed Partials Are Non-Zero

For $f(x,y) = xy$ (the standard saddle):

```
          z
          |     ↗ (tilts up this way)
          |   /
  ────────+────────  y
         /|  ↘
        / |    (tilts down this way)
       x
       
  f_x = y,   f_y = x
  f_{xy} = 1,  f_{yx} = 1  ✓ (Clairaut holds)
```

The mixed partial $f_{xy} = 1$ says: as you move in the $y$-direction, the slope in the $x$-direction increases at rate $1$. The symmetry $f_{yx} = f_{xy}$ is visually obvious on the saddle — the surface has a clean bilinear structure.

### 7.4 Clairaut Failure — Visualization at Origin

For the counterexample $f(x,y) = xy\frac{x^2-y^2}{x^2+y^2}$, the function is smooth away from the origin but has a "pinched" behavior there — the mixed partial derivatives "disagree" about the twist because the surface has an **asymmetric singularity** in its curvature at the origin.

```
  Along x-axis:  f(x,0) = 0  (flat)
  Along y-axis:  f(0,y) = 0  (flat)
  Along y=x:     f(x,x) = 0  (flat)
  Along y=-x:    f(x,-x) = x²  (curves up!)
  
  The surface behaves differently from different approach directions.
  This asymmetry is what causes f_{xy} ≠ f_{yx} at (0,0).
```

## §8. Generalizations

### 8.1 Schwarz's Theorem (Full Generality)

The result is sometimes stated in a stronger form (due to H.A. Schwarz):

> **Theorem (Schwarz).** If $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ merely both **exist** and one of them is **continuous** at $(a,b)$, then they are equal at $(a,b)$.

Clairaut's version (both continuous) is the practically useful form. Schwarz's is the sharpest version.

### 8.2 Young's Theorem ($n$ Variables)

For $f: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ of class $C^k$, any two $k$-th order partial derivatives that use the same multiset of variables are equal.

Explicitly: if $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are permutations of the same index sequence, then $D^\alpha f = D^\beta f$.

### 8.3 Distribution Theory Perspective

In the theory of **distributions** (Sobolev spaces), partial derivatives commute automatically in the distributional sense — the Clairaut condition is always satisfied weakly. This is one reason distributional derivatives are so powerful in PDE theory and modern ML.

## §9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> Clairaut's theorem sits silently behind nearly every numerical optimization algorithm used in modern ML. Its presence is so pervasive it's almost invisible.

### 9.1 Hessian Matrices and Second-Order Optimization

The **Hessian** of a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the matrix of all second-order partial derivatives:

$$H_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i , \partial \theta_j}$$

**Clairaut's theorem says: the Hessian is symmetric**, i.e., $H = H^T$, whenever $\mathcal{L} \in C^2$.

This symmetry is **not cosmetic** — it has profound algorithmic consequences:

|Property of Symmetric $H$|Implication for Optimization|
|---|---|
|Real eigenvalues|Curvature is real-valued; well-defined notion of "concave/convex"|
|Orthogonal eigenvectors|Natural axes for the loss landscape|
|Spectral decomposition $H = Q\Lambda Q^T$|Newton's method, natural gradient are geometrically meaningful|
|Positive definite $\Rightarrow$ local min|The second derivative test works|

Without Clairaut — if $H$ were not symmetric — eigenvalues could be complex, and the geometry of the loss landscape would be fundamentally broken.

### 9.2 Newton's Method and Curvature

**Newton's method** for optimization uses the full Hessian: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - H^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

This requires computing and inverting $H$. The assumption that $H$ is symmetric (Clairaut) is what allows:

- **Cholesky decomposition**: $H = LL^T$ (only valid for symmetric positive definite matrices) — far cheaper than general $LU$
- **Eigendecomposition**: used in understanding saddle points, where $H$ has negative eigenvalues
- **Conjugate gradient solvers**: exploit symmetry for efficiency

**Quasi-Newton methods** (L-BFGS, BFGS) approximate $H^{-1}$ while explicitly maintaining symmetry in each update — a direct enforcement of Clairaut.

### 9.3 Backpropagation and Order of Differentiation

Backprop computes $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}$ — first-order derivatives. **Second-order backprop** (Hessian-vector products, used in Hessian-free optimization) requires computing: $$Hv = \frac{\partial}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}}(\nabla\mathcal{L} \cdot v)$$

This is a mixed partial derivative computation. Clairaut guarantees that it doesn't matter whether you differentiate $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ with respect to $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, or vice versa — the numerical result is the same. Automatic differentiation libraries (PyTorch, JAX) silently rely on this to compute Hessian-vector products efficiently via **forward-over-reverse** or **reverse-over-forward** autodiff — two different orderings of differentiation that give the same answer, by Clairaut.

### 9.4 Fisher Information Matrix and Natural Gradient

The **Fisher Information Matrix** in statistics/ML is: $$\mathcal{F}_{ij} = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \log p_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{x})}{\partial \theta_i} \frac{\partial \log p_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{x})}{\partial \theta_j}\right]$$

$\mathcal{F}$ is symmetric by construction (it's a covariance matrix), and equals the **negative expected Hessian** of the log-likelihood under regularity conditions — another application of Clairaut linking information geometry to second-order calculus.

**Natural gradient descent** (used in TRPO, PPO, and other RL algorithms) uses $\mathcal{F}^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}$ as the update direction. Its validity depends on the symmetric structure guaranteed by Clairaut.

### 9.5 Symmetry in Attention and Transformer Architectures

In **self-attention**, the attention score between query $q$ and key $k$ is: $$\text{score}(q,k) = \frac{q \cdot k}{\sqrt{d_k}}$$

This is symmetric in an informal sense. More concretely: when analyzing the **sensitivity** of transformer outputs to inputs, one computes second-order interactions (analogous to mixed partials of the output with respect to different input tokens). The symmetry of these interaction terms — a continuous analog of Clairaut — is what allows interpretability methods like **integrated gradients** and **SHAP** to attribute model behavior consistently.

### 9.6 Implicit Differentiation and Neural ODEs

**Neural ODEs** define the forward pass via an ODE: $$\frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = f_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{h}, t)$$

Training requires differentiating the loss with respect to $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, which involves the **adjoint method** — a form of implicit differentiation through the ODE. The correctness of the adjoint relies on the commutativity of differentiation in $t$ and $\boldsymbol{\theta}$, which is precisely Clairaut's theorem applied to the joint $(t, \boldsymbol{\theta})$ parameter space.

## §10. The Curl Test — Clairaut's Most Famous Application

For $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q\rangle$ on a simply connected domain, $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative $\Leftrightarrow$ $P_y = Q_x$.

**Why Clairaut?** If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y\rangle$, then: $$P_y = (f_x)_y = f_{xy} = f_{yx} = (f_y)_x = Q_x$$

The equality $f_{xy} = f_{yx}$ — which is Clairaut — is the **entire reason** the curl test works. Without Clairaut, there would be no reason to expect $P_y = Q_x$.

In $\mathbb{R}^3$: $\mathbf{F}$ conservative $\Rightarrow \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$. Explicitly:

$$\text{curl}(\mathbf{F}) = \left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z},; \frac{\partial P}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial x},; \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right) = \mathbf{0}$$

Each component vanishes by Clairaut applied to the corresponding potential $f$.

---

## §11. Summary

$$\boxed{f \in C^2(U) \implies f_{xy} = f_{yx} \text{ on } U}$$

|Item|Detail|
|---|---|
|**Hypothesis**|$f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ both exist and are continuous at the point|
|**Conclusion**|$f_{xy} = f_{yx}$ at that point|
|**Proof technique**|Double increment + MVT twice, then take limits using continuity|
|**Failure mode**|Continuity of mixed partials can fail; gives $f_{xy} \neq f_{yx}$ at origin|
|**Key application**|Curl test for conservative fields (FTLI); Hessian symmetry in ML|
|**Generalization**|$C^k$ functions: all $k$-th mixed partials with same variables are equal|

## §12. Personal Notes

- The proof is longer than Clairaut's theorem deserves to be, but it teaches you something: **the Mean Value Theorem is the fundamental tool in multivariable calculus**, not the definition of the derivative.
- The failure example is genuinely surprising the first time. A function can be differentiable everywhere, have both mixed partials everywhere, and still fail symmetry — because differentiability alone doesn't imply the partials are continuous.
- In practice, every function you encounter in ML is $C^\infty$ (neural networks with smooth activations like GELU, sigmoid, tanh), so Clairaut always holds. The Hessian is always symmetric. The issue in ML is not Clairaut failing — it's the Hessian being enormous ($n \times n$ with $n = 10^9$) and computationally inaccessible.
- There is something philosophically satisfying here: **commutativity of partial derivatives is a regularity statement**. The more regular (smooth) a function is, the more operations commute. This theme runs all the way through PDE theory, functional analysis, and the foundations of modern ML.
- **Remember the mnemonic for Leibniz notation:** $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y,\partial x}$ — the denominator reads right-to-left ($x$ first, then $y$). Get this backwards once on an exam and you'll never forget it.
