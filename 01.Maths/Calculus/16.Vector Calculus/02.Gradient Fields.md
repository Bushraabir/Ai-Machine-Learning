## 1. Motivation

We already know the derivative of $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ captures the _rate of change_ of $f$ at a point. In higher dimensions, $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, the question becomes richer:

> _"In which direction does $f$ increase most rapidly, and at what rate?"_

The gradient answers this — completely and canonically. It packages all partial derivatives into a single geometric object: a vector field that, at every point, aims uphill.

But there is a subtlety hiding here. The **gradient** is an operation on a scalar function. A **gradient field** is the resulting vector field $\nabla f$. Not every vector field is a gradient field. The question of _which ones are_ is one of the most beautiful problems in vector calculus, with deep topological content.

---

## 2. The Gradient of a Scalar Field

> **Definition.** Let $f : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be differentiable on the open set $U$. The **gradient** of $f$ at a point $\mathbf{x} \in U$ is the vector $$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(\mathbf{x}),; \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(\mathbf{x}),; \ldots,; \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(\mathbf{x})\right) \in \mathbb{R}^n$$

In $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$ respectively: $$\nabla f(x,y) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\mathbf{j}$$

$$\nabla f(x,y,z) = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x},\mathbf{i} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y},\mathbf{j} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial z},\mathbf{k}$$

The operator $\nabla$ (nabla or del) is the **vector differential operator**: $$\nabla = \left\langle \frac{\partial}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial}{\partial x_n} \right\rangle$$

> **Crucial point.** $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ is a _vector at a single point_. The **gradient field** $\nabla f : U \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is the function that assigns this vector to every point in $U$.

## 3. Gradient Fields — The Vector Field Perspective

> **Definition.** A vector field $\mathbf{F} : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is a **gradient field** (equivalently, a **conservative field**) if there exists a scalar function $f : U \to \mathbb{R}$ such that $$\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$$ The function $f$ is called the **potential function** (or **scalar potential**) for $\mathbf{F}$.

**Terminology across disciplines:**

|Physics|Mathematics|ML/Optimisation|
|---|---|---|
|Potential $\phi$|Potential function $f$|Loss / objective $\mathcal{L}$|
|Conservative force $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla\phi$|Gradient field $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$|Negative gradient $-\nabla\mathcal{L}$|
|Energy conservation|Path independence|Convergence guarantees|

> _Note the sign convention:_ in physics, forces are $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla\phi$ (particle moves toward lower potential energy). In optimisation, we descend: $-\nabla\mathcal{L}$. In pure mathematics we usually write $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ without the sign.

## 4. Geometric Anatomy

### 4.1 Level Sets and Orthogonality

The **level set** of $f$ at height $c$ is: $$L_c = {\mathbf{x} \in U : f(\mathbf{x}) = c}$$

In $\mathbb{R}^2$ these are **level curves**; in $\mathbb{R}^3$ they are **level surfaces**.

> **Theorem.** If $f$ is $C^1$ and $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0) \neq \mathbf{0}$, then $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ is **perpendicular** to the level set $L_{f(\mathbf{x}_0)}$ at $\mathbf{x}_0$.

**Proof sketch.** Let $\mathbf{r}(t)$ be any smooth curve on the level set $L_c$, so $f(\mathbf{r}(t)) = c$ for all $t$. Differentiating: $$\frac{d}{dt}f(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) = 0$$ So $\nabla f$ is perpendicular to every tangent vector of $L_c$ at $\mathbf{x}_0$, hence perpendicular to the level set. $\blacksquare$

**Visualisation for $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$:**

```
        ∇f points outward (radially)
        ↑
   _____|_____
  /  · ↗ · · \     ← level curve f = 4 (circle r=2)
 | · ↗   ↑ · |
 |↗    origin ↑|
 | · ↘   ↓ · |
  \  · ↘ · · /
   -----------
        ↓
  ∇f ⊥ level curves everywhere
  ∇f = ⟨2x, 2y⟩ — arrows point away from origin
```

### 4.2 Direction and Magnitude

> **Theorem.** Among all unit vectors $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$, the directional derivative $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f(\mathbf{x}_0) = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is:
> 
> - **maximised** when $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \dfrac{\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)}{|\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)|}$ (direction of gradient), with maximum value $|\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)|$
> - **minimised** (steepest descent) when $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = -\dfrac{\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)}{|\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)|}$, with minimum value $-|\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)|$
> - **zero** when $\hat{\mathbf{u}} \perp \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ (tangent to level set)

**Proof.** $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = \nabla f \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}} = |\nabla f||\hat{\mathbf{u}}|\cos\theta = |\nabla f|\cos\theta$. This is maximised at $\theta = 0$ (same direction) and minimised at $\theta = \pi$ (opposite direction). $\blacksquare$

> _The gradient is the compass of optimisation: at every point, it tells you exactly which direction to move to increase $f$ most rapidly._

## 5. Directional Derivatives — The Gradient as a Linear Form

The **total derivative** (Fréchet derivative) of $f$ at $\mathbf{x}_0$ is the unique linear map $Df(\mathbf{x}_0) : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ such that: $$f(\mathbf{x}_0 + \mathbf{h}) = f(\mathbf{x}_0) + Df(\mathbf{x}_0)\mathbf{h} + o(|\mathbf{h}|) \quad \text{as } \mathbf{h} \to \mathbf{0}$$

By the Riesz representation theorem on $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the standard inner product, every linear functional $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is represented by dot product with some fixed vector. That vector is precisely $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)$: $$Df(\mathbf{x}_0)\mathbf{h} = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0) \cdot \mathbf{h}$$

So: _the gradient is the vector that represents the total derivative as a dot product_. This is the coordinate-free definition — it explains why the gradient changes when you change the inner product (e.g., in Riemannian geometry).

> **Directional derivative** (formal definition): $$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f(\mathbf{x}_0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{x}_0 + h\hat{\mathbf{u}}) - f(\mathbf{x}_0)}{h} = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$$

## 6. Critical Points and the Second Derivative Test

> **Definition.** $\mathbf{x}_0$ is a **critical point** of $f$ if $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0) = \mathbf{0}$.

Critical points are where the gradient field vanishes — where the "force" is zero. They are candidates for local minima, local maxima, or saddle points.

### The Hessian

The **Hessian matrix** of $f$ at $\mathbf{x}_0$ is: $$H_f(\mathbf{x}_0) = \left[\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i \partial x_j}(\mathbf{x}_0)\right]_{i,j=1}^n$$

For $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$: $$H_f = \begin{pmatrix} f_{xx} & f_{xy} \ f_{xy} & f_{yy} \end{pmatrix}$$

(Symmetric by Clairaut's theorem if $f \in C^2$.)

> **Second Derivative Test.** At a critical point $\mathbf{x}_0$ with $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0) = \mathbf{0}$:
> 
> - $H_f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ **positive definite** $\implies$ local minimum
> - $H_f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ **negative definite** $\implies$ local maximum
> - $H_f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ **indefinite** $\implies$ saddle point
> - $H_f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ **singular** $\implies$ test inconclusive

In $\mathbb{R}^2$, let $D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - f_{xy}^2 = \det H_f$:

|$D$|$f_{xx}$|Type|
|---|---|---|
|$D > 0$|$> 0$|Local minimum|
|$D > 0$|$< 0$|Local maximum|
|$D < 0$|—|Saddle point|
|$D = 0$|—|Inconclusive|

> **Example.** $f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2$ at $(0,0)$: $\nabla f = \langle 2x, -2y\rangle = \mathbf{0}$ at origin. ✓ Critical point. $H_f = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 0 \ 0 & -2\end{pmatrix}$, $D = -4 < 0$ → **saddle point**. The gradient field flows outward along $x$-axis and inward along $y$-axis.

## 7. Conservative Fields and Potential Functions

### Finding the Potential Function

Given $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$ known to be conservative, recover $f$ such that $\nabla f = \mathbf{F}$:

**Method (integration and matching):**

1. Integrate $P = \partial f/\partial x$ with respect to $x$: $$f(x,y,z) = \int P,dx + g(y,z)$$
2. Differentiate with respect to $y$, set equal to $Q$, solve for $\partial g/\partial y$: $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = (\text{known}) + \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} = Q \implies \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} = Q - (\text{known})$$
3. Integrate to get $g(y,z) = \int(\ldots),dy + h(z)$.
4. Differentiate with respect to $z$, set equal to $R$, solve for $h'(z)$, integrate.

> **Worked example.** $\mathbf{F} = \langle yz,; xz,; xy\rangle$.
> 
> **Step 1.** $f = \int yz,dx = xyz + g(y,z)$.
> 
> **Step 2.** $\partial f/\partial y = xz + \partial g/\partial y = xz \implies \partial g/\partial y = 0 \implies g = h(z)$.
> 
> **Step 3.** $\partial f/\partial z = xy + h'(z) = xy \implies h'(z) = 0 \implies h = C$.
> 
> **Potential:** $f(x,y,z) = xyz + C$. ✓ Check: $\nabla(xyz) = \langle yz, xz, xy\rangle$. ✓

### Uniqueness

> **Proposition.** If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f = \nabla g$ on a connected domain $U$, then $f - g = C$ (constant on $U$).

**Proof.** $\nabla(f-g) = \mathbf{0}$ everywhere on $U$. On a connected open set, a function with zero gradient is constant. $\blacksquare$

So the potential is **unique up to an additive constant** — exactly like indefinite integrals in 1D.

### The Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals

> **Theorem.** Let $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ on an open set $U$ containing a smooth curve $C$ from point $A$ to point $B$. Then: $$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(B) - f(A)$$

**Proof.** Let $\mathbf{r}(t)$, $t \in [a,b]$, parametrise $C$ with $\mathbf{r}(a) = A$, $\mathbf{r}(b) = B$. Then: $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t),dt = \int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}[f(\mathbf{r}(t))],dt = f(\mathbf{r}(b)) - f(\mathbf{r}(a)) = f(B) - f(A)$$ by the chain rule and the 1D Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. $\blacksquare$

This is the _n_-dimensional generalisation of $\int_a^b f'(x),dx = f(b) - f(a)$.

## 8. When is a Vector Field a Gradient Field?

This is the central question of the theory. The answer has a beautiful topological flavour.

### Necessary Condition — Zero Curl

> **Theorem.** If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ and $f \in C^2$, then $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$.

**Proof.** In $\mathbb{R}^2$, the scalar curl is $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y$. If $P = f_x, Q = f_y$: $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = f_{yx} - f_{xy} = 0$$ by Clairaut's theorem (equality of mixed partials for $C^2$ functions). In $\mathbb{R}^3$ all three components of $\nabla \times \nabla f$ vanish by the same argument. $\blacksquare$

### Sufficient Condition — Simply Connected Domains

> **Theorem.** Let $\mathbf{F}$ be a $C^1$ vector field on a **simply connected** open domain $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$. Then: $$\mathbf{F} = \nabla f \text{ for some } f \iff \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0} \text{ on } U$$

**Simply connected** means $U$ has no holes — every closed loop in $U$ can be continuously shrunk to a point within $U$.

> **The topological subtlety.** On a domain with holes, zero curl does NOT imply conservative.
> 
> **Standard counterexample.** Let $U = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {(0,0)}$ and: $$\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \left\langle \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2},; \frac{x}{x^2+y^2} \right\rangle$$
> 
> **Check curl:** $\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y}$. One computes this is $= 0$ on all of $U$.
> 
> **But:** integrate around the unit circle $C: \mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t, \sin t)$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$: $$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi}\left(\frac{-\sin t}{\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t}\cdot(-\sin t) + \frac{\cos t}{\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t}\cdot\cos t\right)dt = \int_0^{2\pi}1,dt = 2\pi \neq 0$$
> 
> Non-zero line integral around a closed loop $\implies$ **not conservative**, despite zero curl. The hole at the origin makes $U$ not simply connected, and the field "detects" the hole via its winding number.
> 
> (This $\mathbf{F}$ is $\nabla\theta$ where $\theta = \arctan(y/x)$ is the polar angle — but $\theta$ is not well-defined as a single-valued function on all of $U$.)

**The full picture** (first de Rham cohomology): The obstruction to a curl-free field being a gradient is measured by $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U)$. For simply connected $U$, $H^1 = 0$ and curl-free $\implies$ exact. For $U = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {0}$, $H^1 \cong \mathbb{R}$ and there is one independent non-exact closed form — the one in the counterexample.

## 9. The Gradient in Curvilinear Coordinates

In coordinates other than Cartesian, the gradient takes a different form. This matters for numerical methods (especially on meshes) and physics.

**Polar coordinates** $(r, \theta)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$: $$\nabla f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$$

**Spherical coordinates** $(r, \theta, \phi)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$: $$\nabla f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial r}\hat{\mathbf{r}} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial f}{\partial \phi}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$$

**General orthogonal curvilinear coordinates** $(u_1, u_2, u_3)$ with scale factors $h_i = |\partial\mathbf{r}/\partial u_i|$: $$\nabla f = \sum_{i=1}^3 \frac{1}{h_i}\frac{\partial f}{\partial u_i}\hat{\mathbf{e}}_i$$

> _In Riemannian geometry with metric tensor $g_{ij}$, the gradient is the vector field dual to $df$ via $g$: $(\nabla f)^i = g^{ij}\partial_j f$. The Cartesian case is just $g_{ij} = \delta_{ij}$._

## 10. Worked Examples

### Example A — Full analysis of $f(x,y) = x^2 y - y^3/3$

**Gradient field:** $$\nabla f = \langle 2xy,; x^2 - y^2 \rangle$$

**Critical points:** Set $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$: $$2xy = 0 \implies x = 0 \text{ or } y = 0$$

- If $x = 0$: $x^2 - y^2 = -y^2 = 0 \implies y = 0$. Point: $(0,0)$.
- If $y = 0$: $x^2 - 0 = x^2 = 0 \implies x = 0$. Same point.

So the only critical point is $(0,0)$.

**Hessian:** $$H_f = \begin{pmatrix} 2y & 2x \ 2x & -2y \end{pmatrix}, \quad H_f(0,0) = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 0 \ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$$

$D = 0$: test inconclusive. Direct inspection: $f(t,0) = 0$ for all $t$, while $f(0,t) = -t^3/3$ changes sign. So $(0,0)$ is **neither a local min nor max** — it is a degenerate saddle.

---

### Example B — Verify conservatism and find potential

$\mathbf{F} = \langle 2xe^y + 1,; x^2 e^y - \sin y\rangle$

**Curl check:** $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 2xe^y - 2xe^y = 0 \checkmark$$

**Find $f$:** $$f = \int (2xe^y + 1),dx = x^2 e^y + x + g(y)$$ $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2 e^y + g'(y) = x^2 e^y - \sin y \implies g'(y) = -\sin y \implies g(y) = \cos y + C$$

$$\boxed{f(x,y) = x^2 e^y + x + \cos y + C}$$

**Line integral shortcut:** Compute $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ from $(0,0)$ to $(1, \pi)$ along _any_ path: $$f(1,\pi) - f(0,0) = (e^\pi + 1 + \cos\pi) - (0 + 0 + \cos 0) = e^\pi + 1 - 1 - 1 = e^\pi - 1$$

## 11. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 11.1 Gradient Descent — Following the Gradient Field

Every training algorithm in deep learning follows the gradient field of the loss. Gradient descent is: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta,\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

This is **discrete-time integration along the trajectory of the vector field $-\nabla\mathcal{L}$**. The continuous-time limit is the **gradient flow ODE**: $$\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(t) = -\nabla\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t))$$

This ODE drives $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ toward critical points of $\mathcal{L}$. Because $\mathcal{L}$ is a potential function, the flow is conservative — it always descends $\mathcal{L}$ and cannot orbit indefinitely.

> **Fact.** Along any gradient flow trajectory, $\dfrac{d}{dt}\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)) = \nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = -|\nabla\mathcal{L}|^2 \leq 0$. The loss is non-increasing. Equality iff $\nabla\mathcal{L} = 0$.

This is the **energy dissipation** property, directly from the theory of conservative fields.

### 11.2 The Loss Landscape as a Scalar Field

The loss $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ for a neural network with $n$ parameters lives in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Its geometry:

|Feature of $\mathcal{L}$|Vector field language|
|---|---|
|Local minimum|$\nabla\mathcal{L} = \mathbf{0}$, $H_\mathcal{L}$ positive definite|
|Saddle point|$\nabla\mathcal{L} = \mathbf{0}$, $H_\mathcal{L}$ indefinite|
|Flat region|$\nabla\mathcal{L} \approx \mathbf{0}$, slow gradient flow|
|Sharp minimum|Large curvature (large eigenvalues of $H_\mathcal{L}$)|
|Wide minimum|Small curvature (small eigenvalues) — generalises better|

### 11.3 Variants of Gradient Descent — Preconditioned Gradient Fields

Modern optimisers do not follow $-\nabla\mathcal{L}$ directly; they follow a **preconditioned** version:

$$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta, M_t^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

where $M_t$ is a preconditioning matrix. This changes the _effective_ vector field being followed.

|Optimiser|Preconditioner $M_t$|
|---|---|
|SGD|$I$ (identity — plain gradient field)|
|Momentum SGD|Exponential moving average of $\nabla\mathcal{L}$|
|AdaGrad|$\text{diag}(\sum_\tau \nabla\mathcal{L}_\tau \odot \nabla\mathcal{L}_\tau)$|
|RMSProp|$\text{diag}(\mathbb{E}[\nabla^2])$ — running average|
|Adam|$\text{diag}(\hat{m}/\sqrt{\hat{v}})$ — bias-corrected moments|
|Natural Gradient|Fisher information matrix $\mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$|

> _Adam does not follow the gradient field of any potential. It is not a conservative vector field. This has deep implications: Adam can get stuck in places gradient descent would not, and vice versa._

### 11.4 Natural Gradient — The Riemannian Gradient

In information geometry, the parameter space of a probabilistic model carries a natural Riemannian metric — the **Fisher information metric**: $$g_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{F}_{ij}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial\log p_\theta}{\partial\theta_i}\frac{\partial\log p_\theta}{\partial\theta_j}\right]$$

The **natural gradient** is the Riemannian gradient of $\mathcal{L}$: $$\tilde{\nabla}\mathcal{L} = \mathcal{F}^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}$$

This is the correct gradient in the intrinsic geometry of the model manifold — it accounts for the fact that equal Euclidean steps in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$-space correspond to very unequal changes in the distribution $p_\theta$. K-FAC and Shampoo are practical approximations.

### 11.5 Gradient Fields in Generative Models

**Score matching.** The **score function** of a distribution $p(\mathbf{x})$ is: $$\mathbf{s}(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p(\mathbf{x})$$

This is a gradient field over _data space_ $\mathbb{R}^d$. It is the gradient of $\log p$, so it is **conservative by construction** — it has $\log p$ as its potential.

**Denoising diffusion models** (DDPM, Score SDE) learn an approximation $\mathbf{s}_\theta(\mathbf{x}, t) \approx \nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p_t(\mathbf{x})$ at each noise level $t$. Sampling is done via **Langevin dynamics**: $$\mathbf{x}_{k+1} = \mathbf{x}_k + \epsilon,\mathbf{s}_\theta(\mathbf{x}_k) + \sqrt{2\epsilon},\boldsymbol{\xi}_k, \qquad \boldsymbol{\xi}_k \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, I)$$

The deterministic part is gradient ascent on $\log p$ (moving toward higher probability). The noise term prevents collapse to a mode.

> _Every image Stable Diffusion generates is obtained by integrating along a learned gradient field._

### 11.6 Energy-Based Models — Explicit Potential Functions

An **energy-based model** (EBM) directly parametrises a potential $E_\theta(\mathbf{x})$ and defines: $$p_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{e^{-E_\theta(\mathbf{x})}}{Z_\theta}, \qquad Z_\theta = \int e^{-E_\theta(\mathbf{x})},d\mathbf{x}$$

The gradient field is: $$\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p_\theta = -\nabla_\mathbf{x} E_\theta$$

Inference dynamics follow $-\nabla E_\theta$: particles roll downhill on the energy landscape to the most probable configurations. **Hopfield networks** are a classical special case with a quadratic energy.

### 11.7 Backpropagation is Gradient Computation

**Backpropagation** is the algorithm for computing $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{L}$ efficiently via the chain rule. For a network $\mathbf{y} = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{x})$ with loss $\ell(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{y}^*)$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_l} = \frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \mathbf{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_L} \cdots \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{l+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_l} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_l}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_l}$$

Each factor is a Jacobian. The full backprop is computing one row of the gradient field of $\mathcal{L}$ per parameter layer, propagated backwards. The gradient field is the _reason_ backprop exists — you need the gradient to descend.

### 11.8 Second-Order Methods — Using the Hessian

**Newton's method** uses the Hessian of the loss: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - H_\mathcal{L}^{-1}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t),\nabla\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

This is not following the gradient field of $\mathcal{L}$, but the gradient field of the **quadratic approximation** to $\mathcal{L}$ at $\boldsymbol{\theta}_t$. At a saddle point, Newton's method can move toward the saddle (it just finds critical points, not minima). Saddle-free Newton and trust-region methods fix this by using $|H|$ instead.
