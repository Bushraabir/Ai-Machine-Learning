

> _"The extended Green's theorem is where topology enters calculus. The shape of the domain — its holes, its connectivity — directly controls what integrals can and cannot vanish. This is not analysis anymore. This is geometry."_

---

## §1. Motivation — What "Extended" Means and Why It's Necessary

The standard Green's theorem assumes $D$ is **simply connected** — no holes. In that setting, $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ throughout $D$ guarantees $\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for every closed curve.

But the real world — and real mathematics — is full of domains with holes:

- The plane minus a point: $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {0}$
- An annular region: ${(x,y): 1 \leq x^2+y^2 \leq 4}$
- A region with multiple punctures

On such domains, shocking things happen. A field can satisfy $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ **everywhere** in the domain and yet produce **nonzero** circulation around certain loops. The standard theorem gives $0 = 0$, useless. The extended version tells you exactly what those nonzero values are, how to compute them, and why the topology of the domain is the controlling factor.

The extended Green's theorem is the bridge between **analysis** (differential conditions on $P$ and $Q$) and **topology** (the shape and connectivity of the domain).

---

## §2. Review — Simply Connected Domains

**Definition.** An open connected set $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ is **simply connected** if every simple closed curve in $U$ can be continuously contracted to a point while staying in $U$.

Intuitively: $U$ has **no holes**.

```
Simply Connected:          NOT Simply Connected:
  ╭──────────╮               ╭──────────╮
  │          │               │  ╭────╮  │
  │    U     │               │  │hole│  │
  │          │               │  ╰────╯  │
  ╰──────────╯               ╰──────────╯
  Any loop shrinks           The loop around the hole
  to a point in U.           CANNOT shrink — it's blocked.
```

**Standard Green's Theorem** (simply connected version): $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \text{ on simply connected } U \implies \oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0 \text{ for all closed } C \subset U$$

**This fails on non-simply connected domains.** The extended theorem repairs this failure.

---

## §3. The Canonical Failure Example

Before stating the extended theorem, we must understand the failure deeply.

**Define on $U = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {(0,0)}$:**

$$\mathbf{F} = \left\langle \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2},; \frac{x}{x^2+y^2} \right\rangle = \langle P, Q\rangle$$

**Step 1 — Check the curl condition:**

$$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{(x^2+y^2) - x \cdot 2x}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{y^2 - x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}$$

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{-(x^2+y^2) + y \cdot 2y}{(x^2+y^2)^2} = \frac{y^2 - x^2}{(x^2+y^2)^2}$$

So $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ on all of $U = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {0}$. The curl is zero everywhere on the domain.

**Step 2 — Compute the circulation around the unit circle $C_1: x^2+y^2=1$ (CCW):**

Parameterize: $x = \cos t$, $y = \sin t$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$.

$$\oint_{C_1} P,dx + Q,dy = \int_0^{2\pi}\left(\frac{-\sin t}{1}(-\sin t) + \frac{\cos t}{1}(\cos t)\right)dt = \int_0^{2\pi} 1,dt = 2\pi \neq 0$$

**Conclusion:** The curl condition $Q_x = P_y$ holds everywhere on $U$, but $\oint_{C_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi \neq 0$. Standard Green's theorem cannot be applied — the origin (where $\mathbf{F}$ is undefined) lies inside $C_1$, and the region enclosed is **not** in $U$.

The number $2\pi$ is not arbitrary. It is a **topological invariant** — it counts how many times $C_1$ winds around the missing point. This winding number is the heart of the extended theory.

## §4. Green's Theorem for Multiply Connected Regions

### 4.1 Setup

Let $D$ be a **multiply connected region**: a bounded domain whose boundary consists of:

- **One outer boundary** $C_0$ (traversed **counterclockwise**)
- **$n$ inner boundaries** $C_1, C_2, \ldots, C_n$ (the boundaries of the holes, each traversed **clockwise**)

The **positively oriented boundary** is: $$\partial D = C_0 \cup C_1^- \cup C_2^- \cup \cdots \cup C_n^-$$

where $C_k^-$ denotes $C_k$ with reversed (clockwise) orientation.

```
        C_0  (CCW)
   ↗─────────────────↖
  ╱                   ╲
 │    ↙──────↗   ↙──────↗  │
 │   │  C_1  │  │  C_2  │  │   (holes, CW)
 │   │ (hole)│  │ (hole)│  │
 │    ↘──────↙   ↘──────↙  │
  ╲                   ╱
   ↘─────────────────↙

D = shaded region (between outer and inner boundaries)
```

### 4.2 The Extended Theorem

> **Theorem (Green's Theorem — Multiply Connected Regions).** Let $D$ be a multiply connected region with positively oriented boundary $\partial D = C_0 \cup C_1^- \cup \cdots \cup C_n^-$. If $P, Q \in C^1$ on an open set containing $\overline{D}$, then:
> 
> $$\iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA = \oint_{C_0} P,dx + Q,dy - \sum_{k=1}^n \oint_{C_k} P,dx + Q,dy$$

Equivalently, if $\partial D$ denotes the **full positively oriented boundary** (outer CCW, holes CW): $$\oint_{\partial D} P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA$$

The theorem statement is **identical** to the standard version — the extension is in understanding what $\partial D$ means for domains with holes.

### 4.3 Proof Strategy

**Cut the domain into simply connected pieces** by introducing line segments ("cuts") connecting the outer boundary to each inner boundary:

```
        C_0
   ↗─────────────────↖
  ╱                   ╲
 │    ┌──────┐          │
 │    │  C_1 │ ←cut→   │
 │    │      │          │
 │    └──────┘          │
  ╲                   ╱
   ↘─────────────────↙
```

Each cut creates two simply connected subregions $D_1, D_2$. Apply standard Green's theorem to each. The **cut edges are traversed twice in opposite directions** and cancel. What remains is the original boundary integral over $\partial D$. $\square$

## §5. The Curl-Zero Case — Topology Controls Everything

**The most important special case:** Suppose $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ throughout a multiply connected domain $U$ with $n$ holes. What can we say?

$$\iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA = 0$$

So the extended Green's theorem gives:

$$\oint_{C_0} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \sum_{k=1}^n \oint_{C_k} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

**The circulation around the outer boundary equals the sum of circulations around each hole.** This is a stunning result:

> When curl $= 0$, the circulation around any closed curve depends only on which holes it encloses, not on its specific shape.

### 5.1 Independence of Path Revisited

**Theorem.** Let $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ on $U$ (possibly multiply connected). Let $C_1, C_2$ be two curves from $A$ to $B$ in $U$ that **enclose no holes between them**. Then:

$$\int_{C_1} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{C_2} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

If $C_1$ and $C_2$ **do enclose a hole** between them, the integrals may differ — by exactly the circulation around that hole.

```
  A ─── C_1 ────── B          A ──── C_1 ────── B
   ╲               ╱            ╲   ╭────╮      ╱
    ╲─── C_2 ─────╱              ╲──│hole│──C_2╱
                                    ╰────╯
  No hole enclosed:              Hole enclosed:
  ∫_{C_1} = ∫_{C_2}             ∫_{C_1} ≠ ∫_{C_2} in general
```

### 5.2 The Circulation Integers $a_k$

For $\mathbf{F}$ with $Q_x = P_y$ on $U$ having holes with boundary curves $\gamma_1, \ldots, \gamma_n$, define:

$$a_k = \oint_{\gamma_k} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}, \qquad k = 1, \ldots, n$$

These are the **periods** of the field $\mathbf{F}$ (or of its potential). They are **topological invariants** of $\mathbf{F}$ on $U$.

For any closed curve $C$ in $U$: $$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \sum_{k=1}^n n_k \cdot a_k$$

where $n_k \in \mathbb{Z}$ is the **winding number** of $C$ around hole $k$.

**For the canonical example** $\mathbf{F} = \left\langle\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}, \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right\rangle$ on $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus{0}$:

There is one hole (at the origin), and $a_1 = 2\pi$.

For any closed curve $C$ in $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus{0}$: $$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi \cdot n(C, 0)$$

where $n(C,0) \in \mathbb{Z}$ is the **winding number** of $C$ around the origin.

## §6. Winding Numbers

**Definition.** The **winding number** $n(C, p)$ of a closed curve $C$ around a point $p \notin C$ is the number of times $C$ winds counterclockwise around $p$ (negative if clockwise).

**Formula:** $$n(C, p) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \oint_C \frac{-(y-p_y),dx + (x-p_x),dy}{(x-p_x)^2 + (y-p_y)^2}$$

This is exactly $\frac{1}{2\pi}\oint_C \mathbf{F}_{p} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F}_p$ is the canonical example centered at $p$.

### Examples of Winding Numbers

```
  n = +1          n = +2          n = -1          n = 0
  
   ──→──           ──→──           ──←──           ──→──
  ╱  ·  ╲        ╱  ·  ╲        ╱  ·  ╲         ──→──  ·
  ╲      ╱       │ ╭────╮│       ╲      ╱
   ───←──         ╰─→───╯╰        ───→──
   
  Simple CCW    Winds twice    Clockwise       Point outside
  loop: n=+1    around: n=+2   loop: n=-1      curve: n=0
```

**Key properties:**

- $n(C,p)$ is an integer for all closed curves $C$ and points $p \notin C$
- $n(C,p)$ is constant on connected components of $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus C$
- $n(C,p) = 0$ for $p$ in the unbounded component

## §7. De Rham Cohomology — The Algebraic Structure

The extended Green's theorem is the gateway to **de Rham cohomology**, which is the systematic study of when closed differential forms are exact.

### 7.1 Differential Forms in the Plane

A **1-form** on $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ is an expression $\omega = P,dx + Q,dy$.

- $\omega$ is **closed** if $d\omega = 0$, i.e., $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 0$
- $\omega$ is **exact** if $\omega = df$ for some function $f$, i.e., $P = f_x$, $Q = f_y$

**Exact $\Rightarrow$ Closed** (always, by Clairaut's theorem): $$\omega = df \implies d\omega = d(df) = 0$$

**Closed $\Rightarrow$ Exact** (only on simply connected domains).

### 7.2 The de Rham Cohomology Group $H^1(U)$

**Define:** $$Z^1(U) = {\text{closed 1-forms on } U} = \ker(d: \Omega^1 \to \Omega^2)$$ $$B^1(U) = {\text{exact 1-forms on } U} = \text{im}(d: \Omega^0 \to \Omega^1)$$

Since exact $\Rightarrow$ closed, $B^1(U) \subseteq Z^1(U)$.

**Definition:** $$H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) = \frac{Z^1(U)}{B^1(U)} = \frac{{\text{closed forms}}}{{\text{exact forms}}}$$

This quotient measures **the obstruction to closedness implying exactness**.

**Key theorem:** $$H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}^n \text{ if } U \text{ has } n \text{ holes}$$

For $U = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {0}$: $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}$, generated by $\omega = \frac{-y,dx + x,dy}{x^2+y^2}$.

For $U = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {p_1, \ldots, p_n}$: $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}^n$, with one generator $\omega_k$ per hole.

**Interpretation:** The periods $a_k = \oint_{\gamma_k} \omega$ are the **coordinates** of $[\omega]$ in $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}^n$. Green's theorem (extended) is the statement that $\oint_C \omega$ depends only on the cohomology class $[\omega]$ and the homology class $[C]$.

### 7.3 The Period Map and Exactness

**Theorem.** Let $\omega$ be a closed 1-form on $U$ with $n$ holes ($\gamma_1, \ldots, \gamma_n$ bounding the holes). Then:

$$\omega \text{ is exact on } U \iff a_k = \oint_{\gamma_k} \omega = 0 \text{ for all } k = 1, \ldots, n$$

This is the complete characterization. A curl-free field is conservative **if and only if** all its periods vanish. Each hole contributes one independent obstruction.

## §8. The Extended Flux-Divergence Form

The flux (divergence) form of Green's theorem also extends directly.

**Setup:** $D$ multiply connected as before; $\mathbf{n}$ the outward unit normal to $\partial D$ (outward from $D$ — away from $D$ on all boundary components).

> **Theorem (Extended Flux-Divergence).** Under the same hypotheses: $$\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n},ds = \iint_D \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F},dA$$

where $\partial D$ includes **all** boundary components with the correct outward orientation:

- On $C_0$: outward = away from $D$ = outward in the usual sense
- On inner boundaries $C_k$: outward from $D$ = **inward** (into the hole)

**Special case — divergence zero:**

If $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 0$ on $D$, then: $$\oint_{C_0} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n},ds = \sum_{k=1}^n \oint_{C_k} \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n},ds$$

The flux through the outer boundary equals the sum of fluxes through inner boundaries. This is the $2D$ version of **Gauss's law**: net flux through a closed surface depends only on the enclosed sources.

## §9. Worked Examples

### Example A — Annular Region, Curl-Zero Field

**Problem.** Let $\mathbf{F} = \left\langle\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}, \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right\rangle$. Let $D$ be the annulus $1 \leq x^2+y^2 \leq 9$. Compute $\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$.

**$\partial D$:** Outer circle $C_0$ (radius 3, CCW) and inner circle $C_1$ (radius 1, **CW**).

Since $Q_x - P_y = 0$ on $D$ (the singularity at the origin is not in $D$): $$\iint_D(Q_x - P_y),dA = 0$$

Extended Green's theorem: $$0 = \oint_{C_0}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} - \oint_{C_1}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

Direct computation (same as the canonical example): $$\oint_{C_0}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi, \quad \oint_{C_1}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi$$

$$2\pi - 2\pi = 0 \checkmark$$

The circulation is the same on any curve enclosing the origin once, regardless of radius. This is path-independence in action — constrained by topology.

---

### Example B — Two Holes, Different Periods

**Domain:** $U = \mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {(0,0), (3,0)}$.

**Fields:** $\omega_1 = \frac{-y,dx + x,dy}{x^2+y^2}$ (period $2\pi$ around origin, $0$ around $(3,0)$)

$\omega_2 = \frac{-(y),dx + (x-3),dy}{(x-3)^2+y^2}$ (period $0$ around origin, $2\pi$ around $(3,0)$)

**General closed form on $U$:** $\omega = \alpha,\omega_1 + \beta,\omega_2$ for $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$.

For any closed curve $C$ with winding numbers $n_1$ around origin and $n_2$ around $(3,0)$: $$\oint_C \omega = 2\pi(\alpha n_1 + \beta n_2)$$

**Specific curve $C$** enclosing both holes once (CCW): $$\oint_C \omega = 2\pi(\alpha + \beta)$$

**This is exactly $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}^2$ at work:** two generators, two periods, full control over all circulations.

---

### Example C — When a Curve Avoids a Hole

**Field:** $\mathbf{F} = \left\langle\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}, \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right\rangle$ on $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus{0}$.

**Curve $C$:** An ellipse $\frac{(x-5)^2}{4} + y^2 = 1$ (centered at $(5,0)$, not enclosing the origin).

Winding number of $C$ around origin: $n(C, 0) = 0$.

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi \cdot 0 = 0$$

Even though $\mathbf{F}$ is defined everywhere on $C$ and has nonzero curl computation might suggest otherwise, the topology (winding number $= 0$) guarantees zero circulation. **No computation needed.**

---

### Example D — Nonzero Divergence with a Hole

**Field:** $\mathbf{F} = \langle x, y\rangle$, $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 2$.

**Domain:** Annulus $D: 1 \leq x^2+y^2 \leq 4$. Area $= \pi(4-1) = 3\pi$.

Extended flux-divergence form: $$\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n},ds = \iint_D 2,dA = 6\pi$$

**Check directly:**

- Outer circle $C_0$ (radius 2): $\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{r} = 2$, length $= 4\pi$. Flux $= 8\pi$.
- Inner circle $C_1$ (radius 1, **inward normal** for outward-from-$D$ convention): $\mathbf{F}\cdot(-\hat{r}) = -1$, length $= 2\pi$. Flux $= -2\pi$.

$$8\pi + (-2\pi) = 6\pi \checkmark$$

## §11. The Full Equivalence Theorem (Multiply Connected Version)

For simply connected domains, we had the clean equivalence: conservative $\Leftrightarrow$ curl-free $\Leftrightarrow$ path-independent $\Leftrightarrow$ all closed integrals zero.

On multiply connected domains, this breaks apart:

|Condition|Simply Connected $U$|Multiply Connected $U$|
|---|---|---|
|$\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ (exact)|$\Leftrightarrow$ curl-free|$\Rightarrow$ curl-free, but **NOT** $\Leftarrow$|
|Curl-free|$\Rightarrow$ conservative|$\Rightarrow$ locally conservative (near each point), **NOT** globally|
|$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for all $C$|$\Leftrightarrow$ curl-free|$\Leftrightarrow$ **exact** (stronger than curl-free)|
|Path-independent|$\Leftrightarrow$ conservative|$\Leftrightarrow$ exact (all periods zero)|

**The refined equivalence (any domain):**

$$\mathbf{F} \text{ conservative (exact)} \iff \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0 \text{ for ALL closed curves } C \text{ in } U$$

$$\mathbf{F} \text{ curl-free (closed)} \iff \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0 \text{ for ALL curves } C \text{ contractible in } U$$

On simply connected domains, every closed curve is contractible, so the two conditions coincide.

## §12. Applications and Consequences

### 12.1 Recovering the Potential Function (Multiply Connected Case)

If $\mathbf{F}$ is curl-free on $U$ (possibly multiply connected) with one hole, and you want a potential, you can define:

$$f(x,y) = \int_{(x_0,y_0)}^{(x,y)} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

**Problem:** If $U$ has a hole, this may not be single-valued — going around the hole contributes $a_1 = \oint_{\gamma_1}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ each time. The potential $f$ is **multi-valued**, like $\arg(z)$ in complex analysis.

**Solution:** Cut the domain — remove a ray from the hole to infinity — to make $U$ simply connected. On the cut domain $U^*$, $f$ is single-valued, but it has a **branch cut** (a jump discontinuity across the cut equal to $a_1$).

For $\mathbf{F} = \left\langle\frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}, \frac{x}{x^2+y^2}\right\rangle$ on $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus{0}$: the potential is $f = \theta = \arctan(y/x)$, the angle function. It is multi-valued (jumps by $2\pi$ crossing the branch cut). This is **the** fundamental example of a multi-valued potential.

### 12.2 Complex Analysis — The Residue Theorem

In complex analysis, for $f: \mathbb{C}\setminus{z_1,\ldots,z_n} \to \mathbb{C}$ holomorphic:

$$\oint_C f(z),dz = 2\pi i \sum_k n(C, z_k)\cdot\text{Res}(f, z_k)$$

This is the **Residue Theorem** — the complex-analytic version of the extended Green's theorem. The **residue** $\text{Res}(f, z_k)$ is the period (up to $2\pi i$) of $f,dz$ around the $k$-th singularity. The winding number $n(C, z_k)$ is exactly the topological invariant we defined.

The Residue Theorem is one of the most powerful results in all of mathematics, and it is the direct complex-variable cousin of everything in this note.

### 12.3 Electrostatics — Gauss's Law

An infinite wire with charge density $\lambda$ along the $z$-axis produces an electric field in the $xy$-plane: $$\mathbf{E} = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0}\cdot\frac{1}{x^2+y^2}\langle x, y\rangle$$

$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$ away from the wire (no charge there). Extended Green's theorem (flux form): $$\oint_C \mathbf{E}\cdot\mathbf{n},ds = \frac{\lambda}{2\pi\varepsilon_0}\cdot 2\pi = \frac{\lambda}{\varepsilon_0}$$

for any curve $C$ enclosing the wire once. This is **Gauss's law** — the flux through any closed curve around the wire gives the enclosed charge. The topology (does the curve enclose the wire?) determines the answer, not the shape of the curve.

## §13. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> The extended Green's theorem encodes the idea that **topology matters** — the shape of a space changes what is globally possible, even when local conditions look identical. This principle has entered machine learning in increasingly profound ways.

### 13.1 Topological Data Analysis (TDA) — Homology and Holes

**Persistent homology** and TDA quantify the "holes" in data by computing exactly the objects the extended Green's theorem is built on:

- **$H_0$**: connected components (zeroth homology)
- **$H_1$**: loops/cycles that cannot be filled (first homology — the holes of the extended theorem)
- **$H_2$**: voids, cavities

The **first Betti number** $\beta_1 = \dim H_1$ counts exactly the number of independent holes in a space — the same number $n$ that appears in $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}^n$.

**Applications in ML:**

- **Neuroscience**: Detecting ring-like attractor structures in recurrent neural network dynamics — neural "loops" correspond to $H_1$ generators
- **Drug discovery (Schrödinger, Insilico Medicine)**: Molecular topology — rings in molecules are $H_1$ features; TDA identifies pharmacophore shapes
- **Latent space analysis**: VAE and diffusion model latent spaces analyzed via TDA to detect topological features; a "hole" in latent space corresponds to a region of data space that is not covered

### 13.2 Non-Simply Connected Optimization Landscapes

The loss landscape $\mathcal{L}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ of a neural network may have **topologically nontrivial sublevel sets** ${\boldsymbol{\theta}: \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \leq c}$.

When these sublevel sets have holes (nontrivial $H_1$), gradient flows on them can exhibit behavior directly analogous to the extended Green's theorem:

- A gradient-like vector field can be curl-free (satisfy local conditions for descent) but fail to be globally conservative
- Optimization paths can "wind around" topological features without converging

This is observed in **loss landscape visualization** studies (Li et al. 2018, Draxler et al. 2018) where different minima are connected by paths of approximately equal loss — these paths form the **1-cycles** of the landscape's topology.

### 13.3 GAN Dynamics — Winding Around Equilibria

GAN training dynamics in the joint parameter space $(\theta_G, \theta_D)$ can be viewed as a vector field: $$\mathbf{v}(\theta_G, \theta_D) = (-\nabla_G \mathcal{L},; \nabla_D \mathcal{L})$$

When this vector field has **nonzero periods** (circulation around the equilibrium point), training cycles. This is precisely the extended Green's theorem in action: the equilibrium point acts as a "hole" in the dynamics, and the period $a_1 = \oint \mathbf{v}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} \neq 0$ quantifies the cycling.

**Consensus optimization** (Mescheder et al. 2017) and **symplectic gradient adjustment** (Balduzzi et al. 2018) attempt to zero out these periods — adding terms to make the dynamics conservative (exact), removing the topological obstruction.

### 13.4 Normalizing Flows on Topologically Nontrivial Spaces

Standard normalizing flows learn diffeomorphisms $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$. But many data distributions live on topologically nontrivial spaces:

- **Directional data** (angles, orientations): lives on $S^1$ (a circle) — has $H_1 \cong \mathbb{Z}$
- **Protein backbone angles** ($\phi, \psi$): lives on the torus $T^2 = S^1 \times S^1$
- **3D rotations**: lives on $SO(3)$ — has nontrivial $\pi_1$

The extended Green's theorem (de Rham cohomology) determines what flows are possible on these spaces. A flow on $S^1$ cannot be globally a gradient flow of a single-valued function — the potential is necessarily multi-valued ($\theta$ on the circle). This forces **special architectures**:

- **Circular flows** (Rezende et al. 2020): flows on $S^1$ use multi-valued potentials, directly implementing the branch-cut construction
- **Moser flows** on manifolds: use the divergence form of Green's/Stokes' theorem to construct volume-preserving maps

### 13.5 Hodge Decomposition in Graph Neural Networks

On a **simplicial complex** (a combinatorial analog of a manifold), the discrete analog of de Rham cohomology gives the **Hodge decomposition**:

$$\Omega^1 = \text{im}(\text{grad}) \oplus \ker(\text{curl}) \cap \ker(\text{div}) \oplus \text{im}(\text{curl}^*)$$

Any edge flow on a graph decomposes into:

1. **Gradient flow** (conservative, exact): derives from a 0-form (node potential)
2. **Harmonic flow** (curl-free and divergence-free): indexes the holes $H^1$ — the periods
3. **Curl flow** (derives from a 2-form / triangle flow)

**Hodge-aware GNNs** (Ebli et al. 2020, Bunch et al. 2020, Barbarossa & Sardellitti 2020) explicitly use this decomposition to process signals on simplicial complexes. The harmonic component — which corresponds exactly to the periods of the extended Green's theorem — carries **global topological information** about the network that no local message-passing scheme can capture.

**Applications:**

- **Traffic flow analysis**: harmonic flows correspond to traffic loops around city blocks (topological features of road networks)
- **Collaborative filtering**: rating patterns on user-item bipartite graphs decomposed into gradient (global trends), harmonic (persistent group preferences), and curl (local inconsistencies)
- **Brain connectivity**: white matter fiber tracts form simplicial complexes; harmonic flows detect persistent loops in connectivity patterns

### 13.6 Physics-Informed ML and Multi-Valued Potentials

Some physical systems have **multi-valued potentials** — the magnetic vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ in regions with magnetic flux but no field, for instance. Training PINNs on such systems requires understanding that the "potential" is not a single function but a section of a line bundle — exactly the multi-valued potential story of the extended Green's theorem.

**The Aharonov-Bohm effect** (quantum mechanics) is the physical manifestation: an electron traveling around a solenoid (even in a region with $\mathbf{B} = 0$) acquires a phase proportional to the magnetic flux through the solenoid — a period of the vector potential. This is the quantum version of the canonical example $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x\rangle/(x^2+y^2)$.

Neural networks trained to model such quantum systems must implicitly represent multi-valued functions — a direct application of the extended Green's theorem framework.

## §14. Summary

$$\boxed{\oint_{\partial D} P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA}$$

_The theorem statement is the same. What changes is what $\partial D$ means and what the theorem implies._

|Setting|Curl-zero implies...|
|---|---|
|Simply connected|Field is conservative; all closed integrals $= 0$|
|One hole|Circulation depends only on winding number around hole|
|$n$ holes|Circulation $= \sum_k n_k a_k$; periods $a_k$ are topological invariants|
|General|$\omega$ exact $\Leftrightarrow$ all periods vanish; $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}^n$|

|Object|Role|
|---|---|
|Closed 1-form $\omega$|Curl-free field: $Q_x = P_y$|
|Exact 1-form $df$|Conservative field: $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$|
|Period $a_k = \oint_{\gamma_k}\omega$|Obstruction to exactness; topological invariant|
|Winding number $n(C, p)$|How many times $C$ winds around hole at $p$; integer|
|$H^1_{\text{dR}}(U)$|Space of all obstructions; $\cong \mathbb{R}^n$ for $n$ holes|
|Residue theorem|Complex-analytic version; $\text{Res} = $ period/$2\pi i$|

## §15. Personal Notes

- The extended theorem is philosophically the most important version: it shows that **analysis and topology are not separate subjects**. The number of holes in a domain is a topological invariant, but it controls something analytic (whether curl-zero implies conservative). This is the first hint of the deep unity that becomes Hodge theory, de Rham cohomology, and ultimately the Atiyah-Singer index theorem.
- Always draw a picture of the domain before computing. Identify the holes, label the boundary components, assign orientations. Half the errors in these problems come from wrong orientation on inner boundaries.
- The canonical example $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x\rangle/(x^2+y^2)$ should be memorized completely — its curl calculation, its potential $\theta$, and its period $2\pi$. It is the simplest possible non-exact closed form, and it generates all the interesting topology in the plane.
- The connection to complex analysis via the residue theorem is deep: the Residue Theorem is a consequence of the extended Green's theorem applied to the real and imaginary parts of a holomorphic function. This is why complex analysis is so powerful — it's Green's theorem with extra algebraic structure.
- In ML: the moment you hear "the optimization cycles" or "the flow doesn't converge," think extended Green's theorem — there is a topological obstruction (a nonzero period) preventing the dynamics from being globally conservative. The fix is always to either modify the domain (remove the hole — change the architecture) or add a correction term to zero the period (symplectic adjustment, consensus optimization).
