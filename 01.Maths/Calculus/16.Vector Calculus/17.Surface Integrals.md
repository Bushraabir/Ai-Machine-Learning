

> _"Integration is the act of adding up infinitely many infinitely small things. On a surface, each infinitely small thing is a tiny parallelogram."_

---

## §0. Prefatory Remarks

A surface integral is the generalization of a line integral to two dimensions: instead of integrating along a curve, we integrate over a surface. There are two fundamentally different kinds:

- **Surface integrals of scalar fields** $\iint_S f, dS$ — integrate a function over the surface, weighted by area
- **Surface integrals of vector fields** $\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$ — integrate the _flux_ of a vector field through the surface

The second type requires the surface to be **oriented** — it must have a consistent notion of "inside" and "outside," or "up" and "down." This orientation is the new conceptual ingredient.

These integrals are the backbone of:

- **Physics:** Electric flux (Gauss's law), magnetic flux, fluid flow, heat transfer
- **Differential geometry:** Integration of differential forms on manifolds
- **Deep learning:** Information geometry, Fisher information matrix as a surface integral
- **PDEs:** Green's theorem, divergence theorem, Stokes' theorem

Everything in this note builds from the parametric surface machinery developed in [[Surfaces_of_Revolution]] and [[Tangent_Planes]].

## §1. Parametric Surfaces — Recollection and Setup

Recall from [[Tangent_Planes]] §4: a **parametric surface** is a map

$$ \mathbf{r}: D \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^3, \quad \mathbf{r}(u,v) = \bigl(x(u,v),; y(u,v),; z(u,v)\bigr), $$

where $D$ is a domain in the $uv$-plane (usually a rectangle or region with piecewise smooth boundary).

**The tangent vectors:** $$ \mathbf{r}_u = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u} = \left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial u}, \frac{\partial y}{\partial u}, \frac{\partial z}{\partial u}\right), \qquad \mathbf{r}_v = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v} = \left(\frac{\partial x}{\partial v}, \frac{\partial y}{\partial v}, \frac{\partial z}{\partial v}\right). $$

**The surface element.** At a point $(u_0, v_0)$, the small patch of surface corresponding to $[u_0, u_0+du] \times [v_0, v_0+dv]$ is approximated by the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{r}_u, du$ and $\mathbf{r}_v, dv$. Its area is:

$$ dS = |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|; du, dv. $$

**Definition 1.1 (Regular Surface).** The parametrization $\mathbf{r}$ is _regular_ at $(u_0,v_0)$ if

$$ \mathbf{r}_u(u_0,v_0) \times \mathbf{r}_v(u_0,v_0) \neq \mathbf{0}. $$

At regular points, the cross product gives a well-defined normal direction, and the tangent plane exists.

### Standard Examples of Parametric Surfaces

**Sphere** $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = a^2$: $$ \mathbf{r}(\phi,\theta) = (a\sin\phi\cos\theta,; a\sin\phi\sin\theta,; a\cos\phi), \quad \phi \in [0,\pi],; \theta \in [0,2\pi]. $$

**Graph** $z = f(x,y)$ over region $D$: $$ \mathbf{r}(x,y) = (x,; y,; f(x,y)), \quad (x,y) \in D. $$

**Cylinder** $x^2 + y^2 = R^2$, $0 \leq z \leq h$: $$ \mathbf{r}(\theta,z) = (R\cos\theta,; R\sin\theta,; z), \quad \theta \in [0,2\pi],; z \in [0,h]. $$

**Surface of revolution** (from [[Surfaces_of_Revolution]]): $$ \mathbf{r}(x,\theta) = (x,; f(x)\cos\theta,; f(x)\sin\theta), \quad x \in [a,b],; \theta \in [0,2\pi]. $$

## §2. Surface Integrals of Scalar Fields

**Definition 2.1 (Surface Integral of a Scalar Field).** Let $f: S \subset \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous and $\mathbf{r}: D \to S$ a regular parametrization. The _surface integral_ of $f$ over $S$ is

$$ \boxed{\iint_S f, dS = \iint_D f(\mathbf{r}(u,v)), |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|; du, dv.} $$

**Motivation.** Partition $D$ into small rectangles $\Delta u_i \times \Delta v_j$. Each maps to a small surface patch of area $\approx |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,\Delta u_i,\Delta v_j$. The Riemann sum $\sum_{i,j} f(\mathbf{r}(u_i,v_j)),|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,\Delta u_i,\Delta v_j$ converges to the integral in the limit.

**Special case: $f \equiv 1$.** Then $\iint_S 1, dS = \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|, du, dv$ is the **surface area** — consistent with [[Surfaces_of_Revolution]] §3.

**For a graph** $z = g(x,y)$: use $\mathbf{r}(x,y) = (x,y,g(x,y))$.

$$ \mathbf{r}_x = (1, 0, g_x), \quad \mathbf{r}_y = (0, 1, g_y), \quad \mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_y = (-g_x, -g_y, 1). $$

$$ |\mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_y| = \sqrt{1 + g_x^2 + g_y^2}. $$

So for a graph: $$ \iint_S f, dS = \iint_D f(x, y, g(x,y))\sqrt{1 + g_x^2 + g_y^2}; dx, dy. $$

### Example 2.1 — Mass of a Surface

A thin metallic sheet has the shape of the hemisphere $x^2+y^2+z^2 = 1$, $z \geq 0$, with surface density $\rho(x,y,z) = z$. Find its total mass.

**Parametrize:** $\mathbf{r}(\phi,\theta) = (\sin\phi\cos\theta, \sin\phi\sin\theta, \cos\phi)$, $\phi \in [0,\pi/2]$, $\theta \in [0,2\pi]$.

**Compute cross product:** $$ \mathbf{r}_\phi = (\cos\phi\cos\theta, \cos\phi\sin\theta, -\sin\phi), \quad \mathbf{r}_\theta = (-\sin\phi\sin\theta, \sin\phi\cos\theta, 0). $$

$$ \mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta = (\sin^2\phi\cos\theta,; \sin^2\phi\sin\theta,; \sin\phi\cos\phi). $$

$$ |\mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = \sin\phi\sqrt{\sin^2\phi + \cos^2\phi} = \sin\phi. $$

**Integrate:** $z = \cos\phi$ on the sphere, so

$$ M = \iint_S z, dS = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2} \cos\phi \cdot \sin\phi; d\phi, d\theta = 2\pi \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin\phi\cos\phi; d\phi = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \pi. \quad \checkmark $$

### Example 2.2 — Surface Integral over a Graph

Compute $\iint_S (x^2+y^2), dS$ where $S$ is the surface $z = 1 + 2x + 3y$ over $D = [0,1]\times[0,1]$.

$$ g_x = 2, ; g_y = 3, \quad \sqrt{1+4+9} = \sqrt{14}. $$

$$ \iint_S (x^2+y^2), dS = \sqrt{14}\int_0^1\int_0^1 (x^2+y^2), dx, dy = \sqrt{14} \cdot \frac{2}{3}. $$

## §3. Oriented Surfaces

This is where the new geometry enters. To integrate a vector field over a surface, we need to know **which side is "positive"** — i.e., which way the normal vector points.

**Definition 3.1 (Orientation).** An _orientation_ of a smooth surface $S$ is a continuous choice of unit normal vector $\hat{\mathbf{n}}(P)$ at each regular point $P \in S$.

A surface is _orientable_ if such a continuous choice exists globally. Most familiar surfaces are orientable: spheres, planes, graphs $z = f(x,y)$, cylinders (if we specify inner or outer). The famous exception is the **Möbius band** — it has only one side.

**Two orientations always exist** on an orientable connected surface: $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ and $-\hat{\mathbf{n}}$. Choosing one is called _orienting_ the surface.

**Standard Conventions:**

|Surface|Positive normal|
|---|---|
|Closed surface (sphere, box)|Outward-pointing|
|Graph $z = f(x,y)$|Upward: $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ has positive $z$-component|
|Parametric surface|$\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v}{|

**Reversing orientation:** Swapping $u \leftrightarrow v$ reverses the sign of $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$, hence reverses the orientation. Swapping the parameters is how you change which side is "positive."

**Definition 3.2 (Oriented Surface Element).** Given orientation $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, define the _vector area element_:

$$ d\mathbf{S} = \hat{\mathbf{n}}, dS = \frac{\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v}{|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|} \cdot |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|; du, dv = (\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v); du, dv. $$

The vector $d\mathbf{S}$ encodes both the area element $dS$ and the direction $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ simultaneously.

## §4. Surface Integrals of Vector Fields (Flux Integrals)

**Definition 4.1 (Flux Integral).** Let $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ be a continuous vector field and $S$ an oriented surface with unit normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$. The _flux_ of $\mathbf{F}$ through $S$ is

$$ \boxed{\iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_S (\mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}}), dS.} $$

In terms of parametrization $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$ with the induced orientation $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v}{|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|}$:

$$ \iint_S \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(u,v)) \cdot (\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v); du, dv. $$

**Physical interpretation.** $\mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = |\mathbf{F}|\cos\theta$ where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{F}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$. This is the component of $\mathbf{F}$ _through_ the surface (not tangent to it).

- If $\mathbf{F}$ is a fluid velocity field: flux = net volume flow rate per unit time through $S$
- If $\mathbf{F}$ is electric field: flux = total electric charge enclosed (Gauss's law)
- If $\mathbf{F}$ is magnetic field: flux = magnetic flux (Faraday's law involves its time derivative)

**For a graph** $z = g(x,y)$, $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ upward:

$$ \mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_y = (-g_x, -g_y, 1), \quad d\mathbf{S} = (-g_x, -g_y, 1), dx, dy. $$

If $\mathbf{F} = (P, Q, R)$:

$$ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \bigl(-Pg_x - Qg_y + R\bigr)\big|_{z=g(x,y)}; dx, dy. $$

## §5. Computing the Cross Product Systematically

Given $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$, the key computational step is always $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$. Here is the systematic approach:

$$ \mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ \partial_u x & \partial_u y & \partial_u z \ \partial_v x & \partial_v y & \partial_v z \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} \partial_u y & \partial_u z \ \partial_v y & \partial_v z \end{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} - \begin{vmatrix} \partial_u x & \partial_u z \ \partial_v x & \partial_v z \end{vmatrix}\mathbf{j} + \begin{vmatrix} \partial_u x & \partial_u y \ \partial_v x & \partial_v y \end{vmatrix}\mathbf{k}. $$

In shorthand using the **Jacobian notation**:

$$ \mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v = \left(\frac{\partial(y,z)}{\partial(u,v)},; \frac{\partial(z,x)}{\partial(u,v)},; \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}\right), $$

where $\frac{\partial(y,z)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} y_u & y_v \ z_u & z_v \end{vmatrix} = y_u z_v - y_v z_u$, etc.

This Jacobian notation reveals the deep structure: each component of $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$ is the area of the projected parallelogram onto one coordinate plane. More on this in §8.

## §6. Worked Examples — Flux Integrals

### Example 6.1 — Flux Through a Sphere

Let $\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = (x, y, z)$ (radial field). Compute the flux outward through the sphere $S: x^2+y^2+z^2 = a^2$.

**Method 1 (geometric shortcut).** On the sphere, $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{(x,y,z)}{a}$ (outward unit normal). So

$$ \mathbf{F} \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{x^2+y^2+z^2}{a} = \frac{a^2}{a} = a. $$

The integrand is constant! So

$$ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = a \cdot \text{Area}(S) = a \cdot 4\pi a^2 = 4\pi a^3. $$

_(Preview: by the Divergence Theorem, $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 3$, and $\iiint_{B} 3, dV = 3 \cdot \frac{4}{3}\pi a^3 = 4\pi a^3$. ✓)_

### Example 6.2 — Flux Through a Cylinder (with caps)

Let $\mathbf{F} = (x, y, 0)$. Compute the outward flux through the closed cylinder $x^2+y^2 \leq 1$, $0 \leq z \leq 1$.

The surface has three pieces: lateral side $S_1$, top cap $S_2$ ($z=1$), bottom cap $S_3$ ($z=0$).

**Lateral side $S_1$:** $\mathbf{r}(\theta,z) = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta, z)$. $$ \mathbf{r}_\theta = (-\sin\theta, \cos\theta, 0), \quad \mathbf{r}_z = (0,0,1). $$ $$ \mathbf{r}_\theta \times \mathbf{r}_z = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta, 0) \quad \text{(outward, since } x\text{-component positive when }\theta\in[0,\pi/2]\text{)}. $$ $$ \mathbf{F}\cdot(\mathbf{r}_\theta \times \mathbf{r}_z) = \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1. $$ $$ \iint_{S_1} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 1; dz, d\theta = 2\pi. $$

**Top cap $S_2$** ($z = 1$, upward normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \mathbf{k}$): $$ \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}} = (x,y,0)\cdot(0,0,1) = 0. \quad \iint_{S_2} = 0. $$

**Bottom cap $S_3$** ($z = 0$, downward normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = -\mathbf{k}$): $$ \mathbf{F}\cdot(-\mathbf{k}) = 0. \quad \iint_{S_3} = 0. $$

**Total flux:** $2\pi + 0 + 0 = 2\pi$.

_(Check via Divergence Theorem: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 2$, volume $= \pi$. $2\pi \cdot 1 = 2\pi$. ✓)_

### Example 6.3 — Flux Through a Graph

Let $\mathbf{F} = (0, 0, z)$ and $S: z = x^2 + y^2$, $0 \leq z \leq 1$ (paraboloid), upward orientation.

$$ \mathbf{r}(x,y) = (x, y, x^2+y^2), \quad D = {x^2+y^2 \leq 1}. $$ $$ g_x = 2x, \quad g_y = 2y, \quad d\mathbf{S} = (-2x, -2y, 1), dx, dy. $$ $$ \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = (0,0, x^2+y^2)\cdot(-2x,-2y,1) = x^2+y^2. $$ $$ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D (x^2+y^2), dx, dy = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^2 \cdot r; dr, d\theta = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{4} = \frac{\pi}{2}. $$

### Example 6.4 — Flux Through the Torus (Conceptual)

The torus has outward normal pointing away from the central circle (see [[Surfaces_of_Revolution]] §4 for parameterization). For a constant field $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{k}$, the flux through the torus is zero by symmetry: for every patch where $\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}} > 0$, there is a symmetric patch where $\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}} < 0$. This is an instance of the general principle: $\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = 0$ for any closed surface when $\mathbf{F}$ is constant and the surface is closed (by the Divergence Theorem, $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 0$).

## §8. The Differential Form Perspective

The surface integral of a vector field has a beautiful rewriting. If $\mathbf{F} = (P, Q, R)$, then

$$ \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \mathbf{F}\cdot(\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v), du, dv = P,\frac{\partial(y,z)}{\partial(u,v)} + Q,\frac{\partial(z,x)}{\partial(u,v)} + R,\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)}; du, dv. $$

This is the **2-form**: $$ \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_S P, dy\wedge dz + Q, dz\wedge dx + R, dx\wedge dy. $$

This notation is not mere decoration — it is the language of differential geometry and Stokes' theorem on manifolds.

**The exterior derivative** of the 1-form $\omega = P,dx + Q,dy + R,dz$ gives a 2-form $d\omega$, and Stokes' theorem says $\int_{\partial \Sigma} \omega = \iint_\Sigma d\omega$ for any oriented 2-manifold $\Sigma$ with boundary $\partial\Sigma$. This unifies:

|Classical theorem|Differential forms|
|---|---|
|Fundamental theorem of calculus|$\int_a^b f',dx = f(b) - f(a)$|
|Green's theorem|$\oint_C P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D (Q_x - P_y),dA$|
|Stokes' theorem|$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$|
|Divergence theorem|$\oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}),dV$|

All are special cases of $\int_{\partial M} \omega = \int_M d\omega$.

## §9. Stokes' Theorem and the Divergence Theorem — Statement

We state these without proof, as they deserve their own notes, but place them here for context.

**Theorem 9.1 (Stokes' Theorem).** Let $S$ be an oriented piecewise-smooth surface bounded by a simple closed curve $\partial S$, oriented consistently (right-hand rule). Let $\mathbf{F}$ be $C^1$ on an open set containing $S$. Then:

$$ \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}. $$

**Theorem 9.2 (Divergence Theorem / Gauss's Theorem).** Let $E$ be a simple solid region with outward-oriented boundary surface $\partial E = S$. Let $\mathbf{F}$ be $C^1$ on an open set containing $E$. Then:

$$ \oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_E (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}), dV. $$

**Mnemonic.** Both theorems say: _the integral of a derivative over a domain equals the integral of the original function over the boundary_. Stokes: 2D → 1D. Divergence: 3D → 2D.

## §10. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 10.1 Fisher Information Matrix as a Surface Integral

The **Fisher information matrix** $I(\theta)$ for a statistical model $p(x;\theta)$ is

$$ I(\theta)_{ij} = \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p(\cdot;\theta)}\left[\frac{\partial \log p}{\partial \theta_i}\frac{\partial \log p}{\partial \theta_j}\right]. $$

In geometric terms, $I(\theta)$ is the **Riemannian metric** on the manifold of probability distributions (Fisher-Rao metric). For a continuous model with density $p(x;\theta)$ over a domain $\Omega \subset \mathbb{R}^2$:

$$ I(\theta)_{ij} = \iint_\Omega \frac{\partial \log p}{\partial \theta_i}\frac{\partial \log p}{\partial \theta_j}, p(x;\theta); dx_1, dx_2. $$

This is a **surface integral of a scalar field** — integrating $\frac{\partial \log p}{\partial \theta_i}\frac{\partial \log p}{\partial \theta_j}$ weighted by the probability measure $p,dA$. **Natural gradient descent** (used in KFAC, Shampoo, and other second-order optimizers) replaces the Euclidean gradient with the Riemannian gradient $I(\theta)^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}$, which is coordinate-invariant.

### 10.2 Flux and Information Flow in Neural Networks

Consider a neural network layer as a map $\mathbf{z} = \sigma(W\mathbf{x})$. The **Jacobian** $J = \frac{\partial \mathbf{z}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}$ maps the input tangent space to the output tangent space. The **singular values** of $J$ measure how much "information flux" passes through the layer.

For a 2D intermediate representation (e.g., a 2D convolutional feature map), the information flux through a surface $S$ in feature space is:

$$ \Phi = \iint_S \mathbf{J}\cdot d\mathbf{S}, $$

where $\mathbf{J}$ is the Jacobian field. Vanishing gradients correspond to $\Phi \to 0$ — the "flux" of gradient information through the network collapses. Exploding gradients correspond to $\Phi \to \infty$.

### 10.3 Gauss's Law and Implicit Neural Surfaces

Implicit neural representations (e.g., Neural Unsigned Distance Functions) represent surfaces as ${F_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = 0}$. **Gauss's law** applied to the learned signed distance function $F_\theta$:

$$ \oiint_{\partial V} \nabla F_\theta \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V \Delta F_\theta; dV, $$

where $\Delta = \nabla^2$ is the Laplacian. For a true signed distance function, $|\nabla F| = 1$ everywhere (Eikonal equation), so $\Delta F = $ (mean curvature of level sets). **Eikonal loss** in training NeRF and NeuS models enforces this constraint:

$$ \mathcal{L}_\text{eikonal} = \mathbb{E}_\mathbf{x}\left[(|\nabla F_\theta(\mathbf{x})| - 1)^2\right]. $$

This is precisely requiring the flux of the gradient field through every surface to be controlled.

### 10.4 Normalizing Flows and Change of Variables on Surfaces

A normalizing flow $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ transforms a base density $p_Z$ to a target density $p_X$. The log-likelihood involves $\log|\det J_T|$. Geometrically:

$$ \log|\det J_T(\mathbf{z})| = \log|\text{volume element distortion at } \mathbf{z}|. $$

For a **2D flow** (e.g., a planar flow representing a 2D surface), $|\det J_T|$ is the ratio of area elements — exactly the $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|$ factor in our surface integral formula! Training a normalizing flow to maximize likelihood is equivalent to learning a parametrization of a surface in probability space that minimizes area distortion.

### 10.5 Stokes' Theorem in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

PINNs solve PDEs by minimizing residuals. For Maxwell's equations (governing electromagnetic fields in physics simulators and neural hardware models):

$$ \nabla \times \mathbf{B} = \mu_0 \mathbf{J} + \mu_0\epsilon_0 \frac{\partial \mathbf{E}}{\partial t}. $$

Stokes' theorem converts this: $\oint_C \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$. A PINN enforces this integral form as a loss:

$$ \mathcal{L}_\text{Stokes} = \left|\oint_C \mathbf{B}_\theta \cdot d\mathbf{r} - \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{B}_\theta)\cdot d\mathbf{S}\right|^2. $$

The surface integral directly appears in the training objective.

### 10.6 Orientation and Signed vs. Unsigned Surfaces in 3D Deep Learning

In 3D point cloud networks (PointNet, PointNet++, DGCNN), surface orientation matters. A mesh-based network must consistently orient faces to define outward normals — exactly the orientation problem of §3. Learning orientation from unoriented point clouds is an active research area (2023–2025), and fails precisely on non-orientable surfaces — a topological obstruction, not a learning failure.

**Watertight meshes** (closed, orientable, consistently oriented) allow the Divergence Theorem to compute volumes from surface integrals:

$$ \text{Vol} = \frac{1}{3}\oiint_S \mathbf{r}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{3}\oiint_S (x,y,z)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}, dS. $$

This formula is used in computational geometry and 3D printing quality checks.

## §12. Summary

|Object|Formula|Key point|
|---|---|---|
|Scalar surface integral|$\iint_S f, dS = \iint_D f(\mathbf{r}),\|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v\|,du,dv$|Area-weighted|
|Area element|$dS = \|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v\|,du,dv$|Jacobian of parametrization|
|Orientation|Continuous choice of $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$|Required for flux integrals|
|Vector area element|$d\mathbf{S} = (\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v),du,dv$|Encodes area + direction|
|**Flux integral**|$\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r})\cdot(\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v),du,dv$|Only normal component contributes|
|Graph $z=g(x,y)$|$d\mathbf{S} = (-g_x, -g_y, 1),dx,dy$ (upward)|Standard form|
|Stokes' theorem|$\oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$|Boundary ↔ interior|
|Divergence theorem|$\oiint_{\partial E}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_E(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}),dV$|Surface ↔ volume|

**The conceptual hierarchy:**

$$ \underbrace{\int_a^b f,dx}_{\text{1D}} \longrightarrow \underbrace{\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}}_{\text{line integral}} \longrightarrow \underbrace{\iint_S f,dS}_{\text{scalar surface}} \longrightarrow \underbrace{\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}}_{\text{flux / vector surface}} $$

Each arrow adds one dimension and one new idea: parameterization, area distortion, orientation. The flux integral is the culmination — it integrates a vector field's interaction with an oriented surface, and it is the object that Gauss, Stokes, and the entire theory of differential forms is built around.
