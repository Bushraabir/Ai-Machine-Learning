## 1. Motivation — Why Space Changes Things

In the plane, the topology of a domain was already interesting: a domain with a hole (like $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus{0}$) allows curl-free fields that are not conservative. In space, this becomes richer.

Curves in $\mathbb{R}^3$ have **torsion** — they can twist out of any plane. A curve may **link** with another curve in a way that has no 2D analogue. These geometric facts have analytic consequences:

- A 1-form in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with zero curl may still fail to be exact if $H^1_{\text{dR}}(\mathbb{R}^3\setminus C) \neq 0$, where $C$ is a closed curve the domain winds around.
- **Stokes' theorem** in 3D relates the line integral around a curve to a surface integral — not a double integral over a plane region as in Green's theorem.
- The **linking number** of two closed curves is a topological invariant computed as a line integral, with deep connections to knot theory and topological field theories.

The line integral in space is also the setting for classical physics: work done by a 3D force, circulation of a magnetic field, Ampère's law, and the Biot–Savart law are all line integrals in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

## 2. Curves in Space — Full Differential Geometry Prerequisites

### 2.1 Smooth Curves and Their Calculus

A **smooth curve** in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is a $C^1$ map $\mathbf{r} : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}^3$: $$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t),; y(t),; z(t)\rangle$$ with $\mathbf{r}'(t) \neq \mathbf{0}$ for all $t$ (regular parametrisation).

**Velocity vector:** $\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle x'(t), y'(t), z'(t)\rangle$ — tangent to the curve, with magnitude equal to the speed.

**Arc length element:** $$ds = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt = \sqrt{(x')^2 + (y')^2 + (z')^2},dt$$

**Arc length from $a$ to $t$:** $$s(t) = \int_a^t |\mathbf{r}'(u)|,du$$

**Arc-length reparametrisation.** If $s$ is the arc length parameter, then $|\mathbf{r}'(s)| = 1$. This is the "unit speed" or "natural" parametrisation. It always exists for regular curves (by the inverse function theorem applied to $s(t)$), though it is rarely computable in closed form.

### 2.2 The Frenet–Serret Frame

The intrinsic geometry of a space curve is captured by three orthonormal vectors and two scalar curvatures. This is not needed for computing line integrals, but it is the correct conceptual setting.

**Unit tangent:** $$\hat{\mathbf{T}}(t) = \frac{\mathbf{r}'(t)}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|}$$

**Curvature:** $\kappa(t) = \left|\dfrac{d\hat{\mathbf{T}}}{ds}\right|$ — measures how fast the curve turns. Zero curvature means straight line.

**Principal normal:** $$\hat{\mathbf{N}}(t) = \frac{1}{\kappa}\frac{d\hat{\mathbf{T}}}{ds}$$ Points toward the centre of curvature (the direction the curve is turning).

**Binormal:** $$\hat{\mathbf{B}}(t) = \hat{\mathbf{T}}(t) \times \hat{\mathbf{N}}(t)$$ Perpendicular to the osculating plane.

**Torsion:** $\tau(t) = -\dfrac{d\hat{\mathbf{B}}}{ds}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{N}}$ — measures how fast the curve twists out of the osculating plane. Zero torsion means the curve is planar.

**Frenet–Serret equations:** $$\frac{d\hat{\mathbf{T}}}{ds} = \kappa\hat{\mathbf{N}}, \qquad \frac{d\hat{\mathbf{N}}}{ds} = -\kappa\hat{\mathbf{T}} + \tau\hat{\mathbf{B}}, \qquad \frac{d\hat{\mathbf{B}}}{ds} = -\tau\hat{\mathbf{N}}$$

> **Fundamental Theorem of Space Curves.** A curve in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is uniquely determined (up to rigid motion) by its curvature $\kappa(s) > 0$ and torsion $\tau(s)$ as functions of arc length.

_Why this matters for line integrals:_ The line integral $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{T}},ds$ involves only the tangential component of $\mathbf{F}$ relative to $\hat{\mathbf{T}}$. The curvature and torsion of the curve affect the geometry of the path but enter the computation only through the parametrisation, not explicitly through $\kappa$ and $\tau$.

### 2.3 Piecewise-Smooth Curves

A **piecewise-smooth** curve is a finite union $C = C_1 \cup C_2 \cup \cdots \cup C_k$ where each $C_i$ is smooth and the endpoint of $C_i$ is the start of $C_{i+1}$. Line integrals over piecewise-smooth curves are defined by additivity: $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \sum_{i=1}^k \int_{C_i}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

## 3. Scalar Line Integrals in Space

> **Definition.** Let $f : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous on an open set containing $C$. The **scalar line integral** is: $$\int_C f,ds = \int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t)),|\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt$$

This is identical in form to the 2D version — the extra $z$-component just enters through $|\mathbf{r}'| = \sqrt{(x')^2+(y')^2+(z')^2}$.

### Physical Applications

**Mass of a space wire:** $$M = \int_C \rho(x,y,z),ds$$ where $\rho$ is linear mass density (mass per unit length).

**Centre of mass:** $$\bar{x} = \frac{1}{M}\int_C x,\rho,ds, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{1}{M}\int_C y,\rho,ds, \qquad \bar{z} = \frac{1}{M}\int_C z,\rho,ds$$

**Moment of inertia** about the $z$-axis: $$I_z = \int_C (x^2 + y^2),\rho,ds$$

**Temperature along a path:** If $T(x,y,z)$ is a temperature field, $\int_C T,ds / L(C)$ is the average temperature along the path.

### Key Example — Scalar Integral over a Helix

Let $C$ be the helix $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle \cos t, \sin t, t\rangle$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$, and $f(x,y,z) = z\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$.

$|\mathbf{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t + 1} = \sqrt{2}$.

On $C$: $x^2 + y^2 = \cos^2 t + \sin^2 t = 1$, so $f = z\cdot 1 = z = t$.

$$\int_C f,ds = \int_0^{2\pi} t\cdot\sqrt{2},dt = \sqrt{2}\cdot\frac{t^2}{2}\Big|_0^{2\pi} = \sqrt{2}\cdot 2\pi^2 = 2\sqrt{2},\pi^2$$

## 4. Vector Line Integrals in Space

> **Definition.** Let $\mathbf{F} : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ be a continuous vector field, $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$. The **vector line integral** along an oriented curve $C$ parametrised by $\mathbf{r}(t)$, $t \in [a,b]$, is: $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t),dt$$

Expanded: $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \left[P(\mathbf{r}(t))x'(t) + Q(\mathbf{r}(t))y'(t) + R(\mathbf{r}(t))z'(t)\right]dt$$

In differential notation: $$\int_C P,dx + Q,dy + R,dz$$

where $dx = x'(t),dt$, $dy = y'(t),dt$, $dz = z'(t),dt$.

### Relation to Scalar Integral

$$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C (\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{T}}),ds$$

Only the component of $\mathbf{F}$ along the tangent contributes. The normal and binormal components do no "work."

### Physical Interpretations

**Work.** If $\mathbf{F}$ is a force field, $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is the work done by $\mathbf{F}$ on a particle traversing $C$.

**Circulation.** For a closed curve: $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is the circulation of $\mathbf{F}$ around $C$ — a measure of the rotational tendency of the field around that loop.

**Ampère's Law (Physics).** For a steady current $I$ producing magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$: $$\oint_C \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \mu_0 I_{\text{enc}}$$ The circulation of $\mathbf{B}$ around any closed loop equals $\mu_0$ times the current enclosed. This is a vector line integral in $\mathbb{R}^3$ at the heart of electromagnetism.

### The Sign Rule

$$\int_{-C}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = -\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

Orientation (direction of travel) is intrinsic to the vector line integral. This is why we must specify orientation when stating theorems like Stokes'.

## 5. The Differential Form Language

This is the _right_ language for line integrals. It unifies the scalar and vector cases and connects cleanly to Stokes' theorem.

### 1-Forms in $\mathbb{R}^3$

A **differential 1-form** $\omega$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is an expression: $$\omega = P(x,y,z),dx + Q(x,y,z),dy + R(x,y,z),dz$$

where $P, Q, R$ are smooth functions. Formally, $dx, dy, dz$ are dual basis covectors to $\partial_x, \partial_y, \partial_z$.

The line integral of $\omega$ along $C$ is: $$\int_C \omega = \int_a^b \left(P\frac{dx}{dt} + Q\frac{dy}{dt} + R\frac{dz}{dt}\right)dt$$

This is exactly the vector line integral with $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$.

### Exact and Closed Forms

A 1-form $\omega$ is:

- **Exact** if $\omega = df$ for some $C^1$ function $f$ (i.e., $P = f_x, Q = f_y, R = f_z$).
- **Closed** if $d\omega = 0$, where $d$ is the exterior derivative.

**The exterior derivative of $\omega = P,dx + Q,dy + R,dz$:** $$d\omega = \left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\right)dy\wedge dz + \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial x}\right)dz\wedge dx + \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dx\wedge dy$$

The coefficients of $d\omega$ are precisely the components of $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$. So: $$\omega \text{ is closed} \iff d\omega = 0 \iff \nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$$ $$\omega \text{ is exact} \iff \omega = df \iff \mathbf{F} = \nabla f \iff \mathbf{F} \text{ is conservative}$$

> **De Rham's Theorem.** On $\mathbb{R}^n$ (or any contractible domain), every closed form is exact. On domains with non-trivial topology, the quotient $\ker(d)/\text{im}(d)$ — the de Rham cohomology $H^k_{\text{dR}}$ — measures the obstruction.

For line integrals (1-forms, $k=1$): $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U)$ measures how many independent "non-conservative" curl-free fields exist on $U$, corresponding to independent non-contractible loops in $U$.

**Examples:**

- $U = \mathbb{R}^3$: $H^1 = 0$, all closed 1-forms are exact.
- $U = \mathbb{R}^3 \setminus {\text{line}}$: $H^1 \cong \mathbb{R}$, one independent non-exact closed form.
- $U = \mathbb{R}^3 \setminus {\text{two parallel lines}}$: $H^1 \cong \mathbb{R}^2$.

### Pullback

The computation of a line integral is a **pullback**: we pull back the 1-form $\omega$ from $\mathbb{R}^3$ to $[a,b]$ via $\mathbf{r}$: $$\mathbf{r}^*\omega = \left(P(x(t),y(t),z(t))x'(t) + Q\cdot y'(t) + R\cdot z'(t)\right)dt$$

Then $\int_C \omega = \int_a^b \mathbf{r}^*\omega$ is an ordinary Riemann integral on $[a,b]$.

## 6. The Fundamental Theorem in Space

Identical statement to the 2D case, but now the curve and potential live in $\mathbb{R}^3$:

> **Theorem (FTC for Line Integrals in $\mathbb{R}^3$).** Let $f : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ be $C^1$, and $C$ a smooth curve in $U$ from $A$ to $B$. Then: $$\int_C \nabla f\cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(B) - f(A)$$

**Proof.** Identical to the 2D proof: $$\int_C \nabla f\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \left(f_x x' + f_y y' + f_z z'\right)dt = \int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}[f(\mathbf{r}(t))],dt = f(\mathbf{r}(b)) - f(\mathbf{r}(a))$$ by the chain rule in $\mathbb{R}^3$ and 1D FTC. $\blacksquare$

The geometry of the curve in 3D — its curvature, torsion, how it winds through space — is irrelevant. Only endpoints matter.

## 7. Conservative Fields in Space — The Full Theory

### The Curl Test in $\mathbb{R}^3$

> **Theorem.** Let $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$ be $C^1$ on a simply connected domain $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$. Then: $$\mathbf{F} = \nabla f \text{ for some } f \iff \nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0} \text{ on } U$$

Explicitly, $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ means all three conditions hold simultaneously: $$\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}, \qquad \frac{\partial P}{\partial z} = \frac{\partial R}{\partial x}, \qquad \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$$

These are six equality-of-mixed-partials conditions (well, three), each necessary by Clairaut's theorem when $f \in C^2$.

### Finding the Potential in $\mathbb{R}^3$

**Algorithm:**

1. Integrate $P$ w.r.t. $x$: $\quad f = \int P,dx + g(y,z)$
2. Differentiate w.r.t. $y$, match to $Q$: $\quad \partial_y f = \cdots + \partial_y g = Q \implies \partial_y g = \cdots$
3. Integrate w.r.t. $y$: $\quad g = \int(\cdots),dy + h(z)$
4. Differentiate w.r.t. $z$, match to $R$: $\quad \partial_z f = \cdots + h'(z) = R \implies h'(z) = \cdots$
5. Integrate to find $h(z)$.

> **Worked example.** $\mathbf{F} = \langle 2xz + y^2,; 2xy,; x^2 + 3z^2\rangle$.
> 
> **Curl check:**
> 
> - $R_y - Q_z = 0 - 0 = 0$ ✓
> - $P_z - R_x = 2x - 2x = 0$ ✓
> - $Q_x - P_y = 2y - 2y = 0$ ✓
> 
> **Find $f$:** $$f = \int (2xz + y^2),dx = x^2 z + xy^2 + g(y,z)$$ $$\partial_y f = 2xy + \partial_y g = 2xy \implies \partial_y g = 0 \implies g = h(z)$$ $$\partial_z f = x^2 + h'(z) = x^2 + 3z^2 \implies h'(z) = 3z^2 \implies h(z) = z^3$$
> 
> $$\boxed{f(x,y,z) = x^2 z + xy^2 + z^3 + C}$$

### Topology in Space — When Does Curl-Free Fail to Imply Conservative?

In $\mathbb{R}^3$, the topological obstruction for 1-forms lives in $H^1_{\text{dR}}$. For $\mathbb{R}^3$ itself, $H^1 = 0$ — no obstruction. But:

**$\mathbb{R}^3$ minus a line.** Remove the $z$-axis: $U = \mathbb{R}^3 \setminus {x = y = 0}$. Then $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}$. The non-exact closed 1-form is the "angular form" $\omega = \dfrac{-y,dx + x,dy}{x^2+y^2}$ (the $d\theta$ form from 2D, pulled into 3D and independent of $z$). Any closed curve that **links** with the $z$-axis has non-zero integral of $\omega$ — the **linking number**.

**$\mathbb{R}^3$ minus a point.** $U = \mathbb{R}^3\setminus{0}$. Here $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U) = 0$ but $H^2_{\text{dR}}(U) \cong \mathbb{R}$ — the obstruction is for 2-forms (flux integrals), not 1-forms. Every curl-free vector field on $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus{0}$ is conservative. (This is why the gravitational and Coulomb fields are conservative despite being singular at the origin.)

> _The contrast between $\mathbb{R}^2\setminus{0}$ and $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus{0}$ is striking: in the plane, removing a point destroys the conservatism of the angular 1-form; in space, removing a point does not — loops can slip around the hole._

## 8. Stokes' Theorem — The 3D Generalisation of Green's

Stokes' theorem relates the line integral around a closed curve to a surface integral over _any_ surface bounded by that curve.

> **Theorem (Stokes).** Let $S$ be an oriented smooth surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with positively oriented boundary curve $\partial S$ (determined by the right-hand rule relative to the normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ of $S$). If $\mathbf{F} \in C^1$ on an open set containing $S$: $$\oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

where $d\mathbf{S} = \hat{\mathbf{n}},dS$ is the oriented area element.

**In differential form language:** $\displaystyle\int_{\partial S}\omega = \int_S d\omega$.

### Orientation Convention

The **right-hand rule**: if the thumb of your right hand points in the direction of $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, your fingers curl in the positive direction of $\partial S$.

```
       ↑ n̂
       |
  _____|_____
 /           \
|      S      |  ← ∂S traversed counterclockwise
 \___________/       when viewed from above (n̂ pointing up)
```

### What Stokes' Theorem Tells Us

1. **The line integral around $\partial S$ depends only on the boundary** — two surfaces with the same boundary give the same surface integral of $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$.
    
2. **Consequences for curl:**
    
    - If $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ everywhere, then $\oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for every closed curve that bounds a surface in $U$. On simply connected domains, every closed curve bounds a surface, so this recovers the curl test.
    - If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$, then $\nabla\times\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$, so $\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = 0$ and $\oint \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ — consistent with FTC.
3. **Green's Theorem is a special case.** Take $S$ to be a flat region $D$ in the $xy$-plane with $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \hat{\mathbf{k}}$: $$\iint_D (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{k}},dA = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA = \oint_{\partial D} P,dx + Q,dy$$ This is exactly Green's theorem.
    

### Proof Sketch (for a graph surface)

Let $S$ be the graph $z = g(x,y)$ over a region $D$ in the $xy$-plane. Parametrise $S$ by $(x,y) \mapsto (x,y,g(x,y))$. The boundary $\partial S$ corresponds to $\partial D$ lifted to the surface. By Green's theorem on $D$ and careful application of the chain rule, one establishes the equality. The general case follows by breaking $S$ into graph patches. Full proof: Spivak, _Calculus on Manifolds_.

## 9. Applications and Computations

### Choosing the Surface in Stokes' Theorem

One of the practical powers of Stokes: you can choose _any_ surface bounded by the given curve. Always choose the simplest one.

> **Strategy.** If $C$ is a closed curve, find the simplest surface $S$ with $\partial S = C$:
> 
> - If $C$ lies in a plane, take $S$ to be the planar region.
> - If $C$ is on a sphere/cylinder, a spherical/cylindrical cap may be simpler.
> - Sometimes $S$ is a Möbius-like surface — but Stokes requires orientable surfaces.

### Faraday's Law of Induction (Physics)

$$\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = -\frac{d}{dt}\iint_S \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

The EMF (line integral of electric field around a loop) equals minus the rate of change of magnetic flux through any surface bounded by that loop. This is Stokes' theorem applied to Maxwell's equations.

### The Magnetic Field of a Wire — Ampère + Stokes

Current $I$ along the $z$-axis produces $\mathbf{B} = \dfrac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi}\cdot\dfrac{1}{r}\hat{\boldsymbol{\phi}}$ in cylindrical coordinates.

For a circle $C$ of radius $r$ in the $xy$-plane: $$\oint_C \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \frac{\mu_0 I}{2\pi r}\cdot 2\pi r = \mu_0 I$$

By Stokes (applied to $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J}$): $$\oint_C \mathbf{B}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{B})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \mu_0\iint_S \mathbf{J}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \mu_0 I$$

## 10. Worked Examples

### Example A — Line integral over a helix (vector)

$\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x, z\rangle$. $C$: helix $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle \cos t, \sin t, t\rangle$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$.

$\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle -\sin t, \cos t, 1\rangle$.

$\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \langle -\sin t, \cos t, t\rangle$.

$$\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{r}' = (-\sin t)(-\sin t) + (\cos t)(\cos t) + t\cdot 1 = \sin^2 t + \cos^2 t + t = 1 + t$$

$$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi}(1+t),dt = \left[t + \frac{t^2}{2}\right]_0^{2\pi} = 2\pi + 2\pi^2$$

---

### Example B — Using Stokes' Theorem

Compute $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F} = \langle y^2, x, z^2\rangle$ and $C$ is the triangle with vertices $(1,0,0), (0,1,0), (0,0,1)$, traversed counterclockwise when viewed from above.

**Compute $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$:** $$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i}&\mathbf{j}&\mathbf{k}\\partial_x&\partial_y&\partial_z\ y^2 & x & z^2\end{vmatrix} = \langle 0-0,; 0-0,; 1-2y\rangle = \langle 0, 0, 1-2y\rangle$$

**Surface $S$:** Take the flat triangle. The plane through the three vertices satisfies $x + y + z = 1$, normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \langle 1,1,1\rangle/\sqrt{3}$, oriented upward-ish (consistent with the given orientation).

Parametrise over $D = {(x,y) : x \geq 0, y \geq 0, x+y \leq 1}$ with $z = 1-x-y$:

$d\mathbf{S} = \langle 1, 1, 1\rangle,dA$ (normal to the plane $x+y+z=1$, not normalised — this is correct for the surface integral with $d\mathbf{S} = \hat{\mathbf{n}},dS$, computed directly as the cross product of parametrisation partial derivatives).

$$\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \langle 0, 0, 1-2y\rangle\cdot\langle 1,1,1\rangle,dA = \iint_D (1-2y),dA$$

$$= \int_0^1\int_0^{1-x}(1-2y),dy,dx = \int_0^1\left[y - y^2\right]_0^{1-x}dx = \int_0^1\left[(1-x)-(1-x)^2\right]dx$$

$$= \int_0^1(1-x)(1-(1-x)),dx = \int_0^1 x(1-x),dx = \int_0^1(x-x^2),dx = \frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6}$$

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \frac{1}{6}$$

### Example C — Showing path-independence in $\mathbb{R}^3$

$\mathbf{F} = \langle 2xy + z^2,; x^2,; 2xz\rangle$. Is $\mathbf{F}$ conservative? Find $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ from $(0,0,0)$ to $(1,2,3)$.

**Curl check:**

- $R_y - Q_z = 0 - 0 = 0$ ✓
- $P_z - R_x = 2z - 2z = 0$ ✓
- $Q_x - P_y = 2x - 2x = 0$ ✓

**Find potential:** $$f = \int(2xy+z^2),dx = x^2 y + xz^2 + g(y,z)$$ $$f_y = x^2 + g_y = x^2 \implies g_y = 0 \implies g = h(z)$$ $$f_z = 2xz + h'(z) = 2xz \implies h'(z) = 0 \implies h = C$$ $$f(x,y,z) = x^2 y + xz^2$$

**Line integral:** $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(1,2,3) - f(0,0,0) = (1\cdot 2 + 1\cdot 9) - 0 = 11$$

## 11. Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

### 11.1 Parameter Trajectories Live in High-Dimensional Space

Neural network parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ trace curves in $n$-dimensional space during training. This is the exact setting of line integrals in space — except the "space" is $\mathbb{R}^n$ with $n$ potentially in the billions.

Everything from 3D generalises directly: $$\Delta\mathcal{L} = \int_C \nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta}, \qquad \text{(FTC, holds for any }n\text{)}$$ $$\text{Gradient flow dissipation: } \int_C \nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} = -\int_0^T|\nabla\mathcal{L}|^2,dt$$

The Frenet–Serret frame of the training trajectory in parameter space — its curvature and torsion — can be used to characterise training dynamics. High curvature in the training path corresponds to rapid direction changes (oscillation); low curvature to smooth convergence.

### 11.2 Curvature of Training Trajectories

The curvature of $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$ in parameter space is: $$\kappa(t) = \frac{|\boldsymbol{\theta}'(t)\times\boldsymbol{\theta}''(t)|}{|\boldsymbol{\theta}'(t)|^3}$$

For gradient flow, $\boldsymbol{\theta}' = -\nabla\mathcal{L}$ and $\boldsymbol{\theta}'' = -H_\mathcal{L}\boldsymbol{\theta}' = H_\mathcal{L}\nabla\mathcal{L}$. The curvature is related to how the Hessian rotates the gradient — high curvature means the Hessian has large off-diagonal components relative to the gradient direction, causing the path to bend.

**Learning rate and curvature:** Too large a learning rate in gradient descent creates a discretisation that "overshoots" the smooth gradient flow curve, producing a high-curvature (oscillatory) trajectory. The stability condition $\eta < 2/\lambda_{\max}(H_\mathcal{L})$ is precisely the condition that keeps the discrete trajectory close to the smooth flow.

### 11.3 The Torsion of Training Trajectories

Torsion measures how a curve twists out of its osculating plane. In the context of training:

- **Zero torsion** would mean the training trajectory lies in a 2D plane in parameter space — the update directions stay in a fixed 2D subspace.
- **High torsion** means the update directions explore genuinely different subspaces over time — the optimiser is discovering new directions in parameter space.

Momentum methods have lower torsion than SGD (they smooth the trajectory); second-order methods are designed to have trajectories with curvature aligned to the loss landscape.

### 11.4 Stokes' Theorem and the Geometry of Optimiser Trajectories

Consider two optimisation paths from $\boldsymbol{\theta}_0$ to $\boldsymbol{\theta}^*$: gradient descent path $C_1$ and Adam path $C_2$. For any vector field $\mathbf{v}$ (representing the optimiser's update rule): $$\int_{C_1}\mathbf{v}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} - \int_{C_2}\mathbf{v}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} = \oint_{C_1 - C_2}\mathbf{v}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{v})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

by Stokes' theorem (where $S$ is any surface bounded by the closed curve $C_1 \cup (-C_2)$).

If $\mathbf{v} = \nabla\mathcal{L}$ (gradient descent), the curl is zero, so the integrals are equal — both paths descend the same total amount of loss (FTC). For Adam's non-conservative update field, the curl is non-zero and the surface integral measures the **difference in loss change** between the two paths.

### 11.5 Holonomy and Residual Connections

**Holonomy** is the failure of parallel transport around a closed loop to return to the original vector. In a curved space (non-zero curvature), parallel-transporting a vector around a closed loop rotates it by an amount equal to the integral of the curvature over the enclosed surface — by Stokes' theorem: $$\text{holonomy angle} \sim \iint_S K,dS = \oint_{\partial S} \omega_{\text{connection}}$$

This appears in deep learning in the analysis of **residual networks** as discretisations of differential equations on manifolds. The skip connections in ResNets ensure that the composition of layers approximates parallel transport with small holonomy — the network's output does not rotate wildly as depth increases.

More concretely: the **path dependence** of the vector line integral over non-conservative update fields is the reason why the order of layers in a network matters and why different initialisation paths to the same loss value can produce networks with very different generalisation properties.

### 11.6 Score Functions Along Stochastic Trajectories

In **diffusion models** (DDPM, Score SDE), the reverse process traces a stochastic trajectory $\mathbf{x}(t)$ in data space $\mathbb{R}^d$. The probability current is: $$\mathbf{J}(\mathbf{x},t) = \mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x},t),p_t(\mathbf{x})$$

Stokes' theorem applied to the probability flux gives the **circulation of probability current** around any closed loop $C$: $$\oint_C \mathbf{J}\cdot d\mathbf{x} = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{J})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

For an irrotational (curl-free) probability flux — which holds for equilibrium distributions — this is zero, confirming that the score field $\nabla\log p$ generates conservative dynamics (no probability "whirlpools").

### 11.7 Linking Numbers and Neural Network Topology

The **linking number** of two closed curves $C_1, C_2$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ (or equivalently in $S^3$) is: $$\text{lk}(C_1, C_2) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\oint_{C_1}\oint_{C_2} \frac{(\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2)}{|\mathbf{r}_1 - \mathbf{r}_2|^3}\cdot(d\mathbf{r}_1\times d\mathbf{r}_2)$$

This is a topological invariant — it does not change under continuous deformations that don't cross. In **topological data analysis (TDA)** applied to neural network representations, persistent homology computes topological invariants of activation manifolds. Linking numbers and related 1-cycle invariants in $H_1$ capture how decision boundaries "link" with data manifolds — a non-zero linking number means the boundary is topologically entangled with the data.

**In practice:** TDA tools compute these invariants to understand when a neural network's representations are topologically complex (high genus, many holes) and whether this correlates with generalisation. A decision boundary that "links" with the data manifold must pass through the data regardless of continuous deformations — it cannot be moved away without tearing, which imposes hard constraints on the classifier.
