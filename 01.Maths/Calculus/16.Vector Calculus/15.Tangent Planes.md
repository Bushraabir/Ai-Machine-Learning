

> _"The derivative is the shadow of a function cast by a linear light."_

## §0. Prefatory Remarks

The tangent _line_ to a curve at a point is the best linear approximation to the curve near that point. The **tangent plane** is the direct generalization: the best linear approximation to a surface at a point.

This is not merely a geometric curiosity. The tangent plane is the foundation of:

- **Linearization** and **error estimation** in multivariable calculus
- **Newton's method** in $\mathbb{R}^n$
- **Backpropagation** in deep learning (the gradient _is_ the tangent hyperplane)
- **Differential geometry** (the tangent space, the central object of manifold theory)
- **Optimization** (first-order methods live entirely in tangent spaces)

The passage from "curve → tangent line" to "surface → tangent plane" is a passage from single-variable to multivariable thinking. Everything that follows is making that passage precise.

## §1. Motivation — From Tangent Lines to Tangent Planes

Recall: if $y = f(x)$ and $f$ is differentiable at $x_0$, the tangent line at $(x_0, f(x_0))$ is

$$ y = f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x - x_0). $$

This line is characterized by two properties:

1. It _passes through_ $(x_0, f(x_0))$.
2. It has the _same slope_ as $f$ at $x_0$.

Equivalently: the error $f(x) - [f(x_0) + f'(x_0)(x-x_0)]$ is $o(x - x_0)$ as $x \to x_0$. The tangent line is the _unique_ linear function making the error smaller than linear.

**Now in two variables.** Given a surface $z = f(x,y)$, we want a plane through $(x_0, y_0, f(x_0,y_0))$ that best approximates $f$ near $(x_0,y_0)$. A plane through this point has the form:

$$ z = f(x_0, y_0) + A(x - x_0) + B(y - y_0) $$

for some constants $A, B$. The question is: what should $A$ and $B$ be?

**Answer:** $A = f_x(x_0, y_0)$ and $B = f_y(x_0, y_0)$.

_Why?_ Restrict to the $y = y_0$ slice: the surface becomes the curve $z = f(x, y_0)$, and the plane becomes the line $z = f(x_0,y_0) + A(x-x_0)$. For this to be the tangent line to the curve, we need $A = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\big|_{(x_0,y_0)}$. Same argument in the $x = x_0$ slice forces $B = f_y$.

## §2. Definition and Existence

**Definition 2.1 (Tangent Plane).** Let $f: U \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be defined on an open set $U$, and let $(x_0, y_0) \in U$. If the partial derivatives $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist at $(x_0, y_0)$, the _tangent plane_ to the surface $z = f(x,y)$ at the point $P_0 = (x_0, y_0, f(x_0,y_0))$ is the plane

$$ \boxed{z = f(x_0, y_0) + f_x(x_0,y_0)(x - x_0) + f_y(x_0,y_0)(y - y_0).} $$

**Critical Warning.** The existence of $f_x$ and $f_y$ does **not** guarantee the tangent plane is a good approximation. We need _differentiability_.

**Definition 2.2 (Differentiability).** $f$ is _differentiable_ at $(x_0, y_0)$ if

$$ \lim_{(h,k)\to(0,0)} \frac{f(x_0+h, y_0+k) - f(x_0,y_0) - f_x(x_0,y_0)h - f_y(x_0,y_0)k}{\sqrt{h^2+k^2}} = 0. $$

In words: the error between $f$ and its tangent plane is $o(|(\Delta x, \Delta y)|)$ — it goes to zero _faster_ than the distance to the point.

**Theorem 2.3.** If $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist _and are continuous_ at $(x_0, y_0)$, then $f$ is differentiable there.

_Proof sketch._ By the Mean Value Theorem applied twice: $$ f(x_0+h, y_0+k) - f(x_0,y_0) = f(x_0+h,y_0+k) - f(x_0,y_0+k) + f(x_0,y_0+k) - f(x_0,y_0) $$ $$ = f_x(x_0 + \xi_1 h, y_0+k)\cdot h + f_y(x_0, y_0 + \xi_2 k)\cdot k $$ for some $\xi_1, \xi_2 \in (0,1)$. Since $f_x$ and $f_y$ are continuous: $$ f_x(x_0+\xi_1 h, y_0+k) \to f_x(x_0,y_0), \quad f_y(x_0, y_0+\xi_2 k) \to f_y(x_0,y_0). $$ So the error term $\to 0$ faster than $\sqrt{h^2+k^2}$. $\blacksquare$

> **Remark — The Pathological Case.** Consider $f(x,y) = \frac{xy}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}$ at the origin (set $f(0,0)=0$). Both $f_x(0,0) = 0$ and $f_y(0,0) = 0$ exist, yet $f$ is **not** differentiable at the origin — the "tangent plane" $z=0$ is not a good approximation along the diagonal $y=x$. Partial derivatives alone are insufficient.

## §3. The Normal Vector and Equation of the Plane

The tangent plane $z - f(x_0,y_0) = f_x(x-x_0) + f_y(y-y_0)$ can be rewritten as

$$ f_x(x-x_0) + f_y(y-y_0) - (z - z_0) = 0, $$

which has the form $\mathbf{n} \cdot (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) = 0$ with

$$ \mathbf{n} = (f_x,; f_y,; -1). $$

This is the **normal vector** to the surface at $P_0$. The minus sign in the $z$-component is not a sign error — it comes from rewriting the plane equation.

**Equivalently**, if the surface is presented as a level set $F(x,y,z) = 0$ where $F(x,y,z) = f(x,y) - z$, then

$$ \nabla F = (f_x, f_y, -1), $$

which is exactly $\mathbf{n}$. This generalizes beautifully.

**Theorem 3.1 (Tangent Plane to a Level Surface).** If $G: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ is $C^1$ and $\nabla G(P_0) \neq \mathbf{0}$, the tangent plane to the level surface $G(x,y,z) = c$ at $P_0 = (x_0,y_0,z_0)$ is

$$ G_x(P_0)(x-x_0) + G_y(P_0)(y-y_0) + G_z(P_0)(z-z_0) = 0. $$

_Proof._ Any smooth curve $\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t)$ lying in the surface satisfies $G(\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t)) = c$. Differentiating: $\nabla G \cdot \boldsymbol{\gamma}'(t) = 0$. So $\nabla G(P_0)$ is perpendicular to every tangent vector to every curve through $P_0$ on the surface. The tangent plane is spanned by all such tangent vectors, and $\nabla G(P_0)$ is its normal. $\blacksquare$

## §4. Tangent Planes to Parametric Surfaces

For a parametric surface $\mathbf{r}(u,v) = (x(u,v), y(u,v), z(u,v))$, the tangent vectors along the parameter curves are $\mathbf{r}_u$ and $\mathbf{r}_v$. The tangent plane at $\mathbf{r}(u_0, v_0)$ is spanned by these two vectors, and the normal is their cross product.

**Definition 4.1.** The tangent plane to $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$ at $(u_0,v_0)$ is the plane through $\mathbf{r}(u_0,v_0)$ with normal

$$ \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{r}_u(u_0,v_0) \times \mathbf{r}_v(u_0,v_0), $$

provided $\mathbf{n} \neq \mathbf{0}$ (the _regular point_ condition).

**Connection to surfaces of revolution.** From our previous note, $\mathbf{r}(x,\theta) = (x, f\cos\theta, f\sin\theta)$ gives

$$ \mathbf{r}_x = (1, f'\cos\theta, f'\sin\theta), \quad \mathbf{r}_\theta = (0, -f\sin\theta, f\cos\theta), $$

$$ \mathbf{n} = \mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta = (f'f,; -f\cos\theta,; -f\sin\theta) = f\cdot(f', -\cos\theta, -\sin\theta). $$

So the unit normal is $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1+(f')^2}}(f', -\cos\theta, -\sin\theta)$. The tangent plane at any point on a surface of revolution is perpendicular to the $r\theta$-plane — as expected from the rotational symmetry.

## §5. Linearization — The Tangent Plane as Approximation

**Definition 5.1 (Linear Approximation / Linearization).** If $f$ is differentiable at $(x_0,y_0)$, the _linearization_ of $f$ at $(x_0,y_0)$ is

$$ L(x,y) = f(x_0,y_0) + f_x(x_0,y_0)(x-x_0) + f_y(x_0,y_0)(y-y_0). $$

The _total differential_ is $$ df = f_x, dx + f_y, dy. $$

**Error Bound.** If $|f_{xx}|, |f_{xy}|, |f_{yy}| \leq M$ on a disk of radius $d$ around $(x_0,y_0)$, then

$$ |f(x,y) - L(x,y)| \leq \frac{M}{2}\left(|x-x_0| + |y-y_0|\right)^2. $$

This is the multivariable Taylor remainder estimate — the tangent plane has _second-order_ error.

### Example 5.1 — Approximating $\sqrt{(1.01)^2 + (1.99)^2}$

Let $f(x,y) = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$. Expand near $(1, 2)$:

$$ f_x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}, \quad f_y = \frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}. $$

At $(1,2)$: $f(1,2) = \sqrt{5}$, $f_x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}$, $f_y = \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}}$.

$$ L(x,y) = \sqrt{5} + \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(x-1) + \frac{2}{\sqrt{5}}(y-2). $$

At $(1.01, 1.99)$: $\Delta x = 0.01$, $\Delta y = -0.01$:

$$ L(1.01, 1.99) = \sqrt{5} + \frac{0.01}{\sqrt{5}} - \frac{0.02}{\sqrt{5}} = \sqrt{5} - \frac{0.01}{\sqrt{5}} \approx 2.2361 - 0.00447 \approx 2.2316. $$

Exact: $\sqrt{(1.01)^2 + (1.99)^2} = \sqrt{1.0201 + 3.9601} = \sqrt{4.9802} \approx 2.2317$. ✓

## §6. Worked Examples

### Example 6.1 — Paraboloid

Find the tangent plane to $z = x^2 + 2y^2$ at $(1, 1, 3)$.

$$ f_x = 2x \big|_{(1,1)} = 2, \quad f_y = 4y\big|_{(1,1)} = 4. $$

$$ z = 3 + 2(x-1) + 4(y-1) = 2x + 4y - 3. $$

**Normal vector:** $\mathbf{n} = (2, 4, -1)$.

### Example 6.2 — Sphere

Find the tangent plane to $x^2 + y^2 + z^2 = 14$ at $(1, 2, 3)$.

Let $G(x,y,z) = x^2+y^2+z^2$. Then $\nabla G = (2x, 2y, 2z)$, so $\nabla G(1,2,3) = (2,4,6)$.

Tangent plane: $2(x-1) + 4(y-2) + 6(z-3) = 0 \implies x + 2y + 3z = 14$.

_Geometric sanity check:_ The normal to a sphere at any point is radial — pointing from the origin to the point. Indeed, $\mathbf{n} = (1,2,3)$ is the position vector. ✓

### Example 6.3 — Implicit Surface

Find the tangent plane to $xyz = 6$ at $(1, 2, 3)$.

$G(x,y,z) = xyz$. $\nabla G = (yz, xz, xy)$. At $(1,2,3)$: $\nabla G = (6, 3, 2)$.

Tangent plane: $6(x-1) + 3(y-2) + 2(z-3) = 0 \implies 6x + 3y + 2z = 18$.

### Example 6.4 — Parametric Surface (Torus)

The torus: $\mathbf{r}(\phi, \theta) = ((R + r\cos\phi)\cos\theta,; (R+r\cos\phi)\sin\theta,; r\sin\phi)$.

$$ \mathbf{r}_\phi = (-r\sin\phi\cos\theta,; -r\sin\phi\sin\theta,; r\cos\phi), $$ $$ \mathbf{r}_\theta = (-(R+r\cos\phi)\sin\theta,; (R+r\cos\phi)\cos\theta,; 0). $$

The normal $\mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta$ at any point gives the outward normal to the torus. At $\phi=0, \theta=0$ (outermost point $(R+r, 0, 0)$): $\mathbf{r}_\phi = (0,0,r)$, $\mathbf{r}_\theta = (0, R+r, 0)$, so $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta = (-r(R+r), 0, 0)$, pointing in the $-x$ direction. Tangent plane is $x = R+r$ — vertical, as expected.

## §8. The Total Differential and Error Propagation

In applications, the total differential quantifies how errors in inputs propagate to outputs.

**Setup.** Suppose $z = f(x,y)$ and we measure $x, y$ with errors $\Delta x, \Delta y$. The resulting error in $z$ is approximately:

$$ \Delta z \approx dz = f_x, \Delta x + f_y, \Delta y. $$

**Example 8.1.** The period of a pendulum is $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$. Suppose $L$ is measured with $1%$ error and $g$ with $0.5%$ error. What is the error in $T$?

$$ \frac{\partial T}{\partial L} = \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{Lg}}, \quad \frac{\partial T}{\partial g} = -\frac{\pi\sqrt{L}}{g^{3/2}}. $$

$$ dT = \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{Lg}} dL - \frac{\pi\sqrt{L}}{g^{3/2}} dg = T\left(\frac{dL}{2L} - \frac{dg}{2g}\right). $$

Relative error: $\frac{|dT|}{T} \leq \frac{1}{2}\cdot 1% + \frac{1}{2}\cdot 0.5% = 0.75%$.

## §9. Tangent Space — The Differential-Geometric View

In differential geometry, the _tangent plane_ becomes the **tangent space** $T_{P_0}S$ — a vector space of all tangent vectors to $S$ at $P_0$.

**Definition 9.1.** For a smooth surface $S \subset \mathbb{R}^3$, the _tangent space_ at $P_0 \in S$ is

$$ T_{P_0}S = {\boldsymbol{\gamma}'(0) : \boldsymbol{\gamma}: (-\epsilon,\epsilon) \to S \text{ smooth}, \boldsymbol{\gamma}(0) = P_0}. $$

This is a 2-dimensional real vector space.

**Why "tangent space" not "tangent plane"?** The tangent space is a vector space anchored at the origin (it's been translated so $P_0$ is the origin). The tangent _plane_ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is the affine subspace $P_0 + T_{P_0}S$.

**For a manifold.** On a smooth $n$-manifold $M$, the tangent space $T_pM$ at every point $p$ is an $n$-dimensional vector space. This generalizes the tangent plane to arbitrary dimensions — the foundation for all of modern differential geometry and theoretical machine learning.

## §10. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 10.1 The Gradient as Normal to Level Sets

In optimization, we minimize $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ over parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^n$. The key geometric fact: $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ is **normal to the level set** ${\boldsymbol{\theta}: \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = c}$ (the _loss contour_).

This is exactly Theorem 3.1. The level set is a hypersurface in parameter space; the gradient is its normal vector. Gradient descent steps in the normal direction to the current loss contour — the direction of steepest ascent/descent.

The **tangent hyperplane** to the loss landscape at $\boldsymbol{\theta}_0$ is:

$$ \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \approx \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0) + \nabla\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0)^T (\boldsymbol{\theta} - \boldsymbol{\theta}_0). $$

This is exactly the linearization formula of §5, now in $n$ dimensions. **Every first-order optimizer (SGD, Adam, RMSProp) is doing tangent plane approximation.**

### 10.2 Backpropagation is Iterated Chain Rule on Tangent Spaces

The chain rule for differentiation — the engine of backpropagation — is exactly the statement that the tangent map (derivative) of a composition is the composition of tangent maps:

$$ D(g \circ f)(x) = Dg(f(x)) \circ Df(x). $$

In coordinates: if $\mathbf{z} = f(\mathbf{x})$ and $L = g(\mathbf{z})$, then

$$ \frac{\partial L}{\partial x_i} = \sum_j \frac{\partial L}{\partial z_j}\frac{\partial z_j}{\partial x_i}. $$

Each $\frac{\partial z_j}{\partial x_i}$ is an entry of the **Jacobian matrix** $Df$ — the matrix representation of the tangent map. **Backprop is the recursive computation of tangent maps through the network's computational graph.**

### 10.3 Newton's Method and Second-Order Optimization

First-order (gradient) methods use the tangent plane. _Second-order_ methods use the **osculating paraboloid** — the second-order Taylor approximation:

$$ \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \approx \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0) + \nabla\mathcal{L}^T \Delta\boldsymbol{\theta} + \frac{1}{2}\Delta\boldsymbol{\theta}^T H \Delta\boldsymbol{\theta}, $$

where $H = \nabla^2\mathcal{L}$ is the **Hessian**. Newton's step minimizes this quadratic: $\Delta\boldsymbol{\theta} = -H^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}$.

The geometry: $H$ measures how fast the tangent plane _tilts_ as you move along the surface — it encodes curvature. **K-FAC, Shampoo, and other second-order optimizers** approximate $H^{-1}$ efficiently to go beyond the tangent plane approximation.

### 10.4 Implicit Neural Representations (INRs)

A neural network $F_\theta: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ can represent a surface implicitly as ${F_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = 0}$ (e.g., NeRF, DeepSDF, NeuralRBF). The tangent plane at a surface point $\mathbf{x}_0$ is then

$$ \nabla_x F_\theta(\mathbf{x}_0) \cdot (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_0) = 0, $$

exactly Theorem 3.1. The network's gradient with respect to _input_ coordinates (not parameters) gives the surface normal — used for shading, collision detection, and physical simulation in neural rendering.

### 10.5 Tangent Spaces in Manifold Learning

The manifold hypothesis says data ${x_i} \subset \mathbb{R}^D$ lies near a $d$-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M}$ with $d \ll D$. Methods like **PCA (local)**, **UMAP**, and **Isomap** all estimate the tangent space $T_{x_i}\mathcal{M}$ at each data point — the $d$-dimensional plane that best fits the data locally.

Local PCA at $x_i$: compute the top-$d$ eigenvectors of the local covariance matrix. These eigenvectors _are_ a basis for the estimated tangent space. The tangent plane formula gives the first-order approximation to the manifold:

$$ \mathcal{M} \approx x_i + \text{span}(v_1, \ldots, v_d) \quad \text{near } x_i. $$

### 10.6 Riemannian Optimization

Modern ML encounters optimization on _matrix manifolds_: orthogonal matrices $O(n)$, positive definite matrices $\text{PD}(n)$, Grassmannians, Stiefel manifolds. Standard gradient descent ignores the manifold constraint and steps off the manifold.

**Riemannian gradient descent** projects the Euclidean gradient onto the tangent space at the current point, then uses a _retraction_ (exponential map) to move along the manifold:

$$ \boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \text{Retr}_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_t}!\left(-\eta, \text{grad}_R \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)\right), $$

where $\text{grad}_R \mathcal{L} = P_{T_{\boldsymbol{\theta}_t}\mathcal{M}}(\nabla \mathcal{L})$ is the Riemannian gradient — the projection of $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ onto the tangent plane. **Without tangent planes, Riemannian optimization is impossible.**

**11.5.** _[ML connection]_ Let $\mathcal{L}(\theta_1, \theta_2) = \frac{1}{2}(\theta_1^2 + 4\theta_2^2)$ (an elongated paraboloid). Write the tangent plane at $\boldsymbol{\theta}_0 = (2, 1)$. In which direction does gradient descent step? Show this is the normal to the level curve $\mathcal{L} = 4$.

## §12. Summary

|Situation|Tangent plane / normal|
|---|---|
|$z = f(x,y)$, explicit|$z = z_0 + f_x(x-x_0) + f_y(y-y_0)$; normal $(f_x, f_y, -1)$|
|$G(x,y,z) = c$, level set|$\nabla G(P_0)\cdot(\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}_0) = 0$; normal $\nabla G(P_0)$|
|$\mathbf{r}(u,v)$, parametric|spanned by $\mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v$; normal $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$|
|$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$, loss function|linearization $L(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0) + \nabla\mathcal{L}^T\Delta\boldsymbol{\theta}$; normal $\nabla\mathcal{L}$|

**The central theorem:** For $C^1$ functions, partial derivatives $\Rightarrow$ differentiability $\Rightarrow$ the tangent plane is a valid linear approximation with quadratic error. Partial derivatives alone are not enough.

**The key principle:** The tangent plane linearizes. Everything in first-order calculus — chain rule, gradient descent, backpropagation, linearization — is a version of the same idea: replace a nonlinear object locally by its tangent plane, work in the plane (where linear algebra applies), then return to the nonlinear world.
