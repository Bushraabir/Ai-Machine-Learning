

> _"Green's theorem is not one theorem. It is two theorems wearing the same coat. The tangential form says: circulation around a boundary equals the curl through the interior. The normal form says: flux through a boundary equals the divergence of the interior. They are the same algebraic identity, rotated ninety degrees, and both are special cases of Stokes' theorem in disguise."_

---

## §1. Motivation — Two Questions About a Planar Field

Let $\mathbf{F} = \langle P(x,y), Q(x,y) \rangle$ be a $C^1$ vector field on a simply connected region $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$, with boundary $C = \partial D$ oriented counterclockwise (positive orientation).

There are exactly two natural scalar quantities you can form from $\mathbf{F}$ and a boundary curve $C$:

1. **Circulation:** $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \oint_C P,dx + Q,dy$ — how much does $\mathbf{F}$ flow _along_ $C$?
2. **Flux:** $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds$ — how much does $\mathbf{F}$ flow _through_ $C$, outward?

Green's theorem in its two vector forms answers both questions in terms of interior integrals:

- **Circulation = $\iint_D$ (curl of $\mathbf{F}$)** — the tangential / circulation form
- **Flux = $\iint_D$ (divergence of $\mathbf{F}$)** — the normal / flux form

These are not two separate theorems — they are the same identity applied to the same field $\mathbf{F}$, or equivalently, applied to the rotated field $\mathbf{F}^\perp$. Understanding why they are the same identity, written twice, is the conceptual key to the whole subject.

---

## §2. Setup and Notation

Throughout: $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ is a bounded, simply connected region with piecewise $C^1$ boundary $C = \partial D$. The boundary is oriented **counterclockwise** (the interior is to the left as you traverse $C$).

Parametrize $C$ by arc length $s$ or by a parameter $t$. The two natural unit vectors on $C$:

$$\hat{\mathbf{T}} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{ds} = \langle \dot{x}, \dot{y}\rangle \quad\text{(unit tangent, counterclockwise)}$$

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \langle \dot{y}, -\dot{x}\rangle \quad\text{(outward unit normal, obtained by rotating $\hat{\mathbf{T}}$ clockwise by $90°$)}$$

> **Why $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \langle \dot{y}, -\dot{x}\rangle$?** If $\hat{\mathbf{T}} = \langle \dot{x}, \dot{y}\rangle$, rotating $90°$ clockwise gives $\langle \dot{y}, -\dot{x}\rangle$. For counterclockwise orientation of $C$, this rotation points _outward_ from $D$. Check: on the right side of a rectangle (traversed upward, $\dot{x} = 0, \dot{y} = 1$), $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \langle 1, 0\rangle$ — pointing right, i.e., outward. $\checkmark$

---

## §3. The Scalar Statement (Reference)

The classical statement of Green's theorem:

$$\oint_{\partial D} P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA$$

This is the starting point. Both vector forms are obtained by choosing $P, Q$ appropriately or by rewriting the line integral in terms of $\mathbf{F}$, $\hat{\mathbf{T}}$, and $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

---

## §4. Form I — The Tangential (Circulation / Curl) Form

### 4.1 Statement

$$\boxed{\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{k},dA = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA}$$

The **circulation** of $\mathbf{F}$ around the boundary equals the integral of the (scalar) **2D curl** over the interior.

### 4.2 Proof

The line integral $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ uses the tangent vector:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{T}},ds = \oint_C \langle P, Q\rangle\cdot\langle dx, dy\rangle = \oint_C P,dx + Q,dy$$

Apply the scalar Green's theorem:

$$\oint_C P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA$$

The integrand $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ is precisely the $z$-component of $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ for the planar field $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, 0\rangle$:

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{k} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$$

So:

$$\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{k},dA \quad\square$$

### 4.3 Geometric Meaning

The curl $(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{k}$ measures the **local counterclockwise rotation** of $\mathbf{F}$ at each point. Integrating it over $D$ accumulates all the local rotation. The theorem says this total local rotation equals the global circulation around the boundary.

**Intuition via tiling:** Tile $D$ with tiny squares. The circulation around each tiny square contributes $(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}),\Delta A$ (by the intrinsic definition of curl). When adjacent squares share an edge, the contribution from that shared edge cancels — adjacent squares are traversed in opposite directions. Only the boundary edges survive. Summing over all tiles gives the total boundary circulation. This is Green's theorem in its most transparent form.

```
  ┌──┬──┬──┐
  │→↑│→↑│→↑│   Each tiny square contributes
  ├──┼──┼──┤   curl · ΔA to the sum.
  │→↑│→↑│→↑│   Interior edges cancel pairwise.
  ├──┼──┼──┤   Only the outer boundary survives.
  │→↑│→↑│→↑│
  └──┴──┴──┘
  
  ∮_C F·dr = ∑_tiles (curl F) ΔA = ∬_D curl F dA
```

### 4.4 Special Case: Irrotational Fields

If $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ on $D$ (irrotational / conservative), then: $$\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$$

Zero curl everywhere $\Rightarrow$ zero circulation around every simple closed curve. This is the analytical content of path-independence for conservative fields.

---

## §5. Form II — The Normal (Flux / Divergence) Form

### 5.1 Statement

$$\boxed{\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \iint_D \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F},dA = \iint_D\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}\right)dA}$$

The **outward flux** of $\mathbf{F}$ through the boundary equals the integral of the **divergence** over the interior.

### 5.2 Proof

Express the flux integral in coordinates. With $C$ parametrized as $(x(t), y(t))$, $dt$ from $a$ to $b$, and $\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \langle \dot{y}, -\dot{x}\rangle,dt$:

$$\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \oint_C\langle P, Q\rangle\cdot\langle \dot{y}, -\dot{x}\rangle,dt = \oint_C P,dy - Q,dx$$

Apply the scalar Green's theorem with the substitution $P \leftarrow -Q$ and $Q \leftarrow P$:

$$\oint_C P,dy - Q,dx = \oint_C (-Q),dx + P,dy = \iint_D\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(-Q)}{\partial y}\right)dA = \iint_D\left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}\right)dA$$

The integrand $\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}$ is exactly $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$. So:

$$\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \iint_D\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F},dA \quad\square$$

### 5.3 Geometric Meaning

$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$ measures the **local outward expansion** of $\mathbf{F}$ at each point. Integrating it over $D$ accumulates all local source/sink strength. The theorem says this total source strength equals the net outward flux through the boundary.

This is the **2D Divergence Theorem** — the $2D$ special case of the full $3D$ Divergence theorem, restricted to planar fields.

**Intuition via tiling:** Same tiling argument. Each tiny square contributes $(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}),\Delta A$ net outward flux. Interior edges cancel. Only boundary flux survives.

```
  ┌──┬──┬──┐
  │↗·│↗·│↗·│   Each tiny square contributes
  ├──┼──┼──┤   div F · ΔA net outward flux.
  │↗·│↗·│↗·│   Interior face fluxes cancel.
  ├──┼──┼──┤   Boundary face fluxes survive.
  │↗·│↗·│↗·│
  └──┴──┴──┘
  
  ∮_C F·n̂ ds = ∑_tiles (div F) ΔA = ∬_D div F dA
```

### 5.4 Special Case: Divergence-Free Fields

If $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 0$ on $D$ (solenoidal), then: $$\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = 0$$

Zero divergence everywhere $\Rightarrow$ zero net outward flux through every simple closed curve. The field has no sources or sinks — every line that enters the region must exit it.

## §6. The Unifying Symmetry — One Identity, Rotated

The two forms are not coincidentally similar. They are **the same identity**, applied to $\mathbf{F}$ and its $90°$-rotation.

Define the **clockwise rotation** of $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q\rangle$: $$\mathbf{F}^\perp = \langle Q, -P\rangle$$

(Rotate each vector $90°$ clockwise.)

**Claim:** The normal form for $\mathbf{F}$ equals the tangential form for $\mathbf{F}^\perp$.

**Proof.**

Tangential form for $\mathbf{F}^\perp = \langle Q, -P\rangle$:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}^\perp\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \oint_C Q,dx + (-P),dy = -\oint_C P,dy - Q,dx = -\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds$$

(using the relation $\oint_C P,dy - Q,dx = \oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds$ from §5.2.)

The curl of $\mathbf{F}^\perp$: $$\frac{\partial(-P)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} = -\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$$

So the tangential form for $\mathbf{F}^\perp$ gives: $$-\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \iint_D(-\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}),dA$$

Multiply by $-1$: $$\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \iint_D\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F},dA \quad\square$$

**Summary of the rotation correspondence:**

|Quantity|For $\mathbf{F}$|For $\mathbf{F}^\perp = \langle Q, -P\rangle$|
|---|---|---|
|Integrand on $C$|$\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{T}}$ (tangential)|$\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ (normal)|
|Interior integrand|$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = Q_x - P_y$ (curl)|$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = P_x + Q_y$ (divergence)|
|Green's form|Circulation = curl|Flux = divergence|

The two theorems are one theorem, seen from two orientations.

## §7. Full Proof of the Scalar Green's Theorem

For completeness — the proof from which both vector forms follow.

> **Theorem (Green).** If $D$ is a region bounded by a piecewise $C^1$ simple closed curve $C = \partial D$ oriented counterclockwise, and $P, Q \in C^1(\bar{D})$, then: $$\oint_C P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D\left(Q_x - P_y\right)dA$$

**Step 1: Reduction.** It suffices to prove separately:

$$\oint_C P,dx = -\iint_D P_y,dA \qquad\text{...(I)}$$ $$\oint_C Q,dy = \iint_D Q_x,dA \qquad\text{...(II)}$$

Adding these gives the theorem.

**Step 2: Prove (I) for a vertically simple region.**

Assume $D = {(x,y) : a \leq x \leq b,; g_1(x) \leq y \leq g_2(x)}$.

Compute $-\iint_D P_y,dA$: $$-\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y},dA = -\int_a^b\int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)}\frac{\partial P}{\partial y},dy,dx = -\int_a^b \left[P(x, g_2(x)) - P(x, g_1(x))\right]dx$$

Compute $\oint_C P,dx$ by decomposing $C$ into four pieces:

```
  y = g₂(x): top curve C₂ (right to left, so traversed as x goes b → a)
  y = g₁(x): bottom curve C₁ (left to right, x: a → b)
  x = a: left side (vertical, dx = 0)
  x = b: right side (vertical, dx = 0)
```

Vertical sides contribute zero (since $dx = 0$). So:

$$\oint_C P,dx = \int_{C_1} P,dx + \int_{C_2} P,dx$$

$$= \int_a^b P(x, g_1(x)),dx + \int_b^a P(x, g_2(x)),dx$$

$$= \int_a^b P(x, g_1(x)),dx - \int_a^b P(x, g_2(x)),dx$$

$$= -\int_a^b\left[P(x,g_2(x)) - P(x,g_1(x))\right]dx = -\iint_D P_y,dA \quad\checkmark$$

**Step 3: Prove (II) for a horizontally simple region** by the symmetric argument (integrating $Q_x$ in $x$ first).

**Step 4: General regions.** Cut $D$ along interior curves into pieces that are both vertically and horizontally simple. The integrals over internal cuts cancel (traversed in opposite directions by adjacent pieces). $\square$

## §8. Green's Identities — Consequences of the Normal Form

The normal form of Green's theorem, combined with the product rule $\nabla\cdot(f\nabla g) = f\nabla^2 g + \nabla f\cdot\nabla g$, yields two fundamental identities in PDE theory.

### 8.1 Green's First Identity

Set $\mathbf{F} = f\nabla g$ in the normal form $\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \iint_D\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F},dA$:

$$\oint_{\partial D} f,\frac{\partial g}{\partial n},ds = \iint_D \left(f\nabla^2 g + \nabla f\cdot\nabla g\right)dA$$

where $\dfrac{\partial g}{\partial n} = \nabla g\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is the **outward normal derivative** of $g$.

Rearranging: $$\iint_D f\nabla^2 g,dA = \oint_{\partial D} f,\frac{\partial g}{\partial n},ds - \iint_D\nabla f\cdot\nabla g,dA$$

This is **integration by parts** for the Laplacian — the 2D analog of $\int u,v'',dx = [u,v']_a^b - \int u',v',dx$.

**Application — Uniqueness of Laplace's equation:** If $\nabla^2 u = 0$ in $D$ and $u = 0$ on $\partial D$, set $f = g = u$:

$$0 = \oint_{\partial D} u,\frac{\partial u}{\partial n},ds - \iint_D|\nabla u|^2,dA$$

The boundary term vanishes (since $u = 0$ on $\partial D$), so $\iint_D|\nabla u|^2,dA = 0$, giving $\nabla u = \mathbf{0}$, hence $u \equiv 0$. $\square$

### 8.2 Green's Second Identity (Green's Theorem in the Classical Sense)

Subtract the first identity with $f, g$ swapped:

$$\iint_D\left(f\nabla^2 g - g\nabla^2 f\right)dA = \oint_{\partial D}\left(f,\frac{\partial g}{\partial n} - g,\frac{\partial f}{\partial n}\right)ds$$

This is **Green's second identity** — symmetric in $f$ and $g$. It is the foundation of boundary integral methods, the Fredholm alternative, and the proof that eigenvalues of the Laplacian are real.

**Application — Representation formula:** Setting $g = G(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}_0)$ (the Green's function of the Laplacian) recovers the value of $f$ at any interior point from its boundary values — the foundation of potential theory.

## §9. Worked Examples

### Example A — Direct Verification: Tangential Form

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x\rangle$, $D$ the disk $x^2+y^2 \leq 1$.**

**Via Green's theorem:** $$Q_x - P_y = \frac{\partial x}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(-y)}{\partial y} = 1 + 1 = 2$$

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D 2,dA = 2\cdot\pi(1)^2 = 2\pi$$

**Direct computation (check):** Parametrize $C$: $x = \cos t$, $y = \sin t$, $t\in[0, 2\pi]$.

$$\oint_C -y,dx + x,dy = \int_0^{2\pi}(-\sin t)(-\sin t),dt + \cos t(\cos t),dt = \int_0^{2\pi}(\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t),dt = 2\pi \quad\checkmark$$

---

### Example B — Direct Verification: Normal Form

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle x, y\rangle$, $D$ the rectangle $[0,2]\times[0,3]$.**

**Via Green's theorem:** $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 1 + 1 = 2$$

$$\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \iint_D 2,dA = 2\cdot(2\cdot 3) = 12$$

**Direct check:** On each side of the rectangle, compute $\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$:

- Bottom ($y=0$, $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = -\mathbf{j}$): $\langle x, 0\rangle\cdot\langle 0,-1\rangle = 0$. Integral = $0$.
- Top ($y=3$, $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \mathbf{j}$): $\langle x, 3\rangle\cdot\langle 0,1\rangle = 3$. Integral = $3\cdot 2 = 6$.
- Left ($x=0$, $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = -\mathbf{i}$): $\langle 0, y\rangle\cdot\langle -1,0\rangle = 0$. Integral = $0$.
- Right ($x=2$, $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \mathbf{i}$): $\langle 2, y\rangle\cdot\langle 1,0\rangle = 2$. Integral = $2\cdot 3 = 6$.

Total: $0 + 6 + 0 + 6 = 12$. $\checkmark$

---

### Example C — Area via Green's Theorem

One of the most elegant applications. The area of $D$ can be computed as a line integral.

From Form I with $\mathbf{F} = \langle 0, x\rangle$: $Q_x - P_y = 1 - 0 = 1$. $$A(D) = \iint_D 1,dA = \oint_{\partial D} x,dy$$

From $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, 0\rangle$: $Q_x - P_y = 0 - (-1) = 1$. $$A(D) = -\oint_{\partial D} y,dx$$

Averaging: $$\boxed{A(D) = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\partial D}(x,dy - y,dx)}$$

**Application — Area of an ellipse $x = a\cos t$, $y = b\sin t$:**

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(a\cos t\cdot b\cos t - b\sin t\cdot(-a\sin t)),dt = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}ab,dt = \pi ab \quad\checkmark$$

**Application — Shoelace formula for a polygon with vertices $(x_1,y_1),\ldots,(x_n,y_n)$:**

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\left|\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i y_{i+1} - x_{i+1}y_i)\right|$$

This is Green's theorem applied to a piecewise-linear boundary.

---

### Example D — Counterintuitive: Nonzero Curl, Zero Circulation

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle xy, 0\rangle$, $D$ the unit square $[0,1]\times[0,1]$.**

**Curl:** $Q_x - P_y = 0 - x = -x$. Not zero everywhere.

**Circulation:** $$\oint_C\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D(-x),dA = -\int_0^1\int_0^1 x,dy,dx = -\int_0^1 x,dx = -\frac{1}{2}$$

So circulation is $-1/2$ — not zero, even though the field has components that look simple. The negative value means the net circulation is clockwise, even though $C$ is oriented counterclockwise.

---

### Example E — Using the Tangential Form to Simplify a Hard Line Integral

**Compute $\oint_C (e^x + y^2),dx + (e^y + x^2),dy$ where $C$ is the unit circle, counterclockwise.**

Direct parametrization is messy. Instead:

$P = e^x + y^2$, $Q = e^y + x^2$.

$$Q_x - P_y = 2x - 2y$$

$$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_{x^2+y^2\leq 1}(2x - 2y),dA$$

By symmetry: $\iint_{D}x,dA = 0$ (odd function over symmetric region) and $\iint_D y,dA = 0$.

$$= 2\iint_D x,dA - 2\iint_D y,dA = 0 - 0 = 0$$

Green's theorem reduced a nasty line integral to a symmetry argument.

---

### Example F — Green's Theorem for Regions with Holes

**$D$ is the annulus $1 \leq x^2+y^2 \leq 4$, $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y/(x^2+y^2),; x/(x^2+y^2)\rangle$.**

The boundary $\partial D$ has **two** components: the outer circle $C_2$ (radius 2, counterclockwise) and the inner circle $C_1$ (radius 1, **clockwise** — because the hole's boundary is traversed so that $D$ is to the left).

Compute curl: for $r \neq 0$, $$Q_x - P_y = \frac{y^2-x^2}{r^4} + \frac{y^2-x^2}{r^4} ;\to; \text{careful computation gives } Q_x - P_y = 0$$

(This field is the classic $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\theta$, irrotational away from origin.)

So: $$\oint_{C_2}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} + \oint_{C_1^-}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D 0,dA = 0$$

$$\oint_{C_2}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \oint_{C_1}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

(both traversed counterclockwise). The circulation on both circles is the same — it equals $2\pi$ on any circle around the origin. **This is the topological obstruction**: the field has zero curl but nonzero circulation because the domain is not simply connected.

```
  At each point on C, the field F splits into two components:

              F
             ↗

           /

### 10.3 The Two Forms as Two Projections

```
                    F at each boundary point
                           ↗ (F)
                          /
                         / θ
        ────────────────●──────────────  C (boundary)
                        │
                        │

  T̂ projection (along boundary):   F·T̂ = |F|cos θ
  n̂ projection (through boundary): F·n̂ = |F|sin θ

  Form I:  ∮ F·T̂ ds  =  ∬ (Q_x - P_y) dA   [CURL form]
  Form II: ∮ F·n̂ ds  =  ∬ (P_x + Q_y) dA   [DIV form]

  One measures the "spinning" part of F on C.
  The other measures the "breathing" part of F on C.

### 10.4 Green's Theorem as 2D Stokes and 2D Divergence

```
  FULL HIERARCHY:

  2D:  ∮_{∂D} F·dr  = ∬_D (∇×F)·k dA      ← Green (tangential)   = Stokes in 2D
  3D:  ∮_{∂S} F·dr  = ∬_S (∇×F)·dS         ← Stokes' theorem
                                               (Green is special case: S flat, n = k)

  2D:  ∮_{∂D} F·n̂ ds = ∬_D ∇·F dA          ← Green (normal)       = Divergence thm in 2D
  3D:  ∯_{∂E} F·dS   = ∭_E ∇·F dV          ← Divergence theorem
                                               (Green is special case: E slab, integrate in z)
```

## §11. Relationship to the Three Fundamental Theorems

All of the major integral theorems of vector calculus are instances of the **same abstract theorem**:

$$\int_{\partial\Omega}\omega = \int_\Omega d\omega$$

"The integral of a differential form over the boundary of a manifold equals the integral of its exterior derivative over the manifold."

|Theorem|$\Omega$|$\partial\Omega$|$\omega$|$d\omega$|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|**FTC**|$[a,b] \subset \mathbb{R}$|${a, b}$|$f$|$f',dx$|
|**FTLI**|curve $C$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$|endpoints|$\nabla f\cdot d\mathbf{r}$|—|
|**Green (tangential)**|region $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$|$\partial D$|$P,dx + Q,dy$|$(Q_x-P_y),dA$|
|**Green (normal)**|region $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$|$\partial D$|$P,dy - Q,dx$|$(P_x+Q_y),dA$|
|**Stokes**|surface $S \subset \mathbb{R}^3$|$\partial S$|$\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$|$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$|
|**Divergence**|solid $E \subset \mathbb{R}^3$|$\partial E$|$\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$|$(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}),dV$|

Green's theorem is the first truly two-dimensional case. Its two forms are the two ways you can write a $1$-form in $\mathbb{R}^2$: tangentially along the boundary, or normally through it.

## §12. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> The two vector forms of Green's theorem — one involving curl, one involving divergence — appear in ML wherever you find **2D latent spaces, planar flows, graph topology, or discrete Hodge theory**. They are also the gateway to understanding why the full 3D theorems matter in ML (see the Curl and Divergence notes). Here we focus on what is specific to the 2D / planar structure.

### 12.1 Planar Normalizing Flows — 2D Divergence Form

A **2D normalizing flow** $\mathbf{g}_\theta: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ transforms a 2D density $p_Z$ to $p_X$. The change-of-variables formula requires $\log|\det J_\mathbf{g}|$. In 2D, the Jacobian is $2\times 2$:

$$\det J_\mathbf{g} = \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial x}\frac{\partial g_2}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial y}\frac{\partial g_2}{\partial x}$$

For an **infinitesimal** flow $\mathbf{g} = \text{id} + \varepsilon\mathbf{F}$:

$$\det J_\mathbf{g} \approx 1 + \varepsilon\left(\frac{\partial F_1}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial F_2}{\partial y}\right) = 1 + \varepsilon,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$$

The **divergence of the flow field** controls the first-order change in log-density. The Green's theorem normal form gives the global version: the total log-density change in a region $D$ is related to the boundary flux. This is the 2D instantiation of the FFJORD formula — and the reason that **divergence-free 2D flows are volume-preserving**: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 0 \Rightarrow \det J = 1$ to first order.

### 12.2 GAN Training Dynamics — 2D Curl Form and Cycling

In a **two-player GAN** with generator parameter $\theta_G \in \mathbb{R}$ and discriminator $\theta_D \in \mathbb{R}$ (the 2D case for intuition), the training dynamics form a vector field in $(\theta_G, \theta_D)$-space:

$$\mathbf{v} = \left(-\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial\theta_G},; \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial\theta_D}\right)$$

The **2D curl** (scalar curl) of this field:

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{v})_z = \frac{\partial v_2}{\partial\theta_G} - \frac{\partial v_1}{\partial\theta_D} = \frac{\partial^2\mathcal{L}}{\partial\theta_G\partial\theta_D} + \frac{\partial^2\mathcal{L}}{\partial\theta_D\partial\theta_G} = 2\mathcal{L}_{\theta_G\theta_D}$$

(the mixed partial derivative of the loss — nonzero for adversarial objectives).

By Green's theorem tangential form:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{v}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} = \iint_D (\nabla\times\mathbf{v})_z,dA = \iint_D 2\mathcal{L}_{\theta_G\theta_D},dA$$

A nonzero circulation integral means gradient descent **cycles** around the equilibrium rather than converging to it. The magnitude of the circulation, given by Green's theorem as the integral of the 2D curl, quantifies how much the training dynamics rotate — and thus how badly vanilla GD will cycle.

**SGA / CGD** algorithms explicitly subtract the curl component from the gradient vector field, making $\nabla\times\mathbf{v} = 0$ (irrotational dynamics) and hence zero circulation — ensuring convergence to the equilibrium rather than cycling. This is, literally, using Green's theorem to diagnose and fix the training.

### 12.3 Area Computation in Latent Space — Shoelace Formula

The area formula $A = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\partial D}(x,dy - y,dx)$ (§9, Example C) has a direct ML application: computing the **area of a 2D latent region** from its boundary samples, without needing to integrate over the interior.

In **variational autoencoders (VAEs)**, analyzing the structure of the latent space often requires measuring the area or volume of regions in latent space. If the latent code traces a closed curve in 2D latent space (e.g., a manifold boundary learned by the encoder), the shoelace formula gives its enclosed area directly from the curve samples — no grid integration needed.

More generally, the formula $A = \oint x,dy$ (one of the equivalent forms) is used in **numerical integration over 2D domains defined by boundaries**, e.g., computing integrals over polytopes or 2D mesh elements in finite-element methods for PINNs.

### 12.4 Discrete Green's Theorem on Graphs — The Graph Laplacian

On a **planar graph** $G = (V, E, F)$ (vertices, edges, faces), the two Green's theorem forms become:

**Tangential (circulation) form → Cycle space:** $$\sum_{e \in \partial f} f_e = (\delta_1 \mathbf{f})_{\text{face}} \qquad \text{(sum of edge flows around each face)}$$

This is the discrete curl. For a face (2-cell) $f$, the sum of edge flows around its boundary is the discrete analog of $\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$.

**Normal (flux) form → Cut space:** $$\sum_{e \sim v} f_e = (\partial_1\mathbf{f})_v \qquad \text{(net flow out of each vertex)}$$

This is the discrete divergence. The Green's theorem identity connecting them is:

$$\underbrace{\langle\partial_1\mathbf{f},, g\rangle_V}_{\text{boundary = divergence}} = \underbrace{\langle\mathbf{f},, \partial_0 g\rangle_E}_{\text{interior = gradient}}$$

This is the **discrete integration by parts** formula — the graph-theoretic Green's identity. It says: the pairing of a flow with the divergence of a node function equals the pairing of the flow's "curl" (boundary) with the node function. This identity underlies:

- **Message passing** in GNNs: the aggregation step computes $\sum_{j\sim i} h_j$ — a discrete divergence of the feature flow.
- **Spectral GNNs**: filters $g(L)$ applied to node signals, where $L$ is the graph Laplacian $L = D - A = \partial_0^T\partial_0$ — the discrete $\nabla\cdot\nabla$.
- **Graph attention networks**: attention weights modulate the edge flows $\mathbf{f}$; the attention mechanism implicitly selects which discrete "flux" to aggregate at each node.

### 12.5 Green's Identities in Kernel Methods and Gaussian Processes

Green's second identity: $$\iint_D(f\nabla^2 g - g\nabla^2 f),dA = \oint_{\partial D}\left(f,\frac{\partial g}{\partial n} - g,\frac{\partial f}{\partial n}\right)ds$$

When $g = G(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}_0)$ is the **Green's function of the Laplacian** (i.e., $\nabla^2 G = \delta(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_0)$), this becomes the **representation formula**:

$$f(\mathbf{x}_0) = \oint_{\partial D}\left(f,\frac{\partial G}{\partial n} - G,\frac{\partial f}{\partial n}\right)ds - \iint_D G,\nabla^2 f,dA$$

This expresses any function in terms of its **boundary values** and **Laplacian in the interior** — the foundation of boundary element methods.

In **Gaussian processes** with Matérn kernels, the kernel is the Green's function of the operator $(I - \nabla^2)^{\nu+d/2}$ — a polynomial in the Laplacian. Green's second identity controls how these kernels handle boundary conditions, and is the key to **Gauss-Markov GPs** on bounded domains. The behavior of the GP near the boundary — a central challenge in GP regression on complex domains — is governed precisely by the boundary terms in Green's identity.

### 12.6 Topology of Loss Landscapes — Poincaré Lemma and Obstructions

The Poincaré lemma: on a **simply connected** domain, every curl-free field is a gradient. Equivalently: on a simply connected domain, if $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for all closed $C$, then $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$.

In ML, the **loss landscape** is a scalar function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$. Its gradient $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ is always curl-free (assuming $\mathcal{L} \in C^2$, by Clairaut). But in **multi-player games**, the joint update vector $\mathbf{v} = (-\nabla_G\mathcal{L}, \nabla_D\mathcal{L})$ is **not** a gradient of any single function — precisely because $\nabla\times\mathbf{v} \neq 0$ (the mixed partial derivatives are not equal).

The **obstruction to convergence** in GAN training is thus topological: the training vector field has nonzero curl, meaning it is not a gradient field, meaning there is no Lyapunov function for the joint dynamics. Green's theorem makes this precise: the circulation $\oint_C\mathbf{v}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is nonzero for loops around the equilibrium, measured by the curl integral over the enclosed region.

This connection — between the topology of the parameter space, the curl of the training dynamics, and the convergence or cycling behavior of optimization — is one of the deepest applications of vector calculus to machine learning theory.

## §13. Summary

### The Two Forms, Side by Side

||**Tangential Form**|**Normal Form**|
|---|---|---|
|**Line integral**|$\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$|$\oint_{\partial D}\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds$|
|**What it measures**|Circulation (flow along $C$)|Flux (flow through $C$)|
|**Area integral**|$\iint_D (Q_x - P_y),dA$|$\iint_D (P_x + Q_y),dA$|
|**Interior operator**|2D curl: $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{k}$|Divergence: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$|
|**Zero $\Rightarrow$**|Conservative field, $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$|Solenoidal field, $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$|
|**3D generalization**|Stokes' theorem|Divergence theorem|
|**ML role**|GAN curl → cycling; topology of loss landscape|Normalizing flows; volume preservation; PINNs|

### The Core Insight

$$\text{Boundary integral} = \text{Interior integral of a derivative}$$

The tangential form accumulates local rotation. The normal form accumulates local expansion. They are the same identity, rotated $90°$, and both are the 2D shadow of the single master theorem $\int_{\partial\Omega}\omega = \int_\Omega d\omega$.

## §14. Personal Notes

- The rotation trick ($\mathbf{F} \mapsto \mathbf{F}^\perp = \langle Q, -P\rangle$ swaps curl and divergence, tangential and normal) is the single most useful observation about the two Green's forms. Once you see it, you never need to memorize which form is which — you derive one from the other.
    
- The proof via vertical/horizontal simple regions is the honest proof. The tiling argument is the intuition. You need both: the tiling argument tells you _why_ the theorem is true; the formal proof tells you _that_ it is true, with the correct hypotheses.
    
- The area formula $A = \frac{1}{2}\oint(x,dy - y,dx)$ is Green's theorem in its most elegant clothing. It is also the shoelace formula for polygons in disguise. It is also the reason planimeters work — a mechanical device that traces the boundary of a region and reads off its area, with zero interior calculation. Planimeters are a physical instantiation of Green's theorem.
    
- Green's identities (§8) are more powerful than they look. The entire theory of elliptic PDEs — Laplace, Poisson, Helmholtz equations — rests on Green's second identity plus the existence of a Green's function. The uniqueness of Laplace's equation (§8.1) is the cleanest proof of a non-trivial PDE uniqueness result, and it fits in four lines.
    
- The connection to GAN training (§12.2) is not a metaphor — it is literally the same mathematical object. The mixed partial $\mathcal{L}_{\theta_G\theta_D}$ is the 2D scalar curl of the training vector field, and its integral over a region in parameter space (by Green's theorem) is the circulation — the quantity that determines whether gradient descent spirals in or cycles. Algorithms like SGA and CGD are direct consequences of analyzing this curl.
    
- Green's theorem is the first result where the boundary of the domain plays a fundamental role — not just as a place where boundary conditions are imposed, but as an object that captures global information about the interior. This is the seed of the entire subject of algebraic topology.
