## 1. Motivation тАФ Why Vector Fields?

In single-variable calculus we asked: _how does a single quantity change?_ In multivariable calculus, the natural next question is: _how does a direction-and-magnitude quantity vary across space?_

**Physical phenomena that demand this language:**

- Wind velocity at each point in the atmosphere
- Electric and magnetic force at each point in space
- Fluid flow inside a pipe
- The force exerted on a charged particle moving through a field

The moment you say _"at every point $(x,y,z)$ there is a force vector"_, you are talking about a vector field. Almost every fundamental law in physics тАФ Maxwell's equations, NavierтАУStokes, Newton's law of gravity тАФ is written in the language of vector fields.

---

## 2. Definition

> **Definition.** Let $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be an open set. A **vector field** on $D$ is a function $$\mathbf{F} : D \to \mathbb{R}^n$$ that assigns to each point $\mathbf{x} \in D$ a vector $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}) \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

In $\mathbb{R}^2$ we write: $$\mathbf{F}(x,y) = P(x,y),\mathbf{i} + Q(x,y),\mathbf{j} = \langle P(x,y),, Q(x,y)\rangle$$

In $\mathbb{R}^3$: $$\mathbf{F}(x,y,z) = P(x,y,z),\mathbf{i} + Q(x,y,z),\mathbf{j} + R(x,y,z),\mathbf{k}$$

**Component functions** $P, Q, R$ are scalar-valued; the vector field is the tuple of them.

> **Smoothness.** We say $\mathbf{F}$ is of class $C^k$ if each component function has continuous partial derivatives up to order $k$. Almost everything in this note assumes at least $C^1$.

## 3. Examples Worth Memorising

### Example 1 тАФ Constant Field (trivial but important)

$$\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \langle 1, 0 \rangle$$ Every arrow points in the $+x$ direction with unit length. This is what a uniform horizontal wind looks like.

### Example 2 тАФ Radial (outward) Field

$$\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \langle x, y \rangle = x,\mathbf{i} + y,\mathbf{j}$$ At each point $(x,y)$, the vector points **directly away** from the origin and has magnitude $\sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ тАФ proportional to distance.

> _Think: a source pumping fluid radially outward from the origin._

### Example 3 тАФ Rotating Field

$$\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \langle -y, x \rangle$$ At $(1,0)$: $\mathbf{F} = \langle 0,1\rangle$ (pointing up). At $(0,1)$: $\mathbf{F} = \langle -1,0\rangle$ (pointing left). The vectors are **tangent to circles** centred at the origin тАФ pure counterclockwise rotation.

### Example 4 тАФ Gradient Field (sneak preview)

$$f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2 \implies \nabla f = \langle 2x, 2y \rangle$$ This is the same as Example 2 scaled by 2. The gradient always points in the direction of steepest ascent of $f$.

### Example 5 тАФ Gravitational Field

$$\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}) = -\frac{GM}{|\mathbf{r}|^3},\mathbf{r} \qquad \mathbf{r} \in \mathbb{R}^3 \setminus {\mathbf{0}}$$ This points **toward** the origin (the mass), and its magnitude falls off as $1/r^2$ тАФ the inverse-square law.

## 4. Visualisation тАФ How to Draw One

The standard way to sketch $\mathbf{F}(x,y) = \langle P, Q\rangle$:

1. Pick a grid of sample points $(x_i, y_j)$.
2. At each sample point, draw an arrow in the direction $\langle P(x_i,y_j), Q(x_i,y_j)\rangle$.
3. Scale all arrows by the same factor so they don't overlap (the _relative_ lengths still convey magnitude).

**ASCII sketch of $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x\rangle$ (counterclockwise rotation):**

```
     тЖС   тЖС   тЖС
тЖР тЖР  +   +   +  тЖТ тЖТ
тЖР тЖР  +   +   +  тЖТ тЖТ
     тЖУ   тЖУ   тЖУ
```

_(Arrows form concentric circles; centre is a "vortex" or saddle point depending on the field.)_

**Key visual features to identify:**

|Feature|What to look for|
|---|---|
|**Source**|Arrows diverging outward from a point|
|**Sink**|Arrows converging inward|
|**Vortex**|Arrows circling a point|
|**Saddle**|Arrows diverging in some directions, converging in others|
|**Uniform**|All arrows parallel and equal length|

## 5. Operations on Vector Fields

### 5.1 The Gradient (тИЗf)

The gradient converts a **scalar field** into a **vector field**: $$\nabla f = \left\langle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x},, \frac{\partial f}{\partial y},, \frac{\partial f}{\partial z} \right\rangle$$

$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ points in the direction of **steepest increase** of $f$ at $\mathbf{x}_0$, with magnitude equal to the rate of increase in that direction.

> **Theorem (Gradient and Level Curves).** $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}_0)$ is **perpendicular** to the level curve (or level surface) of $f$ passing through $\mathbf{x}_0$.

### 5.2 The Divergence (тИЗ ┬╖ F)

The divergence converts a **vector field** into a **scalar field**: $$\operatorname{div}\mathbf{F} = \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}$$

**Intuition.** $(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})(\mathbf{x}_0)$ measures how much the field is _spreading out_ (acting as a source) or _converging_ (acting as a sink) near $\mathbf{x}_0$.

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} > 0 \implies \text{source (fluid leaving a region)}$$ $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} < 0 \implies \text{sink (fluid entering a region)}$$ $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 0 \implies \text{incompressible / divergence-free}$$

> **Example.** For the radial field $\mathbf{F} = \langle x, y, z\rangle$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$: $$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = 1 + 1 + 1 = 3$$ Constant positive divergence: fluid is being created at every point at a uniform rate.

### 5.3 The Curl (тИЗ ├Ч F)

The curl converts a **vector field** in $\mathbb{R}^3$ into another **vector field**: $$\operatorname{curl}\mathbf{F} = \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z \ P & Q & R \end{vmatrix}$$

$$= \left\langle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z},; \frac{\partial P}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial x},; \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right\rangle$$

**Intuition.** $(\nabla \times \mathbf{F})(\mathbf{x}_0)$ measures the tendency of the field to **rotate** near $\mathbf{x}_0$. The direction of the curl vector gives the axis of rotation (via the right-hand rule); the magnitude gives the angular speed of rotation.

> **Example.** For the rotating field $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x, 0\rangle$: $$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left\langle 0,, 0,, \frac{\partial x}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(-y)}{\partial y}\right\rangle = \langle 0, 0, 1+1\rangle = \langle 0,0,2\rangle$$ Curl points in $+z$ direction тАФ counterclockwise rotation when viewed from above. Magnitude $2$ gives the angular velocity.

> **In 2D**, the curl reduces to the scalar quantity $\dfrac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial y}$, sometimes called the _scalar curl_ or _2D curl_.

## 6. Conservative Vector Fields

> **Definition.** $\mathbf{F}$ is **conservative** on $D$ if there exists a scalar function $f : D \to \mathbb{R}$ such that $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$. The function $f$ is called a **potential function** for $\mathbf{F}$.

This is the vector field analogue of a function being an exact derivative.

> **Theorem (Curl Test).** If $\mathbf{F}$ is $C^1$ on a simply connected domain $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$, then: $$\mathbf{F} \text{ is conservative on } D \iff \nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0} \text{ on } D$$

> **Theorem (Path Independence).** $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative $\iff$ $\displaystyle\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is **path-independent** (depends only on endpoints).

> **Theorem (Closed Curves).** $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative $\iff$ $\displaystyle\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for every closed curve $C$ in $D$.

**To find the potential function** $f$ given conservative $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q\rangle$:

1. Integrate $P$ with respect to $x$: $f(x,y) = \int P,dx + g(y)$.
2. Differentiate with respect to $y$ and set equal to $Q$.
3. Solve for $g(y)$.

> **Example.** $\mathbf{F} = \langle 2xy,, x^2 + 1\rangle$. Check: $\partial_y(2xy) = 2x$, $\partial_x(x^2+1) = 2x$. тЬУ Curl-free, so conservative. $f = \int 2xy,dx = x^2 y + g(y)$. $\partial_y f = x^2 + g'(y) = x^2 + 1 \implies g'(y) = 1 \implies g(y) = y$. **Potential:** $f(x,y) = x^2 y + y$.

## 7. Line Integrals Over Vector Fields

Given a curve $C$ parametrised by $\mathbf{r}(t)$, $t \in [a,b]$, and a vector field $\mathbf{F}$:

$$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t),dt$$

**Physical meaning.** This is the **work done** by $\mathbf{F}$ on a particle travelling along $C$.

> **Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals.** If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ on an open set containing $C$ from $A$ to $B$: $$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(B) - f(A)$$ No need to parametrise the curve at all тАФ only the endpoints matter.

> **Example.** $\mathbf{F} = \langle 2xy, x^2+1\rangle$, $f = x^2y + y$. Compute the line integral from $(0,0)$ to $(1,2)$ along **any** path: $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(1,2) - f(0,0) = (1\cdot2 + 2) - 0 = 4$$

## 8. Key Theorems (Big Picture)

These theorems are the profound culmination of the theory тАФ each generalises the **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus** to higher dimensions.

|Theorem|Statement|Dimension|
|---|---|---|
|**Green's Theorem**|$\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA$|2D|
|**Stokes' Theorem**|$\oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$|3D, surface/curve|
|**Divergence Theorem**|$\oiint_{\partial V} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_V (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}),dV$|3D, volume/surface|

_The pattern:_ integral of a derivative over a region = boundary integral of the original field. These are all special cases of the **Generalised Stokes' Theorem** in differential geometry.

## 9. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

This is where vector fields are not just beautiful mathematics but **working engineering**.

### 9.1 The Loss Landscape is a Scalar Field

In training a neural network with parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ (where $n$ might be billions), the loss function

$$\mathcal{L} : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$$

is a scalar field over parameter space. This is exactly the $f$ you would compute the gradient of.

### 9.2 The Gradient is the Key Vector Field

$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}},\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \left\langle \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_1}, \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_n} \right\rangle$$

This is the **gradient vector field** of the loss, evaluated at the current parameter point $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. Every iteration of training, you are:

1. Evaluating this vector field at the current point.
2. Taking a step in the **negative gradient direction** (steepest descent).

$$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta,\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

**Gradient descent is literally following a vector field.**

### 9.3 Divergence and the Continuity of Probability Flow

In **normalising flows** and **score-based generative models** (e.g. diffusion models like DDPM, Stable Diffusion), the probability distribution $p(\mathbf{x})$ evolves according to a **Fokker-Planck equation**, which involves the divergence:

$$\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} = -\nabla \cdot (p,\mathbf{v})$$

This is the **continuity equation** тАФ conservation of probability mass. The divergence of the probability flux $p,\mathbf{v}$ governs how the distribution spreads or concentrates over time.

### 9.4 Score Functions as Vector Fields

The **score function** used in score-based generative models is: $$\mathbf{s}(\mathbf{x}) = \nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \log p(\mathbf{x})$$

This is a vector field over data space $\mathbb{R}^n$ that points in the direction of increasing probability density. Sampling algorithms (Langevin dynamics) follow this vector field, plus noise, to draw samples from $p$.

> _The entire idea of diffusion models is: learn the score vector field from data, then integrate it backwards to generate new samples._

### 9.5 Conservative Fields and Energy Functions

**Hopfield networks** and **energy-based models** explicitly construct a scalar energy $E(\mathbf{x})$ and update rules that follow $-\nabla E$. The vector field $-\nabla E$ is **conservative by construction** (it has a potential function $E$). This guarantees the dynamics converge to a local minimum тАФ they cannot oscillate indefinitely.

**Boltzmann Machines** and **RBMs** are energy-based models where the equilibrium distribution is $p \propto e^{-E(\mathbf{x})}$.

### 9.6 Curl in Neural ODE and Physics-Informed Networks

In **Neural ODEs**, the hidden state $\mathbf{h}(t)$ evolves as: $$\frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = \mathbf{F}_\theta(\mathbf{h}(t), t)$$

where $\mathbf{F}_\theta$ is a learned vector field (parametrised by a neural network). The dynamics of the hidden state are literally integration along trajectories of a vector field.

**Hamiltonian Neural Networks** impose the constraint $\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ (or its Hamiltonian equivalent) to ensure the learned dynamics conserve energy тАФ a hard inductive bias from physics.

### 9.7 Summary Table

|ML Concept|Vector Field Role|
|---|---|
|Gradient descent|Follow $-\nabla\mathcal{L}$ vector field|
|Adam, RMSProp|Preconditioned vector fields (scaled gradients)|
|Diffusion models|Learn score $\nabla\log p$ as a vector field|
|Normalising flows|Divergence-free or volume-preserving vector fields|
|Energy-based models|Conservative vector fields with potential $E$|
|Neural ODEs|Network parametrises an arbitrary vector field|
|Hamiltonian NNs|Constrain curl/divergence to preserve physics|
