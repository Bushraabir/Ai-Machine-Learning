

> _"Green's theorem is the first place where a local property of a vector field — its curl — reveals itself through a global measurement — circulation around a boundary. This is not a coincidence. It is the organizing principle of all of vector calculus."_

---

## §1. Motivation — Two Ways to Measure a Vector Field

Suppose you have a vector field $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q \rangle$ on some region in the plane. You can ask two fundamentally different questions:

1. **Global:** How much does $\mathbf{F}$ circulate around the boundary curve $C$ of a region $D$? $\longrightarrow$ $\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$
2. **Local:** At each interior point of $D$, how much does $\mathbf{F}$ rotate or "curl"? $\longrightarrow$ $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ at each point

**Green's theorem says these two measurements are equal.** The total boundary circulation equals the integrated interior rotation. A global integral over a curve equals a double integral over the enclosed region.

This is the $2D$ instance of a single grand principle that runs through all of vector calculus: **Stokes' theorem**.

---

## §2. Setup

### 2.1 The Region $D$ and Its Boundary $\partial D$

Let $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ be a **bounded, simply connected region** with boundary curve $C = \partial D$. The boundary is oriented **positively (counterclockwise)** — the convention is that as you walk along $C$, the region $D$ is to your **left**.

```
          C = ∂D  (counterclockwise)
         ↗──────────────────↖
        ╱                    ╲
       │      D (interior)    │
       │         ←            │
        ╲    (D is to your   ╱
         ↘   left as you   ↙
          ──────────────────
```

More generally, Green's theorem holds for **regions with holes** (multiply connected), where the outer boundary is traversed counterclockwise and inner boundaries (holes) are traversed clockwise.

### 2.2 The Curl in 2D

For $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q \rangle$, the **scalar curl** (or $z$-component of $\nabla \times \mathbf{F}$) is: $$\text{curl}_z(\mathbf{F}) = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$$

This measures the **local rotational tendency** of $\mathbf{F}$ at each point:

- $\text{curl}_z > 0$: counterclockwise rotation (like the center of a vortex)
- $\text{curl}_z < 0$: clockwise rotation
- $\text{curl}_z = 0$: no local rotation (irrotational — conservative fields)

---

## §3. The Theorem

> **Theorem (Green's Theorem).** Let $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ be a bounded region with piecewise smooth, positively oriented boundary $C = \partial D$. Let $P, Q: \overline{D} \to \mathbb{R}$ have continuous partial derivatives on an open set containing $D$. Then:
> 
> $$\boxed{\oint_C P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA}$$

In vector notation, with $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q \rangle$: $$\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D \text{curl}_z(\mathbf{F}),dA$$

---

## §4. Proof (for a Simple Region)

We prove the theorem for a **Type III region** (simultaneously Type I and Type II). The general case follows by decomposing $D$ into finitely many such pieces and noting that interior boundary contributions cancel.

**It suffices to prove two separate identities:**

$$\oint_C P,dx = -\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y},dA \qquad \text{...(I)}$$

$$\oint_C Q,dy = \iint_D \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x},dA \qquad \text{...(II)}$$

Adding them gives Green's theorem.

---

### Proof of (I): $\oint_C P,dx = -\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y},dA$

Assume $D$ is a **Type I region**: $D = {(x,y) : a \leq x \leq b,; g_1(x) \leq y \leq g_2(x)}$.

**Right side.** By the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus (in $y$):

$$-\iint_D \frac{\partial P}{\partial y},dA = -\int_a^b \int_{g_1(x)}^{g_2(x)} \frac{\partial P}{\partial y},dy,dx = -\int_a^b \bigl[P(x,g_2(x)) - P(x,g_1(x))\bigr]dx$$

**Left side.** Decompose $C = C_1 \cup C_2 \cup C_3 \cup C_4$ (bottom, right, top, left):

- $C_1$ (bottom, left to right): $y = g_1(x)$, $x: a \to b$. $\quad \int_{C_1} P,dx = \int_a^b P(x,g_1(x)),dx$
- $C_3$ (top, right to left): $y = g_2(x)$, $x: b \to a$. $\quad \int_{C_3} P,dx = -\int_a^b P(x,g_2(x)),dx$
- $C_2, C_4$ (vertical sides): $dx = 0$, so $\int P,dx = 0$.

Summing: $$\oint_C P,dx = \int_a^b P(x,g_1(x)),dx - \int_a^b P(x,g_2(x)),dx = -\int_a^b\bigl[P(x,g_2(x)) - P(x,g_1(x))\bigr]dx$$

This equals the right side. ✓

**Proof of (II)** is analogous using a Type II region. $\square$

## §5. Two Forms of Green's Theorem

Green's theorem has two distinct interpretations depending on how you write it.

### 5.1 Circulation-Curl Form (Standard)

$$\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA$$

**Interpretation:** The total **circulation** of $\mathbf{F}$ around the boundary = integral of **curl** over the interior.

### 5.2 Flux-Divergence Form

Let $\mathbf{n}$ be the outward unit normal to $C$, and $d\mathbf{r} = \langle dx, dy\rangle$. The outward normal is $\mathbf{n},ds = \langle dy, -dx\rangle$. Then:

$$\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F} \cdot \mathbf{n},ds = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}\right)dA = \iint_D \nabla \cdot \mathbf{F},dA$$

**Interpretation:** The total **flux** of $\mathbf{F}$ through the boundary = integral of **divergence** over the interior.

This is the $2D$ version of the **Divergence Theorem**.

> _Personal note: The two forms are not two different theorems — they're the same theorem with $\mathbf{F}$ replaced by its $90°$ rotation $\langle Q, -P\rangle$. Always keep both in mind; which form to use depends on whether your integrand looks like a curl or a divergence._

## §6. Worked Examples

### Example A — Direct Application (Circulation)

**Problem.** Evaluate $\oint_C (x^2 - y^3),dx + (x^3 + y^2),dy$ where $C$ is the circle $x^2 + y^2 = 4$, counterclockwise.

Doing this directly would require parameterizing the circle and grinding through trigonometric integrals. Green's theorem:

$$P = x^2 - y^3, \quad Q = x^3 + y^2$$ $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 3x^2 - (-3y^2) = 3x^2 + 3y^2 = 3(x^2 + y^2)$$

$$\oint_C = \iint_D 3(x^2+y^2),dA$$

Switch to polar coordinates over the disk $D: r \leq 2$:

$$= 3\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^2 r^2 \cdot r,dr,d\theta = 3 \int_0^{2\pi}d\theta \cdot \int_0^2 r^3,dr = 3 \cdot 2\pi \cdot 4 = 24\pi$$

---

### Example B — Computing Area via Line Integral

Green's theorem gives elegant **area formulas**. Set $Q_x - P_y = 1$. Three choices:

$$A = \oint_C x,dy = -\oint_C y,dx = \frac{1}{2}\oint_C (x,dy - y,dx)$$

**Area of an ellipse** $\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}=1$. Parameterize: $x = a\cos t$, $y = b\sin t$, $t \in [0,2\pi]$.

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\oint_C (x,dy - y,dx) = \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi}(a\cos t \cdot b\cos t - b\sin t \cdot (-a\sin t)),dt$$ $$= \frac{1}{2}\int_0^{2\pi} ab(\cos^2 t + \sin^2 t),dt = \frac{ab}{2}\cdot 2\pi = \pi ab \checkmark$$

---

### Example C — Flux Form (Divergence)

**Problem.** Compute the flux of $\mathbf{F} = \langle x^2, y^2\rangle$ outward through the boundary of the square $[0,1]\times[0,1]$.

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(x^2) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(y^2) = 2x + 2y$$

$$\text{Flux} = \iint_D (2x+2y),dA = \int_0^1\int_0^1 (2x+2y),dy,dx = \int_0^1 [2xy + y^2]_0^1,dx = \int_0^1 (2x+1),dx = 2$$

---

### Example D — Region with a Hole (Multiply Connected)

Let $D$ be the annulus $1 \leq x^2+y^2 \leq 4$. The boundary is $C = C_{\text{outer}} \cup C_{\text{inner}}$ where $C_{\text{outer}}$ is the circle of radius 2 (CCW) and $C_{\text{inner}}$ is the circle of radius 1 (**CW**, since the hole is to the right as you walk it).

For $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x\rangle$: $\quad Q_x - P_y = 1 - (-1) = 2$.

$$\oint_{C_{\text{outer}}} - \oint_{C_{\text{inner}}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D 2,dA = 2 \cdot \pi(4-1) = 6\pi$$

$$\oint_{C_{\text{outer}}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi(2)^2 = 8\pi, \quad \oint_{C_{\text{inner}}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi(1)^2 = 2\pi$$

$$8\pi - 2\pi = 6\pi \checkmark$$

## §7. Visualization

### 7.1 The Core Intuition — Cancellation of Interior

Imagine $D$ subdivided into infinitesimally small squares. Each tiny square has a boundary contribution — but **interior edges are shared by two adjacent squares and traversed in opposite directions**, so they cancel. What remains is only the **outermost boundary**.

```
  ┌──→──┬──→──┬──→──┐
  ↑  ↺  ↓  ↺  ↓  ↺  ↑   Each cell contributes its local curl.
  ├──←──┼──←──┼──←──┤   Interior edges cancel with neighbors.
  ↑  ↺  ↓  ↺  ↓  ↺  ↑
  ├──←──┼──←──┼──←──┤
  ↑  ↺  ↓  ↺  ↓  ↺  ↑
  └──→──┴──→──┴──→──┘
  ↑________________________↑
  Only outer boundary survives!
```

This "cancellation of interior" is the deepest geometric insight behind Green's theorem — and behind all of Stokes' theorem.

### 7.2 Curl as Local Rotation — Paddle Wheel Test

Place a tiny paddle wheel at a point in the vector field. The **curl** measures how fast it spins:

```
  High positive curl:    Low/zero curl:
  ↑  ↑  ↑               →  →  →
  ↑  ⊙  ↑               →  ⊙  →    Paddle barely moves.
  ↑  ↑  ↑               →  →  →
  Paddle spins fast CCW.
```

Green's theorem says: add up all these local spins across $D$, and you get the total circulation around $\partial D$.

### 7.3 Circulation vs. Flux — Two Views of the Same Field

```
  CIRCULATION FORM              FLUX FORM
  (F · tangent)                 (F · outward normal)

  →  →  →                      ↑  ↑  ↑
  →  [D]  →   ←→  ↗           →  [D]  →   ←→  ↗↗↗
  →  →  →                      ↓  ↓  ↓
  
  "How much does F flow        "How much does F pass
   along the boundary?"         through the boundary?"
  = ∬ curl dA                  = ∬ div dA
```

### 7.4 Orientation Convention

```
  CORRECT (CCW = positive):    WRONG (CW = negative):

     ↗──────↖                    ↙──────↗
    ╱   D    ╲                  ╱   D    ╲
   │    ←     │                │    →     │   Gives the negative
    ╲         ╱                 ╲         ╱   of the answer.
     ↙──────↘                    ↖──────↙

  D is to your LEFT           D is to your RIGHT
  as you traverse C.          — WRONG orientation.
```

## §8. Green's Theorem and Conservative Fields

Green's theorem gives a **direct proof** of the key equivalence for conservative fields.

**Claim:** If $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ throughout a simply connected domain $U$, then $\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for every closed curve $C$ in $U$.

**Proof via Green's theorem:** For any closed curve $C$ bounding a region $D \subset U$: $$\oint_C P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D \underbrace{\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)}_{=,0},dA = 0 \qquad \square$$

This is the cleanest proof that $\text{curl} = 0 \Rightarrow$ conservative (on simply connected domains). Without Green's theorem, you'd need a more delicate path-deformation argument.

## §9. Planimeter — Green's Theorem in Hardware

A **planimeter** is a mechanical device, invented in the 1800s, that computes the area of an irregular region by tracing its boundary with a pointer. As the pointer traces $\partial D$, internal gears compute:

$$A = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\partial D}(x,dy - y,dx)$$

This is Green's theorem in hardware — a physical machine that integrates a line integral by mechanical linkage. It works **exactly** because Green's theorem is true. The planimeter is perhaps the most beautiful physical demonstration of a pure mathematical theorem in existence.

## §10. Place in the Grand Hierarchy

Green's theorem is the $2D$ special case of **Stokes' theorem**:

$$\iint_S (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oint_{\partial S} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

Which itself is a special case of the **generalized Stokes' theorem** on manifolds:

$$\int_M d\omega = \int_{\partial M} \omega$$

where $\omega$ is a differential form and $d$ is the exterior derivative.

```
Generalized Stokes (differential forms)
           │
    ┌──────┴──────┐
    │             │
 Stokes'     Divergence
 Theorem      Theorem
 (surface)    (volume)
    │
    └── Green's Theorem (plane) ──── FTC (line)
```

The **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus** is the $1D$ case: $\int_a^b f'(x)dx = f(b) - f(a)$. Green's, Stokes', and the Divergence Theorem are all the same idea in higher dimensions.

## §11. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> Green's theorem is not directly computed in ML pipelines, but the _ideas_ it embodies — boundary vs. interior, local vs. global, curl and divergence — permeate modern machine learning in profound ways.

### 11.1 The Geometry of GAN Training — Curl and Non-Conservative Dynamics

Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) train a generator $G$ and discriminator $D$ simultaneously. The gradient update vector field in the joint parameter space $(\theta_G, \theta_D)$ is:

$$\mathbf{v}(\theta_G, \theta_D) = \begin{pmatrix} -\nabla_{\theta_G} \mathcal{L} \ \nabla_{\theta_D} \mathcal{L} \end{pmatrix}$$

This vector field is **not conservative** — it has nonzero curl. By Green's theorem, this means:

$$\oint_C \mathbf{v} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} \neq 0$$

for closed curves $C$ in parameter space. Gradient descent can **orbit** the equilibrium without converging — a phenomenon well-documented in GAN training. The curl of the training dynamics vector field, measured by Green's theorem, is precisely the quantity that determines whether GAN training cycles.

Recent work (Balduzzi et al. 2018, Schäfer et al. 2019) explicitly decomposes the GAN gradient field into:

- **Symmetric part** (gradient of a potential → conservative → converges)
- **Antisymmetric part** (curl component → causes cycling)

This is a direct application of Helmholtz decomposition — which is itself an application of the ideas behind Green's and Stokes' theorems.

### 11.2 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) and PDEs

Many PDEs arise from integral laws that are proven using Green's theorem. For example:

**Gauss's law** (from the flux form of Green's theorem in $\mathbb{R}^3$): $$\oiint_{\partial V} \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{S} = \frac{1}{\varepsilon_0}\iiint_V \rho,dV \implies \nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$$

**PINNs** train neural networks to satisfy PDEs like this by including the PDE residual in the loss function. The physical law encoded in the PDE comes directly from divergence/Stokes-type theorems. Training a PINN to solve Laplace's equation $\nabla^2 u = 0$ is training it to satisfy Green's theorem at every interior point simultaneously.

### 11.3 Normalizing Flows and Change of Variables

**Normalizing flows** learn invertible transformations $\mathbf{g}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ to map a simple distribution to a complex one. The change-of-variables formula requires computing the **Jacobian determinant** $|\det J_{\mathbf{g}}|$.

For $2D$ flows, the Jacobian determinant is related to the **curl and divergence** of the flow field — directly the objects appearing in Green's theorem. Specifically:

$$\det J_{\mathbf{g}} = \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial x}\frac{\partial g_2}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial g_1}{\partial y}\frac{\partial g_2}{\partial x}$$

This is a $2 \times 2$ determinant whose structure is exactly the curl $\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)$ when $\mathbf{g} = \langle P, Q\rangle$. Planar normalizing flows compute the exact quantity that appears in Green's theorem.

### 11.4 Topological Data Analysis and Persistent Homology

**TDA** analyzes the "shape" of data by detecting **loops and voids** — topological features. The central tool is **homology**, which asks: are there closed loops that cannot be contracted to a point?

Green's theorem says: on a **simply connected** domain, every closed curve has zero circulation for curl-free fields. But on domains with holes, this fails — because there exist closed loops that cannot be filled in.

TDA uses this idea computationally: the **first Betti number** $\beta_1$ counts the number of independent loops, corresponding exactly to the number of "holes" where Green's theorem fails to hold. This is applied in:

- **Neuroscience**: detecting ring-like structures in neural firing patterns
- **Drug discovery**: analyzing molecular shapes
- **Data manifold analysis**: understanding the topology of latent spaces in VAEs and diffusion models

### 11.5 Stokes' Theorem and Graph Neural Networks

In **Graph Neural Networks (GNNs)** operating on meshes or simplicial complexes, the discrete analogue of Stokes'/Green's theorem is the fundamental identity:

$$\mathbf{B}^T \mathbf{B} = 0$$

where $\mathbf{B}$ is the boundary operator (an incidence matrix). This encodes: _the boundary of a boundary is zero_ — the discrete Stokes' theorem. Modern work on **Hodge-decomposition GNNs** explicitly uses this structure to decompose edge flows into gradient (conservative), curl (rotational), and harmonic components — a direct computational version of the Helmholtz decomposition behind Green's theorem.

### 11.6 Area Computation in Reinforcement Learning

Policy gradient methods in RL compute expectations like:

$$\nabla_\theta J(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_\tau\left[\sum_t \nabla_\theta \log \pi_\theta(a_t|s_t) \cdot R(\tau)\right]$$

The **REINFORCE** algorithm traces trajectories $\tau$ — paths through state space. The geometry of these paths, and how they enclose regions of high/low reward, is a direct analog of Green's theorem thinking: the boundary path integral (trajectory reward) reflects the interior structure of the reward landscape.

## §12. Summary

$$\boxed{\oint_{\partial D} P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA}$$

|Concept|Statement|
|---|---|
|**LHS**|Circulation of $\mathbf{F}$ around $\partial D$|
|**RHS**|Double integral of curl over $D$|
|**Orientation**|Boundary traversed counterclockwise (D to the left)|
|**Hypothesis**|$P, Q \in C^1$ on open set containing $\overline{D}$|
|**Flux form**|$\oint \mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{n},ds = \iint_D \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F},dA$|
|**Special case**|$Q_x - P_y = 0 \Rightarrow \oint = 0$ (conservative field test)|
|**Special case**|$Q_x - P_y = 1 \Rightarrow \oint = \text{Area}(D)$|
|**Parent theorem**|Stokes' theorem on surfaces; generalized Stokes on manifolds|

## §13. Personal Notes

- The proof strategy — prove $\oint P,dx = -\iint P_y,dA$ and $\oint Q,dy = \iint Q_x,dA$ separately — is elegant because each half only needs a Type I or Type II region. Remembering this split is the key to reconstructing the proof from scratch.
- The two forms (circulation-curl and flux-divergence) are the same theorem, just with a $90°$ rotation applied to $\mathbf{F}$. In physics: circulation ↔ Ampère's law; flux ↔ Gauss's law.
- Green's theorem fails for non-simply-connected domains in the naive sense, but it works perfectly if you account for all boundary components with correct orientation. The topology of the domain controls everything.
- The planimeter example should be remembered for intuition: mathematical truth is so clean it can be built into a mechanical device that computes areas by tracing boundaries.
- For exams: the most common use is converting a horrible line integral into a clean double integral (or vice versa). Always check if $Q_x - P_y$ simplifies, especially over symmetric or polar-friendly regions.
- The connection to GAN training dynamics is not just a metaphor — the curl of the gradient vector field literally determines whether training converges or cycles, and this is measurable using the exact formalism of Green's theorem.
