## §0. Prefatory Remarks

This note develops the theory of **surfaces of revolution** from scratch — the definition, parameterization, surface area formula, and the deeper geometric intuition underneath. Everything is proved. Nothing is swept under the rug.

A _surface of revolution_ is born from an act of symmetry: take a planar curve and spin it about a line. What emerges is a surface with a continuous rotational symmetry — a $U(1)$ symmetry, in modern language. This is no mere curiosity. These surfaces appear in:

- Classical mechanics (rotating bodies, potential energy surfaces)
- Differential geometry (minimal surfaces, geodesics)
- **Machine learning / deep learning** (loss landscape geometry, radial basis functions, activation function symmetries)
- Engineering (lenses, turbines, containers)

---

## §1. Setup and Definition

**Setting.** Work in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with coordinates $(x, y, z)$. Fix the $x$-axis as the _axis of revolution_.

**Definition 1.1 (Surface of Revolution).** Let $f: [a,b] \to [0, \infty)$ be a continuous function. The _surface of revolution_ generated by revolving the graph of $f$ about the $x$-axis is the set $$ S = {(x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : a \leq x \leq b,; y^2 + z^2 = [f(x)]^2}. $$

**Geometric interpretation.** At each fixed $x_0$, the cross-section $S \cap {x = x_0}$ is a circle of radius $f(x_0)$ in the plane $x = x_0$. As $x$ varies, these circles sweep out the surface.

> **Remark.** We require $f \geq 0$ to avoid ambiguity — $f(x)$ is the _radius_ at position $x$. Allowing $f < 0$ would just reflect the generating curve through the axis.

---

## §2. Parameterization

The natural parameterization writes the rotation explicitly.

**Proposition 2.1.** The surface $S$ admits the parameterization $$ \mathbf{r}(x, \theta) = \bigl(x,; f(x)\cos\theta,; f(x)\sin\theta\bigr), \quad (x,\theta) \in [a,b] \times [0, 2\pi]. $$

_Proof._ For fixed $x$, varying $\theta \in [0,2\pi]$ traces the circle $y^2 + z^2 = f(x)^2$ in the plane ${X = x}$, which is exactly the cross-section of $S$ at $x$. As $x$ ranges over $[a,b]$, all cross-sections are covered. $\blacksquare$

**Computing the partial derivatives** (needed for area): $$ \mathbf{r}_x = \bigl(1,; f'(x)\cos\theta,; f'(x)\sin\theta\bigr), $$ $$ \mathbf{r}_\theta = \bigl(0,; -f(x)\sin\theta,; f(x)\cos\theta\bigr). $$

**The cross product:** $$ \mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & f'\cos\theta & f'\sin\theta \ 0 & -f\sin\theta & f\cos\theta \end{vmatrix}. $$

Expanding: $$ = \mathbf{i}(f'f\cos^2\theta + f'f\sin^2\theta) - \mathbf{j}(f\cos\theta) + \mathbf{k}(-f\sin\theta) $$ $$ = \bigl(f'f,; -f\cos\theta,; -f\sin\theta\bigr). $$

Wait — let me redo this carefully:

$$ \mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & f'\cos\theta & f'\sin\theta \ 0 & -f\sin\theta & f\cos\theta \end{vmatrix} $$

$$ = \mathbf{i}(f'\cos\theta \cdot f\cos\theta - f'\sin\theta \cdot (-f\sin\theta)) - \mathbf{j}(1 \cdot f\cos\theta - f'\sin\theta \cdot 0) + \mathbf{k}(1 \cdot (-f\sin\theta) - f'\cos\theta \cdot 0) $$

$$ = \mathbf{i}(f'f\cos^2\theta + f'f\sin^2\theta) - \mathbf{j}(f\cos\theta) + \mathbf{k}(-f\sin\theta) $$

$$ = \bigl(f'f,; -f\cos\theta,; -f\sin\theta\bigr). $$

**The magnitude:** $$ |\mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta|^2 = (f'f)^2 + f^2\cos^2\theta + f^2\sin^2\theta = f^2(f')^2 + f^2 = f^2\bigl(1 + (f')^2\bigr). $$

Since $f \geq 0$: $$ |\mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = f(x)\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}. $$

## §3. The Surface Area Formula

**Theorem 3.1 (Surface Area of Revolution).** Let $f \in C^1[a,b]$ with $f \geq 0$. The area of the surface obtained by revolving $y = f(x)$ about the $x$-axis is:

$$ \boxed{A = 2\pi \int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}; dx.} $$

_Proof._ The surface area integral for a parameterized surface $\mathbf{r}: D \to \mathbb{R}^3$ is $$ A = \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta|; dx, d\theta. $$ Using the result of §2: $$ A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}; dx, d\theta = 2\pi \int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}; dx. \quad \blacksquare $$

**Mnemonic.** The integrand $f(x)\sqrt{1 + (f')^2},dx$ is: _(radius)_ × _(arc length element)_. Integrating over the arc and wrapping around ($2\pi$) gives area. This is Pappus's theorem in disguise — see §6.

## §4. Worked Examples

### Example 4.1 — Sphere of Radius $R$

Generate a sphere by revolving the semicircle $f(x) = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2}$ over $[-R, R]$.

$$ f'(x) = \frac{-x}{\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}}, \quad 1 + (f')^2 = 1 + \frac{x^2}{R^2 - x^2} = \frac{R^2}{R^2 - x^2}. $$

$$ \sqrt{1 + (f')^2} = \frac{R}{\sqrt{R^2 - x^2}}. $$

$$ A = 2\pi \int_{-R}^R \sqrt{R^2 - x^2} \cdot \frac{R}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2}}; dx = 2\pi \int_{-R}^R R; dx = 2\pi R \cdot 2R = 4\pi R^2. \checkmark $$

### Example 4.2 — Cone (Frustum)

Revolve $f(x) = mx + c$ (with $m,c > 0$) over $[0,h]$.

$$ f'(x) = m, \quad \sqrt{1 + m^2} = \text{const}. $$

$$ A = 2\pi\sqrt{1+m^2} \int_0^h (mx + c); dx = 2\pi\sqrt{1+m^2}\left[\frac{m h^2}{2} + ch\right]. $$

For a right circular cone ($c = 0$, $f(x) = \frac{r}{h}x$, radius $r = mh$):

$$ A = 2\pi \cdot \frac{r}{h} \cdot \sqrt{1 + \frac{r^2}{h^2}} \cdot \frac{h^2}{2} = \pi r \sqrt{h^2 + r^2} = \pi r \ell, $$

where $\ell = \sqrt{h^2 + r^2}$ is the slant height. Classical formula confirmed. $\checkmark$

### Example 4.3 — Torus

Revolve the circle $(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2 = r^2$ (with $b > r$, so the circle doesn't touch the $x$-axis) about the $x$-axis. Parameterize the generating circle as $x = a + r\cos\phi$, $y = b + r\sin\phi$ for $\phi \in [0, 2\pi]$.

Here $f = b + r\sin\phi$ is the radius, and arc length element is $r,d\phi$ (since the circle has radius $r$):

$$ A = 2\pi \int_0^{2\pi} (b + r\sin\phi), r; d\phi = 2\pi r \left[b\phi - r\cos\phi\right]_0^{2\pi} = 2\pi r \cdot 2\pi b = 4\pi^2 br. $$

This is precisely Pappus's theorem: $A = 2\pi \bar{y} \cdot L$ where $\bar{y} = b$ is the centroid of the generating circle and $L = 2\pi r$ is its circumference.

## §6. Pappus's Centroid Theorem

**Theorem 6.1 (Pappus, ~320 CE).** The area of a surface of revolution generated by rotating a curve $\mathcal{C}$ (not crossing the axis) about an external axis equals

$$ A = 2\pi \bar{r} \cdot L, $$

where $\bar{r}$ is the distance from the _centroid_ of $\mathcal{C}$ to the axis, and $L$ is the arc length of $\mathcal{C}$.

_Proof from Theorem 3.1._ The arc length of $y = f(x)$ over $[a,b]$ is $L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx$. The centroid's $y$-coordinate (= distance to axis) is

$$ \bar{r} = \frac{1}{L}\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1+(f')^2}; dx. $$

Then $2\pi\bar{r} L = 2\pi\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx = A$. $\blacksquare$

> **Intuition.** The surface area depends only on _how far away_ the curve's center of mass is from the axis, not on any detailed shape. This is a deep theorem in the guise of a simple formula.

## §7. The Differential Geometric Perspective

A surface of revolution is a special _Riemannian manifold_ with $SO(2)$ symmetry. Let's see what this buys us.

**The First Fundamental Form.** With $\mathbf{r}(x,\theta) = (x, f\cos\theta, f\sin\theta)$, compute the metric:

$$ g_{xx} = \mathbf{r}_x \cdot \mathbf{r}_x = 1 + (f')^2, \quad g_{x\theta} = \mathbf{r}_x \cdot \mathbf{r}_\theta = 0, \quad g_{\theta\theta} = \mathbf{r}_\theta \cdot \mathbf{r}_\theta = f^2. $$

So the metric tensor is:

$$ [g_{ij}] = \begin{pmatrix} 1 + (f')^2 & 0 \ 0 & f^2 \end{pmatrix}. $$

The area element $dA = \sqrt{\det g}; dx, d\theta = f\sqrt{1+(f')^2}; dx, d\theta$, consistent with §2.

**Principal Curvatures.** Using the second fundamental form (computed from $\hat{n} = \mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta / |\cdots|$), the two principal curvatures of a surface of revolution are:

$$ \kappa_1 = \frac{-f''}{(1+(f')^2)^{3/2}} \quad \text{(meridional curvature)}, $$ $$ \kappa_2 = \frac{1}{f\sqrt{1+(f')^2}} \quad \text{(azimuthal curvature)}. $$

**Minimal Surfaces.** Mean curvature $H = \frac{1}{2}(\kappa_1 + \kappa_2) = 0$ gives the ODE:

$$ \frac{-f''}{(1+(f')^2)^{3/2}} + \frac{1}{f(1+(f')^2)^{1/2}} = 0 \implies ff'' = 1 + (f')^2. $$

The solution is $f(x) = c\cosh\left(\frac{x-x_0}{c}\right)$ — the **catenoid**, the only minimal surface of revolution.

> **The Catenoid:** $f(x) = a\cosh(x/a)$. This is the shape taken by a soap film stretched between two coaxial rings — nature minimizes surface area.

## §8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This section makes explicit the surprising appearances of surfaces of revolution and their closely related mathematics in modern AI.

### 8.1 Loss Landscapes and Radial Symmetry

In neural network training, the _loss landscape_ $\mathcal{L}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is a hypersurface in parameter space. For simple models (e.g., single-layer networks with weight matrix $W$), the loss often depends only on $|W|$ or on singular values — creating approximate **radial symmetry**, which is exactly the symmetry principle behind surfaces of revolution.

More precisely: if $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = h(|\theta|)$ for some $h: \mathbb{R}_{\geq 0} \to \mathbb{R}$, the level sets ${\mathcal{L} = c}$ are _spheres_ — surfaces of revolution about any axis through the origin.

**SGD on a rotationally symmetric loss** follows the gradient radially: $\dot{\theta} = -\nabla \mathcal{L} = -h'(|\theta|)\frac{\theta}{|\theta|}$. The trajectory lies on a ray — one-dimensional motion on a high-dimensional surface.

### 8.2 Radial Basis Functions (RBFs)

A **radial basis function** is any function of the form $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = \phi(|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{c}|)$ — depending only on distance to a center $\mathbf{c}$. Common examples:

|Name|Formula|
|---|---|
|Gaussian|$\phi(r) = e^{-r^2/\sigma^2}$|
|Multiquadric|$\phi(r) = \sqrt{r^2 + c^2}$|
|Thin-plate spline|$\phi(r) = r^2 \log r$|

The graph of $z = \phi(|\mathbf{x}|)$ over $\mathbb{R}^2$ is literally a **surface of revolution** — it is the curve $z = \phi(r)$ revolved about the $z$-axis. The Gaussian RBF produces a bell surface; the multiquadric produces a hyperboloid of revolution.

RBF networks approximate functions as $\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \sum_i w_i \phi(|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{c}_i|)$. They are universal approximators and interpolate scattered data exactly.

### 8.3 Convolutional Neural Networks and Toroidal Topology

The weight-sharing in CNNs can be understood through the topology of their parameter spaces. The space of circular convolution kernels is a torus $T^n = (S^1)^n$ — a product of circles, each of which generates a surface of revolution. Analyzing CNN equivariance requires understanding this toroidal geometry.

### 8.4 Gaussian Processes and Stationary Kernels

A kernel $k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = k(|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{y}|)$ is called _stationary_ and _isotropic_. In 2D, the level set ${(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}): k(|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}|) = c}$ is a circle; the graph of $k$ over $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a surface of revolution. The **spectral density** of such a kernel (its Fourier transform) also has rotational symmetry — this is Bochner's theorem and underlies the Matérn class of GP kernels widely used in Bayesian optimization (e.g., hyperparameter tuning in deep learning).

### 8.5 Manifold Hypothesis and Intrinsic Geometry

The _manifold hypothesis_ posits that high-dimensional data lies near a low-dimensional manifold. If this manifold has approximate rotational symmetry (common in image manifolds, e.g., rotated objects), one can model it as a surface of revolution plus small perturbations. The intrinsic geometry (metric, curvature) of a surface of revolution — computed via §7 — directly governs the quality of manifold learning algorithms like UMAP and t-SNE.

### 8.6 Normalizing Flows and Change of Variables

Normalizing flows transform a simple base distribution (often Gaussian, which is itself radially symmetric — a surface of revolution in density space) into a complex distribution via an invertible map $T$. The change-of-variables formula

$$ p_X(\mathbf{x}) = p_Z(T^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) \left|\det \frac{\partial T^{-1}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\right| $$

has direct parallels with our surface area computation: $|\mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta|$ is precisely the _Jacobian_ of the parameterization map $\mathbf{r}: [a,b]\times[0,2\pi] \to S$. Training a normalizing flow is, in a precise sense, learning an optimal "reparameterization" of a manifold — the same problem as finding the best parameterization of a surface of revolution.

The deeper theme: **rotational symmetry reduces a 2D problem to 1D**. The cross-sectional structure collapses all $\theta$-dependence into the factor $2\pi$, leaving a single integral over the profile curve. This is an instance of the far more general principle: _symmetry reduces dimension_, which appears everywhere from physics to representation theory to the architecture of neural networks.
