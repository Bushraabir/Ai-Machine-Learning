

> _"The divergence asks how much a field spreads out. The curl asks how much it spins. Together they are the complete local description of a vector field — the two numbers that determine everything about its infinitesimal behavior."_

---

## §1. Motivation — Measuring Rotation in a Vector Field

Imagine dropping a small paddle wheel into a flowing fluid. If the fluid rotates the wheel, the field has **curl** at that point. If the wheel translates without spinning, the curl is zero there. The curl captures the **local rotational tendency** of a vector field — how much, and in which direction, the field tends to rotate nearby matter.

This is not the same as the field itself being circular in shape. A field can look perfectly straight and still have nonzero curl; a field can look circular and have zero curl. Curl is an infinitesimal, local measurement — not a global geometric description.

The deepest reason to care: curl is the object that appears on the right side of **Stokes' theorem** and the left side of **Maxwell's equations**. It is the bridge between local field behavior and global flux through surfaces. In physics it governs electromagnetism, fluid mechanics, and elasticity. In mathematics it governs the cohomology of differential forms.

## §2. Definition

### 2.1 The Curl in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Let $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ be a $C^1$ vector field. The **curl** of $\mathbf{F}$ is the vector field:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \text{curl},\mathbf{F} = \left\langle \frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z},; \frac{\partial P}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial R}{\partial x},; \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} \right\rangle$$

**Mnemonic — the formal determinant:**

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix} \mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ \dfrac{\partial}{\partial x} & \dfrac{\partial}{\partial y} & \dfrac{\partial}{\partial z} \[6pt] P & Q & R \end{vmatrix}$$

Expand along the first row: $$= \mathbf{i}\left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z}\right) - \mathbf{j}\left(\frac{\partial R}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial z}\right) + \mathbf{k}\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)$$

> **Warning.** This determinant is a mnemonic, not a true determinant — its entries are not numbers but operators and functions. Treat the expansion mechanically: operator acts on function below it.

### 2.2 Components and Their Meaning

Each component of $\nabla \times \mathbf{F}$ measures rotation in a coordinate plane:

|Component|Formula|Measures rotation in...|About axis...|
|---|---|---|---|
|$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_x$|$R_y - Q_z$|$yz$-plane|$x$-axis|
|$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_y$|$P_z - R_x$|$xz$-plane|$y$-axis|
|$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_z$|$Q_x - P_y$|$xy$-plane|$z$-axis|

The **direction** of $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ is the axis of rotation (by the right-hand rule). The **magnitude** $|\nabla\times\mathbf{F}|$ is the rate of rotation.

### 2.3 The Scalar Curl in $\mathbb{R}^2$

For a planar field $\mathbf{F} = \langle P(x,y), Q(x,y), 0\rangle$, the curl has only a $z$-component:

$$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = \left\langle 0, 0, \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right\rangle$$

We call the scalar $\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}$ the **scalar curl** or **2D curl** of $\mathbf{F}$. This is the quantity appearing in Green's theorem.

## §3. Intrinsic (Coordinate-Free) Definition

The determinant formula is convenient for computation but hides the geometric meaning. The coordinate-free definition is more fundamental.

> **Definition (Curl via Stokes' theorem).** For a $C^1$ vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$, the curl $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ at a point $p$ is the unique vector field such that for every unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$:
> 
> $$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})(p) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}} = \lim_{A(D_\varepsilon)\to 0} \frac{1}{A(D_\varepsilon)} \oint_{\partial D_\varepsilon} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$
> 
> where $D_\varepsilon$ is a disk of area $A(D_\varepsilon)$ centered at $p$ with normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$, and the boundary is oriented by the right-hand rule with respect to $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

**In words:** The component of curl in direction $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is the **circulation per unit area** around infinitesimally small loops perpendicular to $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$.

This is the paddle-wheel interpretation made precise:

- Orient the paddle wheel with its axis pointing in direction $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$
- $(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is proportional to the angular velocity of the wheel
- Maximize over all $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$: the direction that maximizes spin is the direction of $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$

---

## §4. Full Proof: Coordinate Formula from Intrinsic Definition

We verify that the coordinate formula arises from the intrinsic definition. We derive the $z$-component; the others follow by symmetry.

**Setup.** Take $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \mathbf{k}$ (the $z$-axis). Let $D_\varepsilon$ be the disk of radius $\varepsilon$ centered at $(a,b,c)$ in the plane $z = c$, with boundary $C_\varepsilon$ oriented counterclockwise (viewed from above).

**Apply Green's theorem to $C_\varepsilon$:**

$$\oint_{C_\varepsilon} P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_{D_\varepsilon} \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA$$

**Divide by the area $A(D_\varepsilon) = \pi\varepsilon^2$ and take $\varepsilon \to 0$:**

By the **Mean Value Theorem for integrals**, there exists a point $(x_\varepsilon, y_\varepsilon) \in D_\varepsilon$ such that:

$$\frac{1}{\pi\varepsilon^2}\iint_{D_\varepsilon}\left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA = \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)(x_\varepsilon, y_\varepsilon, c)$$

As $\varepsilon \to 0$, $(x_\varepsilon, y_\varepsilon) \to (a,b)$, so by continuity:

$$\lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\frac{1}{A(D_\varepsilon)}\oint_{C_\varepsilon} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)(a,b,c) = (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\mathbf{k} \quad\square$$

Repeating with $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \mathbf{i}$ and $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \mathbf{j}$ gives the other components.

## §5. Fundamental Identities

These identities should be internalized, not just memorized.

### 5.1 Curl of a Gradient is Zero

$$\nabla \times (\nabla f) = \mathbf{0}$$

**Proof.** Compute the $z$-component: $$(\nabla\times\nabla f)_z = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(f_y) - \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(f_x) = f_{yx} - f_{xy} = 0$$

by **Clairaut's theorem** (assuming $f \in C^2$). Similarly for all components. $\square$

**Meaning:** Gradient fields have no rotation. Conservative fields are irrotational. This is the analytical content of the FTLI: a gradient field can do no net work around a closed loop.

### 5.2 Divergence of a Curl is Zero

$$\nabla \cdot (\nabla \times \mathbf{F}) = 0$$

**Proof.** Direct computation (for $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$):

$$\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(R_y - Q_z) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(P_z - R_x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(Q_x - P_y)$$

$$= R_{xy} - Q_{xz} + P_{yz} - R_{yx} + Q_{zx} - P_{zy}$$

$$= (R_{xy} - R_{yx}) + (P_{yz} - P_{zy}) + (Q_{zx} - Q_{xz}) = 0$$

by Clairaut's theorem (assuming $\mathbf{F} \in C^2$). $\square$

**Meaning:** Curl fields have no sources or sinks. A magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ satisfies $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ automatically — there are no magnetic monopoles, as a mathematical consequence.

### 5.3 Vector Laplacian Identity

$$\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = \nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{F}$$

where $\nabla^2\mathbf{F} = \langle \nabla^2 P, \nabla^2 Q, \nabla^2 R\rangle$ is the componentwise Laplacian. This identity is central to electromagnetic wave equations.

### 5.4 Curl of a Cross Product

$$\nabla\times(\mathbf{F}\times\mathbf{G}) = \mathbf{F}(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{G}) - \mathbf{G}(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}) + (\mathbf{G}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{F} - (\mathbf{F}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{G}$$

### 5.5 Curl of $f\mathbf{F}$

$$\nabla\times(f\mathbf{F}) = f(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) + (\nabla f)\times\mathbf{F}$$

## §7. Stokes' Theorem — The Curl's Global Theorem

The intrinsic definition of curl was:

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\frac{1}{A(D_\varepsilon)}\oint_{\partial D_\varepsilon}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

Integrate this over a surface $S$ with unit normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ — you get **Stokes' theorem**:

$$\boxed{\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}}$$

The flux of the curl through a surface equals the circulation of $\mathbf{F}$ around its boundary. Green's theorem is the special case where $S$ is a flat planar region and $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \mathbf{k}$.

**Key observation:** The boundary $\partial S$ on the right depends only on the **boundary** of $S$, not the interior. So for any two surfaces $S_1, S_2$ with the same boundary $C$:

$$\iint_{S_1}(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_{S_2}(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

The flux of curl is **independent of the surface**, depending only on the boundary curve.

## §8. Irrotational Fields and Conservative Fields

> **Theorem.** Let $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$ be simply connected. For a $C^1$ vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on $U$, the following are equivalent:
> 
> 1. $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ for some $f \in C^2(U)$ (**conservative**)
> 2. $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ on $U$ (**irrotational**)
> 3. $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for every closed curve $C$ in $U$
> 4. $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is path-independent in $U$

$(1)\Rightarrow(2)$: Identity $\nabla\times\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$.

$(2)\Rightarrow(3)$: By Stokes' theorem on any surface bounded by $C$.

$(3)\Rightarrow(4)$: Take two paths $C_1, C_2$ with same endpoints; $C_1 - C_2$ is closed.

$(4)\Rightarrow(1)$: Define $f(\mathbf{r}) = \int_{p_0}^{\mathbf{r}}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ (well-defined by path-independence); then $\nabla f = \mathbf{F}$.

**On non-simply connected domains:** $(1)\Rightarrow(2)$ always; $(2)\not\Rightarrow(1)$ in general. The obstruction lives in $H^1_{\text{dR}}(U)$.

## §9. Worked Examples

### Example A — Basic Computation

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle xz, xyz, -y^2\rangle$. Find $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$.**

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_x = \frac{\partial(-y^2)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial(xyz)}{\partial z} = -2y - xy$$

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_y = \frac{\partial(xz)}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial(-y^2)}{\partial x} = x - 0 = x$$

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_z = \frac{\partial(xyz)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(xz)}{\partial y} = yz - 0 = yz$$

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \langle -2y - xy,; x,; yz\rangle$$

---

### Example B — Testing for Conservative Field

**Is $\mathbf{F} = \langle 2xy + z^3,; x^2,; 3xz^2\rangle$ conservative?**

Compute curl:

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_x = \frac{\partial(3xz^2)}{\partial y} - \frac{\partial(x^2)}{\partial z} = 0 - 0 = 0 \checkmark$$

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_y = \frac{\partial(2xy+z^3)}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial(3xz^2)}{\partial x} = 3z^2 - 3z^2 = 0 \checkmark$$

$$(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_z = \frac{\partial(x^2)}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(2xy+z^3)}{\partial y} = 2x - 2x = 0 \checkmark$$

$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$. Conservative on $\mathbb{R}^3$ (simply connected).

**Find the potential:** $f_x = 2xy + z^3 \Rightarrow f = x^2y + xz^3 + g(y,z)$. $f_y = x^2 + g_y = x^2 \Rightarrow g_y = 0 \Rightarrow g = h(z)$. $f_z = 3xz^2 + h'(z) = 3xz^2 \Rightarrow h'(z) = 0$.

$$\boxed{f(x,y,z) = x^2 y + xz^3 + C}$$

---

### Example C — Rotational Fields

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x, 0\rangle$ (rigid rotation about $z$-axis).**

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \left\langle 0 - 0,; 0 - 0,; \frac{\partial x}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial(-y)}{\partial y}\right\rangle = \langle 0, 0, 1-(-1)\rangle = \langle 0, 0, 2\rangle$$

The curl is $2\mathbf{k}$ — a constant vector pointing in the $z$-direction. This makes sense: $\mathbf{F}$ rotates everything about the $z$-axis with angular velocity $\omega$, and the relation between curl and angular velocity is $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = 2\boldsymbol{\omega}$ (see §11).

---

### Example D — Nonzero Curl, Non-Obvious Example

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle 0, 0, y\rangle$ (field pointing in $z$-direction, magnitude varying with $y$).**

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \left\langle \frac{\partial y}{\partial y} - 0,; 0 - \frac{\partial y}{\partial z},; 0 - 0\right\rangle = \langle 1, 0, 0\rangle$$

The curl is $\mathbf{i}$. The field looks like it just points in the $z$-direction — no obvious rotation — yet the curl is nonzero. **Why?** Because the field is _stronger_ at large $y$ than at small $y$: a paddle wheel with its axis pointing in the $x$-direction would indeed spin (the high-$y$ face gets pushed harder in the $z$-direction than the low-$y$ face).

This is the crucial lesson: **curl measures velocity shear**, not just vortical geometry.

---

### Example E — Verifying $\text{div}(\text{curl}) = 0$

For $\mathbf{F} = \langle xy, yz, xz\rangle$:

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \langle z_y - yz_z,; xy_z - xz_x,; yz_x - xy_y\rangle = \langle 0-y,; 0-x,; 0-x\rangle = \langle -y, -x, -x\rangle$$

$$\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = \frac{\partial(-y)}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial(-x)}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial(-x)}{\partial z} = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0 \checkmark$$

## §11. Curl in Physics

### 11.1 Rigid Body Rotation

A rigid body rotating with angular velocity vector $\boldsymbol{\omega} = \omega,\mathbf{k}$ has velocity field: $$\mathbf{v} = \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r} = \omega\mathbf{k}\times\langle x,y,z\rangle = \langle -\omega y, \omega x, 0\rangle$$

Compute: $\nabla\times\mathbf{v} = \langle 0, 0, \omega - (-\omega)\rangle = \langle 0, 0, 2\omega\rangle = 2\boldsymbol{\omega}$.

$$\boxed{\nabla\times\mathbf{v} = 2\boldsymbol{\omega}}$$

**The curl of the velocity field is twice the angular velocity.** This is the precise relationship between local rotation (curl) and macroscopic spin. It is why curl is also called the **vorticity** in fluid mechanics.

### 11.2 Maxwell's Equations

Curl appears in **two** of Maxwell's four equations:

**Faraday's Law (Induction):** $$\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\frac{\partial \mathbf{B}}{\partial t}$$

A time-varying magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ generates a rotating electric field $\mathbf{E}$. The curl of $\mathbf{E}$ is the rate of change of $\mathbf{B}$. This is the principle behind electric generators.

**Ampère-Maxwell Law:** $$\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial\mathbf{E}}{\partial t}$$

Electric current $\mathbf{J}$ and a time-varying electric field generate a rotating magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$. Maxwell's addition of the $\partial\mathbf{E}/\partial t$ term (displacement current) was the key insight that predicted electromagnetic waves.

**Electromagnetic wave equation** (derived from the two curl equations): $$\nabla^2\mathbf{E} = \mu_0\varepsilon_0\frac{\partial^2\mathbf{E}}{\partial t^2}$$

using the identity $\nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{E}) = \nabla(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}) - \nabla^2\mathbf{E}$. The wave speed is $c = 1/\sqrt{\mu_0\varepsilon_0}$.

### 11.3 Fluid Mechanics — Vorticity

In fluid mechanics, the **vorticity** of a velocity field $\mathbf{u}$ is: $$\boldsymbol{\xi} = \nabla\times\mathbf{u}$$

- **Irrotational flow** ($\boldsymbol{\xi} = \mathbf{0}$): no local spinning; typical of potential flows far from boundaries
- **Vortex filaments**: thin tubes where $|\boldsymbol{\xi}|$ is large — like tornadoes or wingtip vortices
- **Vorticity equation** (from Navier-Stokes): $$\frac{\partial\boldsymbol{\xi}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{u}\cdot\nabla)\boldsymbol{\xi} = (\boldsymbol{\xi}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u} + \nu\nabla^2\boldsymbol{\xi}$$

The term $(\boldsymbol{\xi}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{u}$ is **vortex stretching** — the mechanism by which turbulence cascades to smaller scales.

## §12. The Helmholtz Decomposition

**Theorem (Helmholtz).** Every $C^2$ vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on $\mathbb{R}^3$ (decaying sufficiently at infinity) decomposes uniquely as:

$$\mathbf{F} = -\nabla\phi + \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$$

where:

- $\phi$ is a scalar potential: $-\nabla\phi$ is the **irrotational part** (curl-free)
- $\mathbf{A}$ is a vector potential: $\nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ is the **solenoidal part** (divergence-free)

**Explicitly:** $$\phi(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\int_{\mathbb{R}^3}\frac{\nabla'\cdot\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|},dV', \qquad \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\int_{\mathbb{R}^3}\frac{\nabla'\times\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|},dV'$$

**Consequence:** $\mathbf{F}$ is completely determined by its divergence and curl:

- $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$ determines the irrotational part
- $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ determines the solenoidal part

This is **why** Maxwell's equations use both div and curl — together they fully specify the electromagnetic field from its sources (charges and currents).

## §13. Curl in Curvilinear Coordinates

For completeness — these arise in physics and numerical ML (e.g., polar grids).

### 13.1 Cylindrical Coordinates $(r, \theta, z)$

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \left\langle \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial F_z}{\partial\theta} - \frac{\partial F_\theta}{\partial z},; \frac{\partial F_r}{\partial z} - \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial r},; \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial(rF_\theta)}{\partial r} - \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial F_r}{\partial\theta}\right\rangle$$

### 13.2 Spherical Coordinates $(r, \theta, \phi)$

$$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\left\langle \frac{\partial(\sin\theta, F_\phi)}{\partial\theta} - \frac{\partial F_\theta}{\partial\phi},; \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial F_r}{\partial\phi} - \frac{\partial(rF_\phi)}{\partial r},; \frac{\partial(rF_\theta)}{\partial r} - \frac{\partial F_r}{\partial\theta}\right\rangle$$

> _Honestly: always look these up. The Cartesian formula is the one to internalize. The curvilinear versions follow from the metric tensor; derive them from first principles if needed rather than memorizing._

## §14. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> Curl — as a measure of local rotation and antisymmetric structure — appears throughout modern ML in ways that are only recently being made explicit. The keyword to watch for: **antisymmetry**, **rotation**, **non-conservative dynamics**.

### 14.1 GAN Training Dynamics — Curl is the Problem

As discussed in the extended Green's theorem note, GAN training in the joint parameter space $(\theta_G, \theta_D)$ has a gradient vector field: $$\mathbf{v} = (-\nabla_G\mathcal{L},; \nabla_D\mathcal{L})$$

The **curl of $\mathbf{v}$** is the antisymmetric part of the Jacobian $J_\mathbf{v}$: $$(\nabla\times\mathbf{v})_{ij} = \frac{\partial v_j}{\partial \theta_i} - \frac{\partial v_i}{\partial \theta_j} = J_{ij} - J_{ji}$$

When $\nabla\times\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$, gradient descent cycles around the equilibrium. The **magnitude** of the curl quantifies how badly the training cycles.

**Symplectic Gradient Adjustment (SGA)** (Balduzzi et al. 2018) explicitly subtracts the curl component: $$\mathbf{v}_{\text{SGA}} = \mathbf{v} - \lambda,(\text{antisymmetric part of }J_\mathbf{v})\cdot\mathbf{v}$$

This is, literally, computing the discrete curl of the training dynamics and correcting for it.

### 14.2 Second-Order Optimization — The Hessian and Its Antisymmetric Part

The Jacobian of the gradient $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ is the Hessian $H$. By Clairaut's theorem, $H$ is symmetric for smooth loss functions. But in **multi-player games** (GANs, multi-agent RL), the effective "Hessian" of the joint dynamics is **not** the Hessian of a single function — it has an antisymmetric part that represents the curl.

The **Competitive Gradient Descent** algorithm (Schäfer & Anandkumar 2019) computes: $$\text{symmetric part of } J = \frac{J + J^T}{2} \quad (\text{gradient component})$$ $$\text{antisymmetric part of } J = \frac{J - J^T}{2} \quad (\text{curl component})$$

The second-order update uses both, correctly accounting for both descent and rotation.

### 14.3 Neural Network Weight Space — Curl-Free Flows

**Normalizing flows** (RealNVP, Glow, Neural ODE flows) require computing the Jacobian determinant of a learned map $\mathbf{g}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$. For **volume-preserving flows** (important for sampling and density estimation), one constraint is $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{g} = 0$ (divergence-free). But there is a complementary condition: flows can be decomposed (Helmholtz) into gradient (curl-free) and solenoidal (div-free) components, and the **two components contribute differently** to the Jacobian determinant.

**FFJORD** (Chen et al. 2018) and **Neural ODEs** define a continuous flow: $$\frac{d\mathbf{z}}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{z}, t)$$

The log-likelihood change is: $$\frac{d\log p(\mathbf{z})}{dt} = -\nabla\cdot f_\theta$$

Note: the **divergence** of the flow controls log-likelihood. The **curl** of the flow is "free" — it doesn't affect the density. This means curl-free and solenoidal flows have entirely different roles in normalizing flows.

### 14.4 Attention Mechanisms — Antisymmetric Interactions

In **self-attention**, the attention matrix $A_{ij} = \text{softmax}(q_i \cdot k_j / \sqrt{d})$ is generally **not symmetric** — $A_{ij} \neq A_{ji}$. The antisymmetric part of the attention matrix: $$A^{\text{anti}}_{ij} = \frac{A_{ij} - A_{ji}}{2}$$

is the discrete analog of curl — it measures asymmetric, rotational information flow between tokens. Recent work on **Antisymmetric Transformers** and **Directional Graph Attention** explicitly constructs attention mechanisms whose antisymmetric part encodes directed information flow, exploiting the curl structure.

### 14.5 Hamiltonian Neural Networks and Symplectic Structure

**Hamiltonian Neural Networks** (Greydanus et al. 2019) learn energy-conserving dynamics. A Hamiltonian system satisfies: $$\dot{q} = \frac{\partial H}{\partial p}, \qquad \dot{p} = -\frac{\partial H}{\partial q}$$

The vector field $\mathbf{v} = (\dot{q}, \dot{p})$ is:

- **Divergence-free**: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v} = H_{qp} - H_{pq} = 0$ (by Clairaut)
- **Not curl-free**: the dynamics are inherently rotational

In $2D$ phase space, the "curl" of $\mathbf{v}$ is: $$\frac{\partial\dot{p}}{\partial q} - \frac{\partial\dot{q}}{\partial p} = -H_{qq} - H_{pp} = -\nabla^2 H$$

Hamiltonian systems preserve **phase space volume** (Liouville's theorem) and have canonical symplectic structure — the $2D$ analog of being divergence-free while having specific curl structure. HNNs enforce this by parameterizing $\mathbf{v} = J\nabla H$ where $J = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\ -1 & 0\end{pmatrix}$ is the symplectic matrix.

### 14.6 Physics-Informed Neural Networks — Learning Curl-Free and Solenoidal Fields

**PINNs** for fluid dynamics must learn velocity fields satisfying:

- $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$ (incompressibility — divergence-free)
- $\nabla\times\boldsymbol{\xi} = \nabla\times(\nabla\times\mathbf{u})$ (vorticity transport)

The Helmholtz decomposition is used to enforce incompressibility: parameterize $\mathbf{u} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ for a learned vector potential $\mathbf{A}$. Since $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{A}) = 0$ always, incompressibility is automatically satisfied — the PINN only needs to learn $\mathbf{A}$.

This is **curl as a constraint mechanism**: by writing the field as a curl, you enforce the divergence-free condition by construction, reducing the learning problem.

### 14.7 Graph Neural Networks — Discrete Curl and Hodge Theory

On a **simplicial complex** (graph with triangles), the discrete curl is the **coboundary operator** $\delta_1$:

$$(\delta_1 \mathbf{f})_{ijk} = f_{ij} + f_{jk} + f_{ki}$$

for edge flow $\mathbf{f}$, where $(ijk)$ is an oriented triangle. This measures the "circulation" around each triangle — the discrete curl.

The **Hodge decomposition** of edge flows: $$\mathbf{f} = \underbrace{\nabla\phi}_{\text{gradient/irrotational}} + \underbrace{\nabla\times\mathbf{A}}_{\text{curl/solenoidal}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{h}}_{\text{harmonic}}$$

**Hodge-aware GNNs** (Ebli et al. 2020; Yang et al. 2021) decompose signals into these three components, processing each separately. The curl component captures cyclic flow patterns (e.g., loops in citation networks, cycles in traffic flow), which traditional GNNs miss entirely because they only process node features.

**Applications:**

- **Traffic prediction**: $\mathbf{f}$ = traffic flow on road edges; curl detects congestion loops
- **Ranking from pairwise comparisons**: gradient component = consistent global ranking; curl component = cyclic inconsistencies (A beats B, B beats C, C beats A)
- **Signal processing on meshes**: curl-free and solenoidal components of 3D surface flows

### 14.8 Equivariant Neural Networks — SO(3) and Curl

**Equivariant networks** (e3nn, SE(3)-Transformers, NequIP) process 3D data (molecules, point clouds) in a way that respects rotational symmetry. Their features are **irreducible representations of SO(3)**: scalars (invariant), vectors (equivariant), and higher-order tensors.

The **curl** is a key operation in these networks:

- The cross product $\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}$ (related to curl) maps two vectors to a pseudovector
- Tensor products of vector features are decomposed into irreps, and the antisymmetric part (the curl-like component) is separated from the symmetric part

The mathematical backbone — that curl transforms as a pseudovector under rotations — is what allows equivariant networks to correctly handle chirality (left- vs right-handedness) in molecular property prediction.

## §15. Summary

$$\boxed{\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k}\ \partial_x & \partial_y & \partial_z \ P & Q & R\end{vmatrix} = \langle R_y - Q_z,; P_z - R_x,; Q_x - P_y\rangle}$$

|Property|Statement|
|---|---|
|**Geometric meaning**|Circulation per unit area; local rotation axis and rate|
|**Paddle wheel**|Direction of $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ = spin axis; magnitude = twice angular velocity|
|**Curl of gradient**|$\nabla\times\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$ always ($C^2$)|
|**Div of curl**|$\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = \mathbf{0}$ always ($C^2$)|
|**Irrotational**|$\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ ↔ conservative (on simply connected domains)|
|**Stokes' theorem**|$\oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_S(\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S}$|
|**Rigid rotation**|$\mathbf{v} = \boldsymbol{\omega}\times\mathbf{r} \Rightarrow \nabla\times\mathbf{v} = 2\boldsymbol{\omega}$|
|**Maxwell**|$\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial_t\mathbf{B}$; $\nabla\times\mathbf{B} = \mu_0\mathbf{J} + \mu_0\varepsilon_0\partial_t\mathbf{E}$|
|**Helmholtz**|$\mathbf{F} = -\nabla\phi + \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ (irrotational + solenoidal)|
|**ML connection**|GAN curl = cycling; Hamiltonian = divergence-free rotation; Hodge GNN = discrete curl decomposition|

## §16. Personal Notes

- The determinant mnemonic is essential for computation; the intrinsic definition (circulation per unit area) is essential for understanding. Know both. When you forget the formula on an exam, derive it from the intrinsic definition using Green's theorem — you can reconstruct the $z$-component in two lines.
- The shear flow example ($\mathbf{F} = \langle y, 0, 0\rangle$, curl $= -\mathbf{k}$) is the most important conceptual example. It demolishes the naive intuition that "curl means the field goes in circles." It does not. Curl is about **velocity gradients perpendicular to the flow direction**.
- $\nabla\times\nabla f = 0$ and $\nabla\cdot\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = 0$ are both consequences of Clairaut's theorem. They are not independent theorems — they are the same algebraic identity $d\circ d = 0$ in the language of differential forms.
- The Helmholtz decomposition is the vector calculus version of the fact that any matrix decomposes into symmetric + antisymmetric parts. The symmetric part = irrotational (gradient), antisymmetric part = solenoidal (curl). This analogy is exact and worth meditating on.
- In ML: every time you see an antisymmetric matrix, an antisymmetric update rule, or non-conservative dynamics, you are seeing the discrete fingerprint of curl. The recent surge of interest in Hamiltonian networks, symplectic integrators, and Hodge GNNs is the field finally taking curl seriously as a computational object, not just a physics concept.
- The four Maxwell's equations are the most beautiful application of the divergence and curl. Two of the four are $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ (div of curl $= 0$, automatic) and $\nabla\times\mathbf{E} = -\partial_t\mathbf{B}$ (curl = rate of change). The whole structure of electromagnetism is organized around div and curl.
