## 1. Motivation

In single-variable calculus, $\int_a^b f(x),dx$ integrates a function over a straight interval on the $x$-axis. But the world is not flat. We want to:

- Compute the **work** done by a force field $\mathbf{F}$ on a particle moving along a curved path.
- Find the **mass** of a wire shaped like a curve, given its linear density $\rho$.
- Measure the **circulation** of a fluid around a loop.
- Ask whether a vector field is conservative by testing loop integrals.

All of these reduce to one unifying idea: **integration along a curve**. This is the line integral.

There are two fundamentally different kinds, and keeping them straight is crucial:

|Kind|Input field|Output|Physical meaning|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Scalar line integral**|Scalar field $f$|Scalar|Arc-length weighted average; mass of a wire|
|**Vector line integral**|Vector field $\mathbf{F}$|Scalar|Work done; circulation|

## 2. Parametrised Curves â€” Prerequisites

A **smooth curve** $C$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is the image of a $C^1$ map $\mathbf{r} : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}^n$ with $\mathbf{r}'(t) \neq \mathbf{0}$ for all $t \in [a,b]$.

In $\mathbb{R}^2$: $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t)\rangle$. In $\mathbb{R}^3$: $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t), z(t)\rangle$.

**Arc length element:** $$ds = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt = \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dz}{dt}\right)^2},dt$$

**Displacement vector element:** $$d\mathbf{r} = \mathbf{r}'(t),dt = \langle dx, dy, dz\rangle$$

Note the distinction:

- $ds$ is a **scalar** â€” the infinitesimal arc length (always positive).
- $d\mathbf{r}$ is a **vector** â€” the infinitesimal displacement (carries direction).

### Reparametrisation

Different parametrisations of the same geometric curve give the same line integral, up to orientation.

> **Proposition.** If $\mathbf{r}(t)$, $t \in [a,b]$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{r}}(u) = \mathbf{r}(\phi(u))$, $u \in [c,d]$ where $\phi : [c,d] \to [a,b]$ is $C^1$ with $\phi' > 0$ (same orientation), then: $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} \text{ is the same under both parametrisations.}$$ If $\phi' < 0$ (orientation reversed), the integral changes sign.

This is the higher-dimensional analogue of the substitution rule. The _geometric curve_ matters; the _specific parametrisation_ does not (only its direction).

### Common Parametrisations

|Curve|Parametrisation|Range|
|---|---|---|
|Line segment from $A$ to $B$|$\mathbf{r}(t) = (1-t)A + tB$|$t \in [0,1]$|
|Circle of radius $r$ (CCW)|$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle r\cos t, r\sin t\rangle$|$t \in [0,2\pi]$|
|Helix|$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle a\cos t, a\sin t, bt\rangle$|$t \in [0, 2\pi n]$|
|Graph $y = g(x)$|$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle t, g(t)\rangle$|$t \in [a,b]$|

## 3. Line Integrals of Scalar Fields

### Definition

> **Definition.** Let $f : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be continuous on an open set containing $C$. The **scalar line integral** (line integral with respect to arc length) is: $$\int_C f,ds = \int_a^b f(\mathbf{r}(t)),|\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt$$

### Intuition

Slice $C$ into tiny arc-length pieces $ds_i$. On each piece, $f$ is approximately constant at value $f(\mathbf{r}(t_i))$. The contribution is $f(\mathbf{r}(t_i)),ds_i$. Summing and taking the limit gives $\int_C f,ds$.

**Physical interpretation:**

- If $f = \rho(\mathbf{r})$ is the linear mass density of a wire shaped like $C$, then $\int_C \rho,ds$ is the **total mass**.
- If $f = 1$, then $\int_C 1,ds = \text{arc length of } C$.
- If $f = z$ and $C$ is a curve in $\mathbb{R}^3$, then $\int_C z,ds$ is the first moment about the $xy$-plane.

### Arc Length as a Special Case

$$L(C) = \int_C 1,ds = \int_a^b |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt$$

> **Example.** Arc length of one turn of the helix $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle \cos t, \sin t, t\rangle$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$: $$|\mathbf{r}'(t)| = |(-\sin t, \cos t, 1)| = \sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t + 1} = \sqrt{2}$$ $$L = \int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt{2},dt = 2\sqrt{2},\pi$$

### Independence of Parametrisation (Scalar)

The scalar line integral does **not** change sign under orientation reversal: $$\int_{-C} f,ds = \int_C f,ds$$ because $ds = |\mathbf{r}'|,dt$ is always positive regardless of direction of travel.

## 4. Line Integrals of Vector Fields (Work Integrals)

### Definition

> **Definition.** Let $\mathbf{F} : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be a continuous vector field. The **vector line integral** (line integral of $\mathbf{F}$ along $C$) is: $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t),dt$$

Writing $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$ and $d\mathbf{r} = \langle dx, dy, dz\rangle$, this is also written: $$\int_C P,dx + Q,dy + R,dz$$

### Connection Between the Two Types

Let $\hat{\mathbf{T}}(t) = \dfrac{\mathbf{r}'(t)}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|}$ be the unit tangent vector. Then: $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{T}},ds$$

The vector line integral is the scalar line integral of the **tangential component** of $\mathbf{F}$.

**Physical interpretation.**

- If $\mathbf{F}$ is a force field, $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is the **work** done by $\mathbf{F}$ on a particle travelling along $C$.
- Only the component of force **parallel to the motion** does work. Force perpendicular to $d\mathbf{r}$ contributes zero.
- If $\mathbf{F}$ is a fluid velocity field and $C$ is a closed loop, $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is the **circulation** â€” a measure of how much the fluid swirls around the loop.

### Orientation Matters

$$\int_{-C}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = -\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

Reversing the direction of travel negates the work â€” if you walk uphill, gravity does negative work; walk back down, it does positive work. The **scalar** line integral has no such sign change; the **vector** integral does.

### Computation Procedure

Given $\mathbf{F}(x,y,z)$ and curve $C$ parametrised by $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t), z(t)\rangle$:

1. Substitute: replace $x, y, z$ in $\mathbf{F}$ with $x(t), y(t), z(t)$.
2. Compute: $\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle x'(t), y'(t), z'(t)\rangle$.
3. Dot product: $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t)$.
4. Integrate over $t \in [a,b]$.

## 5. Properties

Let $C$, $C_1$, $C_2$ be smooth curves, $\mathbf{F}$, $\mathbf{G}$ continuous vector fields, $\alpha, \beta \in \mathbb{R}$.

**Linearity:** $$\int_C (\alpha\mathbf{F} + \beta\mathbf{G})\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \alpha\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} + \beta\int_C \mathbf{G}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

**Additivity over curves:** If $C = C_1 \cup C_2$ (end of $C_1$ meets start of $C_2$): $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{C_1}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} + \int_{C_2}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

**Orientation reversal:** $$\int_{-C}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = -\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

**Bound estimate:** $$\left|\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}\right| \leq \max_C|\mathbf{F}|\cdot L(C)$$

## 6. The Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals

This is the most important theorem in the subject â€” the direct generalisation of FTC to curves.

> **Theorem (FTC for Line Integrals).** Let $f : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be $C^1$ on an open set $U$, and let $C$ be a smooth curve in $U$ from point $A = \mathbf{r}(a)$ to point $B = \mathbf{r}(b)$. Then: $$\int_C \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(B) - f(A)$$

**Proof.** $$\int_C \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t),dt = \int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}[f(\mathbf{r}(t))],dt = f(\mathbf{r}(b)) - f(\mathbf{r}(a)) = f(B) - f(A)$$

The second equality is the **multivariable chain rule**; the third is the 1D FTC. $\blacksquare$

**Consequences:**

1. For conservative fields, the line integral depends **only on the endpoints** â€” not on the path.
2. For closed curves ($A = B$): $\oint_C \nabla f\cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(A) - f(A) = 0$.
3. To evaluate $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ for a conservative field, simply **find the potential** and plug in endpoints. No parametrisation needed.

> **Example.** Compute $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F} = \langle yz, xz, xy\rangle$ and $C$ is the twisted cubic $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle t, t^2, t^3\rangle$ from $(0,0,0)$ to $(1,1,1)$.
> 
> **Check conservatism:** We showed earlier $\mathbf{F} = \nabla(xyz)$. âś“
> 
> **Apply FTC:** $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = xyz\Big|_{(0,0,0)}^{(1,1,1)} = 1\cdot 1\cdot 1 - 0 = 1$$
> 
> No integration needed.

## 7. Path Independence and Conservative Fields

The following five conditions are equivalent on a connected open domain $U$:

> **Theorem (Equivalence).** For a continuous vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on a connected open set $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, the following are equivalent:
> 
> (i) $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative: $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ for some $f \in C^1(U)$.
> 
> (ii) $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is **path-independent**: depends only on the endpoints of $C$.
> 
> (iii) $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for every **closed** piecewise-smooth curve $C$ in $U$.
> 
> (iv) $\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = P,dx + Q,dy + R,dz$ is an **exact differential form**.
> 
> (v) _(when $U$ is simply connected and $\mathbf{F} \in C^1$)_ $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{0}$ on $U$.

**Proof of (ii) $\Rightarrow$ (i)** (constructing the potential).

Fix a base point $\mathbf{x}_0 \in U$. Define: $$f(\mathbf{x}) = \int_{C_{\mathbf{x}_0 \to \mathbf{x}}} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

By path independence, $f(\mathbf{x})$ is well-defined (independent of which path $C_{\mathbf{x}_0 \to \mathbf{x}}$ we take). One then verifies $\partial f/\partial x_i = F_i$ by choosing a path that travels in the $\hat{e}_i$ direction for the last segment and applying FTC in 1D. $\blacksquare$

This is the constructive proof â€” it tells you _how_ to build the potential from the line integral.

## 8. Green's Theorem â€” The Bridge to Double Integrals

Green's Theorem converts a line integral around a closed curve into a double integral over the enclosed region.

> **Theorem (Green's Theorem).** Let $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ be a bounded region with positively oriented (counterclockwise), piecewise-smooth boundary $\partial D$. If $P, Q : D \to \mathbb{R}$ are $C^1$, then: $$\oint_{\partial D} P,dx + Q,dy = \iint_D \left(\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y}\right)dA$$

**Orientation convention.** _Positively oriented_ means you traverse $\partial D$ so the interior is always to your **left** â€” counterclockwise for simple regions.

### Two interpretations

**Curl form:** $$\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \iint_D (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})_z,dA$$ The circulation around the boundary equals the total (2D) curl inside.

**Flux form (divergence):** Let $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ be the outward unit normal to $\partial D$. Then: $$\oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds = \iint_D \nabla\cdot\mathbf{F},dA$$ The outward flux through the boundary equals the total divergence inside.

### Area formula (elegant application)

$$\text{Area}(D) = \frac{1}{2}\oint_{\partial D} x,dy - y,dx$$

**Proof.** Apply Green's with $P = -y/2$, $Q = x/2$: $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1$. Then $\iint_D 1,dA = \text{Area}(D)$. $\blacksquare$

This formula is used to compute the area enclosed by any parametrised closed curve â€” e.g., an irregular polygon from surveyor's data (the **shoelace formula** is its discrete analogue).

### Why Green's Theorem is deep

Green's Theorem says the behaviour of $\mathbf{F}$ on the **boundary** encodes information about its derivative **inside**. It is the 2D special case of Stokes' theorem, which is the 3D special case of the **Generalised Stokes' Theorem**: $$\int_{\partial M} \omega = \int_M d\omega$$ where $M$ is a manifold with boundary and $\omega$ is a differential form. All of the "big" integration theorems (Green, Stokes, Divergence) are this one equation in different dimensions.

## 9. Worked Examples

### Example A â€” Direct computation

Compute $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F} = \langle x^2, xy\rangle$ and $C$ is the unit circle, counterclockwise.

**Parametrise:** $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle \cos t, \sin t\rangle$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$. $\mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle -\sin t, \cos t\rangle$. $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \langle \cos^2 t, \cos t\sin t\rangle$.

**Dot product:** $$\mathbf{F}\cdot\mathbf{r}' = \cos^2 t(-\sin t) + \cos t\sin t\cos t = -\cos^2 t\sin t + \cos^2 t\sin t = 0$$

$$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} 0,dt = 0$$

_(This happened to be zero but not because $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative â€” check: $\partial Q/\partial x - \partial P/\partial y = y - 0 = y \neq 0$.)_

---

### Example B â€” Using Green's Theorem

Compute $\oint_C -y,dx + x,dy$ where $C$ is the ellipse $x^2/4 + y^2/9 = 1$, counterclockwise.

**Via Green's:** $P = -y$, $Q = x$: $$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} - \frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 1 - (-1) = 2$$

$$\oint_C = \iint_D 2,dA = 2\cdot\text{Area}(\text{ellipse}) = 2\cdot\pi\cdot 2\cdot 3 = 12\pi$$

_(Computing directly via parametrisation would require trigonometric integration over the ellipse â€” Green's theorem reduces it to a trivial area calculation.)_

---

### Example C â€” Piecewise curve

Compute $\int_C y,dx + x^2,dy$ where $C$ consists of:

- $C_1$: line segment from $(0,0)$ to $(1,0)$
- $C_2$: line segment from $(1,0)$ to $(1,1)$

**On $C_1$:** $y = 0$, $dy = 0$, $x$ from $0$ to $1$. $$\int_{C_1} = \int_0^1 0,dx + x^2\cdot 0 = 0$$

**On $C_2$:** $x = 1$, $dx = 0$, $y$ from $0$ to $1$. $$\int_{C_2} = \int_0^1 y\cdot 0 + 1^2,dy = \int_0^1 1,dy = 1$$

$$\int_C = 0 + 1 = 1$$

---

### Example D â€” Conservative field test and potential

$\mathbf{F} = \langle e^x\sin y,; e^x\cos y\rangle$. Compute $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ from $(0,0)$ to $(1, \pi/2)$ along any path.

**Test:** $\partial Q/\partial x = e^x\cos y$, $\partial P/\partial y = e^x\cos y$. Equal. âś“ Conservative.

**Potential:** $f = \int e^x\sin y,dx = e^x\sin y + g(y)$. $\partial f/\partial y = e^x\cos y + g'(y) = e^x\cos y \implies g'(y) = 0 \implies g = C$. So $f(x,y) = e^x\sin y$.

**FTC:** $$\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(1, \pi/2) - f(0,0) = e^1\sin(\pi/2) - e^0\sin(0) = e - 0 = e$$

## 10. Connection to AI / ML / Deep Learning

### 10.1 Loss Change Along an Optimisation Path

When training a neural network, the parameters follow a trajectory $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$ in parameter space. The **total change in loss** along this path is exactly a line integral:

$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(T)) - \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(0)) = \int_0^T \frac{d}{dt}\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)),dt = \int_0^T \nabla\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t))\cdot\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(t),dt = \int_C \nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta}$$

This is the FTC for line integrals in parameter space. Since $\mathcal{L}$ is a scalar field, it is _trivially_ conservative and this line integral depends only on start and end â€” but the _path_ determines _how fast_ and _how stably_ you get there.

### 10.2 Work Done Against the Gradient

For gradient flow $\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = -\nabla\mathcal{L}$, the integrand is: $$\nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot(-\nabla\mathcal{L}) = -|\nabla\mathcal{L}|^2 \leq 0$$

The line integral of $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ along the gradient flow path is: $$\int_C \nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} = -\int_0^T |\nabla\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}(t))|^2,dt$$

This is always negative â€” gradient descent always decreases loss. The magnitude of the decrease is the $L^2$ norm of the gradient integrated over time. Flat regions ($|\nabla\mathcal{L}| \approx 0$) contribute almost nothing to the integral â€” the network is "stuck."

### 10.3 Path-Dependence and the Non-Conservatism of Adam

The gradient field $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ is always conservative (it is literally a gradient). But the vector fields followed by **Adam, RMSProp, Momentum** are _not_ conservative: $$\mathbf{v}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{\hat{m}}{\sqrt{\hat{v}} + \epsilon}$$ is not the gradient of any scalar. Therefore: $$\oint_C \mathbf{v}\cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta} \neq 0 \text{ in general}$$

This means Adam-like optimisers can traverse **closed loops** in parameter space and end up with a _different_ loss than they started with â€” a phenomenon impossible for pure gradient descent. It also means Adam's trajectory is genuinely path-dependent in a deeper sense: the same start and end point can have different "work done" values along different paths.

This is related to why Adam can oscillate, diverge, or converge to solutions that gradient descent would not find.

### 10.4 Circulation and Attention Mechanisms

The **circulation** of a vector field around a loop, $\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$, measures rotational tendency. In the context of **transformers**, the attention weight matrix $A_{ij} = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{q}_i\cdot\mathbf{k}_j/\sqrt{d})$ defines a kind of weighted flow between tokens. While not literally a vector field circulation, the intuition translates: which tokens does information "circulate" around?

More concretely, in **graph neural networks**, line integrals over graph paths appear when studying how information propagates: $\sum_{(i,j)\in\text{path}} \mathbf{h}_i^T W \mathbf{h}_j$ is a discrete approximation of $\int_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F}$ encodes pairwise node interactions.

### 10.5 Score-Based Models and Path Integrals

In **diffusion model** sampling via Langevin dynamics, the algorithm traces a stochastic path $\mathbf{x}(t)$ in data space $\mathbb{R}^d$. The **log-likelihood change** along this path is governed by: $$\log p(\mathbf{x}(T)) - \log p(\mathbf{x}(0)) = \int_0^T \nabla\log p(\mathbf{x}(t))\cdot\dot{\mathbf{x}}(t),dt + \text{(noise correction)}$$

The deterministic part is the line integral of the **score function** $\nabla\log p$ along the sample path. Since the score is a gradient field (of $\log p$), by FTC this equals $\log p(\mathbf{x}(T)) - \log p(\mathbf{x}(0))$ â€” directly.

In **continuous normalising flows** (CNFs), the log-likelihood change under the flow $d\mathbf{x}/dt = \mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}, t)$ is: $$\frac{d\log p(\mathbf{x}(t))}{dt} = -\nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}(t), t)$$

Integrating: $\log p(\mathbf{x}(T)) = \log p(\mathbf{x}(0)) - \int_0^T \nabla\cdot\mathbf{v}_\theta(\mathbf{x}(t), t),dt$. This line integral of the divergence along the flow path is the **instantaneous change of variables formula** â€” the continuous generalisation of the Jacobian determinant.

### 10.6 Geometry of Weight Spaces â€” Geodesics and Length

The scalar line integral $\int_C ds$ measures arc length. In the geometry of neural network weight spaces:

- The **Fisherâ€“Rao metric** gives a Riemannian metric on the manifold of probability distributions.
- The length of a path $\boldsymbol{\theta}(t)$ under this metric is $\int_a^b \sqrt{\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}}^T \mathcal{F}(\boldsymbol{\theta})\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}}},dt$ â€” a scalar line integral with arc-length element $ds = \sqrt{\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}}^T\mathcal{F},\dot{\boldsymbol{\theta}}},dt$.
- **Geodesics** (shortest paths under this metric) correspond to the most efficient parameter updates. Natural gradient descent approximates geodesic motion.

### 10.7 Green's Theorem and Exact Solutions in 2D Flows

When visualising and analysing 2D neural network loss landscapes (as in many theoretical papers that study 2-parameter toy models), Green's theorem is a direct tool:

- To determine if a 2D vector field (e.g., from gradient of a custom loss) has a curl-free region â€” indicative of a conservative, well-behaved optimisation landscape.
- To compute the circulation of gradient flow around a saddle point â€” relates to how likely gradient descent is to escape a saddle vs orbit it.
