## ¬ß0. Prefatory Remarks

Surface area is one of those concepts that feels intuitively obvious ‚Äî and is analytically subtle. The naive approach (approximate by rectangles, take a limit) fails catastrophically: there exist continuous surfaces whose "rectangular" approximations diverge to infinity while the true area is finite. This is the **Schwarz lantern paradox**, and it forces us to be precise about what "area" means.

The correct approach: approximate by _flat triangular patches_ (tangent plane pieces), not by rectangles. This leads to the integral $\iint |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|, du, dv$, which is the formula we derive and prove here.

Surface area appears throughout:

- **Physics**: heat transfer, fluid drag, electrostatics (Gauss's law)
- **Differential geometry**: minimal surfaces, isoperimetric inequalities
- **Computer graphics**: texture mapping, mesh parameterization
- **Machine learning / deep learning**: kernel methods, Gaussian process priors on manifolds, surface-loss functions in neural geometry processing

This note builds the theory from scratch, connects it to the previous notes on [[Surfaces_of_Revolution]] and [[Tangent_Planes]], and proves everything.

## ¬ß1. The Problem ‚Äî Why Rectangles Fail

### 1.1 Arc Length as a Warm-Up

Recall how we measure arc length. For a curve $\boldsymbol{\gamma}: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}^3$, we approximate by straight-line segments:

$$ L \approx \sum_{i} |\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t_{i+1}) - \boldsymbol{\gamma}(t_i)|. $$

As the partition refines, this converges to $\int_a^b |\boldsymbol{\gamma}'(t)|,dt$. The key: we approximate by _line segments tangent to the curve_. This works.

### 1.2 The Schwarz Lantern Paradox

Inscribe a cylinder of radius $1$ and height $1$ with a triangular mesh: divide the height into $n$ horizontal strips and rotate by $\pi/m$ between strips, placing $m$ triangles per strip. The true lateral area of the cylinder is $2\pi$.

As $n, m \to \infty$, what does the mesh area converge to?

If $n = m$ (balanced refinement): the area converges to $2\pi$. ‚úì

If $n = m^2$ (height refined faster than circumference): the area diverges to $\infty$. ‚úó

**Moral.** The limit depends on _how_ you refine, not just _that_ you refine. Rectangular subdivisions of a surface ‚Äî unlike line-segment subdivisions of a curve ‚Äî do not automatically converge to the correct area. We must use a definition that is independent of parameterization and robust to this issue.

**The fix:** use the _tangent plane_ at each point. The correct area element is the area of the parallelogram spanned by the tangent vectors $\mathbf{r}_u , du$ and $\mathbf{r}_v , dv$.

## ¬ß2. The Setup ‚Äî Parameterized Surfaces

**Definition 2.1.** A _smooth parameterized surface_ is a map $\mathbf{r}: D \subset \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$, $(u,v) \mapsto \mathbf{r}(u,v)$, where $D$ is a bounded region, $\mathbf{r} \in C^1(D)$, and the cross product $\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v \neq \mathbf{0}$ everywhere on $D$ (the _regular_ condition).

**The tangent vectors at $(u_0, v_0)$:** $$ \mathbf{r}_u = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial u}, \quad \mathbf{r}_v = \frac{\partial \mathbf{r}}{\partial v}. $$

These span the tangent plane at $\mathbf{r}(u_0,v_0)$ (as established in [[Tangent_Planes]], ¬ß4).

**The area element.** For a small rectangle $[u_0, u_0+\Delta u] \times [v_0, v_0+\Delta v]$ in parameter space, the image on the surface is approximated by the parallelogram with sides $\mathbf{r}_u \Delta u$ and $\mathbf{r}_v \Delta v$. The area of this parallelogram is

$$ |\mathbf{r}_u \Delta u \times \mathbf{r}_v \Delta v| = |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|, \Delta u, \Delta v. $$

Summing over all such patches and taking the limit gives the definition.

## ¬ß3. The Surface Area Formula

**Definition / Theorem 3.1 (Surface Area).** The _surface area_ of a smooth parameterized surface $\mathbf{r}: D \to \mathbb{R}^3$ is

$$ \boxed{A = \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|, dA,} $$

where $dA = du, dv$ is the area element in parameter space.

_Proof (rigorous sketch)._ Partition $D$ into small rectangles $R_{ij} = [u_i, u_{i+1}] \times [v_j, v_{j+1}]$ with $\Delta u = u_{i+1}-u_i$, $\Delta v = v_{j+1}-v_j$. At each rectangle, approximate the surface patch $\mathbf{r}(R_{ij})$ by the parallelogram with sides

$$ \mathbf{r}_u(u_i, v_j),\Delta u \quad \text{and} \quad \mathbf{r}_v(u_i,v_j),\Delta v. $$

The area of this parallelogram is $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|(u_i,v_j),\Delta u,\Delta v$. Summing:

$$ A \approx \sum_{i,j} |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|(u_i,v_j),\Delta u,\Delta v. $$

This is a Riemann sum for $\iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,dA$. Since $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|$ is continuous on the compact set $D$ (as $\mathbf{r} \in C^1$), the Riemann sums converge as $\Delta u, \Delta v \to 0$. This limit is the _definition_ of surface area (independent of parameterization ‚Äî proved in ¬ß5). $\blacksquare$

### The Three Representations

Depending on how the surface is given, the formula takes three forms.

**Case 1 ‚Äî Explicit: $z = f(x,y)$.**

Parameterize by $(x,y)$ itself: $\mathbf{r}(x,y) = (x, y, f(x,y))$.

$$ \mathbf{r}_x = (1, 0, f_x), \quad \mathbf{r}_y = (0, 1, f_y). $$

$$ \mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_y = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k} \ 1 & 0 & f_x \ 0 & 1 & f_y\end{vmatrix} = (-f_x,, -f_y,, 1). $$

$$ |\mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_y| = \sqrt{f_x^2 + f_y^2 + 1}. $$

$$ \boxed{A = \iint_D \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)^2}; dA.} $$

**Case 2 ‚Äî Parametric: $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$.**

$$ \boxed{A = \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|; du, dv.} $$

**Case 3 ‚Äî Implicit: $G(x,y,z) = 0$, solved as $z = f(x,y)$.**

Use Case 1 with $f_x = -G_x/G_z$ and $f_y = -G_y/G_z$:

$$ \sqrt{1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2} = \frac{\sqrt{G_x^2 + G_y^2 + G_z^2}}{|G_z|} = \frac{|\nabla G|}{|G_z|}. $$

$$ \boxed{A = \iint_D \frac{|\nabla G|}{|G_z|}; dA.} $$

## ¬ß4. The First Fundamental Form

The quantity $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|$ can be expressed in terms of the **first fundamental form** (metric tensor) of the surface. Define:

$$ E = \mathbf{r}_u \cdot \mathbf{r}_u, \quad F = \mathbf{r}_u \cdot \mathbf{r}_v, \quad G = \mathbf{r}_v \cdot \mathbf{r}_v. $$

These are the coefficients of the metric $g_{ij}$, and we have the classical identity:

$$ |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|^2 = |\mathbf{r}_u|^2|\mathbf{r}_v|^2 - (\mathbf{r}_u \cdot \mathbf{r}_v)^2 = EG - F^2 = \det[g_{ij}]. $$

_Proof of the identity._ By the Lagrange identity: $|\mathbf{a} \times \mathbf{b}|^2 = |\mathbf{a}|^2|\mathbf{b}|^2 - (\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})^2$. $\blacksquare$

So the area formula becomes:

$$ A = \iint_D \sqrt{EG - F^2}; du, dv. $$

**This is the intrinsic formula** ‚Äî it depends only on the metric, not on the embedding in $\mathbb{R}^3$. From the [[Tangent_Planes]] note, for a surface of revolution $\mathbf{r}(x,\theta) = (x, f\cos\theta, f\sin\theta)$:

$$ E = 1+(f')^2, \quad F = 0, \quad G = f^2 \implies \sqrt{EG-F^2} = f\sqrt{1+(f')^2}, $$

recovering the formula from [[Surfaces_of_Revolution]], ¬ß3. ‚úì

## ¬ß5. Independence of Parameterization

A critical property: the area should not depend on how we parameterize the surface. This is non-obvious ‚Äî the formula $\iint |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv$ explicitly involves $u, v$.

**Theorem 5.1 (Reparameterization Invariance).** Let $\mathbf{r}: D \to S$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{r}}: \tilde{D} \to S$ be two regular parameterizations of the same surface, related by $\mathbf{r}(u,v) = \tilde{\mathbf{r}}(\phi(u,v), \psi(u,v))$ where $(u,v) \mapsto (\phi,\psi)$ is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism with Jacobian $J$. Then

$$ \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv = \iint_{\tilde{D}} |\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi \times \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi|,d\phi,d\psi. $$

_Proof._ By the chain rule: $\mathbf{r}_u = \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi \phi_u + \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi \psi_u$ and $\mathbf{r}_v = \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi \phi_v + \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi \psi_v$. The cross product:

$$ \mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v = (\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi \phi_u + \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi\psi_u) \times (\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi\phi_v + \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi\psi_v). $$

Expanding using bilinearity and $\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{a} = \mathbf{0}$:

$$ = (\phi_u\psi_v - \phi_v\psi_u)(\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi \times \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi) = J \cdot (\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi \times \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi), $$

where $J = \frac{\partial(\phi,\psi)}{\partial(u,v)}$ is the Jacobian determinant. So:

$$ \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv = \iint_D |\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi \times \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi| \cdot |J|,du,dv = \iint_{\tilde{D}} |\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\phi \times \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_\psi|,d\phi,d\psi, $$

by the change-of-variables formula. $\blacksquare$

> **This is the multivariable analogue of the substitution rule for integrals.** The Jacobian of the change of coordinates exactly cancels the change in $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|$.

## ¬ß6. Worked Examples

### Example 6.1 ‚Äî Sphere of Radius $R$

**Method 1: Explicit parameterization.** Use $\mathbf{r}(\phi,\theta) = (R\sin\phi\cos\theta, R\sin\phi\sin\theta, R\cos\phi)$ for $(\phi,\theta) \in [0,\pi]\times[0,2\pi]$.

$$ \mathbf{r}_\phi = R(\cos\phi\cos\theta, \cos\phi\sin\theta, -\sin\phi), $$ $$ \mathbf{r}_\theta = R(-\sin\phi\sin\theta, \sin\phi\cos\theta, 0). $$

Computing the cross product and its magnitude:

$$ E = R^2, \quad F = 0, \quad G = R^2\sin^2\phi, $$ $$ |\mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = R^2\sin\phi. $$

$$ A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi R^2\sin\phi, d\phi, d\theta = 2\pi R^2 \int_0^\pi \sin\phi, d\phi = 2\pi R^2 \cdot 2 = 4\pi R^2. \checkmark $$

**Method 2: Explicit surface $z = f(x,y)$.** Use upper hemisphere $z = \sqrt{R^2 - x^2 - y^2}$ over $D: x^2+y^2 \leq R^2$ and double.

$$ f_x = \frac{-x}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2-y^2}}, \quad f_y = \frac{-y}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2-y^2}}. $$

$$ 1 + f_x^2 + f_y^2 = 1 + \frac{x^2+y^2}{R^2-x^2-y^2} = \frac{R^2}{R^2-x^2-y^2}. $$

$$ A = 2\iint_{x^2+y^2\leq R^2} \frac{R}{\sqrt{R^2-x^2-y^2}}, dA. $$

Switch to polar $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$:

$$ A = 2\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^R \frac{R}{\sqrt{R^2-r^2}}, r, dr, d\theta = 4\pi R \int_0^R \frac{r, dr}{\sqrt{R^2-r^2}}. $$

Let $u = R^2 - r^2$, $du = -2r,dr$:

$$ = 4\pi R \int_{R^2}^0 \frac{-du/2}{\sqrt{u}} = 2\pi R \int_0^{R^2} u^{-1/2},du = 2\pi R \cdot 2R = 4\pi R^2. \checkmark $$

### Example 6.2 ‚Äî Paraboloid $z = x^2 + y^2$ over $x^2+y^2 \leq 1$

$$ f_x = 2x, \quad f_y = 2y, \quad \sqrt{1 + 4x^2 + 4y^2} = \sqrt{1+4r^2}. $$

$$ A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 \sqrt{1+4r^2}, r, dr, d\theta = 2\pi \int_0^1 r\sqrt{1+4r^2}, dr. $$

Let $u = 1 + 4r^2$, $du = 8r,dr$:

$$ A = 2\pi \int_1^5 \sqrt{u}\cdot\frac{du}{8} = \frac{\pi}{4}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\left[u^{3/2}\right]_1^5 = \frac{\pi}{6}(5^{3/2} - 1) = \frac{\pi}{6}(5\sqrt{5}-1). $$

### Example 6.3 ‚Äî Cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ over $x^2+y^2 \leq R^2$

$$ f_x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}, \quad f_y = \frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}}, \quad 1+f_x^2+f_y^2 = 2. $$

$$ A = \iint_{x^2+y^2\leq R^2} \sqrt{2}, dA = \sqrt{2}\cdot \pi R^2. $$

_Sanity check:_ A cone of base radius $R$ and height $R$ (so $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ has slope $1$, height $R$ at radius $R$) has slant height $\ell = R\sqrt{2}$, lateral area $\pi R\ell = \pi R^2\sqrt{2}$. ‚úì

### Example 6.4 ‚Äî Torus

$\mathbf{r}(\phi,\theta) = ((R+r\cos\phi)\cos\theta, (R+r\cos\phi)\sin\theta, r\sin\phi)$.

$$ E = r^2, \quad F = 0, \quad G = (R+r\cos\phi)^2, $$ $$ |\mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = r(R+r\cos\phi). $$

$$ A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} r(R+r\cos\phi), d\phi, d\theta = 2\pi r \int_0^{2\pi}(R + r\cos\phi), d\phi = 2\pi r \cdot 2\pi R = 4\pi^2 Rr. $$

(The $\int_0^{2\pi}\cos\phi,d\phi = 0$ term vanishes.) This is Pappus's theorem: $A = 2\pi R \cdot 2\pi r$. ‚úì

## ¬ß8. Surface Integrals ‚Äî Integrating over the Surface

Once we have $dA = |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv$, we can integrate any function over the surface.

**Definition 8.1 (Surface Integral of a Scalar).** For a continuous function $g: S \to \mathbb{R}$:

$$ \iint_S g, dA = \iint_D g(\mathbf{r}(u,v)), |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|, du, dv. $$

Setting $g \equiv 1$ recovers surface area.

**Example 8.2.** Compute the average $z$-coordinate on the upper unit hemisphere $z = \sqrt{1-x^2-y^2}$.

From Example 6.1 (with $R=1$): $|\mathbf{r}_\phi \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = \sin\phi$, and $z = \cos\phi$ on the sphere.

$$ \bar{z} = \frac{1}{A}\iint_S z, dA = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\phi\sin\phi,d\phi,d\theta = \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot 2\pi\int_0^{\pi/2}\cos\phi\sin\phi,d\phi. $$

$$ = \int_0^{\pi/2}\frac{\sin 2\phi}{2},d\phi = \frac{1}{2}\left[-\frac{\cos 2\phi}{2}\right]_0^{\pi/2} = \frac{1}{2}\cdot 1 = \frac{1}{2}. $$

So the average height on the upper hemisphere is $\frac{1}{2}$ ‚Äî exactly halfway up. Geometric sanity check: the centroid of a hemisphere is at height $R/2$. ‚úì

## ¬ß9. Special Cases and Classical Formulas

### 9.1 Surface Area of Revolution (Revisited)

From [[Surfaces_of_Revolution]], $\mathbf{r}(x,\theta)$, $|\mathbf{r}_x \times \mathbf{r}_\theta| = f(x)\sqrt{1+(f')^2}$:

$$ A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx,d\theta = 2\pi\int_a^b f(x)\sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx. $$

This is now seen as a special case of the general parametric formula.

### 9.2 Flat Surface (Plane)

Plane $z = ax + by + c$ over domain $D$:

$$ f_x = a, \quad f_y = b, \quad A = \sqrt{1+a^2+b^2}\cdot \text{Area}(D). $$

The factor $\sqrt{1+a^2+b^2}$ is the _secant_ of the angle between the plane and the horizontal ‚Äî the area stretching factor.

### 9.3 Cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = R^2$, $0 \leq z \leq h$

Parameterize: $\mathbf{r}(\theta, z) = (R\cos\theta, R\sin\theta, z)$.

$$ \mathbf{r}_\theta = (-R\sin\theta, R\cos\theta, 0), \quad \mathbf{r}_z = (0,0,1). $$

$$ \mathbf{r}_\theta \times \mathbf{r}_z = (R\cos\theta, R\sin\theta, 0), \quad |\cdot| = R. $$

$$ A = \int_0^h\int_0^{2\pi} R,d\theta, dz = 2\pi R h. \checkmark $$

## ¬ß10. Minimal Surfaces

A _minimal surface_ is one whose mean curvature $H = \frac{1}{2}(\kappa_1+\kappa_2) = 0$ vanishes everywhere. Equivalently ‚Äî and this is the variational characterization ‚Äî minimal surfaces are _critical points of the area functional_.

**Variational Setup.** Fix the boundary $\partial S$. Among all surfaces with this boundary, which has smallest area? The Euler-Lagrange equation for the area functional $A[\mathbf{r}] = \iint |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv$ is exactly $H = 0$.

**Theorem 10.1 (Euler-Lagrange for Area).** A smooth surface is a critical point of $A[\mathbf{r}]$ under fixed-boundary perturbations if and only if its mean curvature $H = 0$ everywhere.

_Proof sketch._ Perturb: $\mathbf{r}_\epsilon = \mathbf{r} + \epsilon\phi\hat{n}$ for a compactly supported function $\phi$ and unit normal $\hat{n}$. Compute $\frac{d}{d\epsilon}A[\mathbf{r}_\epsilon]\big|_{\epsilon=0}$ and integrate by parts. The vanishing of this for all $\phi$ forces $H = 0$ (the first variation of area formula). $\blacksquare$

**Examples of minimal surfaces:**

|Surface|Description|
|---|---|
|Plane|$H = 0$ trivially|
|Catenoid|$f(x) = c\cosh(x/c)$; only minimal surface of revolution|
|Helicoid|$\mathbf{r}(u,v) = (u\cos v, u\sin v, v)$; ruled minimal surface|
|Enneper surface|$\mathbf{r}(u,v) = (u - u^3/3 + uv^2, -v + v^3/3 - vu^2, u^2 - v^2)$|
|Scherk surface|$e^z\cos y = \cos x$|

**Plateau's problem** (1760, solved 1930‚Äì31 by Douglas and Rad√≥): Given any closed curve in $\mathbb{R}^3$, does there exist a minimal surface bounded by it? Answer: yes (for rectifiable curves). Douglas won the first Fields Medal partly for this.

## ¬ß11. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 11.1 Surface Area as a Regularizer in Neural Geometry Processing

In _neural implicit surface_ representations (NeRF, NeuralRBF, DeepSDF), a network $F_\theta: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ defines a surface $S = {F_\theta = 0}$. To prevent over-fitting and encourage smooth, minimal surfaces, practitioners add a **surface area regularizer** to the training loss:

$$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{reg}} = \iint_S dA = \iint \frac{|\nabla F_\theta|}{|F_{\theta,z}|}, dA, $$

using the implicit surface formula of ¬ß3. Minimizing this term biases the learned surface toward smaller area ‚Äî towards _minimal surface solutions_. This is Plateau's problem implemented as gradient descent.

### 11.2 Riemannian Metric Learning

The surface area formula $A = \iint \sqrt{EG-F^2},du,dv$ depends only on the first fundamental form $g = {E, F, G}$ ‚Äî the intrinsic metric. In **metric learning**, one directly learns a metric $g$ on the data manifold $\mathcal{M}$ such that geodesic distances reflect semantic similarity. The "volume" of data space (generalized area) is then $\int \sqrt{\det g}, du, dv$ ‚Äî exactly the Riemannian volume form. Controlling this volume controls the density of the learned representation.

### 11.3 Information Geometry and the Fisher Metric

In a statistical model ${p_\theta}$, the **Fisher information metric**

$$ g_{ij}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial \log p_\theta}{\partial\theta_i}\frac{\partial \log p_\theta}{\partial\theta_j}\right] $$

defines a Riemannian metric on the manifold of probability distributions. The _volume_ of parameter space under this metric is $\int\sqrt{\det g},d\theta$ ‚Äî a generalized surface area. **Natural gradient descent** replaces the Euclidean gradient with the Riemannian gradient under $g$ (exactly the Riemannian optimization from [[Tangent_Planes]], ¬ß10.6):

$$ \Delta\theta = -\eta, g^{-1}\nabla\mathcal{L}. $$

This is the mathematical foundation of K-FAC and other second-order optimizers. The Fisher metric quantifies how much the distribution changes per unit parameter change ‚Äî it measures the "area" of the statistical manifold that a gradient step traverses.

### 11.4 Gaussian Processes on Manifolds

A Gaussian process prior on a manifold $\mathcal{M}$ requires a kernel $k: \mathcal{M}\times\mathcal{M}\to\mathbb{R}$. The **heat kernel** on a surface (the fundamental solution to the heat equation $\partial_t u = \Delta_{\mathcal{M}} u$) is intrinsic to the surface geometry and depends on the metric $\sqrt{EG-F^2}$. Stationary kernels on surfaces generalize the RBF kernel, with the geodesic distance replacing Euclidean distance. Their spectral density (Mercer expansion via eigenfunctions of $\Delta_\mathcal{M}$) depends on the Riemannian volume form ‚Äî our area element.

### 11.5 Score Matching and Diffusion on Surfaces

**Diffusion models** (DDPM, score matching) are increasingly generalized to data on manifolds ‚Äî e.g., proteins (data on $SO(3)$), molecular conformations (data on $S^2$), or any geometry-aware generative model. The score function $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ on a manifold is the Riemannian gradient, and the forward/reverse diffusion processes use the **Laplace-Beltrami operator** $\Delta_\mathcal{M}$ ‚Äî which is defined via the metric $\sqrt{EG-F^2}$. Without surface area theory, none of this makes sense.

### 11.6 Mesh-Based Deep Learning and Surface Discretization

In **graph neural networks on meshes** (MeshCNN, DiffusionNet, PoinTr), the surface is discretized into triangles. Each triangle's area is $\frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{e}_1 \times \mathbf{e}_2|$ where $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2$ are edge vectors ‚Äî a discrete version of our cross-product formula. The **cotangent Laplacian** used in spectral mesh processing weights edges by $\cot\alpha + \cot\beta$ (angles opposite the edge), which is exactly the discretization of $\sqrt{EG-F^2}$. Every convolution on a mesh implicitly uses surface area.

**12.2.** Find the area of the part of the plane $x + 2y + 3z = 6$ that lies inside the cylinder $x^2+y^2=4$.

**12.3.** _[Implicit surface]_ Use the formula $A = \iint |\nabla G|/|G_z|,dA$ to find the area of the ellipsoid $\frac{x^2}{a^2}+\frac{y^2}{b^2}+\frac{z^2}{c^2}=1$ in the special case $a=b$ (oblate spheroid). You should obtain an integral that can be evaluated by trigonometric substitution.

**12.4.** Show directly (by computing $E, F, G$) that a sphere of radius $R$ in spherical coordinates has $\sqrt{EG-F^2} = R^2\sin\phi$, and verify the area is $4\pi R^2$.

**12.5.** _[Pappus verification]_ A circle of radius $r$ centered at $(R, 0, 0)$ (with $R > r$) is revolved about the $z$-axis. Use the parametric surface area formula to verify Pappus's theorem: $A = 4\pi^2 Rr$.

**12.6.** _[Minimal surfaces]_ Show that the helicoid $\mathbf{r}(u,v) = (u\cos v, u\sin v, v)$ has $E = 1+v^2$ ‚Äî _wait, recheck_. Actually compute $E, F, G$ for the helicoid and find $H$.

**12.7.** _[ML connection]_ A neural network defines a surface $S = {F_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = 0}$ where $F_\theta(x,y,z) = z - \sin(\pi x)\sin(\pi y)$. Write the surface area integral explicitly and discuss why adding $\lambda A$ to the training loss encourages "flatter" surfaces.

## ¬ß13. Summary

| Surface | $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|$ | Area | |---|---|---| | $z = f(x,y)$ | $\sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2}$ | $\iint_D \sqrt{1+f_x^2+f_y^2},dA$ | | General parametric | $\sqrt{EG-F^2}$ | $\iint_D \sqrt{EG-F^2},du,dv$ | | Surface of revolution | $f(x)\sqrt{1+(f')^2}$ | $2\pi\int_a^b f\sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx$ | | Sphere radius $R$ | $R^2\sin\phi$ | $4\pi R^2$ | | Cylinder radius $R$, height $h$ | $R$ | $2\pi Rh$ | | Cone, base $R$, slant $\ell$ | $\sqrt{2}R$ (for $45¬∞$ cone) | $\pi R\ell$ | | Torus $(R,r)$ | $r(R+r\cos\phi)$ | $4\pi^2 Rr$ |

**The conceptual hierarchy:**

$$ \text{tangent vectors } \mathbf{r}_u, \mathbf{r}_v \xrightarrow{\text{cross product}} \text{area element } |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v| \xrightarrow{\text{integrate}} \text{surface area} $$

$$ \text{metric tensor } g_{ij} \xrightarrow{\det} \sqrt{EG-F^2} \xrightarrow{\text{integrate}} \text{intrinsic area} $$

The deep point: both routes give the same answer, and neither depends on how you parameterize the surface. This invariance ‚Äî under reparameterization ‚Äî is the geometric content of the change-of-variables theorem.
