

> _"The divergence asks a single, precise question at every point: is this a source, a sink, or neither? It is the scalar that measures how much the field breathes — expanding or contracting — at infinitesimal scale. Curl tells you about spin. Divergence tells you about expansion. Together they are the complete local portrait of a vector field."_

---

## §1. Motivation — Sources, Sinks, and the Idea of Flux Density

Picture a vector field as a fluid flow. Drop an infinitesimally small sphere into the flow at a point $p$. Three things can happen:

1. More fluid flows **out** of the sphere than in — $p$ is a **source**. Net outward flux $> 0$.
2. More fluid flows **in** than out — $p$ is a **sink**. Net outward flux $< 0$.
3. The inflow exactly balances the outflow — $p$ is neither. Net outward flux $= 0$.

The **divergence** $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}(p)$ is the net outward flux per unit volume, in the limit as the volume shrinks to $p$. It is a **scalar field** — at each point, a single number that tells you the local source/sink strength.

This is not the same as the field being large or small in magnitude. A field can be enormous and have zero divergence. A field can be tiny and have a huge divergence. Divergence is an infinitesimal, local measurement of **expansion/contraction**, not field strength.

The deepest reason to care: divergence appears on the right side of **Gauss's theorem** (the Divergence theorem), in $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0$ (Gauss's law for electricity), and in $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{B} = 0$ (no magnetic monopoles). It is the bridge between local source density and global flux through closed surfaces. Understanding divergence is prerequisite to understanding electromagnetism, fluid mechanics, heat flow, and — as we will see — deep learning.

## §2. Definition

### 2.1 The Divergence in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Let $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R \rangle : U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ be a $C^1$ vector field. The **divergence** of $\mathbf{F}$ is the scalar field:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \text{div},\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}$$

The notation $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}$ is read "del dot $\mathbf{F}$," treating $\nabla = \langle \partial_x, \partial_y, \partial_z \rangle$ as a formal vector and taking the dot product with $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R \rangle$:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \langle \partial_x, \partial_y, \partial_z \rangle \cdot \langle P, Q, R \rangle = \partial_x P + \partial_y Q + \partial_z R$$

> **Warning.** As with the curl determinant, this is a mnemonic — $\nabla$ is a differential operator, not a vector. Treat the dot product mechanically: each component of $\nabla$ differentiates the corresponding component of $\mathbf{F}$.

### 2.2 Components and Their Meaning

Each term $\partial P / \partial x$, $\partial Q / \partial y$, $\partial R / \partial z$ measures how much the field **spreads out** in a single coordinate direction:

|Term|Measures|
|---|---|
|$\partial P / \partial x$|How much $\mathbf{F}$ accelerates in the $x$-direction as you move in $x$|
|$\partial Q / \partial y$|How much $\mathbf{F}$ accelerates in the $y$-direction as you move in $y$|
|$\partial R / \partial z$|How much $\mathbf{F}$ accelerates in the $z$-direction as you move in $z$|

Their sum captures total volumetric expansion: if all three are positive, the field is diverging away from $p$ in all three coordinate directions simultaneously — a pure source. The sign of $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}$ tells you the net tendency.

### 2.3 Divergence in $\mathbb{R}^2$

For a planar field $\mathbf{F} = \langle P(x,y), Q(x,y) \rangle$:

$$\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y}$$

This is the quantity appearing in Green's theorem (normal form) and in 2D fluid mechanics.

## §3. Intrinsic (Coordinate-Free) Definition

The formula $\partial_x P + \partial_y Q + \partial_z R$ is useful computationally. The geometric definition is more fundamental.

> **Definition (Divergence via Gauss's theorem).** For a $C^1$ vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$, the divergence $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}$ at a point $p$ is:
> 
> $$(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})(p) = \lim_{V(B_\varepsilon) \to 0} \frac{1}{V(B_\varepsilon)} \oiint_{\partial B_\varepsilon} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{S}$$
> 
> where $B_\varepsilon$ is a ball of volume $V(B_\varepsilon)$ centered at $p$, and the surface integral is over its boundary with outward-pointing normals.

**In words:** The divergence is the **net outward flux per unit volume**, in the limit as the volume shrinks to the point. It is independent of coordinates — which is why the formula looks the same after any rotation of axes.

This definition makes the physics transparent:

- $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}(p) > 0$: $p$ is a source — the field pushes outward on net.
- $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}(p) < 0$: $p$ is a sink — the field pulls inward on net.
- $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F}(p) = 0$: $p$ is neither — the field is **divergence-free** (solenoidal) at $p$.

---

## §4. Full Proof: Coordinate Formula from Intrinsic Definition

We derive $\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F} = \partial_x P + \partial_y Q + \partial_z R$ from the limit definition, using the small-box argument.

**Setup.** Fix a point $(a, b, c)$. Consider a small rectangular box $B_\varepsilon$ with sides $\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z$ centered at $(a,b,c)$, with faces perpendicular to the coordinate axes. The total outward flux through $\partial B_\varepsilon$ is the sum of fluxes through the six faces.

**Flux through the $x$-faces.**

The right face (at $x = a + \Delta x/2$) has outward normal $\mathbf{i}$, area $\Delta y,\Delta z$. The left face (at $x = a - \Delta x/2$) has outward normal $-\mathbf{i}$, area $\Delta y,\Delta z$. The net flux in the $x$-direction:

$$\Phi_x \approx P!\left(a + \frac{\Delta x}{2}, b, c\right)\Delta y,\Delta z - P!\left(a - \frac{\Delta x}{2}, b, c\right)\Delta y,\Delta z$$

By the **Mean Value Theorem** applied to $P$ in $x$:

$$P!\left(a + \tfrac{\Delta x}{2}, b, c\right) - P!\left(a - \tfrac{\Delta x}{2}, b, c\right) \approx \frac{\partial P}{\partial x}(a,b,c),\Delta x$$

So $\Phi_x \approx \dfrac{\partial P}{\partial x},\Delta x,\Delta y,\Delta z$.

**Same argument for the $y$- and $z$-faces:**

$$\Phi_y \approx \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y},\Delta x,\Delta y,\Delta z, \qquad \Phi_z \approx \frac{\partial R}{\partial z},\Delta x,\Delta y,\Delta z$$

**Total flux and the limit:**

$$\oiint_{\partial B_\varepsilon} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} \approx \left(\frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}\right)\Delta x,\Delta y,\Delta z$$

Dividing by $V(B_\varepsilon) = \Delta x,\Delta y,\Delta z$ and taking $\Delta x, \Delta y, \Delta z \to 0$:

$$\lim_{V \to 0}\frac{1}{V}\oiint_{\partial B_\varepsilon}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z} \quad \square$$

The formula is coordinate-free in the sense that the **limit** is independent of coordinates; it is a coincidence of Cartesian geometry that the formula takes the symmetric sum-of-partial-derivatives form in this basis.

## §5. The Divergence Theorem

The intrinsic definition was:

$$(\nabla \cdot \mathbf{F})(p) = \lim_{V \to 0} \frac{1}{V}\oiint_{\partial B} \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

Integrate both sides over a solid region $E$ — you get the **Divergence Theorem** (Gauss's theorem):

$$\boxed{\iiint_E (\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}),dV = \oiint_{\partial E}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}}$$

The total source strength inside $E$ equals the net outward flux through its boundary. This is one of the deepest theorems in vector calculus.

**Key consequences:**

- If $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 0$ everywhere inside $E$, then $\oiint_{\partial E}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = 0$ — the field has no net flux through any closed surface enclosing a divergence-free region.
- For a field with a point source at the origin (e.g., gravitational or Coulomb field), the integral $\oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$ equals the same constant for **any** surface surrounding the origin — the flux depends only on the enclosed sources, not the surface shape.
- **Green's theorem (normal form)** is the $2D$ special case: $\iint_D (\partial_x P + \partial_y Q),dA = \oint_{\partial D} \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},ds$.

## §6. Fundamental Identities

### 6.1 Divergence of a Curl is Zero

$$\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = 0$$

**Proof.** For $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$, expand:

$$\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}(R_y - Q_z) + \frac{\partial}{\partial y}(P_z - R_x) + \frac{\partial}{\partial z}(Q_x - P_y)$$

$$= R_{xy} - Q_{xz} + P_{yz} - R_{yx} + Q_{zx} - P_{zy}$$

$$= (R_{xy} - R_{yx}) + (P_{yz} - P_{zy}) + (Q_{zx} - Q_{xz}) = 0$$

by Clairaut's theorem. $\square$

**Meaning:** Curl fields are automatically divergence-free — a curl field has no sources or sinks. The magnetic field $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ satisfies $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ identically — there are no magnetic monopoles, as a mathematical necessity.

### 6.2 Divergence of a Gradient is the Laplacian

$$\nabla\cdot(\nabla f) = \nabla^2 f = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial z^2}$$

The **Laplacian** $\nabla^2 f$ is arguably the most important operator in mathematical physics. It measures how much $f(p)$ differs from the average of $f$ over small spheres around $p$:

$$\nabla^2 f(p) = \lim_{\varepsilon\to 0}\frac{1}{\varepsilon^2}\left(\frac{1}{|S^2_\varepsilon|}\oiint_{S^2_\varepsilon} f,dS - f(p)\right) \cdot C$$

for a dimensional constant $C$. A **harmonic** function ($\nabla^2 f = 0$) takes no value larger or smaller than its surrounding average — this is the mean value property, which implies the maximum principle.

### 6.3 Product Rule for Divergence

$$\nabla\cdot(f\mathbf{F}) = f(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}) + \mathbf{F}\cdot(\nabla f)$$

**Proof.** Compute directly: $$\nabla\cdot(f\mathbf{F}) = \partial_x(fP) + \partial_y(fQ) + \partial_z(fR)$$ $$= (f_x P + f P_x) + (f_y Q + f Q_y) + (f_z R + f R_z)$$ $$= f(\partial_x P + \partial_y Q + \partial_z R) + (f_x P + f_y Q + f_z R) = f,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} + \nabla f\cdot\mathbf{F} \quad\square$$

This is the **integration by parts** formula for divergence. It yields Green's first identity: $$\iiint_E f,\nabla^2 g,dV = \oiint_{\partial E} f,\nabla g\cdot d\mathbf{S} - \iiint_E \nabla f\cdot\nabla g,dV$$

and Green's second identity (symmetric version). These are the fundamental tools for PDE theory.

### 6.4 Divergence of $r^n\hat{\mathbf{r}}$ — The Key Physics Example

For the radial field $\mathbf{F} = r^n\hat{\mathbf{r}} = r^{n-1}\langle x, y, z\rangle$ (where $r = |\mathbf{r}|$):

$$\nabla\cdot(r^n\hat{\mathbf{r}}) = (n+2)r^{n-1}$$

**Special case $n = -2$** (the Coulomb/Newton field $\mathbf{F} = \hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2$):

$$\nabla\cdot\left(\frac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2}\right) = 0 \quad \text{for } r \neq 0$$

Yet $\oiint_S \frac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = 4\pi$ for any sphere surrounding the origin. The resolution: the divergence is **zero everywhere except the origin**, where it is a **Dirac delta**:

$$\nabla\cdot\left(\frac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2}\right) = 4\pi,\delta^3(\mathbf{r})$$

This is the mathematical content of Gauss's law: the point charge at the origin is the source of the Coulomb field.

```
              f ∈ C²

               │ ∇ (gradient)
               ▼
         F = ∇f (conservative)
               │

         G = ∇ × F (curl field)
               │

               ▼

And the full de Rham chain:

  Ω⁰ ──grad──▶ Ω¹ ──curl──▶ Ω² ──div──▶ Ω³
           d             d            d

```

In differential forms, the divergence is the exterior derivative $d$ acting on 2-forms. The identity $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{F}) = 0$ is the single statement $d\circ d = 0$, which encodes all these identities simultaneously.

## §8. Solenoidal Fields and Vector Potentials

> **Theorem.** Let $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$ be simply connected (in fact, contractible suffices). For a $C^1$ vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on $U$, the following are equivalent:
> 
> 1. $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 0$ on $U$ (**divergence-free** / **solenoidal**)
> 2. $\mathbf{F} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ for some $\mathbf{A} \in C^2(U)$ (**vector potential exists**)
> 3. $\oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = 0$ for every closed surface $S$ in $U$
> 4. $\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$ depends only on $\partial S$, not on the surface itself

$(1)\Rightarrow(2)$: Construction via integration (analogous to finding a scalar potential for curl-free fields).

$(2)\Rightarrow(1)$: Identity $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{A}) = 0$.

$(1)\Rightarrow(3)$: Divergence theorem on any region bounded by $S$.

$(3)\Rightarrow(4)$: Two surfaces with the same boundary form a closed surface.

**Physical meaning:** A solenoidal field has no sources or sinks anywhere — the field lines are either closed loops or extend to infinity without termination. The magnetic field $\mathbf{B}$ is always solenoidal ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$) because there are no magnetic monopoles.

**Gauge freedom:** The vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ is not unique — $\mathbf{A}' = \mathbf{A} + \nabla\phi$ gives the same $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}' = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ for any scalar $\phi$. This is **gauge invariance** — the fundamental symmetry of electromagnetism and Yang-Mills theory.

## §9. Worked Examples

### Example A — Basic Computation

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle x^2 y,; y^2 z,; z^2 x \rangle$. Find $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$.**

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial(x^2 y)}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial(y^2 z)}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial(z^2 x)}{\partial z} = 2xy + 2yz + 2zx$$

---

### Example B — Verifying the Divergence Theorem

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle x, y, z\rangle$, $E$ the unit ball $x^2+y^2+z^2 \leq 1$.**

**Via divergence theorem:** $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 1+1+1 = 3$.

$$\iiint_E 3,dV = 3\cdot\frac{4\pi}{3} = 4\pi$$

**Direct check:** On the unit sphere $\mathbf{F} = \hat{\mathbf{r}}$ (outward radial), $\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}} = 1$.

$$\oiint_{S^2}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oiint_{S^2} 1,dS = 4\pi \quad\checkmark$$

---

### Example C — Physically Motivated: Electric Field

**$\mathbf{E} = \dfrac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\dfrac{\hat{\mathbf{r}}}{r^2}$ (Coulomb field of charge $q$ at origin). Compute $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E}$.**

For $r \neq 0$: we showed $\nabla\cdot(\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2) = 0$.

So $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$ away from the origin. But:

$$\oiint_{S^2_R}\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \frac{q}{4\pi\varepsilon_0}\cdot\frac{1}{R^2}\cdot 4\pi R^2 = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}$$

for any sphere of radius $R$ around the origin. By the divergence theorem:

$$\iiint_{B_R}\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E},dV = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0} \implies \nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \frac{q}{\varepsilon_0}\delta^3(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$$

This is **Gauss's law** in differential form: $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0$.

---

### Example D — Counterintuitive: Radially Symmetric Field with Nonzero Divergence

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle x, y, 0\rangle$ (2D radial field, constant in $z$). Compute $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$.**

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 1 + 1 + 0 = 2$$

The divergence is positive everywhere, uniformly. The field arrows point outward from the $z$-axis — this is a source everywhere, not just at the origin. Any tiny sphere in this field will have more fluid leaving it than entering.

Contrast with the Coulomb field: $1/r^2$ decay is precisely the rate needed to make $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$ away from the source.

---

### Example E — Testing for Solenoidal

**Is $\mathbf{F} = \langle yz,; xz,; xy\rangle$ solenoidal?**

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial(yz)}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial(xz)}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial(xy)}{\partial z} = 0 + 0 + 0 = 0 \quad\checkmark$$

Yes. Find the vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ such that $\nabla\times\mathbf{A} = \mathbf{F}$: this requires solving a system of PDEs and is more involved (the construction parallels finding a scalar potential for curl-free fields, but one dimension higher).

---

### Example F — Applying Green's First Identity

**Prove that if $\nabla^2 f = 0$ in $E$ and $f = 0$ on $\partial E$, then $f \equiv 0$.**

Set $\mathbf{F} = f,\nabla f$ in the product rule $\nabla\cdot(f\nabla f) = f\nabla^2 f + |\nabla f|^2$. Apply the divergence theorem:

$$\oiint_{\partial E} f,\nabla f\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iiint_E (f\nabla^2 f + |\nabla f|^2),dV$$

Left side: $f = 0$ on $\partial E$, so the left side is zero. Right side: $\nabla^2 f = 0$ in $E$, so:

$$0 = \iiint_E |\nabla f|^2,dV$$

Since $|\nabla f|^2 \geq 0$ and its integral is zero, $|\nabla f|^2 = 0$ everywhere in $E$. Thus $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$, so $f$ is constant. Since $f = 0$ on $\partial E$, $f \equiv 0$. $\square$

This is the **uniqueness of Laplace's equation** with Dirichlet boundary conditions — derived purely from the divergence theorem and the product rule.

## §11. Divergence in Physics

### 11.1 Maxwell's Equations — Gauss's Laws

Divergence appears in **two** of Maxwell's four equations:

**Gauss's Law for Electricity:** $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \frac{\rho}{\varepsilon_0}$$

The divergence of the electric field at a point equals the local charge density divided by $\varepsilon_0$. Where there is charge, the electric field diverges (or converges). Where there is no charge, $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = 0$ — the electric field is solenoidal. This is the differential form of Coulomb's law generalized to arbitrary charge distributions.

**Gauss's Law for Magnetism:** $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$$

The magnetic field is always divergence-free — everywhere, always. This means: there are no magnetic monopoles. Every magnetic field line that enters a region must leave it. The mathematical reason: $\mathbf{B} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$, and $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{A}) = 0$ identically. The absence of magnetic monopoles is a theorem, given the vector potential.

### 11.2 Fluid Mechanics — Incompressibility and Continuity

The **continuity equation** in fluid mechanics: $$\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{u}) = 0$$

This says: the rate of change of density at a point equals minus the divergence of the mass flux $\rho\mathbf{u}$. If mass flux diverges away from a point (positive divergence), the density there decreases.

For an **incompressible fluid** ($\rho = \text{const}$): $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$$

The velocity field of an incompressible fluid is solenoidal. Every fluid element preserves its volume — the fluid neither compresses nor expands. This is the cornerstone assumption of most CFD (computational fluid dynamics): the Navier-Stokes equations for incompressible flow include $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0$ as a constraint.

### 11.3 Heat Equation and Poisson's Equation

The **heat equation** in $\mathbb{R}^3$: $$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = k,\nabla^2 u = k,\nabla\cdot(\nabla u)$$

Temperature changes at a point when the temperature is above or below the local average — measured by $\nabla^2 u = \nabla\cdot(\nabla u)$.

**Poisson's equation** (electrostatics, gravity, diffusion): $$\nabla^2 \phi = f$$

where $f$ is a given source function. When $f = 0$, this is Laplace's equation $\nabla^2\phi = 0$. Solutions are harmonic functions — the most regular functions in analysis.

### 11.4 General Relativity — The Einstein Tensor

In general relativity, the divergence-free condition appears at the deepest level. The Einstein field equations: $$G_{\mu\nu} + \Lambda g_{\mu\nu} = \frac{8\pi G}{c^4}T_{\mu\nu}$$

require the **Bianchi identity** $\nabla^\mu G_{\mu\nu} = 0$ — the covariant divergence of the Einstein tensor is zero. This is the GR analog of $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$: it encodes energy-momentum conservation as a geometric identity.

## §12. The Helmholtz Decomposition

> **Theorem (Helmholtz).** Every sufficiently smooth $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$ decaying at infinity decomposes uniquely as:
> 
> $$\mathbf{F} = \underbrace{-\nabla\phi}_{\text{irrotational}} + \underbrace{\nabla\times\mathbf{A}}_{\text{solenoidal}}$$

The scalar potential $\phi$ and vector potential $\mathbf{A}$ are determined by: $$\phi(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\int_{\mathbb{R}^3}\frac{\nabla'\cdot\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|},dV', \qquad \mathbf{A}(\mathbf{r}) = \frac{1}{4\pi}\int_{\mathbb{R}^3}\frac{\nabla'\times\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}')}{|\mathbf{r}-\mathbf{r}'|},dV'$$

**What this means in words:** $\mathbf{F}$ is completely and uniquely determined by specifying two scalar/vector functions:

- its divergence $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$ (which determines the irrotational part)
- its curl $\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$ (which determines the solenoidal part)

**The analogy with matrices:** Any matrix $M$ decomposes into symmetric + antisymmetric parts: $M = \frac{M+M^T}{2} + \frac{M-M^T}{2}$. The Helmholtz decomposition is the continuous analog: the gradient (irrotational) part is the "symmetric" component of the field's local behavior; the curl (solenoidal) part is the "antisymmetric" component. This analogy is exact in the language of differential forms.

**Why Maxwell's equations use both div and curl:** The four equations are exactly the specification of $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$ via their divergences (Gauss's laws) and their curls (Faraday and Ampère). By Helmholtz, this completely determines the fields from the sources $\rho$ and $\mathbf{J}$.

## §13. Divergence in Curvilinear Coordinates

### 13.1 Cylindrical Coordinates $(r, \theta, z)$

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial(r F_r)}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial F_\theta}{\partial\theta} + \frac{\partial F_z}{\partial z}$$

The factor $1/r$ in the first term reflects the geometry: the area element at radius $r$ is $r,dr,d\theta$, not $dr,d\theta$.

### 13.2 Spherical Coordinates $(r, \theta, \phi)$

$$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial(r^2 F_r)}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial(\sin\theta, F_\theta)}{\partial\theta} + \frac{1}{r\sin\theta}\frac{\partial F_\phi}{\partial\phi}$$

For a purely radial field $\mathbf{F} = F_r(r)\hat{\mathbf{r}}$: $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{d(r^2 F_r)}{dr}$$

Check: for $F_r = r$ (i.e., $\mathbf{F} = \mathbf{r}$): $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{d(r^3)}{dr} = \frac{3r^2}{r^2} = 3$. Consistent with Cartesian result.

For $F_r = 1/r^2$ (Coulomb): $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{d(1)}{dr} = 0$. Consistent. $\checkmark$

> _As with the curl formulas: look these up rather than memorizing. Derive them from the intrinsic definition using the appropriate volume element if needed._

## §14. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> The divergence — as a measure of local expansion/contraction, a detector of sources and sinks, and the key ingredient in the Helmholtz decomposition — is woven into modern ML in ways that are increasingly recognized as fundamental. The keyword to watch: **volume**, **flow**, **density**, **incompressibility**, **information**.

### 14.1 Normalizing Flows — The Central Role of Divergence

**Normalizing flows** learn a diffeomorphism $\mathbf{g}_\theta: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$ that maps a simple distribution (Gaussian) to a complex data distribution. The change-of-variables formula:

$$\log p_X(\mathbf{x}) = \log p_Z(\mathbf{g}_\theta^{-1}(\mathbf{x})) + \log\left|\det J_{\mathbf{g}_\theta^{-1}}(\mathbf{x})\right|$$

requires computing $\log|\det J|$ — the log of the Jacobian determinant. For a $d\times d$ Jacobian, this is $O(d^3)$ — prohibitive for large $d$.

**FFJORD (Neural ODEs)** (Chen et al. 2018) defines the flow as a continuous ODE: $$\frac{d\mathbf{z}}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{z}, t)$$

By the **instantaneous change-of-variables formula** (Liouville's theorem in continuous time):

$$\frac{d\log p(\mathbf{z}(t))}{dt} = -\nabla\cdot f_\theta(\mathbf{z}(t), t) = -\text{tr}(J_{f_\theta})$$

The log-density evolves at rate equal to minus the **divergence** of the flow field. Instead of computing $\det J$ ($O(d^3)$), you only need $\text{tr}(J_{f_\theta})$ — the divergence — which is $O(d)$ via the Hutchinson estimator:

$$\text{tr}(J_{f_\theta}) \approx \mathbb{E}_{\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\sim\mathcal{N}(0,I)}\left[\boldsymbol{\epsilon}^T J_{f_\theta}\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\right]$$

This is **divergence as the computational bottleneck and solution** in generative modeling. The entire FFJORD framework rests on the fact that the Jacobian determinant of a flow is controlled by its divergence (trace of the Jacobian).

**Key insight:** The divergence-free condition ($\nabla\cdot f = 0$) means the flow preserves volume in latent space — log-density does not change. Volume-preserving flows are "free" in terms of log-likelihood cost. This is why **symplectic flows** (volume-preserving by construction) are computationally attractive.

### 14.2 Score-Based Diffusion Models — Divergence and the Score Function

**Score-based generative models** (Song & Ermon 2019; DDPM, DDIM, Score-SDE) learn the **score function** $\mathbf{s}_\theta(\mathbf{x}, t) \approx \nabla_{\mathbf{x}}\log p_t(\mathbf{x})$ — the gradient of the log-density.

The training objective via **score matching** involves the divergence:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{SM}} = \mathbb{E}_{p(\mathbf{x})}\left[\frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{s}_\theta(\mathbf{x})|^2 + \nabla\cdot\mathbf{s}_\theta(\mathbf{x})\right] + \text{const}$$

Here $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{s}_\theta$ is the **divergence of the score network** — it appears directly in the loss. Computing the exact divergence is $O(d^2)$ (all Jacobian diagonal entries), so in practice:

- **Denoising score matching** avoids computing divergence explicitly by using a surrogate loss.
- **Sliced score matching** uses random projections: $\mathbf{v}^T\nabla(\mathbf{v}^T\mathbf{s}_\theta)$ — a directional derivative, related to a projected divergence.

The **Fokker-Planck equation** for the density evolution under the diffusion SDE: $$\frac{\partial p_t}{\partial t} = -\nabla\cdot(f(\mathbf{x},t)p_t) + \frac{g(t)^2}{2}\nabla^2 p_t$$

is a **divergence equation** — the right side is $-\nabla\cdot(\text{probability flux})$. The probability flux $\mathbf{J} = f p_t - \frac{g^2}{2}\nabla p_t$ has divergence equal to minus the time derivative of the density. The entire theoretical framework of diffusion models rests on this divergence equation.

### 14.3 GAN Training — Divergences Between Distributions

This is a different but related usage of "divergence" — **statistical divergences** between distributions:

- **KL divergence:** $D_{KL}(p|q) = \int p\log(p/q),d\mathbf{x}$ (not a symmetric metric, but related to information theory)
- **Jensen-Shannon divergence:** $D_{JS}(p|q) = \frac{1}{2}D_{KL}(p|m) + \frac{1}{2}D_{KL}(q|m)$, where $m = (p+q)/2$. The original GAN minimizes $D_{JS}$.
- **Wasserstein distance** (Wasserstein GAN): $W_1(p, q) = \sup_{|\phi|_L \leq 1}\mathbb{E}_p[\phi] - \mathbb{E}_q[\phi]$. More stable training.
- **$f$-divergences:** $D_f(p|q) = \int q,f(p/q),d\mathbf{x}$. A general family containing KL and JS.

These "divergences" are not the vector-field divergence $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$ — they are measures of dissimilarity between distributions. However, the **connection** runs deep: in optimal transport theory, the Wasserstein distance is related to the divergence of a vector field via:

$$W_2^2(p, q) = \min_{\mathbf{v}: \frac{\partial p}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot(p\mathbf{v}) = 0} \int_0^1\int |\mathbf{v}|^2 p,d\mathbf{x},dt$$

The continuity equation $\partial_t p + \nabla\cdot(p\mathbf{v}) = 0$ — a **divergence equation** — is the constraint that defines geodesics in the space of probability distributions (Benamou-Brenier formula). The field-theoretic divergence and the probabilistic divergence are genuinely connected via fluid mechanics.

### 14.4 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) — Divergence as a Hard Constraint

PINNs for fluid dynamics enforce: $$\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u} = 0 \quad\text{(incompressibility)}$$

Two approaches:

**Soft constraint:** Add a penalty $\lambda|\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}|^2$ to the loss. This requires computing $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{u}$ at collocation points — automatic differentiation gives $\partial u_1/\partial x_1 + \cdots + \partial u_d/\partial x_d$, which is just the divergence. The network is penalized for having nonzero divergence.

**Hard constraint (divergence-free by construction):** Parameterize the velocity as: $$\mathbf{u} = \nabla\times\mathbf{A}$$

for a learned vector potential $\mathbf{A}$. Since $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{A}) = 0$ always, the incompressibility condition is automatically satisfied — no penalty needed, no error in the constraint. The network only needs to learn $\mathbf{A}$, which has one fewer degree of freedom.

This is a direct application of the theorem: solenoidal fields are exactly curls. **The divergence theorem, used backwards, gives you a parameterization that enforces the physics exactly.**

### 14.5 Equivariant Networks — Divergence-Free Feature Fields

**SE(3)-equivariant networks** (e3nn, NequIP, SEGNN) process 3D molecular data. Their internal representations include **vector fields on point clouds**. When modeling physical quantities:

- **Velocity fields** must be divergence-free (incompressible fluid).
- **Force fields** are gradients of potentials — irrotational, with $\nabla\times\mathbf{F} = 0$.
- **Electromagnetic fields** satisfy specific divergence and curl conditions.

Incorporating these constraints into the network architecture — by parameterizing features as $\nabla f$ (automatically irrotational) or $\nabla\times\mathbf{A}$ (automatically solenoidal) — drastically reduces the hypothesis space and improves data efficiency for physical property prediction.

### 14.6 Optimal Transport and the Wasserstein Geometry of ML

The **Benamou-Brenier formula** (mentioned above) casts optimal transport as a fluid mechanics problem. The probability density evolves via:

$$\frac{\partial\rho}{\partial t} + \nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v}) = 0$$

This is the **continuity equation** — a divergence equation. The velocity field $\mathbf{v}$ that minimizes $\int_0^1\int|\mathbf{v}|^2\rho,d\mathbf{x},dt$ subject to this constraint is the **optimal transport map**.

In practice, this framework underlies:

- **Wasserstein gradient flows** — the training dynamics of many generative models, viewed as gradient descent in the space of probability measures with the Wasserstein metric.
- **JKO scheme** — an implicit time-stepping scheme for diffusion equations: $\rho_{n+1} = \arg\min_\rho \frac{1}{2\tau}W_2^2(\rho, \rho_n) + \mathcal{F}(\rho)$.
- **Distribution shift in domain adaptation** — measuring and correcting for covariate shift using Wasserstein distances.

The divergence equation is the thread connecting all of these: the conservation of probability mass.

### 14.7 Information Geometry — Fisher Information and Divergence

The **Fisher information matrix** $I(\theta)$ defines a Riemannian metric on the space of parameterized distributions $p_\theta$. The **natural gradient** $\tilde{\nabla}\mathcal{L} = I(\theta)^{-1}\nabla\theta\mathcal{L}$ adjusts for the curvature of the distribution space.

The **KL divergence** $D_{KL}(p_\theta|p_{\theta+d\theta}) \approx \frac{1}{2}d\theta^T I(\theta),d\theta$ — the infinitesimal KL divergence is exactly the Fisher-Riemannian metric. This means:

- **Gradient descent on parameters $\theta$** = steepest descent in Euclidean parameter space.
- **Natural gradient descent** = steepest descent in the space of distributions, with distance measured by KL divergence.

The Laplace-Beltrami operator (the curved-space generalization of $\nabla^2 = \nabla\cdot\nabla$) governs how information "diffuses" on the statistical manifold. **Divergence on statistical manifolds** = the generalized $\nabla\cdot$ with respect to the Fisher metric — this is the arena of information geometry.

### 14.8 Hodge Theory on Graphs — Discrete Divergence

On a graph $G = (V, E)$, the **discrete divergence** of an edge flow $\mathbf{f}: E \to \mathbb{R}$ is the **boundary operator** $\partial_1$:

$$(\partial_1\mathbf{f})_v = \sum_{e: v = \text{head}(e)} f_e - \sum_{e: v = \text{tail}(e)} f_e$$

This is exactly the net flow out of each node — the discrete analog of $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$.

The **Hodge decomposition** of edge flows: $$\mathbf{f} = \underbrace{\partial_0^T\phi}_{\text{gradient (irrotational)}} + \underbrace{\partial_2^T \mathbf{g}}_{\text{curl (solenoidal)}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{h}}_{\text{harmonic}}$$

where:

- $\partial_0^T$ is the gradient operator (node values to edge differences)
- $\partial_2^T$ is the curl operator (triangle flows to edge curls)
- $\mathbf{h}$ is in the kernel of both — harmonic flows

The discrete divergence $\partial_1$ is the transpose of the gradient: $\partial_1 = \partial_0^T$'s adjoint. **In graph ML:**

- **Message passing** in GNNs is effectively computing (a learned version of) the discrete Laplacian $L = \partial_0^T\partial_0$ — related to $\nabla\cdot\nabla = \nabla^2$.
- **Hodge-aware GNNs** decompose edge features using the full Hodge decomposition, capturing divergence-free (cyclic) flow separately from gradient (acyclic) flow.
- **Traffic flow prediction**: the divergence-free component captures circular traffic patterns; the gradient component captures source-to-sink flow (e.g., from suburbs to city center).

The **graph Laplacian** $L = D - A$ (degree minus adjacency matrix) is the discrete Laplacian. The normalized version $L_{sym} = I - D^{-1/2}AD^{-1/2}$ is used in spectral GNNs. Both are discrete versions of $\nabla^2 = \nabla\cdot\nabla$.

$$\boxed{\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = \frac{\partial P}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial Q}{\partial y} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial z}}$$

## §16. Personal Notes

- The product rule $\nabla\cdot(f\mathbf{F}) = f,\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} + \nabla f\cdot\mathbf{F}$ is more useful than it looks. It is the engine behind Green's first identity, integration by parts for PDEs, and the derivation of the wave equation energy. Memorize it.
    
- The Coulomb/Newton field $\hat{\mathbf{r}}/r^2$ is the canonical example: zero divergence everywhere except the origin, nonzero flux through any enclosing surface. The resolution requires the Dirac delta. Every physics student must work this out at least once by hand. The dimensional analysis is also illuminating: in $\mathbb{R}^3$, the field decaying as $1/r^2$ has zero divergence; in $\mathbb{R}^2$, it would be $1/r$; in $\mathbb{R}^n$, it would be $r^{-(n-1)}$. This is why the Newtonian potential is $\log r$ in 2D and $1/r$ in 3D.
    
- The Helmholtz decomposition is the most important structural theorem in vector calculus. It says: to know a vector field, know its sources ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$) and its vortices ($\nabla\times\mathbf{F}$). Maxwell's equations are literally a specification of these two quantities for $\mathbf{E}$ and $\mathbf{B}$. The whole of electromagnetism is encoded in this decomposition.
    
- In ML, "divergence" appears in two senses: the vector-calculus divergence ($\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}$, measuring field expansion) and the information-theoretic divergence ($D_{KL}$, $D_{JS}$, measuring distribution dissimilarity). The two are connected by the Benamou-Brenier formula through the continuity equation. When you see $\nabla\cdot(\rho\mathbf{v}) = 0$, you are seeing the bridge between them.
    
- The score function in diffusion models is $\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p(\mathbf{x})$ — the gradient of the log-density. The divergence of the score is $\nabla^2\log p(\mathbf{x})$ — the Laplacian of the log-density. This appears in the score matching objective and in the Stein operator. The Laplacian is the divergence of a gradient; it measures the "concavity" of the log-density, i.e., how much the probability mass concentrates.
    
- The graph Laplacian $L = D - A$ is the discrete version of $-\nabla^2$ (note the sign: $-\nabla^2$ is positive semi-definite for a connected graph, as is $L$). The spectral decomposition of $L$ is the graph analog of Fourier analysis. GNNs that use $L$ are essentially performing a discrete version of the heat equation $\partial_t f = \nabla^2 f = -Lf$ — diffusion on the graph. Understanding divergence is understanding why this works.
    
- $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ is probably the most consequential consequence of the identity $\nabla\cdot(\nabla\times\mathbf{A}) = 0$. The non-existence of magnetic monopoles — a fundamental experimental fact — is in this one algebraic identity. Whether monopoles exist is still an open question in physics, but Maxwell's equations as formulated require $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{B} = 0$ rigorously.
