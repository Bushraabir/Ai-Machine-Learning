

> _"A curve is a map from an interval into space — one parameter, one dimension of freedom. A surface is a map from a region in the plane into space — two parameters, two dimensions of freedom. The whole theory of surface integrals, Stokes' theorem, and curvature flows from this one idea: a surface is not a set of points, it is a parametrization."_

---

## §1. Motivation — Why Parametrize?

A sphere of radius $R$ is the set ${(x,y,z): x^2+y^2+z^2 = R^2}$. This implicit description tells you which points are on the sphere but tells you nothing about how to integrate over it, compute its area, or define a normal vector field on it. For all of those tasks you need a **map** — a function that sweeps through the surface as its inputs vary.

The same principle that lets us integrate over a curve (parametrize it, pull back to a single-variable integral) extends to surfaces: parametrize it, pull back to a double integral. The parametrization introduces coordinates on the surface; the Jacobian of the parametrization tells you how area is distorted.

Almost every computable surface integral — flux, area, work — requires a parametrization. The natural objects of Stokes' theorem (the surface integral of curl) and the divergence theorem (the flux through a closed surface) are defined via parametrizations. This is not a computational trick; it is the correct definition.

---

## §2. Definition

### 2.1 Parametric Surface

> **Definition.** A **parametric surface** is a $C^1$ map $$\mathbf{r}: D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \longrightarrow \mathbb{R}^3$$ $$\mathbf{r}(u, v) = \langle x(u,v),; y(u,v),; z(u,v) \rangle$$ where $D$ is a bounded region in the $uv$-plane (the **parameter domain**).
> 
> The **image** $S = \mathbf{r}(D) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$ is the **surface**.

The variables $(u, v)$ are the **parameters**. Each point $(u_0, v_0) \in D$ maps to a single point $\mathbf{r}(u_0, v_0) \in S$. As $(u,v)$ varies over $D$, the image $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$ sweeps out $S$.

> **Warning.** The parametrization $\mathbf{r}$ and the surface $S = \mathbf{r}(D)$ are different objects. Many different parametrizations can give the same surface. The geometric properties of $S$ (area, normals, curvature) must be independent of the choice of parametrization; the formulas will involve the Jacobian precisely to ensure this independence.

### 2.2 Regular Points and Regular Surfaces

> **Definition.** A point $(u_0, v_0) \in D$ is **regular** if the partial derivatives $\mathbf{r}_u$ and $\mathbf{r}_v$ are linearly independent at $(u_0, v_0)$: $$\mathbf{r}_u(u_0, v_0) \times \mathbf{r}_v(u_0, v_0) \neq \mathbf{0}$$ A **regular surface** is one where every interior point of $D$ is regular.

**Why this matters:** At a regular point, $\mathbf{r}_u$ and $\mathbf{r}_v$ span a 2-dimensional tangent plane at $\mathbf{r}(u_0, v_0)$. At a non-regular (singular) point, the surface may self-intersect, form a cusp, or have no well-defined tangent plane. Singular points must be handled separately (they are often isolated and contribute zero area, so integrals are still well-defined).

## §3. The Tangent Plane and Normal Vector

### 3.1 Partial Derivatives as Tangent Vectors

Fix $(u_0, v_0) \in D$. The curve $u \mapsto \mathbf{r}(u, v_0)$ is a curve on $S$ through $\mathbf{r}(u_0, v_0)$; its tangent at $u_0$ is:

$$\mathbf{r}_u(u_0, v_0) = \left\langle \frac{\partial x}{\partial u},; \frac{\partial y}{\partial u},; \frac{\partial z}{\partial u}\right\rangle$$

Similarly, $v \mapsto \mathbf{r}(u_0, v)$ is a curve through $\mathbf{r}(u_0, v_0)$ with tangent:

$$\mathbf{r}_v(u_0, v_0) = \left\langle \frac{\partial x}{\partial v},; \frac{\partial y}{\partial v},; \frac{\partial z}{\partial v}\right\rangle$$

These two tangent vectors span the **tangent plane** to $S$ at $\mathbf{r}(u_0, v_0)$.

### 3.2 The Normal Vector

The **normal vector** to $S$ at $\mathbf{r}(u_0, v_0)$ is:

$$\mathbf{N}(u, v) = \mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v$$

This vector is perpendicular to both $\mathbf{r}_u$ and $\mathbf{r}_v$, hence perpendicular to the tangent plane — i.e., normal to the surface. The unit normal is:

$$\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v}{|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|}$$

**Orientation:** The choice of parameter ordering $(u,v)$ vs $(v,u)$ determines the direction of $\mathbf{N}$ (flipping the order reverses the sign). A **consistent orientation** of $S$ requires choosing $\mathbf{N}$ continuously across $S$. Not all surfaces admit a consistent orientation (e.g., the Möbius strip does not), but all the surfaces in Stewart and MIT 18.02 do.

### 3.3 The Fundamental Quantity: $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|$

The magnitude $|\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|$ is the **area distortion factor** of the parametrization — it tells you how much a small $du,dv$ rectangle in parameter space gets stretched when mapped to the surface. This is the surface-theoretic analog of $|f'(t)|$ for arc length.

## §4. Surface Area

### 4.1 Definition and Formula

> **Definition.** The **surface area** of a regular parametric surface $\mathbf{r}: D \to \mathbb{R}^3$ is: $$A(S) = \iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u \times \mathbf{r}_v|,dA$$

**Derivation.** Tile $D$ with small rectangles $[u, u+\Delta u]\times[v, v+\Delta v]$. The image of such a rectangle under $\mathbf{r}$ is approximately a parallelogram spanned by the vectors: $$\mathbf{r}_u,\Delta u \quad\text{and}\quad \mathbf{r}_v,\Delta v$$

The area of a parallelogram spanned by vectors $\mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{b}$ is $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|$. So the area of the image rectangle is approximately: $$|\mathbf{r}_u,\Delta u \times \mathbf{r}_v,\Delta v| = |\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,\Delta u,\Delta v$$

Summing over all rectangles and taking the limit gives the double integral. $\square$

### 4.2 Special Case: Graph of a Function

If $S$ is the graph $z = f(x,y)$ over a region $D$ in the $xy$-plane, parametrize by: $$\mathbf{r}(x, y) = \langle x,; y,; f(x,y)\rangle$$

Then: $$\mathbf{r}_x = \langle 1, 0, f_x\rangle, \qquad \mathbf{r}_y = \langle 0, 1, f_y\rangle$$

$$\mathbf{r}_x\times\mathbf{r}_y = \begin{vmatrix}\mathbf{i} & \mathbf{j} & \mathbf{k}\ 1 & 0 & f_x\ 0 & 1 & f_y\end{vmatrix} = \langle -f_x,; -f_y,; 1\rangle$$

$$|\mathbf{r}_x\times\mathbf{r}_y| = \sqrt{f_x^2 + f_y^2 + 1}$$

$$\boxed{A(S) = \iint_D\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\right)^2},dA}$$

This is the formula in Stewart §16.6 — it is a special case of the general formula.

## §5. Surface Integrals

There are two kinds of surface integrals, analogous to the two kinds of line integrals: **scalar** (integrating a function) and **vector** (integrating a vector field — flux).

### 5.1 Scalar Surface Integral

> **Definition.** For a scalar function $f: \mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}$, the **surface integral** of $f$ over $S$ is: $$\iint_S f,dS = \iint_D f(\mathbf{r}(u,v)),|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,dA$$

The element $dS = |\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,dA$ is the **scalar area element** — an infinitesimal patch of surface area.

**What it computes:**

- $f \equiv 1$: gives the surface area $A(S)$
- $f(\mathbf{r}) = $ mass density: gives total mass of the surface
- $f(\mathbf{r}) = $ temperature: gives total "heat content" on the surface

### 5.2 Vector Surface Integral (Flux)

> **Definition.** For a vector field $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}^3$, the **flux integral** (or **vector surface integral**) of $\mathbf{F}$ through $S$ is: $$\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_D \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(u,v))\cdot(\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v),dA$$

The element $d\mathbf{S} = (\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v),dA$ is the **vector area element** — an infinitesimal area patch with direction given by the normal.

**Equivalently:** $\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},dS$, where $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = (\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v)/|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|$ and $dS = |\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,dA$.

**Physical meaning:** The flux integral measures how much of $\mathbf{F}$ passes **through** the surface per unit time. If $\mathbf{F}$ is a fluid velocity field, $\iint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$ is the volumetric flow rate through $S$.

> **Key sign convention.** The sign of the flux integral depends on orientation. Reversing the orientation of $S$ (swapping $(u,v)$ to $(v,u)$) changes the sign of $\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v$, hence the sign of the flux. Always specify the orientation: "outward normal" for closed surfaces, or specify which side.

## §6. Proof: Independence of Parametrization

The area and flux integrals must be independent of the choice of parametrization. We verify this for area.

**Setup.** Let $\mathbf{r}: D \to S$ and $\tilde{\mathbf{r}}: \tilde{D} \to S$ be two regular parametrizations of the same surface, related by a $C^1$ bijection $\varphi: \tilde{D} \to D$ with $\tilde{\mathbf{r}} = \mathbf{r}\circ\varphi$. Let $\varphi(s,t) = (u(s,t), v(s,t))$.

**Claim:** $\iint_D |\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv = \iint_{\tilde{D}} |\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_s\times\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_t|,ds,dt$.

**Proof.** By the chain rule: $$\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_s = \mathbf{r}_u,u_s + \mathbf{r}_v,v_s, \qquad \tilde{\mathbf{r}}_t = \mathbf{r}_u,u_t + \mathbf{r}_v,v_t$$

Compute the cross product: $$\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_s\times\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_t = (\mathbf{r}_u,u_s + \mathbf{r}_v,v_s)\times(\mathbf{r}_u,u_t + \mathbf{r}_v,v_t)$$ $$= (u_s v_t - u_t v_s)(\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v) = J_\varphi\cdot(\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v)$$

where $J_\varphi = u_s v_t - u_t v_s = \det(D\varphi)$ is the Jacobian determinant of $\varphi$. Taking magnitudes: $$|\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_s\times\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_t| = |J_\varphi|\cdot|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|$$

By the change-of-variables theorem for double integrals: $$\iint_{\tilde{D}}|\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_s\times\tilde{\mathbf{r}}_t|,ds,dt = \iint_{\tilde{D}}|J_\varphi|\cdot|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|\circ\varphi,ds,dt = \iint_D|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv \quad\square$$

The $|J_\varphi|$ from the cross product cancels with (or is identified as) the $|J_\varphi|$ from the change-of-variables formula. This is not a coincidence — it is the content of the fact that the integral of a 2-form over a surface is well-defined.

## §7. Standard Examples and Their Parametrizations

### Example A — Sphere

**$S^2_R$: Sphere of radius $R$ centered at origin.**

$$\mathbf{r}(\theta, \phi) = \langle R\sin\phi\cos\theta,; R\sin\phi\sin\theta,; R\cos\phi\rangle$$

with $\theta \in [0, 2\pi]$, $\phi \in [0, \pi]$ (longitude, colatitude).

$$\mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -R\sin\phi\sin\theta,; R\sin\phi\cos\theta,; 0\rangle$$ $$\mathbf{r}_\phi = \langle R\cos\phi\cos\theta,; R\cos\phi\sin\theta,; -R\sin\phi\rangle$$

$$\mathbf{r}_\theta\times\mathbf{r}_\phi = \langle -R^2\sin^2\phi\cos\theta,; -R^2\sin^2\phi\sin\theta,; -R^2\sin\phi\cos\phi\rangle$$

$$|\mathbf{r}_\theta\times\mathbf{r}_\phi| = R^2\sin\phi$$

$$A(S^2_R) = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\pi R^2\sin\phi,d\phi,d\theta = R^2\cdot 2\pi\cdot 2 = 4\pi R^2 \quad\checkmark$$

The vector area element points **inward** (toward origin) in this parametrization — for outward flux, use $\mathbf{r}_\phi\times\mathbf{r}_\theta = -(\mathbf{r}_\theta\times\mathbf{r}_\phi)$, or simply negate the result.

---

### Example B — Cylinder

**$S$: Cylinder $x^2 + y^2 = R^2$, $0 \leq z \leq h$ (lateral surface only).**

$$\mathbf{r}(\theta, z) = \langle R\cos\theta,; R\sin\theta,; z\rangle, \quad \theta\in[0,2\pi],; z\in[0,h]$$

$$\mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -R\sin\theta,; R\cos\theta,; 0\rangle, \qquad \mathbf{r}_z = \langle 0,; 0,; 1\rangle$$

$$\mathbf{r}_\theta\times\mathbf{r}_z = \langle R\cos\theta,; R\sin\theta,; 0\rangle$$

$$|\mathbf{r}_\theta\times\mathbf{r}_z| = R$$

$$A = \int_0^h\int_0^{2\pi} R,d\theta,dz = 2\pi Rh \quad\checkmark$$

---

### Example C — Graph

**$S$: $z = 1 - x^2 - y^2$ over $D = {x^2+y^2\leq 1}$.**

Use $(x,y)$ as parameters directly: $$\mathbf{r}(x,y) = \langle x,; y,; 1-x^2-y^2\rangle$$ $$|\mathbf{r}_x\times\mathbf{r}_y| = \sqrt{1 + 4x^2 + 4y^2}$$

Switching to polar: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$: $$A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1\sqrt{1+4r^2},r,dr,d\theta = 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{8}\cdot\frac{2}{3}(1+4r^2)^{3/2}\Big|_0^1 = \frac{\pi}{6}(5^{3/2}-1)$$

---

### Example D — Torus

**$T$: Torus with major radius $R$, minor radius $r$, $R > r > 0$.**

$$\mathbf{r}(\theta, \phi) = \langle (R+r\cos\phi)\cos\theta,; (R+r\cos\phi)\sin\theta,; r\sin\phi\rangle$$

$\theta \in [0,2\pi]$ (angle around the torus axis), $\phi \in [0,2\pi]$ (angle in the tube cross-section).

$$\mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -(R+r\cos\phi)\sin\theta,; (R+r\cos\phi)\cos\theta,; 0\rangle$$ $$\mathbf{r}_\phi = \langle -r\sin\phi\cos\theta,; -r\sin\phi\sin\theta,; r\cos\phi\rangle$$

$$|\mathbf{r}_\theta\times\mathbf{r}_\phi| = r(R + r\cos\phi)$$

$$A(T) = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi} r(R+r\cos\phi),d\phi,d\theta = r\cdot 2\pi\cdot 2\pi R = 4\pi^2 Rr$$

(**Pappus's theorem** checks this: area $= 2\pi R\cdot(2\pi r)$ = circumference of circle swept $\times$ circumference of tube. $\checkmark$)

---

### Example E — Cone

**$S$: Cone $z = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$, $0\leq z\leq h$.**

Use polar coordinates in the $xy$-plane: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $z = r$: $$\mathbf{r}(r,\theta) = \langle r\cos\theta,; r\sin\theta,; r\rangle, \quad r\in[0,h],;\theta\in[0,2\pi]$$

$$\mathbf{r}_r = \langle \cos\theta,; \sin\theta,; 1\rangle, \qquad \mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -r\sin\theta,; r\cos\theta,; 0\rangle$$

$$\mathbf{r}_r\times\mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle -r\cos\theta,; -r\sin\theta,; r\rangle$$

$$|\mathbf{r}_r\times\mathbf{r}_\theta| = r\sqrt{2}$$

$$A = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^h r\sqrt{2},dr,d\theta = \sqrt{2}\pi h^2$$

## §8. Flux Integrals — Full Worked Examples

### Example F — Flux of $\mathbf{F} = \langle z, y, x\rangle$ Through the Unit Sphere (Outward)

Use the sphere parametrization from Example A with $R = 1$. The outward normal is $\mathbf{r}_\phi\times\mathbf{r}_\theta$:

$$\mathbf{r}_\phi\times\mathbf{r}_\theta = \langle \sin^2\phi\cos\theta,; \sin^2\phi\sin\theta,; \sin\phi\cos\phi\rangle$$

On the unit sphere: $\mathbf{F} = \langle \cos\phi,; \sin\phi\sin\theta,; \sin\phi\cos\theta\rangle$.

$$\mathbf{F}\cdot(\mathbf{r}_\phi\times\mathbf{r}_\theta) = \cos\phi\sin^2\phi\cos\theta + \sin^3\phi\sin^2\theta + \sin^2\phi\cos\phi\cos\theta$$

$$= 2\sin^2\phi\cos\phi\cos\theta + \sin^3\phi\sin^2\theta$$

Integrating over $\theta\in[0,2\pi]$: the first term integrates to zero (odd in $\cos\theta$), and: $$\int_0^{2\pi}\sin^2\theta,d\theta = \pi$$

$$\Phi = \int_0^\pi\pi\sin^3\phi,d\phi = \pi\cdot\frac{4}{3} = \frac{4\pi}{3}$$

---

### Example G — Verifying the Divergence Theorem

**$\mathbf{F} = \langle x, y, z\rangle$, $E$ = unit ball, $S$ = unit sphere (outward).**

**Via surface integral** (from Example F setup with $\mathbf{F} = \langle x,y,z\rangle = \hat{\mathbf{r}}$ on $S^2$):

On the unit sphere, $\mathbf{F} = \hat{\mathbf{r}}$ and the outward unit normal is also $\hat{\mathbf{r}}$, so $\mathbf{F}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}} = 1$: $$\oiint_S \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oiint_S 1,dS = 4\pi$$

**Via divergence theorem:** $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F} = 3$, $\iiint_B 3,dV = 3\cdot\frac{4\pi}{3} = 4\pi$. $\checkmark$

## §10. The First Fundamental Form (Metric Tensor)

The **first fundamental form** encodes the intrinsic geometry of the surface — distances and angles as measured within the surface itself, without reference to the ambient $\mathbb{R}^3$.

> **Definition.** The **first fundamental form** of a parametric surface $\mathbf{r}(u,v)$ is the $2\times 2$ symmetric matrix: $$g = \begin{pmatrix} E & F \ F & G \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_u & \mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_v \ \mathbf{r}_v\cdot\mathbf{r}_u & \mathbf{r}_v\cdot\mathbf{r}_v \end{pmatrix}$$

The area element in terms of $g$: $$|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v| = \sqrt{EG - F^2} = \sqrt{\det g}$$

**Proof.** By the Lagrange identity: $|\mathbf{a}\times\mathbf{b}|^2 = |\mathbf{a}|^2|\mathbf{b}|^2 - (\mathbf{a}\cdot\mathbf{b})^2$. So:

$$|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|^2 = |\mathbf{r}_u|^2|\mathbf{r}_v|^2 - (\mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_v)^2 = EG - F^2 = \det g \quad\square$$

**Meaning of each coefficient:**

- $E = |\mathbf{r}_u|^2$: squared speed along $u$-parameter curves
- $G = |\mathbf{r}_v|^2$: squared speed along $v$-parameter curves
- $F = \mathbf{r}_u\cdot\mathbf{r}_v$: how oblique the coordinate curves are ($F = 0$ means orthogonal parameter curves)

When $F = 0$ (orthogonal parameters), the area element simplifies to $\sqrt{EG},du,dv$.

**Arc length on the surface:** $$ds^2 = E,du^2 + 2F,du,dv + G,dv^2$$

This is the Riemannian metric induced on the surface by the ambient Euclidean metric. Everything about the intrinsic geometry of the surface — distances, angles, geodesics, Gaussian curvature — is determined by $g$.

## §11. Stokes' Theorem and the Divergence Theorem — Revisited

Now that we have the language of parametric surfaces, the two great theorems take their final form.

### 11.1 Stokes' Theorem

> **Theorem (Stokes).** Let $S$ be an oriented regular parametric surface with boundary $\partial S$ (oriented consistently via the right-hand rule), and let $\mathbf{F}$ be a $C^1$ vector field. Then: $$\iint_S (\nabla\times\mathbf{F})\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \oint_{\partial S}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

The surface integral of the curl equals the line integral around the boundary. This requires a parametric surface to even state: $d\mathbf{S}$ is defined via $\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v$.

### 11.2 Divergence Theorem

> **Theorem (Gauss).** Let $E$ be a solid region in $\mathbb{R}^3$ with boundary $\partial E$ (a closed surface, oriented with outward normals), and let $\mathbf{F}$ be $C^1$. Then: $$\iiint_E(\nabla\cdot\mathbf{F}),dV = \oiint_{\partial E}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$$

The boundary integral $\oiint_{\partial E}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$ is a surface integral over a **closed parametric surface** — it requires parametrizing the boundary.

**The connection:** Both theorems say the same thing in different dimensions:

$$\int_{\partial M}\omega = \int_M d\omega$$

This is **Stokes' theorem in differential forms**, where $M$ is a manifold with boundary and $\omega$ is a differential form. For $M$ = curve, this is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus. For $M$ = surface, this is Stokes. For $M$ = solid region, this is the Divergence Theorem. Parametric surfaces are the concrete objects that make $\int_M d\omega$ computable.

## §12. Piecewise Smooth Surfaces

Most physically relevant surfaces are not globally smooth — they have edges and corners. A **piecewise smooth surface** is a finite union of regular parametric surfaces $S_1, S_2, \ldots, S_k$ meeting along curves (edges), with consistent orientation.

**Examples:**

- A cube: 6 flat faces meeting at edges and corners
- A cylinder with caps: lateral surface + top disk + bottom disk
- Any polyhedron

For such surfaces: $$\iint_S\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = \sum_{i=1}^k\iint_{S_i}\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}_i$$

The edge contributions cancel if orientation is consistent, so Stokes' and the divergence theorem still hold.

## §13. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> Parametric surfaces appear in ML wherever a low-dimensional structure is embedded in a high-dimensional space, wherever a model outputs a surface, or wherever geometry is computed on learned manifolds. The keyword: **manifold**, **mesh**, **implicit surface**, **surface reconstruction**, **3D generation**.

### 13.1 Neural Implicit Surfaces — The Zero Level Set

The dominant paradigm in neural 3D representation is to represent a surface **implicitly**: learn a function $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$ such that the surface is the **zero level set**:

$$S = {(x,y,z): f_\theta(x,y,z) = 0}$$

This is how **NeRF** (Mildenhall et al. 2020), **DeepSDF** (Park et al. 2019), and **Occupancy Networks** (Mescheder et al. 2019) represent geometry. The surface is implicit; to compute surface integrals, area, or normals, one must **extract a parametrization** — either analytically (via implicit function theorem) or computationally (via marching cubes).

The **unit normal** at a point of the zero level set is: $$\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \frac{\nabla f_\theta}{|\nabla f_\theta|}$$

This is the gradient of the implicit function, normalized. Computing normals requires computing $\nabla f_\theta$ — automatic differentiation through the network. The **Eikonal constraint** $|\nabla f_\theta| = 1$ (used in **IGR**, **NeuS**) ensures the function is a signed distance function, which stabilizes training and ensures the normal computation is well-conditioned.

**SDF (Signed Distance Functions):** $f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = $ signed distance from $\mathbf{x}$ to $S$. For SDFs:

- $\nabla f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) = \hat{\mathbf{n}}$ at surface points
- The **mean curvature** of $S$ is $H = \frac{1}{2}\nabla\cdot(\nabla f / |\nabla f|) = \frac{1}{2}\nabla\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ — a divergence of the normal field
- The **surface area** can be recovered as $\int |\nabla f|,dV$ via the co-area formula

### 13.2 Neural Parametric Surfaces — Explicit Surface Generation

Some models directly output a **parametrization**:

$$\mathbf{r}_\theta: [0,1]^2 \to \mathbb{R}^3$$

**AtlasNet** (Groueix et al. 2018) represents a 3D shape as a collection of learned maps from the 2D unit square to patches on the surface — literally learned parametric surface patches. The model generates a point cloud by sampling $(u,v)$ from $[0,1]^2$ and evaluating $\mathbf{r}_\theta(u,v, \mathbf{c})$ where $\mathbf{c}$ is a shape latent code.

The geometric quantities of these surfaces — area, normals, curvature — are computed via $\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v$ directly using automatic differentiation.

**Surface area regularization** in these models: $$\mathcal{L}_{\text{area}} = \iint_{[0,1]^2}|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv$$

This prevents the learned surface from self-intersecting or becoming degenerate (the integrand going to zero = parametrization becoming singular). The regularizer is exactly the surface area formula — penalizing large area encourages compact surfaces.

### 13.3 Geometry Processing and Mesh-Based Deep Learning

**Meshes** are piecewise-linear parametric surfaces — a union of triangular patches. Each triangle is parametrized by barycentric coordinates $(u,v)$ with $u, v \geq 0$, $u + v \leq 1$.

For a triangle with vertices $\mathbf{v}_0, \mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2$: $$\mathbf{r}(u,v) = (1-u-v)\mathbf{v}_0 + u\mathbf{v}_1 + v\mathbf{v}_2$$

$$\mathbf{r}_u = \mathbf{v}_1 - \mathbf{v}_0, \qquad \mathbf{r}_v = \mathbf{v}_2 - \mathbf{v}_0$$

$$|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v| = 2\cdot\text{Area(triangle)}$$

This is the foundational formula in **mesh-based neural networks** (MeshCNN, Neural Subdivision, DiffMesh, etc.):

- **Normal prediction**: compute $\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v$ per triangle — face normal
- **Laplace-Beltrami operator on meshes**: the cotangent Laplacian, used as the graph Laplacian in spectral GNNs on 3D shapes, is derived from the cotangents of triangle angles — related to the metric coefficients $E, F, G$
- **Differentiable rendering**: rendering a mesh requires integrating over visible surface patches — a surface integral with visibility weighting. Backpropagating through this requires differentiating $|\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|$ with respect to vertex positions $\mathbf{v}_i$.

### 13.4 Differentiable Rendering — Surface Integrals in 3D Vision

**Differentiable rendering** (SoftRas, DIB-R, NVDiffrast, nvdiffrec) aims to backpropagate gradients through the rendering process to recover 3D geometry from 2D images. The rendering equation is a surface integral:

$$L_o(\mathbf{x}, \omega_o) = \int_{\Omega^+} f_r(\mathbf{x}, \omega_i, \omega_o),L_i(\mathbf{x}, \omega_i),(\omega_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}}),d\omega_i$$

where $L_o$ is outgoing radiance, $f_r$ is the BRDF (bidirectional reflectance distribution function), and $d\omega_i$ is the solid angle measure. This integral is over the hemisphere above the surface — it is a surface integral on the sphere of directions, weighted by the cosine $(\omega_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})$.

The normal $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$ is computed from the parametrization via $\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v$. Backpropagating through the rendering equation to recover the geometry parameters $\theta$ requires differentiating surface integrals with respect to the parametrization — this is why differentiable rendering is hard and why the mathematical machinery of parametric surfaces is central to it.

### 13.5 Gaussian Splatting and Point Cloud Representations

**3D Gaussian Splatting** (Kerbl et al. 2023) represents a scene as a collection of 3D Gaussian "splats" — each Gaussian has a mean position, a covariance matrix, color, and opacity. The covariance matrix $\Sigma = RSS^TR^T$ ($R$ = rotation, $S$ = scaling) implicitly defines a local surface patch: the level set ${x: (x-\mu)^T\Sigma^{-1}(x-\mu) = c}$ is an **ellipsoid** — a parametric surface.

The rendering of Gaussian splatting involves projecting these 3D Gaussians onto the 2D image plane — a projection that depends on the viewing direction and the surface normal (computed from $\Sigma$). The per-Gaussian surface area and normal are computed from the eigendecomposition of $\Sigma$, which is the surface theoretic machinery of the first fundamental form in disguise.

### 13.6 Physics Simulations — FEM and Surface Integrals

**Finite Element Method (FEM)** for physics simulation (used in neural physics simulators like PAC-NeRF, DiffSim, NeuralSim) divides the domain into triangular or tetrahedral elements. Each element is a parametric surface/volume. Simulation quantities — stress, strain, force — involve surface integrals over element boundaries:

$$\mathbf{f}_{\text{internal}} = \oint_{\partial E} \sigma\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}},dS$$

where $\sigma$ is the stress tensor. This is a surface integral over each element's boundary. Neural FEM methods learn to parameterize the displacement field and compute these surface integrals via automatic differentiation through the parametrization.

### 13.7 Manifold Learning and Intrinsic Geometry

**Diffusion maps**, **UMAP**, **isomap**, and other manifold learning methods assume that high-dimensional data lies near a low-dimensional **manifold** — a generalized parametric surface. The goal is to find coordinates $(u_1, \ldots, u_k)$ such that the data is (approximately) the image of a smooth map $\mathbf{r}: \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^n$.

The **Riemannian metric** $g_{ij} = \mathbf{r}_{u_i}\cdot\mathbf{r}_{u_j}$ on the manifold — the first fundamental form — determines geodesic distances, which are what these algorithms try to preserve or compute.

**Intrinsic vs. extrinsic distances:**

- **Extrinsic**: Euclidean distance in the ambient $\mathbb{R}^n$
- **Intrinsic**: geodesic distance along the manifold, measured by the metric $g$

UMAP and isomap approximate intrinsic distances. The correctness of these approximations depends on the geometry of the parametric surface: how curved it is, how the metric $g$ compares to the flat Euclidean metric.

**Latent diffusion models and VAEs** can be understood as learning a parametrization of the data manifold: the decoder $\mathbf{r}_\theta: \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is a parametric map from latent space to data space, and the geometry of this map (Jacobian structure, metric, area distortion) affects the quality of generated samples.

## §15. Personal Notes

- The formula $dS = |\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,du,dv$ is the analogy of $ds = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt$ for arc length. Both involve the norm of a derivative (or cross product of derivatives) as the "stretching factor." For curves: length of tangent vector. For surfaces: area of the parallelogram of two tangent vectors. This pattern extends to $k$-manifolds in $\mathbb{R}^n$: the $k$-dimensional volume element is the norm of the wedge product of $k$ tangent vectors.
    
- The proof of parametrization-independence (§6) is the cleanest illustration of why the cross product and the change-of-variables theorem are duals of each other. The Jacobian of the reparametrization that appears in the cross product formula is exactly the one absorbed by the change-of-variables — this is the statement that a 2-form transforms correctly under diffeomorphism. The surface integral is really the integral of a differential form, and differential forms know how to transform.
    
- The first fundamental form $g = \begin{pmatrix}E & F \ F & G\end{pmatrix}$ is the Riemannian metric. It contains all intrinsic geometry. The Gaussian curvature $K$ (a single function on $S$) is determined entirely by $g$ — this is **Gauss's Theorema Egregium**. Curvature is intrinsic. A flat map of the Earth must distort something (either areas or angles or both), and the quantitative measure of how much distortion is unavoidable is $K$. In ML, the Riemannian metric on the data manifold is what manifold learning algorithms are trying to approximate.
    
- The two surface integrals ($\iint_S f,dS$ scalar vs. $\iint_S\mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{S}$ flux) are genuinely different objects. The scalar integral is orientation-independent (reversing the normal doesn't change $dS = |\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v|,dA$). The flux integral is orientation-dependent (reversing the normal changes $d\mathbf{S} = (\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v),dA$ to $-d\mathbf{S}$). Always check which you are computing.
    
- In ML, the most important surface integral is the **rendering equation** and the most important parametric surface tool is computing normals via $\hat{\mathbf{n}} = \nabla f / |\nabla f|$ for implicit surfaces. Both reduce to the core of this note: $\mathbf{r}_u\times\mathbf{r}_v$ gives normals and area elements. The entire pipeline from 3D neural reconstruction to differentiable rendering runs on this formula.
    
- The fact that the Divergence Theorem and Stokes' Theorem both require surface integrals over **oriented** surfaces is not a technicality — it reflects the physical reality that flux has a sign. Gauss's law $\nabla\cdot\mathbf{E} = \rho/\varepsilon_0$ integrates to $\oiint_S\mathbf{E}\cdot d\mathbf{S} = Q_{\text{enc}}/\varepsilon_0$, where the sign of the enclosed charge determines the sign of the flux. Orientation is physics.
