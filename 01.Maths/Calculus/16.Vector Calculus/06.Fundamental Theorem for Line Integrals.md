## §1. Motivation — Why Should We Care?

Computing a line integral $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ directly is often brutal. You parameterize the curve, substitute, and grind through an ugly integral. But sometimes — if you're lucky — the answer depends **only on the endpoints**, not the path. The Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals (FTLI) tells you exactly when this miracle occurs, and hands you a shortcut that reduces the entire integral to a two-point evaluation.

This is the multivariable soul of the single-variable FTC: $$\int_a^b f'(x),dx = f(b) - f(a)$$

The FTLI says: if $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$, then $$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(\mathbf{r}(b)) - f(\mathbf{r}(a))$$

---

## §2. Setup and Definitions

### 2.1 Vector Fields and Line Integrals

Let $\mathbf{F}: U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be a vector field on an open set $U$. A **smooth curve** $C$ in $U$ is given by a parameterization $\mathbf{r}: [a,b] \to U$, $\mathbf{r} \in C^1$. The **line integral** of $\mathbf{F}$ along $C$ is: $$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t),dt$$

In $\mathbb{R}^2$: if $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q \rangle$ and $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t)\rangle$, $$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \left(P,\frac{dx}{dt} + Q,\frac{dy}{dt}\right)dt = \int_C P,dx + Q,dy$$

### 2.2 Conservative Fields and Potential Functions

**Definition.** A vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on $U$ is called **conservative** (or a _gradient field_) if there exists a scalar function $f: U \to \mathbb{R}$, called a **potential function**, such that: $$\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$$

Explicitly in $\mathbb{R}^3$: $$P = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}, \qquad Q = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}, \qquad R = \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}$$

> **Notation remark.** Some texts write $f$ as $\varphi$ or $\phi$ for the potential. Physics uses $V$ (potential energy) with a sign flip: $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla V$.

## §3. The Theorem

> **Theorem (Fundamental Theorem for Line Integrals).** Let $U \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ be an open set and $f: U \to \mathbb{R}$ a function of class $C^1$. Let $C$ be a piecewise smooth curve in $U$ parameterized by $\mathbf{r}: [a,b] \to U$. Then: $$\boxed{\int_C \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(\mathbf{r}(b)) - f(\mathbf{r}(a))}$$

### Proof.

Apply the chain rule. Since $f \circ \mathbf{r}: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$ is $C^1$, the single-variable FTC applies:

$$\int_C \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_a^b \nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t),dt$$

By the multivariable chain rule: $$\frac{d}{dt}\bigl[f(\mathbf{r}(t))\bigr] = \nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)$$

Therefore: $$\int_a^b \nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t),dt = \int_a^b \frac{d}{dt}\bigl[f(\mathbf{r}(t))\bigr],dt = f(\mathbf{r}(b)) - f(\mathbf{r}(a)) \qquad \square$$

**Note.** The proof works equally for piecewise smooth curves by applying the argument on each smooth piece and telescoping.

## §4. Immediate Corollaries

### Corollary 1: Path Independence

If $\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ on $U$, and $C_1, C_2$ are any two piecewise smooth curves in $U$ with the same initial point $A$ and terminal point $B$, then: $$\int_{C_1} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_{C_2} \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(B) - f(A)$$

The integral depends **only on the endpoints**, not the path.

### Corollary 2: Closed Curves Give Zero

If $C$ is a **closed curve** (i.e., $\mathbf{r}(a) = \mathbf{r}(b)$), then: $$\oint_C \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(\mathbf{r}(b)) - f(\mathbf{r}(a)) = 0$$

This is one of the key characterizations of conservative fields.

### Corollary 3: Equivalences

For a vector field $\mathbf{F}$ on a **simply connected** open domain $U$, the following are equivalent:

1. $\mathbf{F}$ is conservative ($\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ for some $f$)
2. $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ is path-independent in $U$
3. $\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ for every closed curve $C$ in $U$
4. In $\mathbb{R}^2$: $\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}$ (the curl condition)

> **Why simply connected matters.** On a domain with a "hole" (like $\mathbb{R}^2 \setminus {0}$), a field can satisfy the curl condition without being conservative. The canonical example: $\mathbf{F} = \left\langle \frac{-y}{x^2+y^2}, \frac{x}{x^2+y^2} \right\rangle$ satisfies $\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}$ but $\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 2\pi \neq 0$ around the unit circle. The hole at the origin is the obstruction.

## §5. Finding the Potential Function

Given that $\mathbf{F} = \langle P, Q, R\rangle$ is conservative, how do we find $f$?

**Algorithm (method of integration):**

1. Integrate $P$ with respect to $x$: $f = \int P,dx + g(y,z)$
2. Differentiate with respect to $y$, set equal to $Q$, solve for $\partial g/\partial y$
3. Integrate to get $g$, which may depend on $z$
4. Differentiate with respect to $z$, set equal to $R$, determine $g$'s dependence on $z$

**Example.** Let $\mathbf{F} = \langle 2xy + z^2,; x^2,; 2xz\rangle$.

_Check conservativeness:_ $$\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = 2x = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x} \checkmark, \quad \frac{\partial P}{\partial z} = 2z = \frac{\partial R}{\partial x} \checkmark, \quad \frac{\partial Q}{\partial z} = 0 = \frac{\partial R}{\partial y} \checkmark$$

_Find $f$:_

$$f = \int (2xy + z^2),dx = x^2 y + xz^2 + g(y,z)$$

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2 + \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} = x^2 \implies \frac{\partial g}{\partial y} = 0 \implies g = h(z)$$

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial z} = 2xz + h'(z) = 2xz \implies h'(z) = 0 \implies h = C$$

$$\boxed{f(x,y,z) = x^2 y + xz^2 + C}$$

## §6. Worked Examples

### Example A — Direct Application

**Problem.** Evaluate $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ where $\mathbf{F} = \langle ye^x, e^x + 2y\rangle$ and $C$ is any smooth curve from $(0,1)$ to $(1,2)$.

**Solution.** Check: $\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = e^x = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}$. Conservative. ✓

Find $f$: $f = \int ye^x,dx = ye^x + g(y)$. Then $\frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = e^x + g'(y) = e^x + 2y \implies g'(y) = 2y \implies g = y^2$.

So $f(x,y) = ye^x + y^2$.

$$\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(1,2) - f(0,1) = (2e + 4) - (1 \cdot 1 + 1) = 2e + 4 - 2 = 2e + 2$$

No messy parameterization needed. Done.

---

### Example B — Verifying Path Independence Numerically

**Field:** $\mathbf{F} = \langle 2x, 2y\rangle = \nabla(x^2 + y^2)$. Integrate from $A=(0,0)$ to $B=(1,1)$.

_Path 1:_ Along $y = x$, parameterize $\mathbf{r}(t) = (t, t)$, $t \in [0,1]$: $$\int_0^1 (2t, 2t) \cdot (1,1),dt = \int_0^1 4t,dt = 2$$

_Path 2:_ Along $y = x^2$, $\mathbf{r}(t) = (t, t^2)$: $$\int_0^1 (2t, 2t^2) \cdot (1, 2t),dt = \int_0^1 (2t + 4t^3),dt = [t^2 + t^4]_0^1 = 2$$

_Via FTLI:_ $f(B) - f(A) = (1+1) - (0+0) = 2$. ✓

---

### Example C — Non-Conservative Field (Failure Case)

**Field:** $\mathbf{F} = \langle -y, x\rangle$.

Check: $\frac{\partial P}{\partial y} = -1 \neq 1 = \frac{\partial Q}{\partial x}$. **Not conservative.**

Around the unit circle $C: \mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t, \sin t)$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$: $$\oint_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r} = \int_0^{2\pi} (-\sin t, \cos t)\cdot(-\sin t, \cos t),dt = \int_0^{2\pi} 1,dt = 2\pi \neq 0$$

The FTLI does **not** apply here. This field is a rotation field; it does work around loops.

## §7. Visualization

### 7.1 The Big Picture — Conceptual Diagram

```
SCALAR FIELD f(x,y)         VECTOR FIELD F = ∇f
─────────────────────────   ────────────────────────────────
   f measures "height"          F = gradient = arrows pointing
   at each point                uphill, ⊥ to level curves

       f = 5 ─── ─── ─── 
       f = 3  ╲  ↗↗↗   ╱
       f = 1    ╲↗↗↗↗╱
                 ↗↗↗↗
       f = 0 ─── ─── ───
       
  ∫_C ∇f · dr = f(B) - f(A)   (path doesn't matter!)
  
  Like hiking: net altitude gain depends only on
  start and end elevation, NOT the trail you take.
```

### 7.2 Level Curves and Gradient Arrows (ℝ²)

For $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ (paraboloid), level curves are circles, $\nabla f = \langle 2x, 2y\rangle$ points radially outward:

```
        ·····  f=4  ·····
      ·· ↑↖  f=2  ↗↑ ··
     · ←    ↑ f=1 ↑    → ·
      ·· ↙  ↓     ↓ ↘ ··
        ·····  f=4  ·····
           (gradient arrows
           always ⊥ level curves)
```

**Key insight:** Any path from $f=1$ circle to $f=4$ circle, regardless of how it winds, gives $\int \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{r} = 4 - 1 = 3$.

### 7.3 The "Altitude" Analogy

Think of $f$ as a topographic map of elevation.

- $\nabla f$ = the gradient vector = direction and steepness of ascent
- $\mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ = "how much you climb" along each infinitesimal step
- $\int_C \mathbf{F} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$ = total elevation gain along the hike

**FTLI says:** Total elevation gain = (final elevation) − (initial elevation). Whether you switchback, spiral, or go straight up — doesn't matter.

## §8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> This is where the FTLI stops being just a calculus theorem and becomes a **foundational idea behind how neural networks are trained**.

### 8.1 Gradient Descent and Conservative Fields

The **loss function** $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ in machine learning (where $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the parameter vector) plays the role of the potential $f$.

The gradient $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}$ is exactly the vector field $\mathbf{F}$ of the FTLI. Gradient descent updates: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta,\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

This is numerically following $-\nabla f$ — moving **downhill** in the loss landscape. The FTLI tells us that, in principle, the cumulative change in loss along any optimization path depends only on the starting and ending parameter values: $$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{final}}) - \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_{\text{init}}) = \int_C \nabla \mathcal{L} \cdot d\boldsymbol{\theta}$$

### 8.2 Backpropagation and the Chain Rule

The proof of the FTLI is essentially a **chain rule argument**: $$\frac{d}{dt}[f(\mathbf{r}(t))] = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)$$

Backpropagation in deep learning is the **same chain rule**, applied through the composition of layers: $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(1)}} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}^{(L)}} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{a}^{(L)}}{\partial \mathbf{a}^{(L-1)}} \cdots \frac{\partial \mathbf{a}^{(2)}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}^{(1)}}$$

This is literally the multivariable chain rule telescoped through the computational graph — the same mathematical mechanism that proves the FTLI.

### 8.3 Path Independence in Optimization

The FTLI's path-independence corollary has a deep implication: **if the loss landscape were a perfect gradient field** (which it often approximately is locally), then the order of gradient steps wouldn't matter — only the endpoints would. This is why:

- **Stochastic gradient descent (SGD)** with different orderings of mini-batches converges to similar loss values (not exactly, due to noise, but approximately)
- **Learning rate schedules** affect the path $C$ through parameter space, but the final $\mathcal{L}$ depends primarily on where you end up, not how you got there

### 8.4 Conservative vs Non-Conservative Dynamics

Not all vector fields in ML are conservative (gradient fields):

|Scenario|Conservative?|Implication|
|---|---|---|
|Standard gradient descent|Yes|FTLI applies; pure descent|
|Momentum (SGD with momentum)|No|Non-conservative; path matters|
|GAN training dynamics|No|Can cycle; no potential function|
|Natural gradient (Fisher matrix)|Riemannian|Modified FTLI on manifold|

**GANs** are particularly interesting: the generator and discriminator create a game where the dynamics are a **non-conservative vector field** in the joint parameter space. This is why GAN training can cycle and is notoriously unstable — there is no global potential function whose minimum you're descending toward.

### 8.5 Energy Functions in Physics-Inspired ML

In **Energy-Based Models (EBMs)**, the model learns an explicit energy function $E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{x})$ — literally the potential function $f$ of the FTLI. The probability of a data point $\mathbf{x}$ is: $$p_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{e^{-E_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}(\mathbf{x})}}{Z(\boldsymbol{\theta})}$$

Training EBMs involves computing gradients of $E$ — exactly $\nabla E$ — and the geometry of the energy landscape (level curves, gradient flow) directly determines model behavior.

### 8.6 The Geometry of Loss Landscapes

Modern deep learning research studies the **loss landscape** $\mathcal{L}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ (where $n$ can be $10^9$ parameters). Key concepts directly from FTLI/multivariable calculus:

- **Saddle points**: where $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 0$ but it's not a minimum — gradient descent escapes these due to the asymmetry of the Hessian
- **Flat regions / loss plateaus**: where $|\nabla \mathcal{L}|$ is small; gradient steps are tiny
- **Mode connectivity**: recent work shows that different minima of neural networks are often connected by **paths of low loss** — this is a statement about the topology of level sets of $\mathcal{L}$, directly the level curve language of the FTLI

## §9. Summary and Key Takeaways

$$\boxed{\int_C \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{r} = f(\text{endpoint}) - f(\text{startpoint})}$$

|Concept|Key Point|
|---|---|
|**Conservative field**|$\mathbf{F} = \nabla f$ for some scalar $f$|
|**Path independence**|Equivalent to $\mathbf{F}$ being conservative (on simply connected domain)|
|**Closed curve**|$\oint_C \mathbf{F}\cdot d\mathbf{r} = 0$ iff conservative|
|**Curl test**|$\nabla \times \mathbf{F} = 0$ necessary; sufficient on simply connected domain|
|**Proof engine**|Multivariable chain rule + single-variable FTC|
|**ML connection**|$f = \mathcal{L}$, backprop = chain rule, gradient descent = following $-\nabla f$|

## §10. Personal Notes & Things to Remember

- The proof is embarrassingly short once you see it — it's just the chain rule and the FTC working in concert. The hard part is _believing_ path independence is possible.
- The simply connected condition is not just technical hygiene — it's the reason electrostatics works differently outside a charged wire than outside a charged sphere.
- In ML, "loss landscape" is the $n$-dimensional analog of a topographic map, and every intuition from the FTLI (level curves, gradient orthogonality, path independence) carries over, just in $n \gg 3$ dimensions.
- The failure of conservativeness in GANs is not a bug — it's a feature of two-player games. The fixed point is not a minimum of anything; it's a Nash equilibrium.
- Always verify the curl condition **before** hunting for a potential function. Saves a lot of wasted work.
