
> _"Infinity is not a place the function goes — it is a direction the input travels, and we ask what the output approaches."_

---

## §1. Motivation

Limits at a point ask: _what does $f(x)$ approach as $x$ gets close to $a$?_

Limits at infinity ask something geometrically different: _what does $f(x)$ approach as $x$ grows without bound?_ We are not plugging in $\infty$ — that is not a number. We are describing the **long-run behavior** of a function, its eventual tendency as the input escapes toward the horizon.

This is the language of asymptotes, of steady states, of what a neural network's loss converges to after thousands of epochs.

---

## §2. The Formal Definition

> **Definition.** We write $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$ if: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $M > 0$ such that $$x > M \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$$

Compare this with the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition of a finite limit. The role of $\delta$ (a small radius around $a$) is now played by $M$ (a large threshold past which $x$ must lie). Instead of _proximity to a point_, we have _escape to the right_.

Similarly:

> $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L$ if: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $M$ such that $x < M \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$

### Geometric Reading: Horizontal Asymptotes

When $\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$, the line $y = L$ is a **horizontal asymptote** as $x \to +\infty$.

```
 y
 L ·····●·············●·············●·········  ← f(x) hugging y = L
   |        /
   |      /
   |    /
   +--+--+--+--+--+--+--+--+--+-- x
      M
```

A function can have **at most two** horizontal asymptotes (one as $x \to +\infty$, one as $x \to -\infty$), and they need not be the same.

_Example._ $f(x) = \arctan(x)$: $$\lim_{x \to +\infty} \arctan(x) = \frac{\pi}{2}, \qquad \lim_{x \to -\infty} \arctan(x) = -\frac{\pi}{2}$$

Two distinct horizontal asymptotes.

## §3. Infinite Limits at Infinity

We must distinguish carefully between two distinct phenomena:

|Notation|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$|$f(x)$ approaches a **finite value** $L$|
|$\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = \infty$|$f(x)$ grows **without bound**|

The second is **not** a limit in the strict sense — the limit does not exist (no real number is approached). It is a useful notation for the _manner_ of divergence.

> **Definition.** $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = \infty$ means: for every $N > 0$, there exists $M$ such that $x > M \implies f(x) > N$.

You challenge me with any height $N$; I find a point past which $f$ stays above it. Similarly defined for $-\infty$.

## §4. Computing Limits at Infinity — The Core Techniques

### 4.1 The Fundamental Fact

$$\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} \frac{1}{x^r} = 0 \quad \text{for any } r > 0$$

This is the anchor. Every algebraic technique for limits at infinity reduces to this.

### 4.2 Rational Functions: Divide by Dominant Power

For a rational function $\dfrac{p(x)}{q(x)}$, divide numerator and denominator by $x^n$ where $n = \deg(q)$.

**Three cases by degree comparison:**

**Case 1: $\deg(p) < \deg(q)$** — limit is $0$.

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^2 + 1}{x^3 - 5} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3/x + 1/x^3}{1 - 5/x^3} = \frac{0}{1} = 0$$

**Case 2: $\deg(p) = \deg(q)$** — limit is ratio of leading coefficients.

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{4x^3 - 2x}{7x^3 + x^2} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{4 - 2/x^2}{7 + 1/x} = \frac{4}{7}$$

**Case 3: $\deg(p) > \deg(q)$** — limit is $\pm\infty$ (diverges).

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^3 + 1}{x^2 - 3} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x + 1/x^2}{1 - 3/x^2} = \infty$$

> _Mnemonic: Bottom heavy → 0. Equal degree → leading ratio. Top heavy → ∞._

### 4.3 Expressions Involving Radicals

Radical expressions require more care. The technique: **multiply by the conjugate** or **factor out the dominant term**.

_Example._ Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(\sqrt{x^2 + x} - x\right)$.

Naively: $\infty - \infty$, indeterminate. Multiply by conjugate:

$$\sqrt{x^2+x} - x = \frac{(\sqrt{x^2+x} - x)(\sqrt{x^2+x}+x)}{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x} = \frac{x^2 + x - x^2}{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x} = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+x}+x}$$

Now divide numerator and denominator by $x$ (valid for $x > 0$):

$$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{1 + 1/x} + 1} \xrightarrow{x\to\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{1} + 1} = \frac{1}{2}$$

> _Warning: For $x \to -\infty$, $\sqrt{x^2} = |x| = -x$. Sign errors here are extremely common._

_Example._ $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to -\infty} \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+1}}$.

Factor: $\sqrt{x^2+1} = \sqrt{x^2}\sqrt{1+1/x^2} = |x|\sqrt{1+1/x^2} = -x\sqrt{1+1/x^2}$ (since $x < 0$).

$$\frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+1}} = \frac{x}{-x\sqrt{1+1/x^2}} = \frac{-1}{\sqrt{1+1/x^2}} \to -1$$

### 4.4 Exponentials and Logarithms

Hierarchy of growth rates (fastest to slowest):

$$e^x \gg x^n \gg \ln x \quad \text{as } x \to \infty$$

More precisely:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^n}{e^x} = 0 \quad \text{(exponential beats any polynomial)}$$ $$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x^r} = 0 \quad \text{for any } r > 0 \text{ (power beats log)}$$

These are proved via L'Hôpital's rule.

_Example._ $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} x^{100} e^{-x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^{100}}{e^x} = 0$.

No matter how large the polynomial degree, exponential decay wins.

## §5. L'Hôpital's Rule at Infinity

> **Theorem (L'Hôpital).** If $\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = \lim_{x \to \infty} g(x) = 0$ or $\pm\infty$, and $g'(x) \neq 0$ for large $x$, then: $$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{f'(x)}{g'(x)}$$ provided the right-hand limit exists (or is $\pm\infty$).

The rule applies to the indeterminate forms $0/0$ and $\infty/\infty$. Other indeterminate forms must be converted:

|Form|Conversion|
|---|---|
|$\infty - \infty$|Algebraic manipulation (common denominator, conjugate)|
|$0 \cdot \infty$|Write as $f/(1/g)$ to get $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty$|
|$1^\infty$, $0^0$, $\infty^0$|Take logarithm: $\lim e^{g \ln f}$|

_Example._ $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} x e^{-x}$ — form $\infty \cdot 0$.

Rewrite: $\dfrac{x}{e^x}$ — form $\infty/\infty$. Apply L'Hôpital:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x}{e^x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{e^x} = 0$$

_Example._ $\displaystyle\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right)^x$ — form $1^\infty$.

Let $L = $ the limit. Then:

$$\ln L = \lim_{x \to \infty} x \ln!\left(1 + \frac{1}{x}\right) = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln(1+1/x)}{1/x}$$

Form $0/0$. L'Hôpital:

$$= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\frac{-1/x^2}{1+1/x}}{-1/x^2} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{1 + 1/x} = 1$$

So $\ln L = 1$, hence $L = e$. The definition of $e$, recovered.

## §6. Limits at Infinity for Trigonometric Functions

$\sin x$ and $\cos x$ oscillate between $-1$ and $1$ forever. They have **no limit as $x \to \infty$**.

But bounded oscillation combined with decay is a different story:

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 0 \quad \text{(Squeeze Theorem: } -\frac{1}{x} \leq \frac{\sin x}{x} \leq \frac{1}{x}\text{)}$$

> **The Squeeze Theorem at infinity.** If $g(x) \leq f(x) \leq h(x)$ for all large $x$, and $\lim_{x\to\infty} g(x) = \lim_{x\to\infty} h(x) = L$, then $\lim_{x\to\infty} f(x) = L$.

This shows up constantly: any bounded function multiplied by something decaying to 0 has limit 0.

## §7. The Limit $\lim_{x \to \infty}\left(1 + \frac{r}{x}\right)^x = e^r$

This generalization of the definition of $e$ deserves its own entry. It is the continuous-compounding formula from finance, and it appears in the theory of differential equations, probability, and information theory.

**Proof sketch.** Let $y = x/r$. Then as $x \to \infty$, $y \to \infty$, and:

$$\left(1+\frac{r}{x}\right)^x = \left[\left(1+\frac{1}{y}\right)^y\right]^r \to e^r$$

**Application:** Continuous compounding. If principal $P$ is compounded $n$ times per year at rate $r$:

$$A = P\left(1+\frac{r}{n}\right)^{n} \xrightarrow{n \to \infty} Pe^r$$

## §8. Asymptotic Notation and Rate Comparison

When limits at infinity are taken seriously as a comparative tool, we get asymptotic analysis.

> **Definition.** We write $f(x) = O(g(x))$ as $x \to \infty$ if $\lim_{x \to \infty} \left|\dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)}\right| < \infty$ (bounded ratio).

> We write $f(x) \sim g(x)$ if $\lim_{x \to \infty} \dfrac{f(x)}{g(x)} = 1$ (asymptotically equal).

_Examples:_

- $3x^2 + 5x \sim 3x^2$ as $x \to \infty$
- $\ln(x^2 + 1) \sim 2\ln x$ as $x \to \infty$
- $e^x + x^{100} \sim e^x$ as $x \to \infty$

This is the language of algorithm complexity ($O(n \log n)$, $O(n^2)$, etc.) and of asymptotic analysis in probability and statistics.

## §9. Oblique (Slant) Asymptotes

When $\deg(p) = \deg(q) + 1$, a rational function has neither a horizontal asymptote nor pure divergence. It approaches a **line** $y = mx + b$.

**Finding the oblique asymptote:** Perform polynomial long division.

$$f(x) = \frac{x^2 + 3x + 1}{x + 1}$$

Long division: $x^2 + 3x + 1 = (x+1)(x+2) - 1$, so:

$$f(x) = x + 2 - \frac{1}{x+1}$$

As $x \to \infty$, $\dfrac{1}{x+1} \to 0$, so $f(x) \to x + 2$. The oblique asymptote is $y = x + 2$.

```
        y
        |         /  ← y = x + 2 (asymptote)
        |       / ·
        |     /·
        |   /·
        | /·
 -------+------------ x
       /|
      / |
```

## §10. Limits at Infinity in Multiple Variables (18.02)

For $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, the statement $\lim_{|\mathbf{x}| \to \infty} f(\mathbf{x}) = L$ means $f$ approaches $L$ as the input point recedes to infinity in _any direction_.

This requires agreement along all rays and all paths — the same pathological subtleties as finite multivariable limits.

_Example._ Does $f(x,y) = \dfrac{xy}{x^2 + y^2}$ have a limit as $(x,y) \to \infty$?

Along $y = x$: $f = \dfrac{x^2}{2x^2} = \dfrac{1}{2}$.

Along $y = 0$: $f = 0$.

Different limits along different paths — **no limit at infinity**.

_Example._ $f(x,y) = \dfrac{1}{x^2 + y^2}$. As $(x,y) \to \infty$ along any path, $x^2 + y^2 \to \infty$, so $f \to 0$. Limit exists and equals $0$.

## §11. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 11.1 Convergence of Training: The Limit That Matters Most

Training a neural network is iteratively minimizing $\mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$. The question of convergence is precisely:

$$\lim_{t \to \infty} \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) = ?$$

Does this limit exist? Does it equal the global minimum? For convex losses (logistic regression, linear regression), convergence to the global minimum is guaranteed under mild conditions. For non-convex losses (deep networks), the limit may be a local minimum or saddle point — and proving convergence at all requires careful analysis.

**Learning rate schedules** (cosine annealing, exponential decay) are designed to ensure this limit exists and is "good": $$\eta_t = \eta_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda t} \to 0 \quad \text{(ensures the steps shrink to zero)}$$

The intuition is: if step sizes don't go to zero, the optimizer keeps bouncing around even after reaching a minimum.

### 11.2 Activation Functions and Their Asymptotic Behavior

The **asymptotic behavior** of activations as inputs go to $\pm\infty$ directly determines gradient flow and the vanishing gradient problem.

|Activation|$\lim_{x \to +\infty}$|$\lim_{x \to -\infty}$|Consequence|
|---|---|---|---|
|Sigmoid $\sigma(x)$|$1$|$0$|Saturates; $\sigma'(x) \to 0$ — **vanishing gradient**|
|Tanh|$1$|$-1$|Saturates; same issue|
|ReLU|$\infty$|$0$|No saturation for $x > 0$; dead neurons for $x < 0$|
|Leaky ReLU|$\infty$|$-\infty$ (slope $\alpha$)|No dead neurons|
|Softplus $\ln(1+e^x)$|$\infty$|$0$|Smooth ReLU; same asymptotic behavior|
|ELU|$\infty$|$-1$|Bounded below; smooth|

The **vanishing gradient problem** is a limit statement: $\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x)) \to 0$ as $|x| \to \infty$. When activations saturate, gradients die, and early layers of deep networks stop learning.

ReLU was a breakthrough because $\lim_{x \to \infty} \text{ReLU}'(x) = 1 \neq 0$: no saturation in the positive half.

### 11.3 Softmax and the Winner-Take-All Limit

The softmax function:

$$\text{softmax}(\mathbf{z})_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}$$

Consider scaling the logits: $\text{softmax}(z/T)$ where $T > 0$ is **temperature**.

$$\lim_{T \to 0^+} \text{softmax}(\mathbf{z}/T) = \mathbf{e}_{\arg\max_i z_i}$$

In the limit of zero temperature, softmax becomes the **argmax** — a one-hot vector. This is used in **knowledge distillation** (large $T$ softens the distribution, transferring more information) and in **reinforcement learning** (temperature controls exploration vs. exploitation).

This is a genuine limit at infinity ($1/T \to \infty$) giving a discontinuous function as its limit — the argmax.

### 11.4 Batch Size and the $n \to \infty$ Limit

Stochastic gradient descent with batch size $B$ uses a noisy estimate of the gradient:

$$\hat{\nabla}\mathcal{L} = \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i=1}^{B} \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta; x_i)$$

By the Law of Large Numbers:

$$\lim_{B \to \infty} \hat{\nabla}\mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}[\nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta; x)] = \nabla\mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

Full-batch gradient descent is the limit $B \to \infty$. Finite batch sizes introduce beneficial noise that helps escape sharp minima — a deliberate departure from the limit.

### 11.5 Neural Tangent Kernel — The Infinite Width Limit

For an $L$-layer network with width $n$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) \approx \text{(linear model in function space)}$$

In the infinite-width limit, training a neural network becomes equivalent to kernel regression with the **Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)**. This is one of the deepest results in modern deep learning theory (Jacot et al., 2018) — and it is a statement about a limit at infinity in the width parameter.

### 11.6 Asymptotic Complexity in Algorithm Design

The $O(\cdot)$ notation used in algorithm analysis is directly the asymptotic notation from §8:

$$T(n) = O(n^2) \iff \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{T(n)}{n^2} < \infty$$

Choosing between $O(n \log n)$ and $O(n^2)$ sorting is a statement about what happens as $n \to \infty$ — a limit at infinity comparing growth rates.

## §12. Summary of Key Limits

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x^r} = 0 \quad (r > 0)$$

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \begin{cases} 0 & \deg p < \deg q \ \frac{a_n}{b_m} & \deg p = \deg q \ \pm\infty & \deg p > \deg q \end{cases}$$

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^n}{e^x} = 0, \qquad \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{x^r} = 0$$

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(1+\frac{r}{x}\right)^x = e^r$$

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 0 \quad \text{(but } \lim_{x\to\infty}\sin x \text{ does not exist)}$$

## §13. Things Worth Losing Sleep Over

- $\lim_{x \to \infty} \sin(x)/x = 0$ but $\lim_{x \to \infty} \sin(x)$ does not exist. The limit of a product is **not** always the product of the limits when one factor diverges.
- A function can oscillate between two values and still have a limit at infinity — if the oscillation amplitude decays. But if it doesn't decay, the limit fails to exist even if the function stays bounded.
- Horizontal asymptote is a statement about **limit**. A function can **cross** its horizontal asymptote finitely or even infinitely many times and still have that asymptote. (Example: $\sin(x)/x$ crosses $y = 0$ infinitely often.)
- $L$'Hôpital's rule requires the hypotheses $0/0$ or $\infty/\infty$. Applying it to $\dfrac{x + \sin x}{x}$ (which is NOT of that form) gives the wrong answer. Always check the form first.
- Two functions with the same limit at infinity can differ arbitrarily on any bounded interval. The limit at infinity tells you nothing about local behavior.

## §14. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Rational Function

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{5x^3 - 2x + 7}{3x^3 + x^2 - 1}$$

Divide by $x^3$:

$$= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{5 - 2/x^2 + 7/x^3}{3 + 1/x - 1/x^3} = \frac{5 - 0 + 0}{3 + 0 - 0} = \frac{5}{3}$$

---

### Example 2 — Indeterminate $\infty - \infty$ with Radical

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(\sqrt{4x^2 + 3x} - 2x\right)$$

Multiply by conjugate:

$$= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{4x^2 + 3x - 4x^2}{\sqrt{4x^2+3x}+2x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x}{\sqrt{4x^2+3x}+2x}$$

Divide by $x$:

$$= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3}{\sqrt{4 + 3/x}+2} = \frac{3}{\sqrt{4}+2} = \frac{3}{4}$$

---

### Example 3 — $1^\infty$ Form

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \left(\frac{x+3}{x-1}\right)^x$$

Write $\dfrac{x+3}{x-1} = 1 + \dfrac{4}{x-1}$. Let $u = x - 1 \to \infty$:

$$= \lim_{u \to \infty} \left(1 + \frac{4}{u}\right)^{u+1} = \lim_{u \to \infty} \left(1+\frac{4}{u}\right)^u \cdot \underbrace{\left(1+\frac{4}{u}\right)}_{\to 1} = e^4 \cdot 1 = e^4$$

---

### Example 4 — L'Hôpital

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{\ln x}{\sqrt{x}}$$

Form $\infty/\infty$. L'Hôpital:

$$= \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1/x}{1/(2\sqrt{x})} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2\sqrt{x}}{x} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{2}{\sqrt{x}} = 0$$
