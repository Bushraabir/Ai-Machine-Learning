## §0. Why This Matters Before We Start

Before we can differentiate, integrate, or train a neural network with gradient descent, we need a rigorous foundation for what it means for a function to _approach_ a value. The limit laws are the axiomatic scaffolding that make all of that possible — not just heuristics, but provable theorems derived from the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition.

---

## §1. The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ Foundation (The Real Definition)

> **Definition (Stewart §2.4, MIT 18.02 Review Notes).** We say $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $$0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$

**What this is really saying:** No matter how tight a tolerance band $(-\varepsilon, +\varepsilon)$ you draw around $L$, I can find a punctured neighborhood around $a$ whose entire image fits inside that band. The function doesn't need to be _defined_ at $a$ — we explicitly exclude $x = a$ with the condition $0 < |x-a|$.

**Pedagogical note to self:** Students always confuse "limit exists" with "function is defined at $a$". These are _completely independent_ conditions. The classic example:

$$f(x) = \frac{\sin x}{x}, \quad a = 0$$

$f(0)$ is undefined, yet $\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sin x}{x} = 1$. This will matter enormously when we study derivatives (which are themselves limits of difference quotients at points where the quotient is $0/0$).

---

## §2. The Limit Laws (Theorems, Not Postulates)

Let $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ and $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M$, where $L, M \in \mathbb{R}$. Then:

|Law|Statement|Informal Name|
|---|---|---|
|**L1**|$\lim_{x \to a}[f(x) + g(x)] = L + M$|Sum Law|
|**L2**|$\lim_{x \to a}[f(x) - g(x)] = L - M$|Difference Law|
|**L3**|$\lim_{x \to a}[c \cdot f(x)] = cL$|Constant Multiple Law|
|**L4**|$\lim_{x \to a}[f(x) \cdot g(x)] = L \cdot M$|Product Law|
|**L5**|$\lim_{x \to a}\frac{f(x)}{g(x)} = \frac{L}{M}$, provided $M \neq 0$|Quotient Law|
|**L6**|$\lim_{x \to a}[f(x)]^n = L^n$, $n \in \mathbb{Z}^+$|Power Law|
|**L7**|$\lim_{x \to a}\sqrt[n]{f(x)} = \sqrt[n]{L}$ (with domain conditions)|Root Law|

### Two Trivial But Essential Base Cases

$$\lim_{x \to a} c = c \qquad \text{(limit of a constant)}$$ $$\lim_{x \to a} x = a \qquad \text{(limit of the identity)}$$

These two facts, combined with L1–L7, are _sufficient to evaluate the limit of any polynomial_ — which is the content of the Direct Substitution Property.

## §3. Proof of the Sum Law (Full $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ Proof)

_This is the one proof every analyst should be able to reproduce from memory._

**Theorem (Sum Law).** If $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ and $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M$, then $\lim_{x \to a}[f(x)+g(x)] = L + M$.

**Proof.**

Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be arbitrary. We need $|f(x) + g(x) - (L+M)| < \varepsilon$.

By the triangle inequality: $$|f(x) + g(x) - L - M| \leq |f(x) - L| + |g(x) - M|$$

**Strategy:** make each piece $< \varepsilon/2$.

Since $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$, for $\varepsilon/2 > 0$, $\exists, \delta_1 > 0$ such that: $$0 < |x-a| < \delta_1 \implies |f(x) - L| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}$$

Since $\lim_{x \to a} g(x) = M$, for $\varepsilon/2 > 0$, $\exists, \delta_2 > 0$ such that: $$0 < |x-a| < \delta_2 \implies |g(x) - M| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2}$$

Set $\delta = \min(\delta_1, \delta_2)$. Then for $0 < |x-a| < \delta$: $$|f(x)+g(x) - (L+M)| \leq |f(x)-L| + |g(x)-M| < \frac{\varepsilon}{2} + \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon. \qquad \blacksquare$$

> **Note:** The $\min$ trick — taking the smaller of two deltas so _both_ conditions hold simultaneously — appears constantly in analysis. It's the workhorse move.

## §4. The Squeeze Theorem (Sandwich Theorem)

This deserves its own section because it's not derived from L1–L7; it's an independent tool for limits that can't be evaluated directly.

**Theorem (Squeeze).** Suppose $h(x) \leq f(x) \leq g(x)$ near $a$ (except possibly at $a$ itself), and $$\lim_{x \to a} h(x) = \lim_{x \to a} g(x) = L.$$ Then $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$.

**Canonical example:**

$$\lim_{x \to 0} x^2 \sin!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)$$

Since $-1 \leq \sin(1/x) \leq 1$ for all $x \neq 0$: $$-x^2 \leq x^2 \sin!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) \leq x^2$$

Both $-x^2 \to 0$ and $x^2 \to 0$ as $x \to 0$. Therefore by Squeeze: limit $= 0$.

```
    y
    |      .·' x²
    |   .·'
    | ·  ← f(x) trapped here
    |·_____________________ x
    |·
    | ·
    |   '·.
    |      '·. -x²

The function f(x) = x²sin(1/x) oscillates but is
squeezed to 0 as x → 0.
```

## §5. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Polynomial (Direct Substitution)

$$\lim_{x \to 3}(2x^2 - 5x + 1)$$

By L1, L2, L3, L6, and the base cases: $$= 2\lim_{x\to 3} x^2 - 5\lim_{x\to 3} x + \lim_{x\to 3} 1 = 2(9) - 5(3) + 1 = 18 - 15 + 1 = \boxed{4}$$

### Example 2 — Indeterminate Form $0/0$ (Factor and Cancel)

$$\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2}$$

Direct substitution gives $0/0$ — the Quotient Law **does not apply** (since $M = 0$). Instead:

$$\frac{x^2 - 4}{x-2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2, \quad x \neq 2$$

Now the Quotient Law's obstruction is gone (we're looking at a _different_ function that agrees with ours near $x=2$ but not _at_ $x=2$, which is all a limit cares about):

$$\lim_{x \to 2}(x+2) = 4$$

> **Key Insight:** Cancellation is valid here because in the limit, $x \neq 2$. Limits are inherently _punctured_ neighborhoods.

### Example 3 — Rationalization Trick

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{x+4} - 2}{x}$$

Multiply top and bottom by the conjugate $\sqrt{x+4}+2$:

$$\frac{\sqrt{x+4}-2}{x} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{x+4}+2}{\sqrt{x+4}+2} = \frac{(x+4)-4}{x(\sqrt{x+4}+2)} = \frac{x}{x(\sqrt{x+4}+2)} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{x+4}+2}$$

$$\lim_{x \to 0} \frac{1}{\sqrt{x+4}+2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4}+2} = \frac{1}{4}$$

This exact computation is the derivative of $\sqrt{x}$ at $x=4$ from first principles.

## §7. One-Sided Limits

**Definition.** The _right-hand limit_: $$\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L \iff \forall \varepsilon>0,; \exists \delta>0: 0 < x-a < \delta \implies |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon$$

**Theorem.** $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ if and only if $\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L = \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$.

This is the two-sided characterization. It's the tool for checking limits at points of potential discontinuity, like piecewise functions.

**Example:** $$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & x < 1 \ 2x - 1 & x \geq 1 \end{cases}$$

$$\lim_{x \to 1^-} f(x) = 1^2 = 1, \qquad \lim_{x \to 1^+} f(x) = 2(1)-1 = 1$$

Both sides agree $\Rightarrow \lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 1$. (And $f(1) = 1$, so $f$ is actually continuous here.)

---

## §8. Limits at Infinity

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L \iff \forall \varepsilon>0,; \exists M>0: x > M \implies |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon$$

**Key results used everywhere:**

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{1}{x^p} = 0 \quad \text{for } p > 0$$

$$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{a_n x^n + \cdots + a_0}{b_m x^m + \cdots + b_0} = \begin{cases} \frac{a_n}{b_m} & n = m \ 0 & n < m \ \pm\infty & n > m \end{cases}$$

**Technique:** divide numerator and denominator by $x^{\max(n,m)}$.

**Example:** $$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3x^2 - x}{2x^2 + 5} = \lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{3 - 1/x}{2 + 5/x^2} = \frac{3-0}{2+0} = \frac{3}{2}$$

---

## §9. Continuity — The Payoff of All This

**Definition.** $f$ is _continuous at $a$_ if:

1. $f(a)$ is defined,
2. $\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists,
3. $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$.

All three conditions are _independent_. The limit laws immediately give us:

**Corollary.** Every polynomial is continuous everywhere. Every rational function is continuous on its domain.

**The Direct Substitution Property** is precisely the statement that for continuous $f$: $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$ — you can just plug in.

## §10. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

> This section earns its place because every claim below traces back directly to the limit laws.

### 10.1 The Derivative = A Limit

The entire edifice of backpropagation rests on one limit:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

This is an indeterminate form $0/0$. Without limit laws (specifically the algebraic manipulation of $0/0$ forms), we cannot differentiate _anything_. Every weight update in gradient descent is:

$$w \leftarrow w - \eta \cdot \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w}$$

where $\partial \mathcal{L}/\partial w$ is a partial derivative — a limit.

### 10.2 Activation Functions and Limits at Infinity

The sigmoid activation is: $$\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$$

Its behavior at the saturation regime: $$\lim_{x \to +\infty} \sigma(x) = 1, \qquad \lim_{x \to -\infty} \sigma(x) = 0$$

These horizontal asymptotes (limits at infinity) explain the **vanishing gradient problem**: in the tails, $\sigma'(x) \to 0$, so gradients vanish in deep networks. ReLU was introduced precisely to avoid this — $\text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0,x)$ has no saturation for $x > 0$.

The limit $\lim_{x \to 0^-} \text{ReLU}'(x) = 0 \neq 1 = \lim_{x \to 0^+} \text{ReLU}'(x)$ shows ReLU is _not differentiable_ at 0 — a one-sided limit problem. In practice, subgradients handle this.

### 10.3 Loss Landscape and Continuity

For gradient descent to work at all, the loss function $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})$ must be continuous (so small changes in weights don't cause catastrophic jumps in loss). The limit laws guarantee continuity of compositions of continuous functions — since neural networks are compositions of affine maps and continuous activations, the overall network function is continuous. This is not obvious without limit theory.

### 10.4 Batch Normalization — Limits and Stability

Batch norm computes: $$\hat{x}_i = \frac{x_i - \mu_B}{\sqrt{\sigma_B^2 + \varepsilon}}$$

The $\varepsilon > 0$ in the denominator is there to prevent division by zero (i.e., to avoid the case $M = 0$ where the Quotient Law breaks down). As $\varepsilon \to 0^+$, the expression approaches the true normalization — this is a limit in its own right, and the chosen $\varepsilon$ is a practical $\delta$.

### 10.5 Universal Approximation and Limits

The Universal Approximation Theorem (Cybenko, 1989) states that a single hidden layer network with enough neurons can approximate any continuous function $f: [0,1]^n \to \mathbb{R}$ to arbitrary precision $\varepsilon > 0$. The phrase "arbitrary precision $\varepsilon$" _is_ the limit definition in disguise — the theorem is fundamentally an $\varepsilon$-approximation result whose roots lie exactly in the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ language.

### 10.6 Convergence of Training — Sequences and Limits

When we say "the model converged," we mean the sequence of loss values ${\mathcal{L}_t}_{t=1}^{\infty}$ satisfies: $$\lim_{t \to \infty} \mathcal{L}_t = \mathcal{L}^*$$

This is a sequential limit — the analogous notion to $\lim_{x \to a}$ but for discrete sequences. The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition becomes the $\varepsilon$-$N$ definition: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $N$ such that $t > N \implies |\mathcal{L}_t - \mathcal{L}^*| < \varepsilon$. **Same idea, different setting.**

## §11. Common Errors and Traps

|Error|Why It's Wrong|Correct Move|
|---|---|---|
|Applying Quotient Law when $M=0$|Law requires $M \neq 0$ — denominator limit must be nonzero|Factor, rationalize, or use L'Hôpital|
|Concluding limit DNE from one approach|Must check all paths (not just $x \to a^{\pm}$)|For functions of two variables, this gets much worse|
|Writing $\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = f(a)$ always|Only valid if $f$ is continuous at $a$|Verify continuity explicitly|
|Canceling $(x-a)$ before the limit|Valid _because_ $x \neq a$ in a limit; often forgotten in explanation|Always note "since $x \neq a$ in the limit..."|
