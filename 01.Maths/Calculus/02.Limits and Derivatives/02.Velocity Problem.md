
> _"The velocity problem and the tangent problem appear to be different. But they turn out to be the same problem in disguise — and that disguise is the derivative."_

---

## §1. The Physical Motivation

Suppose a ball is dropped from a tower. Physics tells us its height (in metres) at time $t$ (in seconds) is approximately:

$$s(t) = 4.9t^2$$

**Question:** How fast is the ball falling at exactly $t = 2$ seconds?

The phrase _"at exactly $t = 2$"_ is the crux. Speed is ordinarily computed as:

$$\text{speed} = \frac{\text{distance}}{\text{time}}$$

But this ratio requires _two_ moments in time — a start and an end. At a single instant, both numerator and denominator collapse to zero. We have a $\frac{0}{0}$ problem.

This is not a computational inconvenience. It is a genuine conceptual difficulty that Newton and Leibniz spent their lives resolving. The resolution is the **limit**.

---

## §2. Average Velocity vs. Instantaneous Velocity

### Definition: Average Velocity

Over the time interval $[t_1, t_2]$, the **average velocity** is:

$$\bar{v} = \frac{s(t_2) - s(t_1)}{t_2 - t_1} = \frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}$$

This is just slope: the slope of the **secant line** on the position-time graph between $(t_1, s(t_1))$ and $(t_2, s(t_2))$.

### Definition: Instantaneous Velocity

Fix a moment $t = a$. Let $h = \Delta t \to 0$. The **instantaneous velocity** is:

$$v(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{s(a+h) - s(a)}{h}$$

This is the slope of the **tangent line** on the position-time graph at $(a, s(a))$.

**The velocity problem is the tangent problem, with time on the horizontal axis.**

## §3. Worked Example — The Falling Ball

Let $s(t) = 4.9t^2$ metres. Find the instantaneous velocity at $t = 2$.

**Step 1: Compute average velocities over shrinking intervals.**

|Interval $[2,, 2+h]$|$s(2+h)$|$\Delta s$|$\bar{v} = \Delta s / h$|
|---|---|---|---|
|$h = 1$ (i.e., $[2,3]$)|$4.9(9) = 44.1$|$44.1 - 19.6 = 24.5$|$24.5$ m/s|
|$h = 0.5$|$4.9(6.25) = 30.625$|$11.025$|$22.05$ m/s|
|$h = 0.1$|$4.9(4.41) = 21.609$|$2.009$|$20.09$ m/s|
|$h = 0.01$|$4.9(4.0401) = 19.7965$|$0.1965$|$19.65$ m/s|
|$h = 0.001$|—|—|$19.601$ m/s|
|$h \to 0$|—|—|$\mathbf{19.6}$ **m/s**|

**Step 2: Confirm with the limit.**

$$v(2) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{4.9(2+h)^2 - 4.9(2)^2}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{4.9(4 + 4h + h^2) - 4.9(4)}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{4.9(4h + h^2)}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} 4.9(4 + h) = 4.9 \times 4 = \mathbf{19.6 \text{ m/s}}$$

The ball is falling at exactly $19.6$ m/s at $t = 2$ s. Notice $19.6 = 2 \times 9.8$, which is $g \cdot t$ — consistent with Newtonian mechanics ($v = gt$ for free fall from rest).

## §5. The Full Kinematic Chain

Let $s(t)$ be position. Then:

$$v(t) = s'(t) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{s(t+h)-s(t)}{h} \quad \text{(velocity)}$$

$$a(t) = v'(t) = s''(t) \quad \text{(acceleration)}$$

For our falling ball $s(t) = 4.9t^2 = \frac{1}{2}gt^2$:

|Quantity|Function|Value at $t=2$|
|---|---|---|
|Position|$s(t) = 4.9t^2$|$19.6$ m|
|Velocity|$v(t) = 9.8t$|$19.6$ m/s|
|Acceleration|$a(t) = 9.8$|$9.8$ m/s²|

The acceleration is **constant** — this is the defining property of free fall near Earth's surface. Newton's second law $F = ma$ with $F = mg$ gives $a = g$.

## §6. Example 2 — Oscillatory Motion

Let $s(t) = \sin t$ (a particle on a spring, say). Find the velocity at $t = 0$.

$$v(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin(0+h) - \sin 0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin h}{h}$$

This is the **fundamental trigonometric limit**:

$$\lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sin h}{h} = 1$$

So $v(0) = 1$ m/s (in appropriate units). And indeed $\frac{d}{dt}\sin t = \cos t$, which gives $\cos 0 = 1$. ✓

> _This limit is not obvious. It requires the geometric squeeze theorem — the area of a circular sector squeezed between two triangles. See Stewart §3.3._

## §7. Example 3 — Negative Velocity (Motion in Reverse)

Let $s(t) = -t^2 + 4t$ (a projectile thrown upward).

$$v(t) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{[-(t+h)^2 + 4(t+h)] - [-t^2 + 4t]}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{-t^2 - 2th - h^2 + 4t + 4h + t^2 - 4t}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{-2th - h^2 + 4h}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0}(-2t - h + 4) = -2t + 4$$

**Analysis:**

- $v(t) = 0 \Rightarrow t = 2$: the ball is momentarily at rest (peak of trajectory).
- $v(t) > 0$ for $t < 2$: ball moving upward (positive direction).
- $v(t) < 0$ for $t > 2$: ball moving downward (negative velocity).

```
s(t) = -t² + 4t

  s
  4|    *   ← peak at t=2, s=4; velocity = 0 here
  |   / \
  |  /   \
  | /     \
  |/       \
  *_________*_________ t
  0         4

v(t) = -2t + 4: positive before t=2, zero at t=2, negative after.
```

**The sign of the derivative encodes the direction of motion.** Zero derivative = stationary point.

## §8. Formal Definitions and Theorems

**Definition.** Let $s : [0, T] \to \mathbb{R}$ be a position function. The **instantaneous velocity** at time $a \in (0,T)$ is:

$$v(a) = s'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{s(a+h)-s(a)}{h}$$

if the limit exists.

**Definition.** The **speed** at time $a$ is $|v(a)|$ — velocity is a signed scalar (or in higher dimensions, a vector); speed is its magnitude.

**Theorem (Mean Value Theorem for velocity).** If $s$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ and differentiable on $(a,b)$, then there exists $c \in (a,b)$ such that:

$$v(c) = s'(c) = \frac{s(b) - s(a)}{b-a}$$

_In words: at some moment, the instantaneous velocity equals the average velocity over the interval._ This is the MVT — one of the deepest theorems in calculus.

> _Practical consequence: if your average speed on a highway is 120 km/h, then at some moment your speedometer read exactly 120 km/h. The MVT is also why radar speed traps work._

## §9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _The velocity problem is not just a physics curiosity. It is the conceptual prototype for how neural networks learn — and how time-series models reason about change._

### 9.1 Learning as Motion Through Parameter Space

Think of training a neural network as a particle moving through parameter space $\mathbb{R}^n$. The weights $\mathbf{w}(t)$ evolve over "time" (iterations $t$), following:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{w}}{dt} = -\nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})$$

This is **gradient flow** — the continuous-time limit of gradient descent. The velocity of the parameter particle at time $t$ is $-\nabla \mathcal{L}$. Just as $v(t) = s'(t)$ tells us how position changes, the gradient tells us how loss changes as we move through weight space.

The discrete update rule $\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{w}^{(t)} - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}$ is the Euler method for this ODE — the most primitive numerical integration of the velocity problem.

### 9.2 Momentum Optimizers

In physics, a particle with momentum $\mathbf{p} = m\mathbf{v}$ resists sudden changes in direction. The **SGD with momentum** optimizer is exactly this:

$$\mathbf{p}^{(t+1)} = \beta \mathbf{p}^{(t)} - \eta \nabla \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}^{(t)})$$ $$\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{w}^{(t)} + \mathbf{p}^{(t+1)}$$

The parameter $\beta \in (0,1)$ is the **momentum coefficient** — a "friction" term that decays past velocities. The **Adam optimizer** goes further: it tracks both the first moment (velocity) and second moment (variance of velocity) of the gradient, adaptively scaling each parameter's learning rate.

|Physics|Optimizer|
|---|---|
|Position $s(t)$|Weights $\mathbf{w}^{(t)}$|
|Velocity $v(t) = s'(t)$|Gradient $\nabla \mathcal{L}$|
|Momentum $mv$|First moment estimate (Adam's $\hat{m}$)|
|Friction|Momentum decay $\beta$|
|Equilibrium $v = 0$|Convergence $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 0$|

### 9.3 Recurrent Neural Networks and Temporal Derivatives

An RNN processes sequences $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_T$ and maintains a hidden state $h_t$ that evolves over time. The continuous-time limit is the **Neural ODE**:

$$\frac{dh}{dt} = f_\theta(h(t), x(t))$$

Here $\frac{dh}{dt}$ is literally the instantaneous velocity of the hidden state — exactly the velocity problem. Training Neural ODEs requires differentiating through an ODE solver (using the adjoint method, which is itself a calculus result).

### 9.4 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

PINNs solve differential equations (which are _velocity equations_ — equations involving $s'(t)$, $s''(t)$, etc.) by training neural networks whose loss includes a penalty for violating the ODE:

$$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\sum_i (u_\theta(t_i) - u_i)^2}_{\text{data term}} + \underbrace{\sum_j \left(\frac{du_\theta}{dt}\bigg|_{t_j} - f(u_\theta(t_j))\right)^2}_{\text{physics term (velocity residual)}}$$

The physics term is zero only when the network's instantaneous rate of change matches the prescribed ODE. This is a direct application of the velocity problem inside a neural network loss function.

### 9.5 Finite Differences in Time-Series

The difference quotient approximation:

$$v(t) \approx \frac{s(t+h) - s(t)}{h}$$

appears directly as a **feature engineering** step in time-series ML. First differences $\Delta x_t = x_t - x_{t-1}$ are used to **stationarize** time series before feeding them to models — removing trends by working with velocities (rates of change) rather than raw levels.

```
Raw series (non-stationary):         First differences (stationary):
   x                                    Δx
   |    /                               |  *  *     *
   |   /                                | * *   * *
   |  /                                 |*         *
   | /                                  |
   |/_____ t                            |___________ t
   trend present                        trend removed
```

ARIMA models explicitly include an "integrated" component $d$ — the number of times the series must be differenced to become stationary — because $d$-fold differencing approximates the $d$-th order derivative.

## §10. The Conceptual Unity

The table below shows that the velocity problem, the tangent problem, and the learning problem are all the same thing:

|Context|"Position"|"Time/Input"|Difference Quotient|Limit|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Kinematics|$s(t)$|$t$|Average velocity $\frac{\Delta s}{\Delta t}$|Instantaneous velocity $v(a)$|
|Geometry|$f(x)$|$x$|Secant slope $\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}$|Tangent slope $f'(a)$|
|ML training|$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})$|$\mathbf{w}$|Finite diff. gradient approx.|True gradient $\nabla \mathcal{L}$|
|Time series|$x_t$|$t$|First difference $\Delta x_t$|Instantaneous rate of change|
|Neural ODE|$h(t)$|$t$|Discretized state update|$\frac{dh}{dt} = f_\theta(h,x)$|

**They are all the same limit. The derivative is the universal language of change.**

---

## §11. What the Velocity Problem Teaches Us

1. **Instantaneous quantities are limits of average quantities.** There is no shortcut. The real number $v(a)$ exists only because the difference quotient converges.
    
2. **The sign of $v$ encodes direction.** Zero velocity = critical point (maximum, minimum, or inflection in time).
    
3. **Acceleration is the second derivative.** One differentiation gives velocity; a second gives acceleration. Higher derivatives encode higher-order structure of motion — and of functions.
    
4. **Differential equations are velocity equations.** Newton's law $F = ma$ is $s''(t) = F/m$ — an equation constraining the second derivative of position. Virtually all laws of physics are written this way.
    
5. **In ML, the gradient is velocity through loss landscape.** Training dynamics, optimizer design, and convergence theory are all kinematics in disguise.
