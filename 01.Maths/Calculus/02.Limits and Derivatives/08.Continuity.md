## §1. Motivation — Why Continuity?

Calculus is, at its heart, the study of _change_. But for the machinery of derivatives and integrals to work, we need a prior guarantee: that functions don't teleport. A function that jumps arbitrarily makes differentiation meaningless and integration treacherous.

Continuity is that guarantee. It is the precise mathematical way of saying: _small changes in input produce small changes in output._ Before Stewart ever writes $f'(a)$, continuity is doing silent structural work.

---

## §2. The Formal Definition

> **Definition (Continuity at a point).** A function $f$ is **continuous at** $a$ if: $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$$

This deceptively simple equation packs _three_ simultaneous requirements:

|Requirement|What It Demands|
|---|---|
|$f(a)$ is defined|$a$ is in the domain|
|$\lim_{x \to a} f(x)$ exists|left and right limits agree|
|The two are equal|the function actually _arrives_ where the limit points|

Fail any one of these and continuity breaks.

### The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ Formulation

The limit definition hides the real muscle. Unpacking it:

> $f$ is continuous at $a$ if: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $$|x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(a)| < \varepsilon$$

**Reading this carefully:** You hand me any tolerance $\varepsilon$ — how close you want $f(x)$ to $f(a)$ — and I can always find a neighborhood of $a$ (radius $\delta$) inside which $f$ cooperates. This is a _uniform challenge-response game_ that $f$ must win for every $\varepsilon$.

## §3. A Taxonomy of Discontinuities

Understanding continuity is inseparable from cataloguing its failures.

### 3.1 Removable Discontinuity

$$f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1} = x + 1 \quad (x \neq 1)$$

The limit $\lim_{x \to 1} f(x) = 2$ exists, but $f(1)$ is undefined (or perhaps defined as something else). The graph has a **hole**. Surgery: redefine $f(1) = 2$ and continuity is restored.

```
 y
 3|        ●
 2|      ○       ← hole at (1, 2)
 1|    /
  +--+--+-- x
     1
```

### 3.2 Jump Discontinuity

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 1 & x < 0 \ 2 & x \geq 0 \end{cases}$$

Left and right limits exist but are _unequal_: $\lim_{x \to 0^-} f(x) = 1 \neq 2 = \lim_{x \to 0^+} f(x)$. The function **jumps**. No redefinition fixes this.

```
 y
 2|          ●────────
 1|────────○
  +--------+-------- x
           0
```

### 3.3 Infinite (Essential) Discontinuity

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{x} \quad \text{at } x = 0$$

The limit doesn't exist (it diverges). The function explodes.

### 3.4 Oscillatory Discontinuity

$$f(x) = \sin!\left(\frac{1}{x}\right) \quad \text{at } x = 0$$

The function oscillates infinitely fast near 0. No limit exists. The function neither jumps nor blows up — it simply refuses to settle.

## §4. Continuity on Intervals

> **Definition.** $f$ is **continuous on** $(a, b)$ if it is continuous at every point therein.
> 
> $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ if it is continuous on $(a, b)$, and:
> 
> - $\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = f(a)$ (right-continuous at $a$)
> - $\lim_{x \to b^-} f(x) = f(b)$ (left-continuous at $b$)

Polynomials, $\sin$, $\cos$, $e^x$, $\ln x$ (on $(0,\infty)$) — these are all continuous on their natural domains. They are the _smooth citizens_ of analysis.

---

## §5. The Algebra of Continuous Functions

> **Theorem.** If $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $a$, then so are:
> 
> - $f + g$, $f - g$, $f \cdot g$
> - $f/g$ (provided $g(a) \neq 0$)
> - $f \circ g$ (composition), provided $g$ is continuous at $a$ and $f$ is continuous at $g(a)$

The composition rule is particularly important. It says: **continuous functions compose to continuous functions.** This is the structural backbone that lets us treat $e^{\sin x}$ or $\ln(1 + x^2)$ as continuous without hesitation.

## §6. The Great Theorems

These are the _theorems that make calculus work_. Continuity is their hypothesis.

### 6.1 Intermediate Value Theorem (IVT)

> **Theorem (IVT).** If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and $N$ is any number between $f(a)$ and $f(b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ such that $f(c) = N$.

**Geometric reading:** A continuous curve connecting two points cannot avoid any height in between. It must cross every horizontal level.

**Canonical application:** Proving roots exist.

_Example._ Show $p(x) = x^3 - x - 2$ has a root in $(1, 2)$.

$$p(1) = 1 - 1 - 2 = -2 < 0$$ $$p(2) = 8 - 2 - 2 = 4 > 0$$

$p$ is continuous (polynomial), $0$ lies between $-2$ and $4$, so IVT guarantees a $c \in (1, 2)$ with $p(c) = 0$. $\blacksquare$

> _Note: IVT is an existence theorem. It tells you the root is there; it doesn't find it._

### 6.2 Extreme Value Theorem (EVT)

> **Theorem (EVT).** If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$, then $f$ attains both its maximum and minimum values on $[a, b]$.

This seems obvious but is shockingly delicate. Remove either hypothesis — lose the closed endpoint, or lose continuity — and the theorem fails.

_Failure with open interval:_ $f(x) = x$ on $(0,1)$ never attains its supremum 1.

_Failure with discontinuity:_ $f(x) = 1/x$ on $[0,1]$ has no maximum.

## §7. Continuity in Higher Dimensions (18.02 Perspective)

In multivariable calculus, the definition extends naturally:

> **Definition.** $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous at $\mathbf{a}$ if: $$\lim_{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{a}} f(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{a})$$

But the topology is richer. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, a limit requires $f(\mathbf{x}) \to L$ along _every possible path_ to $\mathbf{a}$. This makes discontinuities harder to detect and easier to miss.

**Classic trap:**

$$f(x,y) = \frac{xy}{x^2 + y^2}, \quad f(0,0) = 0$$

Along $y = 0$: limit is 0. Along $y = x$: limit is $\frac{x^2}{2x^2} = \frac{1}{2}$. The limits differ — $f$ is **discontinuous at the origin** despite being continuous along every coordinate axis.

> _Lesson: In $\mathbb{R}^n$, checking limits along axes is not sufficient. You must check all paths, or find a single contradicting path._

### Partial Continuity ≠ Continuity

A function can be continuous in each variable separately while failing to be jointly continuous. This is a genuine pathology of multivariable analysis and should disturb you the first time you see it. Good. It should.

## §8. Uniform Continuity

Standard continuity at a point is _local_ — the $\delta$ may depend on both $\varepsilon$ **and** $a$. Uniform continuity removes the dependence on $a$:

> **Definition.** $f$ is **uniformly continuous** on a set $S$ if: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that for _all_ $x, y \in S$: $$|x - y| < \delta \implies |f(x) - f(y)| < \varepsilon$$

**Why it matters:** $f(x) = x^2$ is continuous on $\mathbb{R}$ but _not_ uniformly continuous — as $x \to \infty$, the function steepens and any fixed $\delta$ becomes insufficient. But $f(x) = x^2$ on $[0, 1]$ _is_ uniformly continuous (it's continuous on a compact set — see below).

> **Theorem (Heine-Cantor).** If $f$ is continuous on a compact set $K$ (closed and bounded in $\mathbb{R}^n$), then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $K$.

## §9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This is where the abstract becomes engineering.

### 9.1 Loss Landscape Continuity

In supervised learning, you minimize a **loss function** $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ where $\theta \in \mathbb{R}^n$ are model parameters. Gradient descent requires:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

This only makes sense if $\mathcal{L}$ is continuous (and differentiable). If $\mathcal{L}$ had jump discontinuities, gradient information would be meaningless — the gradient wouldn't tell you which direction to move.

> _Deep networks use activation functions like ReLU ($\max(0, x)$), sigmoid, tanh — all chosen to be continuous (even if not everywhere differentiable). The continuity of the loss landscape is what makes optimization tractable._

### 9.2 The Universal Approximation Theorem

> **Theorem (Cybenko, 1989; Hornik, 1991).** A feedforward neural network with a single hidden layer containing sufficiently many neurons can approximate any continuous function $f: [a,b]^n \to \mathbb{R}$ to arbitrary precision.

The hypothesis is **continuity of the target function**. Neural networks are themselves continuous (compositions of continuous functions). They approximate within the class of continuous functions. If your target function is discontinuous, the theorem gives no guarantee.

This is IVT and EVT whispering inside your neural architecture.

### 9.3 Lipschitz Continuity in Deep Learning

A function $f$ is **Lipschitz continuous** with constant $L$ if:

$$|f(x) - f(y)| \leq L,|x - y| \quad \forall, x, y$$

This is uniform continuity with a _linear_ modulus. In deep learning:

- **Wasserstein GANs (WGANs)** require the discriminator to be 1-Lipschitz. The gradient penalty term enforces this.
- **Spectral normalization** constrains weight matrices to ensure Lipschitz bounds, stabilizing training.
- **Gradient clipping** is an informal way to enforce approximate Lipschitz behavior.

> _Lipschitz continuity is the practitioner's version of uniform continuity — with a quantitative constant that can be constrained and regularized._

### 9.4 Continuity of the Activation Function

|Activation|Continuous?|Differentiable at 0?|Notes|
|---|---|---|---|
|Sigmoid|Yes|Yes|Saturates; vanishing gradient|
|Tanh|Yes|Yes|Zero-centered; still saturates|
|ReLU|Yes|No (at 0)|Subgradient used; sparse activations|
|Leaky ReLU|Yes|No (at 0)|Fixes dying ReLU problem|
|Softplus|Yes|Yes|Smooth ReLU approximation|
|Step function|No|—|Not used; gradient is zero almost everywhere|

The step function was used in early perceptrons. Its discontinuity made gradient-based learning impossible. The switch to continuous activations (sigmoid, then ReLU) was what made backpropagation viable.

### 9.5 Continuity in Reinforcement Learning

In continuous control (robotics, locomotion), the **policy** $\pi_\theta(a | s)$ and **value function** $V^\pi(s)$ must be continuous in the state $s$ for smooth control. Discontinuous policies cause jerky, unstable behavior in physical systems.

**Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)** explicitly enforce continuity and differentiability of learned solutions to PDEs — continuity is a hard constraint, not just a hope.

## §11. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Three-Part Check

Is $f$ continuous at $x = 2$?

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 - 2 & x \neq 2 \ 1 & x = 2 \end{cases}$$

1. $f(2) = 1$ ✓ (defined)
2. $\lim_{x \to 2} (x^2 - 2) = 4 - 2 = 2$ ✓ (limit exists)
3. $f(2) = 1 \neq 2$ ✗ (mismatch)

**Conclusion:** $f$ is discontinuous at $x = 2$. Removable discontinuity — redefine $f(2) = 2$ to repair.

---

### Example 2 — Using IVT to Locate a Root

Show $\cos x = x$ has a solution.

Let $g(x) = \cos x - x$. Then:

- $g(0) = 1 - 0 = 1 > 0$
- $g(\pi/2) = 0 - \pi/2 < 0$

$g$ is continuous (difference of continuous functions), and $0$ is between $g(0)$ and $g(\pi/2)$. IVT gives $c \in (0, \pi/2)$ with $g(c) = 0$, i.e., $\cos c = c$. $\blacksquare$

_(This fixed point $c \approx 0.739$ is called the **Dottie number**.)_

---

### Example 3 — Multivariable Failure

Show $f(x, y) = \dfrac{x^2 y}{x^4 + y^2}$, $f(0,0) = 0$ is discontinuous at the origin.

Along the parabolic path $y = x^2$:

$$f(x, x^2) = \frac{x^2 \cdot x^2}{x^4 + x^4} = \frac{x^4}{2x^4} = \frac{1}{2}$$

So $\lim_{x \to 0} f(x, x^2) = \frac{1}{2} \neq 0 = f(0,0)$.

The limit doesn't equal the function value. **Discontinuous.** $\blacksquare$

## §12. Things Worth Losing Sleep Over

- Is the composition of two discontinuous functions ever continuous? _(Yes. Take $f = g = $ Dirichlet function. Then $f \circ g \equiv 0$.)_
- A function can be continuous at exactly one point and discontinuous everywhere else. _(Classic: $f(x) = x$ if $x \in \mathbb{Q}$, $f(x) = 0$ if $x \notin \mathbb{Q}$. Continuous only at $x = 0$.)_
- Uniform continuity on $(a,b)$ + continuity implies unique continuous extension to $[a,b]$.
- Every continuous function on $[a,b]$ is Riemann integrable — the proof runs through uniform continuity.
