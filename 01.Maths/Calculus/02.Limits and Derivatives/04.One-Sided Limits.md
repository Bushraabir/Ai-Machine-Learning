## 0. Why Bother? (Motivation Before the Machine)

Before we write a single $\varepsilon$, let's feel the problem.

Consider the function $$ f(x) = \frac{|x|}{x}. $$ Near $x = 0$, the function does not oscillate wildly. It is perfectly calm on either side. To the left, $f(x) = -1$. To the right, $f(x) = +1$. And yet, no two-sided limit exists at $x = 0$.

The classical (two-sided) limit asks: _does the function approach a single value no matter how you approach $a$?_ One-sided limits ask a more refined question: _what does the function approach if I restrict the direction of approach?_

This is not a technicality. It is a fundamental distinction that separates differentiable functions from merely continuous ones, smooth activation functions from hard thresholds, and integrable functions from unruly ones.

## 2. Formal Definitions

### 2.1 Left-Hand Limit (LHL)

> **Definition (Stewart ¬ß2.2; MIT 18.02 spirit).** We say $$\lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) = L$$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $$0 < a - x < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$

**Unpacking the notation.**

- $x \to a^-$ means $x < a$ and $x \to a$. The superscript "$-$" denotes approach from the **left** (below).
- The condition $0 < a - x < \delta$ encodes: $x$ is strictly less than $a$, and within distance $\delta$ of $a$.
- We require $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$: the output is arbitrarily close to $L$.

### 2.2 Right-Hand Limit (RHL)

> **Definition.** We say $$\lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x) = L$$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $$0 < x - a < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$

- $x \to a^+$ means $x > a$ and $x \to a$. The superscript "$+$" denotes approach from the **right** (above).
- The condition $0 < x - a < \delta$ encodes: $x$ is strictly greater than $a$, and within distance $\delta$ of $a$.

## 3. The Bridge Theorem

This theorem is the central link between one-sided and two-sided limits.

> **Theorem.** The two-sided limit $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ exists **if and only if** both one-sided limits exist and are equal: $$\lim_{x \to a^{-}} f(x) = L \quad \text{and} \quad \lim_{x \to a^{+}} f(x) = L.$$

**Proof.**

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$. Let $\varepsilon > 0$. Then $\exists \delta > 0$ such that $0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$. In particular, this holds when $x \in (a - \delta, a)$ (giving the LHL) and when $x \in (a, a + \delta)$ (giving the RHL). Hence both one-sided limits equal $L$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose both one-sided limits equal $L$. Let $\varepsilon > 0$. By hypothesis, $\exists \delta_1, \delta_2 > 0$ such that:

- $0 < a - x < \delta_1 \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$
- $0 < x - a < \delta_2 \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$

Set $\delta = \min(\delta_1, \delta_2)$. Then for $0 < |x - a| < \delta$, either $x < a$ or $x > a$, and in both cases $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$. Hence the two-sided limit exists. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary.** If $\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) \neq \lim_{x \to a^+} f(x)$, the two-sided limit does not exist.

## 4. Worked Examples

### Example 4.1 ‚ÄĒ The Signum Function

$$\text{sgn}(x) = \begin{cases} -1 & x < 0 \ 0 & x = 0 \ 1 & x > 0 \end{cases}$$

**Computing the one-sided limits at $a = 0$:**

$$\lim_{x \to 0^-} \text{sgn}(x) = -1, \qquad \lim_{x \to 0^+} \text{sgn}(x) = 1.$$

Since $-1 \neq 1$, the two-sided limit $\lim_{x \to 0} \text{sgn}(x)$ **does not exist**.

### Example 4.2 ‚ÄĒ The Heaviside Step Function

$$H(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < 0 \ 1 & x \geq 0 \end{cases}$$

$$\lim_{x \to 0^-} H(x) = 0, \qquad \lim_{x \to 0^+} H(x) = 1.$$

The two-sided limit does not exist. Note: $H(0) = 1$ is defined, but it does not affect the limit analysis.

> **Remark.** $H(x)$ is the "hard threshold" activation function. It is _not_ differentiable at $0$, precisely because the one-sided limits disagree. This is why sigmoid and ReLU were introduced ‚ÄĒ to fix differentiability for gradient-based learning.

---

### Example 4.3 ‚ÄĒ A Piecewise Function with Matching Limits

$$g(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 + 1 & x < 2 \ 3x - 1 & x \geq 2 \end{cases}$$

$$\lim_{x \to 2^-} g(x) = (2)^2 + 1 = 5, \qquad \lim_{x \to 2^+} g(x) = 3(2) - 1 = 5.$$

Since both agree, $\lim_{x \to 2} g(x) = 5$. Moreover, $g(2) = 3(2) - 1 = 5$, so $g$ is **continuous** at $x = 2$.

---

### Example 4.4 ‚ÄĒ An $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ Proof of a One-Sided Limit

**Claim:** $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \sqrt{x} = 0$.

**Proof.** Let $\varepsilon > 0$. We need to find $\delta > 0$ such that $$0 < x - 0 < \delta \implies |\sqrt{x} - 0| < \varepsilon.$$ Note $|\sqrt{x}| = \sqrt{x}$ for $x > 0$. We want $\sqrt{x} < \varepsilon$, i.e., $x < \varepsilon^2$. Choose $\delta = \varepsilon^2$. Then $$0 < x < \delta = \varepsilon^2 \implies \sqrt{x} < \sqrt{\varepsilon^2} = \varepsilon. \quad \blacksquare$$

> _The function $\sqrt{x}$ only makes sense for $x \geq 0$, so a right-hand limit is the only sensible notion at $x = 0$. One-sided limits are not just a technicality ‚ÄĒ sometimes they are the only thing that exists._

## 5. One-Sided Limits and Continuity

### 5.1 One-Sided Continuity

> **Definition.** $f$ is **right-continuous** at $a$ if $\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = f(a)$. $f$ is **left-continuous** at $a$ if $\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = f(a)$.

$f$ is (two-sided) **continuous** at $a$ $\iff$ $f$ is both left- and right-continuous at $a$.

### 5.2 The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus Connection

The Riemann integral is defined via step functions. The **floor function** $\lfloor x \rfloor$ (greatest integer $\leq x$) satisfies: $$\lim_{x \to n^-} \lfloor x \rfloor = n - 1, \qquad \lim_{x \to n^+} \lfloor x \rfloor = n, \qquad \lfloor n \rfloor = n.$$

So $\lfloor x \rfloor$ is right-continuous but not left-continuous at every integer. This matters when defining integrals of piecewise functions.

## 7. Connection to Derivatives

The derivative at a point is a two-sided limit: $$f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}.$$

We can define **one-sided derivatives**: $$f'_+(a) = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}, \qquad f'_-(a) = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}.$$

$f$ is differentiable at $a$ $\iff$ $f'_+(a) = f'_-(a)$.

### Example: ReLU

The ReLU function $\sigma(x) = \max(0, x)$ satisfies: $$\sigma'_-(0) = 0, \qquad \sigma'_+(0) = 1.$$ Since these disagree, ReLU is **not differentiable** at $0$. This is precisely the "kink." In practice, one chooses a _subgradient_ (often $0$ or $1$) at this point for backpropagation.

## 8. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

This is not decoration ‚ÄĒ one-sided limits sit at the mathematical heart of several core concepts.

### 8.1 Activation Functions and the Differentiability Problem

|Activation|Formula|LHL at kink|RHL at kink|Differentiable?|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Heaviside $H$|$\mathbf{1}_{x \geq 0}$|0|1|‚úó (No gradient)|
|ReLU|$\max(0, x)$|derivative 0|derivative 1|‚úó (Subgradient used)|
|Leaky ReLU|$\max(\alpha x, x)$|derivative $\alpha$|derivative 1|‚úó (Subgradient used)|
|Sigmoid $\sigma$|$\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$|smooth|smooth|‚úď everywhere|
|GELU|$x \cdot \Phi(x)$|smooth|smooth|‚úď everywhere|

The transition from Heaviside ‚Üí sigmoid ‚Üí ReLU ‚Üí GELU is precisely the story of trying to find activations that are (a) nonlinear, (b) computationally simple, and (c) differentiable or at least subdifferentiable everywhere. One-sided limits diagnose exactly where and why differentiability fails.

### 8.2 Backpropagation and the Chain Rule

Backpropagation is the chain rule applied layer by layer. The chain rule requires derivatives to exist. When activations have kinks (like ReLU), we use **subgradients**, which are defined via one-sided derivatives: $$\partial \sigma(x) = \begin{cases} {0} & x < 0 \ [0, 1] & x = 0 \ {1} & x > 0 \end{cases}$$ The value chosen at $x = 0$ is a convention ‚ÄĒ the mathematics says the one-sided derivatives _disagree_ there.

### 8.3 Loss Functions and Piecewise Structure

The **Hinge loss** used in Support Vector Machines: $$\ell(y, \hat{y}) = \max(0, 1 - y\hat{y})$$ has a kink at $y\hat{y} = 1$. Computing its gradient requires understanding left- and right-hand derivatives at the kink point. One-sided limits are the language for this.

The **Huber loss** was designed to be smooth everywhere by _matching_ the one-sided derivatives at the transition point: $$L_\delta(r) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2}r^2 & |r| \leq \delta \ \delta(|r| - \frac{1}{2}\delta) & |r| > \delta \end{cases}$$ One can verify: $\lim_{r \to \delta^-} L_\delta'(r) = \delta = \lim_{r \to \delta^+} L_\delta'(r)$. The function is $C^1$ by design.

### 8.4 The Cumulative Distribution Function (CDF) in Probabilistic Models

In probabilistic deep learning and Bayesian inference, the CDF $F_X(t) = P(X \leq t)$ is always **right-continuous**: $$\lim_{x \to t^+} F_X(x) = F_X(t).$$ The left-hand limit $\lim_{x \to t^-} F_X(x) = P(X < t)$ may differ at atoms of the distribution. This distinction matters in variational autoencoders, normalizing flows, and any model that works with explicit probability distributions.

### 8.5 Learning Rate Schedules

Cosine annealing and step decay schedules for learning rates are piecewise-defined functions. The one-sided continuity at "restart" points determines whether gradient updates are stable across restarts.

## 9. Common Pitfalls

> ‚ö†ÔłŹ **The value $f(a)$ is irrelevant to the limit.** One-sided limits depend only on the behavior of $f$ _near_ $a$, not _at_ $a$.

> ‚ö†ÔłŹ **"Does not exist" is not the same as "equals infinity."** $\lim_{x \to 0^+} \frac{1}{x} = +\infty$ is a precise statement. The one-sided limit _exists as an extended real number_, even though it is not finite.

> ‚ö†ÔłŹ **Notation discipline.** $\lim_{x \to a^-}$ and $\lim_{x \to a-}$ (without the superscript) are different things in sloppy notation. Always write the superscript clearly: $a^-$, $a^+$.

> ‚ö†ÔłŹ **Domain matters.** For $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$, asking for $\lim_{x \to 0^-} \sqrt{x}$ is asking about a region outside the domain. The left-hand limit does not exist (not just equals zero).

## 10. Summary Theorem Sheet

|Fact|Statement|
|---|---|
|Two-sided ‚ÜĒ One-sided|$\lim_{x \to a} f = L \iff \lim_{x \to a^-} f = \lim_{x \to a^+} f = L$|
|Continuity|$f$ continuous at $a \iff$ both one-sided limits equal $f(a)$|
|Differentiability|$f$ differentiable at $a \iff f'_-(a) = f'_+(a)$|
|Monotone functions|Left- and right-hand limits always exist (may differ)|
|Bounded monotone|$\lim_{x \to a^{\pm}} f(x)$ finite for monotone bounded $f$|
