# The Tangent Problem

> _"The problem of finding the tangent line to a curve is the fundamental problem of differential calculus — and secretly, the fundamental problem of intelligence itself."_

---

## §1. The Classical Geometric Question

Let $C$ be a curve in the plane, and let $P$ be a point on $C$.

**Naïve attempt:** A tangent line at $P$ is a line that "touches" $C$ at $P$ without "crossing" it. This Euclidean intuition (inherited from the tangent to a circle) is _wrong_ in general.

> **Why it fails:** The parabola $y = x^2$ at the origin. The $x$-axis touches the curve at exactly one point and doesn't "cross" it — yet every introductory student correctly names $y = 0$ as the tangent. But the cubic $y = x^3$ at the origin: the $x$-axis _does_ cross the curve, yet it is also the tangent line. The geometric intuition of "not crossing" is not the right criterion.

The correct approach is _limiting_. We must escape statics and enter dynamics.

---

## §2. The Secant-to-Tangent Limit

### Setup

Let $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function. Fix a point $P = (a, f(a))$ on the graph.

Take a second point $Q = (a + h, f(a+h))$ on the graph, with $h \neq 0$.

The line through $P$ and $Q$ is called a **secant line**. Its slope is:

$$m_{PQ} = \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$$

This is the **difference quotient** — the single most important formula in all of calculus.

### The Tangent Line

$$\boxed{m_{\text{tan}} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}}$$

The tangent line at $P = (a, f(a))$ is the line through $P$ with slope $m_{\text{tan}}$:

$$y - f(a) = m_{\text{tan}}(x - a)$$

**This limit, when it exists, is defined to be the derivative $f'(a)$.**

---

## §3. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — The Parabola $f(x) = x^2$

Find the tangent line at $P = (1, 1)$.

$$m = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(1+h)^2 - 1^2}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1 + 2h + h^2 - 1}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{2h + h^2}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} (2 + h) = 2$$

Tangent line: $y - 1 = 2(x - 1)$, i.e., $y = 2x - 1$.

> _Notice: we cannot simply substitute $h=0$ into $(2h+h^2)/h$ — it's $0/0$. The algebraic cancellation of $h$ is not a trick; it is the rigorous resolution of the indeterminate form. This is why limits are necessary._

---

### Example 2 — The Square Root $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$

Find $f'(a)$ for $a > 0$.

$$m = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\sqrt{a+h} - \sqrt{a}}{h}$$

Rationalize by multiplying by $\dfrac{\sqrt{a+h}+\sqrt{a}}{\sqrt{a+h}+\sqrt{a}}$:

$$= \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(a+h) - a}{h(\sqrt{a+h}+\sqrt{a})} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{1}{\sqrt{a+h}+\sqrt{a}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{a}}$$

So $f'(a) = \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{a}}$. The tangent slope decreases as $a$ grows — the curve flattens.

---

### Example 3 — Where the Limit Fails: $f(x) = |x|$ at $x = 0$

$$\lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{|h| - 0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{h}{h} = 1$$

$$\lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{|h| - 0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{-h}{h} = -1$$

The one-sided limits disagree. **The derivative does not exist at $x = 0$.** The graph has a corner, and no unique tangent line can be assigned.

> _This is a canonical example of a continuous-but-not-differentiable point. Continuity is necessary but not sufficient for differentiability._

  |   /
  |  * P  ← as Q → P, secant → tangent
  | /

### Slope as a Function

Once we compute $f'(a)$ for every $a$, we obtain a _new function_ — the **derivative function** $f'(x)$. This is the profound insight: differentiating a function produces another function encoding the entire landscape of slopes.

|$f(x)$|$f'(x)$|Geometric meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$x^2$|$2x$|Slope grows linearly; steeper as $x$ increases|
|$\sqrt{x}$|$\frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$|Slope always positive but decreasing|
|$\sin x$|$\cos x$|Slope oscillates; zero at peaks/troughs|
|$e^x$|$e^x$|Slope equals height — exponential's hallmark|

## §5. The Formal Definition (Rigorous Statement)

**Definition (Stewart §2.7; MIT 18.02).** Let $f$ be defined on an open interval containing $a$. The **derivative of $f$ at $a$** is

$$f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$$

provided the limit exists. If it does, $f$ is said to be **differentiable at $a$**.

**Alternative form** (using $x \to a$ instead of $h \to 0$):

$$f'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}$$

These are identical; the substitution $x = a + h$ converts one to the other.

**Theorem.** _Differentiability implies continuity._ If $f'(a)$ exists, then $f$ is continuous at $a$.

_Proof sketch:_ $$\lim_{h \to 0} [f(a+h) - f(a)] = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \cdot h = f'(a) \cdot 0 = 0$$

Hence $\lim_{h\to 0} f(a+h) = f(a)$. $\blacksquare$

> _The converse fails: $|x|$ is continuous at 0 but not differentiable._

---

## §6. Velocity as the Same Limit

The tangent problem is _isomorphic_ to the velocity problem. If $s(t)$ is the position of a particle at time $t$, then:

$$v(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{s(a+h) - s(a)}{h} = s'(a)$$

The difference quotient $\frac{s(a+h)-s(a)}{h}$ is the **average velocity** over $[a, a+h]$. The derivative is the **instantaneous velocity** — the limit of average velocities over shrinking intervals.

This dual interpretation (geometric slope / physical rate of change) is why the derivative is the central object of calculus. It encodes _how fast things change_.

## §7. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> _Everything that follows is a direct descendant of the difference quotient._

### 7.1 The Loss Landscape

In supervised learning, we have a model parameterized by weights $\mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and a **loss function** $\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})$ measuring prediction error. Training means finding:

$$\mathbf{w}^* = \arg\min_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w})$$

To minimize $\mathcal{L}$, we need to know its slope — that is, we need its **gradient** $\nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L}$. Each partial derivative $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_i}$ is exactly the tangent problem applied to $\mathcal{L}$ in the $w_i$-direction.

### 7.2 Gradient Descent

The update rule for gradient descent is:

$$\mathbf{w}^{(t+1)} = \mathbf{w}^{(t)} - \eta \nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}^{(t)})$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate. The gradient $\nabla_{\mathbf{w}} \mathcal{L}$ tells us the direction of steepest _ascent_; we step in the opposite direction to descend.

**This is the tangent problem, iterated over millions of parameters.**

```
Loss landscape (1D illustration):

  L(w)
  |
  |    *                        *
  |  *   *                    *   *
  | *      *        *        *     *
  |*         *    *   *    *         *
  |             **       **
  |___________________________________________ w
              ↑                   ↑
           local min           global min

  At each w, the tangent slope = ∂L/∂w tells us 
  which direction to move. Gradient descent follows 
  the tangent lines downhill.
```

### 7.3 Backpropagation is the Chain Rule

A deep neural network is a composition of functions:

$$\hat{y} = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{x})$$

To compute $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{w}_k}$ for layer $k$, we apply the **chain rule** (itself a theorem about tangent lines of composite functions):

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{w}_k} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial f_{L-1}} \cdots \frac{\partial f_{k+1}}{\partial f_k} \cdot \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial \mathbf{w}_k}$$

**Backpropagation is just the chain rule applied systematically from output to input.** Every tensor flowing backwards in a neural network is a derivative — a generalized tangent slope.

### 7.4 Numerical Differentiation (Finite Differences)

Before automatic differentiation existed, ML practitioners would _approximate_ derivatives using the definition directly:

$$f'(a) \approx \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h} \quad \text{(forward difference)}$$

$$f'(a) \approx \frac{f(a+h) - f(a-h)}{2h} \quad \text{(central difference, more accurate)}$$

The central difference is $O(h^2)$ error vs $O(h)$ for forward difference — this comes from Taylor expansion. This is still used today to **gradient-check** neural network implementations: verify that autodiff gradients match finite difference approximations.

### 7.5 Activation Functions and Non-Differentiability

The ReLU activation $\sigma(x) = \max(0, x)$ is _not_ differentiable at $x = 0$ — exactly like $|x|$ at 0. In practice, training proceeds anyway because:

- The set ${x = 0}$ has measure zero (the network almost never lands exactly there).
- A **subgradient** (a generalization of derivative) is used: $\partial\sigma(0) = [0,1]$.

The Leaky ReLU and smooth approximations (Swish, GELU) were invented precisely to fix this non-differentiability while preserving the computational advantages of ReLU.

### 7.6 Summary Table

|Calculus concept|ML/DL counterpart|
|---|---|
|Derivative $f'(a)$|Gradient component $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w_i}$|
|Tangent line|Local linear approximation of loss surface|
|Chain rule|Backpropagation|
|Difference quotient|Finite difference / gradient check|
|Non-differentiability|ReLU kink; handled by subgradients|
|Gradient descent|Iterative tangent-following to minimize loss|
|Second derivative|Hessian; used in second-order optimizers (Adam approximates it)|

## §8. A Note on Notation

Various notations you will encounter, all meaning the same thing:

$$f'(a) = \frac{df}{dx}\bigg|_{x=a} = \frac{d}{dx}f(x)\bigg|_{x=a} = Df(a) = \dot{f}(a) \text{ (physics, time derivative)}$$

Leibniz notation $\frac{df}{dx}$ is suggestive: it "looks like" the ratio $\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x}$ in the limit. This is not coincidence — it is why Leibniz notation is so powerful for the chain rule:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

The $du$'s "cancel" (not rigorously, but the notation encodes the chain rule's content).

## §9. What Comes Next

The tangent problem motivates everything that follows:

1. **Differentiation rules** — product, quotient, chain rule: so we never have to return to the limit definition.
2. **Linear approximation** — $f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x-a)$: the tangent line as the best local approximation.
3. **Taylor series** — approximation by polynomials to arbitrary order.
4. **Partial derivatives and gradients** — tangent lines in higher-dimensional spaces.
5. **Optimization** — critical points where $f'(a) = 0$; second derivative test.
6. **Differential equations** — equations involving $f'$, encoding physical and biological laws.

> _Every one of these threads pulls on the same knot: the limit of the difference quotient. Learn this well and the rest of calculus is commentary._
