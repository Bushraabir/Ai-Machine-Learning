## The Central Definition (Don't Skip This)

The derivative of $f$ at $a$ is defined as the limit:

$$f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$$

**The function fails to be differentiable at $a$ if and only if this limit does not exist.**

That's the whole story. Everything below is just taxonomy — the different _ways_ a limit can fail to exist.

> 📌 **Key Principle (Stewart §2.8):** Differentiability at a point requires the limit of the difference quotient to exist _and_ be finite, _and_ be the same from both sides.

---

## The Geometric Intuition

Before the algebra: differentiability means the graph has a **unique, well-defined tangent line** at that point. Failure means:

- The graph has a **corner** (two different tangent directions)
- The graph has a **vertical tangent** (slope is $\pm \infty$)
- The graph **breaks** (discontinuity — no tangent at all)
- The graph **oscillates** too wildly to have a tangent

```
    Corner          Cusp           Discontinuity      Vertical Tangent
      /\              |                |    /              |
     /  \            / \               |   /               |
    /    \          /   \           ---    /                |
                                                            |
```

## Case 1: Corner (Non-Removable Kink)

### The Prototype: $f(x) = |x|$ at $x = 0$

Compute the one-sided limits of the difference quotient:

**Left-hand derivative:** $$\lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{|0+h| - |0|}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{|h|}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{-h}{h} = -1$$

**Right-hand derivative:** $$\lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{|0+h| - |0|}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{h}{h} = +1$$

Since $-1 \neq +1$, the two-sided limit **does not exist**. $f$ is not differentiable at $0$.

### Visualization

```
        |
   \    |    /
    \   |   /
     \  |  /
      \ | /
       \|/
--------+--------
       0
  slope=-1   slope=+1
```

The graph has a sharp corner. The "tangent line" depends on which direction you approach from — so it isn't unique.

### General Principle

A corner occurs precisely when: $$\lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \neq \lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h}$$ but **both** limits exist as finite numbers.

---

## Case 2: Cusp

### The Prototype: $f(x) = x^{2/3}$ at $x = 0$

$$f'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h^{2/3} - 0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} h^{-1/3}$$

As $h \to 0^+$: $h^{-1/3} \to +\infty$  
As $h \to 0^-$: $h^{-1/3} \to -\infty$

The one-sided limits both blow up to infinity (with opposite signs). This is a **cusp**: the graph comes to a sharp point with a _vertical_ tangent from both sides but in different directions.

### Visualization

```
    |    /
    |   /
    |  /
    | /
    |/   <-- cusp here
    |\
    | \
    |  \
    |   \
```

The function is continuous at $0$, but the slope is infinite — no finite tangent line exists.

---

## Case 3: Vertical Tangent

### The Prototype: $f(x) = x^{1/3}$ at $x = 0$

$$f'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h^{1/3}}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} h^{-2/3} = +\infty$$

Both one-sided limits agree — they both go to $+\infty$. Unlike a cusp, the behavior is symmetric. The tangent line _exists_ geometrically (it's the $y$-axis), but its slope is undefined as a real number.

> 📌 **Subtle distinction:** Cusp vs Vertical Tangent
> 
> - **Cusp:** $f'(a^+) = +\infty$ and $f'(a^-) = -\infty$ (or vice versa) — one-sided slopes disagree
> - **Vertical tangent:** $f'(a^+) = f'(a^-) = \pm\infty$ — one-sided slopes agree but are infinite

## Case 4: Discontinuity

### Theorem (Stewart §2.8, Contrapositive)

> **If $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then $f$ is continuous at $a$.**

Equivalently: **discontinuity at $a$ $\Rightarrow$ not differentiable at $a$.**

### Proof

Suppose $f'(a)$ exists. Then: $$\lim_{h \to 0} [f(a+h) - f(a)] = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h)-f(a)}{h} \cdot h = f'(a) \cdot 0 = 0$$

Therefore $\lim_{h \to 0} f(a+h) = f(a)$, which is exactly continuity. $\blacksquare$

### Examples of Discontinuities Causing Non-Differentiability

|Type|Example|Behavior|
|---|---|---|
|Jump|$f(x) = \text{sgn}(x)$ at $x=0$|Difference quotient $\to \pm \infty$ or oscillates|
|Removable|$f(x) = \frac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}$ at $x = 1$ (if not filled)|Limit of quotient may exist, but $f(a)$ is undefined|
|Infinite|$f(x) = 1/x$ at $x = 0$|Function not even defined|

> ⚠️ **The Converse is FALSE.** Continuity does **not** imply differentiability. $|x|$ is continuous everywhere but not differentiable at $0$.

## Case 5: Oscillatory Non-Differentiability

### The Prototype: $f(x) = x \sin(1/x)$ with $f(0) = 0$

This one is subtle. $f$ is **continuous** at $0$ (since $|x \sin(1/x)| \leq |x| \to 0$). Now check differentiability:

$$f'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h\sin(1/h) - 0}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \sin(1/h)$$

This limit **does not exist** — $\sin(1/h)$ oscillates between $-1$ and $+1$ infinitely often as $h \to 0$.

### Visualization (Conceptual)

```
       ___     ___
      /   \   /   \
-----/-----\_/-----\-----
    oscillates wildly near 0
    amplitude → 0, but slope doesn't settle
```

Even though the graph is being "squeezed" toward zero, the slopes are oscillating too rapidly to converge to any value.

## The Weierstrass Function: Nowhere Differentiable

> _"When I first saw this, I didn't believe it was a function."_

Weierstrass (1872) shocked the mathematical world with: $$W(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a^n \cos(b^n \pi x)$$

where $0 < a < 1$, $b$ is a positive odd integer, and $ab > 1 + \frac{3\pi}{2}$.

This function is:

- **Continuous everywhere** (it's a uniformly convergent series of continuous functions)
- **Differentiable nowhere** (corners at every scale — it's a fractal)

### Conceptual Sketch

```
  Zoom level 1:    /\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\
  Zoom level 2:   /\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\
  Zoom level 3:  /\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\/\
                 ↑ no matter how close you zoom, it's still jagged
```

This demolished the 19th-century intuition that "continuous = mostly smooth."

## Rigorous Criterion: Hölder Continuity

A function $f$ is **Hölder continuous** of exponent $\alpha \in (0,1]$ at $a$ if: $$|f(x) - f(a)| \leq C|x - a|^\alpha \quad \text{for some constant } C$$

- If $\alpha = 1$: Lipschitz continuity. If additionally $f'(a)$ exists, then $|f'(a)| \leq C$.
- If $\alpha < 1$: The oscillation is "too slow" — differentiability fails.
- $f(x) = |x|^{1/2}$ is Hölder with $\alpha = 1/2$ but **not** differentiable at $0$.

> This gives a hierarchy: **Differentiable** ⊊ **Lipschitz** ⊊ **Hölder-$\alpha$** ⊊ **Continuous**

## Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

This is where the abstract theory _directly_ shapes how modern AI works.

### 1. Backpropagation and the Chain Rule

Neural networks are trained by computing gradients via **backpropagation**, which is just the chain rule applied iteratively:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial a^{(L)}} \cdot \frac{\partial a^{(L)}}{\partial z^{(L)}} \cdot \frac{\partial z^{(L)}}{\partial w}$$

This entire machinery **assumes differentiability**. If any activation function is not differentiable at a point, the gradient either doesn't exist or is ambiguous there.

### 2. ReLU: The Most Important Corner in AI

The **Rectified Linear Unit**: $$\text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0, x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x \leq 0 \ x & x > 0 \end{cases}$$

This is **exactly** $f(x) = |x|$ shifted — it has a **corner at $x = 0$**. The left derivative is $0$, the right derivative is $1$. Not differentiable at $0$.

Yet ReLU is the dominant activation function in deep learning. How?

**The engineering fix — subgradients:**  
At the corner, practitioners assign an arbitrary value in $[0, 1]$, typically $0$. This is called the **subgradient**, and it works because:

- The set ${x = 0}$ has measure zero — the network almost never sits exactly there
- Stochastic gradient descent is noisy enough that the corner is rarely hit exactly

```
ReLU:              Leaky ReLU:        ELU:
    /                  /                  /
   /                  /                  /
--/----           --/----          -----/----
                  /                ~___/
              (slope 0.01)     (smooth curve)
```

**Leaky ReLU** and **ELU** were invented precisely to fix this: they make the function differentiable everywhere (or at least everywhere practically relevant).

### 3. The Vanishing / Exploding Gradient Problem

The **sigmoid** function $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ is infinitely differentiable. But: $$\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x)) \in (0, 1/4]$$

In deep networks, the chain rule multiplies these derivatives together across layers. A product of $n$ numbers each less than $\frac{1}{4}$ goes to zero exponentially fast — the **vanishing gradient problem**. Gradients at early layers effectively become zero, and the network stops learning.

This is not a failure of differentiability, but a failure of the _magnitude_ of derivatives — directly related to the structure of the derivative.

### 4. Non-Smooth Loss Functions

Some loss functions used in ML are **not differentiable**:

- **Hinge loss** (SVM): $L(y, \hat{y}) = \max(0, 1 - y\hat{y})$ — corner at $y\hat{y} = 1$, same structure as ReLU
- **$L^1$ regularization (LASSO)**: The $\ell^1$ norm $|w|_1 = \sum |w_i|$ is not differentiable when any $w_i = 0$ — a corner in weight space
- **Max-pooling**: $\max(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_k)$ is not differentiable when two inputs are equal — a corner

In all these cases, **subgradient methods** or **smooth approximations** are used. The entire field of **non-smooth optimization** exists to handle these cases rigorously.

### 5. Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) and Differentiability

When neural networks are used to solve PDEs, the network itself must be differentiable enough to compute the required partial derivatives. If the network has non-smooth activations, you need **automatic differentiation** and careful choice of architecture.

### 6. Generative Models and Lipschitz Constraints

**Wasserstein GANs** require the discriminator to be a **Lipschitz function** (i.e., $|f(x) - f(y)| \leq K|x-y|$). This is precisely the "one step above differentiable" condition in our Hölder hierarchy. Practitioners enforce this via **gradient clipping** or **spectral normalization** — explicitly controlling the derivative.

## Proof: Differentiability Implies Continuity (Full)

**Theorem:** If $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then $f$ is continuous at $a$.

**Proof.** Suppose $f'(a)$ exists. We want to show $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$.

Write: $$f(x) - f(a) = \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \cdot (x - a) \quad \text{for } x \neq a$$

Taking limits: $$\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - f(a)] = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \cdot \lim_{x \to a} (x - a) = f'(a) \cdot 0 = 0$$

Therefore $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$. $\blacksquare$

> This proof is clean because it factors the expression cleverly — a technique worth memorizing.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Corner

$f(x) = |x - 2|$ at $x = 2$.

$$f'(2) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{|h|}{h}$$

Left limit: $-1$. Right limit: $+1$. **Not differentiable** — corner.

### Example 2 — Cusp

$f(x) = (x-1)^{2/3}$ at $x = 1$.

Let $u = x - 1$: $$f'(1) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h^{2/3}}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} h^{-1/3}$$

$h^{-1/3} \to +\infty$ from right, $\to -\infty$ from left. **Not differentiable** — cusp.

### Example 3 — Removable Discontinuity

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} x^2 & x \neq 1 \ 5 & x = 1 \end{cases}$$

$f$ is discontinuous at $x=1$, so it's **not differentiable** at $x=1$ regardless of the difference quotient.

### Example 4 — The Tricky One

$f(x) = x^2 \sin(1/x)$ with $f(0) = 0$. Is $f$ differentiable at $0$?

$$f'(0) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{h^2 \sin(1/h)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} h \sin(1/h)$$

Since $|h \sin(1/h)| \leq |h| \to 0$, the limit is $0$. **Yes, differentiable at $0$!** (Contrast with $x\sin(1/x)$.)

> This is a classic exam trap. The $x^2$ factor provides enough "damping" to kill the oscillation.

## Quick Reference: What to Check

When asked "is $f$ differentiable at $a$?" — systematic checklist:

1. **Is $f$ defined at $a$?** If not → ❌ Not differentiable.
2. **Is $f$ continuous at $a$?** If not → ❌ Not differentiable.
3. **Compute $\lim_{h \to 0^-}$ and $\lim_{h \to 0^+}$ of the difference quotient.**
    - If both exist, are finite, and are **equal** → ✅ Differentiable.
    - If both exist and are finite but **unequal** → ❌ Corner.
    - If either is $\pm\infty$ → ❌ Cusp or vertical tangent.
    - If either doesn't exist (oscillation, etc.) → ❌ Not differentiable.
