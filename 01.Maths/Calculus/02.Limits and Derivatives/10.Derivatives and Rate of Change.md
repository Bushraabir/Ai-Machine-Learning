

> _"The derivative is the instantaneous rate of change — a limit that collapses an interval to a point, and in doing so, extracts the geometry of the function at that point."_

---

## §1. Motivation — The Two Problems That Built Calculus

Newton and Leibniz arrived at the derivative from two directions that turn out to be the same thing.

**The tangent problem.** Given a curve $y = f(x)$ and a point $P = (a, f(a))$, find the slope of the line tangent to the curve at $P$. A tangent line touches the curve at one point and captures its _instantaneous direction_.

**The velocity problem.** Given a position function $s(t)$, find the _instantaneous_ velocity at time $t = a$. Average velocity over $[a, a+h]$ is $\Delta s / \Delta t$; but what happens as the time interval collapses?

Both reduce to the same limiting process. The derivative is their common answer.

---

## §2. The Definition

### 2.1 The Difference Quotient

The **average rate of change** of $f$ over $[a, a+h]$ is the slope of the **secant line** through $(a, f(a))$ and $(a+h, f(a+h))$:

$$\frac{\Delta f}{\Delta x} = \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$$

```
 y
 |               ● (a+h, f(a+h))
 |             /
 |           /  ← secant line, slope = Δf/Δx
 |         /
 |       ● (a, f(a))
 |
 +---+---+---+------- x
     a   a+h
```

As $h \to 0$, the second point slides toward $(a, f(a))$, and the secant line rotates toward the **tangent line**.

### 2.2 The Derivative

> **Definition.** The **derivative of $f$ at $a$** is: $$f'(a) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h) - f(a)}{h}$$ provided this limit exists. If it does, $f$ is **differentiable at $a$**.

Equivalent formulation (substituting $x = a + h$):

$$f'(a) = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a}$$

Both forms appear constantly. Know both.

### 2.3 The Derivative as a Function

If $f$ is differentiable at every point in its domain, we get the **derivative function**:

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x+h) - f(x)}{h}$$

This is itself a function — it assigns to each $x$ the slope of the tangent to $f$ at $x$.

### 2.4 Notation

All of the following mean the same thing:

$$f'(x) = y' = \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{df}{dx} = Df(x) = \dot{y} \text{ (physics)}$$

Leibniz notation $dy/dx$ is not a fraction — it is a single symbol, the limit of $\Delta y / \Delta x$. But it behaves like a fraction in chain rule and in differentials, which is one of its great virtues.

## §3. Differentiability and Continuity

> **Theorem.** If $f$ is differentiable at $a$, then $f$ is continuous at $a$.

**Proof.** We want $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$. Write:

$$f(x) - f(a) = \frac{f(x) - f(a)}{x - a} \cdot (x - a)$$

Taking limits:

$$\lim_{x \to a} [f(x) - f(a)] = \lim_{x \to a} \frac{f(x)-f(a)}{x-a} \cdot \lim_{x \to a}(x-a) = f'(a) \cdot 0 = 0$$

So $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = f(a)$. $\blacksquare$

> **The converse is false.** Continuity does not imply differentiability.

**Canonical counterexample:** $f(x) = |x|$ at $x = 0$.

$f$ is continuous at $0$, but:

$$\lim_{h \to 0^+} \frac{|h| - 0}{h} = 1, \qquad \lim_{h \to 0^-} \frac{|h| - 0}{h} = -1$$

Left and right derivatives disagree. The limit does not exist. $f'(0)$ does not exist — there is a **corner**.

### Geometric Picture of Non-Differentiability

```
Corner (|x|)         Cusp (x^{2/3})       Vertical Tangent (x^{1/3})
 y                    y                     y
  \  /                  |                   |/
   \/                   |                   |
   0      x           --+---  x           --+--  x
                                            |
slope: +1/-1 disagree   slope → ∞          slope = ∞
```

Three ways differentiability fails: corners, cusps, vertical tangents (and discontinuities).

## §4. The Differentiation Rules

Computing derivatives from the definition every time is untenable. The differentiation rules are theorems — each provable from the limit definition.

### 4.1 Basic Rules

|Rule|Formula|
|---|---|
|Constant|$(c)' = 0$|
|Power|$(x^n)' = nx^{n-1}$|
|Constant multiple|$(cf)' = cf'$|
|Sum/Difference|$(f \pm g)' = f' \pm g'$|
|Product|$(fg)' = f'g + fg'$|
|Quotient|$\left(\dfrac{f}{g}\right)' = \dfrac{f'g - fg'}{g^2}$|
|Chain Rule|$(f \circ g)'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$|

### 4.2 The Product Rule — Intuition

Why is $(fg)' = f'g + fg'$ and not $f'g'$? Think of $f$ and $g$ as side lengths of a rectangle with area $A = f(x)g(x)$.

```
        g + Δg
   +----------+----+
   |          |    |
 f |  f·g     |f·Δg|
   |          |    |
   +----------+----+
   |    g·Δf  | ε  |  ← second-order small, vanishes
   +----------+----+
      g           Δg
```

$\Delta A = f \cdot \Delta g + g \cdot \Delta f + \Delta f \cdot \Delta g$

Dividing by $\Delta x$ and taking $\Delta x \to 0$: the last term is $(\Delta f / \Delta x) \cdot \Delta g \to f' \cdot 0 = 0$.

So $\dfrac{dA}{dx} = f \cdot g' + g \cdot f'$. $\blacksquare$

### 4.3 The Chain Rule — The Most Important Rule

> If $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, then: $$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

In function notation: $(f \circ g)'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$.

**Reading:** differentiate the outside (leaving the inside alone), then multiply by the derivative of the inside.

_Example._ $y = \sin(x^2)$.

- Outside: $\sin(\square)$, derivative $\cos(\square)$
- Inside: $x^2$, derivative $2x$

$$y' = \cos(x^2) \cdot 2x$$

_Example._ $y = e^{-x^2/2}$ (the Gaussian).

$$y' = e^{-x^2/2} \cdot (-x) = -xe^{-x^2/2}$$

The chain rule chains: $y = f(g(h(x)))$ gives $y' = f'(g(h(x))) \cdot g'(h(x)) \cdot h'(x)$.

### 4.4 Derivatives of Elementary Functions

$$\frac{d}{dx} x^n = nx^{n-1} \qquad \frac{d}{dx} e^x = e^x \qquad \frac{d}{dx} \ln x = \frac{1}{x}$$

$$\frac{d}{dx} \sin x = \cos x \qquad \frac{d}{dx} \cos x = -\sin x \qquad \frac{d}{dx} \tan x = \sec^2 x$$

$$\frac{d}{dx} \arcsin x = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \qquad \frac{d}{dx} \arctan x = \frac{1}{1+x^2}$$

The derivative of $e^x$ equals itself — this is the defining property of the exponential. It is why exponential functions appear everywhere dynamics are modeled.

## §5. Higher-Order Derivatives

The derivative $f'$ is itself a function; we can differentiate it again.

$$f''(x) = (f')'(x) = \frac{d^2y}{dx^2} \qquad f^{(n)}(x) = \frac{d^n y}{dx^n}$$

**Interpretations:**

- $f'(x)$: instantaneous rate of change, slope of tangent
- $f''(x)$: rate of change of slope — **concavity**
- $f'''(x)$: rate of change of concavity — **jerk** in physics

**Physics chain:** $$s(t) \xrightarrow{;\frac{d}{dt};} v(t) \xrightarrow{;\frac{d}{dt};} a(t) \xrightarrow{;\frac{d}{dt};} j(t)$$ position $\to$ velocity $\to$ acceleration $\to$ jerk

Newton's second law $F = ma$ is a statement about the second derivative of position.

**Concavity:**

- $f'' > 0$ on $(a,b)$: $f$ is **concave up** (bowl facing up, slope increasing)
- $f'' < 0$ on $(a,b)$: $f$ is **concave down** (bowl facing down, slope decreasing)

```
Concave up (f'' > 0)        Concave down (f'' < 0)
        ___                   ‾‾‾
      /     \               /       \
tangent lines below        tangent lines above
```

## §6. Implicit Differentiation

Not every curve is expressible as $y = f(x)$. When $x$ and $y$ are related implicitly (e.g., $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$), differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, treating $y$ as a function of $x$ and applying the chain rule.

_Example._ Differentiate $x^2 + y^2 = 25$:

$$2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}$$

At $(3, 4)$: slope $= -3/4$.

_Example._ Differentiate $x^3 + y^3 = 6xy$ (folium of Descartes):

$$3x^2 + 3y^2 y' = 6y + 6xy' \implies y'(3y^2 - 6x) = 6y - 3x^2 \implies y' = \frac{6y - 3x^2}{3y^2 - 6x} = \frac{2y - x^2}{y^2 - 2x}$$

### Logarithmic Differentiation

For products, quotients, and powers of functions, take $\ln$ first.

_Example._ $y = \dfrac{x^3 \sqrt{x+1}}{(2x-1)^4}$

$$\ln y = 3\ln x + \frac{1}{2}\ln(x+1) - 4\ln(2x-1)$$

$$\frac{y'}{y} = \frac{3}{x} + \frac{1}{2(x+1)} - \frac{8}{2x-1}$$

$$y' = y \cdot \left[\frac{3}{x} + \frac{1}{2(x+1)} - \frac{8}{2x-1}\right]$$

This is far cleaner than the quotient and product rules applied directly.

## §7. The Mean Value Theorem

The MVT is the central theorem of differential calculus. Everything about the connection between derivatives and function behavior flows from it.

> **Theorem (MVT).** If $f$ is continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$, then there exists $c \in (a, b)$ such that: $$f'(c) = \frac{f(b) - f(a)}{b - a}$$

**Geometric reading:** There is a point where the instantaneous slope equals the average slope over the interval. The tangent at $c$ is parallel to the secant from $a$ to $b$.

```
 y
 |                    ● (b, f(b))
 |              ·  ·/
 |           · /· /   ← tangent at c parallel to secant
 |          /●/
 |        /  c
 |  (a, f(a))●
 +---+-------+------- x
     a       b
```

**Consequences:**

- $f' = 0$ everywhere on $(a,b)$ $\implies$ $f$ is constant
- $f' > 0$ everywhere $\implies$ $f$ is strictly increasing
- $f' < 0$ everywhere $\implies$ $f$ is strictly decreasing

These seem obvious but require proof — and the proof uses MVT.

**Rolle's Theorem** (special case): if $f(a) = f(b)$, then $f'(c) = 0$ for some $c \in (a, b)$. Between two equal values, the derivative vanishes somewhere.

## §8. Linear Approximation and Differentials

Near $x = a$, a differentiable function looks like a line:

$$f(x) \approx f(a) + f'(a)(x - a) \quad \text{for } x \text{ near } a$$

This is the **tangent line approximation** (or linearization). It is the first-order Taylor polynomial.

_Example._ Approximate $\sqrt{4.02}$.

Let $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$, $a = 4$, $f'(x) = 1/(2\sqrt{x})$, $f'(4) = 1/4$.

$$\sqrt{4.02} \approx \sqrt{4} + \frac{1}{4}(4.02 - 4) = 2 + \frac{0.02}{4} = 2.005$$

(Exact: $2.00499...$)

### Differentials

Write $dy = f'(x), dx$ where $dx = \Delta x$ is a small change in $x$ and $dy$ is the corresponding change in the tangent line (approximating $\Delta y = f(x + \Delta x) - f(x)$).

This notation makes the chain rule transparently a cancellation of differentials:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

and justifies the substitution rule in integration.

## §9. Rates of Change — Applications

### 9.1 Related Rates

When two quantities both depend on time $t$, differentiating an equation relating them gives a relationship between their rates.

_Example._ A ladder 10 m long leans against a wall. The base slides away at $2$ m/s. How fast is the top sliding down when the base is 6 m from the wall?

Let $x$ = distance of base from wall, $y$ = height of top. $x^2 + y^2 = 100$.

Differentiate with respect to $t$:

$$2x\frac{dx}{dt} + 2y\frac{dy}{dt} = 0$$

When $x = 6$: $y = \sqrt{100 - 36} = 8$. Given $dx/dt = 2$:

$$2(6)(2) + 2(8)\frac{dy}{dt} = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dt} = -\frac{24}{16} = -\frac{3}{2} \text{ m/s}$$

The top slides down at $3/2$ m/s.

### 9.2 Optimization

At a local extremum, $f'(c) = 0$ (critical point) or $f'(c)$ does not exist.

**First Derivative Test:** $f'$ changes sign at $c$.

- $+ \to -$: local maximum
- $- \to +$: local minimum

**Second Derivative Test:** At critical point $c$ with $f'(c) = 0$:

- $f''(c) > 0$: local minimum (concave up)
- $f''(c) < 0$: local maximum (concave down)
- $f''(c) = 0$: inconclusive

## §10. Partial Derivatives (18.02 Perspective)

For $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, the **partial derivative** with respect to $x_i$ holds all other variables fixed:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{e}_i) - f(\mathbf{a})}{h}$$

where $\mathbf{e}_i$ is the $i$-th standard basis vector.

**Geometric reading:** $\partial f / \partial x$ is the slope of the slice of the surface $z = f(x,y)$ cut by a plane of constant $y$.

### The Gradient

$$\nabla f = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right)$$

The gradient is the vector of all partial derivatives. Its geometric meaning: it points in the direction of **steepest ascent** of $f$, and its magnitude is the rate of steepest ascent.

> $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ is perpendicular to the level set ${f = f(\mathbf{a})}$ at $\mathbf{a}$.

This is the most important geometric fact in multivariable calculus.

### Differentiability in $\mathbb{R}^n$

In higher dimensions, existence of partial derivatives does _not_ imply differentiability (unlike in $\mathbb{R}$). The function must be **well-approximated by a linear map** near $\mathbf{a}$:

$$f(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{h}) = f(\mathbf{a}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{h} + o(|\mathbf{h}|)$$

If partial derivatives exist and are **continuous** near $\mathbf{a}$, then $f$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$. (Sufficient, not necessary.)

---

## §11. The Jacobian

For $\mathbf{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$, the derivative generalizes to the **Jacobian matrix**:

$$J_\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{pmatrix} \partial f_1/\partial x_1 & \cdots & \partial f_1/\partial x_n \ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ \partial f_m/\partial x_1 & \cdots & \partial f_m/\partial x_n \end{pmatrix}$$

This is the best linear approximation to $\mathbf{f}$ near $\mathbf{x}$:

$$\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{h}) \approx \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}) + J_\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}),\mathbf{h}$$

The chain rule in multiple dimensions: if $\mathbf{h} = \mathbf{g} \circ \mathbf{f}$, then:

$$J_\mathbf{h} = J_\mathbf{g} \cdot J_\mathbf{f}$$

Matrix multiplication of Jacobians.

## §12. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This is not analogy — derivatives are the literal computational object underlying all of modern machine learning.

### 12.1 Gradient Descent is Differentiation Applied

To minimize a loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$, gradient descent takes steps in the direction of steepest descent:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

The gradient $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ is a vector of partial derivatives — one per parameter. A neural network with $10^9$ parameters has a gradient in $\mathbb{R}^{10^9}$.

**The fundamental insight:** the gradient tells you, for each parameter $\theta_i$, how much a small increase in $\theta_i$ increases the loss. Move opposite that direction.

### 12.2 Backpropagation is the Chain Rule

Training a neural network requires computing $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$ where $\mathcal{L}$ is a composition of many functions (layers). This is precisely the chain rule applied to the computational graph.

For a network $\hat{y} = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{x})$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta^{(k)}} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \hat{y}} \cdot \frac{\partial \hat{y}}{\partial f_{L-1}} \cdot \frac{\partial f_{L-1}}{\partial f_{L-2}} \cdots \frac{\partial f_k}{\partial \theta^{(k)}}$$

**Backpropagation is the chain rule evaluated efficiently via dynamic programming** — computing the product from right to left, reusing intermediate values.

In matrix form, the chain rule for Jacobians is:

$$J_{\mathcal{L}, \theta^{(k)}} = J_{\mathcal{L}, f_L} \cdot J_{f_L, f_{L-1}} \cdots J_{f_k, \theta^{(k)}}$$

Every backward pass through a layer is a Jacobian-vector product.

### 12.3 Automatic Differentiation

Modern frameworks (PyTorch, JAX, TensorFlow) implement **automatic differentiation (autograd)** — not numerical differentiation (finite differences) nor symbolic differentiation, but exact differentiation via the chain rule on the computational graph.

Two modes:

- **Forward mode:** propagate $\dot{x}$ (tangent vector) forward through the graph. Efficient when $n \ll m$ (few inputs, many outputs).
- **Reverse mode (backprop):** propagate $\bar{y}$ (adjoint/gradient) backward through the graph. Efficient when $m \ll n$ (few outputs — as in scalar loss — many inputs). This is why backprop is used.

The derivative you write in calculus class — the limit of a difference quotient — is what autograd computes, exactly, for every parameter simultaneously.

### 12.4 Second Derivatives: Curvature and Optimization

The **Hessian** $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$ is the matrix of second partial derivatives:

$$H_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}$$

**Newton's method** uses the Hessian to take curvature-aware steps:

$$\theta \leftarrow \theta - H^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}$$

This converges much faster than gradient descent near a minimum (quadratic vs. linear convergence). But computing and inverting an $n \times n$ Hessian for $n = 10^9$ is catastrophically expensive.

**Quasi-Newton methods** (L-BFGS, Adam) approximate $H^{-1}$ cheaply:

- **Adam** uses running estimates of the gradient mean (first moment) and gradient squared (second moment — diagonal Hessian approximation) to adapt step sizes per parameter.

The second derivative test from single-variable calculus — $f''(c) > 0$ at a critical point means local min — generalizes to: $H$ is positive definite at a critical point means local min. This is what determines whether gradient descent has found a true minimum.

### 12.5 Directional Derivative and the Gradient

The **directional derivative** in direction $\mathbf{u}$ (unit vector):

$$D_\mathbf{u} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u} = |\nabla f| \cos\theta$$

This is maximized when $\mathbf{u} = \nabla f / |\nabla f|$ — the gradient direction. Gradient descent moves in this direction (with a sign flip for minimization).

In deep learning, understanding _which direction to move_ in parameter space is exactly this computation.

### 12.6 The Jacobian in Neural Networks

For a layer $\mathbf{z} = W\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{b}$ followed by activation $\mathbf{h} = \sigma(\mathbf{z})$:

$$J_{\mathbf{h}, \mathbf{a}} = \text{diag}(\sigma'(\mathbf{z})) \cdot W$$

During backprop, the gradient is pre-multiplied by this Jacobian. If $\sigma'(\mathbf{z})$ is near zero (sigmoid saturation), the Jacobian has small entries and the gradient is crushed — the **vanishing gradient** problem, now expressed as a Jacobian becoming nearly zero.

Batch normalization, residual connections (ResNets), and careful weight initialization all address this by keeping Jacobians well-conditioned through the depth of the network.

### 12.7 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

PINNs encode physical laws (ODEs/PDEs) as constraints on the neural network's derivatives. The loss function penalizes violations of the differential equation:

$$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\mathcal{L}_{\text{data}}}_{\text{fit data}} + \underbrace{\lambda,\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}}}_{\text{derivatives must satisfy PDE}}$$

where $\mathcal{L}_{\text{PDE}}$ involves the actual partial derivatives of the network output (computed via autograd). The derivative — the calculus object — appears inside the loss being minimized.

## §14. Things Worth Losing Sleep Over

- A function with $f'(c) = 0$ need not have a local extremum there. ($f(x) = x^3$ at $x = 0$: inflection point, not extremum.)
- Partial derivatives can exist everywhere while the function is **not** differentiable. (The partial derivatives exist but the linear approximation fails.)
- $\partial^2 f / \partial x \partial y = \partial^2 f / \partial y \partial x$ (Clairaut's theorem) requires continuity of the mixed partials — it is not automatic.
- The Jacobian of a composition is the product of Jacobians — this is the chain rule, the product rule, and matrix multiplication unified into one theorem.
- Weierstrass constructed a function that is continuous **everywhere** but differentiable **nowhere**. It looks like noise at every scale. This shattered the 19th-century intuition that continuity implied differentiability "almost everywhere."

## §15. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — From Definition

Find $f'(x)$ for $f(x) = x^2$ using the definition.

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{(x+h)^2 - x^2}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{2xh + h^2}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}(2x + h) = 2x$$

### Example 2 — Chain Rule (nested)

Differentiate $y = \ln(\sin(e^x))$.

$$y' = \frac{1}{\sin(e^x)} \cdot \cos(e^x) \cdot e^x = \frac{e^x \cos(e^x)}{\sin(e^x)} = e^x \cot(e^x)$$

### Example 3 — Implicit Differentiation

Find $y'$ for $e^{xy} = x + y$.

Differentiate both sides w.r.t. $x$:

$$e^{xy}(y + xy') = 1 + y'$$

$$y e^{xy} + xy' e^{xy} = 1 + y'$$

$$y'(xe^{xy} - 1) = 1 - ye^{xy}$$

$$y' = \frac{1 - ye^{xy}}{xe^{xy} - 1}$$

### Example 4 — Linear Approximation

Estimate $\tan(46°)$.

$f(x) = \tan x$, $a = \pi/4$, $f'(x) = \sec^2 x$, $f'(\pi/4) = 2$.

$46° = \pi/4 + \pi/180$.

$$\tan(46°) \approx \tan(45°) + 2 \cdot \frac{\pi}{180} = 1 + \frac{\pi}{90} \approx 1.0349$$

(Exact: $1.0355...$)
