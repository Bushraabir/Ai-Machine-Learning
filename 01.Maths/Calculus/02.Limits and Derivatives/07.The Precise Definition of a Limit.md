## ¬ß0. Historical Motivation ‚Äî Why We Needed Precision

Newton and Leibniz invented calculus in the 1660s‚Äì1680s. They spoke of "infinitely small quantities" ‚Äî quantities that are nonzero when you divide by them, but vanish when you're done. Bishop Berkeley famously mocked these as _"the ghosts of departed quantities."_

For nearly 200 years, calculus worked brilliantly in practice but rested on a philosophical swamp. The precise $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition was the work of **Augustin-Louis Cauchy** (1820s) and **Karl Weierstrass** (1850s‚Äì1860s). It banished infinity from the _definition_ and replaced it with a game between two real numbers.

This is that definition.

---

## ¬ß1. Intuition First ‚Äî The Approach Game

Before the symbols, the idea.

Saying $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ means:

> **You can force $f(x)$ arbitrarily close to $L$ by keeping $x$ sufficiently close to $a$ (but not equal to $a$).**

Think of it as a two-player game:

- **Player $\varepsilon$ (the Challenger):** Names a tolerance. _"I want $f(x)$ within $\varepsilon$ of $L$."_ They're trying to make the task hard by choosing $\varepsilon$ very small.
- **Player $\delta$ (the Defender):** Must respond with a radius. _"Stay within $\delta$ of $a$, and I guarantee $f(x)$ is within $\varepsilon$ of $L$."_

**The limit exists** $\iff$ the Defender can _always_ win ‚Äî no matter how small $\varepsilon$ the Challenger names, a valid $\delta$ exists.

## ¬ß2. The Formal Definition

> **Definition (Stewart ¬ß2.4 / MIT 18.02).** Let $f$ be a function defined on some open interval containing $a$, except possibly at $a$ itself. We write $$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$$ and say _"the limit of $f(x)$ as $x$ approaches $a$ equals $L$"_ if:
> 
> $$\forall, \varepsilon > 0,\quad \exists, \delta > 0 \text{ such that } \quad 0 < |x - a| < \delta \implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon.$$

Every symbol earns its place. Let's dissect.

---

## ¬ß3. Anatomy of the Definition ‚Äî Symbol by Symbol

### $\forall, \varepsilon > 0$ ‚Äî "For every epsilon greater than zero"

- $\varepsilon$ (epsilon) measures **closeness in the output** ‚Äî how close $f(x)$ must be to $L$.
- The quantifier $\forall$ means the Defender must handle _every_ challenge, including $\varepsilon = 0.001$, $\varepsilon = 10^{-100}$, and $\varepsilon = 10^{-10^{10}}$.
- We require $\varepsilon > 0$ because $\varepsilon = 0$ would demand _exact_ equality ‚Äî impossible for a limit that's only approached, not attained.

### $\exists, \delta > 0$ ‚Äî "There exists a delta greater than zero"

- $\delta$ (delta) measures **closeness in the input** ‚Äî how close $x$ must be to $a$.
- Crucially, $\delta$ is allowed to **depend on $\varepsilon$**. For a smaller $\varepsilon$, you may need a smaller $\delta$. This is not a defect ‚Äî it's the whole point.
- $\delta > 0$ ensures we're looking at a genuine (non-degenerate) neighborhood.

### $0 < |x - a| < \delta$ ‚Äî The punctured neighborhood

- $|x - a| < \delta$ says $x$ is within $\delta$ of $a$.
- $0 < |x - a|$ says **$x \neq a$**. This is the _puncture_. We are taking a limit _as $x$ approaches $a$_, not _at_ $a$.

> **This single condition is why limits and function values are independent.**
> 
> Whether $f(a)$ is defined, undefined, or even $f(a) \neq L$ is completely irrelevant. The limit is a statement about the _approach_, not the _arrival_.

### $\implies |f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$ ‚Äî The guarantee

- $|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon$ says $f(x)$ is within $\varepsilon$ of $L$.
- The implication $\implies$ means: _every_ $x$ in the punctured $\delta$-neighborhood must produce an $f(x)$ in the $\varepsilon$-neighborhood of $L$.

---

## ¬ß4. The Logical Structure ‚Äî Unpacked

The full statement is a nested quantifier:

$$\underbrace{\forall, \varepsilon > 0}_{\text{Challenger picks}},\quad \underbrace{\exists, \delta > 0}_{\text{Defender responds}},\quad \underbrace{\forall, x}_{\text{for all inputs}}\colon \underbrace{0 < |x-a| < \delta}_{\text{in the punctured neighborhood}} \implies \underbrace{|f(x) - L| < \varepsilon}_{\text{output is controlled}}$$

The **order of quantifiers is non-negotiable.** Swapping $\forall\varepsilon$ and $\exists\delta$ would produce a meaningless or different statement. In mathematics, quantifier order is everything.

Compare:

- $\forall \varepsilon > 0,; \exists \delta > 0: \ldots$ ‚Äî _$\delta$ may depend on $\varepsilon$_ ‚úì (this is the definition)
- $\exists \delta > 0,; \forall \varepsilon > 0: \ldots$ ‚Äî _one $\delta$ works for all $\varepsilon$_ ‚Äî this would be much stronger and almost never true.

## ¬ß5. Three Worked $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ Proofs

The proof strategy is always the same:

1. **Scratch work** (not part of the proof): manipulate $|f(x)-L|$ to find what $\delta$ should be.
2. **Formal proof**: Given $\varepsilon > 0$, state your $\delta$, then verify the implication holds.

---

### Proof 1 ‚Äî Linear Function

**Claim:** $\lim_{x \to 3}(2x - 1) = 5$.

**Scratch work.** We need $|(2x-1) - 5| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $|2x - 6| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $2|x-3| < \varepsilon$, i.e., $|x-3| < \varepsilon/2$. So choose $\delta = \varepsilon/2$.

**Proof.** Given $\varepsilon > 0$, let $\delta = \varepsilon/2$. Suppose $0 < |x-3| < \delta$. Then: $$|(2x-1) - 5| = |2x - 6| = 2|x-3| < 2\delta = 2 \cdot \frac{\varepsilon}{2} = \varepsilon. \qquad \blacksquare$$

---

### Proof 2 ‚Äî Quadratic (Requires Bounding)

**Claim:** $\lim_{x \to 2} x^2 = 4$.

**Scratch work.** We need $|x^2 - 4| < \varepsilon$. Factor: $|x^2-4| = |x-2||x+2|$. We control $|x-2|$ (it's $< \delta$), but $|x+2|$ floats.

**Bound $|x+2|$:** Restrict $\delta \leq 1$ initially. Then $|x-2|<1 \implies 1 < x < 3 \implies 3 < x+2 < 5 \implies |x+2| < 5$.

So $|x^2 - 4| < 5|x-2| < 5\delta$. We want $5\delta \leq \varepsilon$, so $\delta \leq \varepsilon/5$.

Take $\delta = \min(1, \varepsilon/5)$ to satisfy both constraints simultaneously.

**Proof.** Given $\varepsilon > 0$, let $\delta = \min(1, \varepsilon/5)$. Suppose $0 < |x-2| < \delta$.

Since $\delta \leq 1$, we have $|x-2| < 1$, so $1 < x < 3$, hence $|x+2| < 5$.

Therefore: $$|x^2 - 4| = |x-2||x+2| < \delta \cdot 5 \leq \frac{\varepsilon}{5} \cdot 5 = \varepsilon. \qquad \blacksquare$$

> **The $\min$ trick** appears again. Whenever we need $\delta$ to satisfy two simultaneous conditions, we take the minimum. This is the standard move in analysis.

---

### Proof 3 ‚Äî Limit Does Not Exist ($\lim_{x \to 0} \sin(1/x)$)

Sometimes the most instructive proof is a _disproof_.

**Claim:** $\lim_{x \to 0} \sin(1/x)$ does not exist.

**Proof (by negating the definition).** The negation of $\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L$ is:

$$\exists, \varepsilon_0 > 0 \text{ such that } \forall, \delta > 0,; \exists, x \text{ with } 0 < |x| < \delta \text{ and } |f(x) - L| \geq \varepsilon_0.$$

For any proposed limit $L$, choose $\varepsilon_0 = 1/2$.

For any $\delta > 0$, we can find $n \in \mathbb{Z}$ large enough so that: $$x_1 = \frac{1}{2\pi n + \pi/2} \quad \text{and} \quad x_2 = \frac{1}{2\pi n + 3\pi/2}$$ both lie in $(0, \delta)$, with $f(x_1) = \sin(2\pi n + \pi/2) = 1$ and $f(x_2) = \sin(2\pi n + 3\pi/2) = -1$.

Since $f$ takes values $1$ and $-1$ in every punctured neighborhood of $0$, it cannot be within $1/2$ of any single value $L$ for all such $x$. Hence no limit exists. $\blacksquare$

## ¬ß6. What "Except Possibly at $a$" Really Means

The definition requires $f$ to be defined on a _punctured_ neighborhood of $a$ ‚Äî some interval $(a-r, a+r) \setminus {a}$ ‚Äî but explicitly _not_ at $a$ itself.

This handles three cases uniformly:

|Case|$f(a)$|$\lim_{x\to a} f(x)$|Example|
|---|---|---|---|
|Defined, matches limit|$= L$|$= L$|$f(x) = x^2$ at $a=2$|
|Defined, doesn't match|$\neq L$|$= L$|$g(x) = \begin{cases}x^2 & x\neq 2\ 99 & x=2\end{cases}$|
|Undefined at $a$|DNE|$= L$|$\frac{\sin x}{x}$ at $a=0$|

In all three cases, $\lim_{x\to 2}$ or $\lim_{x\to 0}$ may still equal $L$. The limit is blind to $f(a)$.

## ¬ß7. One-Sided Limits ‚Äî Modifying the Definition

The two-sided limit requires control from both sides. We modify the punctured neighborhood:

**Right-hand limit:** $$\lim_{x \to a^+} f(x) = L \iff \forall,\varepsilon>0,;\exists,\delta>0: \mathbf{0 < x - a < \delta} \implies |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon$$

**Left-hand limit:** $$\lim_{x \to a^-} f(x) = L \iff \forall,\varepsilon>0,;\exists,\delta>0: \mathbf{-\delta < x - a < 0} \implies |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon$$

**Theorem.** $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \iff \lim_{x\to a^-}f(x) = L ;\text{ and }; \lim_{x \to a^+}f(x) = L$.

---

## ¬ß8. Infinite Limits and Limits at Infinity ‚Äî The Same Idea, New Neighborhoods

### Infinite Limit: $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = +\infty$

$$\forall, M > 0,;\exists,\delta>0: 0 < |x-a|<\delta \implies f(x) > M$$

The role of $\varepsilon$ is now played by $M$ ‚Äî the Challenger names a height, the Defender finds a neighborhood where $f$ exceeds it.

### Limit at Infinity: $\lim_{x \to \infty} f(x) = L$

$$\forall,\varepsilon>0,;\exists, N>0: x > N \implies |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon$$

Here $\delta$ is replaced by $N$ ‚Äî instead of a neighborhood around a point, the Defender gives a threshold beyond which $f(x)$ is controlled.

All four combinations ($a$ finite/infinite, $L$ finite/infinite) have analogous definitions ‚Äî the pattern is always: _replace the relevant neighborhood with the appropriate notion of "close" or "large."_

---

## ¬ß9. Uniqueness of Limits

**Theorem.** If $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L$ and $\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = M$, then $L = M$.

**Proof.** Suppose $L \neq M$; WLOG $L < M$. Let $\varepsilon = (M-L)/2 > 0$.

By the definition applied to $L$: $\exists,\delta_1$ such that $0<|x-a|<\delta_1 \implies |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon$, i.e., $f(x) < L+\varepsilon$.

By the definition applied to $M$: $\exists,\delta_2$ such that $0<|x-a|<\delta_2 \implies |f(x)-M|<\varepsilon$, i.e., $f(x) > M-\varepsilon$.

But $L + \varepsilon = L + \frac{M-L}{2} = \frac{L+M}{2}$ and $M - \varepsilon = \frac{L+M}{2}$.

So for $0 < |x-a| < \min(\delta_1,\delta_2)$: $f(x) < \frac{L+M}{2}$ and $f(x) > \frac{L+M}{2}$ simultaneously ‚Äî contradiction. $\blacksquare$

> **Why this matters:** Uniqueness is the reason we can _write_ $\lim_{x\to a} f(x) = L$ with an equals sign. The limit is a _function_ from (function, point) pairs to real numbers ‚Äî it must be single-valued.

## ¬ß10. Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 10.1 The Definition Lives Inside Every Derivative

The weight update in gradient descent is: $$w \leftarrow w - \eta \cdot \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w}$$

That partial derivative is, by definition: $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial w} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{\mathcal{L}(w+h) - \mathcal{L}(w)}{h}$$

Every time a GPU computes a backward pass, it is numerically approximating this limit. The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition is what guarantees the limit _exists_ (and thus the derivative is well-defined) for the smooth loss functions used in practice.

**Numerical differentiation** (finite differences) is literally choosing a small $h = \delta$ and hoping the resulting $f(x+h) - f(x))/h$ is within $\varepsilon$ of the true derivative. The precision-stability tradeoff in choosing $h$ is the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ game made computational.

### 10.2 The Puncture Condition and $0/0$ in Softmax / Cross-Entropy

The cross-entropy loss is $\mathcal{L} = -\log(\hat{p}_y)$ where $\hat{p}_y$ is the softmax probability of the true class. When $\hat{p}_y \to 0^+$, $\mathcal{L} \to +\infty$. This is an infinite limit: $$\lim_{\hat{p} \to 0^+} (-\log \hat{p}) = +\infty$$

The **puncture** ($\hat{p} \neq 0$ required) corresponds to the practical need for numerical clipping: $\hat{p} = \max(\hat{p}, \varepsilon_{\text{clip}})$ for some small $\varepsilon_{\text{clip}} > 0$ ‚Äî exactly choosing a $\delta > 0$ to stay away from the problematic point.

### 10.3 Convergence Proofs in Optimization

When theoreticians prove that stochastic gradient descent converges, they must show: $$\lim_{t \to \infty} \mathbb{E}[|\nabla \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}_t)|^2] = 0$$

This is the $\varepsilon$-$N$ definition: for every desired precision $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists a number of steps $N$ such that after $N$ steps, the expected gradient norm is below $\varepsilon$. The entire convergence theory of deep learning is the $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ framework applied to sequences.

### 10.4 Quantifier Order and Uniform vs. Pointwise Convergence

In deep learning theory, one distinguishes:

- **Pointwise convergence** of a sequence of models $f_n \to f$: for each input $x$, $\lim_{n\to\infty}f_n(x) = f(x)$.
- **Uniform convergence**: $\lim_{n\to\infty}\sup_x|f_n(x)-f(x)| = 0$.

The difference is **exactly quantifier order**:

$$\text{Pointwise: }\forall x,;\forall\varepsilon>0,;\exists N: n>N \implies |f_n(x)-f(x)|<\varepsilon$$ $$\text{Uniform: }\forall\varepsilon>0,;\exists N,;\forall x: n>N \implies |f_n(x)-f(x)|<\varepsilon$$

Uniform convergence (where $N$ doesn't depend on $x$) is what PAC-learning theory and generalization bounds require. This distinction ‚Äî invisible without the precise definition ‚Äî is foundational to statistical learning theory.

## ¬ß11. The Negation ‚Äî When Limits Fail

The precise definition makes it possible to rigorously prove a limit _does not_ exist. Negate the statement:

$$\lim_{x \to a} f(x) \neq L \iff \exists,\varepsilon_0 > 0 \text{ such that } \forall,\delta>0,;\exists, x: 0<|x-a|<\delta ;\text{ and }; |f(x)-L|\geq\varepsilon_0$$

And the limit fails to exist _for any_ $L$ iff this holds for every candidate $L \in \mathbb{R}$.

## ¬ß12. Summary ‚Äî The Definition, Every Piece

$$\boxed{\lim_{x \to a} f(x) = L \iff \forall,\varepsilon>0,;\exists,\delta>0: 0 < |x-a|<\delta \implies |f(x)-L|<\varepsilon}$$

|Piece|Measures|Role|
|---|---|---|
|$\varepsilon > 0$|Output tolerance|The Challenger's demand|
|$\delta > 0$|Input radius|The Defender's response|
|$0 < \|x-a\| < \delta$|Punctured $\delta$-neighborhood|Where $x$ lives (never $= a$)|
|$\|f(x)-L\| < \varepsilon$|Output control|What must be guaranteed|
|$\delta$ depends on $\varepsilon$|‚Äî|Fundamental flexibility of the definition|
|$f(a)$ irrelevant|‚Äî|Limit $\perp$ function value at $a$|

3. Prove $\lim_{x \to 2} \frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{2}$ from the definition. _(Hint: bound $1/|x|$ from above by restricting $\delta \leq 1$.)_

**Conceptual:** 4. Write out the precise negation of $\lim_{x\to 0^+}\sqrt{x} = 0$. Then explain why the negation is false (i.e., prove the limit _does_ equal $0$). 5. **Proof:** 6. Prove: if $\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = L$ and $L > 0$, then there exists $\delta > 0$ such that $f(x) > 0$ for all $x$ in the punctured $\delta$-neighborhood of $a$. _(This is the "sign-preservation" lemma ‚Äî used constantly in analysis.)_
