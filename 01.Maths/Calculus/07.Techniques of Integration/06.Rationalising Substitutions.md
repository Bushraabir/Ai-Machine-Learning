

> _"The art of integration is the art of recognising which disguise the integrand is wearing."_ — a sentiment shared by every analyst who has stared at $\int \frac{dx}{1+\sqrt{x}}$ for too long.

---

## 0. What Problem Are We Actually Solving?

An integrand is **irrational** when it contains an algebraic expression that cannot be written as a ratio of polynomials — typically because of a radical such as $\sqrt[n]{,\cdot,}$ or a fractional power. Standard techniques (partial fractions, IBP) require rational functions. The strategy is therefore:

> **Introduce a new variable $t$ that kills the radical, transforming the integral into a rational one, which we can then attack by partial fractions.**

This is the entirety of the method. Everything else is bookkeeping.

---

## 1. Taxonomy of Rationalising Substitutions

### 1.1 Simple Root: $\sqrt[n]{ax+b}$

**Setting:** The integrand involves $\sqrt[n]{ax+b}$ for integers $n \ge 2$.

**Substitution:** Set $$t = \sqrt[n]{ax+b} \implies x = \frac{t^n - b}{a}, \quad dx = \frac{n,t^{n-1}}{a},dt.$$

**Why it works:** After substitution every occurrence of $\sqrt[n]{ax+b}$ becomes $t$, and $dx$ introduces a polynomial in $t$. The resulting integrand is rational in $t$.

#### Example 1.1

Compute $\displaystyle I = \int \frac{dx}{1 + \sqrt{x}}$.

**Set** $t = \sqrt{x}$, so $x = t^2$, $dx = 2t,dt$: $$I = \int \frac{2t,dt}{1+t} = 2\int \frac{t}{1+t},dt.$$

Write $\dfrac{t}{1+t} = 1 - \dfrac{1}{1+t}$: $$I = 2\int!\left(1 - \frac{1}{1+t}\right)dt = 2\bigl[t - \ln|1+t|\bigr] + C.$$

Back-substitute $t = \sqrt{x}$: $$\boxed{I = 2\sqrt{x} - 2\ln(1+\sqrt{x}) + C.}$$

---

#### Example 1.2

Compute $\displaystyle I = \int \frac{\sqrt[3]{x}}{1+\sqrt[3]{x^2}},dx$.

**Set** $t = \sqrt[3]{x}$, so $x = t^3$, $dx = 3t^2,dt$. Also $\sqrt[3]{x^2} = t^2$: $$I = \int \frac{t}{1+t^2}\cdot 3t^2,dt = 3\int \frac{t^3}{1+t^2},dt.$$

Polynomial long division: $\dfrac{t^3}{1+t^2} = t - \dfrac{t}{1+t^2}$, so $$I = 3\int!\left(t - \frac{t}{1+t^2}\right)dt = 3\left[\frac{t^2}{2} - \frac{1}{2}\ln(1+t^2)\right] + C.$$

Back-substitute: $$\boxed{I = \frac{3}{2}x^{2/3} - \frac{3}{2}\ln!\left(1+x^{2/3}\right) + C.}$$

---

### 1.2 Two Different Roots: $\sqrt[m]{\cdot}$ and $\sqrt[n]{\cdot}$

When two distinct root orders appear, choose $t^k$ where $k = \operatorname{lcm}(m,n)$. This single substitution simultaneously rationalises both radicals.

**Substitution:** $t^k = ax+b$, where $k = \operatorname{lcm}(m,n)$.

#### Example 1.3

Compute $\displaystyle I = \int \frac{dx}{\sqrt{x} + \sqrt[3]{x}}$.

Here $m=2$, $n=3$, so $k = \operatorname{lcm}(2,3) = 6$. Set $t^6 = x$, $dx = 6t^5,dt$: $$\sqrt{x} = t^3,\quad \sqrt[3]{x} = t^2.$$ $$I = \int \frac{6t^5,dt}{t^3 + t^2} = 6\int \frac{t^5}{t^2(t+1)},dt = 6\int \frac{t^3}{t+1},dt.$$

Long division: $\dfrac{t^3}{t+1} = t^2 - t + 1 - \dfrac{1}{t+1}$, so $$I = 6\left[\frac{t^3}{3} - \frac{t^2}{2} + t - \ln|t+1|\right] + C.$$

Back-substitute $t = x^{1/6}$: $$\boxed{I = 2\sqrt{x} - 3x^{1/3} + 6x^{1/6} - 6\ln!\left(1+x^{1/6}\right) + C.}$$

---

### 1.3 Quadratic Under the Root: $\sqrt{ax^2 + bx + c}$

The substitution depends on the sign of $a$ and the discriminant $\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$. Complete the square first: $$ax^2 + bx + c = a!\left(x + \frac{b}{2a}\right)^{!2} + \frac{4ac - b^2}{4a}.$$

After translation $u = x + b/(2a)$, the radicand reduces to one of three forms. For each, a trigonometric or hyperbolic substitution rationalises the root (in the generalised sense of removing the square root):

|Reduced form|Substitution|Identity used|
|---|---|---|
|$u^2 + p^2$|$u = p\tan\theta$|$1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$|
|$p^2 - u^2$|$u = p\sin\theta$|$1 - \sin^2\theta = \cos^2\theta$|
|$u^2 - p^2$|$u = p\sec\theta$|$\sec^2\theta - 1 = \tan^2\theta$|

> **Note.** Trig substitutions are sometimes called _rationalising_ because they convert $\sqrt{\text{quadratic}}$ into a trig polynomial — no longer an algebraic radical. (See Stewart §7.3 and MIT 18.02 notes.)

---

### 1.4 The Weierstrass Substitution (Universal for Trig Rationals)

**Setting:** Integrands of the form $R(\sin x, \cos x)$ — rational in $\sin x$ and $\cos x$.

**Substitution:** $$t = \tan!\frac{x}{2}, \qquad x \in (-\pi, \pi).$$

Then: $$\sin x = \frac{2t}{1+t^2},\quad \cos x = \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2},\quad dx = \frac{2,dt}{1+t^2}.$$

Every occurrence of $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ becomes rational in $t$, and $dx$ is rational in $t$.

#### Derivation (for the notebook)

From the half-angle identities: $\sin x = 2\sin\tfrac{x}{2}\cos\tfrac{x}{2}$ and $\cos x = \cos^2\tfrac{x}{2} - \sin^2\tfrac{x}{2}$. Divide numerator and denominator by $\cos^2\tfrac{x}{2}$: $$\sin x = \frac{2\tan\tfrac{x}{2}}{\sec^2\tfrac{x}{2}} = \frac{2t}{1+t^2},\qquad \cos x = \frac{1-\tan^2\tfrac{x}{2}}{\sec^2\tfrac{x}{2}} = \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2}.$$

For $dx$: since $t = \tan(x/2)$, we have $dt = \tfrac{1}{2}\sec^2(x/2),dx$, so $dx = \dfrac{2,dt}{\sec^2(x/2)} = \dfrac{2,dt}{1+t^2}$.

#### Example 1.4

Compute $\displaystyle I = \int \frac{dx}{1 + \sin x}$.

$$I = \int \frac{1}{1 + \frac{2t}{1+t^2}} \cdot \frac{2,dt}{1+t^2} = \int \frac{2,dt}{(1+t)^2} = -\frac{2}{1+t} + C = -\frac{2}{1+\tan(x/2)} + C.$$

#### Example 1.5

Compute $\displaystyle I = \int \frac{d\theta}{2 + \cos\theta}$.

$$I = \int \frac{1}{2 + \frac{1-t^2}{1+t^2}} \cdot \frac{2,dt}{1+t^2} = \int \frac{2,dt}{2(1+t^2) + (1-t^2)} = \int \frac{2,dt}{3+t^2}.$$

$$= \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\arctan!\frac{t}{\sqrt{3}} + C = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}\arctan!\left(\frac{\tan(\theta/2)}{\sqrt{3}}\right) + C.$$

## 2. The Procedure — A Rigorous Framework

Given $\int f(x),dx$ involving a radical $\sqrt[n]{g(x)}$:

1. **Identify** the simplest radical expression $r(x)$ present.
2. **Set** $t = r(x)$, invert to get $x = \phi(t)$, compute $dx = \phi'(t),dt$.
3. **Substitute** everywhere: $\int f(\phi(t)),\phi'(t),dt$. Verify the integrand is rational in $t$.
4. **Decompose** via partial fractions if needed.
5. **Integrate** term by term.
6. **Back-substitute** $t = r(x)$.

> **Theorem (Change of Variables, Rigorous Form).** Let $\phi: [c,d] \to [a,b]$ be continuously differentiable and bijective with $\phi(c)=a$, $\phi(d)=b$. If $f$ is continuous on $[a,b]$, then $$\int_a^b f(x),dx = \int_c^d f(\phi(t)),\phi'(t),dt.$$ _(Stewart, §5.5; the indefinite version holds wherever $f \circ \phi$ and $\phi'$ are continuous.)_

## 3. Visualisation

### 3.1 Geometric Picture for $t = \sqrt{x}$

The substitution $t = \sqrt{x}$ "unfolds" the curve $y = f(x)$ over $[0, R^2]$ into the curve $y = f(t^2)\cdot 2t$ over $[0, R]$. The area is the same; the shape changes. The key insight is that the Jacobian $dx = 2t,dt$ compensates exactly for the change in domain.

```
x-domain:   0 ────────────────────── R²
                    (compressed near 0)
                        ↕ t = √x
t-domain:   0 ───────────── R
                    (stretched near 0, 2t factor)
```

The factor $2t$ vanishing at $t=0$ reflects the fact that the square root "compresses" $x$ near $0$ — the substitution stretches it back.

### 3.2 Weierstrass as Stereographic Projection

The substitution $t = \tan(x/2)$ has a beautiful geometric interpretation: it is the **stereographic projection** of the unit circle onto the real line, with the projection point at $(-1, 0)$.

```
         Unit Circle
              · (0,1)
             /|
            / |  sin x
           /  |
(-1,0)----/---+------·-------> t-axis
    proj  |   |cos x  
          |   +------ (cos x, sin x)
          · (0,-1)
```

A point $(\cos x, \sin x)$ on the circle is connected to $(-1, 0)$, and the line meets the $t$-axis at $t = \tan(x/2)$. This is why $t$ parametrises **every** rational combination of $\sin x$ and $\cos x$ — the rational map from a circle to a line via stereographic projection.

## 4. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

This is not merely a historical curiosity. Rationalising substitutions appear in modern machine learning in several guises.

### 4.1 Change of Variables in Normalising Flows

A **normalising flow** is a generative model that transforms a simple base distribution $p_Z(z)$ (e.g., $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$) into a complex target $p_X(x)$ via an invertible, differentiable map $f: z \mapsto x = f(z)$.

The exact density is given by the **change-of-variables formula**: $$p_X(x) = p_Z!\left(f^{-1}(x)\right) \cdot \left|\det J_{f^{-1}}(x)\right|,$$ where $J_{f^{-1}}$ is the Jacobian of $f^{-1}$. This is the multidimensional analogue of $dx = \phi'(t),dt$.

A **rational quadratic spline flow** (used in state-of-the-art flows) literally constructs $f$ piecewise as a ratio of quadratic polynomials — a direct descendant of rational function theory. The bijectivity and differentiability requirements are exactly the conditions in the Change of Variables Theorem.

> The Jacobian computation (the "cost" of a flow layer) is precisely the $\phi'(t)$ term in our substitution formula.

### 4.2 Kernel Methods and Rational Approximations

Support Vector Machines (SVMs) and Gaussian Processes use **kernel functions** $k(x, x')$. A common kernel is the **RBF (Radial Basis Function)**: $$k(x,x') = \exp!\left(-\frac{|x - x'|^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).$$

When approximating kernels via **random Fourier features** (Rahimi & Recht, 2007), one needs to compute integrals of the form $\int \hat{k}(\omega),e^{i\omega^\top(x-x')},d\omega$ where $\hat{k}$ is the Fourier transform. For the Matérn kernel, $\hat{k}$ involves expressions like $(1 + |\omega|^2/\nu)^{-(\nu + d/2)}$ — a rational function of $|\omega|^2$, and integration proceeds by partial fractions in spherical coordinates.

### 4.3 Activation Functions and Integration

In **Bayesian deep learning**, one must often compute the **Gaussian expectation** of an activation function $\sigma$: $$\mathbb{E}_{z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, v)}!\left[\sigma(z)\right] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \sigma(z) \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi v}} e^{-(z-\mu)^2/(2v)},dz.$$

For the sigmoid $\sigma(z) = (1 + e^{-z})^{-1}$, this integral has no closed form. But using a **probit approximation** $\sigma(z) \approx \Phi(\lambda z)$ (where $\Phi$ is the Gaussian CDF), one can compute it analytically — again by completing the square in the exponent (a polynomial/algebraic manipulation in the integrand), the same algebraic manoeuvre underlying quadratic rationalisation.

### 4.4 Score-Based Diffusion Models

Score-based diffusion models (Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models, DDPMs) require integration over noise schedules. The log-likelihood involves integrals of the form: $$\mathcal{L} = -\int_0^T \beta(t),\mathbb{E}!\left[|s_\theta(x_t, t)|^2\right]dt,$$ where $\beta(t)$ is a noise schedule (e.g., $\beta(t) = \beta_{\min} + t(\beta_{\max} - \beta_{\min})$). Changing variables $s = \bar\alpha(t) = \exp!\left(-\int_0^t \beta(\tau),d\tau\right)$ is a _rationalising substitution_ in spirit — it transforms a complicated integral over $t$ into a simpler one over the signal-to-noise ratio $s$.

### 4.5 Summary Table

|ML Context|Rationalising Substitution Role|
|---|---|
|Normalising Flows|Change of variables formula $p_X = p_Z \cdot|
|Kernel Approximations|Rational form of spectral densities; partial fractions|
|Bayesian Deep Learning|Completing the square in Gaussian integrals|
|Diffusion Models|Noise-schedule change of variable|
|Variational Inference (ELBO)|Reparametrisation trick: $z = \mu + \sigma\epsilon$|

The **reparametrisation trick** in variational autoencoders (VAEs) is literally the substitution $z = \mu + \sigma\epsilon$ (where $\epsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$), transforming an expectation with respect to a parameter-dependent distribution into one over a fixed distribution — enabling gradient flow through stochastic nodes.

## 5. Common Errors and Pitfalls

1. **Forgetting to transform $dx$.** After setting $t = \sqrt[n]{g(x)}$, one must compute $dx$ in terms of $dt$ and substitute it. Leaving $dx$ alone is the most common mistake.
    
2. **Domain issues.** The substitution $t = \sqrt{x}$ requires $x \ge 0$. For definite integrals, transform the limits: if $x: 0 \to 4$, then $t: 0 \to 2$.
    
3. **Weierstrass at $x = \pi$.** The substitution $t = \tan(x/2)$ is undefined at $x = \pm\pi$. For integrals over $[0, 2\pi]$, split at $\pi$ or use the alternative substitution $t = \cot(x/2)$.
    
4. **Wrong $\operatorname{lcm}$.** When two radicals are present, using $\operatorname{lcm}(m,n) - 1$ instead of $\operatorname{lcm}(m,n)$ will leave residual radicals.
    
5. **Neglecting absolute values.** When $\sqrt{t^{2n}} = |t^n|$, and $t^n$ can be negative, write $|t^n|$ and case-split or restrict the domain appropriately.
