## Motivation: Why Not Just Use Rectangles?

The Riemann sum and the Trapezoid Rule are morally honest — they work. But they waste information. When you approximate $f$ on $[a,b]$, you _have_ the function values at the endpoints of each sub-interval. Why throw away the curvature?

The central idea of Simpson's Rule: **fit a quadratic (parabola) through three consecutive points** and integrate the parabola exactly. Since integrals of polynomials are exact, you're only paying an error for the discrepancy between $f$ and its quadratic approximation — which is small.

> _Trapezoid Rule: linear fit per panel. Simpson's Rule: quadratic fit per panel. A free upgrade in accuracy for almost the same cost._

---

## Setup and Notation

Let $f$ be continuous on $[a, b]$. Divide $[a,b]$ into $n$ **equal** sub-intervals of width $$ h = \frac{b - a}{n}, \quad n \text{ even (mandatory).} $$ Label the nodes: $$ x_k = a + kh, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots, n. $$

Write $f_k := f(x_k)$ for short.

---

## Deriving the Rule (The Real Thing)

### Step 1 — Integrate a Quadratic Over One Pair of Panels

Consider the single sub-interval $[x_0, x_2]$ (two panels, three nodes: $x_0, x_1 = x_0 + h, x_2 = x_0 + 2h$).

There is a unique quadratic $p(x) = Ax^2 + Bx + C$ passing through $(x_0, f_0)$, $(x_1, f_1)$, $(x_2, f_2)$.

**Shift coordinates.** Let $t = x - x_1$ so the three nodes become $t = -h, 0, +h$ and: $$ p(-h) = f_0, \quad p(0) = f_1, \quad p(h) = f_2. $$

Write $p(t) = At^2 + Bt + C$. Then immediately:

- $p(0) = C = f_1$
- $p(h) + p(-h) = 2Ah^2 + 2C = f_0 + f_2 \implies Ah^2 = \dfrac{f_0 + f_2 - 2f_1}{2}$

Now integrate: $$ \int_{-h}^{h} p(t), dt = \int_{-h}^{h} (At^2 + Bt + C), dt. $$ The odd term $Bt$ vanishes by symmetry. So: $$ \int_{-h}^{h} p(t), dt = 2Ah^2 \cdot \frac{h}{1} \cdot \frac{2}{3} + 2Ch = \frac{2h}{3}\Bigl(Ah^2 \cdot 3 + 3C\Bigr). $$

Wait — let me be clean. Direct computation: $$ \int_{-h}^{h} (At^2 + C), dt = \frac{2Ah^3}{3} + 2Ch. $$ Substitute $Ah^2 = \frac{f_0 + f_2 - 2f_1}{2}$ and $C = f_1$: $$ = \frac{h}{3}(f_0 - 2f_1 + f_2) \cdot 2 + 2f_1 h = \frac{h}{3}(f_0 + 4f_1 + f_2). $$

Let me redo this cleanly: $$ \frac{2Ah^3}{3} + 2Ch = \frac{2h^3}{3} \cdot \frac{f_0+f_2-2f_1}{2h^2} + 2hf_1 = \frac{h(f_0+f_2-2f_1)}{3} + 2hf_1 = \frac{h}{3}(f_0 + 4f_1 + f_2). $$

$$\boxed{\int_{x_0}^{x_2} f(x), dx \approx \frac{h}{3}(f_0 + 4f_1 + f_2)}$$

The coefficient pattern **1-4-1** is burned into memory. Every pair of panels contributes this.

### Step 2 — Sum Over All Pairs

Apply the same formula to each consecutive triple $(x_{2k}, x_{2k+1}, x_{2k+2})$ for $k = 0, 1, \ldots, \frac{n}{2}-1$, then add:

$$ \int_a^b f(x), dx \approx S_n = \frac{h}{3}\Bigl[ f_0 + 4f_1 + 2f_2 + 4f_3 + 2f_4 + \cdots + 4f_{n-1} + f_n \Bigr]. $$

### The Compact Formula

$$ \boxed{ S_n = \frac{h}{3}\left[f_0 + f_n + 4\sum_{\substack{k=1 \ k \text{ odd}}}^{n-1} f_k + 2\sum_{\substack{k=2 \ k \text{ even}}}^{n-2} f_k\right] } $$

where $h = (b-a)/n$, $n$ even.

> _Mnemonic for coefficients: 1, 4, 2, 4, 2, …, 4, 1. Ends are 1; interior odd-indexed nodes get 4; interior even-indexed nodes get 2._

## Error Analysis (The Crucial Part)

### Per-Panel Error

On a single pair of panels $[x_{2k}, x_{2k+2}]$, the error of Simpson's Rule is: $$ E_k = -\frac{h^5}{90} f^{(4)}(\xi_k) $$ for some $\xi_k \in (x_{2k}, x_{2k+2})$. This requires $f \in C^4[a,b]$.

**Why $h^5$?** We fit a degree-2 polynomial but, by a happy accident (the symmetry of the integration cancels the cubic error term), the leading error is of order $h^5$, not $h^4$. This is sometimes called _superconvergence_ of Simpson's Rule.

### Global Error

Summing $n/2$ pairs: $$ E_n = -\frac{(b-a)h^4}{180} f^{(4)}(\xi), \quad \xi \in (a,b). $$

$$ \boxed{|E_n| \leq \frac{(b-a)^5}{180 n^4} \max_{x \in [a,b]} |f^{(4)}(x)|} $$

**Key observation:** The error is $O(h^4)$, while the Trapezoid Rule gives $O(h^2)$. **Double the number of panels → error drops by a factor of $2^4 = 16$ for Simpson, versus $2^2 = 4$ for Trapezoid.** This is a dramatic difference in practice.

|Rule|Global Error|Error order|
|---|---|---|
|Midpoint|$O(h^2)$|$f''$|
|Trapezoid|$O(h^2)$|$f''$|
|**Simpson's**|$\mathbf{O(h^4)}$|$\mathbf{f^{(4)}}$|
|Gaussian Quadrature ($n$ pts)|$O(h^{2n})$|high|

> _If $f$ is a polynomial of degree $\leq 3$, Simpson's Rule is **exact**. Check: the error term involves $f^{(4)}$, which vanishes._

## Worked Example

**Compute** $\displaystyle\int_0^1 e^{x^2}, dx$ using Simpson's Rule with $n = 4$.

$h = \frac{1-0}{4} = 0.25$. Nodes: $x_0=0,, x_1=0.25,, x_2=0.5,, x_3=0.75,, x_4=1$.

|$k$|$x_k$|$f_k = e^{x_k^2}$|Weight|
|---|---|---|---|
|0|0.00|1.000000|1|
|1|0.25|1.064494|4|
|2|0.50|1.284025|2|
|3|0.75|1.755054|4|
|4|1.00|2.718282|1|

$$ S_4 = \frac{0.25}{3}\bigl[1 + 4(1.064494) + 2(1.284025) + 4(1.755054) + 2.718282\bigr] $$ $$ = \frac{0.25}{3}\bigl[1 + 4.257976 + 2.568050 + 7.020216 + 2.718282\bigr] = \frac{0.25}{3} \times 17.564524 \approx 1.4637. $$

The true value is $\approx 1.4627$. Error $\approx 0.001$. Not bad for 5 function evaluations.

## Composite vs. Adaptive Simpson

The formula above is **Composite Simpson's Rule** — we partition $[a,b]$ into many sub-intervals and apply the basic rule on each.

**Adaptive Simpson's** recursively halves each sub-interval until the local error falls below a tolerance. This is the strategy used in `scipy.integrate.quad` (under the hood, it's essentially adaptive Gauss-Kronrod, a cousin). The idea: don't waste evaluations where $f$ is smooth; concentrate them where $f$ changes rapidly.

## Connection to Numerical Analysis: Degree of Precision

> _Definition._ A quadrature rule has **degree of precision** $d$ if it integrates all polynomials of degree $\leq d$ exactly but fails for degree $d+1$.

The Trapezoid Rule on $[a,b]$: degree of precision 1 (exact for linears).

Simpson's Rule on $[a,b]$: degree of precision **3** (exact for cubics, even though we only fit a parabola — again, that magical symmetry cancellation).

This is why the error involves $f^{(4)}$: the first derivative that Simpson cannot reproduce exactly is the 4th.

## Relation to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

This is where it gets genuinely interesting. Numerical integration sits at the heart of several corners of modern ML.

### 1. Computing Normalizing Constants (Bayesian Inference)

In Bayesian ML, the posterior is: $$ p(\theta \mid \mathcal{D}) = \frac{p(\mathcal{D} \mid \theta), p(\theta)}{\int p(\mathcal{D} \mid \theta), p(\theta), d\theta}. $$ The denominator — the **marginal likelihood** or _evidence_ — is a high-dimensional integral. For low-dimensional models (1D or 2D posteriors), composite Simpson's Rule or Gaussian quadrature directly approximates this. For high dimensions, one typically turns to MCMC or variational inference, but the principles are the same: discretize and approximate.

### 2. Numerical ODE Solvers → Neural ODEs

The **Neural ODE** (Chen et al., 2018) models the hidden state $h(t)$ of a network as a continuous-time ODE: $$ \frac{dh}{dt} = f_\theta(h(t), t). $$ To **forward-pass** through the network, you must integrate this ODE from $t_0$ to $t_1$. The solver used is typically Runge-Kutta 4/5 (Dormand-Prince), which is a sophisticated adaptive cousin of Simpson's Rule. Specifically, **RK4** has the update: $$ k_1 = f(t_n, y_n), \quad k_2 = f!\left(t_n+\tfrac{h}{2}, y_n + \tfrac{h}{2}k_1\right), \quad \ldots $$ $$ y_{n+1} = y_n + \frac{h}{6}(k_1 + 2k_2 + 2k_3 + k_4). $$ The **1-2-2-1 weights** are structurally identical in spirit to Simpson's **1-4-1** weights: both come from integrating an interpolating polynomial. Neural ODE backpropagation uses the **adjoint method**, which itself requires another numerical ODE integration backward in time.

### 3. Attention and Kernel Integrals

The **continuous attention** mechanism (Katharopoulos et al., 2020; Choromanski et al., 2021) writes attention as: $$ \text{Attn}(q) = \frac{\int K(q, k), v(k), dk}{\int K(q, k), dk}. $$ Approximating this with quadrature (Gauss-Hermite, Simpson on a discretized key space) connects directly to linear attention approximations.

### 4. Computing the Loss in VAEs (ELBO)

The **Evidence Lower Bound** (ELBO) in a Variational Autoencoder involves: $$ \mathcal{L} = \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}\bigl[\log p_\theta(x|z)\bigr] - D_{\mathrm{KL}}(q_\phi | p). $$ The expectation is an integral over the latent space. For 1D latent variables or debugging, Simpson's Rule numerically evaluates this exactly. For high-D, Monte Carlo sampling takes over — but the intuition (approximate the integral with weighted sums) is the same conceptual object.

### 5. Reparameterization and Path Integrals in RL

In **continuous-time reinforcement learning** and policy gradient methods, the **expected return** under a policy involves integrating a trajectory distribution over time. Discretizing the time integral (quadrature) is exactly what happens when you turn a continuous MDP into a discrete one. The quality of that approximation controls the bias of the gradient estimator.

### 6. Quadrature Nodes as Latent Variable Positions

Some recent works (e.g., **Fourier Neural Operators**, **DeepONets**) discretize function spaces using quadrature grids. The choice of nodes — equispaced (as in Simpson) versus Chebyshev versus Gauss-Legendre — determines how much information about $f$ is captured per evaluation. Chebyshev and Gauss nodes can be exponentially better for smooth $f$, which is why many neural operators over function spaces prefer non-uniform grids.

> _Summary table_

|ML Context|Integration Problem|Quadrature Role|
|---|---|---|
|Bayesian inference|Marginal likelihood|Direct (low-dim) or inspires MC|
|Neural ODEs|Integrating $dh/dt = f_\theta$|RK4 = Simpson's cousin|
|VAEs|ELBO expectation|MC replaces, but conceptually same|
|Continuous attention|Kernel integral over keys|Gauss quadrature approximations|
|Neural Operators (FNO)|Function-space discretization|Quadrature grid choice matters|
|Diffusion models|Score integral over time|SDE/ODE solver = quadrature in time|

## Common Mistakes and Traps

1. **$n$ must be even.** There is no Simpson's Rule with $n=3$ panels (4 nodes). If you need odd panels, use Simpson's 3/8 Rule (fits a cubic through 4 nodes).
    
2. **Unequal spacing.** The derivation above assumed $h = \text{const}$. Non-uniform grids require a modified formula.
    
3. **Confusing $n$ (number of panels) with number of nodes.** Nodes = $n+1$.
    
4. **The error bound uses $f^{(4)}$.** If $f$ has only two derivatives, you cannot use the standard error estimate — Simpson may still work, but you cannot bound the error with this formula.
    
5. **Oscillatory integrands.** For highly oscillatory $f$ (e.g., $\sin(100x)$), composite Simpson converges slowly because many panels are needed to resolve the oscillations. Gauss-Legendre or Clenshaw-Curtis quadrature (both used in `scipy.integrate.quad`) handle this better.
