> **Personal note:** IBP is not a formula to memorise — it is the product rule _read backwards_. Once you see it that way, every step becomes inevitable rather than magical. The real skill is not the algebra; it is _choosing_ $u$ and $dv$ so the resulting integral is simpler than the one you started with. That choice is an art, and this note is about developing that art.

---

## §1 · The Derivation — Not a Formula, a Theorem

Start from the **product rule**. For differentiable functions $u(x)$ and $v(x)$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}[u(x)v(x)] = u'(x)v(x) + u(x)v'(x)$$

Integrate both sides over $[a,b]$:

$$\int_a^b \frac{d}{dx}[uv],dx = \int_a^b u'v,dx + \int_a^b uv',dx$$

The left side is just $[u(x)v(x)]_a^b$ by FTC. Rearrange:

$$\boxed{\int_a^b u,v',dx = \big[uv\big]_a^b - \int_a^b v,u',dx}$$

In indefinite form, writing $dv = v'(x),dx$ and $du = u'(x),dx$:

$$\boxed{\int u,dv = uv - \int v,du}$$

> _Observation:_ The formula trades $\int u,dv$ for $\int v,du$. This is only useful if $\int v,du$ is **easier** than $\int u,dv$. Your entire job is to make that true.

---

## §2 · The LIATE Rule — A Heuristic, Not a Law

When faced with a product $f(x)\cdot g(x)$, assign $u$ to whichever function appears **earlier** in this list:

|Priority|Class|Examples|
|---|---|---|
|1st|**L**ogarithm|$\ln x,\ \log_a x,\ \ln(x^2+1)$|
|2nd|**I**nverse trig|$\arcsin x,\ \arctan x,\ \text{arcsec},x$|
|3rd|**A**lgebraic (polynomial)|$x^n,\ \sqrt{x},\ x^{3/2}$|
|4th|**T**rigonometric|$\sin x,\ \cos x,\ \tan x$|
|5th|**E**xponential|$e^x,\ a^x,\ e^{3x}$|

**Why this order works:**

- **L and I** first: their derivatives simplify ($(\ln x)' = 1/x$, $(\arctan x)' = 1/(1+x^2)$) but their integrals are messy. So make them $u$ (differentiate them).
- **E** last: $e^x$ integrates to itself — it doesn't simplify things to let it be $u$.
- **A** in the middle: polynomials reduce degree when differentiated, eventually becoming constants.

> **LIATE is a heuristic.** There are cases where it fails or where the "wrong" assignment is actually correct. The real test is always: does $\int v,du$ look simpler?

## §3 · Canonical Examples with Full Working

### 3.1 Polynomial × Exponential: $\int xe^x,dx$

**Assignment (LIATE: A before E):** $u = x$, $dv = e^x,dx$

$$du = dx, \qquad v = e^x$$

$$\int xe^x,dx = xe^x - \int e^x,dx = xe^x - e^x + C = e^x(x-1) + C$$

**Verification:** $\frac{d}{dx}[e^x(x-1)] = e^x(x-1) + e^x = xe^x$ ✓

---

### 3.2 Polynomial × Exponential (repeated): $\int x^2 e^x,dx$

**Assignment:** $u = x^2$, $dv = e^x,dx$

$$du = 2x,dx, \qquad v = e^x$$

$$\int x^2 e^x,dx = x^2 e^x - \int 2xe^x,dx = x^2 e^x - 2\int xe^x,dx$$

Now apply the result from 3.1:

$$= x^2 e^x - 2e^x(x-1) + C = e^x(x^2 - 2x + 2) + C$$

**Pattern:** $\int x^n e^x,dx$ requires IBP exactly $n$ times. The general result is:

$$\int x^n e^x,dx = e^x\sum_{k=0}^{n}(-1)^{n-k}\frac{n!}{k!}x^k + C$$

---

### 3.3 Logarithm alone: $\int \ln x,dx$

**Trick:** write $\ln x = \ln x \cdot 1$. Assign $u = \ln x$, $dv = dx$.

$$du = \frac{1}{x}dx, \qquad v = x$$

$$\int \ln x,dx = x\ln x - \int x \cdot \frac{1}{x},dx = x\ln x - \int 1,dx = x\ln x - x + C$$

**This is the prototype for all "lone L or I" integrals.** The trick is always: pair with $1$ and assign $dv = dx$.

---

### 3.4 Inverse trig alone: $\int \arctan x,dx$

Same trick: $u = \arctan x$, $dv = dx$

$$du = \frac{1}{1+x^2}dx, \qquad v = x$$

$$\int \arctan x,dx = x\arctan x - \int \frac{x}{1+x^2},dx$$

For the remaining integral, $u$-sub with $w = 1+x^2$:

$$\int \frac{x}{1+x^2},dx = \frac{1}{2}\ln(1+x^2)$$

$$\int \arctan x,dx = x\arctan x - \frac{1}{2}\ln(1+x^2) + C$$

Similarly: $$\int \arcsin x,dx = x\arcsin x + \sqrt{1-x^2} + C$$ $$\int \arccos x,dx = x\arccos x - \sqrt{1-x^2} + C$$

---

### 3.5 Cyclic IBP: $\int e^x \sin x,dx$

This is the most structurally important case. Let $I = \int e^x\sin x,dx$.

**First IBP:** $u = \sin x$, $dv = e^x dx$

$$du = \cos x,dx, \qquad v = e^x$$

$$I = e^x\sin x - \int e^x\cos x,dx$$

**Second IBP** on $\int e^x\cos x,dx$: $u = \cos x$, $dv = e^x dx$

$$du = -\sin x,dx, \qquad v = e^x$$

$$\int e^x\cos x,dx = e^x\cos x - \int e^x(-\sin x),dx = e^x\cos x + I$$

Substituting back:

$$I = e^x\sin x - (e^x\cos x + I) = e^x\sin x - e^x\cos x - I$$

$$2I = e^x(\sin x - \cos x)$$

$$\boxed{I = \frac{e^x(\sin x - \cos x)}{2} + C}$$

> **Critical observation:** The key was that applying IBP _twice with consistent assignment_ (always making the trig function $u$) reproduced $I$ on the right-hand side. This creates an algebraic equation in $I$ which we solve directly. **Never switch the roles of $u$ and $dv$ between the two applications**, or you will undo your work and get $0 = 0$.

Similarly: $$\int e^{ax}\sin bx,dx = \frac{e^{ax}(a\sin bx - b\cos bx)}{a^2+b^2} + C$$ $$\int e^{ax}\cos bx,dx = \frac{e^{ax}(a\cos bx + b\sin bx)}{a^2+b^2} + C$$

## §4 · Reduction Formulae — IBP Iterated Systematically

A **reduction formula** expresses $\int f^n$ in terms of $\int f^{n-1}$ (or $\int f^{n-2}$). These are derived by IBP and are extremely useful when $n$ is large.

### 4.1 $\int \sin^n x,dx$

Write $\sin^n x = \sin^{n-1}x \cdot \sin x$.

**Assignment:** $u = \sin^{n-1}x$, $dv = \sin x,dx$

$$du = (n-1)\sin^{n-2}x\cos x,dx, \qquad v = -\cos x$$

$$\int\sin^n x,dx = -\sin^{n-1}x\cos x + (n-1)\int\sin^{n-2}x\cos^2 x,dx$$

Replace $\cos^2 x = 1 - \sin^2 x$:

$$= -\sin^{n-1}x\cos x + (n-1)\int\sin^{n-2}x,dx - (n-1)\int\sin^n x,dx$$

Let $I_n = \int\sin^n x,dx$, rearrange:

$$\boxed{I_n = -\frac{\sin^{n-1}x\cos x}{n} + \frac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}}$$

**Base cases:** $I_0 = x+C$, $I_1 = -\cos x + C$.

**Example — $\int\sin^4 x,dx$:**

$$I_4 = -\frac{\sin^3 x\cos x}{4} + \frac{3}{4}I_2$$

$$I_2 = -\frac{\sin x\cos x}{2}+\frac{1}{2}I_0 = -\frac{\sin x\cos x}{2}+\frac{x}{2}$$

$$I_4 = -\frac{\sin^3 x\cos x}{4} + \frac{3}{4}\left(-\frac{\sin x\cos x}{2}+\frac{x}{2}\right) = -\frac{\sin^3 x\cos x}{4} - \frac{3\sin x\cos x}{8} + \frac{3x}{8} + C$$

### 4.2 $\int x^n e^{ax},dx$

$$\int x^n e^{ax},dx = \frac{x^n e^{ax}}{a} - \frac{n}{a}\int x^{n-1}e^{ax},dx$$

### 4.3 $\int (\ln x)^n,dx$

$$\int(\ln x)^n,dx = x(\ln x)^n - n\int(\ln x)^{n-1},dx$$

### 4.4 $\int \sec^n x,dx$

$$\int\sec^n x,dx = \frac{\sec^{n-2}x\tan x}{n-1} + \frac{n-2}{n-1}\int\sec^{n-2}x,dx, \quad n \geq 2$$

## §5 · Definite Integrals — IBP with Limits

$$\int_a^b u,dv = \big[uv\big]_a^b - \int_a^b v,du$$

**Example:**

$$\int_0^1 xe^x,dx = \big[xe^x\big]_0^1 - \int_0^1 e^x,dx = e - \big[e^x\big]_0^1 = e - (e-1) = 1$$

**Wallis Integral via reduction formula:**

Using $I_n = \frac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$ with $\int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x,dx$:

$$I_{2n} = \frac{(2n-1)!!}{(2n)!!}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}, \qquad I_{2n+1} = \frac{(2n)!!}{(2n+1)!!}$$

**Wallis product** (taking the limit of $I_{2n}/I_{2n+1}$ as $n\to\infty$):

$$\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{2}{1}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{4}{3}\cdot\frac{4}{5}\cdot\frac{6}{5}\cdot\frac{6}{7}\cdots$$

## §6 · Tabular Integration — IBP at Scale

When $u$ is a polynomial (eventually reduces to 0) and $dv$ is easy to integrate repeatedly:

**$\int x^3 e^{2x},dx$:**

|Sign|Differentiate ($u$ column)|Integrate ($dv$ column)|
|---|---|---|
|$+$|$x^3$|$e^{2x}$|
|$-$|$3x^2$|$\frac{1}{2}e^{2x}$|
|$+$|$6x$|$\frac{1}{4}e^{2x}$|
|$-$|$6$|$\frac{1}{8}e^{2x}$|
|$+$|$0$|$\frac{1}{16}e^{2x}$|

**Multiply diagonals, apply signs:**

$$= x^3\cdot\frac{e^{2x}}{2} - 3x^2\cdot\frac{e^{2x}}{4} + 6x\cdot\frac{e^{2x}}{8} - 6\cdot\frac{e^{2x}}{16} + C$$

$$= e^{2x}\left(\frac{x^3}{2} - \frac{3x^2}{4} + \frac{3x}{4} - \frac{3}{8}\right) + C$$

**Rule:** Stop when the $u$-column reaches 0. The last entry in the $dv$-column is not used.

## §7 · The Tricky Cases — Where Intuition Breaks

### 7.1 When IBP produces a useful equation

We already saw the cyclic case. Another: **$\int\sqrt{1-x^2},dx$**

$u = \sqrt{1-x^2}$, $dv = dx$

$$du = \frac{-x}{\sqrt{1-x^2}}dx, \qquad v = x$$

$$\int\sqrt{1-x^2},dx = x\sqrt{1-x^2} + \int\frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1-x^2}},dx$$

Now write $x^2 = 1-(1-x^2)$ in the numerator:

$$\int\frac{x^2}{\sqrt{1-x^2}},dx = \int\frac{1}{\sqrt{1-x^2}},dx - \int\sqrt{1-x^2},dx = \arcsin x - I$$

So: $$I = x\sqrt{1-x^2} + \arcsin x - I$$ $$2I = x\sqrt{1-x^2} + \arcsin x$$ $$\int\sqrt{1-x^2},dx = \frac{x\sqrt{1-x^2}}{2} + \frac{\arcsin x}{2} + C$$

This is the area formula for a semicircle — check: $\int_{-1}^1\sqrt{1-x^2},dx = \pi/2$. ✓

### 7.2 When $u$-substitution is needed inside IBP

$\int x^3\sqrt{x^2+1},dx$: try $u = x^2+1$ first, giving $\int \frac{(u-1)}{2}\sqrt{u},du = \frac{1}{2}\int(u^{3/2}-u^{1/2}),du$, which is just power rule. IBP is not needed here — recognise when $u$-sub is _cleaner_.

> **Important:** IBP is not always the right tool even when you see a product. $\int xe^{x^2},dx$ yields immediately to $u = x^2$, not IBP.

### 7.3 Integration by Parts in two variables (MIT 18.02)

For operators on functions, IBP generalises. If $u, v$ are functions on $[a,b]$:

$$\int_a^b u,v'',dx = \big[uv'\big]_a^b - \int_a^b u'v',dx$$

Apply IBP again on the right:

$$\int_a^b u,v'',dx = \big[uv'\big]_a^b - \big[u'v\big]_a^b + \int_a^b u''v,dx$$

This is the **adjoint identity** — it says $\langle u, Lv\rangle = \langle Lu, v\rangle + \text{boundary terms}$ for $L = d^2/dx^2$, which makes $L$ self-adjoint when the boundary terms vanish.

## §8 · Visualisation — What IBP Is Geometrically

Consider two increasing functions $u(x)$ and $v(x)$ parametrised by $x \in [a,b]$.

The point $(u(x), v(x))$ traces a curve in the $uv$-plane from $(u(a),v(a))$ to $(u(b),v(b))$.

```
v
^
|         * (u(b),v(b))
|        /
|       / curve (u(x),v(x))
|      /
|*----+
(u(a),v(a))
+-----------> u
```

The rectangle $[u(a),u(b)] \times [v(a),v(b)]$ is divided into two regions by this curve:

- Area to the **left** of curve $= \int_{v(a)}^{v(b)} u,dv$
- Area **below** curve $= \int_{u(a)}^{u(b)} v,du$

The two together make up the full rectangle:

$$\int_{v(a)}^{v(b)} u,dv + \int_{u(a)}^{u(b)} v,du = \big[u(b)v(b) - u(a)v(a)\big]$$

This is **exactly** IBP. The formula $\int u,dv = uv - \int v,du$ is the statement that the two shaded areas sum to the rectangle.

```
v(b) ┤ · · · · ·*
     │ · · · ·/  |
     │ · · ·/    |  ← Region A = ∫u dv
     │ · ·/      |
v(a) ┤ ·/────────┤
     │  ↑        |
     │  Region B = ∫v du
     └───┬────────┬──→ u
       u(a)     u(b)
```

Area(A) + Area(B) = Area of full rectangle = $u(b)v(b) - u(a)v(a)$

## §9 · AI / Machine Learning Connections

### 9.1 Backpropagation is Discrete IBP

In a neural network with loss $\mathcal{L}$ and a layer computing $z = \sigma(Wx+b)$:

The chain rule $\frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial W} = \frac{\partial\mathcal{L}}{\partial z}\cdot\frac{\partial z}{\partial W}$ is the discrete product rule.

Backpropagation sums these over the network depth — which is the discrete analogue of:

$$\int_a^b (uv)',dx = \int_a^b u'v,dx + \int_a^b uv',dx$$

The **reverse-mode automatic differentiation** algorithm is literally the continuous IBP identity applied layer by layer.

---

### 9.2 The Adjoint Method — Neural ODEs

A **Neural ODE** defines the hidden state by: $$\frac{dh}{dt} = f_\theta(h(t), t), \quad h(t_0) = h_0$$

To compute $\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta}$ without storing all intermediate states, define the **adjoint** $a(t) = \frac{d\mathcal{L}}{dh(t)}$.

The adjoint satisfies: $$\frac{da}{dt} = -a(t)^T \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial h}$$

This ODE for $a(t)$ is derived by IBP on the functional: $$\mathcal{L} = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} \langle a(t),\ \dot{h}(t) - f_\theta(h(t),t)\rangle,dt$$

Applying IBP to $\int a,\dot{h},dt = [ah]_{t_0}^{t_1} - \int \dot{a},h,dt$ produces the adjoint equation. This is the IBP formula at the level of **functionals** — the foundation of **optimal control** and **variational methods** in ML.

---

### 9.3 Integration by Parts in Loss Regularisation

**Score matching** (the objective behind diffusion models and energy-based models) asks:

$$\mathcal{J}(\theta) = \frac{1}{2}\mathbb{E}_{p_{data}}\left[\left|\nabla_x\ln p_\theta(x) - \nabla_x\ln p_{data}(x)\right|^2\right]$$

Expanding the square and applying IBP to the cross term:

$$\int p_{data}(x)\langle\nabla_x\ln p_\theta,\nabla_x\ln p_{data}\rangle,dx = -\int p_{data}(x),\Delta_x\ln p_\theta(x),dx$$

where $\Delta$ is the Laplacian. (IBP: $\int \nabla u\cdot\nabla v = -\int u,\Delta v$ when boundary terms vanish.)

This gives the **tractable objective**: $$\mathcal{J}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p_{data}}\left[\text{tr}(\nabla_x^2\ln p_\theta(x)) + \frac{1}{2}|\nabla_x\ln p_\theta(x)|^2\right] + \text{const}$$

No knowledge of $p_{data}$ needed — IBP removed it. This is why **DDPM, score-based models (Song & Ermon, 2019)** are tractable.

---

### 9.4 Fisher Information and IBP

The **Fisher information** is:

$$\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\ln p(x;\theta)\right)^2\right]$$

Under regularity conditions, IBP on $\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\int p(x;\theta),dx = 0$ gives the identity:

$$\mathcal{I}(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\ln p(x;\theta)\right]$$

This is why the Hessian of the log-likelihood (the **observed Fisher information**) is used in **natural gradient descent** and **second-order optimisation** methods like K-FAC.

---

### 9.5 Entropy and the Gibbs Inequality

The **differential entropy** $H(p) = -\int p(x)\ln p(x),dx$.

To show the normal distribution maximises entropy among fixed-variance distributions, one sets up a variational problem and applies IBP to the Euler-Lagrange equation. The result is that the optimal $p^*(x) \propto e^{-\lambda x^2}$, i.e., Gaussian.

This underpins the **rate-distortion theory** behind **variational autoencoders (VAEs)**: the Gaussian prior is not arbitrary — it is information-theoretically optimal, proven via an argument that is IBP at its core.

## §11 · Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

**Mistake 1 — Switching $u$/$dv$ roles between IBP applications.** In cyclic IBP, always assign the same type of function to $u$ in both applications. Switching undoes the work: you get $I = I$, i.e., $0 = 0$.

**Mistake 2 — Forgetting $+C$ when computing $v$.** When integrating $dv$ to get $v$, you technically get $v + C_0$. It turns out adding $C_0$ is harmless (it cancels), but students sometimes propagate it incorrectly.

**Mistake 3 — Using IBP when $u$-sub is simpler.** $\int xe^{x^2}dx$: do NOT set $u=x$. Recognise $x,dx = \frac{1}{2}d(x^2)$ and substitute directly.

**Mistake 4 — Wrong sign in the reduction formula.** The reduction formula for $\sin^n$ has a subtle sign: $I_n = -\frac{\sin^{n-1}x\cos x}{n} + \frac{n-1}{n}I_{n-2}$. Derive it fresh rather than memorising.

**Mistake 5 — Not verifying.** IBP answers are easy to verify: differentiate the result. Algebraic errors are common; always check.
