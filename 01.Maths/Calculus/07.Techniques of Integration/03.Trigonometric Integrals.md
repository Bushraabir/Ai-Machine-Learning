> **Personal note:** Trigonometric integrals are not a collection of tricks вҖ” they are a systematic exploitation of three identities. Every technique in this chapter reduces to one of: the Pythagorean identity, a half-angle identity, or a product-to-sum identity. Once you see _which_ identity is needed and _why_, the method is obvious. My goal here is to make the "why" so clear that you never need to memorise a case вҖ” you can reconstruct every strategy from scratch.

---

## В§1 В· The Three Master Identities

Everything in this chapter flows from these. Know them cold.

### Pythagorean Identities

$$\sin^2 x + \cos^2 x = 1 \tag{P1}$$ $$\tan^2 x + 1 = \sec^2 x \tag{P2}$$ $$\cot^2 x + 1 = \csc^2 x \tag{P3}$$

### Half-Angle (Power-Reduction) Identities

$$\sin^2 x = \frac{1 - \cos 2x}{2} \tag{H1}$$ $$\cos^2 x = \frac{1 + \cos 2x}{2} \tag{H2}$$

### Product-to-Sum Identities

$$\sin mx \cos nx = \frac{1}{2}[\sin(m+n)x + \sin(m-n)x] \tag{Q1}$$ $$\sin mx \sin nx = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(m-n)x - \cos(m+n)x] \tag{Q2}$$ $$\cos mx \cos nx = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(m-n)x + \cos(m+n)x] \tag{Q3}$$

> _Why three families?_ Because $\sin^m x \cos^n x$, $\tan^m x \sec^n x$, and $\sin mx \cos nx$ each require a different structural attack. The Pythagorean identities handle the first two families; the product-to-sum identities handle the third. The half-angle identities are the fallback for even powers in the first family.

## В§2 В· Family I вҖ” Integrals of the Form $\int \sin^m x \cos^n x,dx$

The strategy depends entirely on the **parity** (odd or even) of $m$ and $n$.

---

### Case A вҖ” At least one exponent is **odd**

**Strategy:** Factor out one copy of the odd-powered function, use (P1) to convert the rest to the other function, then $u$-substitute.

**Why it works:** If $m$ is odd, write $\sin^m x = \sin^{m-1}x\cdot\sin x$. Since $m-1$ is even, $\sin^{m-1}x = (1-\cos^2 x)^{(m-1)/2}$ by (P1). Now set $u = \cos x$, $du = -\sin x,dx$ вҖ” the leftover $\sin x,dx$ is exactly $-du$.

---

**Example 1:** $\displaystyle\int \sin^3 x\cos^2 x,dx$

$m=3$ (odd), $n=2$ (even). Factor from $\sin$:

$$= \int \sin^2 x \cdot \cos^2 x \cdot \sin x,dx = \int (1-\cos^2 x)\cos^2 x\sin x,dx$$

Let $u = \cos x$, $du = -\sin x,dx$:

$$= -\int(1-u^2)u^2,du = -\int(u^2 - u^4),du = -\frac{u^3}{3}+\frac{u^5}{5}+C$$

$$= \boxed{-\frac{\cos^3 x}{3}+\frac{\cos^5 x}{5}+C}$$

**Verification:** $\frac{d}{dx}\left[-\frac{\cos^3 x}{3}+\frac{\cos^5 x}{5}\right] = \cos^2 x\sin x - \cos^4 x\sin x = \sin x\cos^2 x(1-\cos^2 x) = \sin^3 x\cos^2 x$ вң“

---

**Example 2:** $\displaystyle\int \sin^2 x\cos^5 x,dx$

$n=5$ (odd). Factor from $\cos$:

$$= \int\sin^2 x\cos^4 x\cdot\cos x,dx = \int\sin^2 x(1-\sin^2 x)^2\cos x,dx$$

Let $u=\sin x$, $du=\cos x,dx$:

$$= \int u^2(1-u^2)^2,du = \int u^2(1-2u^2+u^4),du = \int(u^2-2u^4+u^6),du$$

$$= \frac{u^3}{3}-\frac{2u^5}{5}+\frac{u^7}{7}+C = \boxed{\frac{\sin^3 x}{3}-\frac{2\sin^5 x}{5}+\frac{\sin^7 x}{7}+C}$$

---

**Example 3:** $\displaystyle\int\cos^5 x,dx$

Both exponents: $m=0$ (even), $n=5$ (odd). Factor one $\cos x$:

$$= \int\cos^4 x\cdot\cos x,dx = \int(1-\sin^2 x)^2\cos x,dx$$

Let $u=\sin x$:

$$= \int(1-u^2)^2,du = \int(1-2u^2+u^4),du = u - \frac{2u^3}{3}+\frac{u^5}{5}+C$$

$$= \boxed{\sin x - \frac{2\sin^3 x}{3}+\frac{\sin^5 x}{5}+C}$$

---

### Case B вҖ” Both exponents are **even**

**Strategy:** Apply half-angle identities (H1) and (H2) to reduce powers. Repeat until all powers are odd or the integral is elementary.

**Why it works:** (H1) and (H2) literally halve the exponent. Apply them repeatedly: $\sin^{2k} x \to \sin^{2(k-1)} x + \text{double-angle terms} \to \cdots$. Eventually all terms have exponent 0 or 1.

---

**Example 4:** $\displaystyle\int\sin^2 x,dx$

$$= \int\frac{1-\cos 2x}{2},dx = \frac{x}{2}-\frac{\sin 2x}{4}+C$$

**Example 5:** $\displaystyle\int\cos^2 x,dx$

$$= \int\frac{1+\cos 2x}{2},dx = \frac{x}{2}+\frac{\sin 2x}{4}+C$$

**Example 6:** $\displaystyle\int\sin^4 x,dx$

$$= \int\left(\frac{1-\cos 2x}{2}\right)^2 dx = \frac{1}{4}\int(1-2\cos 2x+\cos^2 2x),dx$$

Apply (H2) to $\cos^2 2x = \frac{1+\cos 4x}{2}$:

$$= \frac{1}{4}\int\left(1-2\cos 2x+\frac{1+\cos 4x}{2}\right)dx = \frac{1}{4}\left[\frac{3x}{2}-\sin 2x+\frac{\sin 4x}{8}\right]+C$$

$$= \boxed{\frac{3x}{8}-\frac{\sin 2x}{4}+\frac{\sin 4x}{32}+C}$$

**Example 7:** $\displaystyle\int\sin^2 x\cos^2 x,dx$

Use both half-angle identities together, or notice:

$$\sin^2 x\cos^2 x = \frac{1}{4}\sin^2 2x = \frac{1}{4}\cdot\frac{1-\cos 4x}{2} = \frac{1-\cos 4x}{8}$$

$$\int\sin^2 x\cos^2 x,dx = \frac{x}{8}-\frac{\sin 4x}{32}+C$$

---

### Summary Table вҖ” $\int\sin^m x\cos^n x,dx$

```
m odd?  вҶ’ Factor sin x, let u = cos x
n odd?  вҶ’ Factor cos x, let u = sin x
Both odd? вҶ’ Either works (choose smaller exponent for simplicity)
Both even? вҶ’ Half-angle identities, reduce, repeat
```

## В§3 В· Family II вҖ” Integrals of the Form $\int\tan^m x\sec^n x,dx$

The key identity is (P2): $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$.

The key derivatives: $(\tan x)' = \sec^2 x$ and $(\sec x)' = \sec x\tan x$.

So: to use $u = \tan x$, you need $\sec^2 x,dx$ left over; to use $u = \sec x$, you need $\sec x\tan x,dx$.

---

### Case A вҖ” $n$ is **even** (the $\sec$ exponent is even)

**Strategy:** Factor out $\sec^2 x$ (for the $du$ in $u=\tan x$ substitution). Convert the remaining even power of $\sec$ to $\tan$ via $\sec^2 x = 1+\tan^2 x$. Let $u=\tan x$.

---

**Example 8:** $\displaystyle\int\tan^3 x\sec^4 x,dx$

Factor $\sec^2 x$: $= \int\tan^3 x\sec^2 x\cdot\sec^2 x,dx$

Convert: $\sec^2 x = 1+\tan^2 x$. Let $u=\tan x$, $du=\sec^2 x,dx$:

$$= \int u^3(1+u^2),du = \int(u^3+u^5),du = \frac{u^4}{4}+\frac{u^6}{6}+C = \boxed{\frac{\tan^4 x}{4}+\frac{\tan^6 x}{6}+C}$$

---

### Case B вҖ” $m$ is **odd** (the $\tan$ exponent is odd)

**Strategy:** Factor out $\sec x\tan x$ (for the $du$ in $u=\sec x$ substitution). Convert the remaining even power of $\tan$ to $\sec$ via $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$. Let $u=\sec x$.

---

**Example 9:** $\displaystyle\int\tan^3 x\sec^3 x,dx$

Factor $\sec x\tan x$: $= \int\tan^2 x\sec^2 x\cdot\sec x\tan x,dx$

Convert: $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x-1$. Let $u=\sec x$, $du=\sec x\tan x,dx$:

$$= \int(u^2-1)u^2,du = \int(u^4-u^2),du = \frac{u^5}{5}-\frac{u^3}{3}+C = \boxed{\frac{\sec^5 x}{5}-\frac{\sec^3 x}{3}+C}$$

---

### Case C вҖ” $m$ even, $n$ odd (the hard case)

**Strategy:** No clean substitution exists. Convert everything to $\sec$ via $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x - 1$, then use the reduction formula for $\int\sec^n x,dx$.

**Example 10:** $\displaystyle\int\tan^2 x\sec x,dx$

$$= \int(\sec^2 x-1)\sec x,dx = \int\sec^3 x,dx - \int\sec x,dx$$

These are both known: $$\int\sec^3 x,dx = \frac{\sec x\tan x}{2}+\frac{1}{2}\ln|\sec x+\tan x|+C \quad \text{(derived below)}$$ $$\int\sec x,dx = \ln|\sec x+\tan x|+C$$

$$= \frac{\sec x\tan x}{2}-\frac{1}{2}\ln|\sec x+\tan x|+C$$

---

### Derivation of $\int\sec^3 x,dx$ вҡ‘

This is important enough to prove carefully. Use IBP with $u=\sec x$, $dv=\sec^2 x,dx$:

$$du = \sec x\tan x,dx, \qquad v = \tan x$$

$$\int\sec^3 x,dx = \sec x\tan x - \int\sec x\tan^2 x,dx$$

$$= \sec x\tan x - \int\sec x(\sec^2 x-1),dx$$

$$= \sec x\tan x - \int\sec^3 x,dx + \int\sec x,dx$$

Let $I = \int\sec^3 x,dx$: $$I = \sec x\tan x - I + \ln|\sec x+\tan x|$$ $$2I = \sec x\tan x + \ln|\sec x+\tan x|$$

$$\boxed{\int\sec^3 x,dx = \frac{\sec x\tan x}{2}+\frac{\ln|\sec x+\tan x|}{2}+C}$$

---

### Reduction Formula for $\int\sec^n x,dx$

$$\int\sec^n x,dx = \frac{\sec^{n-2}x\tan x}{n-1}+\frac{n-2}{n-1}\int\sec^{n-2}x,dx, \quad n\geq 2$$

**Derivation:** IBP with $u=\sec^{n-2}x$, $dv=\sec^2 x,dx$. Identical structure to the $\sec^3$ derivation above.

---

### Summary Table вҖ” $\int\tan^m x\sec^n x,dx$

```
n even? вҶ’ Factor secВІx, u = tan x, convert remaining secВІ вҶ’ 1+tanВІ
m odd?  вҶ’ Factor sec x tan x, u = sec x, convert remaining tanВІ вҶ’ secВІ-1
Both conditions? вҶ’ Either works
m even, n odd вҶ’ Convert tanВІ вҶ’ secВІ-1, use sec reduction formula
```

### Analogous: $\int\cot^m x\csc^n x,dx$

Identical structure, using $\cot^2 x = \csc^2 x-1$, $(\cot x)' = -\csc^2 x$, $(\csc x)' = -\csc x\cot x$.

## В§4 В· Family III вҖ” Products of Sines and Cosines with Different Arguments

For $\int\sin mx\cos nx,dx$, $\int\sin mx\sin nx,dx$, $\int\cos mx\cos nx,dx$: use product-to-sum identities (Q1)вҖ“(Q3) directly.

---

**Example 11:** $\displaystyle\int\sin 5x\cos 3x,dx$

Apply (Q1): $\sin 5x\cos 3x = \frac{1}{2}[\sin 8x+\sin 2x]$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\int(\sin 8x+\sin 2x),dx = -\frac{\cos 8x}{16}-\frac{\cos 2x}{4}+C$$

**Example 12:** $\displaystyle\int\cos 3x\cos 5x,dx$

Apply (Q3): $\cos 3x\cos 5x = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(-2x)+\cos 8x] = \frac{1}{2}[\cos 2x+\cos 8x]$

$$= \frac{\sin 2x}{4}+\frac{\sin 8x}{16}+C$$

**Example 13:** $\displaystyle\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin mx\sin nx,dx$, $m\neq n$ integers

Apply (Q2): $\sin mx\sin nx = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(m-n)x - \cos(m+n)x]$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{\sin(m-n)x}{m-n}-\frac{\sin(m+n)x}{m+n}\right]_{-\pi}^{\pi} = 0$$

Since $\sin(k\pi) = 0$ for any integer $k$.

**When $m=n$:** $$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin^2 mx,dx = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\frac{1-\cos 2mx}{2},dx = \pi$$

> This is the **orthogonality of sine functions** вҖ” the cornerstone of Fourier series. See В§7.

## В§5 В· Powers of $\tan x$ Alone and $\cot x$ Alone

### $\int\tan^n x,dx$

Use the reduction $\tan^n x = \tan^{n-2}x\cdot\tan^2 x = \tan^{n-2}x(\sec^2 x-1)$:

$$\int\tan^n x,dx = \int\tan^{n-2}x\sec^2 x,dx - \int\tan^{n-2}x,dx = \frac{\tan^{n-1}x}{n-1} - \int\tan^{n-2}x,dx$$

**Reduction formula:** $$\int\tan^n x,dx = \frac{\tan^{n-1}x}{n-1} - \int\tan^{n-2}x,dx, \quad n\neq 1$$

**Chain from base cases $\int\tan^0 x = x$ and $\int\tan x = \ln|\sec x|$:**

$$\int\tan^2 x,dx = \tan x - x + C$$ $$\int\tan^3 x,dx = \frac{\tan^2 x}{2} - \ln|\sec x| + C$$ $$\int\tan^4 x,dx = \frac{\tan^3 x}{3} - \tan x + x + C$$

### $\int\cot^n x,dx$

$$\int\cot^n x,dx = -\frac{\cot^{n-1}x}{n-1} - \int\cot^{n-2}x,dx, \quad n\neq 1$$

Base cases: $\int\cot x,dx = \ln|\sin x|+C$, $\int\cot^2 x,dx = -\cot x - x + C$.

## В§6 В· Definite Integrals вҖ” The Wallis Formulas

Using the reduction formula for $\int\sin^n x,dx$ with definite limits on $[0,\pi/2]$:

$$W_n = \int_0^{\pi/2}\sin^n x,dx = \int_0^{\pi/2}\cos^n x,dx$$

The reduction gives: $W_n = \frac{n-1}{n}W_{n-2}$, with $W_0 = \pi/2$, $W_1 = 1$.

**Explicit formulas:**

$$W_{2n} = \frac{(2n-1)!!}{(2n)!!}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} = \frac{1\cdot 3\cdot 5\cdots(2n-1)}{2\cdot 4\cdot 6\cdots(2n)}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2}$$

$$W_{2n+1} = \frac{(2n)!!}{(2n+1)!!} = \frac{2\cdot 4\cdot 6\cdots(2n)}{1\cdot 3\cdot 5\cdots(2n+1)}$$

**First several values:**

|$n$|$W_n$|
|---|---|
|0|$\pi/2$|
|1|$1$|
|2|$\pi/4$|
|3|$2/3$|
|4|$3\pi/16$|
|5|$8/15$|
|6|$5\pi/32$|

**Wallis's product** (from $W_{2n}/W_{2n+1} \to \pi/2$ as $n\to\infty$):

$$\frac{\pi}{2} = \prod_{k=1}^\infty\frac{(2k)(2k)}{(2k-1)(2k+1)} = \frac{2}{1}\cdot\frac{2}{3}\cdot\frac{4}{3}\cdot\frac{4}{5}\cdot\frac{6}{5}\cdot\frac{6}{7}\cdots$$

## В§7 В· Orthogonality вҖ” The Deepest Result in This Chapter

**Theorem (Orthogonality of trigonometric functions on $[-\pi,\pi]$):**

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin mx\sin nx,dx = \pi,\delta_{mn}$$

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\cos mx\cos nx,dx = \pi,\delta_{mn} \quad (m,n \geq 1)$$

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin mx\cos nx,dx = 0 \quad \text{for all integers } m,n$$

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\cos^2 0,dx = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi}1,dx = 2\pi$$

where $\delta_{mn} = 1$ if $m=n$, $0$ otherwise (Kronecker delta).

**Proof of the first (m вү  n case):** Apply product-to-sum (Q2):

$$\sin mx\sin nx = \frac{1}{2}[\cos(m-n)x-\cos(m+n)x]$$

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin mx\sin nx,dx = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{\sin(m-n)x}{m-n}-\frac{\sin(m+n)x}{m+n}\right]_{-\pi}^{\pi} = 0$$

since $\sin(k\pi) = 0$ for all integers $k$.

**Proof of the m = n case:** Apply (H1):

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin^2 nx,dx = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\frac{1-\cos 2nx}{2},dx = \pi - \frac{\sin 2n\pi}{2n}\bigg|_{-\pi}^{\pi} = \pi$$

**Why orthogonality is the right word:** Define the inner product $\langle f,g\rangle = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)g(x),dx$. The set ${1, \cos x, \sin x, \cos 2x, \sin 2x, \ldots}$ is an **orthogonal basis** of $L^2([-\pi,\pi])$ under this inner product вҖ” exactly as the standard basis vectors $\mathbf{e}_i$ are orthogonal under the dot product.

**Fourier coefficients follow immediately:**

$$f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2}+\sum_{n=1}^\infty(a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx)$$

Multiply both sides by $\cos mx$ and integrate over $[-\pi,\pi]$. By orthogonality, every term vanishes except $n=m$:

$$a_m = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\cos mx,dx, \qquad b_m = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\sin mx,dx$$

The integrals from this chapter compute every Fourier coefficient.

## В§10 В· AI / Machine Learning Connections

### 10.1 Orthogonality вҶ’ Fourier Features in ML

The orthogonality relations in В§7 are the foundation of **Random Fourier Features (RFF)** вҖ” a technique for approximating kernel machines at scale.

The **RBF kernel** can be written as: $$K(x,x') = e^{-|x-x'|^2/2\sigma^2} = \mathbb{E}_{\omega\sim\mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^{-2}I)}\left[\cos(\omega^Tx)\cos(\omega^Tx')+\sin(\omega^Tx)\sin(\omega^Tx')\right]$$

This is Bochner's theorem вҖ” a kernel is shift-invariant if and only if it is the Fourier transform of a non-negative measure. The trig integrals of В§4 (and the orthogonality of В§7) are what make the Fourier transform of $e^{-x^2}$ itself a Gaussian.

**Rahimi & Recht (2007)** вҖ” Random Fourier Features: approximate $K(x,x') \approx z(x)^Tz(x')$ where $z(x) = \sqrt{2/D}[\cos(\omega_1^Tx+b_1),\ldots,\cos(\omega_D^Tx+b_D)]$. This converts an $O(n^2)$ kernel computation to $O(nD)$ using the integrals $\int\cos(ax)\cos(bx),dx$ вҖ” exactly В§4.

### 10.2 Fourier Series вҶ’ Convolutional Neural Networks

A 1D CNN layer with filter $w$ computes: $$(w * f)(x) = \int w(x-y)f(y),dy$$

In frequency space (Fourier domain), convolution becomes multiplication: $$\widehat{w*f}(\xi) = \hat{w}(\xi)\cdot\hat{f}(\xi)$$

**Computing $\hat{f}(\xi) = \int f(x)e^{-2\pi i\xi x},dx$** reduces вҖ” via Euler's formula $e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta+i\sin\theta$ вҖ” to exactly the integrals $\int f(x)\cos(2\pi\xi x),dx$ and $\int f(x)\sin(2\pi\xi x),dx$ from this chapter.

The **FFT** computes these at all frequencies simultaneously in $O(n\log n)$, but the integrals themselves вҖ” and why they converge, why they are well-defined, why orthogonality holds вҖ” are the trig integrals of В§7.

### 10.3 Positional Encodings вҖ” Transformers

The positional encoding in the original **Transformer** (Vaswani et al., 2017):

$$PE(pos, 2i) = \sin!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right), \quad PE(pos, 2i+1) = \cos!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d}}\right)$$

The key property needed: for any fixed offset $k$, $PE(pos+k)$ is a **linear function** of $PE(pos)$. This follows from the addition formula:

$$\sin(a+b) = \sin a\cos b+\cos a\sin b, \quad \cos(a+b) = \cos a\cos b - \sin a\sin b$$

These addition formulas вҖ” which are algebraic consequences of the product-to-sum identities (Q1)вҖ“(Q3) вҖ” are exactly why sine and cosine were chosen. Any relative positional relationship can be expressed as a matrix multiplication in embedding space.

### 10.4 Activation Function Analysis

The **Fourier spectrum of activation functions** determines which frequencies a neural network can represent at a given layer.

For a ReLU network, $\text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0,x)$. Its Fourier coefficients on $[-\pi,\pi]$:

$$b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\text{ReLU}(x)\sin nx,dx = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi}x\sin nx,dx$$

This is IBP + trig integrals. The result determines the **spectral bias** of ReLU networks вҖ” they preferentially learn low-frequency components first (Rahaman et al., 2019, _NeurIPS_). This is a fundamental limitation explaining why neural networks generalise in the way they do.

Similarly, for $\tanh(x)$: $$a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\tanh(x)\cos nx,dx$$

The decay rate of these coefficients governs how well depth-$L$ networks approximate smooth functions вҖ” tying trig integrals to **universal approximation theory**.

### 10.5 Signal Processing in Audio/Speech Models

**Mel-frequency cepstral coefficients (MFCCs)**, used as features in almost every pre-deep-learning speech recognition system (and as baselines today):

$$c_n = \sqrt{\frac{2}{N}}\sum_{k=1}^N\log S_k\cos!\left[\frac{\pi n(k-1/2)}{N}\right]$$

This is a **discrete cosine transform (DCT)** вҖ” the discretisation of $\int f(x)\cos(n\pi x/L),dx$, one of the central integrals of this chapter.

In **Whisper** (OpenAI, 2022) and **wav2vec** (Meta), the front-end computes a log-mel spectrogram, which involves:

1. STFT вҶ’ $\int f(t)e^{-i\omega t},dt$ вҖ” trig integrals at every frequency
2. Mel filterbank (weighted sums of spectral energy)
3. Log compression

All three steps have trig integrals at their analytic foundation.

### 10.6 Normalisation Constants in Circular Distributions

**Von Mises distribution** (the "normal distribution on the circle") used in **directional statistics** and in modelling **protein backbone angles** for AlphaFold:

$$p(\theta;\mu,\kappa) = \frac{e^{\kappa\cos(\theta-\mu)}}{2\pi I_0(\kappa)}$$

where $I_0(\kappa) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}e^{\kappa\cos\theta},d\theta$ is the modified Bessel function of order 0.

Computing $I_0(\kappa)$ analytically requires the series expansion: $$e^{\kappa\cos\theta} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty\frac{(\kappa\cos\theta)^n}{n!}$$

and then $\int_0^{2\pi}\cos^n\theta,d\theta$ вҖ” the even-power cosine integrals from В§2, Case B.

This integral is at the heart of **AlphaFold's** learned distributions over torsion angles in protein structure prediction.

## В§11 В· Non-Obvious Worked Examples for Exam Practice

**Example 14:** $\displaystyle\int\frac{\sin^3 x}{\cos^{3/2}x},dx$

Write as $\int\sin^3 x\cos^{-3/2}x,dx$. $m=3$ (odd). Factor $\sin x$:

$$= \int(1-\cos^2 x)\cos^{-3/2}x\sin x,dx$$

Let $u=\cos x$:

$$= -\int(1-u^2)u^{-3/2},du = -\int(u^{-3/2}-u^{1/2}),du = 2u^{-1/2}+\frac{2u^{3/2}}{3}+C$$

$$= \frac{2}{\sqrt{\cos x}}+\frac{2\cos^{3/2}x}{3}+C$$

---

**Example 15:** $\displaystyle\int_0^{\pi}\sin^4 x\cos^2 x,dx$

Both even. Use $\sin^4 x\cos^2 x = (\sin x\cos x)^2\sin^2 x = \frac{\sin^2 2x}{4}\cdot\frac{1-\cos 2x}{2}$:

$$= \frac{1}{8}\int_0^{\pi}\sin^2 2x(1-\cos 2x),dx = \frac{1}{8}\int_0^\pi\sin^2 2x,dx - \frac{1}{8}\int_0^\pi\sin^2 2x\cos 2x,dx$$

First: $\int_0^\pi\frac{1-\cos 4x}{2},dx = \pi/2$.

Second: let $u=\sin 2x$, $du=2\cos 2x,dx$; $u(0)=u(\pi)=0$, so $= 0$.

$$= \frac{1}{8}\cdot\frac{\pi}{2} - 0 = \frac{\pi}{16}$$

---

**Example 16:** $\displaystyle\int\frac{\cos x - \sin x}{\cos x+\sin x},dx$

Observe the numerator is $(\cos x+\sin x)'$ (up to sign вҖ” actually $(\sin x+\cos x)' = \cos x-\sin x$). This is a $u$-sub in disguise:

Let $u = \cos x+\sin x$, $du = (-\sin x+\cos x),dx = (\cos x-\sin x),dx$:

$$= \int\frac{du}{u} = \ln|\cos x+\sin x|+C$$

---

**Example 17** (MIT 18.02 style): $\displaystyle\int_0^{2\pi}|\sin x|,dx$

$|\sin x|$ is non-negative. Split at $\pi$ (where $\sin x$ changes sign):

$$= \int_0^\pi\sin x,dx + \int_\pi^{2\pi}(-\sin x),dx = [-\cos x]_0^\pi + [\cos x]_\pi^{2\pi}$$

$$= (1+1)+(1+1) = 4$$

## В§12 В· Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1 вҖ” Applying the wrong case.** Seeing $\int\sin^4 x\cos^3 x,dx$ and treating it as "both even" because you notice $4$ first. Check both exponents: $\cos$ has exponent $3$ (odd). Factor from $\cos$, use $u=\sin x$.

**Mistake 2 вҖ” Forgetting the Jacobian in substitution.** When $u=\tan x$, $du=\sec^2 x,dx$. You must have exactly $\sec^2 x,dx$ leftover after factoring, or the substitution doesn't close.

**Mistake 3 вҖ” Half-angle with wrong sign.** $\sin^2 x = \frac{1-\cos 2x}{2}$, $\cos^2 x = \frac{1+\cos 2x}{2}$. The minus sign is for $\sin$, plus for $\cos$. Mixing these up gives answers off by $x/2$.

**Mistake 4 вҖ” Not simplifying $\sin^2 x\cos^2 x$ first.** This equals $\frac{1}{4}\sin^2 2x$, which reduces the work dramatically. Students who blindly apply (H1) and (H2) separately get a messier path to the same answer.

**Mistake 5 вҖ” Cyclic reduction with wrong signs in $\tan^n$.** The reduction $\int\tan^n x = \frac{\tan^{n-1}x}{n-1}-\int\tan^{n-2}x$ has a minus sign between the two integrals. This is because $\tan^2 x = \sec^2 x-1$, and the $-1$ term gives $-\int\tan^{n-2}x$.
