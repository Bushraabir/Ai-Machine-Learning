

> _We computed arc length as a number — a fixed $L$ attached to a curve on $[a,b]$. Now we ask a subtler question: what if we let the upper limit move? The arc length function is what you get when you turn arc length into a running total. It is, in a precise sense, the calculus of distance along a curve — and it unlocks all of differential geometry._

## Motivation: From a Number to a Function

In the previous note, arc length was a definite integral — a single number: $$ L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2},dx. $$

Now fix the left endpoint $a$ and let the right endpoint vary. Define:

$$ \boxed{s(x) = \int_a^x \sqrt{1 + [f'(t)]^2},dt, \quad x \in [a, b].} $$

$s(x)$ is the **arc length function**: it measures the distance traveled _along the curve_ from the point $(a, f(a))$ to the point $(x, f(x))$.

Observations immediately:

- $s(a) = 0$ — no distance traveled yet.
- $s(b) = L$ — the total arc length.
- $s$ is **strictly increasing** (provided $f'$ is not identically zero on any interval, i.e., the curve actually moves).
- $s$ is **differentiable** (by FTC), with:

$$ \frac{ds}{dx} = \sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2} > 0. $$

This derivative has a name and a meaning we should linger on.

---

## The Fundamental Identity: $ds/dx$

By the **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus** applied to $s(x) = \int_a^x \sqrt{1+(f')^2},dt$:

$$ \frac{ds}{dx} = \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}. $$

Rewrite this in differential form: $$ ds^2 = dx^2 + dy^2. $$

This is the **Pythagorean theorem in differential form** — the infinitesimal arc length element is the hypotenuse of an infinitesimal right triangle with legs $dx$ and $dy = f'(x),dx$.

```
Zoom into the curve at point (x, f(x)):

           (x + dx,  f(x) + dy)
                *
               /|
          ds  / |  dy = f'(x) dx
             /  |
            *---+
        (x, f(x))
              dx

  ds² = dx² + dy²
  ds  = √(1 + (f')²) dx     ← FTC gives d/dx of s(x)
```

> _The relation $ds^2 = dx^2 + dy^2$ is the 2D Euclidean metric written infinitesimally. In Riemannian geometry this becomes $ds^2 = g_{ij},dx^i,dx^j$ — the generalization to curved spaces. Everything that follows in differential geometry descends from this single identity._

## The Arc Length Function for Parametric Curves

For $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t) \rangle$ (or in 3D: $\langle x(t), y(t), z(t)\rangle$), fix a base point $t = t_0$ and define:

$$ \boxed{s(t) = \int_{t_0}^t |\mathbf{r}'(u)|,du = \int_{t_0}^t \sqrt{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2 + \dot{z}^2},du.} $$

By FTC: $$ \frac{ds}{dt} = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|. $$

**Physical interpretation:** $\mathbf{r}(t)$ is the position of a particle at time $t$. Then $\mathbf{r}'(t)$ is its velocity, $|\mathbf{r}'(t)|$ is its **speed**, and: $$ s(t) = \int_{t_0}^t \text{speed},dt = \text{distance traveled}. $$

The arc length function is literally the odometer reading of a particle moving along the curve.

---

## Reparametrization by Arc Length (Natural Parametrization)

Since $s(t)$ is strictly increasing (assuming $|\mathbf{r}'(t)| > 0$ everywhere — the curve has no **cusps**), it is invertible. We can write $t = t(s)$ and reparametrize the curve as:

$$ \tilde{\mathbf{r}}(s) = \mathbf{r}(t(s)). $$

**The key property:** Under this reparametrization,

$$ \left|\frac{d\tilde{\mathbf{r}}}{ds}\right| = 1. $$

**Proof.** By the chain rule: $$ \frac{d\tilde{\mathbf{r}}}{ds} = \frac{d\mathbf{r}}{dt}\cdot\frac{dt}{ds} = \mathbf{r}'(t) \cdot \frac{1}{ds/dt} = \frac{\mathbf{r}'(t)}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|}. $$ This is a unit vector. $\square$

> _This is a profound fact. No matter how fast or slow you traverse the curve — sprinting, crawling, reversing — the arc-length reparametrization moves at constant unit speed. The curve's geometry (shape, curvature, torsion) is an intrinsic property of the curve itself, independent of how it is traversed. Arc-length parametrization strips away the "clock" and leaves only the "shape."_

**Notation.** The unit tangent vector is: $$ \hat{T}(s) = \frac{d\tilde{\mathbf{r}}}{ds}. $$

In an arbitrary parametrization $t$: $$ \hat{T}(t) = \frac{\mathbf{r}'(t)}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|}. $$

## Worked Example: The Helix

Let $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle a\cos t,, a\sin t,, bt\rangle$, $t \in [0, T]$.

**Step 1 — Compute the speed:** $$ \mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle -a\sin t,, a\cos t,, b\rangle, \quad |\mathbf{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{a^2\sin^2 t + a^2\cos^2 t + b^2} = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}. $$

The speed is **constant** $= \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$. The helix is traversed at uniform speed.

**Step 2 — The arc length function:** $$ s(t) = \int_0^t \sqrt{a^2+b^2},du = t\sqrt{a^2+b^2}. $$

**Step 3 — Invert to get $t(s)$:** $$ t = \frac{s}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}. $$

**Step 4 — Natural parametrization:** $$ \tilde{\mathbf{r}}(s) = \left\langle a\cos\frac{s}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}},; a\sin\frac{s}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}},; \frac{bs}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}\right\rangle. $$

**Step 5 — Verify:** $$ \frac{d\tilde{\mathbf{r}}}{ds} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}\langle -a\sin(\cdot),, a\cos(\cdot),, b\rangle, \quad \left|\frac{d\tilde{\mathbf{r}}}{ds}\right| = \frac{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}}{\sqrt{a^2+b^2}} = 1. \checkmark $$

## Worked Example: Circle of Radius $r$

$\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle r\cos t, r\sin t\rangle$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$.

$$ |\mathbf{r}'(t)| = r, \quad s(t) = rt, \quad t = \frac{s}{r}. $$

Natural parametrization: $$ \tilde{\mathbf{r}}(s) = \left\langle r\cos\frac{s}{r},; r\sin\frac{s}{r}\right\rangle. $$

The parameter $s$ is literally the arc length along the circle — $s = r\theta$ in disguise. At $s = \pi r$, you've traveled half the circumference; at $s = 2\pi r$, the full circle.

## Curvature — The Payoff of Natural Parametrization

Once we have the arc-length parametrization, the **curvature** drops out cleanly.

**Definition.** The curvature $\kappa(s)$ measures how fast the unit tangent vector $\hat{T}$ turns with respect to arc length:

$$ \boxed{\kappa = \left|\frac{d\hat{T}}{ds}\right|.} $$

**Geometric meaning:** A straight line has $\hat{T}$ = constant, so $\kappa = 0$. A circle of radius $r$ turns $\hat{T}$ by $2\pi$ over arc length $2\pi r$, so $\kappa = 1/r$ — constant. High curvature = tight bend; low curvature = gentle curve.

**In arbitrary parametrization** $t$ (via chain rule): $$ \frac{d\hat{T}}{ds} = \frac{d\hat{T}/dt}{ds/dt} = \frac{\hat{T}'(t)}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|}, $$ so: $$ \kappa(t) = \frac{|\hat{T}'(t)|}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|} = \frac{|\mathbf{r}'(t) \times \mathbf{r}''(t)|}{|\mathbf{r}'(t)|^3} \quad \text{(in 3D).} $$

For $y = f(x)$ (2D): $$ \kappa(x) = \frac{|f''(x)|}{(1 + (f'(x))^2)^{3/2}}. $$

> _The denominator $(1+(f')^2)^{3/2}$ is exactly $|ds/dx|^3$ — curvature is the second derivative of position with respect to arc length, not with respect to $x$. This is why the $(f')^2$ correction appears: $x$ is not arc length, and we must adjust._

**The Frenet–Serret Frame** (MIT 18.02 level):

In 3D, at each point along a smooth curve we have three orthonormal vectors: $$ \hat{T} = \frac{\mathbf{r}'}{|\mathbf{r}'|}, \quad \hat{N} = \frac{d\hat{T}/ds}{|d\hat{T}/ds|} = \frac{d\hat{T}/ds}{\kappa}, \quad \hat{B} = \hat{T} \times \hat{N}. $$

- $\hat{T}$: unit tangent (direction of motion)
- $\hat{N}$: principal normal (direction the curve is turning toward)
- $\hat{B}$: binormal (perpendicular to the plane of curvature)

The Frenet–Serret equations: $$ \frac{d\hat{T}}{ds} = \kappa\hat{N}, \quad \frac{d\hat{N}}{ds} = -\kappa\hat{T} + \tau\hat{B}, \quad \frac{d\hat{B}}{ds} = -\tau\hat{N}, $$

where $\tau$ is the **torsion** — how fast the curve twists out of its osculating plane. These equations are defined entirely in terms of $s$.

> _The Frenet–Serret frame is the "moving coordinate system" intrinsic to the curve. Everything — curvature, torsion, shape — is encoded in how $\hat{T}, \hat{N}, \hat{B}$ evolve with arc length. This is the prototype for all of differential geometry: attach a frame to a geometric object, differentiate with respect to the intrinsic parameter, read off the geometry from the result._

## The Radius of Curvature and the Osculating Circle

**Definition.** The **radius of curvature** at a point is: $$ R = \frac{1}{\kappa}. $$

The **osculating circle** at a point $P$ on the curve is the unique circle:

- Passing through $P$
- Having the same tangent as the curve at $P$
- Having the same curvature as the curve at $P$ (radius $= R$)

It is the "best-fit circle" at $P$ — the circle that most closely approximates the curve locally. The center of the osculating circle lies along $\hat{N}$ at distance $R$ from $P$.

```
Curve with osculating circles at three points:

                  ___
               __/   \__          Small osculating circle
              /    *    \         (high curvature, tight bend)
             |     |     |
   ___       |     |     |
__/   \______|     |_____|________
             
  Large osculating         Small osculating
  circle (low κ,           circle (high κ,
  gentle bend)             sharp bend)
```

## Arc Length Function: Properties and Theorems

### Theorem (Smoothness)

If $f \in C^1[a,b]$, then $s \in C^1[a,b]$ with $s'(x) = \sqrt{1+(f'(x))^2} > 0$.

### Theorem (Invertibility)

Since $s'(x) > 0$ everywhere (assuming no stationary points), $s: [a,b] \to [0, L]$ is a $C^1$ diffeomorphism. Its inverse $x = x(s)$ is also $C^1$.

### Theorem (Invariance Under Reparametrization)

Arc length is independent of parametrization. If $\phi: [\alpha,\beta]\to[a,b]$ is a smooth, orientation-preserving reparametrization ($\phi' > 0$), and $\tilde{\mathbf{r}}(\tau) = \mathbf{r}(\phi(\tau))$, then: $$ \int_\alpha^\beta |\tilde{\mathbf{r}}'(\tau)|,d\tau = \int_a^b |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt. $$ **Proof.** By chain rule $\tilde{\mathbf{r}}'(\tau) = \mathbf{r}'(\phi(\tau))\phi'(\tau)$, and $\phi' > 0$: $$ \int_\alpha^\beta |\mathbf{r}'(\phi(\tau))|\phi'(\tau),d\tau = \int_a^b |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt \quad \text{(substitution } t = \phi(\tau)\text{).} \quad \square $$

> _This theorem is what makes arc length a geometric invariant — a property of the curve itself, not of any particular "clock" used to traverse it._

## Why Natural Parametrization Is Rarely Used in Practice

Natural parametrization is the _theoretically_ ideal way to study a curve — but computing it requires:

1. Evaluating $s(t) = \int_{t_0}^t |\mathbf{r}'(u)|,du$ in closed form.
2. Inverting $s(t)$ to get $t(s)$ in closed form.

Both steps almost never yield elementary expressions (as we saw: even the parabola $y=x^2$ gives a messy $s(x)$). So in practice:

- **Theoretical work** (proving theorems about curvature, torsion, geodesics) uses arc-length parametrization because the formulas simplify enormously.
- **Numerical work** (computer graphics, robotics, ML) uses arbitrary parametrizations and compensates by dividing by $|\mathbf{r}'|$ wherever needed.

This is a recurring theme in mathematics: the most natural objects are often computationally intractable, and the art is knowing when to use them.

## Summary: The Chain of Ideas

$$ \text{Arc length formula} ;\xrightarrow{\text{vary upper limit}}; \text{Arc length function } s(t) ;\xrightarrow{\text{FTC}}; \frac{ds}{dt} = |\mathbf{r}'| $$ $$ ;\xrightarrow{\text{invert}}; \text{Natural parametrization } \tilde{\mathbf{r}}(s) ;\xrightarrow{\text{differentiate}}; \hat{T}(s) = \frac{d\tilde{\mathbf{r}}}{ds} ;\xrightarrow{\text{differentiate again}}; \kappa = \left|\frac{d\hat{T}}{ds}\right| $$ $$ ;\xrightarrow{\text{and again}}; \text{Frenet–Serret frame, torsion, osculating circle, ...} $$

Every concept in the classical differential geometry of curves is downstream of the arc length function. It is the master key.

## Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 1. Geodesics and the Arc Length Function on Manifolds

In **geometric deep learning** and **Riemannian ML**, the arc length function generalizes to curved spaces. Given a Riemannian manifold $(\mathcal{M}, g)$ (e.g., the latent space of a VAE equipped with the pullback metric), the arc length of a curve $\gamma: [0,1] \to \mathcal{M}$ is: $$ L[\gamma] = \int_0^1 \sqrt{g_{\gamma(t)}(\dot\gamma(t), \dot\gamma(t))},dt. $$

The **arc length function** $s(t) = \int_0^t\sqrt{g(\dot\gamma,\dot\gamma)},du$ reparametrizes $\gamma$ to unit speed with respect to the manifold's metric. Finding the **geodesic** — the curve minimizing $L[\gamma]$ between two points — requires solving the geodesic ODE: $$ \ddot\gamma^k + \Gamma^k_{ij}\dot\gamma^i\dot\gamma^j = 0, $$ where $\Gamma^k_{ij}$ are the Christoffel symbols encoding the curvature of $\mathcal{M}$. This is the equation of "straight lines" on a curved space — and it is derived by minimizing the arc length functional using the calculus of variations.

### 2. Curvature Regularization in Neural Networks

The curvature $\kappa(s) = |d\hat{T}/ds|$ measures how much a curve bends. In deep learning, the trajectory $\mathbf{h}(t)$ traced by a hidden state (e.g., in a Neural ODE or RNN) can be penalized for high curvature: $$ \mathcal{R}_\kappa = \int_0^T \kappa(t)^2,dt = \int_0^T \frac{|\mathbf{h}'' - (\mathbf{h}'\cdot\hat{T})\mathbf{h}'|^2}{|\mathbf{h}'|^4},dt. $$ Low curvature encourages smooth, interpretable trajectories in state space — a desirable inductive bias. The **elastic net** family of regularizers includes a curvature term in this spirit.

### 3. Unit-Speed Reparametrization in Generative Models

In **flow-based generative models** (normalizing flows, continuous flows), the arc-length-function idea appears as **time reparametrization**. Given a flow $\phi_t$ with velocity field $v_t$, the "arc length" along a trajectory is: $$ s(t) = \int_0^t |v_u(\phi_u(x))|,du. $$ Reparametrizing flows to unit speed (constant $|v|$) simplifies the dynamics and improves numerical stability of ODE solvers — this is the **constant-speed flow** trick used in rectified flows (Liu et al., 2022) and flow matching (Lipman et al., 2022), where the goal is to make trajectories as straight as possible (minimizing arc length curvature) so that simple Euler solvers suffice.

### 4. Natural Gradient and Fisher Arc Length

In **information geometry** (Amari), the parameter space $\Theta$ of a statistical model carries the Fisher information metric $G(\theta)$. The arc length function along a parameter path $\theta(t)$: $$ s(t) = \int_0^t \sqrt{\dot\theta(u)^T G(\theta(u))\dot\theta(u)},du $$ measures the "statistical distance" traveled. **Natural gradient descent** can be understood as gradient descent parametrized by arc length in Fisher metric — each step moves a fixed statistical distance rather than a fixed Euclidean distance. This gives faster convergence near curved regions of the loss landscape because the step size is automatically adapted to the local geometry.

### 5. Curve Discretization and Graph Neural Networks

In **mesh-based learning** (3D shape analysis, computational geometry for deep learning), curves on surfaces are discretized as sequences of points. The arc length function $s(t)$ provides the canonical way to resample these curves uniformly — spacing points equally by arc length rather than by index. This is essential for:

- **Shape correspondence**: matching curves on two surfaces requires arc-length-normalized representations.
- **Spectral graph convolutions**: the Laplace–Beltrami operator on a curve, which defines spectral convolution, is defined using arc length as the intrinsic measure.
- **PointNet and its variants**: when learning on point clouds sampled from curves or surfaces, arc-length-uniform sampling is superior to uniform-in-parameter sampling because it is geometrically intrinsic.

### 6. The Signed Curvature and Attention

In 2D, the **signed curvature** $\kappa(s)$ (positive for left turns, negative for right turns) completely determines a plane curve up to rigid motion (the **Fundamental Theorem of Plane Curves**). This means: a curve in 2D is equivalent to a function $\kappa: [0, L] \to \mathbb{R}$ on arc length.

This encoding — _represent a curve by its curvature as a function of arc length_ — is used in:

- **Shape recognition** (the "curvature scale space" descriptor).
- **Medical image analysis** (characterizing boundaries of tumors, organs).
- **Attention mechanisms over curves**: attending to high-curvature regions (corners, sharp features) of contours in image segmentation.

## Common Mistakes and Traps

1. **Confusing $s(t)$ with $t$.** Arc length is not time. A particle at parameter $t$ has traveled arc length $s(t)$, not $t$ units.
    
2. **Assuming natural parametrization is always computable.** For most curves, $s(t)$ has no closed form. Natural parametrization is a theoretical tool; numerical methods handle practice.
    
3. **Forgetting the chain rule when converting curvature formulas.** $\kappa = |d\hat{T}/ds|$, not $|d\hat{T}/dt|$. The extra $1/|\mathbf{r}'|$ factor comes from the chain rule $d/ds = (1/|\mathbf{r}'|),d/dt$.
    
4. **Cusps.** If $|\mathbf{r}'(t_0)| = 0$, the curve has a cusp at $t_0$: $ds/dt = 0$, the arc length function is not invertible there, and the natural parametrization breaks down. Curvature is undefined at cusps.
    
5. **Orientation.** The arc length function depends on the direction of traversal. Reversing orientation replaces $s(t)$ with $L - s(t)$.
