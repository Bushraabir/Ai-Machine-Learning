

> _Arc length measures distance along a curve. Now rotate that curve around an axis — it sweeps out a surface. The question is: how much surface? The answer is one of the most elegant results in integral calculus, and it rests entirely on the arc length element $ds$ from the previous note._

## Motivation: Rotating a Curve

Take a curve $y = f(x)$ on $[a,b]$ with $f(x) \geq 0$. Rotate it $360°$ around the $x$-axis. The curve sweeps out a **surface of revolution** — a donut-like or vase-like surface depending on $f$.

The central question: what is the **surface area** of this surface?

The strategy is the same as for arc length: **approximate by simple shapes, sum, take a limit.**

---

## Deriving the Formula

### Step 1 — Approximate by Frustums

Divide $[a,b]$ into $n$ sub-intervals. On each sub-interval $[x_{k-1}, x_k]$, approximate the arc by a straight line segment (as in arc length). When this segment is rotated around the $x$-axis, it generates a **frustum** (a truncated cone).

```
One segment rotated:

          r₂ = f(xₖ)
    ___________
   /           \        ← circular top, radius r₂
  /     frustum \
 /_______________\      ← circular bottom, radius r₁ = f(xₖ₋₁)

  Slant height ℓ = arc length of the segment ≈ Δsₖ
```

### Step 2 — Lateral Surface Area of a Frustum

A frustum with radii $r_1, r_2$ and slant height $\ell$ has lateral surface area: $$ A_{\text{frustum}} = \pi(r_1 + r_2),\ell. $$

**Derivation of this formula.** Unroll the frustum into a flat annular sector. The sector has inner radius $R_1$, outer radius $R_2 = R_1 + \ell$, and subtended angle $\theta$. The area is: $$ \frac{1}{2}R_2^2\theta - \frac{1}{2}R_1^2\theta = \frac{\theta}{2}(R_2^2 - R_1^2) = \frac{\theta}{2}(R_2+R_1)\ell. $$ The arc lengths must match: $r_1 = R_1\theta/2\pi \cdot 2\pi \Rightarrow R_1\theta = 2\pi r_1$. Similarly $R_2\theta = 2\pi r_2$. So: $$ A_{\text{frustum}} = \frac{1}{2}(2\pi r_2 + 2\pi r_1)\ell = \pi(r_1 + r_2)\ell. $$

### Step 3 — Apply to the $k$-th Sub-interval

On $[x_{k-1}, x_k]$:

- $r_1 = f(x_{k-1})$, $r_2 = f(x_k)$.
- Slant height $\ell_k = \Delta s_k = \sqrt{(\Delta x_k)^2 + (\Delta y_k)^2} \approx \sqrt{1 + [f'(x_k^*)]^2},\Delta x_k$ (by MVT).

$$ A_k = \pi(f(x_{k-1}) + f(x_k)),\Delta s_k. $$

As $\Delta x_k \to 0$, both $f(x_{k-1})$ and $f(x_k) \to f(x_k^_)$, so: $$ A_k \approx 2\pi f(x_k^_),\Delta s_k = 2\pi f(x_k^_)\sqrt{1+[f'(x_k^_)]^2},\Delta x_k. $$

### Step 4 — Sum and Take the Limit

$$ S = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{k=1}^n 2\pi f(x_k^_)\sqrt{1+[f'(x_k^_)]^2},\Delta x_k. $$

This is a Riemann sum for $2\pi f(x)\sqrt{1+(f'(x))^2}$.

## The Surface Area Formulas

### Rotation About the $x$-axis ($f(x) \geq 0$)

$$ \boxed{S = \int_a^b 2\pi, f(x),\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2},dx = \int_a^b 2\pi, y; ds} $$

### Rotation About the $y$-axis ($x \geq 0$, curve given as $x = g(y)$)

$$ \boxed{S = \int_c^d 2\pi, g(y),\sqrt{1 + [g'(y)]^2},dy = \int_c^d 2\pi, x; ds} $$

### The Unified Mnemonic

Both formulas have the same structure:

$$ S = \int 2\pi \cdot (\text{radius of rotation}) \cdot ds $$

- Rotating about the $x$-axis: radius $= y = f(x)$.
- Rotating about the $y$-axis: radius $= x = g(y)$.
- $ds = \sqrt{1 + (dy/dx)^2},dx$ or $\sqrt{1+(dx/dy)^2},dy$ accordingly.

> _The formula $S = \int 2\pi r,ds$ says: surface area is the integral of circumference ($2\pi r$) times arc length element ($ds$). Each infinitesimal strip of the surface is approximately a thin band of a cylinder, with width $ds$ and circumference $2\pi r$._

```
Infinitesimal strip:

   |←  ds  →|
   |_________|
   |         |   width ds (arc length element)
   |_________|
   
   circumference = 2πr  (where r is distance from axis)
   
   area of strip = 2πr · ds
   
   integrate along the curve → total surface area
```

**Hypotheses.** $f \in C^1[a,b]$, $f(x) \geq 0$ on $[a,b]$. If $f$ touches the axis of rotation ($f(x_0) = 0$ at some interior point), the formula still applies; the strip collapses to a point there.

## For Parametric Curves

If the curve is $x = x(t)$, $y = y(t)$ for $t \in [\alpha, \beta]$, with $y(t) \geq 0$:

$$ \boxed{S = \int_\alpha^\beta 2\pi, y(t),\sqrt{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2},dt} $$

(rotation about $x$-axis). For rotation about $y$-axis, replace $y(t)$ with $x(t)$ (assuming $x(t) \geq 0$).

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Sphere: Rotate $f(x) = \sqrt{r^2 - x^2}$ on $[-r, r]$

This generates a sphere of radius $r$ rotated about the $x$-axis.

$$ f'(x) = \frac{-x}{\sqrt{r^2-x^2}}, \quad 1 + (f')^2 = 1 + \frac{x^2}{r^2 - x^2} = \frac{r^2}{r^2 - x^2}. $$

$$ \sqrt{1+(f')^2} = \frac{r}{\sqrt{r^2-x^2}}. $$

$$ S = \int_{-r}^r 2\pi\sqrt{r^2-x^2} \cdot \frac{r}{\sqrt{r^2-x^2}},dx = \int_{-r}^r 2\pi r,dx = 2\pi r \cdot 2r = 4\pi r^2. $$

$$\boxed{S_{\text{sphere}} = 4\pi r^2.}$$

> _The $\sqrt{r^2-x^2}$ in the numerator and denominator cancel exactly. This miraculous cancellation is not an accident — it reflects the fact that the sphere has constant curvature. The arc length element $ds$ on a sphere is precisely calibrated to make the surface area come out uniformly distributed in $x$. Archimedes knew this: a sphere inscribed in a cylinder has the same lateral surface area as the cylinder. The formula $4\pi r^2$ via calculus is the modern proof of his theorem._

### Example 2 — Cone: Rotate $f(x) = mx$ on $[0, h]$ (slope $m > 0$)

$$ f'(x) = m, \quad \sqrt{1+m^2} = \text{const}. $$

$$ S = \int_0^h 2\pi mx \cdot \sqrt{1+m^2},dx = 2\pi m\sqrt{1+m^2}\int_0^h x,dx = 2\pi m\sqrt{1+m^2}\cdot\frac{h^2}{2} = \pi m h^2\sqrt{1+m^2}. $$

The cone has base radius $R = mh$ and slant height $\ell = h\sqrt{1+m^2}$, so: $$ S = \pi R \ell. \quad \checkmark $$

This recovers the classical formula for the lateral surface area of a cone.

### Example 3 — Paraboloid: Rotate $f(x) = \sqrt{x}$ on $[0, 1]$

$$ f'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}, \quad 1+(f')^2 = 1 + \frac{1}{4x} = \frac{4x+1}{4x}. $$

$$ S = \int_0^1 2\pi\sqrt{x}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{4x+1}}{2\sqrt{x}},dx = \pi\int_0^1\sqrt{4x+1},dx. $$

Let $u = 4x+1$, $du = 4,dx$: $$ S = \frac{\pi}{4}\int_1^5 \sqrt{u},du = \frac{\pi}{4}\cdot\frac{2}{3}u^{3/2}\Big|_1^5 = \frac{\pi}{6}(5^{3/2} - 1) = \frac{\pi}{6}(5\sqrt{5}-1). $$

$$ S = \frac{\pi(5\sqrt{5}-1)}{6} \approx 5.33. $$

### Example 4 — Torus: Rotate a Circle

Rotate the circle $(x - R)^2 + y^2 = r^2$ (center at $(R, 0)$, radius $r < R$) around the $y$-axis. Parametrize: $$ x(t) = R + r\cos t, \quad y(t) = r\sin t, \quad t \in [0, 2\pi]. $$

Rotating about the $y$-axis, radius of rotation $= x(t) = R + r\cos t$: $$ \dot{x} = -r\sin t, \quad \dot{y} = r\cos t, \quad \sqrt{\dot{x}^2+\dot{y}^2} = r. $$

$$ S = \int_0^{2\pi} 2\pi(R + r\cos t)\cdot r,dt = 2\pi r\left[Rt + r\sin t\right]_0^{2\pi} = 2\pi r \cdot 2\pi R. $$

$$ \boxed{S_{\text{torus}} = 4\pi^2 Rr.} $$

> _This is Pappus's theorem in action: $S = 2\pi \bar{x} \cdot L$ where $\bar{x} = R$ is the distance of the centroid of the circle from the axis of rotation, and $L = 2\pi r$ is the circumference of the circle. Pappus's centroid theorem says the surface area of a surface of revolution equals the arc length of the generating curve times the distance traveled by its centroid. The formula $S = \int 2\pi r,ds$ is the infinitesimal version of this theorem._

## Pappus's Centroid Theorem (Surface Area Version)

**Theorem (Pappus, ~300 AD; proved rigorously via calculus).** If a curve $C$ of arc length $L$ is revolved around an external axis, and the centroid of $C$ is at distance $\bar{d}$ from the axis, then the surface area of the resulting surface of revolution is: $$ S = 2\pi\bar{d}\cdot L. $$

**Proof via the formula.** The centroid $\bar{x}$ of the curve with respect to arc length satisfies $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{L}\int x,ds$. So: $$ S = \int 2\pi x,ds = 2\pi \cdot L\bar{x} = 2\pi\bar{d}\cdot L. \quad\square $$

**Power of this theorem.** If you know the centroid and arc length without computing the integral directly, you get the surface area for free. The torus example above is the cleanest illustration.

## Comparison with Arc Length and Volume

It is instructive to see all three measurements of a surface of revolution side by side for $y = f(x) \geq 0$ on $[a,b]$:

|Quantity|Formula|Integrand|
|---|---|---|
|Arc length of curve|$\displaystyle\int_a^b ds$|$\sqrt{1+(f')^2}$|
|Surface area (rot. about $x$)|$\displaystyle\int_a^b 2\pi y,ds$|$2\pi f(x)\sqrt{1+(f')^2}$|
|Volume (rot. about $x$, disk)|$\displaystyle\int_a^b \pi y^2,dx$|$\pi [f(x)]^2$|

> _Arc length and surface area both use $ds$; volume uses $dx$. This is because volume is three-dimensional and $dx$ slices the solid into disks, while surface area is two-dimensional and $ds$ tiles the surface with strips. The extra $y$ in the surface area formula is the radius that sweeps each $ds$ into a ring._

## Gabriel's Horn — A Famous Paradox

Consider $f(x) = 1/x$ on $[1, \infty)$, rotated about the $x$-axis.

**Volume** (disk method): $$ V = \int_1^\infty \pi\left(\frac{1}{x}\right)^2 dx = \pi\int_1^\infty x^{-2},dx = \pi. \quad \textbf{Finite.} $$

**Surface area:** $$ f'(x) = -\frac{1}{x^2}, \quad \sqrt{1+(f')^2} = \sqrt{1 + \frac{1}{x^4}} > 1. $$

$$ S = \int_1^\infty 2\pi\cdot\frac{1}{x}\cdot\sqrt{1+\frac{1}{x^4}},dx > \int_1^\infty \frac{2\pi}{x},dx = \infty. \quad \textbf{Infinite.} $$

**The paradox.** Gabriel's Horn has finite volume ($\pi$ cubic units) but infinite surface area. You could fill it with paint ($\pi$ units), but you could never paint its inner surface (infinite area).

> _The resolution: this is a statement about mathematical idealization, not physical paint. Mathematical paint has zero thickness; physical paint has non-zero thickness. In the mathematical model, the surface area and volume are separate properties with no contradiction. But the intuitive discomfort is real, and it is a good reminder that infinite improper integrals and finite improper integrals can coexist for related quantities of the same object._

### Sphere Decomposition (Archimedes' Insight)

```
Sphere of radius r:

  Vertical slices of equal Δx have equal surface area!
  
  Wide slice (near equator):    Narrow slice (near pole):
  
       _____                       _
      /     \                     | |
   __|       |__     vs.      ____|_|____
  |  |       |  |            |    | |    |
     |_______|
  
  Large circumference,           Small circumference,
  nearly horizontal (small ds)   nearly vertical (large ds)
  
  The two effects cancel: 2πr · ds = 2πr · (r/√(r²-x²)) · dx
                                    = 2πr dx    (constant!)
  
  → Each Δx strip has the same area: 2πr · Δx.
  → Total area = 2πr · 2r = 4πr².   (Archimedes, ~250 BC)
```

## Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

Surface area of revolution is a classical result, but the machinery behind it — integrating over curved surfaces, Riemannian metrics, the idea of "how much surface" — reappears throughout modern ML in sophisticated forms.

### 1. Surface Area in Generative Models (Manifold Hypothesis)

The **manifold hypothesis** in deep learning asserts that high-dimensional data (images, text, audio) lies near a low-dimensional manifold $\mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^D$. The **surface area** (or more precisely, the $k$-dimensional volume) of $\mathcal{M}$ governs how much of the ambient space the data occupies — directly affecting:

- **Sample complexity**: how many data points are needed to cover $\mathcal{M}$ at a given density (related to its $k$-volume).
- **Mode coverage in GANs**: a generator that covers only a small-area sub-manifold of $\mathcal{M}$ suffers mode collapse.
- **Normalization in flows**: the change-of-variables formula for normalizing flows involves the Jacobian determinant, which measures how the flow deforms infinitesimal surface elements — the continuous analogue of $ds$ being mapped to $ds'$.

### 2. The Jacobian and Change of Area in Normalizing Flows

A normalizing flow $f: \mathbb{R}^d \to \mathbb{R}^d$ maps a simple distribution $p_z$ to a complex distribution $p_x$ via: $$ p_x(x) = p_z(f^{-1}(x))\left|\det J_{f^{-1}}(x)\right|. $$ The term $|\det J|$ is the **volume distortion factor** — it measures how the flow stretches or compresses infinitesimal volume elements. For a 2D flow rotating a curve, this is exactly the ratio $ds'/ds$ of new arc length to old arc length. More generally, for a surface: $|\det J|$ measures how surface area elements transform. Training a flow requires computing (or approximating) this determinant, which is why architectures like RealNVP, Glow, and FFJORD are designed to make $|\det J|$ tractable.

### 3. Surface Integrals in Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

**PINNs** (Raissi et al., 2019) solve PDEs by minimizing a physics loss. For PDEs with boundary conditions on a surface $\partial\Omega$, the boundary loss is: $$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{bdry}} = \int_{\partial\Omega} |u_\theta(x) - g(x)|^2,dS, $$ where $dS$ is the surface area element. In 3D, $dS$ is the analogue of $2\pi r,ds$ — the infinitesimal area on the boundary surface. Implementing PINNs requires discretizing this surface integral, which reduces to computing surface areas of patches — the numerical version of exactly what we derived above.

### 4. Gaussian Curvature and Generalization Bounds

The **Gaussian curvature** $K$ of a surface satisfies the **Gauss-Bonnet theorem**: $$ \int_{\mathcal{M}} K,dA = 2\pi\chi(\mathcal{M}), $$ where $\chi$ is the Euler characteristic (a topological invariant: $\chi = 2$ for a sphere, $\chi = 0$ for a torus). The integral $\int K,dA$ is a surface integral of curvature — the "total curvature" of the surface. In learning theory, the curvature of the loss landscape (related to the Hessian) and the curvature of the data manifold both affect **generalization bounds**. The geometry of the loss surface — how much it curves, how large its "surface area" in parameter space is — governs the size of flat minima, which empirically correlates with better generalization (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997; Keskar et al., 2017).

### 5. 3D Shape Learning and Neural Implicit Surfaces

**Neural implicit surfaces** (NeRF, DeepSDF, NeuralMLS) represent 3D shapes as the zero level set of a neural network $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}$: $$ \mathcal{S} = {x \in \mathbb{R}^3 : f_\theta(x) = 0}. $$ Computing the **surface area** of $\mathcal{S}$ requires integrating over the surface: $$ \text{Area}(\mathcal{S}) = \int_{\mathcal{S}} dA = \int_{\mathcal{S}} \frac{|\nabla f_\theta|}{|\nabla f_\theta|},dA. $$ In practice, area is estimated via Monte Carlo sampling on the surface, which requires computing the surface area element — the 3D analogue of $2\pi r,ds$. Regularizing the surface area (penalizing it in the loss) encourages smooth, compact shapes: the **minimal surface regularization** used in many 3D reconstruction networks.

### 6. The Surface Area of the Unit Sphere in $n$ Dimensions

The **surface area** of the unit sphere $\mathbb{S}^{n-1} \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ is: $$ S_{n-1} = \frac{2\pi^{n/2}}{\Gamma(n/2)}. $$ This formula (proved by rotating the sphere and using the Gamma function) appears everywhere in high-dimensional ML:

- **Softmax normalization** in high dimensions: the surface area grows as $\sim (2\pi e/n)^{n/2}$ for large $n$ — concentration of measure on the sphere.
- **von Mises-Fisher distribution**: a distribution on $\mathbb{S}^{n-1}$ whose normalization constant involves $S_{n-1}$.
- **Hyperspherical VAEs** (Davidson et al., 2018): latent spaces on $\mathbb{S}^{n-1}$, requiring integration over the sphere.
- **Concentration of measure**: in high $n$, almost all the surface area of $\mathbb{S}^{n-1}$ is concentrated near the equator — a fact that governs the behavior of dot products between random vectors in high-dimensional embedding spaces.

## Common Mistakes and Traps

1. **Using $dx$ instead of $ds$.** $S = \int 2\pi f(x),dx$ is **wrong**. The correct integrand uses $ds = \sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx$. Missing the arc length factor gives the formula for the area under $2\pi f(x)$, not the surface area.
    
2. **Wrong axis of rotation.** Rotating about the $x$-axis: radius $= y$. Rotating about the $y$-axis: radius $= x$. Swapping these is a common error.
    
3. **Negative $f$ (curve below the axis).** The radius of rotation must be non-negative. If $f(x) < 0$, use $|f(x)|$ as the radius.
    
4. **Confusing surface area with volume.** Surface area uses $ds$; volume (disk/washer method) uses $dx$. They are different integrals of the same function.
    
5. **Forgetting the $2\pi$ factor.** The circumference of a circle of radius $r$ is $2\pi r$, not $\pi r$. The factor of $2\pi$ in $S = \int 2\pi r,ds$ comes from the full $360°$ rotation.
    
6. **Applying to curves with $f < 0$ on part of $[a,b]$.** The formula assumes $f \geq 0$ throughout. If the curve dips below the axis, split the integral at the zeros and use $|f|$.
