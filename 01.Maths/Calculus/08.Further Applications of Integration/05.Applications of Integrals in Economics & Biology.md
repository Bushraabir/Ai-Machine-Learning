## Prelude ‚Äî The Same Engine, Different World

The physics note established that integration is the mathematics of _accumulation_. In economics and biology, the same machinery reappears ‚Äî but what accumulates is different:

|Field|What accumulates|Measure|
|---|---|---|
|Physics|Force, pressure|$dx$, $dA$|
|Economics|Value, surplus, discounted cash flow|$dq$, $dt$|
|Biology|Population, drug dose, probability of survival|$dt$, $dN$|

The Riemann sum template is unchanged: $$Q = \int_a^b f(x), dx = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i^*),\Delta x$$

What changes is the _interpretation_ of $f$ and $x$.

## 1. Consumer Surplus & Producer Surplus

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart ¬ß6.1)

**Setup:** In a market, demand is described by the inverse demand function $p = D(q)$ (willingness to pay) and supply by $p = S(q)$. At equilibrium $(Q^_, P^_)$:

$$D(Q^_) = S(Q^_) = P^*$$

**Consumer Surplus (CS):** Consumers who would have paid _more_ than $P^*$ gain a "surplus" ‚Äî the difference between what they were willing to pay and what they actually paid.

$$\boxed{CS = \int_0^{Q^_} D(q), dq - P^_ \cdot Q^*}$$

**Derivation:** Partition $[0, Q^_]$ into $n$ small intervals of width $\Delta q$. In strip $i$, a consumer buying $\Delta q$ units valued them at $\approx D(q_i^_)$. Surplus on strip $i$: $(D(q_i^_) - P^_)\Delta q$. Sum and take limit: $$CS = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{i=1}^n (D(q_i^_) - P^_)\Delta q = \int_0^{Q^_}(D(q) - P^_), dq \quad \blacksquare$$

**Producer Surplus (PS):** Analogously, producers who would have sold for _less_ than $P^_$ gain surplus: $$\boxed{PS = P^_ \cdot Q^* - \int_0^{Q^*} S(q), dq}$$

**Total Welfare:** $W = CS + PS = \int_0^{Q^*}(D(q) - S(q)), dq$

---

### Example 1.1 ‚Äî Linear Demand & Supply

**Setup:** $D(q) = 120 - 2q$, $S(q) = 20 + 3q$. Find equilibrium and both surpluses.

**Equilibrium:** $$120 - 2q = 20 + 3q \implies 100 = 5q \implies Q^* = 20, \quad P^* = 80$$

**Consumer Surplus:** $$CS = \int_0^{20}(120 - 2q), dq - 80 \cdot 20$$ $$= \left[120q - q^2\right]_0^{20} - 1600 = (2400 - 400) - 1600 = \boxed{400}$$

**Producer Surplus:** $$PS = 80 \cdot 20 - \int_0^{20}(20 + 3q), dq$$ $$= 1600 - \left[20q + \frac{3q^2}{2}\right]_0^{20} = 1600 - (400 + 600) = \boxed{600}$$

**Total welfare:** $W = 400 + 600 = 1000$.

> **Geometric check:** CS is the area of the triangle above $P^* = 80$ and below the demand curve from $q = 0$ to $q = 20$. Base $= 20$, height $= D(0) - P^* = 120 - 80 = 40$. Area $= \frac{1}{2}(20)(40) = 400$. ‚úì

---

### Example 1.2 ‚Äî Nonlinear Demand: Market for a Tech Product

**Setup:** $D(q) = \frac{200}{\sqrt{q+1}}$, $S(q) = 5q + 15$. Find $Q^_$, $P^_$, and $CS$.

**Equilibrium:** $\frac{200}{\sqrt{q+1}} = 5q + 15$. Try $q = 3$: $$\frac{200}{2} = 100, \quad 5(3)+15 = 30. \quad \text{No.}$$

Try $q = 15$: $\frac{200}{4} = 50$, $5(15)+15 = 90$. No. Try $q = 7$: $\frac{200}{\sqrt{8}} = 70.7$, $5(7)+15 = 50$. Getting closer. Try $q = 9$: $\frac{200}{\sqrt{10}} = 63.2$, $60$. Try $q = 24$: $\frac{200}{5} = 40$, $135$. Hmm.

Numerically, equilibrium at approximately $Q^* \approx 9.64$, $P^* \approx 62$.

**CS (exact form):** $$CS = \int_0^{Q^_} \frac{200}{\sqrt{q+1}}, dq - P^_ Q^* = \left[400\sqrt{q+1}\right]_0^{Q^_} - P^_ Q^_$$ $$= 400(\sqrt{Q^_+1} - 1) - P^* Q^*$$

> **Takeaway:** With nonlinear demand, closed-form equilibrium may require numerical methods, but the _surplus integral_ is still analytically tractable once $Q^*$ is known.

## 2. Present Value & Continuous Discounting

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart ¬ß7.8 context)

Money has **time value**: $$1$ received today is worth more than $$1$ received in the future due to earning potential. Under continuous compounding at rate $r$:

$$\text{Future value of } $P \text{ at time } t: \quad FV = Pe^{rt}$$ $$\text{Present value of } $F \text{ received at time } t: \quad PV = Fe^{-rt}$$

For a **continuous income stream** at rate $f(t)$ (dollars per unit time):

$$\boxed{PV = \int_0^T f(t), e^{-rt}, dt}$$

**Derivation:** In time interval $[t, t+dt]$, income received $\approx f(t), dt$. Its present value is $f(t), dt \cdot e^{-rt}$. Accumulate from $0$ to $T$. $\blacksquare$

**Perpetuity** ($T \to \infty$, constant $f$): $$PV = \int_0^\infty f e^{-rt}, dt = f\left[-\frac{e^{-rt}}{r}\right]_0^\infty = \frac{f}{r}$$

---

### Example 2.1 ‚Äî Business Acquisition Valuation

**Setup:** A business generates revenue at rate $f(t) = 50{,}000 + 3{,}000t$ dollars/year (linearly growing). Discount rate $r = 0.06$. What is the present value over a 10-year horizon?

$$PV = \int_0^{10}(50000 + 3000t), e^{-0.06t}, dt$$

Split into two integrals:

**Part 1:** $\int_0^{10} 50000, e^{-0.06t}, dt = 50000 \cdot \left[-\frac{e^{-0.06t}}{0.06}\right]_0^{10}$ $$= \frac{50000}{0.06}(1 - e^{-0.6}) = 833333(1 - 0.5488) = 833333(0.4512) \approx 375{,}963$$

**Part 2:** $\int_0^{10} 3000t, e^{-0.06t}, dt$. By parts: $u = t$, $dv = e^{-0.06t}dt$: $$= 3000\left[-\frac{t, e^{-0.06t}}{0.06} + \frac{1}{0.06}\int_0^{10} e^{-0.06t}, dt\right]$$ $$= 3000\left[-\frac{10 e^{-0.6}}{0.06} + \frac{1}{0.06} \cdot \frac{1-e^{-0.6}}{0.06}\right]$$ $$= 3000\left[-91.47 + 125.33\right] = 3000 \times 33.86 \approx 101{,}580$$

$$\boxed{PV \approx $477{,}543}$$

> **Interpretation:** If you could buy this business for less than $477{,}543 today, it's a positive-NPV investment at a 6% discount rate.

---

### Example 2.2 ‚Äî Continuous Perpetuity

**Setup:** An endowment fund pays out $$80{,}000$/year continuously forever. Discount rate $r = 4%$. What endowment size is needed?

$$PV = \frac{f}{r} = \frac{80000}{0.04} = \boxed{$2{,}000{,}000}$$

The integral $\int_0^\infty 80000, e^{-0.04t}, dt$ converges because the discount factor $e^{-0.04t}$ decays faster than the constant income grows ‚Äî a beautiful consequence of the convergence of improper integrals.

## 3. Total Revenue from Marginal Revenue

### Theoretical Foundation

In economics, **marginal revenue** $MR(q)$ is the revenue from selling one additional unit: $$MR(q) = \frac{d}{dq}[TR(q)] = TR'(q)$$

By the **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus**, total revenue is recovered by: $$\boxed{TR(Q) = TR(0) + \int_0^Q MR(q), dq = \int_0^Q MR(q), dq}$$

(Since $TR(0) = 0$ ‚Äî zero units sold means zero revenue.)

Similarly, **total cost** from marginal cost: $$TC(Q) = FC + \int_0^Q MC(q), dq$$

And **profit**: $$\pi(Q) = \int_0^Q [MR(q) - MC(q)], dq - FC$$

**Profit is maximized where $MR = MC$** ‚Äî this is where the integrand changes sign.

---

### Example 3.1 ‚Äî Monopolist Pricing

**Setup:** A monopolist faces demand $p(q) = 100 - q$, so $TR = pq = (100-q)q = 100q - q^2$ and: $$MR(q) = 100 - 2q$$ $$MC(q) = 10 + q, \quad FC = 200$$

**Profit-maximizing output:** $$MR = MC \implies 100 - 2q = 10 + q \implies 90 = 3q \implies q^* = 30$$

**Profit via integration:** $$\pi = \int_0^{30}(MR - MC), dq - FC = \int_0^{30}(90 - 3q), dq - 200$$ $$= \left[90q - \frac{3q^2}{2}\right]_0^{30} - 200 = (2700 - 1350) - 200 = \boxed{1350 - 200 = 1150}$$

**Cross-check:** $TR(30) = 100(30) - 30^2 = 2100$. $TC(30) = 200 + \int_0^{30}(10+q), dq = 200 + [10q + q^2/2]_0^{30} = 200 + 300 + 450 = 950$. $\pi = 2100 - 950 = 1150$. ‚úì

## 4. Lorenz Curve & Gini Coefficient (Income Inequality)

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart ¬ß6.1 application)

**Lorenz Curve:** $L(x)$ represents the fraction of total income earned by the bottom $x$ fraction of the population. Properties:

- $L(0) = 0$, $L(1) = 1$
- $L(x) \le x$ (lower incomes earn disproportionately less)
- $L$ is convex and increasing

**Perfect equality:** $L(x) = x$ (everyone earns proportionally).

**Gini Coefficient:** $$\boxed{G = 2\int_0^1 [x - L(x)], dx}$$

This measures the area between the line of perfect equality and the Lorenz curve, normalized to $[0,1]$. $G = 0$ is perfect equality; $G = 1$ is perfect inequality.

**Derivation:** The area between $y = x$ and $y = L(x)$ is $\int_0^1 (x - L(x)), dx$. The maximum possible area (perfect inequality: $L(x) = 0$ for $x < 1$) is $\frac{1}{2}$. So $G = \frac{\int_0^1(x - L(x)), dx}{1/2} = 2\int_0^1(x - L(x)), dx$. $\blacksquare$

---

### Example 4.1 ‚Äî Pareto Distribution Lorenz Curve

The **Pareto distribution** (power law) models wealth concentration. Its Lorenz curve: $$L(x) = 1 - (1-x)^{1-1/\alpha} \cdot \frac{...}{}$$

For simplicity, use: $L(x) = x^2$ (a common textbook model).

**Gini coefficient:** $$G = 2\int_0^1 [x - x^2], dx = 2\left[\frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = 2\left[\frac{1}{2} - \frac{1}{3}\right] = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \boxed{\frac{1}{3} \approx 0.333}$$

---

### Example 4.2 ‚Äî Empirical Lorenz Curve

**Setup:** A country's income data gives the Lorenz curve: $$L(x) = \frac{e^{2x} - 1}{e^2 - 1}$$

**Verify:** $L(0) = 0$, $L(1) = 1$. ‚úì $L'(x) = \frac{2e^{2x}}{e^2 - 1} > 0$. ‚úì $L''(x) = \frac{4e^{2x}}{e^2 - 1} > 0$ (convex). ‚úì

**Gini:** $$G = 2\int_0^1\left[x - \frac{e^{2x}-1}{e^2 - 1}\right]dx$$ $$= 2\left[\frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{e^{2x}}{2(e^2-1)} + \frac{x}{e^2-1}\right]_0^1$$ $$= 2\left[\frac{1}{2} - \frac{e^2}{2(e^2-1)} + \frac{1}{e^2-1} + \frac{1}{2(e^2-1)}\right]$$ $$= 2\left[\frac{1}{2} - \frac{e^2 - 1 - 1}{2(e^2-1)}\right] = 2\left[\frac{1}{2} - \frac{e^2-2}{2(e^2-1)}\right]$$ $$= 1 - \frac{e^2-2}{e^2-1} = \frac{e^2-1 - e^2+2}{e^2-1} = \boxed{\frac{1}{e^2-1} \approx 0.156}$$

This is a relatively equal distribution (low Gini). For comparison, Bangladesh's Gini ‚âà 0.33, USA ‚âà 0.41, South Africa ‚âà 0.63.

## 5. Capital Accumulation & Investment

### Theoretical Foundation

If $I(t)$ is the rate of investment (capital added per unit time), then capital stock at time $T$: $$\boxed{K(T) = K(0) + \int_0^T I(t), dt}$$

This is the **FTC in action**: investment is the derivative of capital, so integrating investment recovers capital. This is identical in structure to $\int I, dt = q$ (charge from current) in the physics note.

**Depreciation:** If capital depreciates at rate $\delta$, net capital accumulation: $$\frac{dK}{dt} = I(t) - \delta K(t)$$ This is a first-order linear ODE, solved by the integrating factor method ‚Äî but the _total investment_ $\int_0^T I(t), dt$ is still the core integral.

---

### Example 5.1 ‚Äî Capital Stock Over a Decade

**Setup:** A firm's investment rate follows a business cycle: $I(t) = 5000 + 2000\sin\left(\frac{\pi t}{5}\right)$ million BDT/year. Initial capital $K(0) = 20{,}000$ million BDT. Find $K(10)$.

$$K(10) = 20000 + \int_0^{10}\left(5000 + 2000\sin\frac{\pi t}{5}\right) dt$$ $$= 20000 + \left[5000t - \frac{10000}{\pi}\cos\frac{\pi t}{5}\right]_0^{10}$$ $$= 20000 + \left[(50000 - \frac{10000}{\pi}\cos 2\pi) - (0 - \frac{10000}{\pi})\right]$$ $$= 20000 + 50000 - \frac{10000}{\pi} + \frac{10000}{\pi} = \boxed{70{,}000 \text{ million BDT}}$$

> **Observation:** The sine investment cycle (booms and busts) contributes zero net capital over a full 10-year period ‚Äî the integral of a sine over complete periods is zero. The average rate $5000$/yr $\times 10$ yr $= 50{,}000$ is all that matters.

## 6. Population Growth ‚Äî Logistic Model

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart ¬ß9.4)

**Malthusian (exponential) growth:** $\frac{dN}{dt} = rN$

Solution: $N(t) = N_0 e^{rt}$, which gives $N(t) = N_0 + \int_0^t rN(\tau), d\tau$ ‚Äî a self-referential integral equation.

**Logistic growth:** Carrying capacity $K$ limits growth: $$\frac{dN}{dt} = rN\left(1 - \frac{N}{K}\right)$$

**Exact solution via separation of variables:**

$$\int \frac{dN}{N(1 - N/K)} = \int r, dt$$

**Partial fractions:** $\frac{1}{N(1-N/K)} = \frac{1}{N} + \frac{1/K}{1-N/K}$

$$\int\left(\frac{1}{N} + \frac{1/K}{1-N/K}\right)dN = rt + C$$ $$\ln|N| - \ln|1 - N/K| = rt + C$$ $$\ln\left|\frac{N}{1-N/K}\right| = rt + C$$

Solving for $N$: $$\boxed{N(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \left(\frac{K - N_0}{N_0}\right)e^{-rt}}}$$

**Total growth** from $t=0$ to $t=T$: $$\Delta N = \int_0^T rN\left(1-\frac{N}{K}\right) dt$$ which equals $N(T) - N(0)$ by the FTC ‚Äî a useful consistency check.

---

### Example 6.1 ‚Äî Fish Population in a Lake

**Setup:** A lake initially has $N_0 = 500$ fish. The carrying capacity is $K = 10{,}000$ and growth rate $r = 0.3$/year. When does the population reach 5,000?

**Using logistic formula:** $$5000 = \frac{10000}{1 + 19, e^{-0.3t}}$$ $$1 + 19e^{-0.3t} = 2 \implies 19 e^{-0.3t} = 1 \implies e^{-0.3t} = \frac{1}{19}$$ $$t = \frac{\ln 19}{0.3} = \frac{2.944}{0.3} \approx \boxed{9.81 \text{ years}}$$

**Total fish-years** of stock from $t=0$ to $t=10$ (important for fishery management): $$\int_0^{10} N(t), dt = \int_0^{10} \frac{10000}{1 + 19, e^{-0.3t}}, dt$$

Let $u = 1 + 19e^{-0.3t}$, $du = -5.7 e^{-0.3t}, dt$:

$$= 10000 \int_{u(0)}^{u(10)} \frac{1}{u} \cdot \frac{du}{-5.7\cdot\frac{u-1}{19}} = \frac{10000 \cdot 19}{5.7}\int_{20}^{1+19e^{-3}} \frac{-du}{u(u-1)}$$

This becomes a partial fraction integral. Numerically: $\approx 46{,}500$ fish-years.

> **Ecological significance:** Integrating $N(t)$ over time gives the total biological "resource consumption" or sustainable harvest potential.

## 7. Drug Concentration ‚Äî Pharmacokinetics

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart ¬ß8.5 type)

**One-compartment model:** A drug is administered and eliminated from the body. Concentration $C(t)$ in blood plasma:

- **IV bolus:** $C(t) = C_0 e^{-kt}$ (first-order elimination)
- **Oral/continuous infusion at rate $R$:**

$$\frac{dC}{dt} = R - kC \implies C(t) = \frac{R}{k}(1 - e^{-kt})$$

**Key pharmacokinetic integral ‚Äî Area Under the Curve (AUC):**

$$\boxed{AUC = \int_0^\infty C(t), dt}$$

**Why AUC matters:** By the **bioavailability theorem**, total drug exposure = total amount absorbed / clearance. AUC is proportional to the total amount of drug that reaches systemic circulation. It determines both efficacy and toxicity.

---

### Example 7.1 ‚Äî Intravenous Bolus

**Setup:** A patient receives a 500 mg IV dose. The elimination constant $k = 0.2$ hr‚Åª¬π and volume of distribution $V_d = 20$ L. Thus $C_0 = 500/20 = 25$ mg/L.

$$C(t) = 25 e^{-0.2t} \text{ mg/L}$$

**AUC:** $$AUC = \int_0^\infty 25 e^{-0.2t}, dt = 25 \cdot \left[-\frac{e^{-0.2t}}{0.2}\right]_0^\infty = \frac{25}{0.2} = \boxed{125 \text{ mg¬∑hr/L}}$$

**Half-life:** $t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{k} = \frac{0.693}{0.2} \approx 3.47$ hr.

**Concentration at 8 hours:** $C(8) = 25 e^{-1.6} \approx 5.03$ mg/L.

**Drug remaining** in body at $t = 8$: $V_d \cdot C(8) = 20 \times 5.03 \approx 100.6$ mg.

**Cumulative amount eliminated** by $t = 8$: $$\text{Eliminated} = V_d \int_0^8 k C(t), dt = V_d \cdot k \cdot \frac{C_0}{k}(1 - e^{-0.2 \cdot 8}) = 500(1 - e^{-1.6}) \approx 399.4 \text{ mg}$$ ‚úì (Total: $399.4 + 100.6 = 500$ mg.)

---

### Example 7.2 ‚Äî Repeated Dosing & Steady State

**Setup:** A drug is given as IV bolus dose $D = 100$ mg every $\tau = 6$ hours. Elimination $k = 0.3$/hr. Find the steady-state peak concentration.

After $n$ doses, the trough concentration just before the $(n+1)$th dose accumulates geometrically. At steady state (infinite doses):

$$C_{\text{min,ss}} = \frac{C_0 e^{-k\tau}}{1 - e^{-k\tau}} = \frac{5 \cdot e^{-1.8}}{1 - e^{-1.8}}$$

$e^{-1.8} \approx 0.1653$, $C_0 = D/V_d$. With $V_d = 20$ L: $C_0 = 5$ mg/L.

$$C_{\text{min,ss}} = \frac{5 \times 0.1653}{1 - 0.1653} = \frac{0.827}{0.8347} \approx 0.99 \text{ mg/L}$$ $$C_{\text{max,ss}} = C_{\text{min,ss}} + C_0 = 0.99 + 5 = \boxed{5.99 \text{ mg/L}}$$

**AUC at steady state** (per dosing interval, using integration): $$AUC_{ss} = \int_0^\tau C_{ss}(t), dt = \int_0^6 C_{\text{max,ss}} e^{-0.3t}, dt = \frac{C_{\text{max,ss}}}{k}(1-e^{-k\tau})$$ $$= \frac{5.99}{0.3}(1 - 0.1653) \approx 19.97 \times 0.8347 \approx \boxed{16.67 \text{ mg¬∑hr/L}}$$

## 8. Cardiac Output ‚Äî Dye Dilution Method

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart ¬ß8.5)

**Clinical problem:** How do we measure cardiac output (volume of blood pumped per minute) without invasive surgery?

**Stewart-Hamilton Principle:** Inject a known quantity $A$ mg of dye into a vein. Measure its concentration $c(t)$ at the aorta. Then:

$$\boxed{F = \frac{A}{\int_0^\infty c(t), dt}}$$

where $F$ = cardiac output (L/min).

**Derivation:** In a small time interval $[t, t+dt]$, the volume of blood passing the measurement point is $F, dt$, carrying dye amount $c(t) \cdot F, dt$. Accumulating all dye: $\int_0^\infty c(t) F, dt = A$ (conservation of mass). Solve for $F$. $\blacksquare$

---

### Example 8.1 ‚Äî Dye Dilution Calculation

**Setup:** 5 mg of dye injected. Concentration measurements (mg/L) at the aorta:

|$t$ (min)|0|1|2|3|4|5|6|7|8|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|$c(t)$|0|0.4|1.8|4.0|3.9|2.8|1.6|0.7|0.1|

**Approximate $\int_0^8 c(t), dt$ using Simpson's Rule** ($h = 1$, $n = 8$): $$\int \approx \frac{1}{3}\left[c_0 + 4c_1 + 2c_2 + 4c_3 + 2c_4 + 4c_5 + 2c_6 + 4c_7 + c_8\right]$$ $$= \frac{1}{3}[0 + 1.6 + 3.6 + 16.0 + 7.8 + 11.2 + 3.2 + 2.8 + 0.1]$$ $$= \frac{1}{3}(46.3) \approx 15.43 \text{ mg¬∑min/L}$$

**Cardiac output:** $$F = \frac{5}{15.43} \approx \boxed{0.324 \text{ L/min}} \approx 19.4 \text{ L/hr}$$

_Note: Normal cardiac output is ~5 L/min at rest; this example uses small numbers for clarity._

> **Clinical note:** This formula is used in hospitals today. The integral cannot be computed analytically from real data ‚Äî numerical integration (Simpson's Rule) is essential. This is a direct application of numerical quadrature.

## 9. Survival & Hazard Functions

### Theoretical Foundation

**Survival function** $S(t) = P(T > t)$: probability that a subject survives past time $t$.

**Hazard function** $\lambda(t)$: instantaneous risk of death at time $t$ given survival to $t$: $$\lambda(t) = -\frac{S'(t)}{S(t)} = -\frac{d}{dt}\ln S(t)$$

**Key relationship** (integrating the hazard): $$\boxed{S(t) = e^{-\int_0^t \lambda(u), du} = e^{-\Lambda(t)}}$$

where $\Lambda(t) = \int_0^t \lambda(u), du$ is the **cumulative hazard function**.

**PDF of failure time:** $$f(t) = -S'(t) = \lambda(t), S(t) = \lambda(t), e^{-\Lambda(t)}$$

**Mean lifetime (expected survival time):** $$\mathbb{E}[T] = \int_0^\infty S(t), dt$$

---

### Example 9.1 ‚Äî Weibull Hazard Model

**Setup:** The Weibull distribution has hazard $\lambda(t) = \frac{\beta}{\eta}\left(\frac{t}{\eta}\right)^{\beta-1}$. With $\beta = 2$ (increasing hazard ‚Äî aging), $\eta = 5$ years.

**Cumulative hazard:** $$\Lambda(t) = \int_0^t \frac{2}{\eta}\cdot\frac{u}{\eta}, du = \int_0^t \frac{2u}{\eta^2}, du = \frac{t^2}{\eta^2} = \frac{t^2}{25}$$

**Survival function:** $$S(t) = e^{-t^2/25}$$

**Probability of surviving past 3 years:** $$S(3) = e^{-9/25} = e^{-0.36} \approx 0.698$$

**Mean survival time:** $$\mathbb{E}[T] = \int_0^\infty e^{-t^2/25}, dt = \int_0^\infty e^{-t^2/25}, dt$$

Let $u = t/5$: $= 5\int_0^\infty e^{-u^2}, du = 5 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2} = \frac{5\sqrt{\pi}}{2} \approx \boxed{4.43 \text{ years}}$$

> **The Gaussian integral appears again** ‚Äî this time computing the mean lifetime of a Weibull-distributed survival model. The connection to ¬ß9 of the physics/ML note is deep: the same $\int_0^\infty e^{-u^2}, du = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}$ underlies both the normal distribution and the Weibull mean.

---

### Example 9.2 ‚Äî Median vs Mean Survival (Cancer Study)

For exponential survival $S(t) = e^{-\lambda t}$:

**Mean:** $\mathbb{E}[T] = \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}, dt = \frac{1}{\lambda}$

**Median** $t_{0.5}$: $S(t_{0.5}) = 0.5 \implies e^{-\lambda t_{0.5}} = 0.5 \implies t_{0.5} = \frac{\ln 2}{\lambda}$

**Ratio:** $\frac{t_{0.5}}{\mathbb{E}[T]} = \ln 2 \approx 0.693$

This explains why clinical trials often report **median survival** (robust to outliers) rather than mean ‚Äî but the _mean_ requires the integral $\int_0^\infty S(t), dt$, which clinical statisticians compute via numerical integration from Kaplan-Meier curves.

## 10. Spread of Disease ‚Äî The SIR Model

### Theoretical Foundation

The **SIR model** (Kermack-McKendrick 1927) partitions a population $N$ into:

- $S(t)$: Susceptible
- $I(t)$: Infectious
- $R(t)$: Recovered (immune)

**ODEs:** $$\frac{dS}{dt} = -\beta SI, \qquad \frac{dI}{dt} = \beta SI - \gamma I, \qquad \frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I$$

**Conservation:** $S + I + R = N$ (constant).

**Total infected** over the entire epidemic: $$R(\infty) = \gamma \int_0^\infty I(t), dt$$

**Final size equation** (derived by integrating the SIR equations, dividing, and using separation of variables):

$$\ln\frac{S(0)}{S(\infty)} = \frac{\beta}{\gamma}(S(0) + I(0) - S(\infty)) = \mathcal{R}_0 \cdot \frac{S(0) - S(\infty)}{N}$$

where $\mathcal{R}_0 = \frac{\beta N}{\gamma}$ is the **basic reproduction number**.

---

### Example 10.1 ‚Äî COVID-style Outbreak

**Setup:** A university of $N = 5000$ students. Initially $I_0 = 5$, $S_0 = 4995$. Parameters: $\beta = 0.0003$/person/day, $\gamma = 0.1$/day.

$$\mathcal{R}_0 = \frac{\beta N}{\gamma} = \frac{0.0003 \times 5000}{0.1} = 15$$

**Epidemic will occur** since $\mathcal{R}_0 > 1$.

**Peak infection time** (when $dI/dt = 0$, i.e., $\beta S = \gamma$): $$S^* = \frac{\gamma}{\beta} = \frac{0.1}{0.0003} = 333 \implies \text{peak occurs when } S \text{ drops to } 333$$

**Total fraction eventually infected** $r_\infty = 1 - S(\infty)/N$, solving: $$\ln\frac{S_0}{S_\infty} = \mathcal{R}_0\left(1 - \frac{S_\infty}{N}\right) \approx \mathcal{R}_0 r_\infty$$

For $\mathcal{R}_0 = 15$: $r_\infty \approx 0.9999$ (essentially the entire population). This is consistent with measles-like dynamics.

**Total person-days of infection** (cumulative burden, key for healthcare planning): $$\text{Burden} = \int_0^\infty I(t), dt = \frac{R(\infty)}{\gamma} = \frac{N r_\infty}{\gamma} \approx \frac{4998}{0.1} \approx 49{,}980 \text{ person-days}$$

> **This integral is the fundamental healthcare capacity metric.** Hospital planning requires knowing not just the peak $I(t)$ but the _area under the $I(t)$ curve_ ‚Äî the total patient-days. "Flattening the curve" means reducing the maximum of $I(t)$ while the integral (total infections) may stay similar.

---

### Example 10.2 ‚Äî Herd Immunity Threshold via Integration

The epidemic stops growing when $dI/dt \le 0$, i.e., $S \le \gamma/\beta = N/\mathcal{R}_0$.

**Herd immunity threshold** (fraction that must be immune): $$p_c = 1 - \frac{1}{\mathcal{R}_0}$$

For $\mathcal{R}_0 = 15$: $p_c = 1 - 1/15 = 14/15 \approx 93.3%$.

**Integral verification:** The integral $\int_0^\infty \frac{dS}{dt}, dt = S(\infty) - S(0)$ gives the total reduction in susceptibles, which must equal total infections. This is the FTC applied to population dynamics: accumulating the flow $dS/dt$ recovers the stock $S(t)$.

## ML & AI Connections

> _The same integrals from economics and biology recur directly in modern machine learning._

---

### ML.1 ‚Äî Survival Analysis & Deep Learning (Connection to ¬ß9)

**Cox Proportional Hazards** extended to neural networks (DeepSurv, 2018):

$$\lambda(t \mid \mathbf{x}) = \lambda_0(t) \cdot e^{f_\theta(\mathbf{x})}$$

where $f_\theta$ is a deep neural network. The **partial likelihood** involves:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \prod_{i:, \delta_i=1} \frac{e^{f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_i)}}{\sum_{j \in \mathcal{R}(t_i)} e^{f_\theta(\mathbf{x}_j)}}$$

The **baseline survival** $S_0(t) = e^{-\int_0^t \lambda_0(u), du}$ is computed via numerical integration (Breslow estimator). The entire architecture depends on the cumulative hazard integral from ¬ß9.

---

### ML.2 ‚Äî Expected Reward in Reinforcement Learning (Connection to ¬ß2)

The **discounted return** in RL is: $$G_t = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \gamma^k r_{t+k} \approx \int_0^\infty r(t+\tau) e^{-\lambda \tau}, d\tau$$

This is _exactly_ the present value formula (¬ß2): $$PV = \int_0^T f(t), e^{-rt}, dt$$

with discount factor $e^{-\lambda\tau}$ playing the role of $e^{-rt}$. **The Bellman equation is the discrete version of the PV integral.** Policy gradient methods optimize this integral.

---

### ML.3 ‚Äî Epidemic Modeling ‚Üí Graph Neural Networks

The SIR integral $\int_0^\infty I(t), dt$ is approximated in modern **epidemic GNNs** (e.g., EpiGNN, 2021) which learn $\beta$ and $\gamma$ from data. The ODE constraints: $$S(T) = S(0) - \beta \int_0^T S(t)I(t), dt$$

become physics-informed loss terms. This is **neural ODE meets epidemiology** ‚Äî exactly the connection from the physics note's ¬ß10.7 extended to the SIR system.

---

### ML.4 ‚Äî AUC as a Classification Metric (Connection to ¬ß7)

In machine learning, **AUC-ROC** (Area Under the Receiver Operating Characteristic Curve) is: $$AUC = \int_0^1 TPR(FPR^{-1}(t)), dt = P(\hat{p}(x^+) > \hat{p}(x^-))$$

This is a direct integral ‚Äî the area under the ROC curve. It measures the probability that the model ranks a random positive example higher than a random negative one. Just as pharmacokinetic AUC ($\int C(t), dt$) measures total drug exposure, ML AUC measures total discriminative ability.

---

### ML.5 ‚Äî Gini Impurity & Decision Trees (Connection to ¬ß4)

The **Gini impurity** used in decision tree splits: $$G_{\text{node}} = 1 - \sum_k p_k^2$$

is the discrete analogue of the Gini coefficient from ¬ß4. The Lorenz curve integral $G = 2\int_0^1(x - L(x)), dx$ measures inequality in income; Gini impurity measures "inequality" (mixing) of class labels at a node. Random Forests and XGBoost both use this integral-derived concept at every split.

---

### ML.6 ‚Äî Gaussian Processes & Integral Kernels

A **Gaussian Process** (GP) defines a distribution over functions via a covariance kernel $k(x, x')$. The posterior mean is: $$\mu^*(x) = \mathbf{k}(x, \mathbf{X})\left[\mathbf{K} + \sigma^2 I\right]^{-1}\mathbf{y}$$

The **RBF kernel** $k(x, x') = e^{-|x-x'|^2/2\ell^2}$ is the continuous generalization of the inner product. Its normalization constant $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2\ell^2}, dx = \ell\sqrt{2\pi}$ comes directly from the Gaussian integral (¬ß9.1 of the physics note). GPs underlie Bayesian optimization, which is used for hyperparameter tuning in deep learning.

## Theorems That Recur Constantly

> **FTC (Stock-Flow duality):** $\int_0^T f'(t), dt = f(T) - f(0)$  
> $\quad$ _Investment ‚Üí Capital; Current ‚Üí Charge; $\frac{dN}{dt}$ ‚Üí $N(t)$; $\frac{dR}{dt}$ ‚Üí $R(t)$_
> 
> **Separation of Variables:** $\int \frac{dN}{g(N)} = \int f(t), dt$ ‚Äî the engine of logistic growth, SIR analysis, pharmacokinetics.
> 
> **Improper Integrals:** $\int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda t}, dt = \frac{1}{\lambda}$ ‚Äî appears in present value, AUC, mean survival, pharmacokinetics. All require convergence of $e^{-\lambda t}$ as $t \to \infty$.
> 
> **Gaussian Integral:** $\int_0^\infty e^{-u^2}, du = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}$ ‚Äî Weibull mean lifetime, all Gaussian models in ML.
