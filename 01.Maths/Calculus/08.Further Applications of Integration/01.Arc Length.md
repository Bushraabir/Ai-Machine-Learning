

> _You've been computing areas since Stewart Chapter 5. Areas measure how much a function accumulates vertically. Arc length asks a different question entirely: how far does the curve actually travel? The answer requires a genuinely new idea — and it leads somewhere beautiful._

## Motivation: The Straight-Line Approximation

Take a curve $y = f(x)$ on $[a,b]$. How long is it?

The instinct: break $[a,b]$ into $n$ sub-intervals, approximate the curve on each piece by a straight line segment, sum the lengths, take $n \to \infty$.

On the $k$-th sub-interval $[x_{k-1}, x_k]$ with $\Delta x_k = x_k - x_{k-1}$, the straight-line approximation has length: $$ \ell_k = \sqrt{(\Delta x_k)^2 + (\Delta y_k)^2} $$ where $\Delta y_k = f(x_k) - f(x_{k-1})$.

Factor out $\Delta x_k$: $$ \ell_k = \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\Delta y_k}{\Delta x_k}\right)^2},\Delta x_k. $$

By the **Mean Value Theorem**, since $f$ is differentiable on $[x_{k-1}, x_k]$, there exists $x_k^* \in (x_{k-1}, x_k)$ such that: $$ \frac{\Delta y_k}{\Delta x_k} = f'(x_k^*). $$

So: $$ \ell_k = \sqrt{1 + [f'(x_k^*)]^2},\Delta x_k. $$

Summing and taking the limit: $$ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \sum_{k=1}^n \sqrt{1 + [f'(x_k^*)]^2},\Delta x_k. $$

This is exactly a Riemann sum for $\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}$.

## The Arc Length Formula

$$ \boxed{L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2},dx} $$

**Hypotheses:** $f'$ is continuous on $[a,b]$ (i.e., $f \in C^1[a,b]$). This guarantees the integrand is continuous and the Riemann sums converge.

> _The condition $f \in C^1$ is not just a formality. If $f'$ has jump discontinuities, the MVT argument above fails — the curve may have corners, and arc length still exists but requires more care. Lipschitz functions have arc length even without $C^1$; fractal curves like the Koch snowflake do not._

---

## The Differential of Arc Length

The formula above has an elegant infinitesimal interpretation. Define the **arc length element**:

$$ ds = \sqrt{dx^2 + dy^2} = \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2},dx. $$

Think of $ds$ as the infinitesimal hypotenuse of a right triangle with legs $dx$ (horizontal) and $dy = f'(x),dx$ (vertical):

```
         * (x + dx, y + dy)
        /|
   ds /  | dy = f'(x) dx
      /  |
     *___| 
(x,y)   dx

  ds² = dx² + dy²  →  ds = √(1 + (dy/dx)²) dx
```

Then arc length is simply: $$ L = \int_a^b ds. $$

This notation $ds$ is not merely decorative. It becomes the foundation of:

- **Line integrals** in multivariable calculus (MIT 18.02): $\int_C f,ds$
- **Riemannian geometry**: $ds^2 = g_{ij},dx^i,dx^j$
- **Geodesics** in curved spaces (the straight lines of general relativity and of certain ML models)

## Arc Length for Parametric Curves

When the curve is given parametrically as $x = x(t)$, $y = y(t)$ for $t \in [\alpha, \beta]$:

$$ ds = \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2},dt $$

$$ \boxed{L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{\left(\frac{dx}{dt}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{dt}\right)^2},dt} $$

**Derivation:** On $[t_{k-1}, t_k]$: $$ \ell_k = \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2} = \sqrt{\left(\frac{\Delta x}{\Delta t}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta t}\right)^2},\Delta t \to \sqrt{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2},dt. $$

The parametric form is more general: it handles curves that are not graphs (vertical tangents, self-intersections, closed curves).

**Example — Circle of radius $r$:** $x = r\cos t$, $y = r\sin t$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$. $$ L = \int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt{r^2\sin^2 t + r^2\cos^2 t},dt = \int_0^{2\pi} r,dt = 2\pi r. \checkmark $$

---

## Arc Length in Polar Coordinates

For $r = f(\theta)$, $\theta \in [\alpha, \beta]$, using $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$:

$$ \frac{dx}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\cos\theta - r\sin\theta, \qquad \frac{dy}{d\theta} = \frac{dr}{d\theta}\sin\theta + r\cos\theta. $$

Computing $\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2$: $$ \left(\frac{dx}{d\theta}\right)^2 + \left(\frac{dy}{d\theta}\right)^2 = \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2 + r^2. $$

$$ \boxed{L = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{r^2 + \left(\frac{dr}{d\theta}\right)^2},d\theta} $$

## Arc Length for Space Curves (3D)

For $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle x(t), y(t), z(t)\rangle$, $t \in [\alpha, \beta]$:

$$ \boxed{L = \int_\alpha^\beta |\mathbf{r}'(t)|,dt = \int_\alpha^\beta \sqrt{\dot{x}^2 + \dot{y}^2 + \dot{z}^2},dt} $$

where $|\mathbf{r}'(t)| = |\mathbf{v}(t)|$ is the **speed** of a particle moving along $\mathbf{r}$. This is the central formula of MIT 18.02 — arc length in 3D is the integral of speed.

---

## The Arc Length Function and Natural Parametrization

**Definition.** Fix a base point $a$ on the curve. The **arc length function** is: $$ s(t) = \int_a^t |\mathbf{r}'(u)|,du. $$

By FTC: $\dfrac{ds}{dt} = |\mathbf{r}'(t)|$ (the speed).

**Natural (arc-length) parametrization:** Reparametrize the curve by $s$ itself. If we write $\mathbf{r}$ as a function of $s$: $$ \left|\frac{d\mathbf{r}}{ds}\right| = 1. $$ The tangent vector has unit length. This is the _intrinsic_ way to parametrize a curve — independent of how fast you traverse it.

> _In natural parametrization, the curve reveals its geometry without the distortion of speed. The curvature $\kappa = |d\hat{T}/ds|$ is defined using arc-length parametrization. This is where calculus meets differential geometry._

## Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Catenary: $y = \cosh x$ on $[0, a]$

$$ y' = \sinh x, \quad 1 + (y')^2 = 1 + \sinh^2 x = \cosh^2 x. $$ $$ L = \int_0^a \cosh x,dx = \sinh a. $$

> _The catenary is the shape a hanging cable takes under gravity. Its arc length is $\sinh a$ — a beautiful closed form that would be inaccessible without recognizing the hyperbolic identity $1 + \sinh^2 = \cosh^2$._

### Example 2 — Parabola: $y = x^2$ on $[0, 1]$

$$ y' = 2x, \quad L = \int_0^1 \sqrt{1 + 4x^2},dx. $$

Substitute $2x = \tan\theta$, $dx = \frac{1}{2}\sec^2\theta,d\theta$: $$ \int \sqrt{1+\tan^2\theta} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\sec^2\theta,d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int \sec^3\theta,d\theta. $$

The integral of $\sec^3\theta$ is a classic: $$ \int \sec^3\theta,d\theta = \frac{1}{2}[\sec\theta\tan\theta + \ln|\sec\theta + \tan\theta|] + C. $$

Back-substituting: $\tan\theta = 2x$, $\sec\theta = \sqrt{1+4x^2}$: $$ L = \frac{1}{2}\left[\frac{x\sqrt{1+4x^2}}{1} + \frac{\ln|2x + \sqrt{1+4x^2}|}{2}\right]_0^1 = \frac{\sqrt{5}}{2} + \frac{\ln(2+\sqrt{5})}{4} \approx 1.4789. $$

> _Even a parabola produces a non-trivial computation. This is why arc length integrals almost always require either a clever substitution or numerical methods. The integrand $\sqrt{1+(f')^2}$ is rarely elementary._

### Example 3 — Spiral: $r = \theta$ on $[0, 2\pi]$ (Archimedean Spiral)

$$ \frac{dr}{d\theta} = 1, \quad L = \int_0^{2\pi}\sqrt{\theta^2 + 1},d\theta. $$

This is the same type of integral as Example 2. The result is: $$ L = \frac{1}{2}\left[2\pi\sqrt{4\pi^2+1} + \ln(2\pi + \sqrt{4\pi^2+1})\right] \approx 21.26. $$

### Example 4 — Helix: $\mathbf{r}(t) = \langle \cos t, \sin t, t\rangle$, $t \in [0, 4\pi]$

$$ \mathbf{r}'(t) = \langle -\sin t, \cos t, 1\rangle, \quad |\mathbf{r}'(t)| = \sqrt{\sin^2 t + \cos^2 t + 1} = \sqrt{2}. $$ $$ L = \int_0^{4\pi} \sqrt{2},dt = 4\pi\sqrt{2}. $$

> _The helix is special: constant speed $|\mathbf{r}'| = \sqrt{2}$ means it winds around the cylinder at a steady rate. The extra $\sqrt{2}$ versus $4\pi$ (the circumference of 2 circles) accounts for the vertical climb._

## Why Arc Length Integrals Are Usually Hard

The integrand $\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}$ is almost never elementary. For it to produce a nice antiderivative, you typically need $1 + [f'(x)]^2$ to be a perfect square — which happens only for special functions:

|$f(x)$|$f'(x)$|$1+(f')^2$|Integrable?|
|---|---|---|---|
|$\cosh x$|$\sinh x$|$\cosh^2 x$|✓ Yes|
|$\frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{\ln x}{4}$|$x - \frac{1}{4x}$|$(x + \frac{1}{4x})^2$|✓ Yes|
|$x^2$|$2x$|$1 + 4x^2$|✓ (trig sub, messy)|
|$\sin x$|$\cos x$|$1 + \cos^2 x$|✗ (elliptic integral)|
|$e^x$|$e^x$|$1 + e^{2x}$|✓ (substitution)|

> _The arc length of $y = \sin x$ involves an **elliptic integral** — it cannot be expressed in elementary functions. This is not a failure of technique; it is a theorem of differential Galois theory._

This is precisely why **numerical methods** (Gaussian quadrature, adaptive Simpson) are used whenever arc length appears in applications.

## Curvature (The Next Step)

Arc length is not the end — it is the gateway to curvature.

**Definition.** The **curvature** $\kappa$ of a curve $\mathbf{r}(t)$ measures how fast the unit tangent vector $\hat{T}$ turns with respect to arc length: $$ \kappa = \left|\frac{d\hat{T}}{ds}\right| = \frac{|\mathbf{r}' \times \mathbf{r}''|}{|\mathbf{r}'|^3}. $$

For $y = f(x)$: $$ \kappa = \frac{|f''(x)|}{(1 + (f')^2)^{3/2}}. $$

> _The denominator $(1 + (f')^2)^{3/2}$ is the arc length element cubed — curvature is intrinsically tied to how arc length is measured. A circle of radius $r$ has constant curvature $\kappa = 1/r$._

The **radius of curvature** is $R = 1/\kappa$ — the radius of the "best-fit circle" at that point.

## Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

Arc length and the related differential geometry machinery appear in ML in ways that are deep, not superficial.

### 1. Geodesics in Latent Space (VAEs and Riemannian ML)

A **Variational Autoencoder** encodes data into a latent space $\mathcal{Z} \subset \mathbb{R}^d$. The latent space is not flat in any meaningful probabilistic sense — regions with high data density are "closer together" than regions with low density.

**Riemannian latent space methods** (Arvanitidis et al., 2018) equip the latent space with a metric tensor $G(z)$ derived from the decoder's Jacobian: $$ G(z) = J_f(z)^T J_f(z) + \epsilon I, $$ where $J_f$ is the Jacobian of the decoder $f: \mathcal{Z} \to \mathcal{X}$.

The arc length of a curve $\gamma(t)$ in latent space under this metric is: $$ L[\gamma] = \int_0^1 \sqrt{\dot{\gamma}(t)^T G(\gamma(t)), \dot{\gamma}(t)},dt. $$ This is the **Riemannian arc length** — a direct generalization of $\int|\mathbf{r}'|,dt$ where the inner product is twisted by $G$.

**Geodesics** (shortest paths under this metric) are found by minimizing $L[\gamma]$ over all curves connecting two latent points. Interpolating between two data points along a geodesic produces smoother, more realistic interpolations than straight lines in latent space.

### 2. Neural ODEs and Curve Length in State Space

A Neural ODE defines a trajectory $h(t)$ satisfying $\dot{h} = f_\theta(h,t)$. The arc length of this trajectory in state space is: $$ L = \int_{t_0}^{t_1} |f_\theta(h(t), t)|,dt, $$ which is just $\int|\dot{h}|,dt$ — the integral of speed. This quantity is directly related to the **computational cost** of the ODE solver (the solver must take small steps where the trajectory curves sharply). Regularizing the arc length (penalizing it in the loss) encourages the neural ODE to follow straighter paths, which reduces the number of function evaluations at inference time. The FFJORD model and regularized neural ODEs (Finlay et al., 2020) explicitly minimize trajectory arc length as part of training.

### 3. Fisher Information and the Statistical Manifold

The space of probability distributions ${p_\theta : \theta \in \Theta}$ is a **statistical manifold**. The **Fisher information metric**: $$ g_{ij}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}\left[\frac{\partial \log p_\theta}{\partial \theta_i}\frac{\partial \log p_\theta}{\partial \theta_j}\right] $$ defines a Riemannian metric on parameter space. The arc length under this metric is: $$ L = \int_0^1 \sqrt{\dot\theta(t)^T, G(\theta(t)), \dot\theta(t)},dt. $$

This is the **information-geometric distance** between distributions. It underlies:

- **Natural gradient descent** (Amari, 1998): gradient descent where the step direction accounts for the curvature of the statistical manifold — i.e., moving a fixed arc-length step in distribution space rather than Euclidean parameter space.
- **K-FAC** and second-order optimizers: they approximate the Fisher metric to precondition gradients.
- The **Cramér-Rao bound**: the minimum variance of an estimator is related to the Fisher metric.

> _Natural gradient descent is to ordinary gradient descent what geodesic motion is to straight-line motion — the path that respects the geometry of the space._

### 4. Sequence-to-Sequence Models and Dynamic Time Warping

**Dynamic Time Warping (DTW)** aligns two time series by finding the optimal warping path — a curve in the $(i,j)$ index plane. The cost of a warping path is an analogue of arc length in a weighted 2D grid. DTW is used in time-series classification, speech recognition, and as a differentiable loss for sequence models (soft-DTW, Cuturi & Blondel, 2017).

### 5. Path Length Regularization in GANs

The **path length regularization** in StyleGAN2 (Karras et al., 2020) penalizes the arc length of the mapping from latent codes to generated images varying unpredictably: $$ \mathcal{L}_{\text{PL}} = \mathbb{E}\left[\left(|J_g(z)^T \mathbf{n}| - a\right)^2\right], $$ where $\mathbf{n}$ is a random unit vector and $a$ is a moving average. This encourages the generator to move the same distance in image space for the same arc-length step in latent space — making latent interpolation smooth and controllable. In geometric terms: the generator mapping $g: \mathcal{Z} \to \mathcal{X}$ should be _isometric_ (arc-length-preserving).

### 6. Curve Length in Attention Mechanisms

In **continuous-time sequence models** (e.g., S4, Mamba), the hidden state traces a trajectory through state space over time. The arc length of this trajectory characterizes the model's "expressive capacity" — how much state it can change to process a sequence. Long trajectories correspond to high dynamical complexity; short trajectories correspond to smooth, slowly-varying representations.

## Common Mistakes and Traps

1. **Forgetting the square root.** Arc length is $\int\sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx$, not $\int(1+(f')^2),dx$.
    
2. **Confusing arc length with area.** They use different integrands. Area under $f$: $\int f,dx$. Arc length of $f$: $\int\sqrt{1+(f')^2},dx$.
    
3. **Wrong parametric formula.** $L = \int\sqrt{\dot x^2 + \dot y^2},dt$. Do not differentiate with respect to $x$ when you have a parametric curve.
    
4. **Traversing the curve more than once.** If a parametric curve retraces itself, the integral counts the arc length multiple times. Check whether the parametrization is injective.
    
5. **Applying the Cartesian formula to a non-graph.** If the curve has vertical tangents or self-intersections, use the parametric form instead.
    
6. **Forgetting that most arc length integrals have no closed form.** When you cannot find an antiderivative, use numerical integration. This is expected — not a sign of error.
