## Prelude — Probability IS Integration

There is a profound unity lurking here:

> **Every quantity in continuous probability theory is an integral.**

Probability itself is an integral. Expected value is an integral. Variance is an integral. Information entropy is an integral. The KL divergence is an integral. The evidence lower bound (ELBO) in VAEs is an integral. The likelihood in a Gaussian process is an integral of an integral.

This is not a coincidence — it is the **Kolmogorov foundation**: probability is a measure, and computing probabilities of continuous events _is_ Lebesgue integration. For us, working at the level of Riemann integration, the statement is:

$$\boxed{P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f_X(x), dx}$$

Every formula in this note is a specialization or composition of this single idea.

**The Dictionary:**

|Probability concept|Integration concept|
|---|---|
|Probability of an event|Area under the PDF|
|Expected value|Weighted area ($\int x, f(x), dx$)|
|Variance|Second central moment integral|
|Independence|Product structure of the joint PDF|
|Conditioning|Slice of the joint, renormalized|
|Entropy|$-\int f \ln f, dx$|
|Generating functions|Laplace/Fourier transform of the PDF|

## 1. Probability Density Functions — The Central Definition

### Definition (Rigorous)

A function $f_X: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is a **probability density function (PDF)** for a continuous random variable $X$ if:

**(i) Non-negativity:** $f_X(x) \ge 0$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$

**(ii) Normalization:** $$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x), dx = 1$$

**(iii) Probability via integration:** $$P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f_X(x), dx$$

> **Critical remark:** $f_X(x)$ is _not_ a probability. It is a _density_ — analogous to mass per unit length. The probability requires integrating over an interval. $P(X = a) = \int_a^a f_X(x), dx = 0$ for any single point. This is conceptually sharp: continuous random variables have probability zero of taking any exact value.

---

### Example 1.1 — Verifying a PDF

**Claim:** $f(x) = ce^{-3x}$ for $x \ge 0$ (and $0$ otherwise) is a valid PDF for some $c$. Find $c$.

**Normalization condition:** $$\int_0^\infty ce^{-3x}, dx = 1 \implies c\left[-\frac{e^{-3x}}{3}\right]_0^\infty = 1 \implies \frac{c}{3} = 1 \implies \boxed{c = 3}$$

**Verify:** $f(x) = 3e^{-3x}$ for $x \ge 0$. This is the **Exponential distribution** with parameter $\lambda = 3$.

---

### Example 1.2 — Computing a Probability

**Setup:** $f_X(x) = \frac{3}{8}x^2$ for $0 \le x \le 2$. Find $P(1 \le X \le 2)$.

**Step 1 — Verify normalization:** $$\int_0^2 \frac{3}{8}x^2, dx = \frac{3}{8} \cdot \frac{x^3}{3}\Bigg|_0^2 = \frac{1}{8}(8) = 1 \quad \checkmark$$

**Step 2 — Compute:** $$P(1 \le X \le 2) = \int_1^2 \frac{3}{8}x^2, dx = \frac{3}{8} \cdot \frac{x^3}{3}\Bigg|_1^2 = \frac{1}{8}(8-1) = \boxed{\frac{7}{8}}$$

## 2. Cumulative Distribution Function

### Definition & Fundamental Theorem Connection

$$\boxed{F_X(x) = P(X \le x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f_X(t), dt}$$

**Properties (all derived from the integral):**

- $F$ is non-decreasing (since $f \ge 0$, adding more area cannot decrease $F$)
- $\lim_{x\to-\infty} F(x) = 0$, $\lim_{x\to\infty} F(x) = 1$
- $F$ is right-continuous
- $P(a < X \le b) = F(b) - F(a)$ — **this is the FTC for probability**

**FTC gives back the PDF:** $$f_X(x) = F_X'(x) \quad \text{(almost everywhere)}$$

**The CDF and PDF are a FTC pair.** PDF is the derivative of CDF; CDF is the integral of PDF.

---

### Example 2.1 — CDF of a Power Distribution

**Setup:** $f(x) = 3x^2$ for $0 \le x \le 1$.

$$F(x) = \int_0^x 3t^2, dt = t^3\Big|_0^x = x^3, \quad 0 \le x \le 1$$

**Find the median** $m$: $F(m) = 0.5 \implies m^3 = 0.5 \implies m = (0.5)^{1/3} \approx 0.794$.

**Find the 90th percentile** $p_{0.9}$: $p_{0.9}^3 = 0.9 \implies p_{0.9} = 0.9^{1/3} \approx 0.965$.

> **Connection to §9 (Survival/Biology note):** The survival function $S(x) = 1 - F(x) = 1 - x^3$ gives the probability of exceeding $x$. Every concept in survival analysis is the CDF or its complement, computed via integration.

---

### Example 2.2 — CDF from a Piecewise PDF

**Setup:** $$f(x) = \begin{cases} x & 0 \le x \le 1 \ 2-x & 1 < x \le 2 \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

(Triangular distribution — note: $\int_0^1 x, dx + \int_1^2 (2-x), dx = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} = 1$ ✓)

**CDF for $0 \le x \le 1$:** $$F(x) = \int_0^x t, dt = \frac{x^2}{2}$$

**CDF for $1 < x \le 2$:** $$F(x) = F(1) + \int_1^x (2-t), dt = \frac{1}{2} + \left[2t - \frac{t^2}{2}\right]_1^x = \frac{1}{2} + \left(2x - \frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{3}{2}\right) = 2x - \frac{x^2}{2} - 1$$

**Check:** $F(2) = 4 - 2 - 1 = 1$. ✓

## 3. Expected Value & Moments

### Definition

For a continuous random variable $X$ with PDF $f_X$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x, f_X(x), dx}$$

**Existence:** This integral must _converge absolutely_ — i.e., $\int_{-\infty}^\infty |x|, f_X(x), dx < \infty$. Some distributions (e.g., Cauchy) have **no finite mean** because this integral diverges.

**Interpretation:** $\mathbb{E}[X]$ is the center of mass of the distribution — the balance point of the PDF curve. This is _literally_ the same formula as the center of mass integral from the physics note, with $f_X(x)$ playing the role of linear density $\rho(x)$.

---

### Example 3.1 — Expected Value of Power Distribution

**Setup:** $f(x) = (n+1)x^n$ for $0 \le x \le 1$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^1 x \cdot (n+1)x^n, dx = (n+1)\int_0^1 x^{n+1}, dx = (n+1) \cdot \frac{1}{n+2} = \boxed{\frac{n+1}{n+2}}$$

**Sanity check:** As $n \to \infty$, the distribution concentrates near $x = 1$, and $\mathbb{E}[X] \to 1$. As $n = 0$ (uniform), $\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{1}{2}$. Both are correct. ✓

---

### Example 3.2 — Cauchy Distribution Has No Mean

**PDF:** $f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}$, $x \in \mathbb{R}$.

**Attempt to compute $\mathbb{E}[X]$:** $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{x}{\pi(1+x^2)}, dx = \frac{1}{\pi} \cdot \frac{1}{2}\ln(1+x^2)\Bigg|_{-\infty}^\infty$$

This integral **diverges** (both tails diverge to $\pm\infty$). The Cauchy distribution has **no finite expected value** despite being symmetric about $0$.

> **This is not a mere technicality.** In finance, the Cauchy distribution models fat-tailed returns where "the average return" literally does not exist — the empirical mean of samples from a Cauchy distribution never converges, no matter how much data you collect. The divergence of the integral is the mathematics of this phenomenon.

## 4. Variance, Standard Deviation & Higher Moments

### Definition

**$n$-th moment about the origin:** $$\mu_n' = \mathbb{E}[X^n] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x^n, f_X(x), dx$$

**$n$-th central moment:** $$\mu_n = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^n] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty (x-\mu)^n, f_X(x), dx$$

**Variance ($n=2$):** $$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2] = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2}$$

**Proof of the shortcut formula:** $$\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2] = \int (x-\mu)^2 f(x), dx = \int (x^2 - 2\mu x + \mu^2)f(x), dx$$ $$= \mathbb{E}[X^2] - 2\mu \mathbb{E}[X] + \mu^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - 2\mu^2 + \mu^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mu^2 \quad \blacksquare$$

**Skewness** ($n=3$ central moment, normalized): $\gamma_1 = \frac{\mu_3}{\sigma^3}$ — measures asymmetry.

**Kurtosis** ($n=4$): $\gamma_2 = \frac{\mu_4}{\sigma^4} - 3$ — measures tail heaviness relative to Gaussian.

---

### Example 4.1 — Variance of Triangular Distribution

For the triangular distribution from Example 2.2 with PDF $f(x) = x$ on $[0,1]$ and $f(x) = 2-x$ on $[1,2]$:

**$\mathbb{E}[X]$** (by symmetry about $x=1$): $\mathbb{E}[X] = 1$.

**$\mathbb{E}[X^2]$:** $$= \int_0^1 x^2 \cdot x, dx + \int_1^2 x^2(2-x), dx = \int_0^1 x^3, dx + \int_1^2 (2x^2 - x^3), dx$$ $$= \frac{1}{4} + \left[\frac{2x^3}{3} - \frac{x^4}{4}\right]_1^2 = \frac{1}{4} + \left(\frac{16}{3} - 4\right) - \left(\frac{2}{3} - \frac{1}{4}\right)$$ $$= \frac{1}{4} + \frac{4}{3} - \frac{5}{12} = \frac{3 + 16 - 5}{12} = \frac{14}{12} = \frac{7}{6}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{7}{6} - 1^2 = \frac{1}{6}, \qquad \sigma = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \approx 0.408 \quad \blacksquare$$

## 5. Uniform Distribution

**PDF:** $f(x) = \frac{1}{b-a}$ for $x \in [a,b]$.

**Verification:** $\int_a^b \frac{1}{b-a}, dx = 1$. ✓

**Mean:** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_a^b \frac{x}{b-a}, dx = \frac{1}{b-a} \cdot \frac{b^2-a^2}{2} = \frac{(b-a)(b+a)}{2(b-a)} = \boxed{\frac{a+b}{2}}$$

**Variance:** $$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \frac{1}{b-a}\int_a^b x^2, dx = \frac{b^3-a^3}{3(b-a)} = \frac{a^2+ab+b^2}{3}$$ $$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{a^2+ab+b^2}{3} - \frac{(a+b)^2}{4} = \frac{4(a^2+ab+b^2) - 3(a+b)^2}{12} = \boxed{\frac{(b-a)^2}{12}}$$

---

## 6. Exponential Distribution

**PDF:** $f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x \ge 0$, $\lambda > 0$.

**Normalization:** $\int_0^\infty \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, dx = \lambda \cdot \frac{1}{\lambda} = 1$. ✓

**Mean** (integration by parts: $u=x$, $dv = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}dx$): $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^\infty x \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, dx = \left[-xe^{-\lambda x}\right]_0^\infty + \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda x}, dx = 0 + \frac{1}{\lambda} = \boxed{\frac{1}{\lambda}}$$

**Second moment** (two integrations by parts): $$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \int_0^\infty x^2 \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, dx = \frac{2}{\lambda^2}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} - \frac{1}{\lambda^2} = \boxed{\frac{1}{\lambda^2}}$$

**Memoryless property** (unique to exponential): $$P(X > s+t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)$$

**Proof via integration:** $$P(X > s+t \mid X > s) = \frac{P(X > s+t)}{P(X > s)} = \frac{\int_{s+t}^\infty \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, dx}{\int_s^\infty \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, dx} = \frac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda s}} = e^{-\lambda t} = P(X > t) \quad \blacksquare$$

## 7. The Gaussian — Derived from First Principles

### The Gaussian Integral: Full Proof

**Claim:** $$\boxed{\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}, dx = \sqrt{\pi}}$$

**Proof (MIT 18.02 polar coordinate method):**

Let $I = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}, dx$. Consider $I^2$:

$$I^2 = \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}, dx\right)\left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-y^2}, dy\right) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x^2+y^2)}, dx, dy$$

Convert to polar: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $dA = r, dr, d\theta$:

$$I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2} r, dr, d\theta = 2\pi \int_0^\infty r e^{-r^2}, dr$$

Substitution $u = r^2$, $du = 2r, dr$:

$$= 2\pi \int_0^\infty \frac{1}{2} e^{-u}, du = 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \pi$$

Therefore $I = \sqrt{\pi}$. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary:** With substitution $x = t/(\sigma\sqrt{2})$: $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-t^2/(2\sigma^2)}, dt = \sigma\sqrt{2}\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}, dx = \sigma\sqrt{2\pi}$$

---

### The Normal Distribution

**PDF:** For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$\boxed{f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}}$$

The normalization constant $\frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}$ is _exactly_ $\left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}, dx\right)^{-1} = (\sigma\sqrt{2\pi})^{-1}$, derived above.

**Mean:** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{x}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}, dx$$

Substitute $u = (x-\mu)/\sigma$: $$= \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{\sigma u + \mu}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-u^2/2} \sigma, du = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^\infty u e^{-u^2/2}, du}_{=0 \text{ (odd)}} + \mu\underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-u^2/2}, du}_{=1} = \boxed{\mu}$$

**Variance:** $$\text{Var}(X) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}, dx$$

Substitute $u = (x-\mu)/\sigma$: $$= \sigma^2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty u^2 e^{-u^2/2}, du$$

Integration by parts: $v = u$, $dw = u e^{-u^2/2}, du$: $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty u^2 e^{-u^2/2}, du = \left[-u e^{-u^2/2}\right]_{-\infty}^\infty + \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-u^2/2}, du = 0 + \sqrt{2\pi}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2 \cdot \frac{\sqrt{2\pi}}{\sqrt{2\pi}} = \boxed{\sigma^2}$$

---

### Example 7.1 — The 68-95-99.7 Rule (Exact Integrals)

For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, using the error function $\text{erf}(z) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{\pi}}\int_0^z e^{-t^2}, dt$:

$$P(-k\sigma \le X \le k\sigma) = \text{erf}\left(\frac{k}{\sqrt{2}}\right)$$

|$k$|$P(\mu - k\sigma \le X \le \mu + k\sigma)$|Exact Integral|
|---|---|---|
|1|$\approx 0.6827$|$\text{erf}(1/\sqrt{2})$|
|2|$\approx 0.9545$|$\text{erf}(\sqrt{2})$|
|3|$\approx 0.9973$|$\text{erf}(3/\sqrt{2})$|

These cannot be expressed in elementary closed form — they require the error function, a non-elementary antiderivative defined _by_ the integral. The Gaussian is the canonical example of a function whose integral is non-elementary but profoundly important.

## 8. Gamma Distribution

### The Gamma Function: An Improper Integral

**Definition:** $$\boxed{\Gamma(n) = \int_0^\infty t^{n-1} e^{-t}, dt, \quad n > 0}$$

**Key properties derived from integration:**

**(i) Recursion:** $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)\Gamma(n-1)$

**Proof:** Integration by parts, $u = t^{n-1}$, $dv = e^{-t}, dt$: $$\Gamma(n) = \left[-t^{n-1}e^{-t}\right]_0^\infty + (n-1)\int_0^\infty t^{n-2}e^{-t}, dt = 0 + (n-1)\Gamma(n-1) \quad \blacksquare$$

**(ii) Integer values:** $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ for $n \in \mathbb{N}$

**(iii) Half-integer:** $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$

**Proof:** $\Gamma(1/2) = \int_0^\infty t^{-1/2}e^{-t}, dt$. Let $t = u^2$: $$= \int_0^\infty u^{-1} e^{-u^2} \cdot 2u, du = 2\int_0^\infty e^{-u^2}, du = \sqrt{\pi} \quad \blacksquare$$

---

### Gamma Distribution PDF

**PDF:** $X \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$:

$$f(x) = \frac{x^{\alpha-1} e^{-x/\beta}}{\beta^\alpha, \Gamma(\alpha)}, \quad x > 0$$

**Normalization** (substitution $t = x/\beta$): $$\int_0^\infty \frac{x^{\alpha-1}e^{-x/\beta}}{\beta^\alpha\Gamma(\alpha)}, dx = \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha)}\int_0^\infty t^{\alpha-1}e^{-t}, dt = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)}{\Gamma(\alpha)} = 1 \quad \blacksquare$$

**Mean:** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{1}{\beta^\alpha\Gamma(\alpha)}\int_0^\infty x^\alpha e^{-x/\beta}, dx = \frac{\beta^{\alpha+1}\Gamma(\alpha+1)}{\beta^\alpha\Gamma(\alpha)} = \beta \cdot \alpha = \boxed{\alpha\beta}$$

**Variance:** $\text{Var}(X) = \alpha\beta^2$.

> **Special cases:**
> 
> - $\alpha = 1$: Exponential distribution (§6)
> - $\alpha = n/2$, $\beta = 2$: Chi-squared distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom ($\chi^2_n$) — fundamental in statistics and ML regularization

---

## 9. Beta Distribution

**PDF:** $X \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ on $[0,1]$:

$$f(x) = \frac{x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}}{B(\alpha,\beta)}, \quad x \in [0,1]$$

where the **Beta function** is defined by requiring normalization:

$$B(\alpha,\beta) = \int_0^1 x^{\alpha-1}(1-x)^{\beta-1}, dx$$

**Key identity** (proven via Gamma functions): $$B(\alpha,\beta) = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}$$

**Mean:** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{B(\alpha+1,\beta)}{B(\alpha,\beta)} = \frac{\Gamma(\alpha+1)\Gamma(\alpha+\beta)}{\Gamma(\alpha)\Gamma(\alpha+\beta+1)} = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}$$

**Variance:** $\text{Var}(X) = \frac{\alpha\beta}{(\alpha+\beta)^2(\alpha+\beta+1)}$

> **Why Beta matters for ML:** The Beta distribution is the **conjugate prior** for a Bernoulli/binomial likelihood. In Bayesian deep learning, Beta distributions appear in variational dropout, uncertainty quantification, and Thompson sampling.

---

### Example 9.1 — Bayesian Update via Integration

**Setup:** A classifier's accuracy $\theta \sim \text{Beta}(2,2)$ (prior: slightly centered at 0.5). We observe 7 successes out of 10 trials. Find the posterior.

**Posterior** $\propto$ Likelihood × Prior: $$p(\theta | \text{data}) \propto \theta^7(1-\theta)^3 \cdot \theta^{2-1}(1-\theta)^{2-1} = \theta^8(1-\theta)^4$$

This is proportional to $\text{Beta}(9, 5)$.

**Posterior mean** (normalizing constant found by integration): $$\mathbb{E}[\theta | \text{data}] = \frac{9}{9+5} = \frac{9}{14} \approx 0.643$$

The normalization requires $B(9,5) = \int_0^1 \theta^8(1-\theta)^4, d\theta = \frac{\Gamma(9)\Gamma(5)}{\Gamma(14)} = \frac{8! \cdot 4!}{13!} = \frac{40320 \times 24}{6227020800} = \frac{1}{6435}$.

## 10. Joint Distributions & Marginals

### Definitions

For jointly distributed $(X, Y)$ with **joint PDF** $f_{X,Y}(x,y)$:

$$P((X,Y) \in A) = \iint_A f_{X,Y}(x,y), dA$$

**Marginal PDFs:** $$\boxed{f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_{X,Y}(x,y), dy, \qquad f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx}$$

**Independence:** $X \perp Y$ iff $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_X(x), f_Y(y)$ for all $(x,y)$.

**Covariance:** $$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y] = \iint xy, f_{X,Y}(x,y), dA - \mu_X \mu_Y$$

---

### Example 10.1 — Joint Distribution on a Triangle

**Setup:** $(X,Y)$ has joint PDF $f(x,y) = 8xy$ on the region $0 \le y \le x \le 1$ (a triangle).

**Verify normalization:** $$\int_0^1 \int_0^x 8xy, dy, dx = \int_0^1 8x \cdot \frac{x^2}{2}, dx = \int_0^1 4x^3, dx = [x^4]_0^1 = 1 \quad \checkmark$$

**Marginal of $X$:** $$f_X(x) = \int_0^x 8xy, dy = 8x \cdot \frac{x^2}{2} = 4x^3, \quad 0 \le x \le 1$$

**Marginal of $Y$:** $$f_Y(y) = \int_y^1 8xy, dx = 8y \cdot \frac{1-y^2}{2} = 4y(1-y^2), \quad 0 \le y \le 1$$

**$\mathbb{E}[X]$, $\mathbb{E}[Y]$:** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^1 x \cdot 4x^3, dx = \frac{4}{5}, \qquad \mathbb{E}[Y] = \int_0^1 y \cdot 4y(1-y^2), dy = \int_0^1 (4y^2 - 4y^4), dy = \frac{4}{3} - \frac{4}{5} = \frac{8}{15}$$

**$\mathbb{E}[XY]$:** $$\mathbb{E}[XY] = \int_0^1\int_0^x 8x^2y^2, dy, dx = \int_0^1 \frac{8x^5}{3}, dx = \frac{8}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{6} = \frac{4}{9}$$

$$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \frac{4}{9} - \frac{4}{5}\cdot\frac{8}{15} = \frac{4}{9} - \frac{32}{75} = \frac{100 - 96}{225} = \frac{4}{225} > 0$$

Positive covariance makes sense: the triangle constraint $Y \le X$ means larger $X$ tends to come with larger $Y$.

## 11. Law of the Unconscious Statistician (LOTUS)

### Theorem

If $Y = g(X)$ and $X$ has PDF $f_X$, then: $$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x), f_X(x), dx}$$

You do **not** need the PDF of $Y$ to compute $\mathbb{E}[Y]$ — you compute directly using $f_X$.

---

### Example 11.1 — Expected Squared Deviation

**Setup:** $X \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]$, $g(X) = (X - 0.5)^4$. Find $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$.

$$\mathbb{E}[(X-0.5)^4] = \int_0^1 (x-0.5)^4 \cdot 1, dx$$

Let $u = x - 0.5$: $$= \int_{-0.5}^{0.5} u^4, du = 2\int_0^{0.5} u^4, du = 2 \cdot \frac{u^5}{5}\Bigg|_0^{0.5} = \frac{2}{5} \cdot \frac{1}{32} = \boxed{\frac{1}{80}}$$

## 12. Moment Generating Functions

### Definition

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tx}, f_X(x), dx}$$

**Why it's powerful:** $M_X(t)$ encodes _all_ moments: $$\mathbb{E}[X^n] = M_X^{(n)}(0) = \left.\frac{d^n}{dt^n}M_X(t)\right|_{t=0}$$

**Proof:** $M_X(t) = \int e^{tx} f(x), dx = \int \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(tx)^n}{n!} f(x), dx = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{t^n}{n!}\mathbb{E}[X^n]$. Differentiating $n$ times and setting $t=0$ extracts $\mathbb{E}[X^n]$. $\blacksquare$

---

### Example 12.1 — MGF of the Normal Distribution

For $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$M_X(t) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tx} \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}, dx$$

**Complete the square** in the exponent: $$tx - \frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\left[x^2 - 2(\mu+\sigma^2 t)x + \mu^2\right]$$ $$= -\frac{(x-(\mu+\sigma^2 t))^2}{2\sigma^2} + \mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}$$

Therefore: $$M_X(t) = e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2} \cdot \underbrace{\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(x-(\mu+\sigma^2 t))^2/(2\sigma^2)}, dx}_{=1 \text{ (Normal PDF integrates to 1)}}$$

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2 / 2}}$$

**Extract moments:** $$M_X'(t) = (\mu + \sigma^2 t), e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2} \implies M_X'(0) = \mu = \mathbb{E}[X] \quad \checkmark$$ $$M_X''(t) = (\sigma^2 + (\mu+\sigma^2 t)^2), e^{\mu t+\sigma^2t^2/2} \implies M_X''(0) = \sigma^2 + \mu^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2] \quad \checkmark$$

---

## 13. Convolution of Independent Random Variables

### Theorem

If $X$ and $Y$ are **independent** with PDFs $f_X$ and $f_Y$, then $Z = X + Y$ has PDF:

$$\boxed{f_Z(z) = (f_X * f_Y)(z) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_X(t), f_Y(z-t), dt}$$

**Proof:** The joint PDF of $(X,Y)$ is $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_X(x)f_Y(y)$ (by independence). The CDF of $Z = X+Y$: $$F_Z(z) = P(X+Y \le z) = \iint_{x+y \le z} f_X(x)f_Y(y), dx, dy = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_X(x)\left(\int_{-\infty}^{z-x}f_Y(y), dy\right)dx$$

Differentiate with respect to $z$ (FTC): $$f_Z(z) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_X(x), f_Y(z-x), dx = (f_X * f_Y)(z) \quad \blacksquare$$

**This is the same convolution integral as signal processing** (§8 of the physics note) — the mathematical structure is identical. The sum of independent random variables is the convolution of their distributions.

---

### Example 13.1 — Sum of Two Exponentials

**Setup:** $X, Y \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ independent. Find the PDF of $Z = X+Y$.

$$f_Z(z) = \int_0^z \lambda e^{-\lambda t} \cdot \lambda e^{-\lambda(z-t)}, dt = \lambda^2 e^{-\lambda z} \int_0^z dt = \lambda^2 z e^{-\lambda z}, \quad z \ge 0$$

This is the **Gamma(2, $1/\lambda$)** distribution. ✓

More generally: sum of $n$ independent $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ → $\text{Gamma}(n, 1/\lambda)$. The convolution integral, when applied $n$ times, accumulates the factor from $\frac{z^0}{0!}$ to $\frac{\lambda^n z^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} e^{-\lambda z}$.

---

### Example 13.2 — Sum of Two Normals is Normal

**Setup:** $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$, $Y \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$ independent. Show $Z = X+Y$ is normal.

**Via MGFs (much cleaner than direct convolution):** $$M_Z(t) = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t) = e^{\mu_1 t + \sigma_1^2 t^2/2} \cdot e^{\mu_2 t + \sigma_2^2 t^2/2} = e^{(\mu_1+\mu_2)t + (\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)t^2/2}$$

This is the MGF of $\mathcal{N}(\mu_1+\mu_2,, \sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)$. Since MGFs uniquely determine distributions: $$\boxed{Z \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1+\mu_2,, \sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)} \quad \blacksquare$$

The convolution integral in closed form would require the Gaussian integral machinery — the MGF approach _works because_ the completing-the-square calculation (Example 12.1) was done once.

## 14. Change of Variables — The Jacobian

### Theorem (1D)

If $Y = g(X)$ is monotone and differentiable, and $X$ has PDF $f_X$:

$$\boxed{f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \left|\frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)\right|}$$

**Derivation:** $F_Y(y) = P(Y \le y) = P(g(X) \le y)$. If $g$ is increasing: $= P(X \le g^{-1}(y)) = F_X(g^{-1}(y))$. Differentiate via chain rule: $f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot (g^{-1})'(y)$. $\blacksquare$

**2D Theorem (MIT 18.02 L26):** If $(U, V) = \mathbf{g}(X, Y)$: $$f_{U,V}(u,v) = f_{X,Y}(x(u,v),, y(u,v)) \cdot |J|$$ where the **Jacobian**: $$J = \frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(u,v)} = \begin{vmatrix} \partial x/\partial u & \partial x/\partial v \ \partial y/\partial u & \partial y/\partial v \end{vmatrix}$$

---

### Example 14.1 — Lognormal Distribution

**Setup:** $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. Find the PDF of $Y = e^X$ (lognormal).

$g(x) = e^x \implies g^{-1}(y) = \ln y \implies (g^{-1})'(y) = 1/y$.

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(\ln y) \cdot \frac{1}{y} = \frac{1}{y\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(\ln y - \mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}, \quad y > 0$$

**Mean of lognormal** (requires the MGF of the normal evaluated at $t=1$): $$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \mathbb{E}[e^X] = M_X(1) = e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$$

The $e^{\sigma^2/2}$ factor shows that the mean exceeds $e^\mu$ — the log-normal is right-skewed. **Used in:** stock price models (Black-Scholes), income distributions, rainfall data.

---

### Example 14.2 — Box-Muller Transform (How Computers Sample Gaussians)

**Setup:** $U_1, U_2 \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]$ independent. Define: $$Z_1 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1}\cos(2\pi U_2), \quad Z_2 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1}\sin(2\pi U_2)$$

**Claim:** $Z_1, Z_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ independent.

**Proof via Jacobian:** Let $R = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1}$, $\Theta = 2\pi U_2$. Then $R^2 \sim \text{Exp}(1/2)$ (Chi-squared with 2 df) and $\Theta \sim \text{Uniform}[0,2\pi]$.

Joint PDF of $(R,\Theta)$: $$f_{R,\Theta}(r,\theta) = f_R(r) \cdot f_\Theta(\theta) = \frac{r}{1} e^{-r^2/2} \cdot \frac{1}{2\pi} = \frac{r}{2\pi}e^{-r^2/2}$$

Converting to $(Z_1, Z_2) = (r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$ — polar to Cartesian: Jacobian $= 1/r$ (inverse of Cartesian-to-polar Jacobian $= r$):

$$f_{Z_1,Z_2}(z_1,z_2) = \frac{r}{2\pi}e^{-r^2/2} \cdot \frac{1}{r} = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-(z_1^2+z_2^2)/2} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-z_1^2/2}}_{f_{Z_1}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-z_2^2/2}}_{f_{Z_2}}$$

The joint PDF factors → $Z_1, Z_2$ are independent $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. $\blacksquare$

> **This is how every numerical library (NumPy, PyTorch, TensorFlow) generates Gaussian random numbers.** Every `torch.randn()` call uses a variant of this Jacobian calculation.

## 15. Conditional Expectation as an Integral

### Definition

**Conditional PDF of $Y$ given $X = x$:** $$f_{Y|X}(y|x) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_X(x)}$$

**Conditional expectation:** $$\mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty y, f_{Y|X}(y|x), dy$$

**Law of Total Expectation:** $$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \mathbb{E}_X[\mathbb{E}[Y|X]] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \mathbb{E}[Y|X=x], f_X(x), dx$$

This is a **double integral** — first integrate out $Y$ at fixed $x$, then integrate over $x$. It is Fubini's theorem in probabilistic clothing.

---

### Example 15.1 — Regression Function

**Setup:** Joint PDF $f(x,y) = 6xy^2$ on $0 \le x \le 1$, $0 \le y \le 1$.

**Marginal of $X$:** $f_X(x) = \int_0^1 6xy^2, dy = 6x \cdot \frac{1}{3} = 2x$

**Conditional PDF of $Y|X=x$:** $$f_{Y|X}(y|x) = \frac{6xy^2}{2x} = 3y^2, \quad 0 \le y \le 1$$

(Independent of $x$ — $Y$ and $X$ are independent here.)

**Conditional expectation:** $$\mathbb{E}[Y|X=x] = \int_0^1 y \cdot 3y^2, dy = \frac{3}{4}$$

The regression function is constant — as expected when $X$ and $Y$ are independent.

# Part IV — ML & Deep Learning: Probability Integrals Everywhere

> _This section makes explicit what every deep learning textbook leaves implicit: the integral is the foundation._

---

## ML.1 — Maximum Likelihood Estimation Is Integral Optimization

For i.i.d. data ${x_i}_{i=1}^n$ from distribution $p_\theta$, the **log-likelihood**:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \sum_{i=1}^n \log p_\theta(x_i) \approx n \int \log p_\theta(x), p_{\text{true}}(x), dx$$

This is an **expected value integral** under the true distribution. MLE maximizes the expected log-likelihood — the integral over data space. For continuous models (e.g., VAEs), this expectation cannot be computed exactly and is approximated by Monte Carlo (sample average), just as in §2/§3 above.

---

## ML.2 — Information Entropy

**Shannon entropy** for a continuous distribution:

$$\boxed{H(X) = -\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\log f(x), dx}$$

**Gaussian entropy** (derived from integration):

$$H(\mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)) = -\int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\log f(x), dx$$

$$= -\mathbb{E}\left[-\frac{(X-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2} - \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2)\right] = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) = \frac{1}{2}\log(2\pi e\sigma^2)$$

(Used $\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2] = \sigma^2$.)

**Theorem:** Among all continuous distributions on $\mathbb{R}$ with fixed mean and variance, **the Gaussian maximizes entropy** — proven by calculus of variations on the entropy integral. This is why the Gaussian is the "most uncertain" distribution given second-moment constraints — a fact at the heart of maximum entropy models.

---

## ML.3 — KL Divergence: The Distance Between Distributions

$$\boxed{D_{KL}(P | Q) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}, dx}$$

**Non-negativity** (Gibbs' inequality) — proven by Jensen's inequality applied to the integral:

$$D_{KL}(P|Q) = -\int p(x)\log\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}, dx \ge -\log\int p(x)\cdot\frac{q(x)}{p(x)}, dx = -\log 1 = 0$$

**KL between two Gaussians** (closed form from evaluating the integral):

$$D_{KL}(\mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\sigma_1^2)| \mathcal{N}(\mu_2,\sigma_2^2)) = \log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1} + \frac{\sigma_1^2 + (\mu_1-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2} - \frac{1}{2}$$

This appears in **VAE loss functions** as the regularization term, forcing the encoder to match a standard Gaussian prior. The entire VAE training objective is:

$$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z|x)}[\log p_\theta(x|z)]}_{\text{reconstruction: } \int \log p_\theta \cdot q_\phi, dz} - \underbrace{D_{KL}(q_\phi(z|x) | p(z))}_{\text{two Gaussian integrals}}$$

Both terms are integrals.

---

## ML.4 — The Evidence Lower Bound (ELBO) is an Integral

In **Variational Inference**, we want $\log p(x)$ but it requires an intractable integral:

$$\log p(x) = \log \int p(x|z), p(z), dz$$

We lower bound it (Jensen's inequality on the integral):

$$\log p(x) \ge \int q(z)\log\frac{p(x,z)}{q(z)}, dz =: \text{ELBO}(q)$$

The ELBO is a functional of $q$ — optimizing over it is **variational calculus applied to integrals**. This is the mathematical core of:

- Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
- Bayesian Neural Networks
- Diffusion Models (score matching is related)

---

## ML.5 — Monte Carlo Integration: SGD as Approximate Integration

**Problem:** $I = \int_\mathcal{X} f(x), p(x), dx = \mathbb{E}_p[f(X)]$

**Monte Carlo estimate:** $\hat{I}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i)$, $x_i \sim p$.

**Strong Law:** $\hat{I}_n \to I$ almost surely (law of large numbers).

**Rate of convergence** (Central Limit Theorem): $$\sqrt{n}(\hat{I}_n - I) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \text{Var}_p[f(X)])$$

Error $\sim O(1/\sqrt{n})$, **independent of dimension**. This is why Monte Carlo is preferred over numerical quadrature in high dimensions (quadrature error degrades exponentially with dimension — the "curse of dimensionality").

**SGD is mini-batch Monte Carlo integration** of the gradient:

$$\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} = \int \nabla_\theta \ell(f_\theta(x), y), p(x,y), dx, dy \approx \frac{1}{B}\sum_{i \in \text{batch}} \nabla_\theta \ell(f_\theta(x_i), y_i)$$

Every SGD step is an **unbiased Monte Carlo estimate of the true gradient integral**.

---

## ML.6 — The Reparameterization Trick: Differentiating Through an Integral

**Problem:** In a VAE, the encoder outputs $q_\phi(z|x) = \mathcal{N}(\mu_\phi, \sigma_\phi^2)$ and we need:

$$\nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi}[f(z)] = \nabla_\phi \int f(z), q_\phi(z|x), dz$$

Naive Monte Carlo can't differentiate through the sampling step.

**Reparameterization:** Write $z = \mu_\phi + \sigma_\phi \varepsilon$, $\varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$:

$$\int f(z), q_\phi(z|x), dz = \int f(\mu_\phi + \sigma_\phi \varepsilon), \phi(\varepsilon), d\varepsilon$$

Now $\phi(\varepsilon)$ is _independent of $\phi$_, so we can move $\nabla_\phi$ inside the integral:

$$\nabla_\phi \int f(\mu_\phi + \sigma_\phi \varepsilon), \phi(\varepsilon), d\varepsilon = \int \nabla_\phi f(\mu_\phi + \sigma_\phi \varepsilon), \phi(\varepsilon), d\varepsilon$$

This is the **Leibniz rule** (differentiation under the integral sign), applied to make backpropagation through stochastic layers possible. Without this, VAEs cannot be trained.

**Leibniz Rule (used here):** $$\frac{d}{d\alpha}\int_a^b f(x, \alpha), dx = \int_a^b \frac{\partial f}{\partial \alpha}(x, \alpha), dx$$

(valid when $\partial f/\partial\alpha$ is continuous and bounds $a,b$ are independent of $\alpha$.)

---

## ML.7 — Score Function & Fisher Information

The **score function** is: $$s(x; \theta) = \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x)$$

**Fisher Information Matrix:** $$\mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[s(x;\theta) s(x;\theta)^T] = \int \nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x), (\nabla_\theta \log p_\theta(x))^T, p_\theta(x), dx$$

This is an **integral of an outer product** — a matrix-valued integral. It appears in:

- Natural Gradient descent (K-FAC optimizer)
- Score matching (diffusion models)
- Cramér-Rao bound (fundamental lower bound on variance of any estimator)

**Cramér-Rao Bound:** $$\text{Var}(\hat\theta) \ge \mathcal{I}(\theta)^{-1}$$

The bound is an integral inequality — proven by applying Cauchy-Schwarz to the integral $\int s(x;\theta), p_\theta(x), dx$.

## Master Formula Sheet

$$\text{(PDF normalization)}\quad \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x), dx = 1$$

$$\text{(Probability)}\quad P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x), dx$$

$$\text{(CDF)}\quad F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t), dt, \qquad f(x) = F'(x)$$

$$\text{(Mean)}\quad \mu = \mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x, f(x), dx$$

$$\text{(LOTUS)}\quad \mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x), f(x), dx$$

$$\text{(Variance)}\quad \sigma^2 = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \mu^2 = \int x^2 f(x), dx - \left(\int x, f(x), dx\right)^2$$

$$\text{(Joint → Marginal)}\quad f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_{X,Y}(x,y), dy$$

$$\text{(Convolution)}\quad f_{X+Y}(z) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f_X(t), f_Y(z-t), dt$$

$$\text{(Change of variables)}\quad f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y))\left|\frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)\right|$$

$$\text{(MGF)}\quad M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = \int e^{tx} f(x), dx$$

$$\text{(Entropy)}\quad H(X) = -\int f(x)\log f(x), dx$$

$$\text{(KL divergence)}\quad D_{KL}(P|Q) = \int p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}, dx$$

$$\text{(Gaussian integral)}\quad \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2}, dx = \sqrt{\pi}, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/(2\sigma^2)}, dx = \sigma\sqrt{2\pi}$$

$$\text{(Gamma function)}\quad \Gamma(n) = \int_0^\infty t^{n-1}e^{-t}, dt = (n-1)!, \qquad \Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$$

$$\text{(Leibniz rule)}\quad \frac{d}{d\alpha}\int_a^b f(x,\alpha), dx = \int_a^b \frac{\partial f}{\partial\alpha}(x,\alpha), dx$$
