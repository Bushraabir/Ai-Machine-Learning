## Prelude — Why Integration Is the Language of Accumulation

The central philosophical move of integral calculus is this:

> **A complicated whole = infinitely many simple pieces, summed.**

Whenever a physical quantity varies continuously — force, pressure, density, voltage — you cannot use a single formula. You must _accumulate_. The integral is the rigorous formalization of that accumulation.

**The Master Template (Stewart §5.2, MIT 18.02 L16):**

$$\boxed{Q = \int_a^b f(x), dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^{n} f(x_i^*), \Delta x}$$

Every application below is a specialization of this template. The art lies in correctly identifying $f(x)$ and the appropriate measure $dx$ (or $dA$, $dV$, $ds$…).

## 1. Work Done by a Variable Force

### Theoretical Foundation

Newton's definition of work for a _constant_ force $F$ over displacement $d$ is $W = Fd$. This immediately fails when $F$ varies.

**Theorem (Stewart §6.4):** If a force $F(x)$ acts on an object along the $x$-axis from $x = a$ to $x = b$, then the work done is

$$\boxed{W = \int_a^b F(x), dx}$$

**Proof sketch:** Partition $[a,b]$ into $n$ subintervals of width $\Delta x$. On each subinterval $[x_{i-1}, x_i]$, approximate $F$ as constant $\approx F(x_i^_)$. Work done on piece $i$: $\Delta W_i \approx F(x_i^_)\Delta x$. Total: $W \approx \sum_{i=1}^n F(x_i^*)\Delta x$. Taking $n \to \infty$ recovers the integral by definition of the Riemann integral. $\blacksquare$

---

### Example 1.1 — Spring (Hooke's Law)

**Setup:** A spring has natural length 1 m. It requires 40 N to stretch it 0.1 m beyond natural length. How much work is done stretching it from natural length to 0.4 m beyond?

**Hooke's Law:** $F(x) = kx$, where $x$ is displacement from natural length.

**Step 1 — Find $k$:** $$F(0.1) = 40 \implies k(0.1) = 40 \implies k = 400 \text{ N/m}$$

**Step 2 — Integrate:** $$W = \int_0^{0.4} 400x, dx = 400 \cdot \frac{x^2}{2}\Bigg|_0^{0.4} = 200(0.16) = \boxed{32 \text{ J}}$$

**Sanity check:** The force at $x = 0.4$ is $F(0.4) = 160$ N. The average force is $\frac{0 + 160}{2} = 80$ N. Work $= 80 \times 0.4 = 32$ J. ✓ (Linear force ⟹ average works here.)

---

### Example 1.2 — Lifting a Leaky Bucket (Non-Constant Mass)

**Setup:** A 10 kg bucket is pulled up a 20 m shaft. Water leaks at a constant rate; the bucket starts with 30 kg of water and is empty at the top. What is the total work done against gravity?

**Model:** At height $x$ meters, fraction of rope remaining = $x/20$... rather, fraction of rope _pulled_ = $x/20$. Water mass remaining: $m_w(x) = 30\left(1 - \frac{x}{20}\right) = 30 - \frac{3x}{2}$.

Total mass at height $x$: $$m(x) = 10 + 30 - \frac{3x}{2} = 40 - \frac{3x}{2} \quad \text{kg}$$

Force (weight): $$F(x) = m(x) \cdot g = \left(40 - \frac{3x}{2}\right)(9.8) \text{ N}$$

**Work:** $$W = \int_0^{20} \left(40 - \frac{3x}{2}\right)(9.8), dx$$ $$= 9.8 \int_0^{20} \left(40 - \frac{3x}{2}\right) dx = 9.8 \left[40x - \frac{3x^2}{4}\right]_0^{20}$$ $$= 9.8 \left[800 - \frac{3(400)}{4}\right] = 9.8[800 - 300] = 9.8 \times 500 = \boxed{4900 \text{ J}}$$

> **Annotation:** Notice how the integral automatically handles the variable mass — no clever tricks, just correct modeling and accumulation.

## 2. Center of Mass & Moments

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart §8.3, MIT 18.02 L20)

**Definition:** For a lamina (thin plate) with density $\rho(x,y)$ over region $R$, the **center of mass** $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$ is

$$\bar{x} = \frac{M_y}{m} = \frac{\iint_R x, \rho(x,y), dA}{\iint_R \rho(x,y), dA}, \qquad \bar{y} = \frac{M_x}{m} = \frac{\iint_R y, \rho(x,y), dA}{\iint_R \rho(x,y), dA}$$

For a 1D rod of length $L$ with linear density $\rho(x)$:

$$m = \int_0^L \rho(x), dx, \qquad \bar{x} = \frac{\int_0^L x, \rho(x), dx}{\int_0^L \rho(x), dx}$$

**Why this formula?** The moment $M_y = \int x, dm$ measures the "turning tendency" about the $y$-axis, weighted by distance. The center of mass is where a single pivot would balance the distribution.

---

### Example 2.1 — Non-Uniform Rod

**Setup:** A rod of length 4 m has linear density $\rho(x) = 2 + \sin(\pi x / 4)$ kg/m. Find its center of mass.

**Total mass:** $$m = \int_0^4 \left(2 + \sin\frac{\pi x}{4}\right) dx = \left[2x - \frac{4}{\pi}\cos\frac{\pi x}{4}\right]_0^4$$ $$= \left[8 - \frac{4}{\pi}\cos\pi\right] - \left[0 - \frac{4}{\pi}\cos 0\right] = 8 + \frac{4}{\pi} + \frac{4}{\pi} = 8 + \frac{8}{\pi} \approx 10.546 \text{ kg}$$

**First moment:** $$M_y = \int_0^4 x\left(2 + \sin\frac{\pi x}{4}\right) dx$$

Split: $\int_0^4 2x, dx + \int_0^4 x\sin\frac{\pi x}{4}, dx$

$\int_0^4 2x, dx = [x^2]_0^4 = 16$

For $\int_0^4 x\sin\frac{\pi x}{4}, dx$, integrate by parts: $u = x$, $dv = \sin\frac{\pi x}{4} dx$: $$= \left[-\frac{4x}{\pi}\cos\frac{\pi x}{4}\right]_0^4 + \int_0^4 \frac{4}{\pi}\cos\frac{\pi x}{4}, dx$$ $$= \left[-\frac{16}{\pi}(-1) - 0\right] + \frac{4}{\pi}\left[\frac{4}{\pi}\sin\frac{\pi x}{4}\right]_0^4$$ $$= \frac{16}{\pi} + \frac{16}{\pi^2}[\sin\pi - \sin 0] = \frac{16}{\pi}$$

So $M_y = 16 + \frac{16}{\pi}$.

**Center of mass:** $$\bar{x} = \frac{16 + \frac{16}{\pi}}{8 + \frac{8}{\pi}} = \frac{16\left(1 + \frac{1}{\pi}\right)}{8\left(1 + \frac{1}{\pi}\right)} = \boxed{2 \text{ m}}$$

> **Beautiful result:** $\bar{x} = 2$ m, the geometric midpoint! The sine density is symmetric about $x = 2$, so the center of mass lands at the center. The integral "saw" this symmetry automatically.

---

### Example 2.2 — 2D Lamina with Variable Density

**Setup:** A triangular lamina with vertices $(0,0)$, $(2,0)$, $(0,3)$ has density $\rho(x,y) = xy$. Find $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$.

**Region:** $0 \le x \le 2$, $0 \le y \le 3 - \frac{3x}{2}$ (below the line $\frac{x}{2} + \frac{y}{3} = 1$).

**Mass:** $$m = \int_0^2 \int_0^{3-3x/2} xy, dy, dx = \int_0^2 x \cdot \frac{y^2}{2}\Bigg|_0^{3-3x/2} dx$$ $$= \int_0^2 \frac{x}{2}\left(3 - \frac{3x}{2}\right)^2 dx = \int_0^2 \frac{x}{2} \cdot 9\left(1 - \frac{x}{2}\right)^2 dx$$ $$= \frac{9}{2}\int_0^2 x\left(1 - x + \frac{x^2}{4}\right) dx = \frac{9}{2}\int_0^2 \left(x - x^2 + \frac{x^3}{4}\right) dx$$ $$= \frac{9}{2}\left[2 - \frac{8}{3} + 1\right] = \frac{9}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{3}{2}$$

**$M_y$** (for $\bar{x}$): $$M_y = \int_0^2 \int_0^{3-3x/2} x^2 y, dy, dx = \frac{9}{2}\int_0^2 x^2\left(1-\frac{x}{2}\right)^2 dx = \frac{9}{2} \cdot \frac{4}{15} = \frac{6}{5}$$

$$\bar{x} = \frac{M_y}{m} = \frac{6/5}{3/2} = \frac{4}{5} = 0.8$$

(Finding $\bar{y}$ analogously gives $\bar{y} = \frac{6}{5}$.)

$$\boxed{(\bar{x}, \bar{y}) = (0.8,\ 1.2)}$$

## 3. Fluid Pressure & Hydrostatic Force

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart §8.3)

**Pascal's Principle:** Pressure at depth $d$ in a fluid of density $\rho$ is $P = \rho g d$.

The problem: find the _total force_ on a submerged vertical plate. Since pressure varies with depth, we integrate.

**Setup:** Plate submerged with its top at depth $a$ and bottom at depth $b$. At depth $x$, the plate has horizontal width $w(x)$.

$$\boxed{F = \int_a^b \rho g x \cdot w(x), dx}$$

**Derivation:** Strip at depth $x$ of height $dx$ has area $dA = w(x), dx$. Pressure on strip: $P(x) = \rho g x$. Force on strip: $dF = P(x), dA = \rho g x \cdot w(x), dx$. Integrate. $\blacksquare$

---

### Example 3.1 — Trapezoidal Dam Face

**Setup:** A trapezoidal dam face has its top edge 4 m wide at water surface and bottom edge 2 m wide at depth 3 m. Water density $\rho = 1000$ kg/m³, $g = 9.8$ m/s². Find the total hydrostatic force.

**Width function:** At depth $x$, the width decreases linearly from 4 to 2 as $x$ goes from 0 to 3: $$w(x) = 4 - \frac{2x}{3}$$

**Force:** $$F = \int_0^3 \rho g x \cdot w(x), dx = 1000(9.8)\int_0^3 x\left(4 - \frac{2x}{3}\right) dx$$ $$= 9800 \int_0^3 \left(4x - \frac{2x^2}{3}\right) dx = 9800\left[2x^2 - \frac{2x^3}{9}\right]_0^3$$ $$= 9800\left[18 - 6\right] = 9800 \times 12 = \boxed{117{,}600 \text{ N} \approx 117.6 \text{ kN}}$$

> **Engineering relevance:** Dam engineers must ensure structural supports can withstand this force. The integral gives the _exact_ force accounting for how both pressure and width change with depth.

---

### Example 3.2 — Circular Porthole

**Setup:** A circular porthole of radius 1 m is centered at depth 5 m on a submarine hull. Find the net hydrostatic force on the porthole.

**Setup coordinates:** Center at depth 5. Use $y$ measured from center: depth at height $y$ from center is $5 - y$. Width at height $y$: $w(y) = 2\sqrt{1 - y^2}$.

$$F = \rho g \int_{-1}^{1} (5-y) \cdot 2\sqrt{1-y^2}, dy$$

Split: $$= 2\rho g \left[5\int_{-1}^1 \sqrt{1-y^2}, dy - \int_{-1}^1 y\sqrt{1-y^2}, dy\right]$$

First integral = area of semicircle = $\frac{\pi}{2}$. Second integral = 0 (odd function on symmetric interval).

$$F = 2\rho g \cdot 5 \cdot \frac{\pi}{2} = 5\pi \rho g = 5\pi (1000)(9.8) \approx \boxed{153{,}938 \text{ N}}$$

> **Note:** The 5 meters of depth contributes; the circular shape contributes $\pi r^2$ area. The depth of the centroid times $\rho g$ times area gives the same answer — this is the **centroid shortcut**, but only valid when you already know the result! The integral is the derivation.

## 4. Arc Length & Surface Area of Revolution

### Theoretical Foundation (Stewart §8.1–8.2)

**Arc Length Formula:**

$$\boxed{L = \int_a^b \sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{dy}{dx}\right)^2}, dx}$$

**Derivation:** For a curve $y = f(x)$, partition $[a,b]$. Each piece has length $\approx \sqrt{(\Delta x)^2 + (\Delta y)^2} = \Delta x\sqrt{1 + \left(\frac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}\right)^2}$. Taking the limit as partition is refined gives the formula. $\blacksquare$

**Surface Area of Revolution** (rotating $y = f(x)$ about $x$-axis):

$$\boxed{S = \int_a^b 2\pi f(x)\sqrt{1 + [f'(x)]^2}, dx}$$

_Each infinitesimal strip of arc length $ds = \sqrt{1+[f'(x)]^2}, dx$ traces a band of area $2\pi f(x), ds$._

---

### Example 4.1 — Arc Length of a Parabolic Cable

**Setup:** A suspension bridge cable follows $y = x^2$ from $x = 0$ to $x = 2$ (units in km). Find its length.

$$y' = 2x \implies L = \int_0^2 \sqrt{1 + 4x^2}, dx$$

**Trigonometric substitution:** $2x = \tan\theta$, $dx = \frac{1}{2}\sec^2\theta, d\theta$, $\sqrt{1+4x^2} = \sec\theta$.

$$L = \int_0^{\arctan 4} \sec\theta \cdot \frac{1}{2}\sec^2\theta, d\theta = \frac{1}{2}\int \sec^3\theta, d\theta$$

Using the reduction formula $\int \sec^3\theta, d\theta = \frac{1}{2}[\sec\theta\tan\theta + \ln|\sec\theta + \tan\theta|]$:

$$L = \frac{1}{4}\left[\sec\theta\tan\theta + \ln|\sec\theta + \tan\theta|\right]_0^{\arctan 4}$$

At $\theta = \arctan 4$: $\tan\theta = 4$, $\sec\theta = \sqrt{17}$.

$$L = \frac{1}{4}\left[4\sqrt{17} + \ln(4 + \sqrt{17})\right] \approx \frac{1}{4}[16.49 + \ln(8.123)] \approx \frac{1}{4}[16.49 + 2.094] \approx \boxed{4.65 \text{ km}}$$

---

### Example 4.2 — Surface Area of a Cooling Tower

**Setup:** A cooling tower profile is $y = \cosh(x/a)$ rotated about the $y$-axis. For $a = 1$, from $x = 0$ to $x = 1$, find the surface area.

$$y' = \sinh x \implies S = \int_0^1 2\pi x \sqrt{1 + \sinh^2 x}, dx = 2\pi \int_0^1 x\cosh x, dx$$

(Using $\cosh^2 x - \sinh^2 x = 1 \implies \sqrt{1+\sinh^2 x} = \cosh x$.)

Integration by parts: $u = x$, $dv = \cosh x, dx$: $$= 2\pi\left[x\sinh x - \cosh x\right]_0^1 = 2\pi[\sinh 1 - \cosh 1 + 1]$$ $$= 2\pi[1.1752 - 1.5431 + 1] = 2\pi(0.632) \approx \boxed{3.97 \text{ m}^2}$$

## 5. Electric Charge & Current

### Theoretical Foundation

**Current** $I(t)$ is the rate of charge flow: $I = dq/dt$. To find total charge from current:

$$\boxed{q = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} I(t), dt}$$

**Energy delivered** to a resistor: $$\boxed{E = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} P(t), dt = \int_{t_1}^{t_2} V(t) I(t), dt}$$

**Electric potential** (work per unit charge) from field $\mathbf{E}$: $$V(b) - V(a) = -\int_a^b \mathbf{E} \cdot d\mathbf{r}$$

---

### Example 5.1 — Capacitor Charging

**Setup:** A capacitor charges with current $I(t) = 2e^{-t/3}$ A. How much charge accumulates in the first 6 seconds?

$$q = \int_0^6 2e^{-t/3}, dt = 2\left[-3e^{-t/3}\right]_0^6 = -6\left[e^{-2} - 1\right] = 6\left(1 - e^{-2}\right)$$ $$\approx 6(1 - 0.1353) \approx \boxed{5.17 \text{ C}}$$

**Interpretation:** The current is large at $t=0$ and decays exponentially as the capacitor fills. The integral accumulates this decaying flow.

---

### Example 5.2 — RMS Voltage

**Setup:** AC voltage $V(t) = V_0 \sin(\omega t)$. Find the root-mean-square voltage over one period $T = 2\pi/\omega$.

$$V_{\text{rms}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T V_0^2 \sin^2(\omega t), dt}$$

$$\frac{1}{T}\int_0^T \sin^2(\omega t), dt = \frac{1}{T}\int_0^T \frac{1-\cos(2\omega t)}{2}, dt = \frac{1}{2}$$

(The cosine term integrates to zero over a full period.)

$$V_{\text{rms}} = V_0\sqrt{\frac{1}{2}} = \boxed{\frac{V_0}{\sqrt{2}}}$$

> This is why household 230 V (in Bangladesh/Europe) means $V_0 = 230\sqrt{2} \approx 325$ V peak amplitude.

## 6. Gravitational Potential Energy

### Theoretical Foundation (MIT 18.02 L22)

The gravitational force between two masses $M$ and $m$ separated by $r$ is: $$F(r) = \frac{GMm}{r^2}$$

Work done lifting a mass from $r = R$ (Earth's surface) to $r = \infty$:

$$W = \int_R^\infty \frac{GMm}{r^2}, dr = GMm\left[-\frac{1}{r}\right]_R^\infty = \frac{GMm}{R}$$

This is the **escape energy** — the minimum energy required.

---

### Example 6.1 — Escape Velocity

**Setup:** Find the minimum launch speed from Earth's surface ($R = 6.371 \times 10^6$ m, $M = 5.972 \times 10^{24}$ kg).

Set kinetic energy = potential energy: $$\frac{1}{2}mv_e^2 = \frac{GMm}{R} \implies v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2GM}{R}}$$

$$v_e = \sqrt{\frac{2(6.674\times 10^{-11})(5.972\times 10^{24})}{6.371\times 10^6}} = \sqrt{\frac{7.972\times 10^{14}}{6.371\times 10^6}} = \sqrt{1.251\times 10^8} \approx \boxed{11.18 \text{ km/s}}$$

> **The integral's role:** The work integral from $R$ to $\infty$ correctly accounts for the _decreasing_ gravitational force — something a simple $F \times d$ calculation cannot do.

## 7. Moment of Inertia (Rotational Kinetic Energy)

### Theoretical Foundation (MIT 18.02 L21)

**Moment of inertia** $I$ measures resistance to rotational acceleration:

$$\boxed{I = \int r^2, dm}$$

where $r$ is the perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation.

Kinetic energy of rotation: $KE = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2$.

---

### Example 7.1 — Solid Cylinder

**Setup:** A solid cylinder of mass $M$, radius $R$, and height $h$ rotates about its central axis. Find $I$.

**Setup:** Use cylindrical coordinates. A thin cylindrical shell at radius $r$, thickness $dr$: $$dm = \rho \cdot 2\pi r h, dr, \quad \rho = \frac{M}{\pi R^2 h}$$

$$I = \int_0^R r^2, dm = \int_0^R r^2 \cdot \frac{M}{\pi R^2 h} \cdot 2\pi r h, dr = \frac{2M}{R^2}\int_0^R r^3, dr = \frac{2M}{R^2} \cdot \frac{R^4}{4} = \boxed{\frac{MR^2}{2}}$$

**Application:** A flywheel engine stores energy $E = \frac{1}{2}I\omega^2 = \frac{MR^2\omega^2}{4}$. Increasing $R$ is more efficient than increasing $\omega$ for energy storage.

---

### Example 7.2 — Thin Rod About End vs. Center

For a uniform rod of mass $M$, length $L$, density $\lambda = M/L$:

**About center:** $$I_c = \int_{-L/2}^{L/2} x^2 \lambda, dx = \lambda \cdot \frac{x^3}{3}\Bigg|_{-L/2}^{L/2} = \lambda \cdot \frac{L^3}{12} = \boxed{\frac{ML^2}{12}}$$

**About end:** $$I_e = \int_0^L x^2 \lambda, dx = \lambda \cdot \frac{L^3}{3} = \boxed{\frac{ML^2}{3}}$$

**Parallel Axis Theorem check:** $I_e = I_c + M(L/2)^2 = \frac{ML^2}{12} + \frac{ML^2}{4} = \frac{ML^2}{3}$. ✓

## 8. Signal Processing — Convolution

### Theoretical Foundation

**Convolution** is one of the most powerful integral operations in engineering:

$$\boxed{(f * g)(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(\tau), g(t - \tau), d\tau}$$

**Intuition:** At time $t$, the output is the weighted sum of all past inputs $f(\tau)$, weighted by the impulse response $g(t-\tau)$.

**Applications:**

- Signal filtering (audio, image)
- System response (input $*$ impulse response = output)
- Probability (sum of random variables)

---

### Example 8.1 — RC Circuit Response

**Setup:** An RC circuit has impulse response $g(t) = \frac{1}{RC}e^{-t/RC} \cdot \mathbf{1}_{t \ge 0}$. Input: rectangular pulse $f(t) = \mathbf{1}_{0 \le t \le T}$. Find output $y(t)$ for $0 < t < T$.

$$y(t) = \int_0^t f(\tau) g(t-\tau), d\tau = \int_0^t \frac{1}{RC} e^{-(t-\tau)/RC}, d\tau$$ $$= \frac{e^{-t/RC}}{RC}\int_0^t e^{\tau/RC}, d\tau = \frac{e^{-t/RC}}{RC} \cdot RC\left[e^{\tau/RC}\right]_0^t$$ $$= e^{-t/RC}(e^{t/RC} - 1) = \boxed{1 - e^{-t/RC}}$$

The output rises toward 1, the classic capacitor charging curve — derived directly from the convolution integral.

## 9. Probability & Statistics (Bridge to ML)

### Theoretical Foundation

For a continuous random variable $X$ with **probability density function** (PDF) $f(x)$:

$$P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f(x), dx, \qquad \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x), dx = 1$$

**Expected value:** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x, f(x), dx$$

**Variance:** $$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x^2 f(x), dx - \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty x, f(x), dx\right)^2$$

---

### Example 9.1 — Gaussian (Normal) Distribution

**PDF:** $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-(x-\mu)^2/(2\sigma^2)}$

**Verify it integrates to 1** (the Gaussian integral):

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2}, dx = \sqrt{2\pi}$$

**Proof using polar coordinates (MIT 18.02 L25 style):**

Let $I = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2}, dx$. Then: $$I^2 = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}, dx, dy$$

Convert to polar: $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$, $dA = r, dr, d\theta$: $$I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^\infty e^{-r^2/2} r, dr, d\theta = 2\pi \int_0^\infty r e^{-r^2/2}, dr$$

Substitution $u = r^2/2$: $= 2\pi \int_0^\infty e^{-u}, du = 2\pi$

$\therefore I = \sqrt{2\pi}$. $\blacksquare$

> **This double integral in polar coordinates is the engine of all probability theory for Gaussian distributions** — and Gaussians are everywhere in ML.

## 10. Machine Learning & Deep Learning — Where It All Lives

> _Every topic above has a direct descendant in modern AI. Here we make the connections explicit._

---

### 10.1 Gradient Flow & Backpropagation via the Fundamental Theorem

**Connection to Work integrals.** In optimization, the loss landscape $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ is a surface. The gradient flow equation: $$\frac{d\theta}{dt} = -\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

describes a "particle" rolling downhill. The **total loss change** along a path $\gamma$ from $\theta_0$ to $\theta_T$ is: $$\Delta \mathcal{L} = \int_0^T \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L} \cdot \frac{d\theta}{dt}, dt = -\int_0^T \left|\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}\right|^2 dt$$

This is directly analogous to **work done by a force along a path** (§1). The gradient is the force; the parameter update is the displacement.

---

### 10.2 Convolution in Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)

**This is §8 directly.** A 2D CNN layer computes:

$$(I * K)(i,j) = \sum_{m}\sum_{n} I(i-m, j-n), K(m,n)$$

This is the **discrete analogue** of the continuous convolution integral: $$(f * g)(t) = \int f(\tau) g(t-\tau), d\tau$$

In a CNN:

- $I$ = input image (or feature map)
- $K$ = learned kernel (analogous to impulse response $g$)
- Output = filtered/transformed version of input

**Why does it work?** Convolution is **translation-equivariant**: shifting the input shifts the output by the same amount. This is a direct consequence of the integral's structure: $\int f(\tau-a) g(t-\tau), d\tau = (f*g)(t-a)$.

---

### 10.3 Expected Value & Loss Functions

**Connection to §9.** The empirical risk minimization principle:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{(x,y) \sim \mathcal{D}}\left[\ell(f_\theta(x), y)\right] = \int \ell(f_\theta(x), y), p(x,y), dx, dy$$

The **true loss** is an integral over the data distribution $p(x,y)$. We cannot compute it exactly (we don't know $p$), so we approximate:

$$\hat{\mathcal{L}}(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \ell(f_\theta(x_i), y_i) \approx \int \ell(f_\theta(x), y), p(x,y), dx, dy$$

This is a **Monte Carlo approximation to an integral** — stochastic gradient descent is fundamentally a method for minimizing an integral you can't compute in closed form.

---

### 10.4 Kullback-Leibler Divergence

The **KL divergence** used in variational autoencoders (VAEs), diffusion models, and information theory:

$$D_{KL}(P | Q) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty p(x) \log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)}, dx$$

This is an _integral of probability densities_ (§9). For Gaussians $P = \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$, $Q = \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$:

$$D_{KL}(P|Q) = \log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1} + \frac{\sigma_1^2 + (\mu_1-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2} - \frac{1}{2}$$

**Derived** by directly evaluating the integral using properties of the Gaussian (completing the square, the Gaussian integral result from §9.1).

---

### 10.5 Attention Mechanism as Weighted Average

The **self-attention** in Transformers (GPT, BERT):

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right) V$$

In the continuous limit, this is a _weighted integral_:

$$\text{Attention}(q) = \int \frac{e^{q \cdot k(x)}}{\int e^{q \cdot k(x')}, dx'} v(x), dx$$

This is directly the **expected value** formula (§9): output = $\mathbb{E}[v(x)]$ under the attention distribution $\text{softmax}(\cdot)$. The softmax is a **normalized exponential measure**, and the attention output is a **weighted average** — an integral.

---

### 10.6 Moment of Inertia → Batch Normalization

**Connection to §7.** The variance of activations: $$\text{Var}[x] = \mathbb{E}[x^2] - (\mathbb{E}[x])^2 = \int x^2 p(x), dx - \left(\int x, p(x), dx\right)^2$$

This is the **second central moment** — structurally identical to the moment of inertia formula. Batch Normalization normalizes by $\sqrt{\text{Var}[x] + \epsilon}$, which is precisely dividing by the "radius of gyration" of the activation distribution.

---

### 10.7 Neural ODEs — The Continuous Limit of Deep Networks

**Connection to all of the above.** A residual network block: $h_{t+1} = h_t + f(h_t, \theta_t)$. As the number of layers $\to \infty$ with smaller steps, this becomes the ODE:

$$\frac{dh}{dt} = f(h(t), \theta(t)), \qquad h(T) = h(0) + \int_0^T f(h(t), \theta(t)), dt$$

**Neural ODEs** (Chen et al., 2018) make this the architecture itself. The forward pass is an _integral_. Training requires differentiating through an ODE solver — which requires the **adjoint method**, itself a line integral in function space.

> **FTC I (differentiation):** $\frac{d}{dx}\int_a^x f(t), dt = f(x)$  
> **FTC II (evaluation):** $\int_a^b f(x), dx = F(b) - F(a)$  
> **Integration by Parts:** $\int u, dv = uv - \int v, du$  
> **Substitution:** $\int_a^b f(g(x))g'(x), dx = \int_{g(a)}^{g(b)} f(u), du$
