
> _"A function is a machine. Its inverse is the same machine run backwards — but only if the machine is one-to-one."_

---

## §1. Motivation & The Core Problem

Suppose $f : A \to B$ is a function. We ask: can we "undo" $f$? That is, given an output $y \in B$, can we recover uniquely the input $x \in A$ such that $f(x) = y$?

The answer depends entirely on whether $f$ is **injective** (one-to-one). If two distinct inputs give the same output, the inversion is ambiguous — we cannot know which preimage to return.

**Concrete obstruction:** $f(x) = x^2$ on $\mathbb{R}$. Given $y = 9$, is $x = 3$ or $x = -3$? No unique answer. We must _restrict_ the domain to recover invertibility.

---

## §2. Formal Definitions

**Definition 2.1 (Injective / One-to-One).** A function $f : A \to B$ is _injective_ if $$ f(x_1) = f(x_2) \implies x_1 = x_2 \quad \text{for all } x_1, x_2 \in A. $$ Equivalently: $x_1 \neq x_2 \implies f(x_1) \neq f(x_2)$.

**Definition 2.2 (Surjective / Onto).** $f : A \to B$ is _surjective_ if for every $y \in B$, there exists at least one $x \in A$ with $f(x) = y$.

**Definition 2.3 (Bijective).** $f$ is _bijective_ if it is both injective and surjective. Bijectivity is the _precise_ condition for invertibility.

**Definition 2.4 (Inverse Function).** Let $f : A \to B$ be bijective. The _inverse_ $f^{-1} : B \to A$ is the unique function satisfying: $$ f^{-1}(y) = x \iff f(x) = y. $$

Equivalently, $f^{-1}$ is characterised by the two cancellation identities: $$ \boxed{f^{-1}(f(x)) = x \quad \forall x \in A, \qquad f(f^{-1}(y)) = y \quad \forall y \in B.} $$

> **Notational Warning.** $f^{-1}(x) \neq \dfrac{1}{f(x)}$. The superscript $-1$ here denotes functional inversion, _not_ reciprocal. This distinction bites students repeatedly.

---

## §3. The Horizontal Line Test

A graphical criterion for injectivity on $\mathbb{R}$:

> **Horizontal Line Test.** $f$ is injective $\iff$ every horizontal line $y = c$ intersects the graph of $f$ in _at most one point_.

```
y
│         ╱  ← f(x) = x³  (passes: strictly increasing)
│       ╱
│     ╱
│   ╱
│ ╱
└─────────── x

y
│    ╭──╮
│   ╱    ╲   ← f(x) = x²  (fails: symmetric about y-axis)
│  ╱      ╲
│╱          ╲
└─────────────x
```

**Geometric interpretation of the inverse:** The graph of $f^{-1}$ is the _reflection_ of the graph of $f$ across the line $y = x$. This is not a metaphor — it follows directly from the definition: if $(a, b)$ is on the graph of $f$, then $(b, a)$ is on the graph of $f^{-1}$.

---

## §4. How to Find the Inverse — The Algorithm

Given $y = f(x)$:

1. Replace $f(x)$ with $y$.
2. Solve for $x$ in terms of $y$.
3. Swap labels: write $x$ for the input variable, $y$ for the output.
4. The resulting expression is $f^{-1}(x)$.

> **Step 2 is the hard step.** It may involve algebraic manipulation, logarithms, trigonometric identities, or even be impossible in closed form (e.g., $y = x + \sin x$).

---

## §5. Worked Examples

### Example 5.1 — Linear Function

$$ f(x) = 3x - 7 $$

**Step 1–2.** $y = 3x - 7 \implies x = \dfrac{y + 7}{3}$

**Step 3–4.** $$ f^{-1}(x) = \frac{x + 7}{3} $$

**Verification:** $$ f(f^{-1}(x)) = 3 \cdot \frac{x+7}{3} - 7 = x + 7 - 7 = x \checkmark $$

---

### Example 5.2 — Power Function with Domain Restriction

$$ f(x) = x^2, \quad x \geq 0 $$

The domain restriction $[0, \infty)$ forces injectivity.

$$ y = x^2 \implies x = \sqrt{y} \quad (\text{positive root, since } x \geq 0) $$

$$ f^{-1}(x) = \sqrt{x}, \quad x \geq 0 $$

```
y
│    ╱ f⁻¹(x) = √x
│  ╱
│╱___
│    ╲ (discarded branch x < 0)
└──────x
```

---

### Example 5.3 — Exponential and Logarithm

$$ f(x) = e^x, \quad f : \mathbb{R} \to (0, \infty) $$

This is bijective. From $y = e^x$:

$$ x = \ln y \implies f^{-1}(x) = \ln x, \quad x > 0 $$

The natural logarithm _is_ the inverse of the natural exponential. This is the definition of $\ln$, not a theorem.

---

### Example 5.4 — Arcsin (Domain-Restricted Sine)

$$ f(x) = \sin x, \quad x \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right] $$

On this restricted domain, $f$ is strictly increasing hence injective, and its range is $[-1, 1]$.

$$ f^{-1}(x) = \arcsin x, \quad x \in [-1, 1], \quad \text{output} \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right] $$

**Key identity:** $$ \sin(\arcsin x) = x \quad \text{for } x \in [-1,1] $$ $$ \arcsin(\sin x) = x \quad \text{only for } x \in \left[-\frac{\pi}{2}, \frac{\pi}{2}\right] $$

The second identity fails outside the principal branch. E.g., $\arcsin(\sin(\pi)) = \arcsin(0) = 0 \neq \pi$.

## §6. The Derivative of an Inverse Function

This is where calculus and inverse functions fuse — a result of central importance.

**Theorem 6.1 (Derivative of the Inverse).** Let $f$ be differentiable and injective on an open interval, with $f'(x) \neq 0$. Then $f^{-1}$ is differentiable, and: $$ \boxed{(f^{-1})'(y) = \frac{1}{f'(x)}} \quad \text{where } y = f(x). $$

**Proof.** From $f(f^{-1}(y)) = y$, differentiate both sides with respect to $y$ using the chain rule: $$ f'(f^{-1}(y)) \cdot (f^{-1})'(y) = 1 $$ $$ \implies (f^{-1})'(y) = \frac{1}{f'(f^{-1}(y))} = \frac{1}{f'(x)}. \qquad \square $$

**Geometric interpretation:** At corresponding points $(a, b)$ on $f$ and $(b, a)$ on $f^{-1}$, the slopes are reciprocals. This is the algebraic encoding of the reflection symmetry across $y = x$.

### Example 6.1 — Derivative of $\sqrt{x}$

Let $f(x) = x^2$ on $[0,\infty)$, so $f^{-1}(x) = \sqrt{x}$. Then $f'(x) = 2x$, and with $y = x^2 \implies x = \sqrt{y}$:

$$ (f^{-1})'(y) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} $$

Which matches $\dfrac{d}{dy}\sqrt{y} = \dfrac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}$ computed directly. $\checkmark$

### Example 6.2 — Derivative of $\ln x$

$f(x) = e^x$, $f'(x) = e^x$, $f^{-1}(y) = \ln y$.

$$ (\ln y)' = \frac{1}{e^{\ln y}} = \frac{1}{y} $$

This is **the** standard derivation of $\dfrac{d}{dx}\ln x = \dfrac{1}{x}$.

## §7. Visualisation — Reflection Across $y = x$

```
y
│         ╱ y=x (mirror line)
│       ╱
│     * ← (b, a) on f⁻¹
│   ╱   ╲
│ ╱   * ← (a, b) on f
│╱
└────────────── x
```

The two starred points are reflections of each other. If you fold the plane along $y = x$, the graph of $f$ lands exactly on the graph of $f^{-1}$.

**Consequence for domain and range:** $$ \text{dom}(f^{-1}) = \text{range}(f), \qquad \text{range}(f^{-1}) = \text{dom}(f). $$

This is immediate from the definitions but worth making explicit.

---

## §8. Monotone Functions Are Always Invertible

**Proposition 8.1.** If $f$ is strictly monotone (strictly increasing or strictly decreasing) on an interval $I$, then $f$ is injective on $I$ and thus invertible.

**Proof.** Suppose $f$ is strictly increasing and $x_1 \neq x_2$. WLOG $x_1 < x_2$, then $f(x_1) < f(x_2)$, so $f(x_1) \neq f(x_2)$. Hence injective. $\square$

**Corollary.** Any differentiable function with $f'(x) > 0$ everywhere (or $< 0$ everywhere) on an interval is invertible on that interval.

This connects the algebraic property of injectivity to the analytic property of having a definite sign for $f'$.

## §9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

> _This is not a tangent — inverse functions are structurally embedded in the foundations of modern ML._

### 9.1 Activation Functions and Invertibility

Neural networks apply activation functions $\sigma : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ elementwise. Common choices:

|Activation|Formula|Invertible?|Notes|
|---|---|---|---|
|Sigmoid|$\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$|✓ on $\mathbb{R}$|$\sigma^{-1}(y) = \ln\frac{y}{1-y}$ (logit)|
|ReLU|$\max(0,x)$|✗|Not injective ($f(x)=0$ for all $x \leq 0$)|
|Softplus|$\ln(1+e^x)$|✓|Smooth approximation to ReLU|
|Tanh|$\tanh(x)$|✓ on $\mathbb{R}$|$\tanh^{-1}(y)$ = arctanh|

ReLU's **non-invertibility** is the source of the "dying ReLU" problem — information is irreversibly lost when neurons output $0$.

### 9.2 Normalizing Flows — Invertibility as Architecture

**Normalizing flows** are a class of generative models that transform a simple distribution (e.g., $\mathcal{N}(0,I)$) into a complex one via a sequence of invertible transformations $f_1, f_2, \ldots, f_k$:

$$ z_0 \sim p_0(\cdot), \qquad z_k = f_k \circ \cdots \circ f_1(z_0) $$

The density of the output is:

$$ \log p_k(z_k) = \log p_0(z_0) - \sum_{i=1}^{k} \log \left|\det J_{f_i}(z_{i-1})\right| $$

where $J_{f_i}$ is the Jacobian. This formula comes directly from the **change-of-variables theorem**, which relies on $f_i$ being invertible.

> **The invertibility requirement is not optional.** If any $f_i$ is not injective, the density formula breaks down. Architectures like RealNVP, Glow, and NICE are engineered specifically to guarantee invertible transformations with tractable Jacobians.

### 9.3 Backpropagation and the Chain Rule

Backpropagation is the chain rule applied repeatedly. Computing gradients requires evaluating $f'(x)$ at the **stored forward-pass values** $x$ — not the inverse. But:

- The formula $(f^{-1})'(y) = 1/f'(x)$ is used implicitly whenever we derive gradient formulas from scratch (e.g., gradient of $\log$-likelihood through a sigmoid).
- In **invertible neural networks (INNs)**, the inverse is computed _exactly_, enabling memory-efficient training (no need to store intermediate activations).

### 9.4 Log Probabilities and the Logit

In binary classification, the model outputs $\hat{p} = \sigma(z)$ where $\sigma$ is the sigmoid. The inverse: $$ z = \sigma^{-1}(\hat{p}) = \ln \frac{\hat{p}}{1-\hat{p}} \quad \text{(logit or log-odds)} $$

The logit is the inverse sigmoid. Numerically stable implementations work in logit space and apply the sigmoid only at the end. The cross-entropy loss: $$ \mathcal{L} = -y \log \hat{p} - (1-y)\log(1-\hat{p}) $$ is computed more stably via $\mathcal{L} = \max(z,0) - yz + \log(1 + e^{-|z|})$, using the logit $z$ directly.

### 9.5 Embedding Spaces and Invertibility

Word embeddings and VAE latent spaces map discrete/complex objects into $\mathbb{R}^n$. The encoder $\text{Enc}$ and decoder $\text{Dec}$ are (approximate) inverses:

$$ \text{Dec}(\text{Enc}(x)) \approx x $$

This is an approximate inverse, not exact — the training loss penalises the deviation from $\text{Dec} \circ \text{Enc} = \text{id}$.

## §10. Summary & Key Theorems

|Statement|Condition|
|---|---|
|$f$ is invertible|$f$ is bijective|
|Graph of $f^{-1}$|Reflection of graph of $f$ across $y = x$|
|$(f^{-1})'(y) = ?$|$\dfrac{1}{f'(x)}$ where $f(x) = y$|
|Strictly monotone $\implies$|injective $\implies$ invertible on that interval|
|$\text{dom}(f^{-1})$|$= \text{range}(f)$|
