## 1. The Philosophy of Curve Sketching

Before computers, sketching was _the_ way mathematicians understood functions. Even now, the discipline of extracting geometric information from $f$, $f'$, and $f''$ is not obsolete — it is the exact skill that underlies:

- Reading loss landscapes in optimization
- Understanding activation function behavior
- Analyzing convergence of iterative algorithms
- Interpreting Hessian structure in second-order methods

The goal is not a photorealistic plot. The goal is a **qualitatively correct skeleton** that captures every geometrically meaningful feature.

---

## 2. The Master Checklist — Eight Diagnostic Tools

|Step|Tool|What You Learn|
|---|---|---|
|**A**|Domain|Where does $f$ exist?|
|**B**|Intercepts|Where does $f$ cross the axes?|
|**C**|Symmetry|Even / odd / periodic?|
|**D**|Asymptotes|Where does $f$ blow up or level off?|
|**E**|Intervals of Increase/Decrease|Where is $f' > 0$ or $f' < 0$?|
|**F**|Local Extrema|Peaks and valleys via First Derivative Test|
|**G**|Concavity & Inflection Points|Where is $f'' > 0$ or $f'' < 0$?|
|**H**|Sketch|Assemble everything|

Each tool is a theorem, not a heuristic.

## 3. Each Tool, Rigorously

### A. Domain

The **natural domain** of $f$ is the largest subset of $\mathbb{R}$ on which $f(x)$ is defined.

Common restrictions:

- $\sqrt{g(x)}$: require $g(x) \geq 0$
- $\ln g(x)$: require $g(x) > 0$
- $\frac{1}{g(x)}$: require $g(x) \neq 0$
- $\arcsin g(x)$: require $-1 \leq g(x) \leq 1$

> **Personal note:** Finding the domain first prevents you from computing derivatives at points where the function doesn't even exist. This is where most computational errors begin.

---

### B. Intercepts

**$y$-intercept:** Set $x = 0$, compute $f(0)$ (if $0$ is in the domain).

**$x$-intercepts (zeros):** Solve $f(x) = 0$. These are often hard to find analytically — note that they exist (by IVT) whenever $f$ changes sign.

---

### C. Symmetry

> **Definition 3.1.** $f$ is **even** if $f(-x) = f(x)$ for all $x$ in the domain. Its graph is symmetric about the $y$-axis.

> **Definition 3.2.** $f$ is **odd** if $f(-x) = -f(x)$ for all $x$ in the domain. Its graph has $180°$ rotational symmetry about the origin.

> **Definition 3.3.** $f$ is **periodic** with period $T$ if $f(x+T) = f(x)$ for all $x$.

**Strategy:** If $f$ is even, sketch only $x \geq 0$ and reflect. If $f$ is odd, sketch $x \geq 0$ and rotate.

---

### D. Asymptotes

#### D.1 Horizontal Asymptotes

> **Definition 3.4.** The line $y = L$ is a **horizontal asymptote** of $f$ if $$\lim_{x \to +\infty} f(x) = L \quad \text{or} \quad \lim_{x \to -\infty} f(x) = L$$

A function can have **at most two** horizontal asymptotes (one in each direction), and it _can cross_ a horizontal asymptote for finite $x$.

#### D.2 Vertical Asymptotes

> **Definition 3.5.** The line $x = a$ is a **vertical asymptote** if any of the one-sided limits $\lim_{x \to a^\pm} f(x) = \pm\infty$.

At a vertical asymptote $x = a$, determine the sign of $f(x)$ from each side — the graph approaches $+\infty$ or $-\infty$ independently on the left and right.

#### D.3 Slant (Oblique) Asymptotes

If $\lim_{x \to \infty} [f(x) - (mx + b)] = 0$ for some $m \neq 0$, then $y = mx + b$ is a **slant asymptote**.

> **How to find it:** Perform polynomial long division when $\deg(\text{numerator}) = \deg(\text{denominator}) + 1$. The quotient (ignoring the remainder) gives the slant asymptote.

$$f(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \xrightarrow{\text{long division}} (mx + b) + \frac{r(x)}{q(x)}$$

As $x \to \infty$, the remainder $\frac{r(x)}{q(x)} \to 0$, so $f(x) \to mx + b$.

---

### E. Intervals of Increase and Decrease

Compute $f'(x)$. Find critical numbers: $f'(c) = 0$ or $f'(c)$ DNE.

Build a **sign chart** for $f'$:

- $f'(x) > 0 \Rightarrow f \nearrow$ (increasing)
- $f'(x) < 0 \Rightarrow f \searrow$ (decreasing)

_(See [[Increasing and Decreasing Functions]] for the full rigorous treatment and MVT-based proof.)_

---

### F. Local Extrema

#### First Derivative Test

> **Theorem 3.1 (First Derivative Test).** Let $c$ be a critical number of $f$, and suppose $f$ is continuous at $c$.
> 
> (a) If $f'$ changes from $+$ to $-$ at $c$: **local maximum** at $c$.  
> (b) If $f'$ changes from $-$ to $+$ at $c$: **local minimum** at $c$.  
> (c) If $f'$ does not change sign at $c$: **no extremum** (e.g., $f(x) = x^3$ at $c = 0$).

#### Second Derivative Test (when applicable)

> **Theorem 3.2 (Second Derivative Test).** Suppose $f'(c) = 0$ and $f''(c)$ exists.
> 
> (a) $f''(c) > 0 \Rightarrow$ **local minimum** at $c$ (concave up → "cup").  
> (b) $f''(c) < 0 \Rightarrow$ **local maximum** at $c$ (concave down → "cap").  
> (c) $f''(c) = 0 \Rightarrow$ **inconclusive** — revert to First Derivative Test.

**Proof sketch of (a):** If $f''(c) > 0$, then $f'$ is increasing near $c$. Since $f'(c) = 0$, $f' < 0$ just left of $c$ and $f' > 0$ just right of $c$ — exactly the condition for a local minimum by Theorem 3.1. $\square$

---

### G. Concavity and Inflection Points

> **Definition 3.6.** $f$ is **concave up** on $I$ if $f'$ is increasing on $I$, equivalently $f''(x) > 0$ on $I$.  
> $f$ is **concave down** on $I$ if $f'$ is decreasing on $I$, equivalently $f''(x) < 0$ on $I$.

Geometric meaning:

- Concave up: the graph lies **above** all its tangent lines — like a bowl $\cup$.
- Concave down: the graph lies **below** all its tangent lines — like an arch $\cap$.

> **Definition 3.7 (Inflection Point).** A point $(c, f(c))$ is an **inflection point** if $f$ is continuous at $c$ and **concavity changes** at $c$.

**Warning:** $f''(c) = 0$ is necessary but not sufficient. We need the sign of $f''$ to actually change.

Counterexample: $f(x) = x^4$. $f''(x) = 12x^2$, so $f''(0) = 0$, but $f'' \geq 0$ everywhere — no sign change, no inflection.

**Sign chart for $f''$:**

- $f''(x) > 0$: concave up $\cup$
- $f''(x) < 0$: concave down $\cap$
- Sign change: inflection point

---

### H. Sketch

With all the information assembled, plot in this order:

1. Draw asymptotes as dashed lines.
2. Plot intercepts and labeled extrema.
3. Plot inflection points.
4. Connect with curves, respecting:
    - Increasing/decreasing direction
    - Concavity (cup vs. arch)
    - Behavior near asymptotes (which side, $+\infty$ or $-\infty$)

## 4. The Full Algorithm in Action — Worked Examples

---

### Example 4.1 — Rational Function with Slant Asymptote

$$f(x) = \frac{x^2}{x-1}$$

**A. Domain:** $x \neq 1$, so domain is $(-\infty, 1) \cup (1, \infty)$.

**B. Intercepts:**

- $f(0) = 0$: $y$-intercept at $(0, 0)$.
- $f(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x^2 = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$: $x$-intercept at $(0,0)$.

**C. Symmetry:** $f(-x) = \frac{x^2}{-x-1} \neq f(x)$ and $\neq -f(x)$. No symmetry.

**D. Asymptotes:**

_Vertical:_ $\lim_{x \to 1^-} \frac{x^2}{x-1}$: numerator $\to 1 > 0$, denominator $\to 0^-$, so limit $= -\infty$.  
$\lim_{x \to 1^+} \frac{x^2}{x-1} = +\infty$.  
Vertical asymptote: $x = 1$.

_Horizontal:_ $\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} \frac{x^2}{x-1} = \pm\infty$. No horizontal asymptote.

_Slant:_ Long division: $$\frac{x^2}{x-1} = x + 1 + \frac{1}{x-1}$$ As $x \to \pm\infty$, $\frac{1}{x-1} \to 0$. **Slant asymptote: $y = x + 1$.**

**E. Increase/Decrease:**

$$f'(x) = \frac{2x(x-1) - x^2 \cdot 1}{(x-1)^2} = \frac{x^2 - 2x}{(x-1)^2} = \frac{x(x-2)}{(x-1)^2}$$

Critical numbers: $x = 0$ and $x = 2$ (where $f' = 0$); $x = 1$ is not in domain.

Sign chart for $f'$:

|Interval|Test pt|$x$|$(x-2)$|$(x-1)^2$|$f'$|$f$|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|$(-\infty, 0)$|$-1$|$-$|$-$|$+$|$+$|$\nearrow$|
|$(0, 1)$|$\frac{1}{2}$|$+$|$-$|$+$|$-$|$\searrow$|
|$(1, 2)$|$\frac{3}{2}$|$+$|$-$|$+$|$-$|$\searrow$|
|$(2, \infty)$|$3$|$+$|$+$|$+$|$+$|$\nearrow$|

**F. Local Extrema:**

- $x = 0$: $f'$ goes $+ \to -$ → **local max**, $f(0) = 0$.
- $x = 2$: $f'$ goes $- \to +$ → **local min**, $f(2) = \frac{4}{1} = 4$.

**G. Concavity:**

$$f''(x) = \frac{d}{dx}\left[\frac{x(x-2)}{(x-1)^2}\right]$$

Using quotient rule: Numerator: $(2x-2)(x-1)^2 - x(x-2) \cdot 2(x-1) = (x-1)[(2x-2)(x-1) - 2x(x-2)]$  
$= (x-1)[2x^2 - 4x + 2 - 2x^2 + 4x] = (x-1)(2) = 2(x-1)$

$$f''(x) = \frac{2(x-1)}{(x-1)^4} = \frac{2}{(x-1)^3}$$

Sign of $f''$:

- $x < 1$: $(x-1)^3 < 0 \Rightarrow f'' < 0$ → concave down $\cap$
- $x > 1$: $(x-1)^3 > 0 \Rightarrow f'' > 0$ → concave up $\cup$

No inflection points (concavity changes at $x=1$, but $x=1$ is not in domain).

**H. Sketch:**

```
     f(x) = x²/(x-1)

  f(x)↑       |
               |
    /          |                      /
   /  ←cap     |  cup→             /
  *            |                /
(0,0)──────────|──────────── /─────────── x
               | x=1       * (2,4)
               |  ↕ vert. asymptote
          ─────────────  y = x+1 (slant, dashed)
```

### Example 4.2 — Classic Full Example

$$f(x) = \frac{2x^2}{x^2 + 1}$$

**A. Domain:** All $\mathbb{R}$ (denominator never zero).

**B. Intercepts:** $f(0) = 0$. $f(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$. Only intercept: $(0,0)$.

**C. Symmetry:** $f(-x) = \frac{2x^2}{x^2+1} = f(x)$. **Even function** — symmetric about $y$-axis. Sketch $x \geq 0$ only.

**D. Asymptotes:**

$$\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} \frac{2x^2}{x^2+1} = \lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{2}{1 + 1/x^2} = 2$$

**Horizontal asymptote: $y = 2$** (both sides). No vertical asymptotes.

**E–F. Derivatives:**

$$f'(x) = \frac{4x(x^2+1) - 2x^2 \cdot 2x}{(x^2+1)^2} = \frac{4x}{(x^2+1)^2}$$

- $f'(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$.
- $f' < 0$ for $x < 0$ (decreasing), $f' > 0$ for $x > 0$ (increasing).
- **Local (and global) minimum at $x = 0$**: $f(0) = 0$.

**G. Concavity:**

$$f''(x) = \frac{4(x^2+1)^2 - 4x \cdot 2(x^2+1)(2x)}{(x^2+1)^4} = \frac{4(x^2+1) - 16x^2}{(x^2+1)^3} = \frac{4 - 12x^2}{(x^2+1)^3}$$

$f''(x) = 0 \Rightarrow 4 - 12x^2 = 0 \Rightarrow x = \pm \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$

- $|x| < \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$: $f'' > 0$ → concave up $\cup$
- $|x| > \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}$: $f'' < 0$ → concave down $\cap$

**Inflection points:** $\left(\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}},; f!\left(\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\right)\right) = \left(\pm\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}},; \frac{1}{2}\right)$

**H. Sketch:**

```
  f(x)↑
  2 ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─  ← y = 2 (H.A.)
          ·····         ·····
       ···   ↑         ↑   ···
     ··    infl.     infl.    ··
    ·     (-1/√3)  (1/√3)     ·
  ──·────────────────────────·──→ x
    ·                        ·
  0 *  ← global min at (0,0)
    (symmetric about y-axis)
```

### Example 4.3 — Transcendental Function

$$f(x) = x e^{-x}$$

**A. Domain:** All $\mathbb{R}$.

**B. Intercepts:** $f(0) = 0$. $f(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$.

**C. Symmetry:** Neither even nor odd.

**D. Asymptotes:**

$$\lim_{x \to +\infty} xe^{-x} = 0 \quad (\text{exponential dominates polynomial})$$ $$\lim_{x \to -\infty} xe^{-x} = -\infty \quad (x \to -\infty,; e^{-x} \to +\infty)$$

**Horizontal asymptote: $y = 0$ as $x \to +\infty$ only.**

**E–F.**

$$f'(x) = e^{-x}(1 - x)$$

Critical number: $x = 1$.

- $x < 1$: $f' > 0$ → increasing
- $x > 1$: $f' < 0$ → decreasing

**Local (global) max at $x = 1$:** $f(1) = e^{-1} = 1/e \approx 0.368$.

**G.**

$$f''(x) = -e^{-x}(1-x) + e^{-x}(-1) = e^{-x}(x - 2)$$

$f''(x) = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2$.

- $x < 2$: $f'' < 0$ → concave down $\cap$
- $x > 2$: $f'' > 0$ → concave up $\cup$

**Inflection point at $x = 2$:** $f(2) = 2e^{-2} \approx 0.271$.

**H. Sketch:**

```
  f(x)↑
       |
  1/e  |        * ← global max (1, 1/e)
       |      *   *
       |     *     *
       |    *       * ← inflection (2, 2/e²)
       |  *           *  *  *  *  ·  ·  ·  → y=0 asymptote
 ──────*──────────────────────────────────→ x
  ↙    0    1    2    3    4
(→ -∞)
```

### 5.4 The Slant Asymptote Mechanism

```
f(x) = x + 1 + 1/(x-1)

f(x) for large x:
         /  ← f(x)
        /·
       / ·  ← gap = 1/(x-1) → 0
      /  ·
y=x+1─/─── ← slant asymptote

As x → ∞: gap = 1/(x-1) → 0.
The graph "hugs" the slant line from above (gap > 0 for x > 1).
For x < 1: gap = 1/(x-1) < 0, graph hugs from below.
```

## 6. Connections to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

### 6.1 Loss Landscape Sketching — The Geometer's View of Training

When you train a neural network, you are navigating the graph of a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$. Everything in curve sketching maps directly:

|Curve Sketching Concept|ML Analogue|
|---|---|
|Local minimum|Converged parameter state (good if loss is low)|
|Local maximum|Unstable equilibrium — gradient descent flees|
|Saddle point|Critical point, $\nabla\mathcal{L} = 0$, but not extremum|
|Inflection point|Transition from accelerating to decelerating loss decrease|
|Horizontal asymptote|Irreducible loss (Bayes error floor)|
|Concave up region|Loss decreasing at increasing rate → fast convergence zone|
|Concave down region|Loss decreasing at decreasing rate → convergence slowing|

### 6.2 Activation Functions as Curves — Full Sketch Analysis

Every activation function's behavior is determined by its graph. Here is the full curve-sketching analysis for the most important ones:

#### Sigmoid $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$

- **Domain:** $\mathbb{R}$
- **Range:** $(0, 1)$
- **Symmetry:** $\sigma(x) + \sigma(-x) = 1$ (antisymmetric about $(0, 0.5)$) — odd after shifting
- **Asymptotes:** $y = 0$ as $x\to -\infty$; $y = 1$ as $x\to+\infty$
- **$f'(x) = \sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x)) > 0$:** always increasing
- **$f''(x) = \sigma'(x)(1 - 2\sigma(x))$:** zero when $\sigma(x) = 1/2 \Rightarrow x = 0$
    - Inflection point at $(0, 0.5)$. Concave up for $x < 0$, concave down for $x > 0$.

```
  σ(x)↑
  1 ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ─ ·····  ← y=1 asymptote
                 ····
             ····
  0.5       * ← inflection (0, 0.5)
         ····
     ····
  0 ─·····─────────────────→ x  ← y=0 asymptote
    (concave up)|(concave down)
                0
```

**ML significance:** The inflection at $x=0$ is where the neuron is _most sensitive_ ($f'$ maximal). In the tails, $f' \approx 0$ → vanishing gradients. This is the geometric cause of the vanishing gradient problem.

#### ReLU $R(x) = \max(0, x)$

- **Domain:** $\mathbb{R}$
- **Asymptotes:** None
- **$f'$:** $0$ for $x<0$; $1$ for $x>0$; undefined at $x=0$ (kink)
- **$f''$:** $0$ everywhere except $x=0$ (distributional: $\delta(0)$)
- **No inflection points** in the classical sense

```
  R(x)↑
       |              /
       |             /
       |            /
       |           /
       |          /   ← slope = 1 always (x > 0)
  ─────────────── ────→ x
       |    0
  (flat: f'=0)
  "dead neuron zone"
```

**ML significance:** Flat region ($x < 0$) means $f' = 0$ → zero gradient → weight doesn't update → **dead neuron**. No concavity anywhere → ReLU networks are piecewise linear. They cannot model curvature within a single layer.

#### GELU $\approx x \cdot \Phi(x)$ (where $\Phi$ is the Gaussian CDF)

- **Not monotone:** has a local minimum near $x \approx -0.17$
- **Smooth everywhere** (unlike ReLU)
- **Inflection points** exist; richer curvature structure
- **Used in:** GPT-2, GPT-3, BERT, most modern transformers

```
  GELU(x)↑
         |               /
         |              /
         |             /
  ───────────────────── ────→ x
         |  ↙ slight dip
         | * ← local min ≈ (-0.17, -0.17)
         |
  (non-monotone near x = -0.17)
```

**ML significance:** The slight non-monotonicity allows GELU to "gate" inputs smoothly — small negative values are slightly suppressed, not hard-zeroed like ReLU.

### 6.3 The Hessian = $f''$ in High Dimensions

In multivariable optimization, the **Hessian** $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$ is the matrix analogue of $f''$:

|1D curve sketching|$n$D optimization|
|---|---|
|$f''(c) > 0$ at critical point|$H$ is positive definite at $\theta^*$|
|$f''(c) < 0$ at critical point|$H$ is negative definite|
|$f''(c) = 0$ (inconclusive)|$H$ is singular (degenerate — flat directions)|
|Inflection: concavity changes|Saddle: $H$ has both positive and negative eigenvalues|

**Newton's method** uses $H^{-1}$ to adjust step size based on local curvature — exactly the second-order information that $f''$ provides in 1D.

### 6.4 Learning Rate and the Geometry of the Loss

The shape of the loss graph directly constrains the safe learning rate $\eta$:

$$\text{Safe step size: } \eta < \frac{2}{L}$$

where $L = \max_\theta f''(\theta) = $ the **Lipschitz constant of the gradient** — essentially the maximum curvature of the loss curve.

```
Loss ℒ(θ)↑

  Highly curved region:         Flat region:
  small η needed                large η possible

        *                    ·  ·  ·  ·  ·
       * *                 ·                ·
      *   *              ·                    ·
     *     *                                   ·
    ← tight →           ←───── wide ─────────→

  f'' large here              f'' small here
  → L is large               → L is small
  → small η required         → large η safe
```

This is why **learning rate scheduling** and **adaptive optimizers** (Adam, RMSprop) are necessary: they implicitly adapt to local curvature.

### 6.5 Normalization and Asymptotic Behavior

The horizontal asymptote of an output function determines the **range** of a neuron's output — critical for:

- **Sigmoid output layer:** asymptotes at $0$ and $1$ → probabilities constrained to $(0,1)$
- **Tanh hidden layer:** asymptotes at $-1$ and $+1$ → zero-centered outputs → faster convergence than sigmoid
- **Softmax:** each output's asymptote → proper probability simplex at temperature extremes

## 📌 Quick Reference — The Eight Steps

```
A  Domain          → Where is f defined?
B  Intercepts      → f(0) for y-int; f(x)=0 for x-int
C  Symmetry        → Even (y-axis), Odd (origin), Periodic?
D  Asymptotes      → Vertical (blow-up), Horizontal (limit at ±∞), Slant (long division)
E  f'  sign chart  → Increase ↗ / Decrease ↘
F  Local extrema   → First or Second Derivative Test
G  f'' sign chart  → Concave up ∪ / Concave down ∩ / Inflection points
H  Sketch          → Dashed asymptotes → extrema → inflections → smooth curves
```

$$\boxed{\text{Graph} = \text{Domain} + \text{Asymptotes} + \text{Sign}(f') + \text{Sign}(f'')}$$
