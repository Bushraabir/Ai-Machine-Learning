## What Is a Linear Model?

A function $f: \mathcal{X} \to \mathcal{Y}$ is **linear** if it satisfies two axioms simultaneously:

$$f(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}) = f(\mathbf{x}) + f(\mathbf{y}) \qquad \text{(additivity)}$$ $$f(\alpha \mathbf{x}) = \alpha f(\mathbf{x}) \qquad \text{(homogeneity)}$$

Together these are called **superposition**. Any map satisfying both is linear.

**Affine vs. Linear:** In practice, we almost always mean _affine_: $$f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b$$

The term $b$ (bias or intercept) breaks strict linearity but is essential for modelling. An affine map is a linear map composed with a translation. In ML literature, "linear model" universally means affine.

---

## The One-Variable Case

$$y = mx + b, \qquad m, b \in \mathbb{R}$$

Geometry: a line in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Every point on it satisfies this equation exactly.

**Parameters:**

- $m$ = slope = $\dfrac{\Delta y}{\Delta x}$ — rate of change of $y$ per unit change in $x$
- $b$ = $y$-intercept — value of $y$ when $x = 0$

**Example — Fahrenheit/Celsius:** $$F = \frac{9}{5}C + 32$$

This is an exact linear model. $m = 9/5$, $b = 32$. No residual, no noise, no approximation. Physical law, not a fit.

**Example — Hooke's Law:** $$F = kx$$

Spring force is proportional to displacement. $b = 0$ here (passes through origin — a _strictly linear_ model, not just affine). $k$ is the spring constant, units N/m.

## The Multivariable Case

$$y = w_1 x_1 + w_2 x_2 + \cdots + w_n x_n + b = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b$$

where $\mathbf{w} = (w_1, \ldots, w_n)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the **weight vector** and $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the **feature vector**.

Geometry: a **hyperplane** in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$, or equivalently, the level sets ${\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} = c}$ are hyperplanes in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

**Example — House Price (toy model):** $$\hat{\text{price}} = 150 \cdot (\text{area}) + 20000 \cdot (\text{bedrooms}) - 5000 \cdot (\text{age}) + 30000$$

Each weight has a direct physical interpretation: $150 per additional sq ft, $20,000 per bedroom, etc.

## Matrix Form: The System Perspective

When we have $m$ observations and $n$ features, stack everything:

$$\mathbf{y} = X\mathbf{w} + \mathbf{b} \cdot \mathbf{1}$$

Absorb the bias by appending a column of ones to $X$:

$$\underbrace{\mathbf{y}}_{m \times 1} = \underbrace{X}_{m \times (n+1)}\underbrace{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{(n+1) \times 1}$$

where $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (b, w_1, \ldots, w_n)^\top$. Now the entire dataset fits into one equation.

**Three regimes:**

- $m < n+1$: **underdetermined** — infinitely many solutions (minimum norm solution via pseudoinverse)
- $m = n+1$, $X$ invertible: **exactly determined** — unique solution $\boldsymbol{\theta} = X^{-1}\mathbf{y}$
- $m > n+1$: **overdetermined** — no exact solution in general → _least squares_

## Linear Regression: Fitting a Linear Model to Data

We have data ${(\mathbf{x}_i, y_i)}_{i=1}^m$ and we want to find $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ such that $X\boldsymbol{\theta} \approx \mathbf{y}$.

### The Least Squares Objective

$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = |X\boldsymbol{\theta} - \mathbf{y}|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^m \left(y_i - \boldsymbol{\theta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i\right)^2$$

This is a convex quadratic in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ — it has a unique global minimum.

### The Normal Equations

**Proof of the closed-form solution:**

Set $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L} = 0$:

$$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} |X\boldsymbol{\theta} - \mathbf{y}|^2 = 2X^\top(X\boldsymbol{\theta} - \mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{0}$$

$$\boxed{X^\top X, \boldsymbol{\theta} = X^\top \mathbf{y}}$$

These are the **Normal Equations**. If $X^\top X$ is invertible (columns of $X$ are linearly independent, which requires $m \geq n+1$ and no multicollinearity):

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top \mathbf{y}$$

The matrix $X^+ = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top$ is the **Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse** of $X$ (when $m > n$).

### Geometric Interpretation

The prediction $\hat{\mathbf{y}} = X\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ is the **orthogonal projection** of $\mathbf{y}$ onto the column space $\text{col}(X)$.

```
         y
        /|
       / |  ← residual e = y - ŷ  ⊥ col(X)
      /  |
     /   |
    ŷ────┘
   (in col(X))
```

The residual $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{y} - \hat{\mathbf{y}}$ is orthogonal to every column of $X$, which is precisely what $X^\top \mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$ says. The normal equations _are_ this orthogonality condition.

**Projection matrix:** $H = X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top$ is called the **hat matrix** because $\hat{\mathbf{y}} = H\mathbf{y}$. It satisfies $H^2 = H$ (idempotent) and $H^\top = H$ (symmetric) — the definition of an orthogonal projection.

## Worked Example: Fitting a Line

**Data:** $(x, y) = (1, 2.1),\ (2, 3.9),\ (3, 6.2),\ (4, 7.8),\ (5, 10.1)$

Model: $y = w_1 x + b$. Design matrix (with bias column):

$$X = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \ 1 & 4 \ 1 & 5 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{y} = \begin{pmatrix} 2.1 \ 3.9 \ 6.2 \ 7.8 \ 10.1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Compute: $$X^\top X = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 15 \ 15 & 55 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad X^\top \mathbf{y} = \begin{pmatrix} 30.1 \ 104.3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Solving $(X^\top X)\boldsymbol{\theta} = X^\top \mathbf{y}$: $$\det(X^\top X) = 5 \cdot 55 - 15^2 = 275 - 225 = 50$$

$$(X^\top X)^{-1} = \frac{1}{50}\begin{pmatrix} 55 & -15 \ -15 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = \frac{1}{50}\begin{pmatrix} 55 & -15 \ -15 & 5 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 30.1 \ 104.3 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{50}\begin{pmatrix} 55(30.1) - 15(104.3) \ -15(30.1) + 5(104.3) \end{pmatrix}$$

$$= \frac{1}{50}\begin{pmatrix} 1655.5 - 1564.5 \ -451.5 + 521.5 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{50}\begin{pmatrix} 91 \ 70 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1.82 \ 1.40 \end{pmatrix}$$

So: $\hat{y} = 1.40, x + 1.82$.

**Visualisation concept:**

```
y
10 │              ×
   │         ×
 7 │     ×
   │  ×          ← data points
 4 │×
   │    ────────── fitted line ŷ = 1.4x + 1.82
   └────────────────── x
   1  2  3  4  5
```

## Statistical Interpretation: The Linear Model with Noise

$$y_i = \boldsymbol{\theta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i + \varepsilon_i, \qquad \varepsilon_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

Under this assumption, the **maximum likelihood estimator** (MLE) for $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ coincides exactly with the least squares estimator. Proof:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{MLE}}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \prod_{i=1}^m \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{(y_i - \boldsymbol{\theta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

Taking log: $$\log \mathcal{L}_{\text{MLE}} = -\frac{m}{2}\log(2\pi\sigma^2) - \frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^m (y_i - \boldsymbol{\theta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i)^2$$

Maximising over $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ is equivalent to minimising $\sum_i (y_i - \boldsymbol{\theta}^\top \mathbf{x}_i)^2$. **Least squares = MLE under Gaussian noise.** Not an accident. A theorem.

### Gauss-Markov Theorem

Under the assumptions:

1. $\mathbb{E}[\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}] = \mathbf{0}$
2. $\text{Cov}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}) = \sigma^2 I$ (homoscedastic, uncorrelated errors)
3. $X$ is deterministic and full column rank

The OLS estimator $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top \mathbf{y}$ is the **Best Linear Unbiased Estimator (BLUE)** — it has the smallest variance among all linear unbiased estimators.

## Polynomial Regression: Still Linear!

The model $y = \theta_0 + \theta_1 x + \theta_2 x^2 + \cdots + \theta_d x^d$ is **nonlinear in $x$** but **linear in the parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$**. Define the feature map:

$$\phi(x) = (1, x, x^2, \ldots, x^d)^\top$$

Then $y = \boldsymbol{\theta}^\top \phi(x)$, and the same Normal Equations apply with $\phi(x_i)$ replacing $x_i$. The design matrix becomes:

$$X = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & x_1 & x_1^2 & \cdots & x_1^d \ \vdots & & & & \vdots \ 1 & x_m & x_m^2 & \cdots & x_m^d \end{pmatrix}$$

This is the **Vandermonde matrix**. The model is in the linear model class because $\mathcal{L}$ is convex quadratic in $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. The feature map $\phi$ does the heavy lifting.

**This is the key insight that generalises to machine learning:** by choosing rich enough features $\phi(\mathbf{x})$, a linear model in feature space can represent highly nonlinear functions of the original input $\mathbf{x}$.

## Regularisation: Constraining the Parameter Space

### Ridge Regression ($L^2$ Penalty)

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{ridge}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \left{|X\boldsymbol{\theta} - \mathbf{y}|^2 + \lambda|\boldsymbol{\theta}|^2\right}$$

**Closed form:** Differentiate and set to zero: $$2X^\top(X\boldsymbol{\theta} - \mathbf{y}) + 2\lambda\boldsymbol{\theta} = \mathbf{0}$$

$$\boxed{\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{ridge}} = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top \mathbf{y}}$$

The $\lambda I$ term makes the matrix invertible even when $X^\top X$ is singular (rank-deficient). This is **Tikhonov regularisation**. Probabilistically, this is the MAP estimator under a Gaussian prior $\boldsymbol{\theta} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \frac{\sigma^2}{\lambda}I)$.

### LASSO ($L^1$ Penalty)

$$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_{\text{LASSO}} = \arg\min_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \left{|X\boldsymbol{\theta} - \mathbf{y}|^2 + \lambda|\boldsymbol{\theta}|_1\right}$$

No closed form, but can be solved via coordinate descent or convex optimisation. The $L^1$ penalty induces **sparsity**: many components of $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ are driven to exactly zero. LASSO performs **automatic feature selection**.

**Geometric intuition:**

```
Ridge (L2 ball)          LASSO (L1 diamond)
     ___                      /\
    /   \    ●── θ*          /  \    ●── θ*
   |     |  /               /    \  /
   |  ○  | /               /  ○   \/
   |     |/                \      /\
    \___/                   \    /
                             \  /
                              \/
  θ* touches ellipse        θ* likely hits a corner
  at a smooth point          → one coordinate = 0
```

## Linear Models for Classification

### Logistic Regression

Not a regression — a classifier. The linear model $\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b$ outputs an unbounded real number. Pass it through the sigmoid:

$$p(y=1 \mid \mathbf{x}) = \sigma(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b)}}$$

The **decision boundary** ${\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b = 0}$ is a hyperplane. Points on one side get class 1, the other side class 0.

Loss function (cross-entropy / negative log-likelihood): $$\mathcal{L}(\mathbf{w}, b) = -\sum_{i=1}^m \left[y_i \log \hat{p}_i + (1-y_i)\log(1-\hat{p}_i)\right]$$

This is convex but has no closed form. Solved by gradient descent.

### Support Vector Machines (Hard Margin)

Find the hyperplane $\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b = 0$ that maximises the **margin** $\frac{2}{|\mathbf{w}|}$ between the two classes:

$$\min_{\mathbf{w}, b} \frac{1}{2}|\mathbf{w}|^2 \quad \text{s.t.} \quad y_i(\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}_i + b) \geq 1 \quad \forall, i$$

A constrained quadratic programme. The solution depends only on the **support vectors** — the training points closest to the hyperplane.

## Connection to AI and Deep Learning

### Every Layer of a Neural Network Is a Linear Model

A feedforward network layer is: $$\mathbf{z}^{(\ell)} = W^{(\ell)}\mathbf{a}^{(\ell-1)} + \mathbf{b}^{(\ell)}$$

This is a linear map $\mathbb{R}^{n_{\ell-1}} \to \mathbb{R}^{n_\ell}$ followed by a nonlinear activation $\sigma$. Without $\sigma$, stacking linear layers gives nothing new:

$$W^{(2)}(W^{(1)}\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}^{(1)}) + \mathbf{b}^{(2)} = \underbrace{W^{(2)}W^{(1)}}_{\tilde{W}}\mathbf{x} + \underbrace{W^{(2)}\mathbf{b}^{(1)} + \mathbf{b}^{(2)}}_{\tilde{\mathbf{b}}}$$

A composition of linear maps is still linear. Depth without nonlinearity is useless. The nonlinear activations (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh) are what make deep networks powerful. But the linear maps carry the representation; the nonlinearities merely fold and cut the space.

### The Kernel Trick: Linear in Feature Space = Nonlinear in Input Space

For a feature map $\phi: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathcal{H}$ (possibly infinite-dimensional), define the **kernel**:

$$k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \langle \phi(\mathbf{x}),, \phi(\mathbf{x}') \rangle_{\mathcal{H}}$$

The kernel RBF $k(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \exp(-|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'|^2 / 2\sigma^2)$ corresponds to an _infinite-dimensional_ feature map — yet we never compute $\phi(\mathbf{x})$ explicitly. The SVM dual and kernel regression are linear in $\phi$-space but highly nonlinear in $\mathbf{x}$-space.

Deep learning implicitly learns a feature map $\phi$ (all layers except the last) and then fits a linear classifier on top. The last layer of almost every deep network — a softmax over $W\mathbf{h} + \mathbf{b}$ — is logistic regression on learned features $\mathbf{h}$.

### Gradient Descent on Linear Models

For a linear model with squared loss: $$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{1}{m}|X\boldsymbol{\theta} - \mathbf{y}|^2$$

Gradient: $$\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{L} = \frac{2}{m}X^\top(X\boldsymbol{\theta} - \mathbf{y})$$

Gradient descent update: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta \cdot \frac{2}{m}X^\top(X\boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \mathbf{y})$$

This is a linear recurrence in $\boldsymbol{\theta}_t$. Convergence is guaranteed if $\eta < \frac{1}{\lambda_{\max}(X^\top X / m)}$ where $\lambda_{\max}$ is the largest eigenvalue of $X^\top X/m$. The **condition number** $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ governs how fast (ill-conditioned = slow). This analysis is exact for linear models and motivates preconditioning and adaptive learning rates (Adam, RMSProp) in deep learning.

### Linear Attention and Transformers

The attention mechanism in a Transformer can be written: $$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

**Linear Attention** (Katharopoulos et al., 2020) approximates the softmax by a kernel $\phi(Q)\phi(K)^\top$ with a positive feature map $\phi$. This makes attention linear in sequence length rather than quadratic — a direct application of the kernel trick to replace a nonlinear similarity with a linear inner product in feature space.

## Assumptions, Violations, and Diagnostics

The linear model $\mathbf{y} = X\boldsymbol{\theta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ carries assumptions that must be interrogated:

|Assumption|Violation|Consequence|Fix|
|---|---|---|---|
|Linearity|True $f$ is nonlinear|Systematic residual pattern|Feature engineering, polynomial terms, neural networks|
|$\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon_i] = 0$|Omitted variable bias|$\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$ biased and inconsistent|Include missing variables|
|Homoscedasticity|Variance $\sigma^2$ varies with $x$|OLS inefficient; SEs wrong|WLS, robust standard errors|
|No multicollinearity|Columns of $X$ nearly dependent|$(X^\top X)^{-1}$ near-singular; large variance in $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$|Ridge, PCA, drop variables|
|Independence|Autocorrelated errors (time series)|OLS inefficient; SEs wrong|GLS, time-series models|

**Residual diagnostics:**

- Plot $e_i$ vs. $\hat{y}_i$ → should show no pattern
- Q-Q plot of $e_i$ → should lie on diagonal if Gaussian
- $|e_i|$ vs. $\hat{y}_i$ → should be flat (homoscedasticity)

## Bias-Variance in Linear Models

For the linear model $\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^\top \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}$:

$$\text{Bias}[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_0)]^2 = \left(\mathbf{x}_0^\top \boldsymbol{\theta}^* - \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{x}_0^\top \hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}]\right)^2$$

Under correct model specification (true $f$ is linear), OLS is unbiased by Gauss-Markov: $\mathbb{E}[\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}] = \boldsymbol{\theta}^*$, so bias = 0.

Variance: $$\text{Var}[\hat{f}(\mathbf{x}_0)] = \sigma^2 \mathbf{x}_0^\top (X^\top X)^{-1} \mathbf{x}_0$$

Large when $\mathbf{x}_0$ is far from the training data, when $\sigma^2$ is large, or when $X^\top X$ is ill-conditioned.

**Ridge regression deliberately introduces bias** ($\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_\lambda \neq \boldsymbol{\theta}^*$ in general) in exchange for reduced variance: $$\text{Var}[\hat{f}_\lambda(\mathbf{x}_0)] = \sigma^2 \mathbf{x}_0^\top (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-2} X^\top X, \mathbf{x}_0 < \text{Var}_{\text{OLS}}$$

For some $\lambda > 0$, the reduction in variance dominates the increase in bias, reducing total MSE. This is not ad hoc — it follows from the **James-Stein phenomenon** (shrinkage estimators dominate OLS in $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n \geq 3$).

## Summary

$$\boxed{\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}} = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top \mathbf{y}}$$

This single equation — four matrices, one formula — is the foundation of:

- Classical statistics (OLS, ANOVA, GLMs)
- Signal processing (Wiener filter, matched filter)
- Machine learning (linear regression, logistic regression, SVMs, last-layer classifiers)
- Numerical linear algebra (least squares solvers, pseudoinverse)

The linear model is not a simplification to graduate beyond. It is the **skeleton** on which everything else is built. Understanding it deeply — its geometry, its statistics, its failure modes — is non-negotiable.
