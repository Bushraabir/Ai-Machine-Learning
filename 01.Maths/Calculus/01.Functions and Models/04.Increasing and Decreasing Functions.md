## 1. Definitions — The Rigorous Foundation

Let $f$ be defined on an interval $I$.

> **Definition 1.1 (Increasing Function).**  
> $f$ is _increasing_ on $I$ if for every $x_1, x_2 \in I$: $$x_1 < x_2 \implies f(x_1) < f(x_2)$$

> **Definition 1.2 (Decreasing Function).**  
> $f$ is _decreasing_ on $I$ if for every $x_1, x_2 \in I$: $$x_1 < x_2 \implies f(x_1) > f(x_2)$$

> **Definition 1.3 (Non-decreasing / Non-increasing).**  
> Replace strict inequalities with $\leq$ or $\geq$ to get the _monotone_ (non-strict) versions. These matter in optimization theory and activation functions.

**Personal note:** The definitions are _purely order-theoretic_ — no calculus required yet. A function can be increasing on $I$ without being differentiable. The derivative enters only when we want a _practical test_.

## 2. The Derivative Test — The Workhorse Theorem

> **Theorem 2.1 (Increasing/Decreasing Test).**  
> Let $f$ be continuous on $[a, b]$ and differentiable on $(a, b)$.
> 
> (a) If $f'(x) > 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is **increasing** on $[a,b]$.  
> (b) If $f'(x) < 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is **decreasing** on $[a,b]$.  
> (c) If $f'(x) = 0$ for all $x \in (a,b)$, then $f$ is **constant** on $[a,b]$.

**Proof of (a).**

Let $x_1, x_2 \in [a,b]$ with $x_1 < x_2$. Since $f$ is continuous on $[x_1, x_2]$ and differentiable on $(x_1, x_2)$, the **Mean Value Theorem** applies:

$$\exists, c \in (x_1, x_2) \text{ such that } f'(c) = \frac{f(x_2) - f(x_1)}{x_2 - x_1}$$

Since $f'(c) > 0$ and $x_2 - x_1 > 0$, we conclude:

$$f(x_2) - f(x_1) = f'(c)(x_2 - x_1) > 0$$

$$\therefore\quad f(x_1) < f(x_2) \qquad \square$$

**Proof of (b)** is symmetric. **Proof of (c)** follows from MVT with $f'(c) = 0 \Rightarrow f(x_2) = f(x_1)$ for all pairs.

> **Remark.** The converse is _almost_ true: if $f$ is differentiable and increasing on $(a,b)$, then $f'(x) \geq 0$ on $(a,b)$ — but not necessarily $f'(x) > 0$ everywhere. The function $f(x) = x^3$ is increasing on $\mathbb{R}$ yet $f'(0) = 0$.

## 3. Finding Intervals of Increase and Decrease — The Algorithm

Given a differentiable function $f$, the procedure is:

**Step 1.** Compute $f'(x)$.

**Step 2.** Find all **critical numbers**: values $c$ in the domain of $f$ where $$f'(c) = 0 \quad \text{or} \quad f'(c) \text{ does not exist}$$

**Step 3.** These critical numbers, together with the endpoints and discontinuities of the domain, partition the real line into **open intervals**.

**Step 4.** On each interval, pick a **test point** and evaluate the sign of $f'$.

- $f' > 0 \Rightarrow f$ increasing $(\nearrow)$
- $f' < 0 \Rightarrow f$ decreasing $(\searrow)$

**Step 5.** Conclude. At transitions from $\nearrow$ to $\searrow$: **local maximum**. From $\searrow$ to $\nearrow$: **local minimum**.

> **Why does this work?** Because $f'$ is continuous on each open interval (assuming $f$ is $C^1$), and a continuous function that is nonzero at one point must maintain its sign on the entire interval (Intermediate Value Theorem for derivatives, i.e., Darboux's Theorem).

## 4. Worked Examples

### Example 4.1 — Polynomial

Let $f(x) = x^3 - 3x^2 - 9x + 5$.

**Step 1.** $f'(x) = 3x^2 - 6x - 9 = 3(x^2 - 2x - 3) = 3(x-3)(x+1)$

**Step 2.** Critical numbers: $x = -1,; x = 3$.

**Step 3.** Intervals: $(-\infty, -1),; (-1, 3),; (3, \infty)$.

**Step 4.** Sign chart:

|Interval|Test Point|$f'$|Behavior|
|---|---|---|---|
|$(-\infty, -1)$|$x = -2$|$3(-5)(-1) = +15 > 0$|$\nearrow$ increasing|
|$(-1, 3)$|$x = 0$|$3(-3)(1) = -9 < 0$|$\searrow$ decreasing|
|$(3, \infty)$|$x = 4$|$3(1)(5) = +15 > 0$|$\nearrow$ increasing|

**Step 5.** Conclusions:

- Local **maximum** at $x = -1$: $f(-1) = -1 - 3 + 9 + 5 = 10$
- Local **minimum** at $x = 3$: $f(3) = 27 - 27 - 27 + 5 = -22$

---

### Example 4.2 — Transcendental Function

Let $f(x) = xe^{-x}$ on $\mathbb{R}$.

**Step 1.** $f'(x) = e^{-x} + x(-e^{-x}) = e^{-x}(1 - x)$

**Step 2.** $e^{-x} > 0$ always, so $f'(x) = 0 \iff 1 - x = 0 \iff x = 1$.

**Step 3.** Intervals: $(-\infty, 1),; (1, \infty)$.

**Step 4.**

- For $x < 1$: $(1-x) > 0 \Rightarrow f' > 0$ — increasing.
- For $x > 1$: $(1-x) < 0 \Rightarrow f' < 0$ — decreasing.

**Step 5.** Global maximum at $x = 1$: $f(1) = e^{-1} = 1/e \approx 0.368$.

> _This function appears in probability theory (exponential distributions) and also models attention decay in sequential models._

---

### Example 4.3 — Trigonometric (Important for Fourier / Signal Processing)

Let $f(x) = \sin x - x$ on $[0, 2\pi]$.

**Step 1.** $f'(x) = \cos x - 1$

**Step 2.** $\cos x - 1 = 0 \iff \cos x = 1 \iff x = 0, 2\pi$ (on $[0, 2\pi]$).

**Step 4.** For $x \in (0, 2\pi)$: $\cos x < 1 \Rightarrow f'(x) < 0$.

$f$ is **strictly decreasing** on $[0, 2\pi]$. No interior local extrema.

---

### Example 4.4 — A Piecewise Subtlety

Let $f(x) = |x|$.

$f'(x) = \begin{cases} -1 & x < 0 \ 1 & x > 0 \end{cases}$, undefined at $x = 0$.

$x = 0$ is a critical number. Sign chart confirms: decreasing on $(-\infty, 0)$, increasing on $(0, \infty)$. Local (and global) minimum at $x = 0$.

> **Note:** $f'(0)$ does not exist — yet $x = 0$ is still a critical number and a genuine local min. The algorithm handles this because we include points where $f'$ fails to exist in Step 2.

## 6. Edge Cases and Subtleties

### 6.1 $f'(c) = 0$ does NOT guarantee extremum

$f(x) = x^3$: $f'(0) = 0$, yet $f$ is **strictly increasing** through $x = 0$.

Proof: $f'(x) = 3x^2 \geq 0$, equals zero only at $x = 0$ — which is an isolated point. A single zero of $f'$ doesn't break monotonicity.

**Lesson:** Always check the sign of $f'$ on entire intervals, not just at the critical point.

### 6.2 Monotone Functions and Invertibility

> **Theorem 6.1.** If $f$ is strictly increasing (or strictly decreasing) on $I$, then $f$ is **injective** on $I$ and $f^{-1}$ exists on $f(I)$.

This is the theoretical backbone behind why log is the inverse of exp, why arcsin is defined only on $[-\pi/2, \pi/2]$, and why normalizing flows in ML require **monotone** transformations.

### 6.3 Strict vs. Non-Strict Monotonicity in Practice

In optimization:

- A **strictly convex** loss function has a **strictly decreasing** gradient (loss decreasing along gradient flow), implying a **unique** global minimum.
- A **non-strictly convex** loss may have flat regions — problematic for gradient-based methods.

## 7. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

This section is where the pure mathematics becomes engineering.

### 7.1 Gradient Descent — The Entire Algorithm is About $f'$

The fundamental ML optimization loop:

$$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$

**Why does this work?**

If $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) > 0$, the loss $\mathcal{L}$ is _increasing_ in $\theta$ — so we step _left_ (subtract).  
If $\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta_t) < 0$, the loss is _decreasing_ — so we step _right_ (add).

In both cases, we move in the direction where the loss _decreases_. This is literally the Increasing/Decreasing Test applied to update rules.

$$\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta} > 0 \Rightarrow \mathcal{L} \text{ increasing} \Rightarrow \text{decrease } \theta$$ $$\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta} < 0 \Rightarrow \mathcal{L} \text{ decreasing} \Rightarrow \text{increase } \theta$$

The algorithm terminates when $\nabla \mathcal{L} = 0$ — the critical point condition.

### 7.2 Learning Rate and Sign Charts

The sign chart intuition directly maps to the loss landscape:

```
Loss ℒ(θ)
  ↑
  |       *
  |     *   *          ← local min at θ*
  |   *       *
  | *           *
  +───────────────→ θ
        θ*

  Left of θ*:  ℒ'(θ) < 0  → ℒ is decreasing → gradient descent moves RIGHT ✓
  Right of θ*: ℒ'(θ) > 0  → ℒ is increasing → gradient descent moves LEFT ✓
```

### 7.3 Activation Functions — Monotonicity as a Design Constraint

|Activation|Formula|Monotone?|$f'$ sign|Why It Matters|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Sigmoid|$\frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$|✅ Always|$\geq 0$|Smooth, bounded; vanishing gradient for large $\|x\|$|
|Tanh|$\tanh(x)$|✅ Always|$\geq 0$|Centered at 0; same vanishing gradient issue|
|ReLU|$\max(0,x)$|✅ Non-decreasing|$\in {0,1}$|Non-saturating for $x>0$; dead neuron for $x<0$|
|Leaky ReLU|$\max(\alpha x, x)$|✅ Always|$> 0$|Fixes dead neuron; $f' = \alpha$ for $x<0$|
|GELU|$x \cdot \Phi(x)$|❌ Not globally|Mixed|Slight non-monotonicity near $x \approx -0.17$|
|Swish|$x \cdot \sigma(x)$|❌ Not globally|Mixed|Non-monotone: can represent more complex functions|

**Key insight:** Non-monotone activations (GELU, Swish) have regions where $f' < 0$ — they can "suppress" large negative activations while passing large positive ones. This added flexibility is why modern transformers (BERT, GPT) prefer GELU over ReLU.

### 7.4 Monotone Functions in Normalizing Flows

**Normalizing flows** are generative models that learn a bijection $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ transforming a simple distribution (e.g., Gaussian) to a complex data distribution.

For $f$ to be a bijection, it must be **strictly monotone** in each dimension (1D case). The change-of-variables formula:

$$p_X(x) = p_Z(f^{-1}(x)) \cdot \left|\det J_{f^{-1}}(x)\right|$$

requires $f$ to be invertible, which is guaranteed by strict monotonicity. Models like **Real NVP**, **Glow**, and **Neural Spline Flows** all rely on this.

### 7.5 Loss Landscape Analysis

A function $\mathcal{L}(\theta)$ has a **saddle point** at $\theta_0$ if:

- $\nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_0) = 0$ (critical point), but
- $\mathcal{L}$ is increasing in some directions and decreasing in others around $\theta_0$.

In high-dimensional deep networks, most critical points are saddle points, not true local minima (Dauphin et al., 2014). Gradient descent often escapes saddle points due to the structure of the loss's increasing/decreasing regions.

### 7.6 Backpropagation and the Chain Rule

During backpropagation, gradients flow through activation functions:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial a} \cdot f'(x)$$

where $a = f(x)$ is the activation. If $f'(x) \approx 0$ (activation in saturation region — where $f$ is nearly constant, neither increasing nor decreasing), gradients **vanish**. This is the **vanishing gradient problem** — directly caused by flat regions of activation functions.

```
Sigmoid saturation:
                          f'(x) ≈ 0 here
                         ↙              ↙
  σ(x)  ·····          ←→              ←→        ·····
                   (flat)              (flat)
  σ'(x) ≈ 0 for |x| >> 1

Backprop through k layers with sigmoid:
  gradient ≈ (σ'(x))^k → 0 exponentially as k grows
```

ReLU fixes this: $f'(x) = 1$ for $x > 0$, so gradients don't decay multiplicatively.

### 7.7 Monotone Operators in Optimization Theory

In convex optimization — the mathematical backbone of ML — a function $f$ is **convex** iff its gradient map $\nabla f$ is a **monotone operator**:

$$\langle \nabla f(x) - \nabla f(y),; x - y \rangle \geq 0 \quad \forall, x, y$$

This is the multivariable generalization of "$f'$ non-decreasing $\Leftrightarrow$ $f$ convex." Convex losses guarantee no spurious local minima — a fundamental assumption in linear regression, logistic regression, SVMs, etc.
