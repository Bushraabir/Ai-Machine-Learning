
> _"A rational function is a fraction whose numerator and denominator are polynomials. Its soul lives in its singularities — the places where the denominator vanishes."_

---

## §1. Definition & Basic Setup

**Definition 1.1 (Rational Function).** A _rational function_ is any function of the form $$ r(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} $$ where $p(x)$ and $q(x)$ are polynomials and $q(x) \not\equiv 0$.

**Domain.** The natural domain of $r$ is all real $x$ where $q(x) \neq 0$: $$ \text{dom}(r) = {x \in \mathbb{R} : q(x) \neq 0}. $$

> **Convention.** We always assume $p$ and $q$ share no common factors — i.e., $r$ is in _lowest terms_. If they do share a factor $(x - a)$, cancelling it removes a _removable discontinuity_ (a hole), not a vertical asymptote.

**Examples at a glance:**

|Function|$p(x)$|$q(x)$|Domain|
|---|---|---|---|
|$\dfrac{1}{x}$|$1$|$x$|$\mathbb{R}\setminus{0}$|
|$\dfrac{x^2 - 1}{x - 1}$|$x^2-1$|$x-1$|$\mathbb{R}\setminus{1}$ — has a **hole** at $x=1$|
|$\dfrac{3x^2 + 1}{x^2 + x - 6}$|$3x^2+1$|$(x-2)(x+3)$|$\mathbb{R}\setminus{-3, 2}$|
|$\dfrac{x^3}{x^2+1}$|$x^3$|$x^2+1$|$\mathbb{R}$ — denominator never zero|

## §2. Zeros and Poles

**Definition 2.1 (Zero).** $x = a$ is a _zero_ of $r$ if $r(a) = 0$, i.e., $p(a) = 0$ and $q(a) \neq 0$.

**Definition 2.2 (Pole).** $x = a$ is a _pole_ of $r$ if $q(a) = 0$ and $p(a) \neq 0$ (after full cancellation of common factors). The _order_ of the pole is the multiplicity of $(x-a)$ as a factor of $q$.

**Behaviour at a pole of order $n$:** $$ \lim_{x \to a} |r(x)| = +\infty. $$ The _parity_ of $n$ determines whether $r$ changes sign:

- **Odd order pole:** $r(x) \to +\infty$ from one side, $-\infty$ from the other. Graph crosses the vertical asymptote "direction-wise."
- **Even order pole:** $r(x) \to +\infty$ from both sides (or $-\infty$ from both sides).

**Example 2.1.** $$ r(x) = \frac{1}{(x-1)^2(x+2)} $$

- Pole of order 2 at $x = 1$ (even): $r \to +\infty$ from both sides.
- Pole of order 1 at $x = -2$ (odd): $r \to +\infty$ from one side, $-\infty$ from the other.

## §3. Asymptotes — The Skeleton of the Graph

Asymptotes encode the global behaviour of $r$. There are three types.

### 3.1 Vertical Asymptotes

$x = a$ is a _vertical asymptote_ if $q(a) = 0$ and the factor $(x-a)$ does not cancel with $p(x)$.

$$ \lim_{x \to a^+} r(x) = \pm\infty \quad \text{and/or} \quad \lim_{x \to a^-} r(x) = \pm\infty. $$

### 3.2 Horizontal Asymptotes

Let $\deg p = m$, $\deg q = n$, with leading coefficients $a_m$ and $b_n$.

$$ \lim_{x \to \pm\infty} r(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{if } m < n \ \dfrac{a_m}{b_n} & \text{if } m = n \ \pm\infty & \text{if } m > n \end{cases} $$

When $m < n$ or $m = n$: horizontal asymptote $y = 0$ or $y = a_m/b_n$. When $m > n$: no horizontal asymptote (but possibly an oblique one).

### 3.3 Oblique (Slant) Asymptotes

Occur exactly when $\deg p = \deg q + 1$. Perform polynomial long division: $$ r(x) = \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = (ax + b) + \frac{\text{remainder}(x)}{q(x)}. $$ Since $\deg(\text{remainder}) < \deg q$, the remainder term $\to 0$ as $x \to \pm\infty$, so: $$ r(x) \approx ax + b \quad \text{as } x \to \pm\infty. $$ The line $y = ax + b$ is the oblique asymptote.

**Example 3.1.** $$ r(x) = \frac{x^2 + 3}{x - 1} $$ Long division: $x^2 + 3 = (x-1)(x+1) + 4$, so $$ r(x) = x + 1 + \frac{4}{x-1}. $$ Oblique asymptote: $y = x + 1$. Vertical asymptote: $x = 1$.

## §4. Holes vs. Vertical Asymptotes — A Critical Distinction

When a factor $(x - a)$ appears in _both_ $p$ and $q$:

$$ r(x) = \frac{(x-a)^m \cdot \tilde{p}(x)}{(x-a)^n \cdot \tilde{q}(x)}, \quad \tilde{q}(a) \neq 0. $$

- If $m \geq n$: factor cancels fully or partially. The singularity at $x = a$ is **removable** — a _hole_ in the graph.
- If $m < n$: a factor remains in denominator. **Vertical asymptote** at $x = a$.

**Example 4.1 — Hole.** $$ r(x) = \frac{x^2 - 4}{x - 2} = \frac{(x-2)(x+2)}{x-2} = x + 2, \quad x \neq 2. $$ The graph is the line $y = x + 2$ with a single point punched out at $(2, 4)$.

The limit exists: $\lim_{x \to 2} r(x) = 4$, but $r(2)$ is undefined. This is a removable discontinuity.

```
y
│        ○  ← hole at (2, 4)
│      ╱
│    ╱
│  ╱
│╱
└────────── x
         2
```

## §5. Graphing Strategy — A Complete Procedure

To sketch $r(x) = p(x)/q(x)$ in lowest terms:

1. **Domain:** Solve $q(x) = 0$; exclude those $x$.
2. **Holes:** Factor both $p$ and $q$; cancel common factors; note the $x$-values removed.
3. **Zeros:** Solve $p(x) = 0$ (with $q(x) \neq 0$). Note multiplicities.
4. **$y$-intercept:** Compute $r(0)$ if $0$ is in the domain.
5. **Vertical asymptotes:** Uncancelled zeros of $q$; determine sign behaviour (odd/even order).
6. **Horizontal or oblique asymptote:** Compare degrees.
7. **Sign chart:** Between zeros and poles, determine the sign of $r$.
8. **End behaviour:** Confirm consistency with asymptotes.
9. **Sketch.**

## §6. Worked Examples — Full Analysis

### Example 6.1

$$ r(x) = \frac{2x}{x^2 - 1} = \frac{2x}{(x-1)(x+1)} $$

- **Domain:** $\mathbb{R} \setminus {-1, 1}$
- **Zeros:** $x = 0$ (numerator $= 0$, denominator $\neq 0$)
- **$y$-intercept:** $r(0) = 0$
- **Vertical asymptotes:** $x = 1$ (order 1, odd), $x = -1$ (order 1, odd)
- **Horizontal asymptote:** $\deg p = 1 < \deg q = 2$, so $y = 0$
- **Symmetry:** $r(-x) = -r(x)$: the function is **odd** — symmetric about the origin.

Sign chart:

```
 x:   -∞    -1     0     1     +∞
 r:    −  |  ↑↓  | +  | ↑↓  |  +
         VA      0     VA
```

(Sign changes at each zero and each odd-order pole.)

---

### Example 6.2

$$ r(x) = \frac{x^2 - x - 2}{x^2 - 4} = \frac{(x-2)(x+1)}{(x-2)(x+2)} $$

Cancel $(x - 2)$: $$ r(x) = \frac{x+1}{x+2}, \quad x \neq 2 \quad (\text{hole at } x = 2). $$

- **Hole:** At $x = 2$: $\lim_{x\to 2} r(x) = \frac{3}{4}$. Point $(2, 3/4)$ is absent.
- **Vertical asymptote:** $x = -2$ (from $x + 2 = 0$)
- **Horizontal asymptote:** Degrees equal ($\deg = 1$ each), leading coefficients both $1$: $y = 1$
- **Zero:** $x = -1$
- **$y$-intercept:** $r(0) = \frac{1}{2}$

---

### Example 6.3 — Oblique Asymptote

$$ r(x) = \frac{x^3}{x^2 + 1} $$

Long division: $x^3 \div (x^2 + 1)$:

$$ x^3 = x \cdot (x^2 + 1) - x $$

$$ r(x) = x - \frac{x}{x^2 + 1} $$

- **Oblique asymptote:** $y = x$
- **Domain:** $\mathbb{R}$ (denominator $x^2 + 1 > 0$ always)
- **Zero:** $x = 0$
- **Symmetry:** Odd function ($r(-x) = -r(x)$)

As $x \to \pm\infty$: $r(x) \to x$. The graph hugs the line $y = x$ asymptotically.

## §7. Limits and Continuity

**Proposition 7.1.** Every rational function $r = p/q$ is continuous on its domain.

**Proof.** Both $p$ and $q$ are polynomials, hence continuous everywhere. On the set ${x : q(x) \neq 0}$, $r$ is a quotient of continuous functions with nonzero denominator, hence continuous. $\square$

**Limits at poles.** Near a simple pole $x = a$, write $q(x) = (x-a)\tilde{q}(x)$ with $\tilde{q}(a) \neq 0$:

$$ r(x) = \frac{p(x)}{(x-a)\tilde{q}(x)} \to \frac{p(a)}{0^{\pm} \cdot \tilde{q}(a)} = \pm\infty. $$

The sign depends on the sign of $p(a)/\tilde{q}(a)$ and the direction of approach.

## §8. Calculus of Rational Functions

### 8.1 Differentiation

Using the quotient rule: $$ r'(x) = \frac{p'(x)q(x) - p(x)q'(x)}{[q(x)]^2}. $$

The result is again a rational function (with $[q(x)]^2$ in the denominator). Critical points of $r$ occur where the numerator $p'q - pq' = 0$.

**Example 8.1.** $r(x) = \dfrac{1}{x}$, so $p = 1$, $q = x$: $$ r'(x) = \frac{0 \cdot x - 1 \cdot 1}{x^2} = -\frac{1}{x^2} < 0 \quad \forall x \neq 0. $$ $r$ is strictly decreasing on each interval of its domain. No local extrema.

### 8.2 Integration via Partial Fractions

Integrating rational functions reduces to partial fraction decomposition — the systematic algebraic splitting of $r$ into simpler summands.

**The master strategy.** If $\deg p \geq \deg q$, first perform long division to write: $$ r(x) = s(x) + \frac{\text{rem}(x)}{q(x)}, \quad \deg(\text{rem}) < \deg q. $$ Then decompose the proper fraction $\text{rem}/q$.

**Partial fraction forms** (after factoring $q$ over $\mathbb{R}$):

|Factor in $q(x)$|Contribution to decomposition|
|---|---|
|$(x - a)$|$\dfrac{A}{x - a}$|
|$(x - a)^k$|$\dfrac{A_1}{x-a} + \dfrac{A_2}{(x-a)^2} + \cdots + \dfrac{A_k}{(x-a)^k}$|
|$(x^2 + bx + c)$ irreducible|$\dfrac{Ax + B}{x^2 + bx + c}$|
|$(x^2 + bx + c)^k$|$\dfrac{A_1 x + B_1}{x^2 + bx + c} + \cdots + \dfrac{A_k x + B_k}{(x^2 + bx + c)^k}$|

**Example 8.2.** $$ \int \frac{2x+1}{(x-1)(x+2)}, dx $$

Decompose: $\dfrac{2x+1}{(x-1)(x+2)} = \dfrac{A}{x-1} + \dfrac{B}{x+2}$.

Multiply through: $2x + 1 = A(x+2) + B(x-1)$.

Set $x = 1$: $3 = 3A \implies A = 1$. Set $x = -2$: $-3 = -3B \implies B = 1$.

$$ \int \frac{2x+1}{(x-1)(x+2)}, dx = \int \frac{1}{x-1},dx + \int\frac{1}{x+2},dx = \ln|x-1| + \ln|x+2| + C. $$

## §9. Visualisation — Anatomy of a Rational Function

```
         ↑ y
         │
    ─────┼──────────────── y = L  (horizontal asymptote)
         │         ╱
         │      ─╱─
    ─────┼────╱───────────
         │  ╱
    ─────│─╱─── x=a  (vertical asymptote)
        ─│─
         │
   ──────┴─────────────── x
         │
    The graph cannot cross a vertical asymptote.
    It CAN cross a horizontal asymptote (finitely many times).
```

> **Common misconception:** Graphs _never_ cross vertical asymptotes (by definition — $r$ is undefined there). But horizontal asymptotes are a statement about _limits at infinity_ — the graph absolutely can cross the horizontal asymptote for finite $x$.

**Example:** $r(x) = \dfrac{\sin x \cdot e^{-x^2}}{1}$ is not rational, but illustrates the principle. For rational functions, a simple example:

$$ r(x) = \frac{x}{x^2 + 1}, \quad \text{horizontal asymptote } y = 0. $$ $r(0) = 0$: the graph crosses $y = 0$ at $x = 0$.

## §10. Partial Fractions and the Residue — Bridge to Complex Analysis

The coefficients in the partial fraction decomposition for simple poles have a beautiful interpretation. For a simple pole at $x = a$:

$$ A = \lim_{x \to a} (x - a), r(x) = \frac{p(a)}{q'(a)}. $$

This is called the **residue** of $r$ at $a$. In complex analysis, the residue theorem turns this algebraic observation into a powerful integration technique over complex contours. The partial fractions you compute in Calculus II are the real-line shadow of that theory.

## §11. Connection to AI, Machine Learning & Deep Learning

> _Rational functions appear in ML in places that are easy to overlook but structurally important._

### 11.1 Rational Approximation of Activation Functions

The **GELU** activation, used in GPT and BERT, is defined as: $$ \text{GELU}(x) = x \cdot \Phi(x) $$ where $\Phi$ is the Gaussian CDF — not closed form. In practice, it is approximated by a rational function for fast GPU evaluation: $$ \text{GELU}(x) \approx \frac{x}{1 + e^{-1.702 x}} $$ or via a degree-$(3,2)$ Padé approximant. The **Padé approximant** is the canonical rational-function approximation to a smooth function — analogous to Taylor series but often superior near singularities.

**Padé approximant of order $(m, n)$** to $f$ at $x = 0$: $$ R_{m,n}(x) = \frac{a_0 + a_1 x + \cdots + a_m x^m}{1 + b_1 x + \cdots + b_n x^n} $$ chosen so that $R_{m,n}$ and $f$ agree to order $m + n$ in their Taylor expansion.

### 11.2 Rational Activations (RAU)

Recent work (Molina et al., 2019) proposes replacing fixed activations with _learnable rational functions_: $$ \sigma(x) = \frac{P(x)}{Q(x)}, \quad P, Q \text{ polynomials with learnable coefficients}. $$ These **Rational Activation Units (RAU)** can approximate ReLU, Leaky ReLU, GELU, etc., and the network learns the best activation shape during training. The key constraint: $Q(x) > 0$ must be enforced (e.g., $Q(x) = |q(x)| + \epsilon$) to prevent poles inside the domain.

### 11.3 Transformer Attention is a Rational Function

Scaled dot-product attention: $$ \text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^\top}{\sqrt{d}}\right) V $$ The softmax of a linear expression is a rational function componentwise: $$ \text{softmax}(z)_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}. $$ This is a ratio of exponentials — technically an _exponential rational function_, but its algebraic structure (numerator/denominator both positive, output sums to 1) mirrors the rational function setting. The gradient of softmax inherits the quotient-rule structure: $$ \frac{\partial \text{softmax}(z)_i}{\partial z_j} = \text{softmax}(z)_i (\delta_{ij} - \text{softmax}(z)_j). $$

### 11.4 Rational Interpolation in Gaussian Processes

Kernel functions in Gaussian Processes (GPs) are sometimes rational: $$ k(x, x') = \frac{\sigma^2}{1 + \left(\frac{x - x'}{l}\right)^2} $$ This is the **rational quadratic kernel** — a scale mixture of squared-exponential kernels. Its rational structure gives heavier tails than the Gaussian kernel, better modelling functions with varying lengthscales.

### 11.5 Stability Analysis of Recurrent Networks

The hidden-state update of a linear RNN is: $$ h_t = W h_{t-1} + U x_t. $$ Unrolling and taking the $z$-transform gives a _transfer function_: $$ H(z) = (zI - W)^{-1} U, $$ which is a matrix of rational functions of $z$ (the matrix inverse of a polynomial matrix is rational). The **poles** of $H(z)$ are the eigenvalues of $W$. Stability requires all poles inside the unit disk $|z| < 1$ — a condition on the zeros of $\det(zI - W)$, which is a polynomial. The theory of poles of rational functions is the exact tool used in signal processing and control theory to analyse such systems.

|Concept|Key Fact|
|---|---|
