## 1. The Unit Circle — The Right Way to Think About It

Most calculus courses introduce trig via right triangles. That's pedagogically convenient but _conceptually limiting_. The correct foundational picture is the **unit circle**.

**Definition (Unit Circle).** The unit circle $S^1 \subset \mathbb{R}^2$ is the set $$S^1 = {(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2 \mid x^2 + y^2 = 1}.$$

Fix a real number $\theta$ (measured in **radians**). Consider the point $P(\theta)$ obtained by starting at $(1, 0)$ and traversing an arc of signed length $\theta$ counterclockwise along $S^1$.

$$\cos\theta := x\text{-coordinate of }P(\theta), \qquad \sin\theta := y\text{-coordinate of }P(\theta).$$

**Why radians?** One radian is the angle subtended at the center of a circle by an arc whose length equals the radius. With this convention, arc length $= r\theta$, and the derivative formulas take their simplest form. If you use degrees, you drag a factor of $\pi/180$ everywhere — ugly and unnatural.

> **Personal note:** Stewart §1.4 uses exactly this picture. MIT 18.02 formalizes it further once we parametrize $S^1$ as $\mathbf{r}(t) = (\cos t, \sin t)$, giving velocity $\mathbf{r}'(t) = (-\sin t, \cos t)$ — a vector always _tangent_ to the circle. This is the seed of everything.

## 2. Formal Definitions

### The Six Trigonometric Functions

|Function|Symbol|Definition|Undefined When|
|---|---|---|---|
|Sine|$\sin\theta$|$y$-coord on unit circle|—|
|Cosine|$\cos\theta$|$x$-coord on unit circle|—|
|Tangent|$\tan\theta$|$\sin\theta / \cos\theta$|$\cos\theta = 0$, i.e. $\theta = \pi/2 + n\pi$|

|Secant|$\sec\theta$|$1/\cos\theta$|$\cos\theta = 0$|
|Cotangent|$\cot\theta$|$\cos\theta/\sin\theta$|$\sin\theta = 0$|

### Special Values — The Short Table You Should Know Cold

|$\theta$|$0$|$\pi/6$|$\pi/4$|$\pi/3$|$\pi/2$|$\pi$|$3\pi/2$|$2\pi$|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|

**Memory trick for $\sin$:** Values go $0, \tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}, \tfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}, 1$ — the numerators are $\sqrt{0}, \sqrt{1}, \sqrt{2}, \sqrt{3}, \sqrt{4}$ divided by 2.

## 3. Domain, Codomain, and Periodicity

$$\sin, \cos : \mathbb{R} \to [-1, 1]$$ $$\tan : \mathbb{R} \setminus {\pi/2 + n\pi \mid n \in \mathbb{Z}} \to \mathbb{R}$$

**Theorem (Periodicity).** $$\sin(\theta + 2\pi) = \sin\theta, \qquad \cos(\theta + 2\pi) = \cos\theta \quad \forall \theta \in \mathbb{R}.$$ $$\tan(\theta + \pi) = \tan\theta \quad \forall \theta \text{ in the domain.}$$

_Proof sketch:_ Adding $2\pi$ to the arc parameter returns $P(\theta)$ to the same point on $S^1$. $\square$

**Parity:**

- $\sin$ is **odd**: $\sin(-\theta) = -\sin\theta$
- $\cos$ is **even**: $\cos(-\theta) = \cos\theta$
- $\tan$ is **odd**: $\tan(-\theta) = -\tan\theta$

_These follow immediately from the unit circle picture: reflecting $(x,y)$ across the $x$-axis negates $y$ but preserves $x$._

## 4. Fundamental Identities — A Derived, Not Memorized, Framework

### 4.1 Pythagorean Identity

Since $P(\theta) = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)$ lies on $x^2 + y^2 = 1$:

$$\boxed{\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1}$$

Dividing by $\cos^2\theta$: $\quad 1 + \tan^2\theta = \sec^2\theta$

Dividing by $\sin^2\theta$: $\quad \cot^2\theta + 1 = \csc^2\theta$

### 4.2 Angle Addition Formulas

$$\sin(\alpha + \beta) = \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta$$ $$\cos(\alpha + \beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta$$

**Derivation (via rotation matrices, MIT 18.02 style).** A rotation by angle $\theta$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is the linear map $$R_\theta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}.$$ Composing rotations: $R_\alpha R_\beta = R_{\alpha+\beta}$. Expanding the matrix product: $$R_\alpha R_\beta = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta & -(\cos\alpha\sin\beta + \sin\alpha\cos\beta) \ \sin\alpha\cos\beta + \cos\alpha\sin\beta & \cos\alpha\cos\beta - \sin\alpha\sin\beta \end{pmatrix}.$$ Reading off $(1,1)$ and $(2,1)$ entries gives the addition formulas. $\square$

> This derivation is more satisfying than the geometric one — it shows that trig identities are really statements about _linear algebra_.

### 4.3 Double-Angle Formulas

Set $\beta = \alpha$: $$\sin(2\theta) = 2\sin\theta\cos\theta$$ $$\cos(2\theta) = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta = 2\cos^2\theta - 1$$

### 4.4 Half-Angle / Power-Reduction Formulas

From $\cos(2\theta) = 1 - 2\sin^2\theta$: $$\sin^2\theta = \frac{1 - \cos(2\theta)}{2}, \qquad \cos^2\theta = \frac{1 + \cos(2\theta)}{2}$$

> **Why this matters:** These are the engine of trigonometric integration. Any power $\sin^n\theta$ or $\cos^n\theta$ can be reduced to lower powers using these repeatedly.

### 4.5 Product-to-Sum Formulas

$$\sin\alpha\cos\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\sin(\alpha+\beta) + \sin(\alpha-\beta)]$$ $$\cos\alpha\cos\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(\alpha-\beta) + \cos(\alpha+\beta)]$$ $$\sin\alpha\sin\beta = \tfrac{1}{2}[\cos(\alpha-\beta) - \cos(\alpha+\beta)]$$

## 5. Graphs and Geometric Intuition

### ASCII Visualization of $\sin$ and $\cos$

```
  y
  1 |     *         *         *
    |   *   *     *   *     *   *
    |  *     *   *     *   *     *
  0 |*---------*---------*---------→ x (θ)
    |           *     *           
 -1 |             * *              
    0   π/2   π   3π/2   2π

  ---- sin(θ) — starts at 0, peaks at π/2
  ···· cos(θ) — starts at 1, equals sin(θ + π/2)
```

### Key Geometric Observations

**1. Phase Relationship:** $$\cos\theta = \sin!\left(\theta + \frac{\pi}{2}\right)$$ Cosine is sine _shifted left_ by $\pi/2$. They are the same wave, just out of phase.

**2. Amplitude, Period, Phase Shift.** For the general sinusoid: $$f(\theta) = A\sin(B\theta + C) + D$$

- **Amplitude:** $|A|$ — vertical stretch
- **Period:** $2\pi / |B|$ — horizontal stretch
- **Phase shift:** $-C/B$ — horizontal translation
- **Vertical shift:** $D$

**Example.** $f(\theta) = 3\sin(2\theta - \pi/4) + 1$:

- Amplitude $= 3$, Period $= \pi$, Phase shift $= \pi/8$ (right), Vertical shift $= 1$.

**3. Tangent Graph.** $\tan\theta$ has vertical asymptotes at $\theta = \pi/2 + n\pi$ and period $\pi$. Between consecutive asymptotes, $\tan$ is strictly increasing from $-\infty$ to $+\infty$.

## 6. Inverse Trigonometric Functions

To invert $\sin, \cos, \tan$, we must **restrict domains** to make them injective.

|Function|Restricted Domain|Range|Notation|
|---|---|---|---|
|$\arcsin$|$[-\pi/2, \pi/2]$|$[-1, 1]$|$\sin^{-1}$|
|$\arccos$|$[0, \pi]$|$[-1, 1]$|$\cos^{-1}$|
|$\arctan$|$(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$|$\mathbb{R}$|$\tan^{-1}$|

**Careful!** $\sin^{-1}$ notation is ambiguous. In this note I'll write $\arcsin$.

**Key identity:** $$\arcsin(x) + \arccos(x) = \frac{\pi}{2} \quad \forall x \in [-1, 1].$$ _Proof:_ If $\arcsin x = \theta$, then $\sin\theta = x$, so $\cos(\pi/2 - \theta) = x$, so $\arccos x = \pi/2 - \theta$. $\square$

**Useful limit:** $$\lim_{x \to \pm\infty} \arctan(x) = \pm\frac{\pi}{2}.$$ $\arctan$ has **horizontal asymptotes** at $y = \pm\pi/2$ — important in ML (sigmoid is related to $\arctan$).

## 7. Limits Involving Trig Functions

### The Fundamental Limit

$$\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1$$

**Rigorous proof (Squeeze Theorem).**

For $0 < \theta < \pi/2$, compare areas:

- Area of triangle $OAP$ (inner) $= \frac{1}{2}\sin\theta$
- Area of circular sector $OAP$ $= \frac{1}{2}\theta$
- Area of triangle $OAT$ (outer) $= \frac{1}{2}\tan\theta$

So: $\quad\frac{1}{2}\sin\theta \leq \frac{1}{2}\theta \leq \frac{1}{2}\tan\theta$

Dividing by $\frac{1}{2}\sin\theta > 0$: $$1 \leq \frac{\theta}{\sin\theta} \leq \frac{1}{\cos\theta}$$

Taking reciprocals (reversing inequalities): $$\cos\theta \leq \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} \leq 1$$

Since $\cos\theta \to 1$ as $\theta \to 0$, the Squeeze Theorem gives $\displaystyle\lim_{\theta\to 0}\frac{\sin\theta}{\theta} = 1$. $\square$

The case $\theta \to 0^-$ follows by the odd symmetry of $\sin\theta / \theta$.

### Corollary

$$\lim_{\theta \to 0} \frac{1 - \cos\theta}{\theta} = 0$$

_Proof:_ $$\frac{1-\cos\theta}{\theta} = \frac{(1-\cos\theta)(1+\cos\theta)}{\theta(1+\cos\theta)} = \frac{\sin^2\theta}{\theta(1+\cos\theta)} = \frac{\sin\theta}{\theta}\cdot\frac{\sin\theta}{1+\cos\theta} \to 1 \cdot 0 = 0. \quad\square$$

## 8. Derivatives

### 8.1 The Sine Derivative — Full Derivation

$$\frac{d}{d\theta}\sin\theta = \cos\theta$$

**Proof.** $$\frac{d}{d\theta}\sin\theta = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sin(\theta+h)-\sin\theta}{h}$$ Apply the angle addition formula: $$= \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\sin\theta\cos h + \cos\theta\sin h - \sin\theta}{h}$$ $$= \lim_{h\to 0}\left[\sin\theta\cdot\frac{\cos h - 1}{h} + \cos\theta\cdot\frac{\sin h}{h}\right]$$ $$= \sin\theta\cdot 0 + \cos\theta\cdot 1 = \cos\theta. \quad\square$$

### 8.2 Complete Derivative Table

|$f(\theta)$|$f'(\theta)$|Mnemonic|
|---|---|---|
|$\sin\theta$|$\cos\theta$||
|$\cos\theta$|$-\sin\theta$|Sign flips|
|$\tan\theta$|$\sec^2\theta$||
|$\cot\theta$|$-\csc^2\theta$||
|$\sec\theta$|$\sec\theta\tan\theta$||
|$\csc\theta$|$-\csc\theta\cot\theta$||
|$\arcsin x$|$1/\sqrt{1-x^2}$||
|$\arccos x$|$-1/\sqrt{1-x^2}$||
|$\arctan x$|$1/(1+x^2)$||

**Derivation of $\arctan'$:** Let $y = \arctan x$, so $\tan y = x$. Differentiating implicitly: $$\sec^2 y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 1 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{1}{\sec^2 y} = \frac{1}{1 + \tan^2 y} = \frac{1}{1 + x^2}. \quad\square$$

### 8.3 Chain Rule Examples

$$\frac{d}{dx}\sin(x^2) = \cos(x^2)\cdot 2x$$ $$\frac{d}{dx}\arctan(e^x) = \frac{e^x}{1 + e^{2x}}$$

> **Note:** The sigmoid function $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$ satisfies $\sigma'(x) = \sigma(x)(1-\sigma(x))$. This is related to $\arctan$ via $\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2}\tanh(x/2)$. More on this in §12.

## 9. Integrals

### 9.1 Basic Antiderivatives

$$\int \sin\theta, d\theta = -\cos\theta + C, \qquad \int \cos\theta, d\theta = \sin\theta + C$$ $$\int \tan\theta, d\theta = -\ln|\cos\theta| + C = \ln|\sec\theta| + C$$ $$\int \sec^2\theta, d\theta = \tan\theta + C, \qquad \int \csc^2\theta, d\theta = -\cot\theta + C$$

**Derivation of $\int \tan\theta, d\theta$:** $$\int\frac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}, d\theta = -\int\frac{-\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}, d\theta = -\ln|\cos\theta| + C.$$ (Substitution: $u = \cos\theta$, $du = -\sin\theta, d\theta$.) $\square$

### 9.2 Powers of Sine and Cosine

**Strategy for $\int \sin^n\theta, d\theta$:**

_Case 1: $n$ odd._ Factor off $\sin\theta$, convert $\sin^{n-1}\theta = (1-\cos^2\theta)^{(n-1)/2}$, substitute $u = \cos\theta$.

_Example:_ $$\int\sin^3\theta, d\theta = \int\sin^2\theta\cdot\sin\theta, d\theta = \int(1-\cos^2\theta)\sin\theta, d\theta$$ Let $u = \cos\theta$: $$= -\int(1-u^2), du = -u + \frac{u^3}{3} + C = -\cos\theta + \frac{\cos^3\theta}{3} + C.$$

_Case 2: $n$ even._ Use power-reduction: $\sin^2\theta = \frac{1-\cos(2\theta)}{2}$.

_Example:_ $$\int\sin^2\theta, d\theta = \int\frac{1-\cos(2\theta)}{2}, d\theta = \frac{\theta}{2} - \frac{\sin(2\theta)}{4} + C.$$

### 9.3 Important Integral

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin(m\theta)\sin(n\theta), d\theta = \begin{cases} 0 & m \neq n \ \pi & m = n \end{cases}$$

This is the **orthogonality of the Fourier basis** — foundational for Fourier analysis, signal processing, and attention mechanisms in transformers. See §12.

## 10. Taylor / Maclaurin Series

Using the Maclaurin series (Taylor series centered at 0):

$$\sin x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} = x - \frac{x^3}{6} + \frac{x^5}{120} - \cdots$$

$$\cos x = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!} = 1 - \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^4}{24} - \cdots$$

Both series converge for **all** $x \in \mathbb{R}$ (radius of convergence $= \infty$, by the ratio test).

**Immediate consequences:**

- $\displaystyle\lim_{x\to 0}\frac{\sin x}{x} = \lim_{x\to 0}\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{6} + \cdots\right) = 1$ ✓
- For small $x$: $\sin x \approx x$ (first-order approximation), $\cos x \approx 1 - x^2/2$ (second-order).

> **ML relevance:** The small-angle approximation $\sin\theta \approx \theta$ shows up in linearization of neural ODE flows and in the analysis of gradient descent near fixed points.

## 11. Complex Exponential — The Unifying Perspective

### Euler's Formula

$$\boxed{e^{i\theta} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta}$$

**Proof via Taylor series.** Expand $e^{i\theta}$: $$e^{i\theta} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(i\theta)^n}{n!} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(i\theta)^{2k}}{(2k)!} + \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(i\theta)^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!}.$$ Using $i^{2k} = (-1)^k$ and $i^{2k+1} = i(-1)^k$: $$= \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k\theta^{2k}}{(2k)!} + i\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k\theta^{2k+1}}{(2k+1)!} = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta. \quad\square$$

### Consequences

$$\cos\theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} + e^{-i\theta}}{2}, \qquad \sin\theta = \frac{e^{i\theta} - e^{-i\theta}}{2i}$$

These are the **inverse Euler formulas**. They make all trig identities trivial — they reduce to algebra with exponentials.

**Example.** Prove $\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = \cos(2\theta)$: $$\cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = \left(\frac{e^{i\theta}+e^{-i\theta}}{2}\right)^2 - \left(\frac{e^{i\theta}-e^{-i\theta}}{2i}\right)^2$$ $$= \frac{e^{2i\theta}+2+e^{-2i\theta}}{4} + \frac{e^{2i\theta}-2+e^{-2i\theta}}{4} = \frac{e^{2i\theta}+e^{-2i\theta}}{2} = \cos(2\theta). \quad\square$$

### Euler's Identity

Setting $\theta = \pi$: $$e^{i\pi} + 1 = 0$$ Five of the most fundamental constants in mathematics in one equation.

## 12. Connections to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

This section is the payoff. Trigonometric functions appear _everywhere_ in modern ML.

---

### 12.1 Activation Functions: Sigmoid and Tanh

The **sigmoid** function: $$\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{1+e^{-x}}$$

The **hyperbolic tangent**: $$\tanh(x) = \frac{e^x - e^{-x}}{e^x + e^{-x}}$$

These are directly related to trig via **hyperbolic functions** (replace $i\theta$ with real $\theta$ in Euler's formula): $$\cos(ix) = \cosh(x), \qquad \sin(ix) = i\sinh(x).$$

The connection to sigmoid: $$\sigma(x) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \tanh\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)\right).$$

**Properties inherited from trig:**

- $\tanh$ is odd: $\tanh(-x) = -\tanh(x)$, just like $\tan$ and $\sin$
- $\tanh'(x) = 1 - \tanh^2(x)$ — analogous to $\sec^2 = 1 + \tan^2$
- Both saturate (output bounded), causing the **vanishing gradient problem**

> **Insight:** The reason tanh saturates is the same reason $\sin$ flattens near $\pm\pi/2$: these are transcendental functions with bounded range. In the early 2010s this motivated the switch to ReLU.

---

### 12.2 Fourier Analysis and Spectral Methods

The **Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)** expresses any signal ${x_0, \ldots, x_{N-1}}$ as: $$X_k = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n, e^{-2\pi i kn/N} = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} x_n \left[\cos!\left(\frac{2\pi kn}{N}\right) - i\sin!\left(\frac{2\pi kn}{N}\right)\right].$$

This is a change of basis from the **standard basis** to the **Fourier basis** ${e^{2\pi ikn/N}}_{k=0}^{N-1}$, which is an orthonormal basis by the integral orthogonality of §9.3.

**ML Applications:**

- **Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs):** Convolution in the time/spatial domain = pointwise multiplication in the Fourier domain. CNNs implicitly learn frequency filters.
- **Spectral Graph Neural Networks:** Graph convolutions are defined via the graph Laplacian's eigenvectors, which generalize Fourier modes to non-Euclidean domains.
- **FNO (Fourier Neural Operator):** Solves PDEs by learning in Fourier space — directly uses $\sin$ and $\cos$ as basis functions.

---

### 12.3 Positional Encoding in Transformers

This is arguably the most striking direct appearance of trig in modern deep learning.

The **Transformer architecture** (Vaswani et al., 2017) needs to encode the _position_ of each token in a sequence. The positional encoding for position $pos$ and dimension $i$ is:

$$\text{PE}(pos, 2i) = \sin!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)$$ $$\text{PE}(pos, 2i+1) = \cos!\left(\frac{pos}{10000^{2i/d_{\text{model}}}}\right)$$

**Why sinusoids?**

1. **Uniqueness:** Each position gets a unique encoding (sinusoids at different frequencies create a unique "fingerprint").
2. **Relative position:** For any fixed offset $k$, the encoding of position $pos + k$ is a _linear transformation_ of the encoding of position $pos$. This follows from the angle addition formula: $$\sin(pos + k)\omega = \sin(pos\cdot\omega)\cos(k\omega) + \cos(pos\cdot\omega)\sin(k\omega).$$ The model can learn to attend to relative positions using a fixed linear map — this is only possible because of the addition formula.
3. **Generalization:** Sinusoids extrapolate — the model sees longer sequences than it was trained on.

> **Personal note:** The fact that the attention mechanism can represent relative positions because of a 2000-year-old identity ($\sin(\alpha+\beta)$ addition formula) is one of the most beautiful connections between classical math and modern AI I know of.

---

### 12.4 Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE)

Used in LLaMA, GPT-NeoX, and many modern LLMs. The key idea: encode position by _rotating_ query and key vectors in embedding space.

For a 2D subspace, position $m$ applies the rotation matrix: $$R_m = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(m\theta) & -\sin(m\theta) \ \sin(m\theta) & \cos(m\theta) \end{pmatrix}$$

The dot product of query at position $m$ and key at position $n$ depends only on $m - n$: $$\langle R_m \mathbf{q},, R_n \mathbf{k}\rangle = \langle R_{m-n}\mathbf{q},, \mathbf{k}\rangle.$$

This is _exactly_ the rotation-matrix composition property from MIT 18.02: $R_m^\top R_n = R_{n-m}$. Positional information is encoded in the angle between vectors in the attention computation.

---

### 12.5 Random Fourier Features (Kernel Methods)

The **RBF (Gaussian) kernel** can be approximated using random sinusoids (Rahimi & Recht, 2007): $$k(x, y) = e^{-|x-y|^2/2\sigma^2} \approx \frac{1}{D}\sum_{j=1}^{D} \cos(\omega_j^\top x + b_j)\cos(\omega_j^\top y + b_j)$$ where $\omega_j \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I/\sigma^2)$ and $b_j \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 2\pi)$.

This is Bochner's theorem: every positive definite kernel is the Fourier transform of a non-negative measure. Trig functions are the bridge between kernel methods and explicit feature maps.

---

### 12.6 Periodic Activations — SIREN Networks

**SIREN** (Sinusoidal Representation Networks, Sitzmann et al., 2020) use $\sin$ as the activation function: $$\phi_i(\mathbf{x}) = \sin(W_i\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_i)$$

**Why?**

- $\frac{d}{dx}\sin(\omega x) = \omega\cos(\omega x)$ — derivatives are also sinusoids. A SIREN's gradient network is itself a SIREN.
- Periodic activations represent high-frequency signals (images, audio, physical simulations) far more compactly than ReLU networks.
- The Taylor series of $\sin$ has _all_ odd-degree terms — SIREN naturally represents odd/symmetric functions.

The initialization scheme sets weights so that $W_i\mathbf{x} \sim \text{Uniform}(-\pi, \pi)$, ensuring the sine inputs span a full period.

---

### 12.7 Signal Processing in Audio/Vision Deep Learning

CNNs for audio (WaveNet, Wav2Vec) implicitly perform short-time Fourier transforms. Mel-spectrograms — the standard audio feature — are: $$S(m, t) = \left|\sum_n x(n), e^{-2\pi i, mn/N}\right|^2 \text{ (filtered through mel filterbank)}$$

Every spectrogram you feed to a speech recognition model is a visualization of trig functions applied to the signal.
