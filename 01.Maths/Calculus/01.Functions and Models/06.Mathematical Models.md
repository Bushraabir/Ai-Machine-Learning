## What Is a Mathematical Model?

A **mathematical model** is a description of a system using mathematical concepts and language — equations, functions, inequalities, probability distributions, differential equations, matrices. The act of modelling is the deliberate choice to _abstract away_ physical complexity and retain only what matters for the question at hand.

**Formally:** Given a real-world system $S$, a mathematical model $\mathcal{M}$ is a tuple:

$$\mathcal{M} = (\mathcal{X},\ \mathcal{Y},\ f,\ \Theta)$$

where:

- $\mathcal{X}$ — the **input space** (independent variables, features, causes)
- $\mathcal{Y}$ — the **output space** (dependent variables, responses, targets)
- $f: \mathcal{X} \times \Theta \to \mathcal{Y}$ — the **model map** (the equation relating inputs to outputs, parameterised)
- $\Theta$ — the **parameter space** (constants to be determined or fitted)

The _art_ of modelling is choosing which variables to include and which to discard.

---

## A Taxonomy of Mathematical Models

```
Mathematical Models
│
├── By Time Dependence
│   ├── Static (no time variable)
│   └── Dynamic (time-varying; ODEs, PDEs)
│
├── By Randomness
│   ├── Deterministic (same input → same output always)
│   └── Stochastic (incorporates probability; same input → distribution of outputs)
│
├── By Linearity
│   ├── Linear (superposition holds)
│   └── Nonlinear (superposition fails; much richer behaviour)
│
└── By How They Are Built
    ├── Mechanistic / First-Principles (derived from physics, chemistry, biology)
    └── Empirical / Data-Driven (fitted to observed data)
```

> **Note to self:** Machine learning models almost always live in the _empirical, nonlinear, stochastic_ corner. Classical physics models are _mechanistic, deterministic, often linear_ (or linearised).

---

## The Modelling Cycle

$$\text{Reality} \xrightarrow{\text{abstraction}} \text{Model} \xrightarrow{\text{analysis}} \text{Predictions} \xrightarrow{\text{validation}} \text{Reality}$$

1. **Formulation** — Identify variables, assumptions, governing relationships.
2. **Mathematical Analysis** — Solve, simulate, or approximate $f$.
3. **Prediction** — Use $f(\mathbf{x}; \theta)$ to produce outputs for new inputs.
4. **Validation** — Compare predictions against data. Refine if necessary.
5. **Interpretation** — Extract meaning, not just numbers.

## Canonical Examples

### 1. Linear Model (Static, Deterministic)

$$y = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b, \quad \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n,\ y \in \mathbb{R}$$

The simplest non-trivial model. $\mathbf{w}$ are weights, $b$ is a bias. Every linear regression, every hyperplane classifier, every filter in early signal processing is secretly this.

**Example — Hooke's Law:** $F = kx$. Force is a linear model of displacement. One parameter $k$.

**Example — Temperature conversion:** $F = \frac{9}{5}C + 32$. Exact, no randomness, first-principles derivation trivial.

---

### 2. Exponential Growth / Decay (Dynamic, Deterministic)

$$\frac{dy}{dt} = ky, \quad y(0) = y_0$$

Solution: $y(t) = y_0 e^{kt}$

- $k > 0$: growth (population, compound interest, early epidemic spread)
- $k < 0$: decay (radioactive decay, drug clearance)

**Example — Carbon-14 Dating:** Half-life $t_{1/2} = 5730$ years. If $y(t) = y_0 e^{kt}$ and $y(t_{1/2}) = y_0/2$:

$$e^{k \cdot 5730} = \frac{1}{2} \implies k = \frac{-\ln 2}{5730} \approx -1.21 \times 10^{-4}\ \text{yr}^{-1}$$

Given a sample with 75% of original C-14 remaining: $$0.75, y_0 = y_0, e^{kt} \implies t = \frac{\ln(0.75)}{k} \approx 2378\ \text{years}$$

---

### 3. Logistic Growth (Dynamic, Nonlinear)

$$\frac{dP}{dt} = rP!\left(1 - \frac{P}{K}\right)$$

Corrects exponential growth with a **carrying capacity** $K$. The term $(1 - P/K)$ is the nonlinearity — it suppresses growth as $P \to K$.

**Exact solution:** $$P(t) = \frac{K}{1 + \left(\frac{K - P_0}{P_0}\right)e^{-rt}}$$

**Visualisation concept:**

```
P
K ──────────────────────────────── (asymptote)
│                    ___─────────
│              ___─── 
│         ___──
│    ___──
│___──
└────────────────────────────── t
     inflection at P = K/2
```

This exact sigmoid curve is _foundational_ to machine learning. See §[[#Connection to AI and Machine Learning]] below.

---

### 4. Linear Systems (Static, Deterministic)

$$A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}, \quad A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$$

Encodes $m$ linear constraints on $n$ unknowns. The entire machinery of linear algebra — eigenvalues, SVD, pseudoinverse — is about understanding these systems.

**Example — Traffic Flow:** At each junction, flow in = flow out. Each junction gives one linear equation. The system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ models the entire network.

---

### 5. Probabilistic / Statistical Models (Stochastic)

$$y = f(\mathbf{x}; \theta) + \varepsilon, \quad \varepsilon \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

The additive noise $\varepsilon$ acknowledges that no deterministic model is exact. This is _not_ a weakness — it is epistemically honest. From this single assumption, ordinary least squares, maximum likelihood estimation, and Bayesian inference all follow naturally.

## Dimensionless Analysis and Scaling

One of the most powerful (and criminally underused) techniques. By non-dimensionalising a model, we:

1. Reduce the number of free parameters.
2. Identify which terms dominate.
3. Make the model universal.

**Example — Simple Pendulum:** $$\frac{d^2\theta}{dt^2} + \frac{g}{L}\sin\theta = 0$$

Let $\tau = t\sqrt{g/L}$ (dimensionless time). Then: $$\frac{d^2\theta}{d\tau^2} + \sin\theta = 0$$

All pendulums, regardless of length or gravitational field, obey the _same_ equation in dimensionless time. One equation rules them all.

For small $\theta$: $\sin\theta \approx \theta$, so $\theta(\tau) = A\cos(\tau + \phi)$ — simple harmonic motion.

## Connection to AI and Machine Learning

> This is where classical modelling and modern ML fuse in ways that are deeply elegant.

### Neural Networks as Parameterised Function Approximators

A deep neural network is a mathematical model: $$f(\mathbf{x}; \theta) = (L_k \circ \sigma \circ L_{k-1} \circ \cdots \circ \sigma \circ L_1)(\mathbf{x})$$

where $L_i(\mathbf{z}) = W_i\mathbf{z} + \mathbf{b}_i$ are affine maps and $\sigma$ is a nonlinear activation. This is just a highly nested, highly parameterised function in $\Theta = {W_i, \mathbf{b}_i}$.

**The Universal Approximation Theorem** (Cybenko 1989, Hornik 1991) guarantees that a sufficiently wide one-hidden-layer network can approximate any continuous function on a compact set to arbitrary precision. Deep networks extend this with exponentially more efficient representations.

---

### The Sigmoid: From Logistic ODE to Activation Function

The solution to the logistic ODE above is the **logistic function**:

$$\sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}} = \frac{K}{1 + \left(\frac{K-P_0}{P_0}\right)e^{-rt}}\Bigg|_{K=1,\ r=1,\ P_0 = \frac{1}{1+e^{z_0}}}$$

In logistic regression and early neural networks, this is used as the activation function. It maps $\mathbb{R} \to (0,1)$, perfect for probabilities.

The **ReLU** $\sigma(z) = \max(0,z)$ dominates modern practice because it avoids vanishing gradients — but it is a piecewise linear model, just as the logistic is a smooth nonlinear one.

---

### Linear Regression: A First-Principles Model Fitted to Data

$$\hat{y} = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x} + b$$

Fitting this model = finding $\theta = (\mathbf{w}, b)$ that minimises the loss:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \left(y_i - \hat{y}_i\right)^2$$

This has a closed-form solution (the **Normal Equations**): $$\hat{\theta} = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top \mathbf{y}$$

But for large-scale ML, we use **gradient descent** instead: $$\theta \leftarrow \theta - \eta, \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}$$

where $\eta$ is the learning rate. All of deep learning optimisation is gradient descent on a very complicated $\mathcal{L}$.

---

### Differential Equations in Deep Learning: Neural ODEs

A modern and beautiful idea (Chen et al., NeurIPS 2018): model the hidden state of a network as the solution of an ODE:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{h}(t)}{dt} = f(\mathbf{h}(t),\ t;\ \theta)$$

where $f$ is a neural network. A residual network $\mathbf{h}_{t+1} = \mathbf{h}_t + f(\mathbf{h}_t)$ is the **Euler discretisation** of this ODE with step size 1. This connection is not metaphorical — it is exact. Neural ODEs allow continuous-depth models and are elegant tools in time-series and physical simulation.

---

### Inductive Bias: The Hidden Modelling Choice

Every ML model encodes prior beliefs about $f$ through its architecture. This is called **inductive bias**:

|Architecture|Inductive Bias|
|---|---|
|Linear model|$f$ is affine|
|CNN|$f$ has local spatial correlations; translation equivariance|
|RNN / LSTM|$f$ depends on sequential history|
|Transformer|$f$ depends on global pairwise relationships|
|GNN|$f$ respects graph symmetries|

Choosing the right architecture _is_ the modelling step in deep learning. It is the same intellectual act as choosing whether to model population dynamics with an exponential or a logistic — you are encoding your beliefs about the structure of reality.

## Worked Example: Epidemic Modelling (SIR Model)

A beautiful mechanistic model that became globally prominent in 2020.

**Variables:**

- $S(t)$ — Susceptible fraction of population
- $I(t)$ — Infected fraction
- $R(t)$ — Recovered (immune) fraction

**Constraint:** $S + I + R = 1$ (closed population)

**Governing ODEs:** $$\frac{dS}{dt} = -\beta S I$$ $$\frac{dI}{dt} = \beta S I - \gamma I$$ $$\frac{dR}{dt} = \gamma I$$

**Parameters:**

- $\beta$ — transmission rate
- $\gamma$ — recovery rate
- $\mathcal{R}_0 = \beta/\gamma$ — **basic reproduction number**

**Key insight:** The epidemic grows iff $dI/dt > 0$, i.e. $\beta S > \gamma$, i.e. $S > \gamma/\beta = 1/\mathcal{R}_0$.

Herd immunity is achieved when $S < 1/\mathcal{R}_0$. For measles $\mathcal{R}_0 \approx 15$, so $\sim 93%$ immunity needed.

**Qualitative phase portrait concept:**

```
I
│    *
│   * *
│  *   *
│ *      *
│*         ──────────────
└────────────────────────── S
  S decreases monotonically →
```

The system has no closed-form solution in general — it is studied via phase plane analysis, numerical integration (Runge-Kutta), and perturbation theory for small $I_0$.

**AI connection:** This ODE system can be embedded into a neural ODE, where $\beta$ and $\gamma$ are _learnt from data_ rather than estimated epidemiologically. The model structure (the equations) is the mechanistic prior; the network fits the parameters. This hybrid approach is called **physics-informed machine learning**.

## Error, Uncertainty, and Model Validation

A model without a notion of its own error is dangerous.

**Residuals:** $e_i = y_i - f(x_i; \hat{\theta})$. If the model is correct, residuals should look like noise.

**Coefficient of determination:** $$R^2 = 1 - \frac{\sum_i e_i^2}{\sum_i (y_i - \bar{y})^2} \in (-\infty, 1]$$

$R^2 = 1$ means perfect fit. $R^2 = 0$ means the model explains nothing beyond the mean. $R^2 < 0$ means the model is worse than a constant.

**Overfitting:** A model with too many parameters fits the training data perfectly but fails on new data. The polynomial of degree $n-1$ through $n$ points has $R^2 = 1$ but is useless.

**Bias-variance tradeoff:** $$\mathbb{E}[(y - \hat{f})^2] = \underbrace{\text{Bias}[\hat{f}]^2}_{\text{underfitting}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}[\hat{f}]}_{\text{overfitting}} + \underbrace{\sigma^2}_{\text{irreducible noise}}$$

This is perhaps the most important equation in all of statistical learning theory. Simple models have high bias, low variance. Complex models have low bias, high variance. The art — and the active research — is in finding the right balance.

## Summary: What Makes a Good Model?

> _"All models are wrong, but some are useful."_ — George Box

|Criterion|What It Means|
|---|---|
|**Parsimony**|Uses the fewest parameters necessary (Occam's Razor)|
|**Predictive accuracy**|Generalises to unseen data|
|**Interpretability**|Parameters have physical/statistical meaning|
|**Tractability**|Can be analysed or solved (exactly or approximately)|
|**Robustness**|Small perturbations in data → small changes in output|

No model satisfies all five simultaneously. Every modelling choice is a trade-off. This is the intellectual content of the discipline.
