## 0. Orientation

A **power function** is the simplest non-trivial function: one variable, one exponent, one parameter. Yet the behavior changes _radically_ as we vary the exponent — integer, rational, real, even complex. Understanding this single family of functions is the foundation for:

- Polynomials (finite sums of power functions)
- Taylor series (infinite sums)
- Scaling laws in physics and ML
- Activation functions and loss landscapes in deep learning

Do not rush this. The function $f(x) = x^r$ is deceptively rich.

---

## 1. Definition

> **Definition.** A _power function_ is any function of the form $$f(x) = x^r$$ where $r \in \mathbb{R}$ is a fixed constant called the **exponent** (or **power**), and $x$ is the variable.

**Contrast with exponential functions:** In $f(x) = x^r$, the _base_ is the variable and the _exponent_ is fixed. In $g(x) = a^x$, the _exponent_ is the variable. This distinction is fundamental and constantly confused.

|Function type|Form|Base|Exponent|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Power**|$x^r$|variable $x$|fixed $r$|
|**Exponential**|$a^x$|fixed $a$|variable $x$|
|**Monomial**|$cx^r$|variable $x$|fixed $r$|

## 2. Domain Considerations

The domain of $f(x) = x^r$ depends critically on $r$.

### 2.1 Case Table

|Exponent $r$|Domain of $x^r$|Notes|
|---|---|---|
|$r \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ (positive integer)|$\mathbb{R}$|Always defined|
|$r = 0$|$\mathbb{R} \setminus {0}$|$x^0 = 1$ except $0^0$ is indeterminate|
|$r \in \mathbb{Z}^-$ (negative integer)|$\mathbb{R} \setminus {0}$|$x^{-n} = 1/x^n$, undefined at 0|
|$r = p/q$, $q$ odd (rational, reduced)|$\mathbb{R}$|$x^{1/3} = \sqrt[3]{x}$ for all $x$|
|$r = p/q$, $q$ even (rational, reduced)|$[0, \infty)$|Even root requires $x \geq 0$|
|$r \in \mathbb{R} \setminus \mathbb{Q}$ (irrational)|$(0, \infty)$|Defined via $x^r = e^{r \ln x}$|

> **Remark.** For irrational $r$ (e.g., $x^\pi$, $x^\sqrt{2}$), we _define_ $x^r := e^{r \ln x}$ for $x > 0$. This is not a convenience — it is the only rigorous definition available. The expression $(-1)^{\sqrt{2}}$ is genuinely undefined over $\mathbb{R}$.

## 3. The Exponent Zoo — A Taxonomy

### 3.1 Positive Integer Powers ($r = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$)

These are the monomials: $x, x^2, x^3, x^4, \ldots$

**Parity determines symmetry:**

- $r$ even: $f(-x) = f(x)$ — **even function**, symmetric about the $y$-axis.
- $r$ odd: $f(-x) = -f(x)$ — **odd function**, symmetric about the origin.

**Growth:** $x^n \to +\infty$ as $x \to +\infty$ for all $n \geq 1$.

**Comparison of growth rates:** $$x \ll x^2 \ll x^3 \ll \cdots \text{ as } x \to \infty$$

More precisely: $\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^m}{x^n} = 0$ whenever $m < n$. Higher powers _dominate_.

```
        x⁴
        |       /
        |      /  x³
        |     /  /
        |    / /  x²
        |   //  /
        |  // /  x
        | ///
        |///
        +─────────────── x
        0   1   2
```

---

### 3.2 Negative Integer Powers ($r = -1, -2, \ldots$)

$$f(x) = x^{-n} = \frac{1}{x^n}, \qquad x \neq 0$$

**Key case: $r = -1$, the hyperbola.**

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{x}$$

- Domain: $\mathbb{R} \setminus {0}$.
- Vertical asymptote at $x = 0$.
- Horizontal asymptote $y = 0$ as $x \to \pm\infty$.
- Odd function: $f(-x) = -f(x)$.
- Defines two branches: one in QI, one in QIII.

**General $r = -n$:**

- Even $n$: both branches positive (even function).
- Odd $n$: branches in QI and QIII (odd function).

```
  f(x) = 1/x          f(x) = 1/x²

      |  /                  |
   \  | /                \  |  /
    \ |/                  \ | /
─────+──────── x        ───+──────── x
    /|\                    \|/
   / | \                    +
  /  |  \
```

---

### 3.3 Rational Powers — Roots ($r = 1/n$)

$$f(x) = x^{1/n} = \sqrt[n]{x}$$

**$n$ odd (e.g., cube root $x^{1/3}$):**

- Domain: $\mathbb{R}$.
- Odd function.
- Always passes through $(-1,-1), (0,0), (1,1)$.
- Slope is infinite at $x = 0$ (vertical tangent).

**$n$ even (e.g., square root $x^{1/2}$):**

- Domain: $[0, \infty)$.
- Not defined for $x < 0$ (over $\mathbb{R}$).
- Concave down, increasing.

**Key shape:** Root functions grow slower than linear: $$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^{1/n}}{x} = \lim_{x\to\infty} x^{1/n - 1} = 0 \quad (n > 1)$$

```
  y = √x             y = x^(1/3)

      |  /              |   /
      | /               |  /
      |/                | /
──────+──────── x   ────+──────── x
                       /|
                      / |
                     /  |
```

---

### 3.4 General Real Powers ($r \in \mathbb{R}$, $x > 0$)

For $x > 0$, defined as $f(x) = e^{r \ln x}$.

**Behavior at the anchor points:**

- $f(0^+)$: $\to 0$ if $r > 0$; $\to +\infty$ if $r < 0$; $= 1$ if $r = 0$.
- $f(1) = 1$ for all $r$ — every power function passes through $(1, 1)$.
- $f(\infty)$: $\to \infty$ if $r > 0$; $\to 0$ if $r < 0$.

> **Observation.** The point $(1, 1)$ is the **universal fixed point** of all power functions. It is the natural "anchor" — the place where all power functions agree.

**Behavior hierarchy as $x \to \infty$:** $$\ln x \ll x^r \ll e^x \text{ for any } r > 0$$

This is a theorem, not an approximation. Exponentials eventually dominate all power functions; logarithms grow slower than all positive powers.

## 4. Calculus of Power Functions

### 4.1 The Power Rule — General Version

> **Theorem (Power Rule).** For $f(x) = x^r$ with $r \in \mathbb{R}$: $$\frac{d}{dx}\left[x^r\right] = r x^{r-1}$$ wherever $f$ is differentiable.

**Proof for $r \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ (from first principles):**

$$f'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{(x+h)^r - x^r}{h}$$

Expand $(x+h)^r$ by the Binomial Theorem: $$(x+h)^r = x^r + rx^{r-1}h + \binom{r}{2}x^{r-2}h^2 + \cdots + h^r$$

Subtract $x^r$, divide by $h$: $$\frac{(x+h)^r - x^r}{h} = rx^{r-1} + \binom{r}{2}x^{r-2}h + \cdots + h^{r-1}$$

Take $h \to 0$: all terms with a factor of $h$ vanish, leaving $rx^{r-1}$. $\square$

**Proof for $r \in \mathbb{R}$ (using logarithmic differentiation):**

Write $f(x) = e^{r \ln x}$. Then: $$f'(x) = e^{r \ln x} \cdot \frac{r}{x} = x^r \cdot \frac{r}{x} = r x^{r-1} \qquad \square$$

> **Remark.** This second proof reveals something deep: the power rule for general real exponents is really a consequence of the chain rule and the fundamental relationship between $e^x$ and $\ln x$. The power rule is not "elementary" — it secretly involves exponential functions.

### 4.2 Derivative Table

|$f(x)$|$f'(x)$|Domain of $f'$|
|---|---|---|
|$x^2$|$2x$|$\mathbb{R}$|
|$x^3$|$3x^2$|$\mathbb{R}$|
|$x^{-1} = 1/x$|$-x^{-2} = -1/x^2$|$\mathbb{R} \setminus {0}$|
|$x^{1/2} = \sqrt{x}$|$\frac{1}{2}x^{-1/2} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$|$(0, \infty)$|
|$x^{1/3}$|$\frac{1}{3}x^{-2/3}$|$\mathbb{R} \setminus {0}$|
|$x^{-1/2}$|$-\frac{1}{2}x^{-3/2}$|$(0, \infty)$|
|$x^\pi$|$\pi x^{\pi - 1}$|$(0, \infty)$|
|$x^r$ (general)|$r x^{r-1}$|depends on $r$|

### 4.3 Integration

The antiderivative follows directly from the derivative:

> **Theorem (Power Rule for Integration).** For $r \neq -1$: $$\int x^r , dx = \frac{x^{r+1}}{r+1} + C$$

**Why $r = -1$ is excluded:** $\int x^{-1} dx = \ln|x| + C$. The formula $\frac{x^0}{0}$ is undefined — the antiderivative of $1/x$ genuinely escapes the power function family and lands in the logarithm.

This is not a coincidence. The logarithm _is_ the area function of $1/x$: $$\ln(a) = \int_1^a \frac{1}{t},dt$$

This is in fact one clean way to _define_ the natural logarithm — as the unique antiderivative of $x^{-1}$ satisfying $\ln(1) = 0$.

---

### 4.4 Concavity and Shape

For $f(x) = x^r$ on $(0, \infty)$:

$$f''(x) = r(r-1)x^{r-2}$$

|Condition|Sign of $f''$|Shape|
|---|---|---|
|$r > 1$|$f'' > 0$|Convex (concave up)|
|$0 < r < 1$|$f'' < 0$|Concave (concave down)|
|$r < 0$|$f'' > 0$ for $x > 0$|Convex|
|$r = 1$|$f'' = 0$|Linear (no curvature)|

**Geometric summary:**

```
r > 1: convex           0 < r < 1: concave       r < 0: convex, decreasing

      /                   ___                      \
     /                  /                           \
    /                  /                             \___
   /                  /
  /                  /
```

## 5. Monotonicity and Inverse Functions

### 5.1 Monotonicity

For $x > 0$:

- $r > 0$: $f$ is **strictly increasing** (larger $x$ gives larger $x^r$).
- $r < 0$: $f$ is **strictly decreasing** (larger $x$ gives smaller $x^r$).
- $r = 0$: constant function $f = 1$.

**Proof.** $f'(x) = rx^{r-1}$. For $x > 0$, $x^{r-1} > 0$ always. Sign of $f'$ equals sign of $r$. $\square$

### 5.2 Inverse Functions

On the appropriate domain:

$$f(x) = x^r \implies f^{-1}(x) = x^{1/r}$$

**Proof.** $f(f^{-1}(x)) = (x^{1/r})^r = x^{r/r} = x^1 = x$. $\square$

**Example:** $f(x) = x^3$ has inverse $f^{-1}(x) = x^{1/3}$. The graphs are reflections across the line $y = x$.

## 6. Power Functions vs. Exponential — A Critical Distinction

This deserves its own section because it is the most common conceptual error.

### 6.1 Growth Rate Comparison

**Theorem.** For any $r > 0$ and any $a > 1$: $$\lim_{x \to \infty} \frac{x^r}{a^x} = 0$$

That is, every exponential function $a^x$ _eventually_ dominates every power function $x^r$, no matter how large $r$ is.

**Proof.** Apply L'Hôpital's rule $\lceil r \rceil$ times. Each application differentiates $x^r$ to reduce the exponent; differentiating $a^x$ merely multiplies by $\ln a$. After $\lceil r \rceil$ steps, the numerator is constant while the denominator is still $\sim a^x$. $\square$

**Consequence:** $$\text{polynomial} \ll \text{power (positive)} \ll \text{exponential}$$

```
                          e^x
                       /
                      /
              x^3   /
             /    /
            /  x /
           / /
          //
─────────/──────────────────── x
```

### 6.2 Near $x = 0$: Power Functions Win

For $x \in (0,1)$, the order reverses: $e^x \approx 1 + x + \ldots$ is nearly constant, while $x^r$ for $r > 1$ plunges toward 0. This is why power functions are preferred for modeling small-$x$ phenomena (percolation thresholds, phase transitions near critical points).

## 7. Scaling Laws — The Physical Content of Power Functions

> **The defining property of power functions is scale invariance.**

**Theorem.** $f(x) = x^r$ satisfies the _scaling relation_: $$f(\lambda x) = \lambda^r f(x)$$ for all $\lambda > 0$.

**Proof.** $f(\lambda x) = (\lambda x)^r = \lambda^r x^r = \lambda^r f(x)$. $\square$

This property — that scaling the input by $\lambda$ scales the output by $\lambda^r$ — is called **homogeneity of degree $r$**.

> **Converse (Stewart §1.2 flavor).** If a continuous function $f: (0,\infty) \to \mathbb{R}$ satisfies $f(\lambda x) = \lambda^r f(x)$ for all $\lambda, x > 0$, then $f(x) = f(1) \cdot x^r$. **Power functions are the only scale-invariant functions.**

**Physical examples:**

- Area of a square: $A = s^2$. Double the side ($\lambda = 2$): area quadruples ($2^2 = 4$).
- Volume of a sphere: $V = \frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$. Triple the radius: volume increases by $3^3 = 27$.
- Gravitational force: $F \propto r^{-2}$. Double the distance: force drops to $1/4$.

## 8. Log-Log Plots and Power Law Detection

### 8.1 The Key Trick

If $y = Cx^r$, take $\log$ of both sides: $$\log y = r \log x + \log C$$

Setting $Y = \log y$ and $X = \log x$: $$Y = rX + \log C$$

**This is a straight line with slope $r$ and intercept $\log C$.**

> **Consequence.** A power law appears as a straight line on a log-log plot. The slope _is_ the exponent. This is the standard empirical test for power laws.

```
  log y
    |         /  slope = r
    |        /
    |       /
    |      /
    |     /
    |    /
    |   /
    +─────────── log x
```

### 8.2 Example: Kepler's Third Law

$T^2 = k a^3$ (orbital period vs. semi-major axis). On a log-log plot: slope $= 3/2$.

|Planet|$a$ (AU)|$T$ (yr)|$\log a$|$\log T$|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|Mercury|0.387|0.241|-0.412|-0.618|
|Earth|1.000|1.000|0.000|0.000|
|Jupiter|5.203|11.86|0.716|1.074|

Slope of $\log T$ vs $\log a$: $\approx 1.5 = 3/2$. ✓

## 9. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Full Analysis of $f(x) = x^{2/3}$

**Domain:** $\mathbb{R}$ (cube root of $x^2$, defined everywhere).

**Symmetry:** $f(-x) = (-x)^{2/3} = (x^2)^{1/3} = x^{2/3} = f(x)$. Even function.

**Derivative:** $$f'(x) = \frac{2}{3}x^{-1/3} = \frac{2}{3x^{1/3}}$$

- $f'(0)$ does not exist (vertical tangent at $x = 0$).
- $f'(x) > 0$ for $x > 0$: increasing on $(0, \infty)$.
- $f'(x) < 0$ for $x < 0$: decreasing on $(-\infty, 0)$.
- $x = 0$ is a **cusp** (minimum, but not differentiable).

**Second derivative:** $$f''(x) = \frac{2}{3} \cdot \left(-\frac{1}{3}\right) x^{-4/3} = -\frac{2}{9}x^{-4/3} < 0 \text{ for } x \neq 0$$

Concave down everywhere (except at the cusp).

**Sketch:**

```
        |
       _|_
      / | \
     /  |  \
    /   |   \
───/────+────\───── x
  /     0     \
```

---

### Example 2: Power Rule for $f(x) = x^{-3/4}$

$$f'(x) = -\frac{3}{4} x^{-3/4 - 1} = -\frac{3}{4} x^{-7/4} = -\frac{3}{4x^{7/4}}$$

Domain of $f'$: $(0, \infty)$.

At $x = 16$: $f'(16) = -\frac{3}{4} \cdot 16^{-7/4} = -\frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{(16^{1/4})^7} = -\frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2^7} = -\frac{3}{512}$.

---

### Example 3: Solving $f(x) = x^r$ type equations

**Problem.** Find $x$ such that $x^{3/2} = 8$.

$$x^{3/2} = 8 \implies x = 8^{2/3} = (8^{1/3})^2 = 2^2 = 4$$

**General strategy:** To solve $x^r = a$, raise both sides to the power $1/r$: $x = a^{1/r}$.

---

### Example 4: Comparing Growth Rates

**Show that $x^{100}$ grows slower than $e^x$ as $x \to \infty$.**

Apply L'Hôpital 100 times to $\lim_{x\to\infty} \frac{x^{100}}{e^x}$:

$$\frac{x^{100}}{e^x} \to \frac{100x^{99}}{e^x} \to \frac{100 \cdot 99 x^{98}}{e^x} \to \cdots \to \frac{100!}{e^x} \to 0 \qquad \square$$

## 10. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 10.1 Power Laws in Neural Scaling

> _This is one of the most empirically striking facts in modern deep learning._

**Observation (Kaplan et al., 2020 — OpenAI Scaling Laws):** The test loss $\mathcal{L}$ of a language model follows a power law in the number of parameters $N$, training tokens $D$, and compute $C$:

$$\mathcal{L}(N) \approx \left(\frac{N_c}{N}\right)^{\alpha_N}, \qquad \alpha_N \approx 0.076$$

On a log-log plot: a straight line. This is _exactly_ the signature of a power function.

**Why does this matter?** It means:

- Doubling model size gives a predictable, consistent improvement.
- You can extrapolate performance _before_ training — the exponent $\alpha$ is stable across scales.
- The optimal allocation of compute between model size and data follows a power law (the **Chinchilla scaling law**, Hoffmann et al. 2022).

```
log(Loss)
    |  \
    |   \   slope ≈ -0.076
    |    \
    |     \
    |      \
    |       \
    +──────────────── log(Parameters)
```

### 10.2 Activation Functions as Power Functions

Several key activation functions are power functions or closely related:

**ReLU (Rectified Linear Unit):** $$\text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0, x) = \begin{cases} x & x \geq 0 \ 0 & x < 0 \end{cases}$$ This is a piecewise power function: $x^1$ for $x \geq 0$. Its derivative is a step function (piecewise $x^0$).

**Leaky ReLU:** $$\text{LeakyReLU}(x) = \begin{cases} x & x \geq 0 \ \alpha x & x < 0 \end{cases}$$ Still piecewise linear (degree 1 power).

**ELU and SELU** approximate $e^x - 1 \approx x + x^2/2 + \ldots$ for $x < 0$ — Taylor series using power functions.

**Polynomial activations** are used explicitly in:

- **Kolmogorov-Arnold Networks (KANs, 2024):** replace fixed activations with learnable splines (piecewise polynomials).
- **Implicit Neural Representations (SIREN):** use $\sin(\omega_0 x)$ whose Taylor expansion is a power series.

---

### 10.3 Loss Functions and Power Functions

**MSE (Mean Squared Error):** $$\mathcal{L}(\hat{y}, y) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i (y_i - \hat{y}_i)^2$$ The squaring is $x^2$ — a power function. It penalizes large errors more than small ones, quadratically.

**MAE (Mean Absolute Error):** $$\mathcal{L}(\hat{y}, y) = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i |y_i - \hat{y}_i|$$ This is $|x|^1$ — a power function with $r = 1$. Less sensitive to outliers than MSE.

**Huber Loss** interpolates: $$\mathcal{L}_\delta(x) = \begin{cases} \frac{1}{2}x^2 & |x| \leq \delta \ \delta(|x| - \frac{\delta}{2}) & |x| > \delta \end{cases}$$ Quadratic (power 2) near zero, linear (power 1) in the tails. This is explicitly a piecewise power function.

**General $L^p$ loss:** $$\mathcal{L}_p = \sum_i |y_i - \hat{y}_i|^p$$

- $p = 1$: MAE (robust to outliers)
- $p = 2$: MSE (efficient, assumes Gaussian noise)
- $p \to \infty$: Chebyshev norm (controls worst-case error)

The choice of $p$ — the exponent of a power function — completely determines the geometry of the loss landscape.

---

### 10.4 Attention and Softmax — Power Functions in Disguise

The **softmax function** is: $$\text{softmax}(x_i) = \frac{e^{x_i}}{\sum_j e^{x_j}}$$

For large inputs, this concentrates mass on the maximum. But consider the **sparsemax** and **$\alpha$-entmax** generalizations used in modern attention:

$$\text{$\alpha$-entmax}(\mathbf{z}) = \arg\max_{\mathbf{p} \in \Delta} \left[\mathbf{p}^\top \mathbf{z} + H_\alpha(\mathbf{p})\right]$$

For $\alpha = 2$: the output is piecewise linear — a power function of rank 1 in the inputs. The entire family of attention mechanisms interpolates between $\alpha = 1$ (softmax) and $\alpha = 2$ (sparsemax) by varying the exponent of a power-function-based entropy term.

---

### 10.5 Weight Initialization and Power Laws in Spectra

**Xavier/Glorot initialization** sets weights $W \sim U\left[-\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}, \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\right]$. The $1/\sqrt{n}$ is $n^{-1/2}$ — a power function of the layer width.

**He initialization** (for ReLU networks): $W \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2/n)$, standard deviation $\propto n^{-1/2}$.

In both cases, the initialization scale follows a power law in network width. This ensures variance is preserved across layers — a direct consequence of the scaling property $f(\lambda x) = \lambda^r f(x)$.

**Eigenvalue spectra of weight matrices** in trained deep networks often follow power laws (Martin & Mahoney, 2019): $$\rho(\lambda) \sim \lambda^{-\alpha}$$ Networks with $\alpha \in (2, 4)$ tend to generalize better. This is active research — the exponent of the spectral power law as a diagnostic of model quality.

---

### 10.6 Gradient Flow and Power Function Dynamics

Consider the gradient flow ODE for loss $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \theta^r$: $$\dot{\theta} = -\frac{d\mathcal{L}}{d\theta} = -r\theta^{r-1}$$

**Solution:** Depends on $r$.

- $r = 2$ (quadratic loss): $\dot{\theta} = -2\theta \implies \theta(t) = \theta_0 e^{-2t}$. **Exponential convergence.**
- $r = 4$ (quartic loss): $\dot{\theta} = -4\theta^3 \implies \theta(t) = \theta_0(1 + 8\theta_0^2 t)^{-1/2}$. **Power law convergence** — much slower.

> **Key insight.** The shape of the loss landscape — which is determined by the power $r$ — controls the _speed_ of gradient-based optimization. Flat (high $r$) losses are hard to optimize; quadratic ($r=2$) losses are ideal. This is why second-order methods (Newton, curvature) try to locally transform the landscape toward $r = 2$.
