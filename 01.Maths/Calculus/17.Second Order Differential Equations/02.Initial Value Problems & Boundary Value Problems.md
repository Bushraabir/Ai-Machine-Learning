## § 0 — The Central Question

Solving a differential equation gives you a **family** of solutions — parameterized by arbitrary constants. For a second order ODE, two constants $c_1, c_2$. The solution space is a plane.

Imposing **conditions** means picking a specific solution from this family. But conditions come in fundamentally different flavors, and the difference is not merely technical — it reflects a deep physical distinction about **what kind of information we have**.

---

## § 1 — Two Ways to Specify a Solution

### 1.1 Initial Value Problems (IVP)

An **Initial Value Problem** specifies the solution and its derivative **at a single point** $x_0$:

$$y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x), \qquad y(x_0) = y_0,\quad y'(x_0) = y_0'$$

Both conditions are imposed **at the same location** $x_0$ — the "initial" point.

**Physical meaning:** You know the complete state of the system at time $t_0$: position _and_ velocity. From this, you predict the future (and past). This is the **Cauchy problem** — the formulation of classical mechanics.

### 1.2 Boundary Value Problems (BVP)

A **Boundary Value Problem** specifies conditions **at two different points** $x = a$ and $x = b$:

$$y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x), \qquad \mathcal{B}_1[y](https://claude.ai/chat/a) = \alpha, \quad \mathcal{B}_2[y](https://claude.ai/chat/b) = \beta$$

where $\mathcal{B}_1, \mathcal{B}_2$ are boundary operators (often evaluation of $y$ or $y'$).

**Physical meaning:** You know something about the system **at both ends** of an interval, but nothing in between. This is the formulation of **equilibrium problems** — a beam deflecting between two supports, a wire with fixed endpoints, the temperature distribution in a rod with fixed endpoint temperatures.

### 1.3 The Fundamental Difference

||**IVP**|**BVP**|
|---|---|---|
|Conditions at|One point $x_0$|Two points $a$, $b$|
|Information|State at one moment|Endpoint constraints|
|Physical type|Evolution in time|Spatial equilibrium|
|Existence/Uniqueness|Always (if continuous coefficients)|Not guaranteed|
|Solution method|Direct integration, characteristic eq.|Shooting, finite difference, eigenfunction expansion|

> **Key insight:** For an IVP, existence and uniqueness are essentially free. For a BVP, they are delicate — a BVP may have no solution, exactly one, or infinitely many. This is the central surprise of the theory.

## § 2 — Initial Value Problems: The Complete Theory

### 2.1 Existence and Uniqueness

> **Theorem (Picard–Lindelöf for second order linear ODEs).** Let $p$, $q$, $g$ be continuous on an open interval $I$ containing $x_0$. Then the IVP:
> 
> $$y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x), \quad y(x_0) = y_0, \quad y'(x_0) = y_0'$$
> 
> has a **unique** solution defined on **all of** $I$.

**Proof sketch.** Write the IVP as a first order system by setting $\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix} y \ y' \end{pmatrix}$:

$$\mathbf{u}' = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -q(x) & -p(x) \end{pmatrix} \mathbf{u} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ g(x) \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{u}(x_0) = \begin{pmatrix} y_0 \ y_0' \end{pmatrix}$$

The right-hand side is linear in $\mathbf{u}$ with continuous coefficient matrix. A linear system with continuous coefficients satisfies a global Lipschitz condition, so Picard iteration converges on all of $I$. Uniqueness follows from the Gronwall inequality. $\blacksquare$

**What this theorem buys:** Once you know $y(x_0)$ and $y'(x_0)$, the solution is completely and uniquely determined everywhere. No ambiguity whatsoever.

### 2.2 The Solution Algorithm

**Step 1.** Solve the homogeneous equation $L[y] = 0$ to find a fundamental set ${y_1, y_2}$.

**Step 2.** Find a particular solution $y_p$ (undetermined coefficients or variation of parameters).

**Step 3.** Write the general solution: $y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 + y_p$.

**Step 4.** Apply initial conditions to determine $c_1, c_2$:

$$\begin{cases} c_1 y_1(x_0) + c_2 y_2(x_0) = y_0 - y_p(x_0) \ c_1 y_1'(x_0) + c_2 y_2'(x_0) = y_0' - y_p'(x_0) \end{cases}$$

The coefficient matrix of this system is $\begin{pmatrix} y_1(x_0) & y_2(x_0) \ y_1'(x_0) & y_2'(x_0) \end{pmatrix}$, whose determinant is $W(x_0) \neq 0$ (since ${y_1, y_2}$ is a fundamental set). So the system always has a unique solution. This is exactly why IVPs always have unique solutions when the homogeneous equation is solved correctly.

### 2.3 Worked Example: Full IVP

**Problem:** Solve $y'' - 3y' + 2y = e^{3x}$, $y(0) = 1$, $y'(0) = 0$.

**Step 1:** Characteristic equation: $r^2 - 3r + 2 = (r-1)(r-2) = 0$. So $y_1 = e^x$, $y_2 = e^{2x}$.

**Step 2:** Since $e^{3x}$ is not a homogeneous solution, try $y_p = Ae^{3x}$: $$9A - 9A + 2A = 1 \implies A = \frac{1}{2}$$ So $y_p = \frac{1}{2}e^{3x}$.

**Step 3:** General solution: $y = c_1 e^x + c_2 e^{2x} + \frac{1}{2}e^{3x}$.

**Step 4:** Apply $y(0) = 1$ and $y'(0) = 0$: $$c_1 + c_2 + \frac{1}{2} = 1 \implies c_1 + c_2 = \frac{1}{2}$$ $$c_1 + 2c_2 + \frac{3}{2} = 0 \implies c_1 + 2c_2 = -\frac{3}{2}$$

Subtracting: $c_2 = -2$. Then $c_1 = \frac{5}{2}$.

$$\boxed{y = \frac{5}{2}e^x - 2e^{2x} + \frac{1}{2}e^{3x}}$$

### 2.4 Nonlinear IVPs: Local vs. Global Existence

For **nonlinear** IVPs $y'' = f(x, y, y')$, the situation is more delicate.

> **Theorem (Local Existence).** If $f$, $\partial f/\partial y$, $\partial f/\partial y'$ are continuous near $(x_0, y_0, y_0')$, then the IVP has a unique solution on some interval $(x_0 - h, x_0 + h)$.

The solution may **blow up** in finite time. Example: $y' = y^2$, $y(0) = 1$ gives $y = \frac{1}{1-x}$ — which blows up at $x = 1$. Linearity is what prevents this from happening in the linear case.

## § 3 — Boundary Value Problems: Where Things Get Interesting

### 3.1 The Setup

Consider the second order linear BVP on $[a, b]$:

$$y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x)$$

with **boundary conditions (BCs)** of one of these types:

|Type|Condition|Name|
|---|---|---|
|**Dirichlet**|$y(a) = \alpha$, $y(b) = \beta$|Prescribed values|
|**Neumann**|$y'(a) = \alpha$, $y'(b) = \beta$|Prescribed derivatives (flux)|
|**Robin (Mixed)**|$y'(a) - h_1 y(a) = \alpha$, $y'(b) + h_2 y(b) = \beta$|Mixed value and flux|
|**Periodic**|$y(a) = y(b)$, $y'(a) = y'(b)$|Periodicity|

The most common in practice (and in Stewart) are **Dirichlet** conditions.

### 3.2 The Shocking Difference from IVPs: Three Cases

Unlike IVPs, a BVP may have **no solution, a unique solution, or infinitely many solutions**. This is not a theorem — it is a fact you must check case by case. Here are three examples that make this vivid.

**Example A: Unique Solution**

$$y'' + y = x, \quad y(0) = 0, \quad y(\pi/2) = 0$$

Homogeneous solution: $y_h = c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x$. Particular solution: $y_p = x$.

General: $y = c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x + x$.

Apply BCs:

- $y(0) = 0$: $c_1 = 0$.
- $y(\pi/2) = 0$: $c_2 + \pi/2 = 0 \implies c_2 = -\pi/2$.

Unique solution: $y = -\frac{\pi}{2}\sin x + x$. ✓

---

**Example B: No Solution**

$$y'' + y = 0, \quad y(0) = 0, \quad y(\pi) = 1$$

Homogeneous solution: $y = c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x$.

Apply BCs:

- $y(0) = 0$: $c_1 = 0$. So $y = c_2 \sin x$.
- $y(\pi) = 1$: $c_2 \sin\pi = 0 \neq 1$. **Contradiction.**

No solution exists. ✗

---

**Example C: Infinitely Many Solutions**

$$y'' + y = 0, \quad y(0) = 0, \quad y(\pi) = 0$$

Apply BCs:

- $y(0) = 0$: $c_1 = 0$. So $y = c_2 \sin x$.
- $y(\pi) = 0$: $c_2 \sin\pi = 0$, satisfied for **any** $c_2$.

Solution: $y = c_2 \sin x$ for any $c_2 \in \mathbb{R}$. ✗ (infinitely many)

> **What just happened?** The boundary condition $y(\pi) = 0$ with $y = c_2 \sin x$ is automatically satisfied because $\sin\pi = 0$ regardless of $c_2$. The boundary "accidentally" coincides with the structure of the homogeneous solution. This is not a coincidence — it is the phenomenon of **eigenvalues**, which is the heart of the BVP theory.

### 3.3 The Algebraic Reason: When Does Uniqueness Fail?

After applying the general solution $y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 + y_p$ to Dirichlet BCs $y(a) = \alpha$, $y(b) = \beta$, we get the linear system:

$$\begin{pmatrix} y_1(a) & y_2(a) \ y_1(b) & y_2(b) \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_1 \ c_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \alpha - y_p(a) \ \beta - y_p(b) \end{pmatrix}$$

Let $D = y_1(a)y_2(b) - y_1(b)y_2(a)$ (the determinant of the boundary matrix).

- **$D \neq 0$:** Unique solution always exists (for any $\alpha, \beta, g$).
- **$D = 0$:** Either no solution or infinitely many — depending on whether the right-hand side is consistent.

Note: $D$ is **not** the Wronskian $W(x)$ (which is never zero). $D$ is the Wronskian-like expression evaluated at the **two different boundary points** $a$ and $b$. It can be zero even when $W \neq 0$.

**This is the fundamental obstacle in BVP theory, absent in IVP theory.**

## § 4 — Eigenvalue Problems and Sturm–Liouville Theory

### 4.1 The Eigenvalue Problem

Consider the **parameterized** homogeneous BVP:

$$y'' + \lambda y = 0, \quad y(0) = 0, \quad y(L) = 0$$

For which values of $\lambda$ does a **nontrivial** solution exist?

**Case 1: $\lambda < 0$.** Write $\lambda = -\mu^2$, $\mu > 0$. General solution: $y = c_1 e^{\mu x} + c_2 e^{-\mu x}$.

- $y(0) = 0$: $c_1 + c_2 = 0$.
- $y(L) = 0$: $c_1(e^{\mu L} - e^{-\mu L}) = 0 \implies c_1 = 0$ (since $e^{\mu L} \neq e^{-\mu L}$).
- Only trivial solution $y = 0$. No eigenvalue here.

**Case 2: $\lambda = 0$.** $y'' = 0 \Rightarrow y = c_1 + c_2 x$. BCs force $c_1 = c_2 = 0$. No eigenvalue.

**Case 3: $\lambda > 0$.** Write $\lambda = \mu^2$, $\mu > 0$. General solution: $y = c_1 \cos\mu x + c_2 \sin\mu x$.

- $y(0) = 0$: $c_1 = 0$. So $y = c_2 \sin\mu x$.
- $y(L) = 0$: $c_2 \sin\mu L = 0$. For nontrivial solution: $\sin\mu L = 0 \Rightarrow \mu L = n\pi$ for $n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$

**Eigenvalues:**

$$\lambda_n = \frac{n^2 \pi^2}{L^2}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

**Eigenfunctions:**

$$y_n(x) = \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)$$

These eigenfunctions are the **natural modes** of the system. The BVP has nontrivial solutions if and only if $\lambda$ is one of these eigenvalues.

### 4.2 Orthogonality of Eigenfunctions

The eigenfunctions ${y_n}$ satisfy a remarkable property:

$$\int_0^L \sin\frac{m\pi x}{L} \sin\frac{n\pi x}{L},dx = \begin{cases} 0 & m \neq n \ L/2 & m = n \end{cases}$$

The eigenfunctions are **orthogonal** in $L^2([0,L])$. This is not a coincidence — it is a consequence of the **Sturm–Liouville theorem**.

### 4.3 Sturm–Liouville Theory

The general **Sturm–Liouville problem** is:

$$-\frac{d}{dx}\left[p(x)y'\right] + q(x)y = \lambda w(x) y, \quad a < x < b$$

with boundary conditions, where $p > 0$, $w > 0$ (weight function).

> **Theorem (Sturm–Liouville).** Under appropriate conditions:
> 
> 1. All eigenvalues $\lambda_n$ are **real**.
> 2. The eigenvalues form an increasing sequence $\lambda_1 < \lambda_2 < \lambda_3 < \cdots \to +\infty$.
> 3. Each eigenvalue has exactly **one** linearly independent eigenfunction $y_n$.
> 4. The eigenfunctions ${y_n}$ are **orthogonal** with respect to the weight $w$: $$\int_a^b y_m(x) y_n(x) w(x),dx = 0, \quad m \neq n$$
> 5. The eigenfunctions form a **complete** orthogonal basis of $L^2_w([a,b])$: any square-integrable function can be expanded as $f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty c_n y_n(x)$.

This is why **Fourier series** exist. The sine and cosine functions are eigenfunctions of $-d^2/dx^2$ with Dirichlet and Neumann boundary conditions respectively. Fourier series is Sturm–Liouville theory for the simplest operator.

**More examples of Sturm–Liouville problems and their eigenfunctions:**

|Operator|Domain|BCs|Eigenfunctions|
|---|---|---|---|
|$-d^2/dx^2$|$[0, L]$|$y(0) = y(L) = 0$|$\sin(n\pi x/L)$|
|$-d^2/dx^2$|$[0, L]$|$y'(0) = y'(L) = 0$|$\cos(n\pi x/L)$|
|$-\frac{1}{x}\frac{d}{dx}(x\frac{d}{dx})$|$[0,1]$|Regularity + $y(1) = 0$|Bessel functions $J_0(\lambda_n x)$|
|$-(1-x^2)\frac{d^2}{dx^2} + 2x\frac{d}{dx}$|$[-1,1]$|Regularity|Legendre polynomials $P_n(x)$|

## § 5 — Shooting Method: Solving BVPs Numerically

The **shooting method** converts a BVP into a sequence of IVPs — exploiting the clean existence/uniqueness theory of IVPs to handle the messy BVP.

### 5.1 The Idea

For the BVP $y'' = f(x, y, y')$, $y(a) = \alpha$, $y(b) = \beta$:

**We know** $y(a) = \alpha$ but **don't know** $y'(a)$.

**Guess** $y'(a) = s$ (the "shooting parameter"). Solve the IVP: $$y'' = f(x, y, y'), \quad y(a) = \alpha, \quad y'(a) = s$$

This gives a solution $y(x; s)$. Define the **residual**: $$F(s) = y(b; s) - \beta$$

We want $F(s) = 0$. Use **root-finding** (bisection, Newton's method) to find $s^_$ such that $F(s^_) = 0$.

```
  y
  |        β ------- target
  |       /
  |      /  ← too low (s too small)
  |   α /
  |    / ← correct s*
  |   /\  ← too high (s too large)
  +---+----------> x
      a          b
```

For a **linear** BVP, $F(s)$ is linear in $s$, so we only need two shots.

### 5.2 Linear Shooting Method

For the linear BVP $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x)$, $y(a) = \alpha$, $y(b) = \beta$:

Solve two IVPs:

**IVP 1:** $u'' + pu' + qu = g$, $u(a) = \alpha$, $u'(a) = 0$.

**IVP 2:** $v'' + pv' + qv = 0$, $v(a) = 0$, $v'(a) = 1$.

By linearity, $y = u + cv$ solves the ODE for any constant $c$. Apply the boundary condition at $b$:

$$y(b) = u(b) + cv(b) = \beta \implies c = \frac{\beta - u(b)}{v(b)}$$

provided $v(b) \neq 0$ (which is exactly the condition $D \neq 0$ from §3.3 — the unique solution condition).

The solution is $y(x) = u(x) + \frac{\beta - u(b)}{v(b)} v(x)$.

## § 6 — Green's Functions: The BVP Analogue of Variation of Parameters

For the BVP: $$L[y] = y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x), \quad y(a) = 0, \quad y(b) = 0$$

(homogeneous Dirichlet BCs), assuming unique solvability, there exists a **Green's function** $G(x, t)$ such that:

$$y(x) = \int_a^b G(x, t) g(t),dt$$

### 6.1 Construction of $G(x, t)$

Let ${y_1, y_2}$ be the fundamental set of $L[y] = 0$ with:

- $y_1$ satisfying the **left** BC: $y_1(a) = 0$.
- $y_2$ satisfying the **right** BC: $y_2(b) = 0$.

Then:

$$G(x, t) = \frac{1}{W(t)} \begin{cases} y_1(x)y_2(t) & a \leq x \leq t \ y_1(t)y_2(x) & t \leq x \leq b \end{cases}$$

where $W(t) = W(y_1, y_2)(t)$ is the Wronskian.

### 6.2 Properties of the Green's Function

- $G(x, t)$ is **continuous** at $x = t$.
- $G_x(x, t)$ has a **jump discontinuity** at $x = t$: $G_x(t^+, t) - G_x(t^-, t) = \frac{1}{p(t)}$ (from the jump condition).
- $G(x, t) = G(t, x)$ when $L$ is **self-adjoint** (Sturm–Liouville type) — **symmetry** of the Green's function.
- $L_x[G(x,t)] = \delta(x - t)$, where $\delta$ is the Dirac delta — $G$ is the "impulse response" of the operator $L$.

**The physical interpretation:** $G(x, t)$ is the displacement at position $x$ of a string (or beam) due to a unit point load at position $t$. The total displacement under a distributed load $g(t)$ is the superposition integral $\int G(x,t)g(t),dt$.

### 6.3 Worked Example: Green's Function for $-y'' = g$, $y(0) = y(1) = 0$

This is the deflection of a string under load $g(x)$.

Homogeneous equation $-y'' = 0 \Rightarrow y'' = 0$: general solution $y = Ax + B$.

- $y_1$ satisfies $y_1(0) = 0$: take $y_1 = x$.
- $y_2$ satisfies $y_2(1) = 0$: take $y_2 = 1 - x$.
- Wronskian: $W = y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2 = x(-1) - 1(1-x) = -1$.

$$G(x, t) = \frac{1}{-1}\begin{cases} x(1-t) & 0 \leq x \leq t \ t(1-x) & t \leq x \leq 1 \end{cases} = \begin{cases} -x(1-t) & x \leq t \ -t(1-x) & x \geq t \end{cases}$$

Wait — let me recheck the sign. The equation is $-y'' = g \Rightarrow L[y] = -y''$, so $p = 0$, $q = 0$, $a = -1$ in $L[y] = ay''$. The standard form $y'' = -g$ gives the Green's function for $y'' = \delta$:

$$G(x, t) = \begin{cases} -x(1-t) & x \leq t \ -t(1-x) & x \geq t \end{cases}$$

Note $G \leq 0$ — deflection is downward under a downward load. Note also $G(x,t) = G(t,x)$ — the symmetry of the self-adjoint operator. The solution:

$$y(x) = \int_0^1 G(x, t)(-g(t)),dt$$

For $g = 1$ (uniform load): $y(x) = \frac{x(1-x)}{2}$ — a parabola, as expected for a uniformly loaded string.

## § 7 — Finite Difference Methods for BVPs

When analytic methods fail, we discretize. Replace the continuous BVP by a system of algebraic equations on a grid.

### 7.1 The Discretization

Divide $[a, b]$ into $N+1$ equal subintervals: $x_i = a + ih$, $h = (b-a)/(N+1)$, $i = 0, 1, \ldots, N+1$.

The **second-order finite difference approximations**:

$$y''(x_i) \approx \frac{y_{i-1} - 2y_i + y_{i+1}}{h^2}, \quad y'(x_i) \approx \frac{y_{i+1} - y_{i-1}}{2h}$$

### 7.2 The Linear System

For the BVP $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x)$, $y(a) = \alpha$, $y(b) = \beta$, substituting the approximations at each interior node $x_i$ ($i = 1, \ldots, N$):

$$\frac{y_{i-1} - 2y_i + y_{i+1}}{h^2} + p_i \frac{y_{i+1} - y_{i-1}}{2h} + q_i y_i = g_i$$

Multiplying through by $h^2$:

$$\left(1 - \frac{h}{2}p_i\right)y_{i-1} + (-2 + h^2 q_i)y_i + \left(1 + \frac{h}{2}p_i\right)y_{i+1} = h^2 g_i$$

With $y_0 = \alpha$ and $y_{N+1} = \beta$ absorbed into the right-hand side, this is a **tridiagonal linear system** $A\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{b}$ of size $N \times N$:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} -2 + h^2 q_1 & 1 + \frac{h}{2}p_1 & & \ 1 - \frac{h}{2}p_2 & -2 + h^2 q_2 & 1 + \frac{h}{2}p_2 & \ & \ddots & \ddots & \ddots \ & & 1 - \frac{h}{2}p_N & -2 + h^2 q_N \end{pmatrix}$$

Tridiagonal systems are solved in $O(N)$ operations by the **Thomas algorithm** (tridiagonal LU factorization).

## § 8 — Worked Examples: BVPs in Full

### Example 1: BVP with Unique Solution

**Problem:** $y'' + 2y' + y = e^{-x}$, $y(0) = 0$, $y(1) = 1$.

**Homogeneous:** $r^2 + 2r + 1 = (r+1)^2 = 0$. Repeated root $r = -1$. $$y_h = (c_1 + c_2 x)e^{-x}$$

**Particular:** $e^{-x}$ satisfies the homogeneous equation (double). Try $y_p = Ax^2 e^{-x}$: $$y_p' = A(2x - x^2)e^{-x}, \quad y_p'' = A(2 - 4x + x^2)e^{-x}$$ $$y_p'' + 2y_p' + y_p = A(2 - 4x + x^2 + 4x - 2x^2 + x^2)e^{-x} = 2Ae^{-x} = e^{-x}$$ $$\implies A = \frac{1}{2}$$

**General:** $y = \left(c_1 + c_2 x + \frac{x^2}{2}\right)e^{-x}$.

**BCs:**

- $y(0) = 0$: $c_1 = 0$.
- $y(1) = 1$: $(c_2 + \frac{1}{2})e^{-1} = 1 \implies c_2 = e - \frac{1}{2}$.

$$y = \left[\left(e - \frac{1}{2}\right)x + \frac{x^2}{2}\right]e^{-x}$$

---

### Example 2: Checking for Nonexistence

**Problem:** $y'' + \pi^2 y = \sin(\pi x)$, $y(0) = 0$, $y(1) = 0$.

**Homogeneous:** $r^2 + \pi^2 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \pm i\pi$. So $y_h = c_1\cos\pi x + c_2\sin\pi x$.

**Particular:** $\sin(\pi x)$ is a **homogeneous solution** (resonance). Try $y_p = x(A\cos\pi x + B\sin\pi x)$:

Computing: $$y_p'' = \pi^2(-Ax\cos\pi x - Bx\sin\pi x) + 2\pi(-A\sin\pi x + B\cos\pi x)$$ $$y_p'' + \pi^2 y_p = 2\pi(-A\sin\pi x + B\cos\pi x) = \sin\pi x$$

So $-2\pi A = 1 \Rightarrow A = -\frac{1}{2\pi}$ and $B = 0$.

$$y_p = -\frac{x}{2\pi}\cos\pi x$$

**General:** $y = c_1\cos\pi x + c_2\sin\pi x - \frac{x}{2\pi}\cos\pi x$.

**BCs:**

- $y(0) = 0$: $c_1 = 0$. So $y = c_2\sin\pi x - \frac{x}{2\pi}\cos\pi x$.
- $y(1) = 0$: $c_2 \cdot 0 - \frac{1}{2\pi}\cdot(-1) = \frac{1}{2\pi} \neq 0$.

**No solution exists.** ✗

The forcing $\sin(\pi x)$ happens to be at the resonant eigenfrequency of the boundary problem, and the BCs cannot be simultaneously satisfied.

## § 9 — Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 9.1 Neural Networks as BVP Solvers: PINNs

Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs) are trained to solve BVPs:

$$L[y] = g \text{ on } (a,b), \quad y(a) = \alpha, \quad y(b) = \beta$$

The network $y_\theta(x)$ minimizes:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \underbrace{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N |L[y_\theta](https://claude.ai/chat/x_i) - g(x_i)|^2}_{\text{interior residual}} + \underbrace{|y_\theta(a) - \alpha|^2 + |y_\theta(b) - \beta|^2}_{\text{boundary residual}}$$

The IVP vs BVP distinction matters critically here:

- **IVP:** Only the left boundary appears in the loss. Integration marches from left to right. This is numerically stable (Runge–Kutta).
- **BVP:** Both boundaries constrain simultaneously. The optimizer must satisfy conditions on two sides at once. This is inherently harder — the loss landscape has ridges and saddle points that gradient descent struggles with near eigenvalues. When $\lambda$ is close to an eigenvalue of $L$, the problem is nearly singular, and the neural network must "learn" the shooting method implicitly.

### 9.2 Boundary Conditions in PDE-solving Networks

For higher-dimensional PDEs (the natural extension of BVPs), enforcing boundary conditions is an active research problem:

- **Soft enforcement (penalty):** Add boundary residual to the loss. Simple but the boundary may not be exactly satisfied.
- **Hard enforcement:** Parameterize the network as $y_\theta(x) = A(x) + B(x)N_\theta(x)$, where $A(x)$ satisfies the BCs exactly and $B(x)$ vanishes on the boundary. Then $N_\theta$ is unconstrained. This is analogous to the shooting method's linear decomposition $y = u + cv$.

### 9.3 IVPs in Sequence Models: Autoregressive Generation

**Autoregressive language models** (GPT, etc.) solve a discrete IVP: given the "initial condition" (the prompt = $y_0, y_0', \ldots$), march forward in time to generate the sequence. The existence and uniqueness theorem for IVPs is mirrored in the fact that, given a prompt, the model deterministically (at temperature 0) generates a unique continuation — the discrete analog of the unique IVP solution.

The contrast with BVPs appears in **constrained generation**:

- Generate text that starts with A (left BC) **and** ends with B (right BC).
- This is a discrete BVP, and it may have no solution, one solution, or many.
- Controlled generation methods (like FUDGE, PPLM, classifier guidance in diffusion) are essentially **numerical BVP solvers** operating in the space of text.

### 9.4 Diffusion Models as IVPs Running Backwards

**Score-based diffusion models** define a forward SDE (adding noise):

$$d\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}, t),dt + g(t),d\mathbf{W}$$

with initial condition $\mathbf{x}(0) \sim p_{\text{data}}$. This is an IVP in distribution space.

The reverse (denoising) process is also an IVP, running backwards from $t = T$ (pure noise as initial condition) to $t = 0$ (generated sample):

$$d\mathbf{x} = \left[\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x}, t) - g(t)^2 \nabla_\mathbf{x} \log p_t(\mathbf{x})\right]dt$$

The learned score $\nabla_\mathbf{x} \log p_t(\mathbf{x})$ plays the role of $p(x)$ in the ODE. The IVP structure guarantees that, given an initial noise vector, there is a unique denoising trajectory — which is why diffusion models are deterministic at inference given a seed.

### 9.5 Green's Functions and Attention

The output of **self-attention** in a transformer can be written as:

$$\text{Attention}(\mathbf{Q}, \mathbf{K}, \mathbf{V})_i = \sum_j A_{ij} \mathbf{V}_j$$

where $A_{ij} = \text{softmax}(\mathbf{Q}_i \cdot \mathbf{K}_j / \sqrt{d})$.

This is a **discrete integral operator** with kernel $A_{ij}$ — precisely the structure of a Green's function integral $y(x) = \int G(x,t)g(t),dt$. The attention kernel $A_{ij}$ plays the role of the Green's function $G(x_i, t_j)$, and the values $\mathbf{V}_j$ play the role of the forcing $g(t_j)$.

More precisely: attention learns a **data-dependent Green's function** for the implicit operator that maps inputs to outputs. This connection is made rigorous in recent work on **attention as integral operators** and **neural operators** (Li et al., 2020 — Fourier Neural Operator), where the network directly parameterizes the Green's function of a PDE operator.

### 9.6 Eigenvalues, Stability, and the Spectrum of Neural Networks

The Sturm–Liouville eigenvalue theory has a direct analogue in deep learning:

- The **Hessian** $\nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$ of the loss function plays the role of $L$ in $L[y] = \lambda y$.
- The **eigenvalues** of the Hessian determine convergence rate: directions with large $\lambda$ (sharp curvature) are hard to optimize; directions with small $\lambda$ are flat.
- **Batch normalization** implicitly shifts the eigenvalue spectrum of layer-wise Jacobians — it is an attempt to solve a "spectral BVP" at each layer: keep the singular values of the weight Jacobian near 1.
- **Residual connections** (ResNets) modify the spectrum: $\mathbf{h}_{l+1} = \mathbf{h}_l + F(\mathbf{h}_l)$ shifts all eigenvalues by 1, preventing eigenvalues from being too close to 0 (vanishing gradients = small eigenvalues of the chain product Jacobian).

The **S4 / Mamba state space models** explicitly parameterize their recurrent matrices to have eigenvalues with negative real part — mirroring the Sturm–Liouville condition that eigenvalues are real and bounded below. This guarantees the IVP stability of the recurrence, preventing the exponential blow-up (exploding gradients) that plagues vanilla RNNs.

## § 10 — Summary

### IVP: $y(x_0) = y_0$, $y'(x_0) = y_0'$

$$\boxed{\text{Always has a unique solution on } I \text{ if } p, q, g \in C(I)}$$

- Two conditions at **one** point.
- Solve: find general solution, apply ICs to get unique $c_1, c_2$.
- Nonlinear IVPs: may blow up in finite time.

### BVP: $y(a) = \alpha$, $y(b) = \beta$

$$\boxed{\text{May have 0, 1, or } \infty \text{ solutions — check the boundary matrix determinant } D}$$

- Two conditions at **two** different points.
- Solve: shooting method, finite differences, eigenfunction expansion.
- Eigenvalue problems: nontrivial solutions exist only for special $\lambda$ — the **eigenvalues**.
- Eigenfunctions form a complete orthogonal basis (Sturm–Liouville) — the foundation of Fourier series and spectral methods.

### Key Comparison

||IVP|BVP|
|---|---|---|
|Conditions|Same point|Boundary points|
|Existence/Uniqueness|Guaranteed|Case-by-case|
|Failure mode|None (linear case)|$D = 0$ (resonance at eigenvalue)|
|Numerical method|Runge–Kutta|Shooting, FD, spectral|
|Green's function|Variation of parameters|Symmetric $G(x,t) = G(t,x)$|
|AI analogue|Autoregressive models, diffusion|Constrained generation, PINNs|
