

> _"The whole of mechanics — classical, quantum, and relativistic — is secretly a second order linear ODE wearing different clothes."_

---

## § 0 — Why Second Order?

First order ODEs describe rates. Second order ODEs describe **how rates change** — and that is where physics lives.

Newton's second law: $F = ma = m\ddot{x}$. Acceleration is a second derivative. The moment you write a physical law involving force, you get a second order ODE. The same is true for:

- A mass on a spring: $m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)$
- An RLC electrical circuit: $L\ddot{q} + R\dot{q} + \frac{1}{C}q = \mathcal{E}(t)$
- Heat conduction, wave propagation, quantum mechanics ($\hat{H}\psi = E\psi$)
- Beam deflection in structural engineering

The general theory of second order linear ODEs is not just a collection of techniques. It is a **complete, elegant algebraic structure** — and understanding it deeply unlocks everything above.

## § 1 — The General Form

### 1.1 Standard Form

A **second order linear ODE** has the form:

$$P(x)y'' + Q(x)y' + R(x)y = G(x)$$

Dividing through by $P(x)$ (assuming $P(x) \neq 0$), we get the **standard form**:

$$y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x)$$

where $p, q, g$ are continuous functions on some interval $I$.

- If $g(x) = 0$: the equation is **homogeneous**.
- If $g(x) \neq 0$: the equation is **nonhomogeneous**.

> **Critical remark:** The word "linear" refers to linearity in $y$ and its derivatives — not in $x$. The coefficients $p(x), q(x)$ can be any functions of $x$. The equation is linear because $y$ appears only to the first power, with no products like $yy'$.

### 1.2 The Operator Language

Define the **linear differential operator**:

$$L[y] = y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y$$

The equation becomes $L[y] = g(x)$. The key property:

$$L[\alpha y_1 + \beta y_2] = \alpha L[y_1] + \beta L[y_2]$$

for any constants $\alpha, \beta$. **Linearity of $L$ is the engine of the entire theory.** Everything that follows — superposition, the structure of solution sets, variation of parameters — flows from this one property.

## § 2 — The Homogeneous Equation: Structure of Solutions

### 2.1 The Superposition Principle

> **Theorem.** If $y_1$ and $y_2$ are both solutions of $L[y] = 0$ on an interval $I$, then so is any linear combination $y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$ for constants $c_1, c_2 \in \mathbb{R}$.

**Proof.** Direct computation:

$$L[c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2] = c_1 L[y_1] + c_2 L[y_2] = c_1 \cdot 0 + c_2 \cdot 0 = 0 \qquad \blacksquare$$

The set of all solutions to $L[y] = 0$ forms a **vector space** — specifically, a subspace of $C^2(I)$.

### 2.2 The Wronskian

Given two solutions $y_1, y_2$ of $L[y] = 0$, the **Wronskian** is:

$$W(y_1, y_2)(x) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \ y_1' & y_2' \end{vmatrix} = y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2$$

> **Theorem (Abel).** If $y_1, y_2$ solve $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0$, then:
> 
> $$W(y_1, y_2)(x) = W(y_1, y_2)(x_0) \cdot e^{-\int_{x_0}^x p(t),dt}$$

**Proof.** Differentiate $W$:

$$W' = y_1 y_2'' - y_1'' y_2$$

Since $y_1'' = -p y_1' - q y_1$ and $y_2'' = -p y_2' - q y_2$:

$$W' = y_1(-p y_2' - q y_2) - (-p y_1' - q y_1)y_2 = -p(y_1 y_2' - y_1' y_2) = -pW$$

So $W' + p(x) W = 0$, a first order linear ODE. Solving: $W(x) = Ce^{-\int p,dx}$. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary (Abel):** Either $W(x) \neq 0$ for all $x \in I$, or $W(x) = 0$ for all $x \in I$. There is no middle ground.

### 2.3 Fundamental Sets of Solutions

> **Definition.** Two solutions $y_1, y_2$ of $L[y] = 0$ form a **fundamental set of solutions** if $W(y_1, y_2)(x) \neq 0$ on $I$ — i.e., they are **linearly independent**.

> **Theorem (Existence of a Fundamental Set).** If $p, q$ are continuous on $I$, then there exists a fundamental set of solutions ${y_1, y_2}$ for $L[y] = 0$ on $I$.

> **Theorem (General Solution).** If ${y_1, y_2}$ is a fundamental set, then **every** solution of $L[y] = 0$ can be written as:
> 
> $$y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$$

**Proof sketch.** Given any solution $y$, pick $x_0 \in I$ and consider the initial conditions $y(x_0) = \alpha$, $y'(x_0) = \beta$. Since $W(x_0) \neq 0$, the linear system:

$$c_1 y_1(x_0) + c_2 y_2(x_0) = \alpha, \quad c_1 y_1'(x_0) + c_2 y_2'(x_0) = \beta$$

has a unique solution $(c_1, c_2)$ (its determinant is $W(x_0) \neq 0$). By the uniqueness theorem for ODEs, $y \equiv c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$. $\blacksquare$

> The solution space of $L[y] = 0$ is a **2-dimensional vector space** over $\mathbb{R}$. A fundamental set is a **basis** for this space.

This is the central structural fact. Everything else is about finding a good basis.

## § 3 — Constant Coefficient Homogeneous Equations

### 3.1 The Characteristic Equation

Consider:

$$ay'' + by' + cy = 0, \quad a, b, c \in \mathbb{R},\ a \neq 0$$

**Guess:** $y = e^{rx}$ for some $r$ to be determined. Then $y' = re^{rx}$, $y'' = r^2 e^{rx}$. Substituting:

$$e^{rx}(ar^2 + br + c) = 0$$

Since $e^{rx} \neq 0$, we need:

$$\boxed{ar^2 + br + c = 0}$$

This is the **characteristic equation**. Its roots determine everything.

### 3.2 Case I: Two Distinct Real Roots ($b^2 - 4ac > 0$)

Roots $r_1 \neq r_2 \in \mathbb{R}$. Two solutions:

$$y_1 = e^{r_1 x}, \quad y_2 = e^{r_2 x}$$

Wronskian: $W = (r_2 - r_1)e^{(r_1 + r_2)x} \neq 0$. Linearly independent. General solution:

$$y = c_1 e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2 x}$$

**Example:** $y'' - 5y' + 6y = 0$.

Characteristic equation: $r^2 - 5r + 6 = (r-2)(r-3) = 0$, so $r_1 = 2$, $r_2 = 3$.

$$y = c_1 e^{2x} + c_2 e^{3x}$$

### 3.3 Case II: Repeated Real Root ($b^2 - 4ac = 0$)

One root $r_1 = r_2 = r = -b/(2a)$. We only get one solution $y_1 = e^{rx}$ from the characteristic equation. We need a second, linearly independent solution.

**Reduction of order:** Assume $y_2 = v(x) e^{rx}$. Substituting into $ay'' + by' + cy = 0$ and using $ar^2 + br + c = 0$ and $2ar + b = 0$ (since $r$ is a double root):

$$ae^{rx}(v'' + 2rv' + r^2 v) + be^{rx}(v' + rv) + ce^{rx}v = 0$$ $$e^{rx}\left[av'' + (2ar + b)v' + (ar^2 + br + c)v\right] = 0$$ $$e^{rx}\left[av'' + 0 \cdot v' + 0\right] = 0 \implies v'' = 0$$

So $v(x) = c_1 + c_2 x$. Taking $v = x$ gives the second solution:

$$y_2 = xe^{rx}$$

General solution:

$$y = (c_1 + c_2 x)e^{rx}$$

**Example:** $y'' - 4y' + 4y = 0$.

$r^2 - 4r + 4 = (r-2)^2 = 0$, so $r = 2$.

$$y = (c_1 + c_2 x)e^{2x}$$

> **Why does $xe^{rx}$ appear?** This is the ODE analogue of a **Jordan block** in linear algebra. When an eigenvalue is repeated, the eigenspace is deficient; the generalized eigenvector contributes the factor of $x$. The analogy is precise — the theory of constant-coefficient ODEs _is_ linear algebra on the space of exponentials.

### 3.4 Case III: Complex Conjugate Roots ($b^2 - 4ac < 0$)

Roots $r_{1,2} = \alpha \pm i\beta$ where $\alpha = -b/(2a)$, $\beta = \sqrt{4ac - b^2}/(2a) > 0$.

Complex solutions: $e^{(\alpha + i\beta)x}$ and $e^{(\alpha - i\beta)x}$. By **Euler's formula**:

$$e^{(\alpha \pm i\beta)x} = e^{\alpha x}(\cos\beta x \pm i \sin\beta x)$$

Taking real and imaginary parts (both are real solutions by linearity):

$$y_1 = e^{\alpha x}\cos\beta x, \quad y_2 = e^{\alpha x}\sin\beta x$$

Wronskian: $W = \beta e^{2\alpha x} \neq 0$. General solution:

$$y = e^{\alpha x}(c_1 \cos\beta x + c_2 \sin\beta x)$$

**Example:** $y'' + 2y' + 5y = 0$.

$r^2 + 2r + 5 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 20}}{2} = -1 \pm 2i$.

So $\alpha = -1$, $\beta = 2$:

$$y = e^{-x}(c_1 \cos 2x + c_2 \sin 2x)$$

This is a **damped oscillation**: amplitude decays like $e^{-x}$, oscillation frequency $2$.

### 3.5 Summary: The Three Cases

```
Discriminant Δ = b² - 4ac

        Δ > 0                 Δ = 0                 Δ < 0
    (two real roots)      (repeated root)       (complex roots)
          |                      |                      |
  y = c₁eʳ¹ˣ + c₂eʳ²ˣ   y = (c₁+c₂x)eʳˣ    y = eᵅˣ(c₁cosβx + c₂sinβx)
          |                      |                      |
   exponential growth     polynomial × exp      damped oscillation
```

## § 4 — The Nonhomogeneous Equation

### 4.1 The Structure Theorem

> **Theorem.** The general solution of $L[y] = g(x)$ is:
> 
> $$y = y_p + y_h = y_p + c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$$
> 
> where $y_p$ is **any particular solution** of $L[y] = g$, and $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$ is the general solution of the homogeneous equation $L[y] = 0$.

**Proof.** If $y_p$ and $y$ both solve $L[y] = g$, then $L[y - y_p] = L[y] - L[y_p] = g - g = 0$. So $y - y_p$ solves the homogeneous equation, hence $y - y_p = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$. $\blacksquare$

The problem reduces to: **(1)** find a fundamental set for the homogeneous part, **(2)** find any one particular solution.

### 4.2 Method of Undetermined Coefficients

Valid when the equation has **constant coefficients** and $g(x)$ is a **simple** function: polynomials, exponentials, sines, cosines, or products thereof.

**The rule:** Guess $y_p$ in the same "family" as $g(x)$.

|$g(x)$|Trial $y_p$|
|---|---|
|$P_n(x)$ (degree $n$ polynomial)|$x^s(A_n x^n + \cdots + A_1 x + A_0)$|
|$e^{\alpha x}$|$x^s A e^{\alpha x}$|
|$e^{\alpha x}\cos\beta x$ or $e^{\alpha x}\sin\beta x$|$x^s e^{\alpha x}(A\cos\beta x + B\sin\beta x)$|
|Sum of above|Sum of corresponding trials|

where $s = 0$ if the trial function is **not** a solution of the homogeneous equation, $s = 1$ if it is (simple resonance), $s = 2$ if it is a double solution (double resonance).

**Example 1 (no resonance):** $y'' - 3y' - 4y = 3e^{2x}$.

Characteristic roots: $r^2 - 3r - 4 = (r-4)(r+1) = 0$, so $r = 4, -1$.

$e^{2x}$ is **not** a solution of the homogeneous (since $2 \neq 4, -1$). Try $y_p = Ae^{2x}$:

$$4Ae^{2x} - 6Ae^{2x} - 4Ae^{2x} = 3e^{2x} \implies -6A = 3 \implies A = -\frac{1}{2}$$

$$y = c_1 e^{4x} + c_2 e^{-x} - \frac{1}{2}e^{2x}$$

**Example 2 (resonance):** $y'' - 4y = e^{2x}$.

Characteristic roots: $r = \pm 2$. Since $e^{2x}$ **is** a homogeneous solution ($r = 2$), multiply by $x$. Try $y_p = Axe^{2x}$:

$$y_p' = A(1 + 2x)e^{2x}, \quad y_p'' = A(4 + 4x)e^{2x}$$

$$A(4 + 4x)e^{2x} - 4Axe^{2x} = 4Ae^{2x} = e^{2x} \implies A = \frac{1}{4}$$

$$y = c_1 e^{2x} + c_2 e^{-2x} + \frac{x}{4}e^{2x}$$

> **Resonance** — when the forcing term matches a natural frequency of the homogeneous equation — produces the $xe^{rx}$ factor. In physical systems, this is **resonance**: forced oscillation at the natural frequency causes growing amplitude. Bridges collapse this way.

### 4.3 Variation of Parameters

This method works for **any** $g(x)$, even when the coefficients are not constant (as long as we have a fundamental set ${y_1, y_2}$).

**Setup:** Assume $y_p = u_1(x) y_1(x) + u_2(x) y_2(x)$ where $u_1, u_2$ are functions to be determined.

**The system (derived by imposing $u_1'y_1 + u_2'y_2 = 0$ and substituting):**

$$\begin{pmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \ y_1' & y_2' \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} u_1' \ u_2' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ g(x) \end{pmatrix}$$

By Cramer's rule (denominator is the Wronskian $W$):

$$u_1' = -\frac{y_2 g}{W}, \qquad u_2' = \frac{y_1 g}{W}$$

Integrating:

$$u_1 = -\int \frac{y_2(x) g(x)}{W(x)},dx, \qquad u_2 = \int \frac{y_1(x) g(x)}{W(x)},dx$$

**The particular solution:**

$$\boxed{y_p = -y_1 \int \frac{y_2 g}{W},dx + y_2 \int \frac{y_1 g}{W},dx}$$

**Why impose $u_1'y_1 + u_2'y_2 = 0$?** We have two unknowns $u_1, u_2$ and one equation (the ODE). This extra condition eliminates $u_1''$ and $u_2''$ from the second derivative of $y_p$, making the algebra tractable.

**Example:** $y'' + y = \sec x$, $x \in (-\pi/2, \pi/2)$.

Homogeneous: $r^2 + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \pm i$. So $y_1 = \cos x$, $y_2 = \sin x$, $W = 1$.

$$u_1' = -\frac{\sin x \cdot \sec x}{1} = -\tan x \implies u_1 = \ln|\cos x|$$

$$u_2' = \frac{\cos x \cdot \sec x}{1} = 1 \implies u_2 = x$$

$$y_p = \cos x \cdot \ln|\cos x| + x\sin x$$

$$y = c_1 \cos x + c_2 \sin x + \cos x \ln|\cos x| + x\sin x$$

Undetermined coefficients could never handle $\sec x$. Variation of parameters handles any $g$.

## § 5 — Initial Value Problems and Existence/Uniqueness

> **Theorem (Existence and Uniqueness).** Let $p, q, g$ be continuous on an open interval $I$ containing $x_0$. Then the IVP:
> 
> $$y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x), \quad y(x_0) = y_0,\ y'(x_0) = y_0'$$
> 
> has a **unique** solution on all of $I$.

This is profound: continuity of coefficients alone guarantees both existence and uniqueness on the **entire** interval — no restriction on the size of $y_0$ or $y_0'$.

**Example:** $y'' + (\tan x) y = 0$, $y(0) = 1$, $y'(0) = 0$.

Here $p(x) = \tan x$ is continuous on $(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$. The theorem guarantees a unique solution on this entire interval, even though we may not be able to write it in closed form.

## § 6 — The Vibrating Spring: The Physical Archetype

The equation:

$$m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t)$$

is a second order linear ODE with constant coefficients. Here:

- $m > 0$: mass
- $c \geq 0$: damping coefficient
- $k > 0$: spring constant
- $F(t)$: external forcing

Divide by $m$: $\ddot{x} + \frac{c}{m}\dot{x} + \frac{k}{m}x = \frac{F(t)}{m}$.

Let $\gamma = c/(2m)$ (damping), $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ (natural frequency). Characteristic roots:

$$r = -\gamma \pm \sqrt{\gamma^2 - \omega_0^2}$$

|Condition|Name|Behavior|
|---|---|---|
|$\gamma = 0$|Undamped|Pure oscillation at $\omega_0$: $x = c_1\cos\omega_0 t + c_2\sin\omega_0 t$|
|$0 < \gamma < \omega_0$|Underdamped|Decaying oscillation: $x = e^{-\gamma t}(c_1\cos\omega_d t + c_2\sin\omega_d t)$, $\omega_d = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \gamma^2}$|
|$\gamma = \omega_0$|Critically damped|Fastest decay without oscillation: $x = (c_1 + c_2 t)e^{-\gamma t}$|
|$\gamma > \omega_0$|Overdamped|Slow exponential decay, no oscillation|

```
  Amplitude
     |
     |  Underdamped         Critically damped     Overdamped
     |  /\/\/\__            /‾‾‾\_____            /‾‾‾‾‾‾‾‾___
     | /        ‾‾‾---___  /            ‾---___  /
     |/                   /                     /
     +---------------------------------> time
```

**Forced oscillation with $F(t) = F_0 \cos\omega t$:**

The particular solution when $\omega \neq \omega_0$:

$$x_p = \frac{F_0/m}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}\cos\omega t$$

As $\omega \to \omega_0$, the amplitude $\to \infty$. When $\omega = \omega_0$ exactly (with no damping), the particular solution is:

$$x_p = \frac{F_0}{2m\omega_0} t\sin\omega_0 t$$

The factor of $t$ causes **unbounded growth** — this is **resonance**. The Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940) collapsed due to aeroelastic flutter near a resonant frequency.

## § 7 — Euler–Cauchy Equations

A special variable-coefficient equation that reduces to constant coefficients:

$$x^2 y'' + \alpha x y' + \beta y = 0, \quad x > 0$$

**Key substitution:** $x = e^t$, i.e., $t = \ln x$. Then $x\frac{d}{dx} = \frac{d}{dt}$, and:

$$x^2 y'' = \frac{d^2 y}{dt^2} - \frac{dy}{dt}$$

The equation becomes: $\frac{d^2y}{dt^2} + (\alpha - 1)\frac{dy}{dt} + \beta y = 0$ — constant coefficients in $t$.

**Alternatively**, try $y = x^m$ directly. Then $y' = mx^{m-1}$, $y'' = m(m-1)x^{m-2}$:

$$x^2 \cdot m(m-1)x^{m-2} + \alpha x \cdot mx^{m-1} + \beta x^m = 0$$ $$x^m[m(m-1) + \alpha m + \beta] = 0$$

The **indicial equation**: $m^2 + (\alpha - 1)m + \beta = 0$.

**Three cases** (mirroring the constant coefficient theory):

- Two distinct real roots $m_1 \neq m_2$: $y = c_1 x^{m_1} + c_2 x^{m_2}$
- Repeated root $m_1 = m_2 = m$: $y = x^m(c_1 + c_2 \ln x)$
- Complex roots $m = \mu \pm i\nu$: $y = x^\mu[c_1 \cos(\nu \ln x) + c_2 \sin(\nu \ln x)]$

## § 8 — Series Solutions (Power Series Method)

When coefficients are not constant and the equation is not Euler–Cauchy, we use **power series**.

### 8.1 Ordinary Points

$x_0$ is an **ordinary point** of $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = 0$ if $p$ and $q$ are analytic at $x_0$.

**Theorem:** Near an ordinary point $x_0$, the equation has two linearly independent power series solutions:

$$y = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n (x - x_0)^n$$

converging in $|x - x_0| < R$ where $R$ is at least the distance from $x_0$ to the nearest singular point in $\mathbb{C}$.

**Example:** $y'' + xy' + y = 0$ about $x_0 = 0$.

Assume $y = \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n$. Then:

$$y'' = \sum_{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)a_n x^{n-2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2}x^n$$

$$xy' = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n a_n x^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty n a_n x^n$$

Substituting and collecting powers of $x^n$:

$$(n+2)(n+1)a_{n+2} + (n+1)a_n = 0 \implies a_{n+2} = -\frac{a_n}{n+2}$$

This **recurrence relation** gives all coefficients in terms of $a_0$ and $a_1$ (the two free parameters, corresponding to the two-dimensional solution space):

$$y = a_0\left(1 - \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^4}{8} - \cdots\right) + a_1\left(x - \frac{x^3}{6} + \cdots\right)$$

## § 9 — Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

> The reach of second order linear ODEs into modern AI is deeper than it first appears.

### 9.1 Neural ODEs and Second Order Dynamics

**Neural ODEs** (Chen et al., 2018) model continuous-depth networks as ODEs $\dot{\mathbf{h}} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}, t)$. First order — but limiting.

**Second order Neural ODEs** model acceleration, not just velocity:

$$\ddot{\mathbf{h}} + \gamma \dot{\mathbf{h}} + \omega^2 \mathbf{h} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}, t)$$

This is precisely the damped oscillator equation with a neural forcing term. These models:

- Better capture oscillatory dynamics in time series (physiological signals, physical simulations).
- Have **symplectic structure** when undamped, conserving energy — crucial for Hamiltonian dynamics.
- Are connected to **momentum methods** in optimization (see below).

### 9.2 Momentum-Based Optimizers: The ODE View

**Heavy ball method** (Polyak, 1964) and **Nesterov momentum** can be written as:

$$m\ddot{\theta} + c\dot{\theta} = -\nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

This is a **damped harmonic oscillator driven by the negative gradient**. The gradient is the "force," inertia ($m$) resists rapid direction changes, and damping ($c$) prevents oscillation.

The continuous-time limit of **Adam** is more subtle but still maps to a second order system with adaptive damping. The connection is:

|ODE Parameter|Optimizer Analogue|
|---|---|
|Mass $m$|Momentum coefficient $\beta_1$|
|Damping $c$|Learning rate schedule, $\beta_2$|
|$-\nabla\mathcal{L}$|Gradient signal|
|Natural frequency $\omega_0$|Curvature of the loss landscape|
|Resonance|Learning rate too large → divergence|
|Critical damping|Optimal convergence rate|

**Critical damping** in the ODE corresponds to the **optimal momentum coefficient** for a quadratic loss — the one that gives fastest convergence without oscillation. Sutskever et al. (2013) derived optimal Nesterov momentum via exactly this analogy.

The characteristic equation of the optimizer's ODE determines whether:

- $\Delta > 0$ (overdamped): slow convergence, no oscillation — too much damping.
- $\Delta = 0$ (critically damped): optimal convergence.
- $\Delta < 0$ (underdamped): oscillation around the minimum — can still converge but oscillates.

### 9.3 Attention and the Wave Equation

The wave equation $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$ is a second order linear PDE. It factors as $(∂_t - c\partial_x)(\partial_t + c\partial_x)u = 0$ — two first order operators.

**Transformer attention**, when interpreted geometrically, can be seen as learning operators that propagate information along characteristic curves — the solution curves of the wave equation. Formally connecting attention to hyperbolic PDEs is an active research area (e.g., neural wave operators for physical simulation).

### 9.4 Recurrent Neural Networks: Characteristic Roots and Stability

A linear RNN is $\mathbf{h}_t = A\mathbf{h}_{t-1} + B\mathbf{x}_t$. For a scalar second order linear recurrence:

$$h_t - b_1 h_{t-1} - b_2 h_{t-2} = x_t$$

This is the **discrete analogue** of a second order linear ODE. The **characteristic equation** is $r^2 - b_1 r - b_2 = 0$, and:

- If $|r_i| < 1$ for all roots: stable (exponential decay of homogeneous response).
- If $|r_i| > 1$: unstable (exponential blow-up).
- If $|r_i| = 1$: marginally stable (oscillation without decay).

**Vanishing and exploding gradients** in RNNs are directly caused by the characteristic roots of the recurrence being too small or too large. This is why **LSTMs** and **GRUs** were designed: they implicitly modulate the characteristic roots to keep them near the unit circle. The **HiPPO matrix** (Gu et al., 2020) and **S4 state space models** explicitly parameterize the characteristic polynomial to achieve stable, long-range memory — the Euler–Cauchy equation and Legendre polynomials appear directly in the construction.

### 9.5 Physics-Informed Neural Networks: Solving Second Order ODEs

PINNs solve ODEs like:

$$y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g(x), \quad y(x_0) = y_0,\ y'(x_0) = y_0'$$

by training a neural network $y_\theta(x)$ to minimize:

$$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{\int_I |y_\theta'' + p y_\theta' + q y_\theta - g|^2,dx}_{\text{PDE residual}} + \underbrace{|y_\theta(x_0) - y_0|^2 + |y_\theta'(x_0) - y_0'|^2}_{\text{IC residual}}$$

The theory guarantees a unique target solution exists (§5). The challenge is that neural networks must match **second derivatives** accurately — their approximation of $y''$ is less stable than of $y$ itself, and this is a core technical difficulty in PINN training.

### 9.6 Koopman Operator Theory and Linear Embeddings

The **Koopman operator** linearizes nonlinear dynamics. Given a nonlinear system $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = f(\mathbf{x})$, Koopman theory lifts the state to a (possibly infinite-dimensional) function space where the dynamics become **linear** — governed by a linear operator, whose eigenfunctions satisfy second order linear ODEs (or systems thereof).

**Deep learning** is used to approximate the Koopman eigenfunctions directly. When the Koopman operator has purely imaginary eigenvalues $\pm i\omega$, the eigenfunctions satisfy $\phi'' + \omega^2 \phi = 0$ — the simple harmonic oscillator. The eigenvalue spectrum of the Koopman operator determines the intrinsic frequencies of the nonlinear system, discovered via neural network regression.

|---|---|---|
|Not converting to standard form before applying theorems|The theorems assume $y'' + p(x)y' + q(x)y = g$ with leading coefficient 1|Always divide through by $P(x)$ first|
|Forgetting the $x^s$ multiplier in undetermined coefficients when resonance occurs|The trial function is a homogeneous solution, giving $0 = g(x)$ upon substitution|Check each piece of the trial against homogeneous solutions|
|Claiming $W \neq 0$ at one point implies independence everywhere|True for ODEs (by Abel's theorem), not for arbitrary functions|This is a special property of ODE solutions|
|Taking $c_1, c_2$ as functions in the general solution|The general solution formula $c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2 + y_p$ has **constant** $c_1, c_2$|Only variation of parameters uses functions $u_1(x), u_2(x)$|
|Applying undetermined coefficients to variable-coefficient equations|The method requires constant coefficients|Use variation of parameters or series methods|
|Ignoring the imaginary part in complex roots|$e^{(\alpha + i\beta)x}$ is a complex solution — the real solutions are $e^{\alpha x}\cos\beta x$ and $e^{\alpha x}\sin\beta x$|Always take real and imaginary parts separately|

## § 11 — Summary

### The Homogeneous Equation $L[y] = 0$:

- Solution space is a **2-dimensional vector space**.
- A **fundamental set** ${y_1, y_2}$ is any linearly independent pair of solutions ($W \neq 0$).
- **Abel's theorem:** $W(x) = W(x_0)e^{-\int p,dx}$ — either always zero or never zero.
- **General solution:** $y = c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2$.

### Constant Coefficients — Characteristic Roots:

$$\boxed{ar^2 + br + c = 0}$$

|$\Delta = b^2 - 4ac$|Roots|General Solution|
|---|---|---|
|$> 0$|$r_1 \neq r_2 \in \mathbb{R}$|$c_1e^{r_1 x} + c_2 e^{r_2 x}$|
|$= 0$|$r_1 = r_2 = r$|$(c_1 + c_2 x)e^{rx}$|
|$< 0$|$\alpha \pm i\beta$|$e^{\alpha x}(c_1\cos\beta x + c_2\sin\beta x)$|

### The Nonhomogeneous Equation $L[y] = g$:

$$y_{\text{general}} = \underbrace{y_p}_{\text{any particular solution}} + \underbrace{c_1 y_1 + c_2 y_2}_{\text{homogeneous general solution}}$$

- **Undetermined coefficients:** constant coefficients, simple $g(x)$. Fast but limited.
- **Variation of parameters:** any $g(x)$, any (solvable) homogeneous part. Always works.

$$y_p = -y_1 \int \frac{y_2 g}{W},dx + y_2 \int \frac{y_1 g}{W},dx$$
