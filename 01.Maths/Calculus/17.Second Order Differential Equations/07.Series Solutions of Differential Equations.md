

> _"When the equation resists closed form, we do not surrender — we expand. A power series is not an approximation. It is an exact representation, dressed in infinitely many terms."_

## § 0 — Personal Preamble

Every ODE we have solved so far has yielded solutions expressible in terms of elementary functions: exponentials, sinusoids, polynomials. But nature is not obliged to produce equations with such convenient solutions.

The equation $y'' - xy = 0$ (Airy's equation, governing light diffraction) has no solution in terms of elementary functions. Neither does $(1 - x^2)y'' - 2xy' + n(n+1)y = 0$ (Legendre's equation, governing gravitational and quantum potentials on spheres).

**Series solutions** are the systematic answer. We assume the solution is a power series, substitute into the ODE, and determine the coefficients recursively. The result is not an approximation — it is an _exact_ representation of the solution as a convergent infinite series.

This method is the gateway to the **special functions** of mathematical physics — Legendre polynomials, Bessel functions, Hermite polynomials — which are the eigenfunctions of quantum mechanics, the basis of modern signal processing, and the activation-function cousins of deep learning.

- Stewart, James. _Calculus_, 8th ed. — §17.4 (Series Solutions)

## § 1 — Review: Power Series

### 1.1 Definition

A **power series centered at $x = a$** is:

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n (x - a)^n = c_0 + c_1(x-a) + c_2(x-a)^2 + \cdots \tag{1.1}$$

We work almost exclusively with $a = 0$ (series centered at the origin):

$$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n x^n \tag{1.2}$$

### 1.2 Radius of Convergence

Every power series has a **radius of convergence** $R \in [0, \infty]$ such that the series:

- **Converges absolutely** for $|x - a| < R$
- **Diverges** for $|x - a| > R$
- May converge or diverge at the endpoints $x = a \pm R$

By the **ratio test**: $R = \lim_{n\to\infty} \left|\frac{c_n}{c_{n+1}}\right|$ (when this limit exists).

### 1.3 Term-by-Term Differentiation

If $y = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n$ converges on $|x| < R$, then on the same interval:

$$y' = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n c_n x^{n-1} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (n+1)c_{n+1} x^n \tag{1.3}$$

$$y'' = \sum_{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)c_n x^{n-2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (n+2)(n+1)c_{n+2} x^n \tag{1.4}$$

> **The re-indexing trick** — shifting the summation index so that all series start at $x^n$ with the same power $n$ — is the single most important algebraic manipulation in series solutions. Always re-index before matching coefficients.

### 1.4 Identity Principle

If $\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n = 0$ for all $x$ in an interval containing 0, then $a_n = 0$ for all $n$.

This is the key that unlocks series solutions: we force all coefficients to vanish, generating a **recurrence relation**.

## § 2 — Ordinary and Singular Points

The behavior of solutions near a point $x_0$ depends critically on the structure of the ODE there.

Write the ODE in **standard form**:

$$y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0 \tag{2.1}$$

> **Definition.** $x_0$ is an **ordinary point** of $(2.1)$ if $P(x)$ and $Q(x)$ are both analytic (representable as convergent power series) at $x_0$.

> **Definition.** $x_0$ is a **singular point** if it is not an ordinary point — i.e., at least one of $P, Q$ fails to be analytic at $x_0$.

### 2.1 Regular vs. Irregular Singular Points

At a singular point $x_0$, classify further:

> **Definition.** $x_0$ is a **regular singular point** if $(x - x_0)P(x)$ and $(x-x_0)^2 Q(x)$ are both analytic at $x_0$.

> **Definition.** $x_0$ is an **irregular singular point** otherwise.

**The Method of Frobenius** (§4) applies at regular singular points. Irregular singular points require more advanced methods (asymptotic expansions) beyond this note.

### 2.2 Examples of Classification

|ODE|$P(x)$|$Q(x)$|Singular points|Type|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|$y'' + xy' + y = 0$|$x$|$1$|None|All ordinary|
|$(1-x^2)y'' - 2xy' + 2y = 0$|$\frac{-2x}{1-x^2}$|$\frac{2}{1-x^2}$|$x = \pm 1$|Regular|
|$x^2 y'' + xy' + (x^2 - \nu^2)y = 0$|$1/x$|$1 - \nu^2/x^2$|$x = 0$|Regular|
|$x^3 y'' + y = 0$|$0$|$1/x^3$|$x = 0$|Irregular|

### 2.3 Theorem: Radius of Convergence Guarantee

> **Theorem.** If $x_0$ is an ordinary point of $(2.1)$, and $P(x), Q(x)$ have power series convergent for $|x - x_0| < R$, then every solution of $(2.1)$ has a power series representation convergent in the same disk $|x - x_0| < R$.

This theorem justifies the power series ansatz: we are guaranteed convergence at least up to the nearest singular point.

**Practical corollary:** The radius of convergence of the series solution is at least as large as the distance from $x_0$ to the nearest singular point in the complex plane.

## § 3 — Power Series Method at Ordinary Points

### 3.1 The Algorithm

**Step 1.** Verify $x_0 = 0$ is an ordinary point.

**Step 2.** Assume $y = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n$.

**Step 3.** Compute $y', y''$ and substitute into the ODE.

**Step 4.** Re-index all series so every term involves $x^n$.

**Step 5.** Collect terms at each power of $x^n$ and set the coefficient to zero (Identity Principle).

**Step 6.** Extract a **recurrence relation** giving $c_{n+k}$ in terms of earlier $c_j$.

**Step 7.** Determine $c_0, c_1$ (free parameters — they become the two arbitrary constants of the general solution). Compute subsequent coefficients from the recurrence.

**Step 8.** Identify the two linearly independent solutions $y_1, y_2$ (one involving $c_0$, the other $c_1$).

---

### 3.2 Example 1 — Airy's Equation: $y'' - xy = 0$

This governs light near a caustic — the bright line on the bottom of a swimming pool. No elementary solution exists.

**Step 1.** $P = 0$, $Q = -x$: analytic everywhere. $x = 0$ is ordinary.

**Step 2.** Let $y = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n$.

**Step 3.** $$y'' = \sum_{n=2}^\infty n(n-1)c_n x^{n-2}$$ $$xy = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^{n+1}$$

**Step 4.** Re-index $y''$: let $m = n-2$, so $n = m+2$: $$y'' = \sum_{m=0}^\infty (m+2)(m+1)c_{m+2} x^m$$

Re-index $xy$: let $m = n+1$, so $n = m-1$: $$xy = \sum_{m=1}^\infty c_{m-1} x^m$$

The ODE $y'' - xy = 0$ becomes: $$\sum_{m=0}^\infty (m+2)(m+1)c_{m+2} x^m - \sum_{m=1}^\infty c_{m-1}x^m = 0$$

**Step 5.** Separate the $m = 0$ term: $$m = 0: \quad 2 \cdot 1 \cdot c_2 = 0 \Rightarrow c_2 = 0$$

For $m \geq 1$: $$(m+2)(m+1)c_{m+2} - c_{m-1} = 0$$

**Recurrence relation:** $$\boxed{c_{m+2} = \frac{c_{m-1}}{(m+2)(m+1)}, \quad m \geq 1} \tag{3.1}$$

**Step 6.** This relates $c_{n+3}$ to $c_n$ (shift by 3). So $c_0, c_1, c_2$ are the "seeds," but $c_2 = 0$.

Free parameters: $c_0$ and $c_1$.

**Coefficients from $c_0$ (even-ish group):**

$m=1$: $c_3 = \frac{c_0}{3 \cdot 2} = \frac{c_0}{6}$

$m=4$: $c_6 = \frac{c_3}{6 \cdot 5} = \frac{c_0}{6 \cdot 30} = \frac{c_0}{180}$

$m=7$: $c_9 = \frac{c_6}{9 \cdot 8} = \frac{c_0}{180 \cdot 72} = \frac{c_0}{12960}$

**Coefficients from $c_1$:**

$m=2$: $c_4 = \frac{c_1}{4 \cdot 3} = \frac{c_1}{12}$

$m=5$: $c_7 = \frac{c_4}{7 \cdot 6} = \frac{c_1}{12 \cdot 42} = \frac{c_1}{504}$

All $c_n$ with $n \equiv 2 \pmod{3}$: zero (since $c_2 = 0$ and the recurrence propagates this).

**Two linearly independent solutions:**

$$y_1 = c_0!\left(1 + \frac{x^3}{6} + \frac{x^6}{180} + \frac{x^9}{12960} + \cdots\right)$$

$$y_2 = c_1!\left(x + \frac{x^4}{12} + \frac{x^7}{504} + \frac{x^{10}}{45360} + \cdots\right)$$

**General solution:** $y = c_0 y_1 + c_1 y_2$.

The functions $\text{Ai}(x)$ and $\text{Bi}(x)$ (Airy functions) are specific linear combinations of $y_1, y_2$ normalized by convention.

**Radius of convergence:** $R = \infty$ (no singular points anywhere).

### 3.4 Example 2 — Hermite's Equation: $y'' - 2xy' + 2ny = 0$

This is the ODE of the quantum harmonic oscillator. When $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$, the series solution **terminates** — giving a polynomial. These are the **Hermite polynomials**.

Let $y = \sum_{k=0}^\infty c_k x^k$.

$$y' = \sum_{k=1}^\infty kc_k x^{k-1}, \quad y'' = \sum_{k=2}^\infty k(k-1)c_k x^{k-2}$$

Substituting and re-indexing:

$$\sum_{k=0}^\infty (k+2)(k+1)c_{k+2}x^k - \sum_{k=0}^\infty 2kc_k x^k + \sum_{k=0}^\infty 2nc_k x^k = 0$$

Collecting $x^k$:

$$(k+2)(k+1)c_{k+2} + (2n - 2k)c_k = 0$$

**Recurrence:**

$$\boxed{c_{k+2} = \frac{2(k-n)}{(k+2)(k+1)}c_k} \tag{3.2}$$

**Key observation:** When $n$ is a non-negative integer, $c_{n+2} = \frac{2(n-n)}{(n+2)(n+1)} \cdot c_n = 0$.

And since $c_{k+2}$ depends only on $c_k$, this zero propagates: $c_{n+4} = c_{n+6} = \cdots = 0$.

The series **terminates** at $x^n$ — giving a polynomial of degree $n$.

**The Hermite polynomials** (choosing $c_0$ or $c_1$ to give standard normalization):

$$H_0(x) = 1, \quad H_1(x) = 2x, \quad H_2(x) = 4x^2 - 2$$ $$H_3(x) = 8x^3 - 12x, \quad H_4(x) = 16x^4 - 48x^2 + 12$$

These are the **wavefunctions of the quantum harmonic oscillator** (up to a Gaussian factor $e^{-x^2/2}$).

### 3.5 Example 3 — A Simple Nonstandard Equation

$$y'' + xy' + y = 0, \quad y(0) = 1, ; y'(0) = 0$$

$P = x$, $Q = 1$: ordinary everywhere.

Let $y = \sum c_n x^n$:

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty (n+2)(n+1)c_{n+2}x^n + \sum_{n=0}^\infty nc_n x^n + \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n = 0$$

$$\sum_{n=0}^\infty \left[(n+2)(n+1)c_{n+2} + (n+1)c_n\right]x^n = 0$$

**Recurrence:**

$$c_{n+2} = \frac{-(n+1)c_n}{(n+2)(n+1)} = \frac{-c_n}{n+2} \tag{3.3}$$

ICs: $c_0 = y(0) = 1$, $c_1 = y'(0) = 0$.

Since $c_1 = 0$: all odd coefficients vanish.

Even coefficients: $$c_2 = -\frac{c_0}{2} = -\frac{1}{2}$$ $$c_4 = -\frac{c_2}{4} = \frac{1}{8}$$ $$c_6 = -\frac{c_4}{6} = -\frac{1}{48}$$

$$y = 1 - \frac{x^2}{2} + \frac{x^4}{8} - \frac{x^6}{48} + \cdots = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n}{2^n \cdot n!}x^{2n}$$

**Recognition:** This is $e^{-x^2/2}$ — check: $\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-x^2/2)^n}{n!} = \sum \frac{(-1)^n}{2^n n!}x^{2n}$. ✓

The series solution has recognized the Gaussian — a function whose series representation is more illuminating than its closed form.

## § 4 — The Method of Frobenius (Regular Singular Points)

At a **regular singular point**, the power series method fails because we cannot guarantee analytic solutions. Instead, we look for solutions of the form:

$$y = x^r \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^{n+r}, \quad c_0 \neq 0 \tag{4.1}$$

where $r$ is a (possibly non-integer, possibly complex) number to be determined. This is a **Frobenius series** or **generalized power series**.

### 4.1 The Indicial Equation

Consider $(2.1)$ with regular singular point at $x = 0$. Multiply through by $x^2$:

$$x^2 y'' + x\cdot[xP(x)]\cdot y' + [x^2 Q(x)]\cdot y = 0$$

Since $xP(x)$ and $x^2 Q(x)$ are analytic at 0:

$$xP(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty p_n x^n, \quad x^2 Q(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty q_n x^n$$

Substituting the Frobenius ansatz $y = \sum c_n x^{n+r}$ and collecting the **lowest power** $x^r$ (from $n = 0$):

$$c_0[r(r-1) + p_0 r + q_0] = 0$$

Since $c_0 \neq 0$, we must have:

$$\boxed{r(r-1) + p_0 r + q_0 = 0} \tag{4.2}$$

This is the **indicial equation** — a quadratic in $r$ whose roots $r_1, r_2$ (with $\text{Re}(r_1) \geq \text{Re}(r_2)$) determine the form of the two linearly independent solutions.

### 4.2 Three Cases for the Solutions

Let $\rho = r_1 - r_2$.

**Case 1: $\rho \notin \mathbb{Z}$ (roots do not differ by an integer)**

Two independent Frobenius series: $$y_1 = x^{r_1}\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n, \quad y_2 = x^{r_2}\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n x^n$$

**Case 2: $\rho = 0$ (repeated root $r_1 = r_2 = r$)**

$$y_1 = x^r \sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n$$ $$y_2 = y_1 \ln x + x^r \sum_{n=1}^\infty b_n x^n$$

The $\ln x$ term is unavoidable — it is forced by the repeated root, analogous to repeated roots giving $te^{rt}$ in constant-coefficient equations.

**Case 3: $\rho \in \mathbb{Z}^+$ (roots differ by a positive integer)**

$$y_1 = x^{r_1}\sum_{n=0}^\infty a_n x^n$$ $$y_2 = cy_1\ln x + x^{r_2}\sum_{n=0}^\infty b_n x^n$$

The constant $c$ may or may not be zero — it must be determined from the recurrence. If $c = 0$, the second solution is also a pure Frobenius series.

### 4.3 Example — Bessel's Equation of Order $\nu$

$$x^2 y'' + xy' + (x^2 - \nu^2)y = 0, \quad \nu \geq 0 \tag{4.3}$$

This governs vibrating circular membranes (drums), heat diffusion in cylinders, and wave propagation in cylindrical coordinates. It is arguably the most important ODE in classical physics.

**Standard form:** $y'' + \frac{1}{x}y' + \left(1 - \frac{\nu^2}{x^2}\right)y = 0$.

$P = 1/x$, $Q = 1 - \nu^2/x^2$: $x = 0$ is a regular singular point.

$xP(x) = 1 \Rightarrow p_0 = 1$, $x^2 Q(x) = x^2 - \nu^2 \Rightarrow q_0 = -\nu^2$.

**Indicial equation:** $r(r-1) + r - \nu^2 = 0 \Rightarrow r^2 - \nu^2 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \pm\nu$.

So $r_1 = \nu$, $r_2 = -\nu$, $\rho = 2\nu$.

**Case $\nu \notin \mathbb{Z}$ (non-integer order):** Both Frobenius series work independently.

Let $y = \sum_{n=0}^\infty c_n x^{n+r}$ with $r = \nu$. Substituting into $(4.3)$:

The recurrence (after careful computation) is:

$$c_n = \frac{-c_{n-2}}{n(n + 2\nu)}, \quad n \geq 2; \qquad c_1 = 0$$

All odd coefficients vanish. For even $n = 2m$:

$$c_{2m} = \frac{(-1)^m c_0}{m!,\Gamma(\nu + m + 1) \cdot 2^{2m}}$$

(using the Gamma function to handle non-integer $\nu$). Choosing $c_0 = \frac{1}{2^\nu \Gamma(\nu+1)}$:

$$\boxed{J_\nu(x) = \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^m}{m!,\Gamma(\nu + m + 1)}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^{2m+\nu}} \tag{4.4}$$

This is the **Bessel function of the first kind of order $\nu$**. It is one of the most important functions in all of applied mathematics.

**For integer $\nu = n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$:**

$$J_n(x) = \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^m}{m!,(m+n)!}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^{2m+n} \tag{4.5}$$

**Special cases:**

$$J_0(x) = 1 - \frac{x^2}{4} + \frac{x^4}{64} - \frac{x^6}{2304} + \cdots$$

$$J_1(x) = \frac{x}{2} - \frac{x^3}{16} + \frac{x^5}{384} - \cdots$$

### 4.5 Example — Legendre's Equation: $(1-x^2)y'' - 2xy' + n(n+1)y = 0$

In standard form: $y'' - \frac{2x}{1-x^2}y' + \frac{n(n+1)}{1-x^2}y = 0$.

Singular points at $x = \pm 1$. Point $x = 0$ is ordinary.

Power series method (at $x = 0$) gives recurrence:

$$c_{k+2} = -\frac{(n-k)(n+k+1)}{(k+2)(k+1)}c_k \tag{4.6}$$

When $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$: the series **terminates** at $k = n$ (since $c_{n+2} = 0$), giving a polynomial.

The **Legendre polynomials** (normalized by $P_n(1) = 1$):

$$P_0(x) = 1, \quad P_1(x) = x, \quad P_2(x) = \frac{1}{2}(3x^2 - 1)$$ $$P_3(x) = \frac{1}{2}(5x^3 - 3x), \quad P_4(x) = \frac{1}{8}(35x^4 - 30x^2 + 3)$$

**Orthogonality:** $$\int_{-1}^1 P_m(x)P_n(x),dx = \frac{2}{2n+1}\delta_{mn} \tag{4.7}$$

The Legendre polynomials form an **orthogonal basis** for $L^2[-1,1]$ — they are the analogue of Fourier modes for the interval $[-1,1]$.

They appear in the solution of Laplace's equation in spherical coordinates (gravitational and electric potentials), quantum mechanical angular momentum eigenfunctions (spherical harmonics), and approximation theory.

## § 5 — The Recurrence Relation as Algorithm

The recurrence relation is the heart of the method. It is always a linear relation, often of the form:

$$c_{n+k} = f(n), c_n \quad \text{or} \quad c_{n+k} = f(n), c_n + g(n), c_{n-j}$$

**Generating coefficients systematically:**

For the Airy equation recurrence $c_{m+3} = \frac{c_m}{(m+3)(m+2)}$ (rewriting 3.1):

```
Dependency graph:

c₀ ──→ c₃ ──→ c₆ ──→ c₉ ──→ ...   (y₁ branch)
c₁ ──→ c₄ ──→ c₇ ──→ c₁₀ ──→ ...  (y₂ branch)
c₂ = 0 ──→ c₅=0 ──→ c₈=0 ──→ ...  (zero branch)

The three families are decoupled.
Two free parameters c₀, c₁ → two independent solutions.
```

This dependency graph structure is general: the recurrence has "step" $k$ means indices split into $k$ independent families, each seeded by a different $c_j$ ($0 \leq j < k$).

## § 6 — Convergence Analysis

### 6.1 The Key Theorem (Restated Sharply)

> **Theorem.** If $x_0$ is an ordinary point of $y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0$, then the general solution is: $$y = c_0 y_1(x) + c_1 y_2(x)$$ where $y_1, y_2$ are power series centered at $x_0$, convergent at least for $|x - x_0| < R$, where $R$ is the distance from $x_0$ to the nearest singularity of $P$ or $Q$ in the **complex plane**.

> **Why the complex plane?** The equation $y'' + \frac{1}{1+x^2}y = 0$ has $P = 0$, $Q = (1+x^2)^{-1}$ — analytic on all of $\mathbb{R}$. Yet the radius of convergence of the series solution at $x_0 = 0$ is $R = 1$, because the singular points $x = \pm i$ (in $\mathbb{C}$) are distance 1 from the origin.

### 6.2 Ratio Test for Bessel Functions

For $J_0(x) = \sum_{m=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^m}{(m!)^2}\left(\frac{x}{2}\right)^{2m}$:

$$\left|\frac{a_{m+1}}{a_m}\right| = \frac{x^2}{4(m+1)^2} \to 0 \text{ as } m \to \infty$$

So $R = \infty$ — $J_0$ converges everywhere. This is expected: Bessel's equation has its only singular point at $x = 0$, and $J_0$ is the solution regular at $x = 0$.

## § 7 — Worked Example: Full Frobenius Solution

### Euler's Equation as a Test Case

$$x^2 y'' + \alpha xy' + \beta y = 0 \tag{7.1}$$

$x = 0$ is a regular singular point. $xP = \alpha$, $x^2 Q = \beta$, so $p_0 = \alpha$, $q_0 = \beta$.

**Indicial equation:** $r(r-1) + \alpha r + \beta = 0$.

With the Frobenius ansatz $y = x^r \sum c_n x^n$:

Substituting, the $n$-th term gives: $$[(r+n)(r+n-1) + \alpha(r+n) + \beta],c_n = 0$$

For $n \geq 1$: the bracket is $(r+n)(r+n-1) + \alpha(r+n) + \beta$. This is the indicial polynomial evaluated at $r + n$ — and since $r$ is a root of the indicial equation, $r + n$ (for $n \geq 1$) need not be a root, so the bracket $\neq 0$, forcing $c_n = 0$ for all $n \geq 1$.

The Frobenius series **collapses to a single term**: $y = c_0 x^r$.

This recovers the known result: Euler's equation always has solutions $y = x^r$. The Frobenius method is consistent. $\blacksquare$

## § 8 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 8.1 Hermite Polynomials and Probabilistic ML

The Hermite polynomials $H_n(x)$ from §3.4 are orthogonal with respect to the Gaussian weight $e^{-x^2}$: $$\int_{-\infty}^\infty H_m(x)H_n(x)e^{-x^2},dx = \sqrt{\pi},2^n n!,\delta_{mn}$$

In **probabilistic machine learning**:

- **Gaussian processes**: the covariance functions of stationary GPs can be expanded in Hermite polynomials — this gives the connection between GP kernels and frequency-domain representations
- **Score-based generative models** (diffusion models, DDPM): the score function $\nabla_x \log p_t(x)$ for a Gaussian prior satisfies a Hermite-polynomial expansion
- **Gauss-Hermite quadrature**: numerical integration of expectations under Gaussian distributions (used in variational inference, Laplace approximation) is based on the roots of $H_n(x)$

The quantum harmonic oscillator wavefunctions $\psi_n(x) = H_n(x)e^{-x^2/2}$ are precisely the **basis functions** that diagonalize the quantum number operator — analogous to how Fourier modes diagonalize translation-invariant operators, and how eigenvectors diagonalize weight matrices in linear neural networks.

### 8.2 Bessel Functions and Graph Neural Networks

Bessel functions appear naturally on **graphs and manifolds**. The graph Laplacian $\Delta_G$ has eigenfunctions that, on regular lattices, reduce to discrete Fourier modes. On **radially symmetric graphs** (like ego networks), the eigenfunctions of the Laplacian are discrete analogues of Bessel functions.

**Spectral Graph Convolutional Networks** (Kipf & Welling, 2017) use the eigenfunctions of the graph Laplacian as the basis for convolution. The "spectral" filters are essentially polynomial approximations to Bessel-type functions. The **ChebNet** approximation uses Chebyshev polynomials (themselves solutions to a Sturm-Liouville ODE) to avoid explicit eigendecomposition.

### 8.3 Legendre Polynomials and Positional Encoding

The **HiPPO framework** (Gu et al., 2020) for long-range sequence modeling — which underlies the S4 and Mamba architectures — computes online approximations to signal history using **Legendre polynomial projections**:

$$u(t) \approx \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} c_n(t) P_n(\tau)$$

where $c_n(t)$ satisfies a linear ODE that can be updated in $O(N)$ per step. The optimal $A$ matrix in state-space models comes directly from the ODE recurrence $(4.6)$ for Legendre polynomials.

This is series solutions of ODEs embedded into the architecture of a language model.

### 8.4 The Neural ODE as a Series Solution

A Neural ODE $\dot{\mathbf{h}} = f(\mathbf{h}, t, \theta)$ with small $t$ can be expanded via the **Picard iteration** / **Magnus expansion**:

$$\mathbf{h}(t) = \mathbf{h}(0) + t f(\mathbf{h}(0), 0) + \frac{t^2}{2}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} + f \cdot \nabla_h f\right) + \cdots$$

This is a **Taylor series in $t$** — exactly the power series method applied to a nonlinear ODE. The truncated Magnus expansion gives the **ResNet** (each term = one residual block). A full series solution = infinitely many layers = continuous depth.

### 8.5 Special Functions as Activation Functions

The standard neural network activation functions are non-analytic at most points. But **polynomial network** architectures use:

- **Hermite activations**: $\sigma(x) = H_n(x)e^{-x^2/2}$ — guaranteed orthogonal feature maps under Gaussian input distributions. This directly minimizes feature correlation in layers receiving Gaussian-distributed inputs.
- **Legendre activations** on bounded domains: $\sigma(x) = P_n(x)$ for $x \in [-1,1]$ — used in **FNO** (Fourier Neural Operator) and related PDE-solving architectures.

The recurrence relations for these polynomials (e.g., $(n+1)P_{n+1} = (2n+1)xP_n - nP_{n-1}$) allow efficient computation of all activation values up to order $N$ in $O(N)$ operations — making them computationally competitive with ReLU networks.

### 8.6 Airy Functions and Phase Transitions in Learning

The Airy function $\text{Ai}(x)$ describes the **edge of a semicircle law** in random matrix theory. The empirical spectral distribution of a random weight matrix $W/\sqrt{n}$ converges to the Marchenko-Pastur law, and fluctuations at the spectral edge are governed by the **Tracy-Widom distribution** — whose density involves $\text{Ai}(x)$.

This matters for deep learning initialization theory: the distribution of singular values of weight matrices at initialization, and how these evolve during training, follows random matrix universality. The Airy-governed edge controls how information propagates through very deep networks — too close to the edge and gradients explode; too far and they vanish.
