

> _"Where undetermined coefficients asks 'what shape should the answer be?', variation of parameters asks 'what if the constants were allowed to breathe?' — and in that breath, the entire method is born."_

---

## § 0 — Personal Preamble

The method of undetermined coefficients is elegant but narrow — it demands that $g(x)$ belong to a privileged family of functions closed under differentiation. The moment $g(x) = \ln x$ or $g(x) = \sec x$, the earlier method collapses.

**Variation of parameters** (due to Lagrange, 1774) is the universal answer. Its central idea is strikingly bold: take the complementary solution $y_c = C_1 y_1 + C_2 y_2$ — which works because $C_1, C_2$ are _constants_ — and ask: _what if they weren't?_ What if $C_1$ and $C_2$ were allowed to be functions of $x$?

This is not a trick. It is a systematic exploitation of the degrees of freedom that the homogeneous solution leaves behind.

**References:**

- Stewart, James. _Calculus_, 8th ed. — §17.3
- MIT OCW 18.02 — ODE supplementary notes (Haynes Miller)

---

## § 1 — The Setting

We work with the **second-order linear ODE** in standard form:

$$y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = g(x) \tag{1}$$

> **Standard form is essential.** If the equation is $ay'' + by' + cy = g$, divide through by $a$ first. The Wronskian formula below depends on the leading coefficient being 1.

Assume $P, Q, g$ are continuous on an interval $I$. Let $y_1, y_2$ be two **linearly independent solutions** of the associated homogeneous equation:

$$y'' + P(x)y' + Q(x)y = 0 \tag{2}$$

So $y_c = C_1 y_1 + C_2 y_2$.

---

## § 2 — The Core Idea: Letting the Constants Vary

**Ansatz.** Seek a particular solution of the form:

$$y_p = u_1(x), y_1(x) + u_2(x), y_2(x) \tag{3}$$

where $u_1, u_2$ are _unknown functions_ to be determined. This replaces the constants $C_1, C_2$ with functions — hence the name.

We have **two unknown functions** but only **one equation** to satisfy. This gives us freedom to impose an additional constraint. We choose it to simplify the algebra.

---

## § 3 — Derivation (Full)

Differentiate $y_p$:

$$y_p' = u_1' y_1 + u_1 y_1' + u_2' y_2 + u_2 y_2'$$

**Impose the constraint:**

$$\boxed{u_1' y_1 + u_2' y_2 = 0} \tag{I}$$

This eliminates the $u_1', u_2'$ terms from $y_p'$, giving:

$$y_p' = u_1 y_1' + u_2 y_2'$$

Differentiate again:

$$y_p'' = u_1' y_1' + u_1 y_1'' + u_2' y_2' + u_2 y_2''$$

Substitute $y_p, y_p', y_p''$ into equation $(1)$:

$$\bigl(u_1' y_1' + u_1 y_1'' + u_2' y_2' + u_2 y_2''\bigr) + P(u_1 y_1' + u_2 y_2') + Q(u_1 y_1 + u_2 y_2) = g$$

Regroup by collecting $u_1$ and $u_2$ terms:

$$u_1\underbrace{(y_1'' + Py_1' + Qy_1)}_{=,0} + u_2\underbrace{(y_2'' + Py_2' + Qy_2)}_{=,0} + u_1'y_1' + u_2'y_2' = g$$

Since $y_1, y_2$ solve the homogeneous equation, both bracketed expressions vanish. We are left with:

$$\boxed{u_1' y_1' + u_2' y_2' = g} \tag{II}$$

---

## § 4 — The Linear System and the Wronskian

Equations (I) and (II) form a $2 \times 2$ linear system for the unknowns $u_1'$ and $u_2'$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \ y_1' & y_2' \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} u_1' \ u_2' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ g \end{pmatrix}$$

The coefficient matrix is exactly the **Wronskian matrix**, and its determinant is the **Wronskian**:

$$W = W(y_1, y_2)(x) = \begin{vmatrix} y_1 & y_2 \ y_1' & y_2' \end{vmatrix} = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1'$$

> **Abel's Theorem.** If $y_1, y_2$ are solutions of $(2)$, then: $$W(x) = W(x_0), \exp!\left(-\int_{x_0}^x P(t), dt\right)$$ In particular, $W$ is either identically zero or never zero on $I$. Since $y_1, y_2$ are linearly independent, $W \neq 0$ on $I$. So the system is always uniquely solvable.

By **Cramer's Rule**:

$$u_1' = \frac{\begin{vmatrix} 0 & y_2 \ g & y_2' \end{vmatrix}}{W} = \frac{-y_2 g}{W} \tag{4}$$

$$u_2' = \frac{\begin{vmatrix} y_1 & 0 \ y_1' & g \end{vmatrix}}{W} = \frac{y_1 g}{W} \tag{5}$$

Integrate to obtain $u_1$ and $u_2$ (no constants of integration — those belong to $y_c$):

$$u_1 = -\int \frac{y_2(x), g(x)}{W(x)}, dx, \qquad u_2 = \int \frac{y_1(x), g(x)}{W(x)}, dx \tag{6}$$

**The particular solution is:**

$$\boxed{y_p = -y_1 \int \frac{y_2, g}{W}, dx ;+; y_2 \int \frac{y_1, g}{W}, dx} \tag{7}$$

---

## § 5 — Existence and Uniqueness (Theoretical Note)

> **Theorem.** Let $P, Q, g$ be continuous on an open interval $I$, and let $y_1, y_2$ be linearly independent solutions of the homogeneous equation on $I$. Then formula $(7)$ yields a particular solution $y_p$ of $(1)$ defined on all of $I$, and the general solution is $y = C_1 y_1 + C_2 y_2 + y_p$.

This is a global existence result — not just local. The continuity of $g$ (not a polynomial or exponential, but _any_ continuous function) is all that is required.

This is the fundamental superiority of variation of parameters over undetermined coefficients.

---

## § 6 — Algorithm Summary

**Step 1.** Write the ODE in **standard form**: $y'' + Py' + Qy = g$.

**Step 2.** Solve the homogeneous equation to find $y_1, y_2$ (linearly independent).

**Step 3.** Compute the Wronskian: $W = y_1 y_2' - y_2 y_1'$.

**Step 4.** Compute: $$u_1 = -\int \frac{y_2 g}{W}, dx, \qquad u_2 = \int \frac{y_1 g}{W}, dx$$

**Step 5.** Write $y_p = u_1 y_1 + u_2 y_2$.

**Step 6.** General solution: $y = C_1 y_1 + C_2 y_2 + y_p$.

> **Watch:** Always divide by $a$ before computing $g$. If the original equation is $2y'' + y' - y = e^x\ln x$, then $g(x) = \frac{e^x \ln x}{2}$, not $e^x \ln x$.

## § 7 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — The Classic: $g(x) = \sec x$

$$y'' + y = \sec x$$

This is impossible for undetermined coefficients. $\sec x$ is not closed under differentiation.

**Step 1.** Already in standard form: $P = 0$, $Q = 1$, $g = \sec x$.

**Step 2.** Homogeneous: $r^2 + 1 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \pm i$. $$y_1 = \cos x, \quad y_2 = \sin x$$

**Step 3.** Wronskian: $$W = \cos x \cdot \cos x - \sin x \cdot (-\sin x) = \cos^2 x + \sin^2 x = 1$$

**Step 4.** $$u_1 = -\int \frac{\sin x \cdot \sec x}{1}, dx = -\int \frac{\sin x}{\cos x}, dx = -\int \tan x, dx = \ln|\cos x|$$

$$u_2 = \int \frac{\cos x \cdot \sec x}{1}, dx = \int 1, dx = x$$

**Step 5.** $$y_p = \cos x \cdot \ln|\cos x| + x \sin x$$

**General solution:** $$\boxed{y = C_1 \cos x + C_2 \sin x + \cos x \ln|\cos x| + x \sin x}$$

No undetermined coefficients method could produce this. The $\ln|\cos x|$ is qualitatively different from any function in the admissible table.

---

### Example 2 — Polynomial Coefficient: $g(x) = x^{-1}e^x$

$$y'' - 2y' + y = \frac{e^x}{x}, \quad x > 0$$

**Homogeneous:** $(r-1)^2 = 0$, repeated root $r = 1$. $$y_1 = e^x, \quad y_2 = xe^x$$

**Wronskian:** $$W = e^x(e^x + xe^x) - xe^x \cdot e^x = e^{2x} + xe^{2x} - xe^{2x} = e^{2x}$$

**Compute $u_1, u_2$:** $$u_1 = -\int \frac{xe^x \cdot \frac{e^x}{x}}{e^{2x}}, dx = -\int \frac{xe^{2x}}{xe^{2x}}, dx = -\int 1, dx = -x$$

$$u_2 = \int \frac{e^x \cdot \frac{e^x}{x}}{e^{2x}}, dx = \int \frac{1}{x}, dx = \ln x$$

**Particular solution:** $$y_p = (-x)e^x + (\ln x)(xe^x) = xe^x(\ln x - 1)$$

**General solution:** $$\boxed{y = C_1 e^x + C_2 xe^x + xe^x(\ln x - 1)}$$

> **Observe:** We can absorb the $-xe^x$ term into $C_2 xe^x$ by redefining $C_2' = C_2 - 1$, so effectively $y_p = xe^x \ln x$.

---

### Example 3 — Trigonometric Forcing with Non-Constant Coefficients

$$y'' + 4y = \csc(2x), \quad x \in (0, \pi/2)$$

**Homogeneous:** $r^2 + 4 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \pm 2i$. $$y_1 = \cos(2x), \quad y_2 = \sin(2x)$$

**Wronskian:** $$W = \cos(2x) \cdot 2\cos(2x) - \sin(2x) \cdot (-2\sin(2x)) = 2(\cos^2(2x) + \sin^2(2x)) = 2$$

**Compute $u_1, u_2$:** $$u_1 = -\int \frac{\sin(2x)\csc(2x)}{2}, dx = -\int \frac{1}{2}, dx = -\frac{x}{2}$$

$$u_2 = \int \frac{\cos(2x)\csc(2x)}{2}, dx = \int \frac{\cos(2x)}{2\sin(2x)}, dx = \frac{1}{4}\ln|\sin(2x)|$$

**Particular solution:** $$y_p = -\frac{x}{2}\cos(2x) + \frac{1}{4}\sin(2x)\ln|\sin(2x)|$$

**General solution:** $$\boxed{y = C_1\cos(2x) + C_2\sin(2x) - \frac{x}{2}\cos(2x) + \frac{1}{4}\sin(2x)\ln|\sin(2x)|}$$

---

### Example 4 — Verification that $W$ Never Vanishes (Abel's Theorem Check)

For $y'' - 2y' + y = 0$, $P(x) = -2$. Abel says: $$W(x) = W(0),e^{-\int_0^x (-2),dt} = W(0),e^{2x}$$

With $y_1 = e^x$, $y_2 = xe^x$: $W(0) = e^0(e^0 + 0) - 0 = 1$.

So $W(x) = e^{2x}$ — consistent with our direct computation. Abel's theorem is not just theoretical; it is a computational shortcut.

## § 8 — Visualization: What "Variation" Really Means

Consider $y_c = C_1\cos x + C_2\sin x$ — a family of circles in the phase plane $(y, y')$.

When we promote $C_1 \to u_1(x)$ and $C_2 \to u_2(x)$:

```
Phase Plane (y, y')
                    .  .  .
               .              .
           .      y_c circle      .
         .                          .
        .                            .
        .    y_p spirals outward     .  <-- u_1(x), u_2(x) changing
        .    as x increases          .
         .                          .
           .                      .
               .              .
                    .  .  .

The "constants" C_1, C_2 are now moving along the circle
as x changes — the solution point traces a DEFORMED path
that is no longer periodic, tracking g(x).
```

More precisely: at each $x$, the point $(u_1(x), u_2(x))$ traces a curve in the $(u_1, u_2)$-plane, driven by equations (4) and (5). The constraint (I) — $u_1'y_1 + u_2'y_2 = 0$ — forces this motion to be **perpendicular to $(y_1, y_2)^T$** at each step, keeping the first-derivative formula clean.

## § 9 — Green's Function Interpretation

The variation of parameters formula $(7)$ can be written as a convolution:

$$y_p(x) = \int_{x_0}^{x} G(x, t), g(t), dt$$

where the **Green's function** is:

$$G(x, t) = \frac{y_1(t)y_2(x) - y_2(t)y_1(x)}{W(t)}$$

> **Why this matters.** $G(x, t)$ is the _response_ at position $x$ due to a _unit impulse_ at position $t$. The particular solution is the superposition (integral) of all these impulse responses over the domain.

This is the ODE version of a fundamental principle: **linear systems respond to general inputs by convolving with their impulse response.** This connects directly to:

- Fourier and Laplace transform methods
- Signal processing (the ODE operator as a linear filter)
- Deep learning (attention as learned kernel convolution)

## § 10 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 10.1 — Variation of Parameters as the Blueprint for Attention

The Green's function representation: $$y_p(x) = \int G(x, t), g(t), dt$$

is structurally identical to the **attention mechanism** in Transformers:

$$\text{Attention}(Q, K, V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

In both cases:

- A **kernel** ($G(x,t)$ or the attention weight $\text{softmax}(QK^T/\sqrt{d_k})$) measures the "relevance" of source position $t$ to target position $x$
- The **output** is a weighted integral/sum over the source
- The system is **linear** in the values ($g(t)$ or $V$)

The fundamental difference: $G(x,t)$ is analytically derived from the ODE; the attention kernel is _learned_. Variation of parameters is the mathematical archetype of which attention is a learned, nonlinear generalization.

### 10.2 — Impulse Response and Convolutional Neural Networks

The Green's function is the **impulse response** of the differential operator $L$. This maps directly to CNNs:

|ODE / Variation of Parameters|CNN|
|---|---|
|Operator $L$ (linear, shift-invariant if const. coeff.)|Convolutional layer|
|Green's function $G(x,t)$|Learned kernel/filter|
|Particular solution $y_p = G * g$|Feature map = kernel $*$ input|
|Superposition principle|Linearity of convolution|

When $P, Q$ are constants (constant-coefficient ODE), $G(x, t) = G(x - t)$ — the Green's function depends only on the _difference_ $x - t$. This is **shift invariance** — the defining property of convolution and the motivation for weight sharing in CNNs.

### 10.3 — Neural ODEs and the Adjoint Method

In Neural ODEs, training requires computing gradients of a loss $\mathcal{L}$ with respect to parameters $\theta$. The **adjoint method** solves a _backwards ODE_:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{a}}{dt} = -\mathbf{a}(t)^T \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{h}}$$

where $\mathbf{a}(t) = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}(t)}$ is the adjoint (co-state). This backward ODE is solved by variation of parameters: the adjoint is a particular solution with the "forcing" coming from the gradient of the loss.

Every time you train a Neural ODE, the backpropagation _is_ variation of parameters applied to the adjoint system.

### 10.4 — Kernel Methods and Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Spaces

The Green's function $G(x, t)$ of a self-adjoint ODE operator $L$ is a **positive definite kernel** — it satisfies: $$\sum_{i,j} c_i c_j G(x_i, x_j) \geq 0$$

This makes it a valid kernel for a **Reproducing Kernel Hilbert Space (RKHS)**. Kernel SVMs, Gaussian Processes, and kernel regression all operate in RKHSs. The "kernel trick" in ML — replacing dot products with kernel evaluations — is, for differential operators, exactly the Green's function.

Gaussian Process regression with a Matérn kernel is equivalent to Bayesian inference for a specific ODE with a stochastic forcing term — the posterior mean is the variation of parameters solution, and the posterior variance encodes uncertainty about $g$.

### 10.5 — PINNs: Learning $g$ When It's Unknown

In Physics-Informed Neural Networks, one scenario is **inverse problems**: $g(x)$ is unknown and must be inferred from observations of $y$. The forward map $g \mapsto y_p$ is precisely variation of parameters (formula 7). Learning $g$ from data is inverting this map — a deconvolution problem.

The neural network parametrizes $g_\theta$, and the loss penalizes: $$\left|y_{\text{observed}} - \left(y_c + \int G(x,t)g_\theta(t),dt\right)\right|^2$$

The gradient of this loss with respect to $\theta$ passes through the integral operator — it requires differentiating variation of parameters, which is again an ODE.

### 10.6 — Long-Range Dependencies: SSMs and the Green's Function

State Space Models (S4, Mamba) parametrize the Green's function directly:

$$y(t) = \int_0^t \underbrace{Ce^{A(t-s)}B}_{G(t,s)}, u(s), ds$$

This is variation of parameters for the first-order system $\mathbf{x}' = A\mathbf{x} + Bu$. The design of SSMs is the design of $G$ — choosing $A, B, C$ so that $G(t,s)$ captures long-range dependencies efficiently. HiPPO theory (Gu et al., 2020) gives an optimal $A$ matrix so that $G$ best approximates function history in an $L^2$ sense.

**This is variation of parameters, elevated to a learned architectural principle.**

## § 11 — Comparison: Undetermined Coefficients vs. Variation of Parameters

|Feature|Undetermined Coefficients|Variation of Parameters|
|---|---|---|
|**Applicability**|Only for specific $g(x)$ forms|Any continuous $g(x)$|
|**Computation**|Algebraic (coefficient matching)|Requires integration|
|**Integration needed?**|No|Yes (can be hard)|
|**Modification rule?**|Yes (resonance case)|Not needed — handles automatically|
|**Generalizes to variable-coefficient?**|No|Yes|
|**Connection to Green's functions**|No|Direct|
|**ML relevance**|Limited|Deep (attention, kernels, SSMs)|

> **In practice:** Use undetermined coefficients when $g(x)$ is in the admissible family (it's faster). Use variation of parameters when it isn't, or when you need the Green's function for theoretical analysis.

## § 12 — Extension to Higher Order

For $n$-th order equations $y^{(n)} + p_{n-1}y^{(n-1)} + \cdots + p_0 y = g$, with $n$ independent solutions $y_1, \ldots, y_n$:

$$y_p = \sum_{k=1}^n u_k(x), y_k(x)$$

The system for $u_k'$ becomes:

$$\begin{pmatrix} y_1 & y_2 & \cdots & y_n \ y_1' & y_2' & \cdots & y_n' \ \vdots & & & \vdots \ y_1^{(n-1)} & y_2^{(n-1)} & \cdots & y_n^{(n-1)} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} u_1' \ u_2' \ \vdots \ u_n' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ \vdots \ g \end{pmatrix}$$

The coefficient matrix is the **Wronskian matrix** $W[y_1, \ldots, y_n]$, and $u_k' = W_k / W$ by Cramer's rule, where $W_k$ is $W$ with the $k$-th column replaced by $(0, \ldots, 0, g)^T$.
