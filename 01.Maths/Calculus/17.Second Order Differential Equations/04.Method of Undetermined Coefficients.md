## § 0 — Personal Preamble

This note lives at the intersection of structure and intuition. The method of undetermined coefficients is, at its heart, an **educated guess made rigorous**. We exploit the fact that certain families of functions are _closed under differentiation_ — polynomials differentiate to polynomials, exponentials to exponentials, sinusoids to sinusoids. If the forcing function $g(x)$ belongs to one of these families, we can **guess the form** of a particular solution, plug it in, and _determine_ the coefficients algebraically.

It is not magic. It is pattern recognition elevated to theorem.

**References consulted:**

- Stewart, James. _Calculus_, 8th ed. — §17.2
- MIT OCW 18.02 Multivariable Calculus (Supplementary ODE notes)

## § 1 — The Setup: Second-Order Linear ODEs

We are working with equations of the form:

$$ay'' + by' + cy = g(x) \tag{1}$$

where $a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}$, $a \neq 0$, and $g(x)$ is the **forcing function** (or _nonhomogeneous term_).

### The General Solution Decomposition

> **Theorem (Structure of the General Solution).** Let $y_c$ be the _complementary solution_ — the general solution to the associated homogeneous equation $ay'' + by' + cy = 0$ — and let $y_p$ be _any_ particular solution to $(1)$. Then every solution to $(1)$ is of the form: $$y = y_c + y_p$$

**Proof sketch.** If $y$ solves $(1)$ and $y_p$ solves $(1)$, then $y - y_p$ satisfies the homogeneous equation (by linearity of $L = aD^2 + bD + c$). Hence $y - y_p = y_c$ for some $y_c \in \ker(L)$. $\blacksquare$

The method of undetermined coefficients is a systematic procedure for finding $y_p$ when $g(x)$ has a particular form.

---

## § 2 — The Admissible Forms of $g(x)$

The method applies when $g(x)$ is a **finite linear combination** of functions from the following table:

|Type of $g(x)$|Trial Form $y_p$|
|---|---|
|$P_n(x)$ (polynomial of degree $n$)|$A_nx^n + A_{n-1}x^{n-1} + \cdots + A_0$|
|$e^{\alpha x}$|$Ae^{\alpha x}$|
|$\cos(\beta x)$ or $\sin(\beta x)$|$A\cos(\beta x) + B\sin(\beta x)$|
|$P_n(x)e^{\alpha x}$|$(A_nx^n + \cdots + A_0)e^{\alpha x}$|
|$e^{\alpha x}\cos(\beta x)$ or $e^{\alpha x}\sin(\beta x)$|$e^{\alpha x}(A\cos(\beta x) + B\sin(\beta x))$|
|Products/sums of the above|Products/sums of corresponding trial forms|

> **Why these families?** Because $\frac{d}{dx}$ maps each family _into itself_. A polynomial differentiates to a polynomial. An exponential $e^{\alpha x}$ differentiates to $\alpha e^{\alpha x}$. A sinusoid differentiates to another sinusoid. This **closure property** guarantees that $L[y_p]$ will have the same structural form as $g(x)$, making coefficient matching possible.

---

## § 3 — The Algorithm

**Step 1.** Solve the homogeneous equation $ay'' + by' + cy = 0$ to find $y_c$.

**Step 2.** Identify the form of $g(x)$ and write down the **trial particular solution** $y_p$ with undetermined coefficients.

**Step 3 (Modification Rule — Critical!).** If any term in $y_p$ duplicates a term in $y_c$, multiply that term (or the entire trial solution if $y_c$ has repeated roots matching the trial) by $x^s$, where $s$ is the smallest positive integer that eliminates the duplication.

**Step 4.** Substitute $y_p$ into the ODE. Equate coefficients of like terms. Solve the resulting algebraic system.

**Step 5.** Write the general solution: $y = y_c + y_p$.

## § 4 — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Polynomial Forcing

$$y'' - 3y' + 2y = 4x^2$$

**Step 1: Homogeneous solution.** Characteristic equation: $r^2 - 3r + 2 = 0 \Rightarrow (r-1)(r-2) = 0$. $$y_c = C_1 e^x + C_2 e^{2x}$$

**Step 2: Trial solution.** $g(x) = 4x^2$ is a degree-2 polynomial, so try: $$y_p = Ax^2 + Bx + C$$

**Step 3: No overlap** with $y_c$ (which contains only exponentials). No modification needed.

**Step 4: Substitute.** $$y_p'' - 3y_p' + 2y_p = 2A - 3(2Ax + B) + 2(Ax^2 + Bx + C)$$ $$= 2Ax^2 + (-6A + 2B)x + (2A - 3B + 2C) = 4x^2$$

Matching coefficients:

- $x^2$: $2A = 4 \Rightarrow A = 2$
- $x^1$: $-6A + 2B = 0 \Rightarrow B = 6$
- $x^0$: $2A - 3B + 2C = 0 \Rightarrow C = 7$

$$y_p = 2x^2 + 6x + 7$$

**General solution:** $$\boxed{y = C_1 e^x + C_2 e^{2x} + 2x^2 + 6x + 7}$$

---

### Example 2 — Exponential Forcing (No Resonance)

$$y'' + y' - 2y = 3e^{4x}$$

**Homogeneous:** $r^2 + r - 2 = (r+2)(r-1) = 0$, so $y_c = C_1 e^{-2x} + C_2 e^x$.

**Trial:** $y_p = Ae^{4x}$. No overlap with $y_c$.

**Substitute:** $16Ae^{4x} + 4Ae^{4x} - 2Ae^{4x} = 3e^{4x}$ $$18A = 3 \Rightarrow A = \frac{1}{6}$$

$$\boxed{y = C_1 e^{-2x} + C_2 e^x + \frac{1}{6}e^{4x}}$$

---

### Example 3 — Resonance (The Modification Rule in Action)

$$y'' - y' = 2e^x$$

**Homogeneous:** $r^2 - r = r(r-1) = 0$, so $y_c = C_1 + C_2 e^x$.

**Trial attempt:** $y_p = Ae^x$. **PROBLEM:** $e^x$ already appears in $y_c$!

**Modification:** Multiply by $x$: $y_p = Axe^x$.

**Substitute:** $$y_p' = Ae^x + Axe^x, \quad y_p'' = 2Ae^x + Axe^x$$ $$y_p'' - y_p' = (2Ae^x + Axe^x) - (Ae^x + Axe^x) = Ae^x = 2e^x$$ $$A = 2$$

$$\boxed{y = C_1 + C_2 e^x + 2xe^x}$$

> **Why does resonance require $x$-multiplication?** Physically: when the natural frequency of the system matches the forcing frequency, energy accumulates — the response grows. Mathematically: $e^x \in \ker(L)$, so $L[Ae^x] = 0 \neq 2e^x$. The factor of $x$ "shifts" the trial solution out of the kernel.

---

### Example 4 — Sinusoidal Forcing

$$y'' + 4y = \sin(3x)$$

**Homogeneous:** $r^2 + 4 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \pm 2i$, so $y_c = C_1\cos(2x) + C_2\sin(2x)$.

**Trial:** $y_p = A\cos(3x) + B\sin(3x)$. No overlap (frequency 3 ≠ 2).

**Substitute:** $$y_p'' + 4y_p = (-9A + 4A)\cos(3x) + (-9B + 4B)\sin(3x)$$ $$= -5A\cos(3x) - 5B\sin(3x) = \sin(3x)$$

So $A = 0$, $B = -\frac{1}{5}$.

$$\boxed{y = C_1\cos(2x) + C_2\sin(2x) - \frac{1}{5}\sin(3x)}$$

> **Note on sinusoids:** Always include **both** $\cos$ and $\sin$ in the trial, even if $g(x)$ contains only one of them. Differentiation mixes them.

---

### Example 5 — Superposition of Forcing Terms

$$y'' - 2y' + y = xe^x + \cos x$$

**Homogeneous:** $(r-1)^2 = 0 \Rightarrow r = 1$ (repeated), so $y_c = (C_1 + C_2 x)e^x$.

**Use superposition:** $g(x) = g_1(x) + g_2(x)$ where $g_1 = xe^x$ and $g_2 = \cos x$.

_For $g_1 = xe^x$:_ Trial would be $(Ax + B)e^x$, but $e^x$ and $xe^x$ are both in $y_c$ (repeated root!). Multiply by $x^2$: $$y_{p_1} = x^2(Ax + B)e^x = (Ax^3 + Bx^2)e^x$$

After substitution: $6Ax + 2B = x \Rightarrow A = \frac{1}{6},, B = 0$. $$y_{p_1} = \frac{x^3}{6}e^x$$

_For $g_2 = \cos x$:_ Trial $y_{p_2} = A\cos x + B\sin x$ (no overlap).

After substitution: $A = 0,, B = \frac{1}{2}$. $$y_{p_2} = \frac{1}{2}\sin x$$

**General solution:** $$\boxed{y = (C_1 + C_2 x)e^x + \frac{x^3}{6}e^x + \frac{1}{2}\sin x}$$

## § 6 — The Modification Rule: A Rigorous Justification

> **Theorem (Annihilator Interpretation).** The trial solution must be chosen so that $y_p \notin \ker(L)$. If the natural guess $\tilde{y}_p \in \ker(L)$, then $L[\tilde{y}_p] = 0$ identically, making it impossible to match the nonzero $g(x)$. Multiplying by $x^s$ uses the fact that if $r_0$ is a root of the characteristic polynomial of multiplicity $m$, then $x^k e^{r_0 x}$ (for $k < m$) is in $\ker(L)$, but $x^m e^{r_0 x}$ is not.

More precisely: if $r_0$ is a root of multiplicity $m$, then: $$\ker(L) \ni e^{r_0 x}, xe^{r_0 x}, \ldots, x^{m-1}e^{r_0 x}$$ but $x^m e^{r_0 x} \notin \ker(L)$.

So $s = m$ (the multiplicity of the overlap) is the correct shift.

## § 7 — When This Method Fails (And What To Do)

The method of undetermined coefficients is **not universal**. It fails when $g(x)$ is:

- $\ln x$, $x^{-1}$, $\tan x$, $\sec x$ — these are _not_ closed under differentiation
- Any function not expressible as a finite combination of the admissible types

**In these cases, use:** Variation of Parameters (Stewart §17.3), which works for _any_ continuous $g(x)$ and is more general but computationally heavier.

## § 8 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

This is where classical analysis secretly lives inside modern learning systems.

### 8.1 — ODEs as Continuous-Time Dynamical Systems

Neural ODEs (Chen et al., 2018) replace discrete residual layers: $$\mathbf{h}_{t+1} = \mathbf{h}_t + f(\mathbf{h}_t, \theta)$$ with a **continuous ODE**: $$\frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = f(\mathbf{h}(t), t, \theta)$$

Understanding particular solutions — how a system responds to forcing — is directly analogous to understanding how a network's hidden state evolves under the influence of input data.

### 8.2 — The Forcing Function as Input Signal

In a linear ODE model, $g(x)$ is the **input** and $y$ is the **output**. The particular solution $y_p$ is the system's _response to the input_, while $y_c$ encodes the _intrinsic dynamics_ (how it behaves without input).

In signal processing and sequence modeling (RNNs, LSTMs, SSMs):

- $y_c$ ↔ **hidden state dynamics** (how the model evolves intrinsically)
- $y_p$ ↔ **input-driven response** (how the model reacts to data)

### 8.3 — State Space Models and Mamba

Modern **State Space Models** (e.g., Mamba, S4) are literally discretized versions of: $$\mathbf{x}'(t) = A\mathbf{x}(t) + B\mathbf{u}(t)$$ $$\mathbf{y}(t) = C\mathbf{x}(t)$$

This is a **first-order linear ODE system** with forcing $B\mathbf{u}(t)$. The particular solution theory underpins the _transfer function_ of these models — the frequency-domain characterization of how different inputs are amplified or suppressed.

### 8.4 — Resonance as Gradient Explosion / Instability

The resonance phenomenon (§4, §5) has a deep ML analogue: **gradient explosion** in RNNs. When the recurrent weight matrix has eigenvalues close to 1 (analogous to $\omega_{\text{forcing}} \approx \omega_0$), gradients grow without bound during backpropagation through time — mathematically the same secular growth as $t\sin(\omega_0 t)$.

LSTM's gating mechanism and careful initialization (e.g., orthogonal initialization) are engineering solutions to this resonance problem.

### 8.5 — Fourier / Spectral Methods

The fact that $\cos(\beta x)$ and $\sin(\beta x)$ form closed families under $L$ is the seed of **spectral methods**. In deep learning:

- **Attention mechanisms** can be interpreted through the lens of kernel functions that decompose inputs spectrally
- **Positional encodings** in Transformers use $\sin$ and $\cos$ precisely because they span a basis that is stable under linear operations — the same reason they work in undetermined coefficients

### 8.6 — Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

PINNs minimize: $$\mathcal{L} = \underbrace{|L[u_\theta] - g|^2}_{\text{ODE residual}} + \underbrace{\text{boundary terms}}_{}$$

Here $u_\theta$ is a neural network approximating the solution. The particular solution $y_p$ found by undetermined coefficients is the _exact_ version of what a PINN tries to approximate. Understanding the analytic structure of $y_p$ helps in initializing PINNs, choosing architectures, and diagnosing training failures.

## § 9 — Summary Table

|Situation|Action|
|---|---|
|$g(x)$ is polynomial, exponential, sinusoid, or products/sums thereof|Apply method of undetermined coefficients|
|Trial $y_p$ contains no term in $y_c$|Use standard trial form|
|Trial $y_p$ overlaps with $y_c$|Multiply by $x^s$ where $s$ = multiplicity of root|
|$g(x) = g_1 + g_2 + \cdots$|Use superposition: solve for each $g_i$ separately, add results|
|$g(x)$ is a function not closed under differentiation|Use Variation of Parameters|
