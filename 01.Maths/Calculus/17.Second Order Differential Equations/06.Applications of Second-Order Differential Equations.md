## § 0 — Personal Preamble

Second-order linear ODEs with constant coefficients:

$$ay'' + by' + cy = g(t) \tag{★}$$

are not merely a mathematical exercise. They are the universal language of **oscillating systems** — mechanical, electrical, biological, financial, and computational. The four applications in this note — free vibration, damped vibration, forced vibration, and RLC circuits — are all instances of $(★)$. The parameters $a, b, c$ change interpretation (mass, resistance, capacitance), but the mathematics is identical.

This note builds each application from physical first principles, derives the ODE rigorously, and solves it completely. Every phenomenon — resonance, critical damping, beats — is a mathematical theorem wearing a physical disguise.

## § 1 — The Undamped Vibrating Spring (Simple Harmonic Motion)

### 1.1 Physical Setup

A mass $m$ (kg) hangs from a spring of natural length $L$ with spring constant $k$ (N/m). We measure displacement $x(t)$ from the **equilibrium position** (where the spring force exactly balances gravity).

```
   __|__
  |     |   ← spring (constant k)
  |_____|
     |
    [m]     ← mass
     |
   x(t)     ← displacement from equilibrium (positive downward)
```

### 1.2 Derivation from Newton's Second Law

At equilibrium, the spring is stretched by $\delta$ where $k\delta = mg$.

When displaced by $x(t)$ from equilibrium, the net forces on $m$ are:

- **Gravity:** $+mg$ (downward)
- **Spring restoring force:** $-k(\delta + x)$ (upward; spring is further stretched by $x$)

Newton's second law: $m\ddot{x} = mg - k\delta - kx = -kx$ (since $k\delta = mg$ cancels).

$$\boxed{m\ddot{x} + kx = 0} \tag{1.1}$$

Dividing by $m$, let $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ (**natural angular frequency**, rad/s):

$$\ddot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0 \tag{1.2}$$

### 1.3 Solution

Characteristic equation: $r^2 + \omega_0^2 = 0 \Rightarrow r = \pm i\omega_0$.

$$x(t) = C_1\cos(\omega_0 t) + C_2\sin(\omega_0 t) \tag{1.3}$$

**Amplitude-phase form.** Let $A = \sqrt{C_1^2 + C_2^2}$ and $\phi = \arctan(C_2/C_1)$:

$$\boxed{x(t) = A\cos(\omega_0 t - \phi)} \tag{1.4}$$

where:

- $A$ = amplitude (m)
- $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ = natural frequency (rad/s)
- $T = 2\pi/\omega_0$ = period (s)
- $\phi$ = phase shift (rad), determined by initial conditions

> **Key fact:** $T = 2\pi\sqrt{m/k}$ is **independent of amplitude** $A$. This isochrony (Galileo, 1602) is the foundation of mechanical timekeeping.

### 1.4 Example

A 0.5 kg mass stretches a spring 0.1 m at equilibrium.

$k = mg/\delta = (0.5)(9.8)/0.1 = 49$ N/m.  
$\omega_0 = \sqrt{49/0.5} = \sqrt{98} \approx 9.9$ rad/s.  
$T = 2\pi/9.9 \approx 0.635$ s.

With initial conditions $x(0) = 0.2$ m (pulled down 20 cm), $\dot{x}(0) = 0$: $$x(0) = C_1 = 0.2, \quad \dot{x}(0) = \omega_0 C_2 = 0 \Rightarrow C_2 = 0$$ $$x(t) = 0.2\cos(9.9t) \text{ m}$$

### 1.5 Visualization

```
x(t) = A cos(ω₀t)

  A |     *           *
    |   *   *       *   *
    |  *     *     *     *
  0 |*---------*---------*------→ t
    |           *     *
    |            *   *
 -A |              *
    |
    |←—— T ——→|←—— T ——→|

No energy loss. Perpetual oscillation.
Amplitude A = constant for all t.
```

## § 2 — Damped Vibrations

### 2.1 Physical Setup

Now introduce a **damping force** (dashpot/shock absorber) proportional to velocity:

$$F_{\text{damping}} = -c\dot{x}, \quad c > 0 \text{ (damping constant, N·s/m)}$$

The minus sign: damping opposes motion.

### 2.2 The ODE

Newton's second law with spring + damping:

$$m\ddot{x} = -kx - c\dot{x}$$

$$\boxed{m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = 0} \tag{2.1}$$

Dividing by $m$, define:

- $2\gamma = c/m$ (**damping coefficient**, s⁻¹)
- $\omega_0^2 = k/m$ (natural frequency squared)

$$\ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = 0 \tag{2.2}$$

Characteristic equation: $r^2 + 2\gamma r + \omega_0^2 = 0$

$$r = -\gamma \pm \sqrt{\gamma^2 - \omega_0^2} \tag{2.3}$$

The discriminant $\Delta = \gamma^2 - \omega_0^2$ determines the **regime**.

---

### 2.3 Case I — Underdamped: $\gamma < \omega_0$ ($\Delta < 0$)

Define $\omega_1 = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \gamma^2}$ (**damped natural frequency**).

Roots: $r = -\gamma \pm i\omega_1$ (complex conjugate pair).

$$\boxed{x(t) = e^{-\gamma t}(C_1\cos(\omega_1 t) + C_2\sin(\omega_1 t)) = Ae^{-\gamma t}\cos(\omega_1 t - \phi)} \tag{2.4}$$

**Interpretation:** Oscillation with exponentially decaying envelope $Ae^{-\gamma t}$. The system oscillates but energy leaks away.

```
x(t) — Underdamped

  A |   *
    |  * *          Ae^{-γt} envelope (dashed)
    | *   *       ·
    |*     *   · 
  0 |-------*·*--------*·------→ t
    |          ·  *   *  ·
    |           · * * ·
    |            ·*·
    |             ↑
    |         exponential decay
    
    Period T₁ = 2π/ω₁ > T₀ = 2π/ω₀
    (damping slows the oscillation)
```

---

### 2.4 Case II — Critically Damped: $\gamma = \omega_0$ ($\Delta = 0$)

Repeated root: $r = -\gamma$ (multiplicity 2).

$$\boxed{x(t) = (C_1 + C_2 t)e^{-\gamma t}} \tag{2.5}$$

**Interpretation:** No oscillation. Returns to equilibrium **as fast as possible** without overshooting. This is the design goal for car suspensions and door closers.

```
x(t) — Three regimes compared

  A |·
    | ·    (A) Underdamped: oscillates, decays slowly
    |  ·     
    |   ·   (B) Critically damped: fastest return, no overshoot ←─ optimal
    |    ·
    |     ·  ·  ·  (C) Overdamped: slow return, no oscillation
    |      · ·· ···················
  0 |────────────────────────────────→ t
    
    (A) wiggles under 0
    (B) touches 0 exactly once
    (C) approaches 0 from above, very slowly
```

---

### 2.5 Case III — Overdamped: $\gamma > \omega_0$ ($\Delta > 0$)

Two distinct real negative roots: $r_{1,2} = -\gamma \pm \sqrt{\gamma^2 - \omega_0^2} < 0$.

$$\boxed{x(t) = C_1 e^{r_1 t} + C_2 e^{r_2 t}} \tag{2.6}$$

**Interpretation:** Returns to equilibrium without oscillating, but slower than critical damping. Think: a door closer set too stiff.

### 2.6 The Damping Ratio $\zeta$

Engineers parametrize this with the **damping ratio**:

$$\zeta = \frac{\gamma}{\omega_0} = \frac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}}$$

|$\zeta$|Regime|Behavior|
|---|---|---|
|$0$|Undamped|Perpetual oscillation|
|$0 < \zeta < 1$|Underdamped|Decaying oscillation|
|$\zeta = 1$|Critically damped|Fastest return, no overshoot|
|$\zeta > 1$|Overdamped|Sluggish return, no oscillation|

### 2.7 Example

$m = 1$ kg, $k = 25$ N/m ($\omega_0 = 5$ rad/s), $c = 6$ N·s/m ($\gamma = 3$).

$\zeta = 3/5 = 0.6 < 1$ → underdamped.

$\omega_1 = \sqrt{25 - 9} = 4$ rad/s.

With $x(0) = 1$, $\dot{x}(0) = 0$:

$C_1 = 1$. $\dot{x}(0) = -\gamma C_1 + \omega_1 C_2 = 0 \Rightarrow C_2 = \gamma/\omega_1 = 3/4$.

$$x(t) = e^{-3t}!\left(\cos 4t + \frac{3}{4}\sin 4t\right) = \frac{5}{4}e^{-3t}\cos(4t - \phi)$$

where $\phi = \arctan(3/4) \approx 36.87°$.

## § 3 — Forced Vibrations

### 3.1 Physical Setup

Now apply an **external periodic force** to the mass:

$$F_{\text{ext}}(t) = F_0\cos(\omega t)$$

where $F_0$ = forcing amplitude (N) and $\omega$ = forcing frequency (rad/s).

### 3.2 The ODE

$$m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F_0\cos(\omega t) \tag{3.1}$$

$$\ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = \frac{F_0}{m}\cos(\omega t) \tag{3.2}$$

The general solution splits: $x(t) = x_c(t) + x_p(t)$ where $x_c$ (the complementary/transient solution from §2) **decays to zero**, and $x_p$ (the particular/steady-state solution) persists.

### 3.3 Steady-State Solution (Underdamped Case, $\gamma > 0$)

Trial: $x_p = A\cos(\omega t) + B\sin(\omega t)$.

After substitution and matching coefficients:

$$x_p(t) = \frac{F_0/m}{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + 4\gamma^2\omega^2} \left[(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)\cos(\omega t) + 2\gamma\omega\sin(\omega t)\right]$$

In amplitude-phase form, define the **steady-state amplitude**:

$$\boxed{G(\omega) = \frac{F_0/m}{\sqrt{(\omega_0^2 - \omega^2)^2 + 4\gamma^2\omega^2}}} \tag{3.3}$$

So $x_p(t) = G(\omega)\cos(\omega t - \delta)$ where $\tan\delta = \frac{2\gamma\omega}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}$.

### 3.4 Resonance: The Phenomenon and its Mathematics

> **Definition.** The system is in **resonance** when $G(\omega)$ is maximized over $\omega$.

Differentiate $G(\omega)^{-2}$ with respect to $\omega^2$ and set to zero:

$$\omega_{\text{res}} = \sqrt{\omega_0^2 - 2\gamma^2} \tag{3.4}$$

The **resonant amplitude** is:

$$G(\omega_{\text{res}}) = \frac{F_0/m}{2\gamma\sqrt{\omega_0^2 - \gamma^2}} \tag{3.5}$$

> **Critical observation:** As $\gamma \to 0$, $G(\omega_{\text{res}}) \to \infty$. In an undamped system forced at exactly $\omega = \omega_0$, the amplitude grows **without bound** — the system tears itself apart. This is the Tacoma Narrows Bridge (1940).

### 3.5 The Frequency Response Curve

```
G(ω) — Amplitude vs. Forcing Frequency

      |
      |   ↑ ζ = 0.1 (nearly undamped) — TALL, sharp peak
      |   |
      |   |  ↑ ζ = 0.3
      |   | /|
      |   |/ | ↑ ζ = 0.7
G(ω) |   /  |/
      |  /   /
      | /  _/  ↑ ζ = 1.0 (critically damped) — flat, no peak
      |/__/____________________→ ω
      0      ω₀
             ↑
         ω_res (shifts left as ζ increases)

As ζ → 0: peak height → ∞, width → 0
As ζ → 1/√2 ≈ 0.707: peak disappears
```

### 3.6 The Undamped Resonance Case: Beats and Pure Resonance

**Case A: $\omega \neq \omega_0$, $\gamma = 0$ (Beats)**

$$\ddot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = \frac{F_0}{m}\cos(\omega t)$$

Particular solution (by undetermined coefficients, no resonance):

$$x_p = \frac{F_0/m}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}\cos(\omega t)$$

With $x(0) = \dot{x}(0) = 0$:

$$x(t) = \frac{F_0/m}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}(\cos(\omega t) - \cos(\omega_0 t))$$

Using sum-to-product:

$$\boxed{x(t) = \frac{2F_0/m}{\omega_0^2 - \omega^2}\sin!\left(\frac{\omega_0 - \omega}{2}t\right)\sin!\left(\frac{\omega_0 + \omega}{2}t\right)} \tag{3.6}$$

This is a **high-frequency oscillation** $\sin!\left(\frac{\omega_0+\omega}{2}t\right)$ modulated by a **slow amplitude envelope** $\sin!\left(\frac{\omega_0-\omega}{2}t\right)$. The result is **beats** — the periodic waxing and waning of amplitude heard when two tuning forks of nearly equal frequency are struck simultaneously.

```
x(t) — Beats (ω close to ω₀)

  A |   ***         ***
    |  *   *       *   *
    | *     *     *     *
  0 |*       *   *       *   *
    |         * *         * *
    |          *           *
 -A |
    |←beat period→|←beat period→|
    
    Fast oscillation at (ω₀+ω)/2
    Slow envelope at (ω₀-ω)/2
```

**Case B: $\omega = \omega_0$, $\gamma = 0$ (Pure Resonance)**

The trial $x_p = A\cos(\omega_0 t)$ overlaps with $x_c$ → modification rule: $x_p = t(A\cos(\omega_0 t) + B\sin(\omega_0 t))$.

Substituting:

$$\boxed{x_p = \frac{F_0}{2m\omega_0}t\sin(\omega_0 t)} \tag{3.7}$$

The amplitude **grows linearly**: $\frac{F_0}{2m\omega_0}t$. The system is not periodic — it is **catastrophically unstable**.

```
x(t) — Pure Resonance (ω = ω₀, no damping)

      |           /\            /\
      |          /  \          /  \
      |     /\  /    \    /\  /    \
      |    /  \/      \  /  \/      \
      |   /            \/            \
    0 |--/---------------------------→ t
      |                
      | Envelope: (F₀/2mω₀)t → ∞
      |
      Growing amplitude: UNBOUNDED
```

### 3.7 Example: Forced Underdamped Spring

$m = 1$ kg, $k = 9$ N/m ($\omega_0 = 3$), $c = 2$ N·s/m ($\gamma = 1$), forced at $\omega = 2$ rad/s with $F_0 = 10$ N.

Steady-state amplitude: $$G(2) = \frac{10}{\sqrt{(9-4)^2 + 4(1)^2(4)}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{25 + 16}} = \frac{10}{\sqrt{41}} \approx 1.56 \text{ m}$$

Phase lag: $\tan\delta = \frac{2(1)(2)}{9-4} = \frac{4}{5}$, so $\delta \approx 38.66°$.

The transient $x_c = e^{-t}(C_1\cos(2\sqrt{2}t) + C_2\sin(2\sqrt{2}t))$ decays and after several time constants, only $x_p$ remains.

## § 4 — Electric Circuits (RLC Circuits)

### 4.1 The Physical Components

An **RLC series circuit** consists of:

- **Resistor** $R$ (ohms, Ω) — dissipates energy (analogous to damping)
- **Inductor** $L$ (henries, H) — stores energy in magnetic field (analogous to mass)
- **Capacitor** $C$ (farads, F) — stores energy in electric field (analogous to spring)
- **EMF source** $E(t)$ (volts) — external forcing

```
       R           L
  ┌───/\/\/───────UUUU───────┐
  │                          │
 E(t)                       ═╪═  C
  │                          │
  └──────────────────────────┘
  
  Charge on capacitor: Q(t)
  Current: I = dQ/dt
```

### 4.2 Derivation via Kirchhoff's Voltage Law

The voltage drops across each element:

- Resistor: $V_R = RI$ (Ohm's law)
- Inductor: $V_L = L\frac{dI}{dt} = L\ddot{Q}$
- Capacitor: $V_C = Q/C$

Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL): the sum of voltage drops equals the EMF:

$$L\ddot{Q} + R\dot{Q} + \frac{1}{C}Q = E(t) \tag{4.1}$$

This is **identical in form** to equation $(3.1)$. The analogy is exact:

|Mechanical|Electrical|
|---|---|
|Mass $m$|Inductance $L$|
|Damping $c$|Resistance $R$|
|Spring constant $k$|$1/C$ (reciprocal capacitance)|
|Displacement $x$|Charge $Q$|
|Velocity $\dot{x}$|Current $I = \dot{Q}$|
|External force $F(t)$|EMF $E(t)$|
|Natural frequency $\sqrt{k/m}$|$1/\sqrt{LC}$|
|Damping ratio $c/2\sqrt{mk}$|$R/2\sqrt{L/C} = R/(2Z_0)$|

### 4.3 Characteristic Impedance and $Q$-Factor

Define:

- **Resonant frequency:** $\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}$
- **Characteristic impedance:** $Z_0 = \sqrt{L/C}$ (ohms)
- **Quality factor:** $Q_{\text{factor}} = Z_0/R = \frac{1}{R}\sqrt{L/C}$

$Q_{\text{factor}}$ is the electrical analogue of $1/(2\zeta)$ in mechanics. High $Q$ → sharp resonance → low damping.

### 4.4 Free Oscillations ($E = 0$)

$$L\ddot{Q} + R\dot{Q} + \frac{Q}{C} = 0$$

Three cases mirror §2:

**Underdamped** ($R < 2\sqrt{L/C}$): $$Q(t) = e^{-Rt/2L}(A\cos(\omega_1 t) + B\sin(\omega_1 t))$$ where $\omega_1 = \sqrt{1/LC - R^2/4L^2}$.

Circuit oscillates — energy sloshes between inductor ($\frac{1}{2}LI^2$) and capacitor ($Q^2/2C$) while resistor dissipates it.

**Critically damped** ($R = 2\sqrt{L/C}$): $$Q(t) = (A + Bt)e^{-Rt/2L}$$

**Overdamped** ($R > 2\sqrt{L/C}$): $$Q(t) = Ae^{r_1 t} + Be^{r_2 t}$$

### 4.5 Forced Oscillations: AC Circuit

Let $E(t) = E_0\cos(\omega t)$ (AC source). The steady-state charge and current:

$$Q_p(t) = \frac{E_0}{\sqrt{(1/C - L\omega^2)^2 + R^2\omega^2}}\cos(\omega t - \delta)$$

The **steady-state current** $I_p = \dot{Q}_p$ has amplitude:

$$I_0 = \frac{E_0\omega}{\sqrt{(1/C - L\omega^2)^2 + R^2\omega^2}} = \frac{E_0}{Z(\omega)} \tag{4.2}$$

where $Z(\omega) = \sqrt{R^2 + (L\omega - 1/C\omega)^2}$ is the **impedance** of the circuit (the electrical analogue of steady-state amplitude denominator in §3.3).

**Resonance occurs at $\omega = \omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{LC}$:** $$Z(\omega_0) = R \quad (\text{minimum impedance}) \Rightarrow I_0 = E_0/R \quad (\text{maximum current})$$

The reactive terms $L\omega$ and $1/(C\omega)$ cancel at resonance — the circuit is purely resistive.

### 4.6 Example

$L = 1$ H, $R = 20$ Ω, $C = 0.002$ F (2 mF), $E(t) = 100\cos(10t)$ V.

$\omega_0 = 1/\sqrt{1 \times 0.002} = \sqrt{500} \approx 22.4$ rad/s.

Forcing $\omega = 10$ rad/s ≠ $\omega_0$.

Homogeneous: $r = \frac{-20 \pm \sqrt{400 - 4/0.002}}{2} = \frac{-20 \pm \sqrt{400 - 2000}}{2}$.

$\Delta = 400 - 2000 = -1600 < 0$: **underdamped**.

$r = -10 \pm 20i$.

$Q_c = e^{-10t}(A\cos(20t) + B\sin(20t))$ — transient, decays with time constant $\tau = 1/10 = 0.1$ s.

Steady-state: $Q_p(t) = A\cos(10t) + B\sin(10t)$.

Impedance at $\omega = 10$: $Z = \sqrt{400 + (10 - 50)^2} = \sqrt{400 + 1600} = \sqrt{2000} \approx 44.7$ Ω.

$I_0 = E_0/Z = 100/44.7 \approx 2.24$ A.

Phase: $\tan\delta = (L\omega - 1/C\omega)/R = (10 - 50)/20 = -2$, so $\delta \approx -63.4°$ (current leads voltage since $1/C\omega > L\omega$).

## § 5 — The Unified Picture

All four applications are instances of:

$$\ddot{x} + 2\gamma\dot{x} + \omega_0^2 x = f(t)$$

with:

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┐
│                    UNIVERSAL OSCILLATOR                          │
│                                                                  │
│         ẍ  +  2γẋ  +  ω₀²x  =  f(t)                           │
│          ↑      ↑       ↑         ↑                              │
│       inertia damping stiffness forcing                          │
│                                                                  │
│  Spring:   m, c, k, F₀cos(ωt)                                   │
│  Circuit:  L, R, 1/C, E(t)                                      │
│  Pendulum: ml², b, mgl, τ(t)                                    │
│  Economy:  M, B, K, shock(t)   ← price oscillation models       │
│  Neuron:   membrane capacitance, leak, ..., stimulus             │
└─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

The solution structure is always:

$$x(t) = \underbrace{x_c(t)}_{\text{transient (dies)}} + \underbrace{x_p(t)}_{\text{steady-state (persists)}}$$

## § 6 — Energy Analysis

### 6.1 Spring Energy

The total mechanical energy is:

$$E(t) = \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}m\dot{x}^2}_{\text{kinetic}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}kx^2}_{\text{potential}} \tag{6.1}$$

For undamped free vibration: $\frac{dE}{dt} = m\dot{x}\ddot{x} + kx\dot{x} = \dot{x}(m\ddot{x} + kx) = \dot{x} \cdot 0 = 0$. Energy is **conserved**.

With damping: $\frac{dE}{dt} = \dot{x}(m\ddot{x} + kx) = \dot{x}(-c\dot{x}) = -c\dot{x}^2 \leq 0$. Energy **dissipates** at rate $c\dot{x}^2$.

### 6.2 Circuit Energy

$$E_{\text{circuit}} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{2}LI^2}_{\text{inductor}} + \underbrace{\frac{Q^2}{2C}}_{\text{capacitor}} \tag{6.2}$$

Rate of energy dissipation: $-RI^2$ (Joule heating). This is the direct electrical analogue of $-c\dot{x}^2$.

## § 7 — Connection to AI / Machine Learning / Deep Learning

### 7.1 Oscillations in Gradient Descent (Momentum and Damping)

The **momentum update** in SGD with momentum:

$$v_{t+1} = \beta v_t - \alpha \nabla \mathcal{L}(\theta_t)$$ $$\theta_{t+1} = \theta_t + v_{t+1}$$

can be written (near a minimum where $\nabla \mathcal{L} \approx H\theta$, $H$ = Hessian) as:

$$\theta_{t+1} - (1+\beta)\theta_t + \beta\theta_{t-1} = -\alpha H\theta_t$$

This is a **discretized second-order ODE**:

$$\ddot{\theta} + (1-\beta)\dot{\theta} + \alpha H \theta = 0$$

Compare to the damped spring! The momentum parameter $\beta$ plays the role of the **damping coefficient** $c$:

- $\beta = 0$: pure gradient descent (overdamped — slow)
- $\beta$ near 1: near-undamped — fast but may oscillate
- Optimal $\beta$: analogous to critical damping — fastest convergence without oscillation

The **learning rate** $\alpha$ controls $\omega_0^2 = \alpha\lambda$ (where $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue of $H$). Choosing $\alpha$ too large → resonance → divergence. Choosing $\alpha$ optimally → critical damping → fastest convergence.

### 7.2 Resonance and Loss Landscape Instability

The condition for gradient explosion in SDG mirrors resonance:

$$\alpha \lambda_{\max} > 2(1+\beta)$$

(where $\lambda_{\max}$ is the largest Hessian eigenvalue). This is the **stability boundary** — exactly the overdamped/underdamped phase boundary. Loss landscapes with sharp curvature ($\lambda_{\max}$ large) → small learning rate required → slow training. This is why **adaptive learning rates** (Adam, RMSProp) are important: they effectively normalize by the curvature, maintaining consistent "damping" across parameter dimensions.

### 7.3 RNNs as Coupled Oscillators

A vanilla RNN: $\mathbf{h}_{t+1} = \tanh(W_h\mathbf{h}_t + W_x\mathbf{x}_t)$.

Linearized near $\mathbf{h} = 0$: $\mathbf{h}_{t+1} \approx W_h\mathbf{h}_t + W_x\mathbf{x}_t$.

This is a **system of first-order discrete ODEs** — equivalent to a coupled system of second-order oscillators in the eigenspace of $W_h$. Each eigenvalue $\lambda_i$ of $W_h$ controls:

- $|\lambda_i| < 1$: damped mode (information fades — vanishing gradients)
- $|\lambda_i| = 1$: undamped mode (information persists — LSTM/GRU are engineered to maintain this)
- $|\lambda_i| > 1$: resonant/growing mode (gradient explosion)

**Orthogonal RNNs** force $|\lambda_i| = 1$ for all $i$ — the electrical analogue of a lossless LC circuit.

### 7.4 Attention Resonance: Query-Key Alignment

In self-attention, the softmax of $QK^T/\sqrt{d}$ produces large weights when query $q_i$ and key $k_j$ are aligned. This is **amplitude amplification** — the same mechanism as resonance in forced vibration. When $q_i \cdot k_j$ is large (resonant match), the attention "rings" — that position dominates the output. Transformers tune this through temperature scaling ($\sqrt{d}$), analogous to adding damping to prevent single-position dominance.

### 7.5 Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Oscillators

To solve the spring equation with a PINN, the network $u_\theta(t)$ minimizes:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \underbrace{\int_0^T |m\ddot{u}_\theta + c\dot{u}_\theta + ku_\theta - F(t)|^2, dt}_{\text{ODE residual}} + \underbrace{|u_\theta(0) - x_0|^2 + |\dot{u}_\theta(0) - v_0|^2}_{\text{IC residual}}$$

The gradients $\ddot{u}_\theta$ are computed by **automatic differentiation** through the network. The ODE residual enforces the physics; the IC residual pins the solution. Training converges to the true particular solution for _any_ $F(t)$, including forms that are analytically intractable.

Resonance near $\omega \approx \omega_0$ creates **stiff training dynamics** — the PINN loss landscape itself becomes ill-conditioned, requiring careful learning rate scheduling. The physics of the problem bleeds into the mathematics of training.

### 7.6 Electrical Analogy for Neural Circuit Models

The Hodgkin-Huxley neuron model is a **nonlinear RLC circuit**:

$$C_m\dot{V} = -g_{Na}m^3h(V - E_{Na}) - g_K n^4(V - E_K) - g_L(V - E_L) + I_{\text{ext}}$$

The membrane capacitance $C_m$ plays the role of capacitor $C$; the ion conductances play the role of resistors; the Nernst potentials are EMF sources. Action potentials are the nonlinear analogue of resonance: a suprathreshold stimulus (forcing) drives the system past a bifurcation into a large-amplitude oscillation.

Deep learning models of neural circuits (spiking neural networks, neural field theories) are direct descendants of this RLC analogy.
