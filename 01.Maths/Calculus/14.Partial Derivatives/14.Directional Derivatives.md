## §0. Motivation: Why Partial Derivatives Are Not Enough

Suppose you are standing on a mountain whose height is given by $f(x,y)$. The partial derivatives $\partial f/\partial x$ and $\partial f/\partial y$ tell you the rate of change only along the **east** and **north** directions, respectively.

But you might want to hike **northeast**, or **southwest**, or in any arbitrary direction. The _directional derivative_ is precisely the tool that answers: **"How fast is $f$ changing as I walk in direction $\mathbf{u}$?"**

This is not a philosophical question — in machine learning, gradient descent asks exactly this, and the directional derivative is the engine underneath.

---

## §1. Setup and Notation

Let:

- $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a scalar field (a function of $n$ real variables)
- $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a point in the domain
- $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a **unit vector** (i.e., $|\mathbf{u}| = 1$) indicating a direction

We want to measure how $f$ changes at $\mathbf{a}$ in the direction $\mathbf{u}$.

> **Convention (Stewart & MIT 18.02):** The direction vector $\mathbf{u}$ must be a unit vector. If you are given a direction vector $\mathbf{v}$ that is not normalized, compute $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{v}/|\mathbf{v}|$ first.

---

## §2. The Formal Definition

### Definition 2.1 (Directional Derivative)

The **directional derivative** of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$ in the direction $\mathbf{u}$ is defined as:

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) := \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u}) - f(\mathbf{a})}{h}$$

provided this limit exists.

**Unpacking the definition:**

- $\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u}$ is a point on the line through $\mathbf{a}$ in direction $\mathbf{u}$, at signed distance $h$ from $\mathbf{a}$
- The numerator is the change in $f$ over that displacement
- Dividing by $h$ and taking $h \to 0$ gives the instantaneous rate of change

**Geometric picture (2D):** Consider the vertical plane through $\mathbf{a}$ in direction $\mathbf{u}$. This plane slices the surface $z = f(x,y)$ in a curve. The directional derivative is the **slope of this curve** at the point above $\mathbf{a}$.

```
         z = f(x,y)
              |   /← slope here is D_u f(a)
              |  /
              | /
     ─────────●──────────────► direction u
              a
```

### Remark: Partial Derivatives as Special Cases

The partial derivatives are directional derivatives in the coordinate directions: $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = D_{\mathbf{e}_i} f(\mathbf{a})$$ where $\mathbf{e}_i$ is the $i$-th standard basis vector. The directional derivative strictly generalizes this.

## §3. The Computational Theorem

The limit definition is conceptually clean but computationally painful. The key theorem connects directional derivatives to the gradient.

### Theorem 3.1 (Gradient Formula)

If $f$ is **differentiable** at $\mathbf{a}$, then for any unit vector $\mathbf{u}$:

$$\boxed{D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u}}$$

where $\nabla f(\mathbf{a}) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right)$ is the **gradient** of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$.

### Proof (Full Rigorous Proof)

**Setup:** Let $g: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be defined by: $$g(t) = f(\mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{u})$$

Then by definition: $$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u}) - f(\mathbf{a})}{h} = g'(0)$$

**Applying the Chain Rule:** Write $\mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{u}$, so $\mathbf{x}(0) = \mathbf{a}$ and $\mathbf{x}'(t) = \mathbf{u}$. Then $g(t) = f(\mathbf{x}(t))$.

Since $f$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$, by the multivariable chain rule:

$$g'(t) = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{x}'(t) = \nabla f(\mathbf{x}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{u}$$

Evaluating at $t = 0$:

$$g'(0) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u}$$

Therefore $D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u}$. $\blacksquare$

> **Critical hypothesis:** The theorem requires $f$ to be **differentiable** at $\mathbf{a}$, not merely that partial derivatives exist. Existence of partials alone is insufficient (see §6 for a counterexample).

## §4. Geometric Interpretation via the Dot Product

Write the dot product formula using the angle $\theta$ between $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ and $\mathbf{u}$:

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u} = |\nabla f(\mathbf{a})| \cdot \underbrace{|\mathbf{u}|}_{=1} \cdot \cos\theta = |\nabla f(\mathbf{a})| \cos\theta$$

This yields three fundamental facts:

|Condition|$D_{\mathbf{u}} f$|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$\theta = 0$ (u parallel to $\nabla f$)|$\|\nabla f\|$ (maximum)|Steepest ascent|
|$\theta = \pi$ (u anti-parallel to $\nabla f$)|$-\|\nabla f\|$ (minimum)|Steepest descent|
|$\theta = \pi/2$ (u perpendicular to $\nabla f$)|$0$|No change — level curve direction|

### Corollaries

**Corollary 4.1 (Direction of Steepest Ascent):** The gradient $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ points in the direction of **maximum increase** of $f$, and $|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})|$ is the maximum rate of change.

**Corollary 4.2 (Level Sets):** The gradient $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ is **perpendicular** to the level curve (or level surface) $f = c$ passing through $\mathbf{a}$.

```
Level curves of f(x,y):

        f = 3
      f = 2   ↑ ∇f  (perpendicular to level curves)
    f = 1   ╱
          ╱
        ●  ← point a
        
The gradient arrows always point "uphill", 
perpendicular to the contour lines.
```

## §5. Worked Examples

### Example 5.1 (Stewart-style)

**Problem:** Let $f(x,y) = x^2 y + e^{xy}$. Find $D_{\mathbf{u}} f(1, 0)$ in the direction $\mathbf{v} = \langle 3, 4 \rangle$.

**Step 1: Normalize.** $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{9 + 16} = 5$, so $\mathbf{u} = \langle 3/5, 4/5 \rangle$.

**Step 2: Compute the gradient.** $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2xy + ye^{xy}, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} = x^2 + xe^{xy}$$

At $(1,0)$: $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(1,0) = 0 + 0 \cdot 1 = 0, \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(1,0) = 1 + 1 \cdot 1 = 2$$

So $\nabla f(1,0) = \langle 0, 2 \rangle$.

**Step 3: Take the dot product.** $$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(1,0) = \langle 0, 2 \rangle \cdot \langle 3/5, 4/5 \rangle = 0 \cdot \frac{3}{5} + 2 \cdot \frac{4}{5} = \frac{8}{5}$$

**Answer:** $D_{\mathbf{u}} f(1,0) = 8/5$.

---

### Example 5.2 (3D — MIT 18.02 style)

**Problem:** Let $f(x,y,z) = x^2 + 2y^2 - z^2$. At the point $P = (1, 1, 1)$:

1. Find the direction of steepest ascent.
2. Find the maximum rate of change.
3. Find a direction in which $f$ does not change.

**Gradient:** $$\nabla f = \langle 2x, 4y, -2z \rangle \implies \nabla f(1,1,1) = \langle 2, 4, -2 \rangle$$

1. **Steepest ascent direction:** $\mathbf{u} = \nabla f / |\nabla f|$. $$|\nabla f| = \sqrt{4 + 16 + 4} = \sqrt{24} = 2\sqrt{6}$$ $$\mathbf{u} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{6}} \langle 2, 4, -2 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \langle 1, 2, -1 \rangle$$
    
2. **Maximum rate of change:** $|\nabla f(1,1,1)| = 2\sqrt{6}$.
    
3. **Direction of zero change:** Any $\mathbf{u}$ with $\nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$, i.e., any vector perpendicular to $\langle 2, 4, -2 \rangle$. For example, $\langle 2, 0, 2 \rangle$ (check: $2\cdot2 + 4\cdot0 + (-2)\cdot2 = 0$ ✓). Normalize: $\mathbf{u} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \langle 1, 0, 1 \rangle$.

### Example 5.3 (Using the Definition Directly)

**Problem:** Verify the gradient formula using the definition for $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ at $\mathbf{a} = (1,1)$ in direction $\mathbf{u} = \langle 1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2} \rangle$.

**Via definition:** $$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(1,1) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{(1 + h/\sqrt{2})^2 + (1 + h/\sqrt{2})^2 - 2}{h}$$ $$= \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{2(1 + h/\sqrt{2})^2 - 2}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{2[1 + 2h/\sqrt{2} + h^2/2] - 2}{h}$$ $$= \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{2h\sqrt{2} + h^2}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} (2\sqrt{2} + h) = 2\sqrt{2}$$

**Via gradient:** $\nabla f = \langle 2x, 2y \rangle \implies \nabla f(1,1) = \langle 2,2 \rangle$. $$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(1,1) = \langle 2,2 \rangle \cdot \langle 1/\sqrt{2}, 1/\sqrt{2} \rangle = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} + \frac{2}{\sqrt{2}} = 2\sqrt{2} \checkmark$$

## §6. The Differentiability Hypothesis: A Cautionary Example

The gradient formula $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$ requires differentiability, not merely existence of partials. Here is the classic counterexample.

### Example 6.1

Define: $$f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{x^2 y}{x^2 + y^2} & (x,y) \neq (0,0) \ 0 & (x,y) = (0,0) \end{cases}$$

**Partial derivatives at origin exist:** $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(0,0) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(h,0) - 0}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{0}{h} = 0$$ Similarly $\partial f / \partial y (0,0) = 0$, so $\nabla f(0,0) = \langle 0,0 \rangle$.

**But directional derivative in direction $\mathbf{u} = \langle \cos\theta, \sin\theta \rangle$:** $$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(0,0) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(h\cos\theta, h\sin\theta)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{h^2\cos^2\theta \cdot h\sin\theta}{h(h^2\cos^2\theta + h^2\sin^2\theta)} = \cos^2\theta \sin\theta$$

**Contradiction:** The gradient formula would predict $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \langle 0,0 \rangle \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0$ for all $\mathbf{u}$. But we computed $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \cos^2\theta \sin\theta$, which is nonzero for $\theta = \pi/4$.

**Conclusion:** $f$ is **not differentiable** at the origin (even though partials exist), so the formula fails. Differentiability is a strictly stronger condition than existence of partial derivatives.

## §7. The General Case: $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$

For vector-valued functions $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$, $\mathbf{F} = (F_1, \ldots, F_m)$, the directional derivative is:

$$D_{\mathbf{u}} \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a} + h\mathbf{u}) - \mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a})}{h} = J_{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u}$$

where $J_{\mathbf{F}}(\mathbf{a})$ is the **Jacobian matrix** ($m \times n$): $$(J_{\mathbf{F}})_{ij} = \frac{\partial F_i}{\partial x_j}$$

This is the full generalization: the scalar case has Jacobian = gradient (row vector), and the dot product collapses to a scalar.

## §8. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This section is not decoration — directional derivatives are the **mathematical spine** of modern ML.

### 8.1 Gradient Descent is a Directional Derivative Statement

In supervised learning, we minimize a loss function $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ over parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

The **gradient descent update rule** is: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

**Why $-\nabla \mathcal{L}$?**

By Corollary 4.1, the direction of **steepest descent** is $\mathbf{u}^* = -\nabla \mathcal{L} / |\nabla \mathcal{L}|$. The directional derivative in this direction equals $-|\nabla \mathcal{L}|$, the most negative possible value. Hence moving in $-\nabla \mathcal{L}$ decreases $\mathcal{L}$ as fast as possible.

In short: **gradient descent is the algorithm that always moves in the direction of most negative directional derivative**.

### 8.2 Directional Derivatives in Neural Network Analysis

**Width of Loss Landscapes:** Understanding a neural network's behavior involves understanding $D_{\mathbf{u}} \mathcal{L}$ for various directions $\mathbf{u}$ in parameter space. Sharp minima have large $|D_{\mathbf{u}} \mathcal{L}|$ in many directions; flat minima have small values. This is directly related to generalization.

**Sharpness-Aware Minimization (SAM):** The SAM optimizer (Foret et al., 2021) explicitly maximizes $D_{\mathbf{u}} \mathcal{L}$ over a neighborhood to find flat minima. It solves: $$\max_{|\boldsymbol{\epsilon}| \leq \rho} \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta} + \boldsymbol{\epsilon}) \approx \mathcal{L}\left(\boldsymbol{\theta} + \rho \frac{\nabla \mathcal{L}}{|\nabla \mathcal{L}|}\right)$$ This is exactly a directional derivative calculation.

### 8.3 Backpropagation is Directional Derivative Bookkeeping

During backpropagation, we compute $\partial \mathcal{L} / \partial \theta_i$ for each parameter — these are partial derivatives. The full gradient $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}$ is assembled from these partials. The gradient is then used as the "directional object" that gradient descent queries through a dot product.

The **chain rule proof** in §3 is precisely what backpropagation computes layer-by-layer. If the network is $\mathbf{F} = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1$, then backprop computes $\nabla \mathcal{L}$ by chaining Jacobians — the multivariable version of Theorem 3.1.

### 8.4 Natural Gradient Descent

Standard gradient descent measures steepness in **Euclidean parameter space**. But in probability model families (used in RL, variational inference), the natural geometry is not Euclidean — it is the **Fisher information metric**.

The **natural gradient** $\tilde{\nabla} \mathcal{L} = F^{-1} \nabla \mathcal{L}$ (where $F$ is the Fisher matrix) is the gradient in this curved geometry. This is equivalent to taking directional derivatives with respect to the **natural inner product** $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle_F = \mathbf{u}^T F \mathbf{v}$ instead of the dot product. Amari's natural gradient descent uses exactly this.

### 8.5 Directional Derivatives in Adversarial Robustness

In adversarial attacks (FGSM — Fast Gradient Sign Method), the adversarial perturbation is: $$\boldsymbol{\delta} = \epsilon \cdot \text{sign}(\nabla_{\mathbf{x}} \mathcal{L})$$

This perturbs the input $\mathbf{x}$ in the direction that most increases the loss — again, the direction of maximum directional derivative. The gradient is the primary object, and the attack is literally "move in the direction of steepest ascent of the loss with respect to the input."

### Summary Table: ML Applications

|ML Concept|Directional Derivative Role|
|---|---|
|Gradient Descent|Move in direction of steepest descent: $\mathbf{u}^* = -\nabla \mathcal{L}/\|\nabla \mathcal{L}\|$|
|Backpropagation|Chain rule = Thm 3.1 applied layer-by-layer|

## §9. Properties and Identities

Let $f, g$ be differentiable scalar fields, $c \in \mathbb{R}$.

|Property|Formula|
|---|---|
|Linearity|$D_{\mathbf{u}}(f + g) = D_{\mathbf{u}} f + D_{\mathbf{u}} g$|
|Scalar multiple|$D_{\mathbf{u}}(cf) = c \cdot D_{\mathbf{u}} f$|
|Product rule|$D_{\mathbf{u}}(fg) = g \cdot D_{\mathbf{u}} f + f \cdot D_{\mathbf{u}} g$|
|Chain rule|$D_{\mathbf{u}}(h \circ f) = h'(f) \cdot D_{\mathbf{u}} f$|
|Direction linearity|$D_{\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}} f = D_{\mathbf{u}} f + D_{\mathbf{v}} f$ (when $\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}$ is unit) — **use with care**|
|Anti-symmetry|$D_{-\mathbf{u}} f = -D_{\mathbf{u}} f$|

## §10. The Gradient as a Linear Functional (Abstract View)

From a functional analysis perspective: if $f$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$, the **total derivative** (Fréchet derivative) $Df(\mathbf{a})$ is a linear map $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ such that:

$$f(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{h}) = f(\mathbf{a}) + Df(\mathbf{a})[\mathbf{h}] + o(|\mathbf{h}|)$$

The directional derivative is simply: $$D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = Df(\mathbf{a})[\mathbf{u}]$$

By the Riesz representation theorem (in $\mathbb{R}^n$ with the Euclidean inner product), every bounded linear functional $L: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ has a unique vector $\mathbf{g}$ such that $L(\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{g} \cdot \mathbf{u}$. This vector is exactly $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$.

**Takeaway:** The gradient is the **Riesz representer** of the total derivative. This is the abstract reason the formula $D_{\mathbf{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}$ holds. In Riemannian geometry (relevant for natural gradient in ML), the gradient changes because the inner product changes, but the directional derivative $Df(\mathbf{a})[\mathbf{u}]$ is metric-independent.

## §12. Key Theorems — Summary Card

$$\boxed{D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{a}+h\mathbf{u})-f(\mathbf{a})}{h} = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{u} \quad (\text{if } f \text{ differentiable at } \mathbf{a})}$$

$$\boxed{\max_{|\mathbf{u}|=1} D_{\mathbf{u}} f(\mathbf{a}) = |\nabla f(\mathbf{a})|, \quad \text{achieved at } \mathbf{u} = \frac{\nabla f(\mathbf{a})}{|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})|}}$$

$$\boxed{\nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \perp \text{level set } {f = f(\mathbf{a})}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Differentiability} \implies \text{directional derivatives exist and equal } \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}}$$ $$\boxed{\text{Partial derivatives exist} \not\Longrightarrow \text{directional derivatives exist (or equal } \nabla f \cdot \mathbf{u}\text{)}}$$
