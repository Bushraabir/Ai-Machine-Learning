

> _"In single-variable calculus, a point on the real line has exactly two sides — you approach from the left or from the right. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, a point is surrounded by an entire circle of directions. In $\mathbb{R}^n$, by an $(n-1)$-sphere. The definition of limit must survive all of them simultaneously — and that one requirement changes everything."_

---

## 0. Why This Topic Is Genuinely Hard

In single-variable calculus, $\lim_{x\to a}f(x) = L$ is geometrically clear: the graph approaches a single height from two directions. Pathological behavior (jump discontinuities, removable singularities) is easy to detect visually.

In two or more variables, the graph is a **surface** (or worse, invisible). A point $(a,b)$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ can be approached along:

- Horizontal and vertical lines
- Diagonal lines at any angle
- Parabolas, cubics, spirals, any curve whatsoever

A limit exists only if **every single path** gives the same value $L$. This is what makes multivariable limits subtle: confirming existence requires a global argument (typically a norm bound or squeeze), while disproving existence requires finding only two paths that disagree.

This asymmetry — **two paths to disprove, a norm bound to prove** — is the organizing principle of the entire subject.

---

## 1. Open and Closed Sets in $\mathbb{R}^n$

Before defining limits, we need the correct topological language.

### Balls and Neighborhoods

The **open ball** of radius $r > 0$ centered at $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$B(\mathbf{a}, r) = {\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n : |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}| < r}$$

The **deleted (punctured) ball** (excludes the center):

$$B'(\mathbf{a}, r) = {\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n : 0 < |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}| < r}$$

Here $|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - a_i)^2}$ is the Euclidean norm.

### Interior, Boundary, Open, Closed

Let $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$.

- **Interior point:** $\mathbf{a}$ is an interior point of $D$ if $\exists r > 0$ such that $B(\mathbf{a},r) \subseteq D$.
- **Boundary point:** $\mathbf{a}$ is a boundary point of $D$ if every ball $B(\mathbf{a},r)$ contains points both in $D$ and in $D^c$.
- **Open set:** $D$ is open if every point of $D$ is an interior point.
- **Closed set:** $D$ is closed if it contains all its boundary points.
- **Bounded set:** $D$ is bounded if $\exists M > 0$ such that $D \subseteq B(\mathbf{0}, M)$.
- **Compact set:** $D$ is compact if it is closed and bounded (in $\mathbb{R}^n$, by Heine–Borel).

**Examples in $\mathbb{R}^2$:**

|Set|Open?|Closed?|Bounded?|
|---|---|---|---|
|${(x,y): x^2+y^2 < 1}$|✓|✗|✓|
|${(x,y): x^2+y^2 \leq 1}$|✗|✓|✓|
|${(x,y): x^2+y^2 = 1}$|✗|✓|✓|
|${(x,y): x > 0}$|✓|✗|✗|
|$\mathbb{R}^2$|✓|✓|✗|

Note: $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $\emptyset$ are simultaneously open and closed — the only such sets in a connected space.

## 2. The Limit: Precise Definition

### Definition ($\varepsilon$-$\delta$)

Let $f: D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ and let $\mathbf{a}$ be a limit point of $D$ (every deleted ball around $\mathbf{a}$ meets $D$). We write:

$$\lim_{\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{a}} f(\mathbf{x}) = L$$

if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that:

$$\mathbf{x} \in D \text{ and } 0 < |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}| < \delta \implies |f(\mathbf{x}) - L| < \varepsilon$$

**For two variables** $(x,y) \to (a,b)$: the condition $0 < |\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}| < \delta$ becomes $0 < \sqrt{(x-a)^2+(y-b)^2} < \delta$.

### Unpacking the Definition

The definition says: no matter how small a tolerance $\varepsilon$ you demand around $L$, I can find a punctured ball of radius $\delta$ around $\mathbf{a}$ such that $f$ maps every point in that punctured ball to within $\varepsilon$ of $L$.

Crucially:

- The **center $\mathbf{a}$ itself** plays no role (the punctured ball excludes it).
- $f$ need not even be defined at $\mathbf{a}$.
- $\delta$ may depend on $\varepsilon$, but **not on $\mathbf{x}$** — the same $\delta$ works for all $\mathbf{x}$ in the punctured ball.

## 3. Limit Laws

The standard limit laws extend verbatim to $\mathbb{R}^n$. If $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}f(\mathbf{x}) = L$ and $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}g(\mathbf{x}) = M$, then:

$$\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}[f(\mathbf{x})+g(\mathbf{x})] = L+M$$

$$\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}[f(\mathbf{x})\cdot g(\mathbf{x})] = L\cdot M$$

$$\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}\frac{f(\mathbf{x})}{g(\mathbf{x})} = \frac{L}{M} \quad (M\neq 0)$$

$$\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}[f(\mathbf{x})]^p = L^p \quad (p \in \mathbb{Z}^+)$$

**Proofs:** All proofs carry over from the single-variable case, replacing $|x-a|$ with $|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{a}|$.

### The Squeeze Theorem (Multivariable)

If $g(\mathbf{x}) \leq f(\mathbf{x}) \leq h(\mathbf{x})$ near $\mathbf{a}$, and $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}g(\mathbf{x}) = \lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}h(\mathbf{x}) = L$, then $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}f(\mathbf{x}) = L$.

This is the **workhorse for proving limits exist** when direct substitution fails.

---

## 4. Two Fundamental Techniques

### Technique 1: Proving a Limit Does NOT Exist — The Two-Path Test

> **Theorem.** If $f(\mathbf{x})$ approaches different values $L_1 \neq L_2$ along two different paths to $\mathbf{a}$, then $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}f(\mathbf{x})$ does not exist.

**Warning:** If two paths give the _same_ value, that proves nothing — the limit may still fail to exist along some other path.

**Strategy for choosing paths:** Try simple ones first.

1. Along axes: $y = 0$ then $x = 0$
2. Along $y = mx$ for various slopes $m$
3. Along $y = x^k$ (parabolic/power paths) — useful when $y = mx$ all give the same value
4. Along $y = x\sin(1/x)$ or similar — for highly oscillatory functions

### Technique 2: Proving a Limit EXISTS — Norm Bound (Squeeze)

Convert to polar coordinates or bound $|f(\mathbf{x}) - L|$ in terms of $r = |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}|$. If:

$$|f(\mathbf{x}) - L| \leq C \cdot r^k \quad (k > 0, C > 0 \text{ constant})$$

then as $r = |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}| \to 0$, we get $|f(\mathbf{x}) - L| \to 0$, so $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}f(\mathbf{x}) = L$.

**Key inequalities** for bounding (WLOG, limit at origin):

$$|x| \leq \sqrt{x^2+y^2} = r, \qquad |y| \leq r$$

$$x^2 \leq r^2, \qquad y^2 \leq r^2, \qquad x^2+y^2 = r^2$$

$$|xy| \leq \frac{x^2+y^2}{2} = \frac{r^2}{2} \quad\text{(AM-GM)}$$

$$\frac{x^2}{x^2+y^2} \leq 1, \quad \frac{y^2}{x^2+y^2} \leq 1 \quad\text{(key for rational functions)}$$

## 5. Polar Coordinates for Limits at the Origin

For limits $(x,y) \to (0,0)$, the substitution $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$ converts the two-variable limit into a one-variable limit as $r \to 0^+$, **provided the result is independent of $\theta$**.

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} f(x,y) = \lim_{r\to 0^+} f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta)$$

**Critical caveat:** If the resulting expression in $r$ and $\theta$ depends on $\theta$, this does NOT immediately show the limit fails — it shows polar coordinates are insufficient. The limit fails only if for some $\theta$ (i.e., some specific path), the one-sided limit differs from others.

**Example where polar works cleanly:** $f(x,y) = \dfrac{x^2 y}{x^2+y^2}$

$$f(r\cos\theta, r\sin\theta) = \frac{r^2\cos^2\theta\cdot r\sin\theta}{r^2} = r\cos^2\theta\sin\theta$$

Since $|r\cos^2\theta\sin\theta| \leq r \to 0$ as $r\to 0$, we have $\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}f = 0$.

**Example where polar reveals nonexistence:** $f(x,y) = \dfrac{x^2 - y^2}{x^2+y^2}$

$$f(r\cos\theta,r\sin\theta) = \frac{r^2\cos^2\theta - r^2\sin^2\theta}{r^2} = \cos^2\theta - \sin^2\theta = \cos 2\theta$$

This is independent of $r$ — so as $r\to 0$, $f$ stays at $\cos 2\theta$, which depends on the direction. Along $\theta = 0$ (the $x$-axis): $f = 1$. Along $\theta = \pi/2$ (the $y$-axis): $f = -1$. Limit does not exist.

## 6. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Limit Exists — Squeeze

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{x^3 + y^3}{x^2+y^2}$$

**Bound:** Using $|x|^3 \leq |x|\cdot r^2$ (since $|x| \leq r$) and similarly for $y$:

$$\left|\frac{x^3+y^3}{x^2+y^2}\right| \leq \frac{|x|^3 + |y|^3}{r^2} \leq \frac{r^3 + r^3}{r^2} = 2r \to 0$$

Therefore the limit is $\mathbf{0}$.

---

### Example 2: Limit Does Not Exist — Linear Paths Differ

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} \frac{xy}{x^2+y^2}$$

**Path 1:** $y = 0$ (the $x$-axis, $x \neq 0$): $$f(x,0) = \frac{x\cdot 0}{x^2+0} = 0 \to 0$$

**Path 2:** $y = x$: $$f(x,x) = \frac{x\cdot x}{x^2+x^2} = \frac{x^2}{2x^2} = \frac{1}{2} \to \frac{1}{2}$$

Two paths give $0 \neq \frac{1}{2}$. **Limit does not exist.** $\square$

_In polar:_ $f = \frac{r^2\cos\theta\sin\theta}{r^2} = \frac{\sin 2\theta}{2}$ — angle-dependent. Confirms nonexistence.

---

### Example 3: Limit Does Not Exist — Linear Paths All Agree, But Parabolic Path Differs

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{x^2 y}{x^4 + y^2}$$

**Along $y = mx$** (all linear paths through origin): $$f(x,mx) = \frac{x^2\cdot mx}{x^4 + m^2x^2} = \frac{mx^3}{x^2(x^2+m^2)} = \frac{mx}{x^2+m^2} \to 0$$

Every linear path gives limit $0$ — naively one might conclude the limit is $0$.

**Along $y = x^2$** (parabolic path): $$f(x,x^2) = \frac{x^2\cdot x^2}{x^4 + x^4} = \frac{x^4}{2x^4} = \frac{1}{2} \to \frac{1}{2}$$

So the linear paths all give $0$, but the parabolic path gives $\frac{1}{2}$. **Limit does not exist.** $\square$

> **Moral:** Checking only linear paths is never sufficient. Always think about which curves might "fit" the singularity structure of the denominator.

---

### Example 4: Limit Exists — Trigonometric Bound

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} (x^2+y^2)\sin!\left(\frac{1}{x^2+y^2}\right)$$

**Bound:** Since $|\sin(\theta)| \leq 1$ for all $\theta$:

$$\left|(x^2+y^2)\sin!\left(\frac{1}{x^2+y^2}\right)\right| \leq x^2+y^2 = r^2 \to 0$$

**Limit is $0$.** Notice the function is not even defined at the origin, but the limit exists.

---

### Example 5: Three Variables

$$\lim_{(x,y,z)\to(0,0,0)}\frac{x^2 y^2 z^2}{x^2+y^2+z^2}$$

Let $r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$. Then:

$$\frac{x^2 y^2 z^2}{x^2+y^2+z^2} \leq \frac{x^2 y^2 z^2}{???}$$

Better: use AM-GM. We have $x^2 \leq r^2$, $y^2 \leq r^2$, $z^2 \leq r^2$, so:

$$0 \leq \frac{x^2 y^2 z^2}{r^2} \leq \frac{r^2\cdot r^2\cdot r^2}{r^2} = r^4 \to 0$$

**Limit is $0$.**

---

### Example 6: $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ Proof Explicitly

**Claim:** $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(1,2)}(3x - y + 1) = 2$.

**Proof.** Let $\varepsilon > 0$ be given. We need $\delta > 0$ such that $\sqrt{(x-1)^2+(y-2)^2} < \delta$ implies $|(3x-y+1) - 2| < \varepsilon$.

Compute: $$|(3x-y+1)-2| = |3(x-1)-(y-2)| \leq 3|x-1| + |y-2|$$

Now $|x-1| \leq \sqrt{(x-1)^2+(y-2)^2} < \delta$ and $|y-2| \leq \sqrt{(x-1)^2+(y-2)^2} < \delta$. So:

$$|(3x-y+1)-2| \leq 3\delta + \delta = 4\delta$$

Choose $\delta = \varepsilon/4$. Then $\sqrt{(x-1)^2+(y-2)^2} < \delta$ implies $|(3x-y+1)-2| \leq 4\delta = \varepsilon$. $\square$

## 7. Continuity

### Definition

A function $f: D\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is **continuous at $\mathbf{a} \in D$** if:

$$\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}f(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{a})$$

This requires three things simultaneously:

1. $f(\mathbf{a})$ is defined.
2. $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}f(\mathbf{x})$ exists.
3. They are equal.

$f$ is **continuous on $D$** if it is continuous at every $\mathbf{a} \in D$.

### Sequential Characterization

Equivalent formulation (often more useful for proofs):

> $f$ is continuous at $\mathbf{a}$ $\iff$ for every sequence ${\mathbf{x}_k}$ in $D$ with $\mathbf{x}_k \to \mathbf{a}$, we have $f(\mathbf{x}_k) \to f(\mathbf{a})$.

### Continuity Preserved by Operations

If $f$ and $g$ are continuous at $\mathbf{a}$, then:

- $f + g$, $f - g$, $f \cdot g$ are continuous at $\mathbf{a}$
- $f/g$ is continuous at $\mathbf{a}$ if $g(\mathbf{a}) \neq 0$
- $|f|$ is continuous at $\mathbf{a}$

### Composition

If $g: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous at $\mathbf{a}$ and $h: \mathbb{R}\to\mathbb{R}$ is continuous at $g(\mathbf{a})$, then $h \circ g$ is continuous at $\mathbf{a}$.

**Consequence:** All elementary functions (polynomials, rationals where denominator $\neq 0$, $\sin$, $\cos$, $\exp$, $\ln$ on its domain, etc.) composed with continuous functions of several variables are continuous. Most functions you write down are continuous on their natural domain.

## 8. Types of Discontinuity in Higher Dimensions

Unlike the one-variable case (jump, removable, infinite, oscillatory), higher-dimensional discontinuities can be more complex. The key types:

### Removable Discontinuity

$f$ is not defined at $\mathbf{a}$ (or defined with the wrong value), but $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}f(\mathbf{x}) = L$ exists. Redefining $f(\mathbf{a}) = L$ makes $f$ continuous. These limits can be computed via squeeze or polar coordinates.

### Essential Discontinuity (Limit Does Not Exist)

The limit fails to exist — different paths give different values. Examples 2 and 3 above are of this type. There is no way to "fix" the function at the point.

### Unbounded Discontinuity

$|f(\mathbf{x})| \to \infty$ as $\mathbf{x} \to \mathbf{a}$. Example: $f(x,y) = 1/(x^2+y^2)$ near the origin.

## 9. Important Theorems on Continuity

### Extreme Value Theorem (Generalization)

> **Theorem.** If $f: D \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous and $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is compact (closed and bounded), then $f$ attains its maximum and minimum on $D$.

That is, there exist $\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q} \in D$ such that $f(\mathbf{p}) \leq f(\mathbf{x}) \leq f(\mathbf{q})$ for all $\mathbf{x} \in D$.

This theorem guarantees optimization problems have solutions — a fact used constantly in ML (loss minimization over compact parameter spaces, though in practice parameter spaces are rarely compact).

### Intermediate Value Theorem (Generalization)

> **Theorem.** If $f: D \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous, $D$ is connected, and $f(\mathbf{a}) = A$, $f(\mathbf{b}) = B$ with $A < B$, then for every $c \in (A,B)$, there exists $\mathbf{p} \in D$ with $f(\mathbf{p}) = c$.

Geometric consequence: the image of a connected set under a continuous map is connected — hence an interval.

### Continuity and Level Sets

If $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ is continuous, then:

- Every level set ${f = c}$ is **closed** (preimage of a closed set under a continuous map).
- Every superlevel set ${f > c}$ is **open**.
- Every sublevel set ${f < c}$ is **open**.

These facts are foundational in optimization — sublevel sets of continuous functions on compact domains are compact, guaranteeing minima exist.

---

## 10. Uniform Continuity

$f: D\to\mathbb{R}$ is **uniformly continuous** on $D$ if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that:

$$\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\in D \text{ and } |\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}| < \delta \implies |f(\mathbf{x})-f(\mathbf{y})| < \varepsilon$$

The difference from ordinary continuity: $\delta$ depends only on $\varepsilon$, not on the particular point $\mathbf{x}$.

> **Theorem (Heine-Cantor).** If $f$ is continuous on a compact set $D$, then $f$ is uniformly continuous on $D$.

## 11. Differentiability vs. Continuity: The Subtle Hierarchy

This is where multivariable calculus diverges sharply from single-variable. We have the following **strict hierarchy** of conditions:

```
Differentiable  ⟹  Continuous  ⟹  (merely) Limit exists at each point
      ⟸ NOT                ⟸ NOT

Continuous partial derivatives  ⟹  Differentiable
      ⟸ NOT

Partial derivatives exist  ⟹ / ⟹  Continuous
```

**The shocking fact:** A function can have **all partial derivatives exist at a point** and yet be **discontinuous there**.

**Example.** Define: $$f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy}{x^2+y^2} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \ 0 & (x,y) = (0,0) \end{cases}$$

At $(0,0)$: $$f_x(0,0) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(h,0)-f(0,0)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{0}{h} = 0$$

Similarly $f_y(0,0) = 0$. Both partial derivatives exist.

But as shown in Example 2, $\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}f(x,y)$ does not exist (along $y=x$ the limit is $1/2$). So $f$ is **not continuous** at the origin, despite having partial derivatives everywhere.

**Why?** Partial derivatives only probe $f$ along the two coordinate axes. They say nothing about behavior along diagonal or curved paths.

**The correct condition for differentiability** (to be treated in detail in [[Partial Derivatives]]): $f$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$ if there exists a linear map $L:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ such that:

$$\lim_{\mathbf{h}\to\mathbf{0}}\frac{f(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}) - f(\mathbf{a}) - L(\mathbf{h})}{|\mathbf{h}|} = 0$$

Differentiability implies continuity. Existence of partial derivatives does not.

## 13. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

Limits and continuity in multivariable calculus are not abstract tools — they are the **mathematical bedrock** on which the guarantees of modern machine learning rest. Every theorem about optimization, generalization, and convergence ultimately assumes or proves continuity of something.

### 13.1 Continuity of Neural Networks

A feedforward neural network $f_\theta: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is a composition of affine maps and activation functions:

$$f_\theta = \sigma_L \circ W_L \circ \cdots \circ \sigma_1 \circ W_1$$

Since affine maps and standard activations (ReLU, sigmoid, tanh, GELU) are **continuous**, compositions of continuous functions are continuous, so $f_\theta$ is continuous in its inputs $\mathbf{x}$ for any fixed $\theta$.

But continuity in $\theta$ also matters: the map $\theta \mapsto f_\theta(\mathbf{x})$ is continuous (even smooth) for smooth activations, and continuous for ReLU networks (though not differentiable everywhere in $\theta$). This continuity guarantees that **small perturbations to parameters produce small changes in output** — essential for gradient-based training to make sense.

### 13.2 Continuity of the Loss Function

The loss function $\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}[\ell(f_\theta(\mathbf{x}), y)]$ is continuous in $\theta$ under mild conditions. This matters because:

- **Gradient descent requires continuity:** If $\mathcal{L}$ had jump discontinuities in $\theta$, gradients would be meaningless near those jumps.
- **The Extreme Value Theorem** guarantees that $\mathcal{L}$ attains a minimum on any compact subset of parameter space — a theoretical existence guarantee for optimal parameters.
- **Level sets** ${\theta : \mathcal{L}(\theta) \leq c}$ are closed (and bounded for coercive losses), hence compact — so training within a sublevel set is bounded.

### 13.3 The Two-Path Problem in Optimization Landscapes

The multivariable two-path test for nonexistence of limits has a direct analog in **optimization landscape analysis**. Consider the question: does gradient flow from initialization $\theta_0$ converge to the same minimum regardless of path taken?

- **If the loss landscape has a unique minimum:** All gradient flow paths converge to the same point — analogous to a limit existing along all paths.
- **If there are multiple local minima:** Different paths (initializations) lead to different endpoints — analogous to different paths giving different limits.

The study of **loss landscape connectivity** (Draxler et al., 2018; Garipov et al., 2018) is precisely an analysis of path-dependence in high-dimensional parameter space.

### 13.4 ReLU Discontinuity of Gradients

ReLU: $\sigma(x) = \max(0,x)$ is **continuous** but **not differentiable** at $x = 0$.

In a ReLU network, the gradient $\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}$ may not exist at certain parameter values (where any pre-activation hits exactly $0$). This means:

- The loss surface has **kinks** (gradient discontinuities) — codimension-1 hyperplanes in $\theta$-space where the gradient jumps.
- In practice, these measure zero and can be ignored; training works because gradient descent never lands exactly on a kink (almost surely).
- **Subgradient methods** extend the gradient concept to continuous but non-differentiable functions — continuity is the key requirement, not differentiability.

The **Clarke subdifferential** generalizes the gradient to Lipschitz-continuous (hence continuous) functions. ReLU networks satisfy Lipschitz continuity: $|f_\theta(\mathbf{x}) - f_\theta(\mathbf{y})| \leq C|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}|$ — a quantitative form of continuity.

### 13.5 Lipschitz Continuity and Generalization

A function $f: \mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ is **$K$-Lipschitz** if:

$$|f(\mathbf{x}) - f(\mathbf{y})| \leq K|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y}| \quad\forall,\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}$$

This is uniform continuity with an explicit modulus: $\delta = \varepsilon/K$.

In deep learning, Lipschitz continuity appears everywhere:

- **Spectral normalization** (Miyato et al., 2018): Constrains each layer's spectral norm so the network is globally Lipschitz — used in GANs for training stability.
- **Wasserstein GANs** (Arjovsky et al., 2017): The discriminator must be $1$-Lipschitz — enforced via gradient penalty (penalizing $|\nabla f| > 1$, exactly the gradient of a Lipschitz bound).
- **Robustness guarantees:** A network with Lipschitz constant $K$ satisfies $|f(\mathbf{x}) - f(\mathbf{x}+\boldsymbol\delta)| \leq K|\boldsymbol\delta|$ — the perturbation bound for adversarial robustness.
- **PAC-Bayes generalization bounds** often depend on the Lipschitz constant of the loss as a function of parameters.

### 13.6 Limits in Infinite-Dimensional Spaces: Function Spaces

The framework of limits and continuity generalizes to **infinite-dimensional** spaces — and this is where modern deep learning theory lives.

A **functional** is a map $F: \mathcal{F} \to \mathbb{R}$ from a space of functions to the reals. For example:

$$F[f] = \int_\Omega |f(\mathbf{x})|^2,d\mathbf{x} \qquad\text{(the $L^2$ norm squared)}$$

Limits in function spaces use norms like $|f - g|_{L^2}$, $|f-g|_{\infty}$, etc. Continuity of $F$ with respect to these norms is the analog of our $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ definition. Neural networks as function approximators are studied in this setting — convergence theorems (universal approximation, NTK theory) are statements about limits in function spaces.

### 13.7 Automatic Differentiation and Continuity

**Automatic differentiation (autograd)** in PyTorch/JAX computes gradients by tracing operations. Its correctness relies on:

1. **Continuity of composed operations:** Chain rule is valid only for differentiable (hence continuous) functions.
2. **Handling of discontinuities:** At points of non-differentiability (ReLU at 0), frameworks return a **subgradient** (typically $0$ or left/right derivative). This is mathematically valid only because ReLU is still **continuous** there.
3. **The limit definition of derivative:** Autograd builds a computational graph that computes $\lim_{h\to 0}(f(\mathbf{x}+h\mathbf{e}_i)-f(\mathbf{x}))/h$ symbolically, not numerically — exploiting the limit laws and chain rule.

|Universal approximation|Convergence of $f_\theta \to f^*$ in $L^2$ or $\sup$ norm = continuity in function space|

**Key inequalities to memorize:**

$$|x|, |y| \leq r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}, \quad |xy| \leq \frac{r^2}{2}, \quad \frac{x^2}{x^2+y^2}\leq 1$$

**Continuity** = limit equals function value. Preserved under $+,-,\times,\div,\circ$.

**The crucial hierarchy:** $$\text{Cont. partial derivatives} \Rightarrow \text{Differentiable} \Rightarrow \text{Continuous} \Rightarrow \text{Limit exists}$$

None of these arrows reverses. In particular: **partial derivatives existing does not imply continuity.**

In machine learning, continuity is the minimum regularity needed for optimization to function. Lipschitz continuity — a uniform, quantitative version — underpins generalization bounds, GAN training stability, and robustness guarantees.
