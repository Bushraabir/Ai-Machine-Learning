

> _"A function is differentiable at a point if and only if it can be well-approximated by a linear map near that point. The tangent plane is that linear map, made geometric."_

---

## §1. Motivation — From Tangent Lines to Tangent Planes

In single-variable calculus, given $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ differentiable at $x = a$, the **tangent line** is:

$$L(x) = f(a) + f'(a)(x - a)$$

This is the best linear approximation to $f$ near $a$. It _touches_ the curve and shares its slope.

Now let $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, i.e., $z = f(x, y)$. The graph is a **surface** in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The natural question: _what plays the role of the tangent line?_

The answer is a plane — the **tangent plane** — and it is the unique plane that:

1. Passes through the point $(a, b, f(a,b))$ on the surface
2. Has the "correct" slope in every direction through that point

The existence of such a plane is not automatic. It requires **differentiability**, which in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is a strictly stronger condition than the existence of partial derivatives.

---

## §2. Partial Derivatives — The Two Obvious Slopes

Fix a point $(a, b)$ in the domain of $f$.

**Definition.** The _partial derivatives_ of $f$ at $(a,b)$ are:

$$f_x(a,b) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a+h,, b) - f(a,b)}{h}$$

$$f_y(a,b) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a,, b+k) - f(a,b)}{k}$$

Geometrically: $f_x$ is the slope of the curve obtained by slicing the surface with the plane $y = b$; similarly $f_y$ with $x = a$.

**Key warning.** Existence of $f_x$ and $f_y$ does **not** guarantee differentiability. The classical counterexample:

$$f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy}{x^2 + y^2} & (x,y) \neq (0,0) \ 0 & (x,y) = (0,0) \end{cases}$$

has $f_x(0,0) = f_y(0,0) = 0$, but $f$ is **not** even continuous at $(0,0)$ (approach along $y = x$: limit is $\frac{1}{2}$). So no tangent plane exists there.

---

## §3. Differentiability — The Right Definition

**Definition** _(Stewart §14.4, MIT 18.02 Notes)._ $f$ is **differentiable** at $(a,b)$ if there exist constants $A, B$ such that:

$$\lim_{(h,k) \to (0,0)} \frac{f(a+h,, b+k) - f(a,b) - Ah - Bk}{\sqrt{h^2 + k^2}} = 0$$

When this holds, one can show $A = f_x(a,b)$ and $B = f_y(a,b)$.

**Interpretation.** The error in approximating $f(a+h, b+k)$ by $f(a,b) + Ah + Bk$ vanishes _faster than the distance_ $\sqrt{h^2+k^2}$ to the base point. This is exactly what it means for a linear map to be "tangent."

**Sufficient condition** _(very useful in practice)_. If $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist in a neighborhood of $(a,b)$ and are **continuous** at $(a,b)$, then $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$.

Most functions encountered in practice ($C^1$ functions) satisfy this automatically.

---

## §4. The Tangent Plane — Definition and Equation

**Definition.** If $f$ is differentiable at $(a, b)$, the **tangent plane** to the surface $z = f(x,y)$ at the point $P = (a, b, f(a,b))$ is:

$$\boxed{z = f(a,b) + f_x(a,b)(x - a) + f_y(a,b)(y - b)}$$

**Why is this a plane?** Rewrite as:

$$z - f(a,b) = f_x(a,b)(x-a) + f_y(a,b)(y-b)$$

This is of the form $\mathbf{n} \cdot (\mathbf{r} - \mathbf{r}_0) = 0$ with normal vector $\mathbf{n} = \langle f_x(a,b),, f_y(a,b),, -1 \rangle$.

**Uniqueness.** The tangent plane is the unique plane through $P$ such that every curve on the surface through $P$ has its tangent line lying in the plane. This is what "tangent" means geometrically for surfaces.

---

## §5. The Linear Approximation (Linearization)

The tangent plane gives the **linearization** of $f$ near $(a,b)$:

$$L(x,y) = f(a,b) + f_x(a,b)(x-a) + f_y(a,b)(y-b)$$

For $(x,y)$ near $(a,b)$:

$$f(x,y) \approx L(x,y)$$

The error is $o(|(x-a, y-b)|)$, i.e., it goes to zero faster than the distance.

**Total differential.** Writing $\Delta x = x - a$, $\Delta y = y - b$, and $\Delta z = f(x,y) - f(a,b)$:

$$\Delta z \approx dz = f_x, dx + f_y, dy$$

This is the **total differential** — an infinitesimal version of the tangent plane approximation, and arguably the single most important formula in multivariable differential calculus.

## §6. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Explicit Computation

Let $f(x,y) = x^2 y + \sin(\pi y)$. Find the tangent plane at $(1, 1)$.

**Step 1.** Evaluate: $f(1,1) = 1 \cdot 1 + \sin(\pi) = 1$.

**Step 2.** Partial derivatives: $$f_x = 2xy \implies f_x(1,1) = 2$$ $$f_y = x^2 + \pi\cos(\pi y) \implies f_y(1,1) = 1 + \pi\cos(\pi) = 1 - \pi$$

**Step 3.** Tangent plane: $$z = 1 + 2(x-1) + (1-\pi)(y-1)$$

**Step 4.** Check: at $(1,1)$, $z = 1$. ✓

---

### Example 2 — Linearization for Approximation

Approximate $f(x,y) = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ near $(3, 4)$.

$f(3,4) = \sqrt{9+16} = 5$.

$$f_x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}} \implies f_x(3,4) = \frac{3}{5}$$ $$f_y = \frac{y}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}} \implies f_y(3,4) = \frac{4}{5}$$

Linearization: $L(x,y) = 5 + \frac{3}{5}(x-3) + \frac{4}{5}(y-4)$

Approximate $f(3.02, 3.97)$: $$L(3.02, 3.97) = 5 + \frac{3}{5}(0.02) + \frac{4}{5}(-0.03) = 5 + 0.012 - 0.024 = 4.988$$

Exact: $\sqrt{(3.02)^2 + (3.97)^2} = \sqrt{9.1204 + 15.7609} = \sqrt{24.8813} \approx 4.988$. Very close. ✓

---

### Example 3 — Error Estimation via Total Differential

A cylindrical can has radius $r = 5,\text{cm}$ and height $h = 10,\text{cm}$, with measurement errors $dr = 0.05,\text{cm}$, $dh = 0.1,\text{cm}$. Estimate the error in the volume $V = \pi r^2 h$.

$$dV = \frac{\partial V}{\partial r},dr + \frac{\partial V}{\partial h},dh = 2\pi r h, dr + \pi r^2, dh$$ $$= 2\pi(5)(10)(0.05) + \pi(25)(0.1) = 5\pi + 2.5\pi = 7.5\pi \approx 23.6,\text{cm}^3$$

## §7. Geometric Visualization

```
          z
          |      ← surface z = f(x,y)
          |   /~~~~/
          |  /~~~~/ ← tangent plane at P
          | /  P·
          |/__________ y
         /
        x
```

Think of it this way: place a flat board tangent to a curved hill at a point $P$. The board sits flush against the hill at $P$ — touching it, matching the slope in both the east-west and north-south directions. That board is the tangent plane.

The normal vector to the plane is $\mathbf{n} = \langle f_x(a,b),, f_y(a,b),, -1 \rangle$. It points "away" from the surface in the direction of steepest local tilt.

**Implicit surfaces.** For a surface defined by $F(x,y,z) = 0$, the tangent plane at $(a,b,c)$ is:

$$F_x(a,b,c)(x-a) + F_y(a,b,c)(y-b) + F_z(a,b,c)(z-c) = 0$$

with normal $\nabla F = \langle F_x, F_y, F_z \rangle$. This is the gradient theorem: **the gradient is always normal to level sets/surfaces.**

## §8. Connection to the Gradient and Directional Derivatives

The tangent plane formula can be written as:

$$z - f(a,b) = \nabla f(a,b) \cdot \langle x-a,, y-b \rangle$$

where $\nabla f = \langle f_x, f_y \rangle$ is the **gradient**.

The directional derivative in direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is:

$$D_{\hat{u}} f = \nabla f \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$$

This is the slope of the tangent plane in direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$. The tangent plane encodes all directional derivatives simultaneously — it is the gradient's geometric incarnation.

Maximum slope occurs in direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \frac{\nabla f}{|\nabla f|}$, with value $|\nabla f|$.

## §9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This is where the tangent plane stops being pure mathematics and becomes the engine of modern AI.

### 9.1 — Loss Landscapes and Local Linearization

In machine learning, we have a **loss function** $\mathcal{L}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ where $n$ can be billions (the number of parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ of a neural network). The tangent plane at any point $\boldsymbol{\theta}_0$ in parameter space is:

$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \approx \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0) + \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0)^{\top}(\boldsymbol{\theta} - \boldsymbol{\theta}_0)$$

This is exactly the $n$-dimensional tangent hyperplane. **Gradient descent** moves along this plane to find the steepest downhill direction:

$$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta, \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$

Every gradient descent step is an implicit invocation of the tangent plane: we linearize the loss locally and take a step in the direction of steepest descent on that linearization.

### 9.2 — Backpropagation is Repeated Chain Rule on Tangent Planes

Consider a neural network $f = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1$ where each $f_i$ is a layer. Backpropagation computes $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}}$ via the chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_i} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial f_L} \cdot \frac{\partial f_L}{\partial f_{L-1}} \cdots \frac{\partial f_{i+1}}{\partial f_i} \cdot \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_i}$$

Each factor $\frac{\partial f_k}{\partial f_{k-1}}$ is a **Jacobian matrix** — the multivariable generalization of the derivative, which is precisely the linear map whose graph is the tangent plane (or tangent hyperplane) of $f_k$. Backpropagation is a recursive computation of tangent hyperplanes through the network.

### 9.3 — The Neural Tangent Kernel (NTK)

In the infinite-width limit, training a neural network is equivalent to **kernel regression** with the **Neural Tangent Kernel**:

$$K(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} f(\mathbf{x}; \boldsymbol{\theta})^{\top} \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} f(\mathbf{x}'; \boldsymbol{\theta})$$

This kernel measures the inner product of the tangent vectors (gradients) of the output with respect to parameters. In this regime, the network dynamics are entirely governed by local linearization — the network behaves like its own tangent plane around initialization, and the loss evolves linearly. This is one of the deepest theoretical results in deep learning (Jacot et al., 2018).

### 9.4 — Second-Order Methods and the Hessian

The tangent plane is a first-order (linear) approximation. A second-order approximation uses the **Hessian** $H = \nabla^2 \mathcal{L}$:

$$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) \approx \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_0) + \nabla \mathcal{L}^{\top}\delta\boldsymbol{\theta} + \frac{1}{2}\delta\boldsymbol{\theta}^{\top} H, \delta\boldsymbol{\theta}$$

This is a **quadratic approximation** — a paraboloid tangent to the loss surface. Methods like Newton's method and natural gradient descent exploit this. The tangent plane is the zeroth-order piece of this expansion; understanding it is prerequisite to understanding curvature.

### 9.5 — Feature Space Geometry

In classification, a linear classifier defines a **hyperplane** (tangent-plane-like object) in feature space. Support Vector Machines find the _optimal_ separating hyperplane. When the data is nonlinear, a kernel maps data to a high-dimensional space where it becomes linearly separable — one is finding a tangent plane in the lifted space. Deep networks can be understood as learning a feature map $\phi$ such that $\mathcal{L}(\phi(\mathbf{x}))$ has a tractable tangent structure.

## §11. Theorems to Know Cold

**Theorem 1** _(Differentiability $\Rightarrow$ Continuity)._ If $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$, then $f$ is continuous at $(a,b)$.

**Theorem 2** _(Sufficient Conditions for Differentiability)._ If $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist in an open disk about $(a,b)$ and are continuous at $(a,b)$, then $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$.

**Theorem 3** _(Gradient and Level Curves)._ $\nabla f(a,b)$ is perpendicular to the level curve $f(x,y) = c$ at $(a,b)$.

**Theorem 4** _(Tangent Plane for Implicit Surfaces)._ If $F(x,y,z) = 0$ defines a surface and $F$ is differentiable with $\nabla F \neq \mathbf{0}$, the tangent plane at $(a,b,c)$ is $\nabla F(a,b,c) \cdot \langle x-a, y-b, z-c \rangle = 0$.

**Theorem 5** _(Chain Rule, Gradient Form)._ If $\mathbf{r}(t) = (x(t), y(t))$ is a smooth curve and $f$ is differentiable: $$\frac{d}{dt} f(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t)$$

## §12. Things That Will Trip You Up

> **Partial derivatives exist $\not\Rightarrow$ Differentiable.** The pathological example above ($f = xy/(x^2+y^2)$ at origin) is the canonical counterexample. Always cite it on exams.

> **The tangent plane only makes sense when $f$ is differentiable.** Do not write the tangent plane formula for a point of non-differentiability.

> **The gradient $\nabla f \in \mathbb{R}^2$, not $\mathbb{R}^3$.** The normal to the tangent plane is $\langle f_x, f_y, -1 \rangle$, which includes the $-1$ from the $-z$ term.

> **Linearization $\neq$ exact.** $L(x,y)$ is an approximation; the quality depends on second derivatives (curvature of the surface).
