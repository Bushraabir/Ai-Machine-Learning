

> _"Local extrema tell you about the neighborhood. Absolute extrema tell you about the whole kingdom."_

## 0. Preamble — What Question Are We Really Asking?

A local extremum answers: _"Is this the best point nearby?"_

An absolute extremum answers: _"Is this the best point anywhere in the domain?"_

These are fundamentally different questions. A mountain range may have many local peaks, but only one highest summit. The theory of absolute extrema tells us: under what conditions does a highest summit _exist_, and how do we find it?

The answer rests on three pillars:

1. **Existence** — the Extreme Value Theorem
2. **Interior candidates** — critical points ($\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$)
3. **Boundary candidates** — the boundary of the domain

Miss any pillar, and you will miss the answer.

---

## 1. Definitions

**Definition.** Let $f : D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$.

- $f$ attains an **absolute maximum** at $\mathbf{a} \in D$ if: $$f(\mathbf{a}) \geq f(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \in D$$ The value $f(\mathbf{a})$ is called the **absolute maximum value**.
    
- $f$ attains an **absolute minimum** at $\mathbf{a} \in D$ if: $$f(\mathbf{a}) \leq f(\mathbf{x}) \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \in D$$ The value $f(\mathbf{a})$ is called the **absolute minimum value**.
    

> **Distinction from local extrema.** A local maximum satisfies $f(\mathbf{a}) \geq f(\mathbf{x})$ only for $\mathbf{x}$ _near_ $\mathbf{a}$. An absolute maximum satisfies it for _all_ $\mathbf{x} \in D$. Every absolute extremum is a local extremum (on the interior), but not vice versa.

---

## 2. Existence — The Extreme Value Theorem

Before hunting for extrema, we must know they exist. The following theorem guarantees this.

**Theorem (Extreme Value Theorem — EVT).** _If $f : D \to \mathbb{R}$ is continuous on a closed and bounded set $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, then $f$ attains both its absolute maximum and absolute minimum on $D$. That is, there exist $\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \in D$ such that:_ $$f(\mathbf{a}) \leq f(\mathbf{x}) \leq f(\mathbf{b}) \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \in D$$

This is the multivariable generalization of the single-variable theorem from Stewart §4.1. The proof relies on:

- Continuity $\implies$ $f(D)$ is a connected, bounded subset of $\mathbb{R}$
- Closed and bounded $\implies$ $D$ is **compact** (Heine–Borel)
- Continuous image of compact set is compact $\implies$ $f(D)$ is closed and bounded $\implies$ $f(D)$ contains its supremum and infimum

### 2.1 Why Both Conditions Are Necessary

**"Closed" cannot be dropped.**

Let $f(x) = x$ on the _open_ interval $(0, 1) \subset \mathbb{R}$.

- $f$ is continuous, domain is bounded.
- $\sup f = 1$, but $f(x) = 1$ has no solution in $(0,1)$.
- No absolute maximum exists.

In $\mathbb{R}^2$: $f(x,y) = x$ on the open disk $x^2 + y^2 < 1$. Again, no maximum attained.

**"Bounded" cannot be dropped.**

Let $f(x) = x$ on the _closed_ interval $[1, \infty) \subset \mathbb{R}$.

- $f$ is continuous, domain is closed.
- $f$ is unbounded above — no absolute maximum exists.

In $\mathbb{R}^2$: $f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$ on the closed set $\mathbb{R}^2$. No absolute maximum.

**"Continuous" cannot be dropped.**

Let $f : [0,1] \to \mathbb{R}$ be $f(x) = 1/x$ for $x \in (0,1]$ and $f(0) = 0$.

- Domain is closed and bounded.
- $f$ is not continuous at $0$.
- $f$ is unbounded — no absolute maximum exists.

> The EVT is an **existence** theorem — it guarantees a maximum and minimum exist but says nothing about _where_ they are or _how many_ there are. Finding them requires the method below.

## 3. The Closed Domain Method

This is the algorithm for finding absolute extrema of $f$ on a closed bounded domain $D$.

$$\boxed{\text{Absolute extrema} = \max/\min \text{ of } {f \text{ at interior critical points}} \cup {f \text{ on } \partial D}}$$

**Algorithm:**

> **Step 1.** Find all interior critical points: solve $\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}$ for $\mathbf{x} \in \text{int}(D)$.
> 
> **Step 2.** Find the extreme values of $f$ restricted to the **boundary** $\partial D$. This is itself an optimization problem (on a lower-dimensional set), solved by parameterization or Lagrange multipliers.
> 
> **Step 3.** Evaluate $f$ at all candidates from Steps 1 and 2. The largest value is the absolute maximum; the smallest is the absolute minimum.

**Why this is complete.** By the EVT, absolute extrema exist. Each one is either:

- In the interior of $D$: then by Fermat's theorem it is a critical point → found in Step 1.
- On the boundary $\partial D$: found in Step 2.

No candidates are missed.

## 4. Worked Example I — Triangular Domain

**Problem.** Find the absolute maximum and minimum values of $$f(x,y) = x^2 - xy + y^2 - y$$ on the closed triangular region $D$ with vertices $(0,0)$, $(2,0)$, $(0,2)$.

### Step 1 — Interior Critical Points

$$\nabla f = \langle 2x - y,; -x + 2y - 1 \rangle = \mathbf{0}$$

From the first equation: $y = 2x$. Substituting into the second: $-x + 4x - 1 = 0 \implies x = \frac{1}{3}$, $y = \frac{2}{3}$.

Check: is $\left(\frac{1}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right)$ in the interior of $D$? The triangle has edges $x \geq 0$, $y \geq 0$, $x + y \leq 2$. Check: $\frac{1}{3} + \frac{2}{3} = 1 \leq 2$. ✓

$$f!\left(\tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{2}{3}\right) = \tfrac{1}{9} - \tfrac{2}{9} + \tfrac{4}{9} - \tfrac{2}{3} = \tfrac{3}{9} - \tfrac{6}{9} = -\tfrac{1}{3}$$

### Step 2 — Boundary Analysis

The triangle $D$ has three edges:

**Edge 1:** $y = 0$, $x \in [0, 2]$. $$f(x, 0) = x^2$$ Single-variable: increasing on $[0,2]$. Min at $x=0$: $f=0$. Max at $x=2$: $f=4$.

**Edge 2:** $x = 0$, $y \in [0, 2]$. $$f(0, y) = y^2 - y = y(y-1)$$ Derivative: $2y - 1 = 0 \implies y = \frac{1}{2}$ (interior of this edge). $f!\left(0, \frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{2} = -\frac{1}{4}$. At endpoints: $f(0,0) = 0$, $f(0,2) = 2$.

**Edge 3:** $x + y = 2$, so $x = 2 - y$, $y \in [0, 2]$. $$f(2-y, y) = (2-y)^2 - (2-y)y + y^2 - y$$ $$= 4 - 4y + y^2 - 2y + y^2 + y^2 - y = 3y^2 - 7y + 4$$ Derivative: $6y - 7 = 0 \implies y = \frac{7}{6}$, $x = 2 - \frac{7}{6} = \frac{5}{6}$. $$f!\left(\tfrac{5}{6}, \tfrac{7}{6}\right) = 3 \cdot \tfrac{49}{36} - 7 \cdot \tfrac{7}{6} + 4 = \tfrac{49}{12} - \tfrac{49}{6} + 4 = -\tfrac{49}{12} + 4 = -\tfrac{1}{12}$$ At endpoints: $f(2,0) = 4$, $f(0,2) = 2$.

### Step 3 — Compare All Candidates

|Location|Value|
|---|---|
|$\left(\frac{1}{3}, \frac{2}{3}\right)$ — interior critical point|$-\frac{1}{3} \approx -0.333$|
|$(0, 0)$ — vertex|$0$|
|$(2, 0)$ — vertex|$4$|
|$(0, 2)$ — vertex|$2$|
|$\left(0, \frac{1}{2}\right)$ — Edge 2 critical|$-\frac{1}{4} = -0.25$|
|$\left(\frac{5}{6}, \frac{7}{6}\right)$ — Edge 3 critical|$-\frac{1}{12} \approx -0.083$|

$$\text{Absolute maximum: } 4 \text{ at } (2, 0)$$ $$\text{Absolute minimum: } -\tfrac{1}{3} \text{ at } \left(\tfrac{1}{3}, \tfrac{2}{3}\right)$$

## 5. Worked Example II — Rectangular Domain

**Problem.** Find the absolute extrema of $$f(x,y) = 4x^2 + 9y^2 - 8x - 12y + 4$$ on the rectangle $D = {(x,y) : 0 \leq x \leq 2,; 0 \leq y \leq 2}$.

### Step 1 — Interior Critical Points

$$\nabla f = \langle 8x - 8,; 18y - 12 \rangle = \mathbf{0}$$ $$x = 1, \quad y = \frac{2}{3}$$

Point $\left(1, \frac{2}{3}\right)$ is interior to $D$. ✓

$$f!\left(1, \tfrac{2}{3}\right) = 4 - 8 + 4 - 8 + 4 = -4$$

Let me recompute carefully: $= 4(1) + 9(\frac{4}{9}) - 8(1) - 12(\frac{2}{3}) + 4 = 4 + 4 - 8 - 8 + 4 = -4$.

### Step 2 — Boundary (Four Edges)

**Bottom:** $y = 0$, $x \in [0,2]$: $f = 4x^2 - 8x + 4 = 4(x-1)^2$. Critical: $x = 1$, $f = 0$. Endpoints: $f(0,0) = 4$, $f(2,0) = 0$.

**Top:** $y = 2$, $x \in [0,2]$: $f = 4x^2 - 8x + 4 + 36 - 24 = 4(x-1)^2 + 16$. Min at $x=1$: $f = 16$. Endpoints: $f(0,2) = 20$, $f(2,2) = 16$.

**Left:** $x = 0$, $y \in [0,2]$: $f = 9y^2 - 12y + 4 = (3y-2)^2$. Critical: $y = \frac{2}{3}$, $f = 0$. Endpoints: $f(0,0) = 4$, $f(0,2) = 16$.

**Right:** $x = 2$, $y \in [0,2]$: $f = 16 + 9y^2 - 16 - 12y + 4 = 9y^2 - 12y + 4 = (3y-2)^2$. Critical: $y = \frac{2}{3}$, $f = 0$. Endpoints: $f(2,0) = 0$, $f(2,2) = 16$.

### Step 3 — Compare

All candidate values: ${-4, 0, 4, 16, 20}$.

$$\text{Absolute maximum: } 20 \text{ at } (0, 2)$$ $$\text{Absolute minimum: } -4 \text{ at } \left(1, \frac{2}{3}\right)$$

## 6. Worked Example III — Disk Domain

**Problem.** Find the absolute extrema of $$f(x,y) = x^2 + 2y^2 - 2x$$ on the closed disk $D = {(x,y) : x^2 + y^2 \leq 4}$.

### Step 1 — Interior Critical Points

$$\nabla f = \langle 2x - 2,; 4y \rangle = \mathbf{0} \implies x = 1,; y = 0$$

Check: $1^2 + 0^2 = 1 \leq 4$, so $(1,0)$ is interior. ✓

$$f(1, 0) = 1 + 0 - 2 = -1$$

### Step 2 — Boundary: Circle $x^2 + y^2 = 4$

**Parameterize:** $x = 2\cos t$, $y = 2\sin t$, $t \in [0, 2\pi]$.

$$f(2\cos t, 2\sin t) = 4\cos^2 t + 8\sin^2 t - 4\cos t$$ $$= 4\cos^2 t + 8(1 - \cos^2 t) - 4\cos t$$ $$= 8 - 4\cos^2 t - 4\cos t$$

Let $u = \cos t \in [-1, 1]$. Minimize/maximize $h(u) = -4u^2 - 4u + 8$.

$$h'(u) = -8u - 4 = 0 \implies u = -\frac{1}{2}$$

$h!\left(-\frac{1}{2}\right) = -4 \cdot \frac{1}{4} + 2 + 8 = 9$.

At endpoints: $h(1) = -4 - 4 + 8 = 0$ and $h(-1) = -4 + 4 + 8 = 8$.

So on the boundary:

- Max: $f = 9$ at $\cos t = -\frac{1}{2}$, i.e., $t = \frac{2\pi}{3}$ or $\frac{4\pi}{3}$, giving points $\left(-1, \pm\sqrt{3}\right)$.
- Min: $f = 0$ at $t = 0$, i.e., point $(2, 0)$.

### Step 3 — Compare

|Candidate|Value|
|---|---|
|$(1,0)$ — interior|$-1$|
|$(-1, \pm\sqrt{3})$ — boundary|$9$|
|$(2, 0)$ — boundary|$0$|

$$\text{Absolute maximum: } 9 \text{ at } (-1, \pm\sqrt{3})$$ $$\text{Absolute minimum: } -1 \text{ at } (1, 0)$$

## 7. Visualization

### 7.1 Why Boundary Matters — A Schematic

```
     Domain D (closed rectangle):

     ┌──────────────────────┐  ← boundary ∂D
     │                      │
     │   ×  local max       │
     │                      │
     │         ★ abs. max   │  ← abs max ON boundary
     │                      │
     │   ·  local min       │
     │                      │
     └──────────────────────┘
          ↑
          abs. min (interior critical point here)

  The absolute max lives on the boundary, NOT at any interior
  critical point. If you only check ∇f = 0, you miss it entirely.
```

### 7.2 Level Curve View

```
  f(x,y) = x² + y²    on disk x² + y² ≤ 4

        f = 4  ← boundary circle
      f = 3
    f = 2
   f = 1
  f = 0  ← absolute minimum at origin

  Level curves are concentric circles.
  Absolute min: origin (interior critical point ∇f = 0).
  Absolute max: entire boundary circle x²+y² = 4.
  The max is attained everywhere on ∂D — not unique.
```

### 7.3 Cross-Section Intuition

```
  z = f(x, y) viewed as a surface:

         z
         │        ·  ·  ·   ← high values at boundary
         │      ·           (absolute max somewhere on ∂D)
         │    ·
  ───────┼──·──────·──────── D (projection onto xy-plane)
         │·               ↑
         ·                boundary ∂D
    absolute min
    (interior dip)

  The surface may dip lowest in the interior (interior min)
  or rise highest at the boundary (boundary max).
  We must check BOTH regions to be sure.
```

## 8. Special Case — Absolute Extrema on a Curve (1D Constraint)

Sometimes the domain $D$ is itself a curve $g(x,y) = c$ (a 1D set). This arises naturally as the boundary problem in Step 2. Two methods:

### Method A — Parameterization

Parameterize the curve $\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t)$ and optimize the single-variable function $f(\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t))$.

Best when the curve has a clean parameterization (circle → $(\cos t, \sin t)$, line segment → $(1-t)\mathbf{a} + t\mathbf{b}$).

### Method B — Lagrange Multipliers

Solve $\nabla f = \lambda \nabla g$, $g = c$.

Best when parameterization is messy. Gives the same candidates.

> Both methods must be used with care: neither automatically delivers the absolute max/min. You still need to evaluate $f$ at all candidates and compare.

## 9. Common Mistakes and How to Avoid Them

|Mistake|Why It Fails|Correction|
|---|---|---|
|Only solving $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$|Misses absolute extrema on $\partial D$|Always analyze the boundary|
|Forgetting to check if critical point is in $D$|A point outside $D$ is not a candidate|Verify each critical point lies in $D$|
|Not checking all boundary pieces|A rectangular domain has 4 edges and 4 corners|Be systematic; list every piece|
|Assuming interior min/max is absolute|A local extremum need not be global|Compare against all boundary values|
|Forgetting corner points of boundary|Corners are endpoints of edge sub-problems|Always evaluate $f$ at all vertices/corners|
|Applying EVT on open domain|EVT requires closed and bounded|Verify domain before applying EVT|

## 10. Connection to Machine Learning and Deep Learning

### 10.1 Absolute Minimum = The Goal of Training

When training a neural network, we seek the **absolute minimum** of the loss function $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ over the parameter space. This is the global optimum — the parameter setting that makes the model best on training data.

The EVT guarantees: if we restrict to a closed bounded parameter region and $\mathcal{L}$ is continuous, the absolute minimum exists. In practice, $\boldsymbol{\theta} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ (unbounded), so the EVT does not directly apply — existence of a global minimum is a non-trivial property of the loss function.

### 10.2 Why Absolute Minima Are Hard to Find in Deep Learning

In $\mathbb{R}^n$ with $n \sim 10^9$ (modern LLMs):

- There are exponentially many critical points.
- Most critical points are saddle points (Hessian is indefinite), not local minima.
- The **boundary** of parameter space is at $|\boldsymbol{\theta}| \to \infty$, where loss typically blows up — so boundary analysis is trivial (exclude).
- Local minima exist, but the **absolute minimum** is essentially unreachable by gradient descent.

Strikingly, this turns out to be _fine_ in practice: overparameterized networks have many local minima with nearly identical (and very low) loss values. The "absolute" minimum vs a "good local" minimum distinction becomes irrelevant — gradient descent finds solutions that generalize well even without reaching the true global minimum.

### 10.3 Constrained Absolute Optimization in ML

**Weight constraints.** Regularization imposes effective constraints: minimizing $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) + \lambda|\boldsymbol{\theta}|^2$ is equivalent to minimizing $\mathcal{L}$ on the closed ball $|\boldsymbol{\theta}| \leq R$. On this closed bounded set, the EVT applies — the absolute minimum exists.

**Projection methods.** Some algorithms explicitly project $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ back to a feasible closed set after each gradient step, enforcing constraints. This is a direct application of the closed domain method: minimize over interior (unconstrained gradient step) then handle boundary (projection).

**Hyperparameter tuning.** When searching for optimal hyperparameters (learning rate, batch size) over a closed bounded grid, the EVT guarantees the absolute best hyperparameter combination exists within the grid — then we search for it systematically, exactly analogous to the closed domain method.

### 10.4 The Closed Domain Method as a Template for Search

The three-step method — (1) interior candidates, (2) boundary candidates, (3) compare — is the template for _any_ complete optimization procedure:

|ML Setting|"Interior" candidates|"Boundary" candidates|
|---|---|---|
|Gradient descent training|Converged parameter (approx $\nabla\mathcal{L} = 0$)|Constrained region boundary (clipping, projection)|
|Architecture search|Interior model configs|Edge cases (depth = 1, width = 1)|
|Hyperparameter search|Optima found by Bayesian opt|Grid boundary values|
|Reinforcement learning|Stationary policy|Terminal states / boundary of state space|

The mathematical structure is identical: complete search requires covering both the interior and the boundary.

## 11. Summary

The method to find absolute extrema of a continuous $f$ on a closed bounded domain $D$:

$$\underbrace{\nabla f = \mathbf{0} \text{ in int}(D)}_{\text{Step 1: interior}} \quad \cup \quad \underbrace{f\big|_{\partial D}}_{\text{Step 2: boundary}} \quad \xrightarrow{\text{evaluate and compare}} \quad \text{absolute max and min}$$

The EVT guarantees they exist. The closed domain method guarantees you find them. Together they are complete.
