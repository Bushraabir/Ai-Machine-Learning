## 0. The Central Question

When we write $\frac{df}{dx}$ in single-variable calculus, the interpretation is unambiguous: it is the instantaneous rate of change of $f$ as $x$ moves, and geometrically it is the slope of the tangent line.

In multivariable calculus, $f$ lives on a surface (or hypersurface). There is no single "slope" — the surface rises and falls differently in every direction. The partial derivative is one specific answer to the question: _"what is the slope if I walk in a coordinate direction?"_

The richness of this note is in understanding exactly what that means — geometrically, analytically, physically, and computationally.

---

## 1. The Frozen-Variable Interpretation

### Setup

Let $f(x, y)$ be defined near $(a, b)$. Consider the two functions obtained by freezing one variable:

$$g(x) := f(x,, b) \qquad \text{(freeze } y = b\text{)}$$ $$h(y) := f(a,, y) \qquad \text{(freeze } x = a\text{)}$$

These are perfectly ordinary single-variable functions. Then:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(a,b) = g'(a), \qquad \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(a,b) = h'(b)$$

**Interpretation:** $f_x(a,b)$ is the rate at which $f$ changes per unit increase in $x$, when $y$ is held fixed at $b$.

### Why "Held Fixed" Matters

Suppose $f(x,y) = x^2 + xy$. At the point $(1,2)$:

$$f_x = 2x + y \implies f_x(1,2) = 4$$

This says: if we increase $x$ from $1$ by a tiny amount $\Delta x$, while keeping $y$ locked at $2$, the function increases by approximately $4\Delta x$.

If instead $y$ were also changing — say along some curve — the rate of change of $f$ would be completely different. That computation requires the **total derivative**, not the partial.

## 2. Geometric Interpretation — Slicing the Surface

This is the most important picture to have in your mind.

### The Surface and Its Slices

$z = f(x,y)$ defines a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Fix a point $P = (a, b, f(a,b))$ on this surface.

**Slice with the plane $y = b$:** This cuts the surface along a curve $C_1$, which lies in the $xz$-plane (translated to $y=b$). The curve is parameterized by $x \mapsto (x, b, f(x,b))$.

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(a,b) = \text{slope of the tangent line to } C_1 \text{ at } P$$

**Slice with the plane $x = a$:** This cuts the surface along a curve $C_2$. The curve is parameterized by $y \mapsto (a, y, f(a,y))$.

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial y}(a,b) = \text{slope of the tangent line to } C_2 \text{ at } P$$

```
           z
           |
           |       surface z = f(x,y)
           |      ·····P·····
           |    ··/    |    \··
           |  ·· /     |     \ ··
           | ·  C₁     |      C₂ ·
           |·  slope   |   slope  ·
           |  = f_x    |   = f_y
           +----------------------------  x
          /
         / y

  C₁: slice y=b → slope in x-direction → f_x(a,b)
  C₂: slice x=a → slope in y-direction → f_y(a,b)
```

**Key remark:** These two tangent lines, together, span the **tangent plane** to the surface at $P$. The tangent plane is:

$$z - f(a,b) = f_x(a,b)(x-a) + f_y(a,b)(y-b)$$

So the partial derivatives are not just slopes — they are the _coefficients that define the best linear approximation to the surface_.

### Example 2.1 — Visualizing a Saddle

Let $f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2$ (a hyperbolic paraboloid — a saddle).

$$f_x = 2x, \quad f_y = -2y$$

At the origin $(0,0)$: $f_x = 0$, $f_y = 0$. The tangent plane is $z = 0$ — flat.

At $(1,1)$: $f_x = 2$, $f_y = -2$. Interpretation:

- Walking in the $+x$ direction at $(1,1)$: the surface rises with slope $2$.
- Walking in the $+y$ direction at $(1,1)$: the surface falls with slope $-2$.

The same point, two different slopes, two different directions. This is the fundamental new feature of multivariable calculus.

## 3. Rate-of-Change Interpretation — Units and Dimensions

In applied contexts, partial derivatives carry units and must be interpreted accordingly.

### Example 3.1 — Ideal Gas Law

The pressure of an ideal gas: $P = \frac{nRT}{V}$, where $T$ = temperature, $V$ = volume, $n$ = moles, $R$ = gas constant.

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial T}\bigg|_V = \frac{nR}{V}$$

**Interpretation:** At constant volume, pressure increases at a rate of $nR/V$ pascals per kelvin. The subscript $|_V$ is physics notation for "holding $V$ fixed" — this is precisely the partial derivative.

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\bigg|_T = -\frac{nRT}{V^2}$$

**Interpretation:** At constant temperature, pressure decreases as volume increases. The negative sign reflects Boyle's Law. The rate of decrease is faster when $V$ is small (denominator is $V^2$).

### Example 3.2 — Economics: Marginal Products

Suppose output $Q = f(K, L) = K^{0.4} L^{0.6}$ (Cobb-Douglas production function).

$$\frac{\partial Q}{\partial L} = 0.6 K^{0.4} L^{-0.4} = 0.6 \cdot \frac{Q}{L}$$

**Interpretation:** This is the **marginal product of labor** — the additional output from one more unit of labor, holding capital $K$ fixed. Note it decreases as $L$ increases (diminishing returns), since $L^{-0.4}$ falls as $L$ grows.

**General principle:** In any applied model, $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}$ has units of $\frac{[f]}{[x_i]}$ and measures the _marginal sensitivity_ of $f$ to $x_i$, all else being equal. The phrase "all else equal" (_ceteris paribus_) in economics _is_ the mathematical statement of holding other variables fixed.

## 4. The Linear Approximation Interpretation

This is arguably the most computationally powerful interpretation.

### Theorem (Differentiability and Linear Approximation)

If $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$, then for small $\Delta x, \Delta y$:

$$f(a + \Delta x,, b + \Delta y) \approx f(a,b) + f_x(a,b),\Delta x + f_y(a,b),\Delta y$$

The error in this approximation is $o(\sqrt{\Delta x^2 + \Delta y^2})$ — it goes to zero faster than the distance $|(\Delta x, \Delta y)|$.

**Interpretation of each term:**

- $f_x(a,b),\Delta x$: contribution to the change in $f$ from moving $\Delta x$ in the $x$-direction
- $f_y(a,b),\Delta y$: contribution from moving $\Delta y$ in the $y$-direction

These contributions are _additive_ — that is the content of linearity, and it's an approximation that becomes exact only in the limit.

### Example 4.1 — Error Estimation

The volume of a cylinder: $V = \pi r^2 h$. Measured values: $r = 5$ cm, $h = 10$ cm, with measurement errors $\Delta r = 0.1$ cm, $\Delta h = 0.2$ cm. Estimate the error in $V$.

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial r} = 2\pi r h = 100\pi, \qquad \frac{\partial V}{\partial h} = \pi r^2 = 25\pi$$

$$\Delta V \approx \frac{\partial V}{\partial r}\Delta r + \frac{\partial V}{\partial h}\Delta h = 100\pi(0.1) + 25\pi(0.2) = 10\pi + 5\pi = 15\pi \approx 47.1 \text{ cm}^3$$

The partial derivatives tell us _which variable's error matters more_: here, $r$ contributes $10\pi$ and $h$ contributes $5\pi$ — the radius error is twice as damaging, because $V$ depends on $r^2$.

## 5. Directional Derivative — Partial Derivatives as Special Cases

The partial derivatives are the most restrictive possible directional rates of change: they measure change only along coordinate axes. The full picture requires the **directional derivative**.

### Definition

For a unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = (u_1, u_2)$, the directional derivative of $f$ at $(a,b)$ in direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is:

$$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(a,b) = \lim_{t \to 0} \frac{f(a + tu_1,, b + tu_2) - f(a,b)}{t}$$

**Theorem:** If $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$, then:

$$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(a,b) = \nabla f(a,b) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}} = f_x(a,b),u_1 + f_y(a,b),u_2$$

**Special cases:**

- $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = (1,0)$: $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = f_x$ — the partial derivative in $x$
- $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = (0,1)$: $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = f_y$ — the partial derivative in $y$
- $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \frac{\nabla f}{|\nabla f|}$: $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = |\nabla f|$ — **maximum rate of increase**
- $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = -\frac{\nabla f}{|\nabla f|}$: $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = -|\nabla f|$ — **maximum rate of decrease**
- $\hat{\mathbf{u}} \perp \nabla f$: $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = 0$ — **no change** (moving along a level curve)

**The gradient is the master object.** Once you know $\nabla f$, you know the rate of change in _every_ direction. The partial derivatives are simply the components that assemble it.

```
                ∇f (steepest ascent)
               ↑
               |
    ← f_x →   |       ← level curve (Df = 0)
               |     /
     f_y ↑     |    /
               |   /
               |  /  ← arbitrary direction û
               | /
               |/
               P
```

## 6. Sign and Magnitude — What They Tell You

Given $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(a)$:

|Value|Interpretation|
|---|---|
|$> 0$|$f$ increases as $x_i$ increases (holding others fixed)|
|$< 0$|$f$ decreases as $x_i$ increases|
|$= 0$|$f$ has no first-order sensitivity to $x_i$ at this point|
|Large $|{\ }|
|Small $|{\ }|

### Example 6.1 — Sensitivity Analysis

$f(x,y) = e^{x} + \ln(1+y^2)$ at $(0,0)$:

$$f_x(0,0) = e^0 = 1, \qquad f_y(0,0) = \frac{2y}{1+y^2}\bigg|_{(0,0)} = 0$$

Near the origin, $f$ is sensitive to $x$ but has **zero first-order sensitivity** to $y$. This means a small perturbation in $y$ changes $f$ only at second order — the function is locally "flat" in the $y$-direction at this point.

This is critical in optimization: $f_y = 0$ at a point means $y$ is at a potential extremum in the $y$-direction (it's a necessary condition, not sufficient).

## 7. Interpretation via the Tangent Plane

The tangent plane at $(a, b, f(a,b))$ to the surface $z = f(x,y)$ is:

$$z = f(a,b) + f_x(a,b)(x-a) + f_y(a,b)(y-b)$$

**Interpretation of the coefficients:**

- $f_x(a,b)$: the slope of the tangent plane in the $x$-direction
- $f_y(a,b)$: the slope of the tangent plane in the $y$-direction

The tangent plane is the **best linear approximation** to the surface at $P$. It is to a surface what the tangent line is to a curve.

The normal vector to the tangent plane is:

$$\mathbf{n} = \langle f_x(a,b),, f_y(a,b),, -1 \rangle$$

(This follows because the plane $z = f(a,b) + f_x(x-a) + f_y(y-b)$ can be written $f_x(x-a) + f_y(y-b) - (z - f(a,b)) = 0$.)

### Example 7.1

$f(x,y) = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$ (a cone). At $(3,4)$:

$$f = 5, \quad f_x = \frac{x}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2}} = \frac{3}{5}, \quad f_y = \frac{4}{5}$$

Tangent plane: $z = 5 + \frac{3}{5}(x-3) + \frac{4}{5}(y-4)$

The slope $\frac{3}{5}$ in the $x$-direction reflects that at $(3,4)$, moving in $x$ raises the cone surface, but gently — the slope is less than $1$ because we're not moving radially outward.

## 8. Interpretation of Second-Order Partials

The second partials have their own interpretations:

$$f_{xx}(a,b) = \text{concavity of the cross-section curve } C_1 \text{ at } P$$ $$f_{yy}(a,b) = \text{concavity of the cross-section curve } C_2 \text{ at } P$$ $$f_{xy}(a,b) = \text{rate of change of the } x\text{-slope as we move in the } y\text{-direction}$$

The mixed partial $f_{xy}$ has a subtle interpretation: it asks _"as I move in the $y$-direction, how does the slope in the $x$-direction change?"_ If $f_{xy} > 0$, the $x$-slope increases as $y$ increases — the surface is twisting.

### The Second Derivative Test (Multivariable)

At a critical point $(a,b)$ where $\nabla f = \mathbf{0}$, define the **discriminant**:

$$D = f_{xx} f_{yy} - (f_{xy})^2 = \det(H)$$

where $H$ is the Hessian matrix. Then:

- $D > 0$, $f_{xx} > 0$: local minimum
- $D > 0$, $f_{xx} < 0$: local maximum
- $D < 0$: saddle point
- $D = 0$: inconclusive

**Interpretation:** $D$ measures whether the second-order behavior is "bowl-shaped" (both directions concave up/down) or "saddle-shaped" (one direction curves up, the other down). The role of $f_{xy}^2$ is to account for the twisting of the surface between coordinate directions.

## 9. Interpretation in AI and Machine Learning

### 9.1 Gradients as Sensitivity Maps

In a trained neural network, the gradient $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i}$ at the current parameter values tells you the sensitivity of the loss to each individual weight.

- A **large** $\left|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i}\right|$: this weight matters — changing it changes the loss significantly.
- A **near-zero** gradient: the weight is at a local extremum in its direction, or the loss is insensitive to it.

This is directly the rate-of-change interpretation applied to the loss landscape.

### 9.2 Saliency Maps in Deep Learning (Input Gradients)

Given a trained image classifier $f(\mathbf{x})$ where $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^{H \times W \times 3}$ (an image), compute:

$$S_{ij} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_{ij}}$$

This is the partial derivative of the model output with respect to each pixel intensity. **Interpretation:** it measures how much the predicted class score changes if we perturb pixel $(i,j)$ by a tiny amount, with all other pixels held fixed. Pixels with large $|S_{ij}|$ are the ones the model is "looking at" — this gives a **saliency map**, a visual explanation of what the model found important.

```
  Input Image         Saliency Map (|∂f/∂x_ij|)
  +-----------+        +-----------+
  |           |        |    ···    |
  |  cat 🐱   |  →     |  · HOT ·  |
  |           |        |    ···    |
  +-----------+        +-----------+
                       bright = high |∂f/∂x_ij|
                       = model attends here
```

### 9.3 Learning Rate Sensitivity

The update rule in gradient descent:

$$\theta_i \leftarrow \theta_i - \eta \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i}$$

The _magnitude_ of the partial derivative determines how large a step is taken for a fixed learning rate $\eta$. If $\left|\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i}\right|$ is very large for some $i$ (steep landscape), training can be unstable — this is the source of the **exploding gradients** problem in deep networks.

Gradient clipping, batch normalization, and careful initialization are all techniques that manage the _magnitudes_ of partial derivatives during training.

### 9.4 Hessian Eigenvalues and Loss Geometry

The second-order partial interpretations directly explain the geometry of the loss landscape:

- $f_{xx} > 0$ and $f_{yy} > 0$ at a critical point → local minimum → gradient descent will converge here.
- $f_{xx} > 0$ and $f_{yy} < 0$ → saddle point → gradient descent may escape, but saddle points are actually common and benign in high dimensions.
- Large Hessian eigenvalues → sharp curvature → small learning rate required; **sharp minima** tend to generalize worse (Hochreiter & Schmidhuber, 1997).
- Small eigenvalues → flat directions in loss landscape → large learning rates tolerable; **flat minima** tend to generalize better.

### 9.5 Partial Derivatives in Attention — A Concrete Calculation

In a transformer's scaled dot-product attention:

$$\text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}!\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

During backpropagation, we need $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial Q_{ij}}$ — the partial of the loss with respect to each entry of the query matrix. This chains through the softmax and matrix multiplications, all via the multivariable chain rule. The geometric content: we are asking "how sensitive is the overall loss to this one attention query weight, holding all others fixed?"

## 10. The Partial Derivative Does NOT Tell You Everything

This is worth its own section, because it is the most common conceptual error.

### 10.1 What It Misses: Off-Axis Directions

$f_x(a,b)$ only measures the rate of change along the $x$-axis. The rate of change in a diagonal direction, say $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1,1)$, is:

$$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = \frac{f_x + f_y}{\sqrt{2}}$$

which is not determined by $f_x$ alone.

### 10.2 Partials Exist but Function is Discontinuous

Consider the classic example:

$$f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy}{x^2 + y^2} & (x,y) \neq (0,0) \ 0 & (x,y) = (0,0) \end{cases}$$

$$f_x(0,0) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(h,0) - f(0,0)}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{0}{h} = 0$$

Similarly $f_y(0,0) = 0$. Both partial derivatives exist. But:

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)} f(x,y) \text{ does not exist}$$

(approaching along $y=x$ gives $\frac{1}{2}$; along axes gives $0$). So $f$ is **discontinuous** at the origin, despite having both partial derivatives there.

**Moral:** The partial derivative is a fundamentally limited probe. It only tests the function along two specific paths through the point. Differentiability (which requires the linear approximation to work in _all_ directions) is the correct notion of "smoothness."

## 11. Summary — A Hierarchy of Interpretations

```
PARTIAL DERIVATIVE f_x(a,b)
│
├─ GEOMETRIC: slope of the surface slice y = b at P
│
├─ ANALYTICAL: derivative of g(x) = f(x,b) at x = a
│
├─ APPROXIMATION: coefficient in the tangent plane / linear approximation
│   Δf ≈ f_x Δx + f_y Δy
│
├─ SENSITIVITY: rate of change of f per unit increase in x, holding y fixed
│   units = [f] / [x]
│
├─ DIRECTIONAL: special case of D_û f when û = ê_x = (1,0)
│   → gradient packages all such cases
│
└─ OPTIMIZATION: 
    f_x = 0 is necessary for x to be at an extremum (in x-direction)
    |f_x| large → steep landscape → sensitive to parameter x
    → backpropagation, gradient descent, saliency maps
```

## 12. Worked Example — Full Interpretation

Let $f(x,y) = x^2 y + \sin(\pi y)$.

**Compute partials:** $$f_x = 2xy, \qquad f_y = x^2 + \pi\cos(\pi y)$$

**At the point $(2, 1)$:** $$f_x(2,1) = 4, \qquad f_y(2,1) = 4 + \pi\cos(\pi) = 4 - \pi \approx 0.858$$

**Geometric:** The surface $z = f(x,y)$, sliced by $y = 1$, has a tangent line of slope $4$ at $x=2$. Sliced by $x=2$, the tangent line has slope $\approx 0.858$ at $y=1$.

**Tangent plane:** $z = f(2,1) + 4(x-2) + (4-\pi)(y-1)$

$f(2,1) = 4(1) + \sin(\pi) = 4$, so: $z = 4 + 4(x-2) + (4-\pi)(y-1)$

**Linear approximation:** Near $(2,1)$, $f(2.1, 0.9) \approx 4 + 4(0.1) + (4-\pi)(-0.1) = 4 + 0.4 - 0.0858 = 4.314$

Exact: $f(2.1, 0.9) = (2.1)^2(0.9) + \sin(0.9\pi) = 3.969 + \sin(162°) = 3.969 + 0.309 = 4.278$

The approximation (4.314) vs exact (4.278): error of $\approx 0.036$, small as expected. ✓

**Sensitivity:** At $(2,1)$, $f$ is much more sensitive to $x$ (slope 4) than to $y$ (slope $\approx 0.858$). A 1% change in $x$ causes roughly $4.7\times$ more change in $f$ than a 1% change in $y$.
