

> _"The gradient is not merely a computational device. It is the geometric soul of a differentiable function."_

---

## 0. Preamble — Why Should We Care?

Before we define anything, let me state the central thesis of this note:

> **The gradient $\nabla f$ at a point $\mathbf{p}$ encodes, in a single vector, the complete first-order local behaviour of $f$ in all directions simultaneously.**

This is remarkable. A scalar function $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ has infinitely many directional derivatives at any point. Yet one vector — the gradient — determines all of them. Understanding _why_ this is true, and what geometric picture it paints, is the entire content of this note.

---

## 1. Setup and Definitions

### 1.1 Partial Derivatives — The Raw Ingredients

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function of $n$ variables. Write $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$.

**Definition.** The _partial derivative_ of $f$ with respect to $x_i$ at $\mathbf{a}$ is: $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{a} + h,\mathbf{e}_i) - f(\mathbf{a})}{h}$$ where $\mathbf{e}_i$ is the $i$-th standard basis vector.

This is just the ordinary derivative of $f$ when all variables except $x_i$ are frozen at their values in $\mathbf{a}$.

### 1.2 The Gradient — Assembling the Partials

**Definition.** If all partial derivatives of $f$ exist at $\mathbf{a}$, the _gradient_ of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$ is the vector: $$\nabla f(\mathbf{a}) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}(\mathbf{a}),; \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}(\mathbf{a}),; \ldots,; \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}(\mathbf{a})\right) \in \mathbb{R}^n$$

In two variables, this is just: $$\nabla f(x,y) = \left\langle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x},; \frac{\partial f}{\partial y} \right\rangle$$

> ⚠️ **A crucial remark.** Existence of all partials does **not** guarantee differentiability (i.e., a good linear approximation). The function $f(x,y) = \frac{xy}{x^2+y^2}$ (with $f(0,0)=0$) has both partials zero at the origin, yet is not even continuous there. Differentiability requires the stronger condition that the linear map $\mathbf{h} \mapsto \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{h}$ approximates $f(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}) - f(\mathbf{a})$ with error $o(|\mathbf{h}|)$. In what follows, we assume $f$ is $C^1$ (continuous partial derivatives), which _does_ guarantee differentiability.

## 2. The Directional Derivative — Rates of Change in Any Direction

### 2.1 Definition

**Definition.** Let $\hat{\mathbf{u}} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a unit vector ($|\hat{\mathbf{u}}| = 1$). The _directional derivative_ of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$ in direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is: $$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(\mathbf{a} + h\hat{\mathbf{u}}) - f(\mathbf{a})}{h}$$

Note that $D_{\mathbf{e}_i} f = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}$, so partial derivatives are special cases.

### 2.2 The Fundamental Theorem — The Gradient Does It All

**Theorem.** _If $f$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$, then for any unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$:_ $$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$$

**Proof.** Define $g(t) = f(\mathbf{a} + t\hat{\mathbf{u}})$. Then $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(\mathbf{a}) = g'(0)$.

By the chain rule applied to $g(t) = f(a_1 + t u_1, \ldots, a_n + t u_n)$: $$g'(t) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a} + t\hat{\mathbf{u}}) \cdot u_i$$

Setting $t = 0$: $$g'(0) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) \cdot u_i = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}} \qquad \square$$

This is the engine behind everything. A single dot product with $\nabla f$ gives you the rate of change in _any_ direction.

## 3. The Four Geometric Significances

### 3.1 Significance I — Direction of Steepest Ascent

By the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality: $$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}} = |\nabla f(\mathbf{a})| \cos\theta$$ where $\theta$ is the angle between $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ and $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$.

This has immediate consequences:

|Direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$|$\cos\theta$|$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f$|Geometric meaning|
|---|---|---|---|
|Along $\nabla f$|$1$|$\|\nabla f\|$|**Steepest ascent**|
|Against $\nabla f$|$-1$|$-\|\nabla f\|$|**Steepest descent**|
|$\perp$ to $\nabla f$|$0$|$0$|**No change** (tangent to level set)|

> **Therefore:** $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ points in the direction of the _maximum rate of increase_ of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$, and its magnitude $|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})|$ _is_ that maximum rate.

**Example.** Let $f(x,y) = x^2 + 4y^2$ (an elliptic paraboloid). At $\mathbf{a} = (1, 1)$: $$\nabla f(1,1) = \langle 2x, 8y \rangle\big|_{(1,1)} = \langle 2, 8 \rangle$$

- Direction of steepest ascent: $\frac{1}{\sqrt{68}}\langle 2, 8\rangle$
- Maximum rate of increase: $|\langle 2,8\rangle| = \sqrt{68} = 2\sqrt{17}$
- If we walk in the direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \langle 1, 0\rangle$ (purely along $x$): $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = \langle 2,8\rangle \cdot \langle 1,0\rangle = 2$. This is exactly $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = 2$. ✓

### 3.2 Significance II — Perpendicularity to Level Sets (Curves/Surfaces)

This is perhaps the deepest geometric fact about the gradient.

**Theorem.** _Let $c = f(\mathbf{a})$ and let $S = {\mathbf{x} : f(\mathbf{x}) = c}$ be the level set through $\mathbf{a}$. If $\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t)$ is any smooth curve in $S$ with $\boldsymbol{\gamma}(0) = \mathbf{a}$, then:_ $$\nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \perp \boldsymbol{\gamma}'(0)$$

**Proof.** Since $\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t) \in S$ for all $t$, we have $f(\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t)) = c$ for all $t$. Differentiating with the chain rule: $$\frac{d}{dt}\bigl[f(\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t))\bigr] = \nabla f(\boldsymbol{\gamma}(t)) \cdot \boldsymbol{\gamma}'(t) = 0$$

At $t = 0$: $\nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \boldsymbol{\gamma}'(0) = 0$. Since this holds for _any_ tangent vector to $S$ at $\mathbf{a}$, we conclude $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ is normal to $S$ at $\mathbf{a}$. $\square$

**In $\mathbb{R}^2$:** The gradient is perpendicular to level curves.

**In $\mathbb{R}^3$:** The gradient is perpendicular to level surfaces; it is the normal vector to the surface $f(x,y,z) = c$.

**Application — Tangent Planes.** The equation of the tangent plane to $f(x,y,z) = c$ at $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, a_2, a_3)$ is: $$\nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}) = 0$$ i.e., $$f_x(\mathbf{a})(x - a_1) + f_y(\mathbf{a})(y - a_2) + f_z(\mathbf{a})(z - a_3) = 0$$

**Example.** Tangent plane to the ellipsoid $x^2 + 2y^2 + 3z^2 = 6$ at $(1, 1, 1)$:

Let $f(x,y,z) = x^2 + 2y^2 + 3z^2$. Then $\nabla f = \langle 2x, 4y, 6z\rangle$.

At $(1,1,1)$: $\nabla f = \langle 2, 4, 6\rangle$.

Tangent plane: $2(x-1) + 4(y-1) + 6(z-1) = 0 \implies x + 2y + 3z = 6$.

### 3.3 Significance III — The Gradient as the Best Linear Approximation

The full force of differentiability says that near $\mathbf{a}$: $$f(\mathbf{a} + \mathbf{h}) \approx f(\mathbf{a}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{h}$$ with error $o(|\mathbf{h}|)$. This is the _linearization_ of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$.

Written out in two variables: $$L(x,y) = f(a,b) + f_x(a,b)(x-a) + f_y(a,b)(y-b)$$

This is the tangent plane to the _graph_ $z = f(x,y)$ at the point $(a, b, f(a,b))$.

> The gradient is the coefficient vector of the unique affine function that best approximates $f$ locally.

### 3.4 Significance IV — The Magnitude Measures How "Steep" the Landscape Is

$|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})|$ is the maximum possible directional derivative at $\mathbf{a}$. Where $|\nabla f|$ is large, the function changes rapidly; where it is small (or zero, at a critical point), the function is "flat" locally.

At a **critical point** $\nabla f(\mathbf{a}) = \mathbf{0}$: every directional derivative vanishes, meaning $\mathbf{a}$ is a candidate for a local extremum or saddle point.

## 4. A Worked Example — Reading the Gradient Field

Let $f(x,y) = \sin(x)\cos(y)$.

$$\nabla f = \langle \cos x \cos y,; -\sin x \sin y\rangle$$

At $(0, 0)$: $\nabla f = \langle 1, 0\rangle$ — the function increases most rapidly in the $+x$ direction, and the level curve through the origin has a horizontal tangent (level curve is $f = 0$, i.e., $\sin x \cos y = 0$, which is $x = 0$ near the origin — a vertical line, and indeed $\nabla f = \langle 1,0\rangle \perp \langle 0,1\rangle$). ✓

At $(\pi/2, 0)$: $\nabla f = \langle 0, 0\rangle$ — critical point. Indeed $f(\pi/2, 0) = 1$ is a local maximum. ✓

## 6. The Gradient in Machine Learning and Deep Learning

This is where the gradient moves from being a beautiful theoretical object to the engine driving virtually all of modern AI.

### 6.1 Loss Minimization as a Geometric Problem

In ML, we have a _loss function_ $\mathcal{L} : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ that maps model parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_n)$ to a scalar error. The goal is: $$\boldsymbol{\theta}^* = \underset{\boldsymbol{\theta}}{\arg\min}; \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$

This is precisely the problem of finding a minimum of a scalar function in high-dimensional space. The gradient is the tool.

### 6.2 Gradient Descent — Following the Steepest Descent Direction

From Significance I: $-\nabla \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is the direction of _steepest decrease_ of $\mathcal{L}$.

**Gradient Descent update rule:** $$\boldsymbol{\theta}_{t+1} = \boldsymbol{\theta}_t - \eta \cdot \nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}} \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}_t)$$ where $\eta > 0$ is the _learning rate_ (step size).

Geometrically: at each step, we stand on the loss landscape and take a step in the direction the mountain descends fastest. This is exactly the gradient perpendicularity principle applied operationally.

**Why does this work?** For small $\eta$, by the linear approximation (Significance III): $$\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta} - \eta\nabla\mathcal{L}) \approx \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) - \eta|\nabla\mathcal{L}|^2 < \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$ as long as $\nabla\mathcal{L} \neq \mathbf{0}$. The loss is guaranteed to decrease locally.

### 6.3 Backpropagation — Efficiently Computing $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{L}$

In a deep neural network, the loss $\mathcal{L}$ is a composition of many functions (layers): $$\mathcal{L} = \ell \circ f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1$$

The gradient $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{L}$ is computed via the **chain rule** applied repeatedly — this is exactly what _backpropagation_ is. It is the multivariable chain rule in disguise.

For layer $k$ with parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta}_k$ and output $\mathbf{a}_k = f_k(\mathbf{a}_{k-1}; \boldsymbol{\theta}_k)$: $$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_k} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_k} \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{a}_k}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_k}$$

The term $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_k}$ (the upstream gradient) is propagated backward through the network — hence "backpropagation." The entire algorithm is nothing but systematic gradient computation via the chain rule.

### 6.4 Geometric Picture of the Loss Landscape

The loss surface $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ is a hypersurface in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$. The level sets ${\boldsymbol{\theta} : \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = c}$ are "iso-loss" hypersurfaces in parameter space. By Significance II, $\nabla_{\boldsymbol{\theta}}\mathcal{L}$ is perpendicular to these surfaces.

- **Critical points** ($\nabla\mathcal{L} = \mathbf{0}$): local minima, saddle points, local maxima. In deep networks with millions of parameters, the landscape is extraordinarily complex.
- **Saddle points** dominate in high dimensions (exponentially more saddle points than local minima). Understanding the gradient near saddle points drives research in optimization.

### 6.5 Variants Driven by the Same Principle

|Method|Key Idea|Gradient Role|
|---|---|---|
|**SGD**|Approximate $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ from mini-batch|Noisy gradient, but direction still valid|
|**Momentum**|Accumulate gradient history|Smoothed gradient direction|
|**Adam**|Adaptive per-parameter learning rates|Gradient scaled by running statistics|
|**Natural Gradient**|Use Fisher information metric|Gradient in parameter space geometry|
|**Attention (Transformers)**|Softmax + dot products|Backprop through gradient of composition|

Every optimizer is a variation on: _compute or approximate $\nabla\mathcal{L}$, then use it to update $\boldsymbol{\theta}$._

### 6.6 Vanishing and Exploding Gradients

In deep networks, $\nabla\mathcal{L}$ is computed by multiplying many Jacobians (matrix analogues of the gradient) together via the chain rule. If these matrices have eigenvalues $< 1$ or $> 1$, the gradient **vanishes** or **explodes** exponentially with depth. This is the _vanishing/exploding gradient problem_. It is a direct consequence of the geometry of the gradient under repeated composition, and it motivates ResNets, batch normalization, careful weight initialization, etc.

## 7. The Gradient as a Linear Map — The Mature View

Let me step back and give the more mature perspective that connects this to linear algebra.

If $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$, then the _derivative_ $Df(\mathbf{a})$ is a linear map $\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$, i.e., an element of the dual space $(\mathbb{R}^n)^*$.

The Riesz representation theorem (in the Euclidean inner product) says every such linear functional is represented by a unique vector via the dot product. The gradient $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ is precisely this representing vector: $$Df(\mathbf{a})[\mathbf{h}] = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \mathbf{h}$$

This is why the formula $D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f = \nabla f \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$ holds for all directions: it is just the linear functional $Df(\mathbf{a})$ evaluated at $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$.

> The gradient is the Riesz representative of the derivative. It lives in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (same space as the domain) because we used the Euclidean inner product to identify $\mathbb{R}^n$ with its dual. On a Riemannian manifold with a non-Euclidean metric, the gradient changes accordingly — this is the foundation of the natural gradient in ML.
