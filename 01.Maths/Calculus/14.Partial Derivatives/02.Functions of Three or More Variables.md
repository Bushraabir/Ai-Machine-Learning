

> _"When a function depends on two variables, we can still draw it — a surface hovering over a plane. When it depends on three, we lose that luxury. The graph lives in $\mathbb{R}^4$, invisible to us. And yet the calculus generalizes perfectly. This is the point where you learn to think analytically rather than geometrically — a skill that will serve you in every dimension you ever encounter."_

---

## 0. Motivation: Why More Variables?

Almost nothing in the real world depends on fewer than three quantities simultaneously.

- **Temperature** in a room: $T(x, y, z)$ — three spatial coordinates.
- **Pressure** in a fluid: $P(x, y, z, t)$ — three space + one time.
- **Gravitational potential**: $\phi(x,y,z) = -GM/\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}$.
- **A neural network layer**: output $f(\mathbf{x}) : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ where $n$ can be millions.
- **The loss function** of a model: $\mathcal{L}(\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_p)$ where $p \sim 10^{10}$.

The theory of functions of two variables (surfaces, level curves, partial derivatives) extends to three or more variables with essentially **no new difficulty** — just new notation and a willingness to stop asking "what does this look like?"

## 1. Functions of Three Variables

### Definition

A **function of three variables** is a rule that assigns a unique real number to each point in some domain $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$:

$$f : D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad (x,y,z) \mapsto f(x,y,z)$$

**Domain** $D$: the set of all $(x,y,z)$ for which the expression is defined. **Range**: the set of all values $f(x,y,z)$ as $(x,y,z)$ varies over $D$.

### Examples

**Example 1.** $f(x,y,z) = \sqrt{1 - x^2 - y^2 - z^2}$

Domain: ${(x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3 : x^2 + y^2 + z^2 \leq 1}$ — the closed unit ball $\overline{B}(0,1)$. Range: $[0,1]$.

**Example 2.** $f(x,y,z) = \ln(z - x^2 - y^2)$

Domain: ${(x,y,z) : z > x^2 + y^2}$ — the open region above the paraboloid $z = x^2 + y^2$. Range: $\mathbb{R}$ (since $\ln$ achieves all real values on $(0,\infty)$).

**Example 3.** $f(x,y,z) = \dfrac{xyz}{x^2 + y^2 + z^2}$

Domain: $\mathbb{R}^3 \setminus {(0,0,0)}$ — all of $\mathbb{R}^3$ except the origin.

**Example 4 (Physics).** Gravitational potential: $$\phi(x,y,z) = -\frac{GM}{\sqrt{x^2+y^2+z^2}}$$

Domain: $\mathbb{R}^3\setminus{(0,0,0)}$. The force field is $\mathbf{F} = -\nabla\phi$ — the gradient of a scalar function.

## 2. Level Surfaces

For a function $f(x,y,z)$, we cannot draw its graph (that would live in $\mathbb{R}^4$). The geometric substitute is the **level surface**.

### Definition

The **level surface** of $f$ at value $c$ is:

$$S_c = {(x,y,z) \in D : f(x,y,z) = c}$$

This is a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — the set of all points where $f$ takes the value $c$.

Level surfaces are the three-variable analog of level curves. They replace the graph as the primary geometric object.

### Examples

**$f(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2$:** Level surfaces are **spheres** $x^2+y^2+z^2 = c$ (for $c > 0$), a point (for $c=0$), empty (for $c < 0$). These are the "shells" at constant distance from the origin.

**$f(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 - z^2$:**

- $c > 0$: Hyperboloid of one sheet
- $c = 0$: Double cone $x^2+y^2 = z^2$
- $c < 0$: Hyperboloid of two sheets

**$f(x,y,z) = ax + by + cz$:** Level surfaces are **planes** $ax + by + cz = k$. All parallel, normal to $(a,b,c)$.

**Gravitational potential** $\phi = -GM/r$: Level surfaces are spheres $r = $ const. — points of constant potential energy ("equipotential surfaces").

### Visualization: Nested Level Surfaces

```
     f = 3  ← outer shell
    ╭───────────────╮
    │   f = 2       │
    │  ╭─────────╮  │
    │  │  f = 1  │  │
    │  │  ╭───╮  │  │
    │  │  │f=0│  │  │
    │  │  ╰───╯  │  │
    │  ╰─────────╯  │
    ╰───────────────╯

Example: f(x,y,z) = x²+y²+z²
Level surfaces are concentric spheres.
Moving outward = increasing f.
```

The **gradient** $\nabla f$ at any point is perpendicular to the level surface through that point and points in the direction of fastest increase. (This will be proved when we study gradients.)

## 3. Functions of $n$ Variables

### The General Definition

For $n \geq 1$, a **function of $n$ variables** is:

$$f : D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad \mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n) \mapsto f(\mathbf{x})$$

The domain $D$ is a subset of $\mathbb{R}^n$; the range is a subset of $\mathbb{R}$.

**Notation conventions:**

|Notation|Usage|
|---|---|
|$f(x,y,z)$|Emphasize individual variables (3 vars)|
|$f(\mathbf{x})$|Vector input (any $n$)|
|$f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$|Index notation (general $n$)|

### Level Sets in $\mathbb{R}^n$

For $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$, the level set at $c$ is: $$S_c = {{\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n : f(\mathbf{x}) = c}}$$

Generically, this is an $(n-1)$-dimensional **hypersurface** in $\mathbb{R}^n$:

|$n$|Level set|Name|
|---|---|---|
|2|1D curve|Level curve|
|3|2D surface|Level surface|
|4|3D hypersurface|Level hypersurface|
|$n$|$(n-1)$D manifold|Level hypersurface|

## 4. Limits and Continuity in $\mathbb{R}^n$

### The $\varepsilon$-$\delta$ Definition

Let $f: D\subseteq\mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ and let $\mathbf{a}$ be a limit point of $D$. We say:

$$\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}} f(\mathbf{x}) = L$$

if for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that:

$$0 < |\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}| < \delta \implies |f(\mathbf{x}) - L| < \varepsilon$$

where $|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - a_i)^2}$ is the Euclidean distance in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

This is identical to the two-variable definition — the Euclidean norm generalizes seamlessly.

### Continuity

$f$ is **continuous at $\mathbf{a}$** if: $$\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}} f(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{a})$$

$f$ is continuous on $D$ if it is continuous at every point of $D$.

### The Subtlety: Paths in $\mathbb{R}^n$

For the limit $\lim_{\mathbf{x}\to\mathbf{a}}f(\mathbf{x})$ to exist, $f(\mathbf{x})$ must approach $L$ along **every possible path** to $\mathbf{a}$ — and in $\mathbb{R}^n$ with $n \geq 2$, there are uncountably many paths.

**Standard technique to prove a limit does NOT exist:** Find two paths giving different limits.

**Example.** Show $\lim_{(x,y,z)\to(0,0,0)}\dfrac{xyz}{x^2+y^2+z^2}$ does not exist.

_Path 1:_ $x=t, y=t, z=t$ as $t\to 0$: $$f = \frac{t^3}{3t^2} = \frac{t}{3} \to 0$$

_Path 2:_ $x=t, y=t, z=0$: $$f = \frac{0}{2t^2} = 0 \to 0$$

_Path 3:_ $x=t, y=t, z=t^{1/2}$ (approaching along a curved path) gives a more complex expression. Let us instead try a cleaner example:

**Example.** $g(x,y,z) = \dfrac{x^2 y z}{x^4 + y^2 + z^2}$ at the origin.

_Path:_ $x = t, y = t^2, z = t^2$: $$g = \frac{t^2 \cdot t^2 \cdot t^2}{t^4 + t^4 + t^4} = \frac{t^6}{3t^4} = \frac{t^2}{3} \to 0$$

_Path:_ $x = t, y = t^2, z = 0$: $$g = \frac{0}{t^4 + t^4} = 0 \to 0$$

_Path:_ $x = t, y = t^2, z = t^2$ at the limit $y = x^2$: $$g\big|_{y=x^2, z=x^2} = \frac{x^2\cdot x^2 \cdot x^2}{x^4 + x^4 + x^4} = \frac{x^6}{3x^4} = \frac{x^2}{3}\to 0$$

But along $y = x^2, z = x^2$: actually the limit is $0$ here. The function $g$ actually goes to $0$ along all polynomial paths — but may not if we allow more exotic approach directions.

> **Remark (the Curse of Paths):** In $\mathbb{R}^n$, $n \geq 2$, showing a limit exists requires either a squeeze or estimating with the Euclidean norm. Showing it doesn't exist requires two paths. The squeezing technique is: $$|f(\mathbf{x}) - L| \leq g(|\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{a}|) \to 0 \text{ as } |\mathbf{x}|\to|\mathbf{a}|$$

## 5. Partial Derivatives of Functions of Several Variables

### Definition

For $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$, the **partial derivative with respect to $x_i$** at a point $\mathbf{a}$ is:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a_1,\ldots, a_i+h,\ldots,a_n) - f(a_1,\ldots,a_i,\ldots,a_n)}{h}$$

**Computationally:** treat all variables except $x_i$ as constants, differentiate with respect to $x_i$.

### For Three Variables Explicitly

For $f(x,y,z)$:

$$f_x = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}: \text{ differentiate w.r.t. } x, \text{ treating } y,z \text{ as constants}$$

$$f_y = \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}: \text{ differentiate w.r.t. } y, \text{ treating } x,z \text{ as constants}$$

$$f_z = \frac{\partial f}{\partial z}: \text{ differentiate w.r.t. } z, \text{ treating } x,y \text{ as constants}$$

### Example

$f(x,y,z) = x^3 y - y^2 z + e^{xz}\sin y$

$$f_x = 3x^2 y + ze^{xz}\sin y$$

$$f_y = x^3 - 2yz + e^{xz}\cos y$$

$$f_z = -y^2 + xe^{xz}\sin y$$

### The Gradient

The **gradient** of $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ is the vector of all partial derivatives:

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \left\langle \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1},, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2},, \ldots,, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} \right\rangle$$

For three variables: $$\nabla f(x,y,z) = \langle f_x, f_y, f_z\rangle$$

The gradient is the **generalization of the derivative** to higher dimensions. Its key properties:

- $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ points in the direction of **steepest increase** of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$.
- $|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})|$ is the **rate of steepest ascent**.
- $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})$ is **perpendicular to the level surface** $f = f(\mathbf{a})$ at $\mathbf{a}$.

**Proof of the last point:** Let $\mathbf{r}(t)$ be any curve lying in the level surface $f(\mathbf{x}) = c$, so $f(\mathbf{r}(t)) = c$ for all $t$. By the chain rule: $$\frac{d}{dt}f(\mathbf{r}(t)) = \nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t))\cdot\mathbf{r}'(t) = 0$$

So $\nabla f \perp \mathbf{r}'(t)$ for every curve in the level surface — hence $\nabla f$ is perpendicular to the level surface. $\square$

## 6. The Chain Rule in $\mathbb{R}^n$

Suppose $f(x_1, \ldots, x_n)$ and each $x_i = x_i(t_1, \ldots, t_m)$. Then:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial t_j} = \sum_{i=1}^{n}\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}\cdot\frac{\partial x_i}{\partial t_j}$$

In matrix/vector form, if $\mathbf{x}: \mathbb{R}^m\to\mathbb{R}^n$ and $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{t}} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial \mathbf{x}}\cdot\frac{\partial \mathbf{x}}{\partial \mathbf{t}} = \nabla_\mathbf{x} f \cdot J_\mathbf{x}$$

where $J_\mathbf{x} = \partial\mathbf{x}/\partial\mathbf{t}$ is the **Jacobian matrix**.

**Example (three variables, two parameters):** $f(x,y,z)$ where $x=s^2t$, $y=st^2$, $z=e^{st}$:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial s} = f_x\cdot 2st + f_y\cdot t^2 + f_z\cdot te^{st}$$

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial t} = f_x\cdot s^2 + f_y\cdot 2st + f_z\cdot se^{st}$$

## 7. Directional Derivatives

For $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ and a unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = (u_1,\ldots,u_n)$, the **directional derivative** of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$ in direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is:

$$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f(\mathbf{a}) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(\mathbf{a}+h\hat{\mathbf{u}}) - f(\mathbf{a})}{h} = \nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}$$

**Proof:** Define $g(h) = f(\mathbf{a}+h\hat{\mathbf{u}})$. Then $g'(0)$ is the directional derivative, and by the chain rule: $$g'(h) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}+h\hat{\mathbf{u}})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}} \implies g'(0) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}. \quad\square$$

**Maximum directional derivative:** By Cauchy–Schwarz: $$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}}f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}} \leq |\nabla f(\mathbf{a})|$$

with equality when $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \nabla f(\mathbf{a})/|\nabla f(\mathbf{a})|$. The gradient points in the **direction of maximum rate of increase**.

## 8. Higher-Order Partials and Clairaut's Theorem

### Second Partial Derivatives

For $f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$, we can take partial derivatives of partial derivatives:

$$f_{x_i x_j} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_j,\partial x_i} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x_j}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}\right)$$

Note the order convention: $\partial x_j,\partial x_i$ means differentiate first w.r.t. $x_i$, then $x_j$.

### Clairaut's Theorem (Symmetry of Mixed Partials)

> **Theorem.** If $f$ is defined near $\mathbf{a}$ and $f_{x_i x_j}$, $f_{x_j x_i}$ are both continuous at $\mathbf{a}$, then: $$\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_j,\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x_i,\partial x_j}(\mathbf{a})$$

**Consequence:** For smooth functions, the **order of differentiation doesn't matter**. For $f(x,y,z)$:

$$f_{xy} = f_{yx}, \quad f_{xz} = f_{zx}, \quad f_{yz} = f_{zy}, \quad f_{xyz} = f_{xzy} = f_{yxz} = \cdots$$

For a function of $n$ variables with all mixed partials of order $k$ continuous, all $k!$ orderings give the same result.

### The Hessian Matrix

The **Hessian** of $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ is the $n\times n$ matrix of all second partials:

$$H_f(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{pmatrix} f_{x_1x_1} & f_{x_1x_2} & \cdots & f_{x_1x_n} \ f_{x_2x_1} & f_{x_2x_2} & \cdots & f_{x_2x_n} \ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \ f_{x_nx_1} & f_{x_nx_2} & \cdots & f_{x_nx_n} \end{pmatrix}$$

By Clairaut, $H_f$ is **symmetric**: $H_f = H_f^T$.

The Hessian generalizes the second derivative to higher dimensions and controls:

- Local convexity/concavity of $f$
- Classification of critical points
- Second-order Taylor expansion

## 9. The Taylor Expansion in $\mathbb{R}^n$

For $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$ smooth near $\mathbf{a}$, the **second-order Taylor expansion** at $\mathbf{a}$ is:

$$f(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}) = f(\mathbf{a}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{h} + \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{h}^T H_f(\mathbf{a}),\mathbf{h} + O(|\mathbf{h}|^3)$$

This is the master approximation formula. Each term has a name:

- $f(\mathbf{a})$: zeroth-order (value)
- $\nabla f(\mathbf{a})\cdot\mathbf{h}$: first-order (linear/gradient term)
- $\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{h}^T H_f,\mathbf{h}$: second-order (quadratic/curvature term)

For $n=3$ with $\mathbf{h} = (h_1, h_2, h_3)$:

$$f(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}) \approx f(\mathbf{a}) + f_x h_1 + f_y h_2 + f_z h_3 + \tfrac{1}{2}(f_{xx}h_1^2 + f_{yy}h_2^2 + f_{zz}h_3^2 + 2f_{xy}h_1h_2 + 2f_{xz}h_1h_3 + 2f_{yz}h_2h_3)$$

---

## 10. Critical Points and the Second Derivative Test in $\mathbb{R}^n$

### Critical Points

$\mathbf{a}$ is a **critical point** of $f$ if $\nabla f(\mathbf{a}) = \mathbf{0}$, i.e., all partial derivatives vanish simultaneously.

### Classification via the Hessian

At a critical point $\mathbf{a}$, the behavior of $f$ near $\mathbf{a}$ is determined by $H_f(\mathbf{a})$:

From the Taylor expansion: $f(\mathbf{a}+\mathbf{h}) - f(\mathbf{a}) \approx \tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{h}^T H_f(\mathbf{a}),\mathbf{h}$

The sign of the quadratic form $\mathbf{h}^T H_f,\mathbf{h}$ determines the type:

|$H_f(\mathbf{a})$|Type|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|**Positive definite** (all eigenvalues $> 0$)|Local minimum|$f$ increases in all directions|
|**Negative definite** (all eigenvalues $< 0$)|Local maximum|$f$ decreases in all directions|
|**Indefinite** (mixed eigenvalue signs)|Saddle point|Increases some directions, decreases others|
|**Positive/negative semidefinite**|Degenerate|Test inconclusive|

**For $n = 2$** (two-variable case): $D = f_{xx}f_{yy} - f_{xy}^2 = \det H_f$

- $D > 0, f_{xx} > 0$: local min
- $D > 0, f_{xx} < 0$: local max
- $D < 0$: saddle
- $D = 0$: inconclusive

**For $n \geq 3$:** Check eigenvalues of $H_f$ (or use Sylvester's criterion for positive definiteness).

## 11. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Domain and Level Surfaces

$f(x,y,z) = \ln(4 - x^2 - y^2 - z^2)$

**Domain:** Need $4 - x^2 - y^2 - z^2 > 0$, i.e., $x^2+y^2+z^2 < 4$ — the open ball of radius $2$.

**Level surfaces:** $\ln(4-x^2-y^2-z^2) = c \iff x^2+y^2+z^2 = 4 - e^c$

Valid when $4-e^c > 0$, i.e., $c < \ln 4$. The level surfaces are **concentric spheres** of radius $\sqrt{4-e^c}$ centered at the origin, ranging from radius $0$ (as $c\to\ln 4^-$) to radius $2$ (as $c\to-\infty$).

---

### Example 2: Partial Derivatives of a Physical Function

The **ideal gas law**: $PV = nRT$, so $P(V,T) = nRT/V$.

For a gas with $n=1$ mole, $R = 8.314$ J/(mol·K):

$$P(V,T) = \frac{RT}{V}$$

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial T} = \frac{R}{V} > 0 \quad\text{(pressure increases with temperature at fixed volume)}$$

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V} = -\frac{RT}{V^2} < 0 \quad\text{(pressure decreases as volume increases at fixed temperature)}$$

$$\frac{\partial^2 P}{\partial V^2} = \frac{2RT}{V^3} > 0 \quad\text{(convex in } V\text{)}$$

---

### Example 3: Critical Points in $\mathbb{R}^3$

$f(x,y,z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 - 2x + 4y - 6z + 14$

**Find critical points:** $$f_x = 2x - 2 = 0 \implies x = 1$$ $$f_y = 2y + 4 = 0 \implies y = -2$$ $$f_z = 2z - 6 = 0 \implies z = 3$$

One critical point: $(1, -2, 3)$.

**Hessian:** $$H_f = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = 2I$$

Positive definite (all eigenvalues $= 2 > 0$). So $(1,-2,3)$ is a **global minimum**.

$f(1,-2,3) = 1 + 4 + 9 - 2 - 8 - 18 + 14 = 0$.

We can verify: $f(x,y,z) = (x-1)^2 + (y+2)^2 + (z-3)^2 \geq 0$. ✓

---

### Example 4: Limit via Squeeze

Show $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y,z)\to(0,0,0)}\frac{x^2 y z}{x^2 + y^2 + z^2} = 0$.

**Squeeze:** By AM-GM, $x^2 \leq x^2 + y^2 + z^2$, so $\dfrac{x^2}{x^2+y^2+z^2} \leq 1$. Also:

$$\left|\frac{x^2 yz}{x^2+y^2+z^2}\right| = \frac{x^2}{x^2+y^2+z^2}\cdot|y|\cdot|z| \leq |y||z| \leq \frac{y^2+z^2}{2} \leq \frac{x^2+y^2+z^2}{2} = \frac{r^2}{2}$$

where $r = |(x,y,z)|$. As $(x,y,z)\to(0,0,0)$, $r\to 0$, so the expression $\to 0$. $\square$

## 12. Visualization Strategies (Since We Can't Draw the Graph)

When you can't visualize the graph of $f:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}$, use:

**1. Level surfaces** — fix $c$ and draw/describe $f(x,y,z) = c$.

**2. Restriction to lower-dimensional slices:**

- Fix $z = z_0$: get a function of two variables $f(x,y,z_0)$ — draw its level curves.
- Fix $y = 0, z = 0$: get $f(x,0,0)$ — a standard one-variable graph.

**3. The gradient field:** $\nabla f(x,y,z)$ is a vector field in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Its arrows show direction of fastest increase.

**4. Color-coded cross-sections:** In computational tools (Python, Mathematica), one plots $f(x,y,z_0)$ as a heatmap for several values of $z_0$.

```
Strategy for understanding f(x,y,z):

  Full function                Slice at z = z₀
  (invisible in ℝ⁴)  →→→     f(x,y,z₀): a surface in ℝ³
                      →→→     Level curves of f(x,y,z₀): curves in ℝ²

  Level surfaces of f  →→→    Nested shells in ℝ³
  ∇f field             →→→    Arrows ⊥ to level surfaces
```

## 13. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

Functions of many variables are not a generalization of interest only to physicists. They are the **central object of study in machine learning** — almost every concept in this section reappears, at billion-dimensional scale.

### 13.1 The Loss Function Is a Function of Billions of Variables

A neural network with parameters $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_p) \in \mathbb{R}^p$ defines a **loss function**:

$$\mathcal{L} : \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \ell(f_{\boldsymbol\theta}(\mathbf{x}_i), y_i)$$

This is literally a function of $p$ variables — with $p \sim 10^{10}$ for GPT-scale models. Everything in this note applies:

- **Domain:** The set of $\boldsymbol{\theta}$ for which the network is defined (all of $\mathbb{R}^p$, typically).
- **Level sets:** ${\boldsymbol{\theta} : \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = c}$ — the set of parameters achieving loss $c$. Modern theory shows these level sets are extremely complex, high-dimensional manifolds.
- **Gradient:** $\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}$ — the direction of steepest ascent, whose negative gives the gradient descent direction. Computed by **backpropagation**, which is an efficient algorithm for the chain rule in $\mathbb{R}^p$.
- **Hessian:** $H_\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta)$ — a $p\times p$ matrix. For $p = 10^{10}$, this has $10^{20}$ entries — cannot be stored. This is why second-order optimization methods are approximated (L-BFGS, K-FAC, Shampoo).
- **Critical points:** $\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L} = \mathbf{0}$ — the training algorithm tries to find these, but in high dimensions most critical points are saddle points, not local minima (Dauphin et al., 2014).

### 13.2 Backpropagation = Chain Rule in $\mathbb{R}^n$

The **chain rule in $\mathbb{R}^n$** is the mathematical foundation of deep learning. Given a neural network $f = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_l} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_L}\cdot\frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_L}{\partial \mathbf{h}_{L-1}}\cdots\frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{l+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_l}\cdot\frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_l}{\partial \boldsymbol{\theta}_l}$$

Each factor $\partial\mathbf{h}_{k+1}/\partial\mathbf{h}_k$ is a **Jacobian matrix**. Backpropagation is the efficient right-to-left evaluation of this chain of matrix products — exactly the multi-variable chain rule, structured to avoid redundant computation.

### 13.3 Level Sets as "Weight Space Symmetries"

The level sets ${\boldsymbol\theta : \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta) = c}$ for small $c$ are the "good parameter regions." Recent work shows:

- For overparameterized networks ($p \gg N$), the global minimum level set is a high-dimensional connected manifold.
- **Neuron permutation symmetry:** Swapping two hidden neurons gives a different $\boldsymbol\theta$ on the same level set — the level set has a large discrete symmetry group of size $\prod_l (n_l!)$.
- **Mode connectivity** (Garipov et al., 2018): Two independently trained minima $\boldsymbol\theta^__1, \boldsymbol\theta^__2$ are often connected by a path of low loss — they lie on the same level-set component.

### 13.4 The Hessian in Deep Learning

The Hessian $H_\mathcal{L}$ at a minimum encodes:

- **Sharpness:** $\lambda_{\max}(H_\mathcal{L})$ — the largest eigenvalue. Sharp minima (large $\lambda_{\max}$) generalize poorly; flat minima generalize well. This is the theoretical basis for **SAM (Sharpness-Aware Minimization)** (Foret et al., 2021).
- **Learning rate stability:** Gradient descent is stable only when $\eta < 2/\lambda_{\max}$ — a direct consequence of the quadratic Taylor approximation.
- **Natural gradient:** The Fisher information matrix $F_\theta$ is the expected Hessian of the log-likelihood. The natural gradient $F_\theta^{-1}\nabla_\theta\mathcal{L}$ accounts for the Riemannian geometry of the model manifold (Amari, 1998).

### 13.5 Directional Derivatives and Sensitivity Analysis

The directional derivative $D_{\hat\mathbf{u}}\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta) = \nabla\mathcal{L}\cdot\hat\mathbf{u}$ answers: "if I perturb the parameters in direction $\hat\mathbf{u}$, how does the loss change?"

Applications:

- **Adversarial examples** (Goodfellow et al., 2014): Find $\hat\mathbf{u}$ (in input space) that maximizes $D_{\hat\mathbf{u}}f$ — the direction that fools the classifier. The **FGSM attack** is literally $\mathbf{x}^{\text{adv}} = \mathbf{x} + \varepsilon\cdot\text{sign}(\nabla_\mathbf{x}\mathcal{L})$ — a step in the gradient direction in input space.
- **Influence functions:** How does the loss on a test point change if we perturb the training data? Answered by directional derivatives in function space.
- **Saliency maps:** $\partial f/\partial x_i$ for each input pixel $x_i$ — a partial derivative of the network output with respect to inputs, used for interpretability.

### 13.6 Functions of Many Variables in Attention

A transformer attention head computes:

$$\text{Attention}(Q,K,V) = \text{softmax}\left(\frac{QK^T}{\sqrt{d_k}}\right)V$$

The softmax output $A_{ij}$ is a function of **all** entries of $Q$ and $K$ simultaneously. The partial derivatives $\partial A_{ij}/\partial Q_{kl}$ and $\partial A_{ij}/\partial K_{kl}$ are the components of the Jacobian computed during backpropagation. Each attention head is a function from $(Q,K,V) \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times d_k}\times\mathbb{R}^{n\times d_k}\times\mathbb{R}^{n\times d_v}$ to $\mathbb{R}^{n\times d_v}$ — a function of hundreds of thousands of variables, differentiated billions of times per second during training.

### 13.7 Gaussian Processes and Functions of Infinitely Many Variables

A **Gaussian Process** (GP) is a probability distribution over functions $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$. The GP can be viewed as the limit of a Bayesian neural network as the width $\to\infty$ (Neal, 1994; Lee et al., 2018). In this limit, the "function of parameters" becomes a "function of infinitely many variables" — and the calculus of variations and functional derivatives generalize the partial derivative framework of this note to infinite dimensions.

## 14. Summary

|Concept|Two Variables|Three Variables|$n$ Variables|
|---|---|---|---|
|Graph|Surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$|Hypersurface in $\mathbb{R}^4$ (invisible)|—|
|Level set|Curve in $\mathbb{R}^2$|Surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$|$(n-1)$-manifold|
|Partial derivatives|$f_x, f_y$|$f_x, f_y, f_z$|$\partial f/\partial x_i$, $i=1,\ldots,n$|
|Gradient|$\langle f_x, f_y\rangle \in \mathbb{R}^2$|$\langle f_x,f_y,f_z\rangle\in\mathbb{R}^3$|$\nabla f\in\mathbb{R}^n$|
|Hessian|$2\times 2$ matrix|$3\times 3$ matrix|$n\times n$ symmetric matrix|
|Taylor|degree 2 polynomial in 2 vars|degree 2 in 3 vars|$f(\mathbf{a})+\nabla f\cdot\mathbf{h}+\tfrac{1}{2}\mathbf{h}^TH\mathbf{h}+\cdots$|
|Critical point test|$\det H$, sign of $f_{xx}$|eigenvalues of $H$|eigenvalues of $H$|

**The key geometric principle:** What level curves do for $f:\mathbb{R}^2\to\mathbb{R}$, **level surfaces** do for $f:\mathbb{R}^3\to\mathbb{R}$, and **level hypersurfaces** do for $f:\mathbb{R}^n\to\mathbb{R}$. The gradient is always perpendicular to these, always points toward steepest increase, and always satisfies $D_{\hat\mathbf{u}}f = \nabla f\cdot\hat\mathbf{u}$.

In machine learning, every one of these concepts operates at scale — the domain is $\mathbb{R}^{10^{10}}$, the level sets are high-dimensional manifolds, the gradient is computed by backpropagation, and the Hessian is approximated rather than stored.
