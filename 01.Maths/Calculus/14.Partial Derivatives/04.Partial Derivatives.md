## 0. Motivation — Why Partial Derivatives?

In single-variable calculus, we ask: _how does $f(x)$ change as $x$ changes?_ The answer is $f'(x)$.

Now suppose temperature in a room depends on position and time: $T(x, y, z, t)$. Or suppose the loss of a neural network depends on millions of weights $\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_n$. We cannot ask "how does $f$ change" without specifying _with respect to which variable_, while treating the others as **frozen constants**.

That is precisely the partial derivative.

---

## 1. Definition

Let $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be a function of $n$ variables. The **partial derivative of $f$ with respect to $x_i$** at a point $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n)$ is:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) := \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(a_1, \ldots, a_i + h, \ldots, a_n) - f(a_1, \ldots, a_i, \ldots, a_n)}{h}$$

provided this limit exists.

**Reading this carefully:** every variable except $x_i$ is _held fixed_. The function $g(t) = f(a_1, \ldots, t, \ldots, a_n)$ is an ordinary single-variable function, and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i}(\mathbf{a}) = g'(a_i)$ in the classical sense.

### Notation Zoo

All of the following mean the same thing:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x} = f_x = \partial_x f = D_1 f \quad \text{(when } x \text{ is the 1st variable)}$$

Stewart uses $f_x$ and $\partial f / \partial x$ interchangeably. MIT 18.02 often prefers subscript notation in physics contexts.

---

## 2. Geometric Meaning

Consider $z = f(x, y)$, a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

- **$f_x(a,b)$** = slope of the curve obtained by _cutting the surface with the plane $y = b$_ — a curve that runs in the $xz$-plane.
- **$f_y(a,b)$** = slope of the curve obtained by cutting with the plane $x = a$.

```
        z
        |         /surface z = f(x,y)
        |        /
        |    ---*---   <- slice y = b
        |      / \
        |     /   \   slope here = f_x(a,b)
        +------------- x
       /
      / y
```

**Intuition:** partial derivatives measure the _instantaneous rate of change along coordinate directions_. They do NOT capture all directional information — that requires the **gradient** and **directional derivative**.

---

## 3. Computing Partial Derivatives — The Practical Rule

**Rule:** To compute $\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}$, treat every other variable as a _constant_ and differentiate with respect to $x$ using all ordinary differentiation rules.

### Example 3.1 — Polynomial

$$f(x, y) = x^3 y^2 + 5x^2 - 7y + 3$$

$$f_x = 3x^2 y^2 + 10x$$ $$f_y = 2x^3 y - 7$$

_In computing $f_x$: the $5x^2$ becomes $10x$, the $-7y$ vanishes (constant in $x$), the $+3$ vanishes._

### Example 3.2 — Mixed Transcendental

$$f(x, y) = e^{xy} \sin(y^2)$$

$$f_x = y e^{xy} \sin(y^2)$$ $$f_y = x e^{xy} \sin(y^2) + e^{xy} \cdot 2y\cos(y^2)$$

_In $f_y$: the $e^{xy}$ requires the chain rule since $xy$ depends on $y$; $\sin(y^2)$ also requires chain rule._

### Example 3.3 — Three Variables

$$f(x, y, z) = x^2 \ln(yz) + z e^x$$

$$f_x = 2x\ln(yz) + ze^x$$ $$f_y = \frac{x^2}{y}$$ $$f_z = \frac{x^2}{z} + e^x$$

## 4. Higher-Order Partial Derivatives

We can differentiate again. For $f(x,y)$, there are four second-order partials:

$$f_{xx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2}, \quad f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}, \quad f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}, \quad f_{yx} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y \partial x}$$

**Caution on notation:** $f_{xy}$ means _differentiate with respect to $x$ first, then $y$_. In $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}$, the convention is the _opposite_: the denominator is read right-to-left (differentiate $y$ first, then $x$). This is a common source of confusion. Stewart and MIT 18.02 both note this.

$$f_{xy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y , \partial x}$$

### Theorem 4.1 — Clairaut's Theorem (Symmetry of Mixed Partials)

> **Theorem (Clairaut/Schwarz):** If $f : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ is defined on an open set $D$, and if $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$ are both **continuous** on $D$, then: $$f_{xy}(x,y) = f_{yx}(x,y) \quad \text{for all } (x,y) \in D.$$

**Proof sketch.** Consider the second-order difference quotient:

$$\Delta = f(a+h, b+k) - f(a+h, b) - f(a, b+k) + f(a,b)$$

Define $\phi(x) = f(x, b+k) - f(x,b)$. Then $\Delta = \phi(a+h) - \phi(a)$. By the Mean Value Theorem applied twice:

$$\Delta = h\phi'(a + \theta_1 h) = hk f_{xy}(a + \theta_1 h,, b + \theta_2 k)$$

for some $\theta_1, \theta_2 \in (0,1)$. Similarly, define $\psi(y) = f(a+h, y) - f(a,y)$ and obtain:

$$\Delta = hk f_{yx}(a + \theta_3 h,, b + \theta_4 k)$$

Taking $h, k \to 0$ and using continuity of $f_{xy}$ and $f_{yx}$, both expressions converge to the same limit. $\blacksquare$

**This is not trivial.** There exist functions where $f_{xy}(0,0) \neq f_{yx}(0,0)$; continuity of the mixed partials is essential.

#### Counterexample to Clairaut without continuity:

$$f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{xy(x^2 - y^2)}{x^2 + y^2} & (x,y) \neq (0,0) \ 0 & (x,y) = (0,0) \end{cases}$$

One can verify $f_{xy}(0,0) = -1$ but $f_{yx}(0,0) = 1$. Mixed partials exist but are discontinuous at origin.

## 5. Partial Derivatives and the Gradient

The **gradient** of $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is the vector of all partial derivatives:

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{x}) = \left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1}, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2}, \ldots, \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n}\right)$$

The gradient:

- Points in the direction of **steepest ascent** of $f$
- Is **perpendicular to level curves** (in $\mathbb{R}^2$) or level surfaces (in $\mathbb{R}^3$)
- Has magnitude $|\nabla f|$ equal to the maximum rate of change

### The Directional Derivative

The rate of change of $f$ in the direction of a unit vector $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is:

$$D_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} f(\mathbf{a}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$$

Partial derivatives are the _special case_ where $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \mathbf{e}_i$ (standard basis vectors).

## 6. The Chain Rule for Partial Derivatives

### Case: $z = f(x,y)$, with $x = x(s,t)$, $y = y(s,t)$

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}$$

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}$$

**Tree diagram mnemonic (MIT 18.02 style):**

```
        z
       / \
      x   y
     / \ / \
    s  t s  t
```

To find $\partial z / \partial s$: sum over all paths from $z$ to $s$, multiplying partial derivatives along each branch.

### General Chain Rule

If $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ and $\mathbf{g} : \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}^n$, with $h = f \circ \mathbf{g}$:

$$\frac{\partial h}{\partial t_j} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_i} \frac{\partial g_i}{\partial t_j}$$

In matrix form: the **Jacobian** — this is where linear algebra and calculus fuse.

---

## 7. The Total Differential

If $f$ is differentiable, the **total differential** is:

$$df = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_1} dx_1 + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_2} dx_2 + \cdots + \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_n} dx_n = \nabla f \cdot d\mathbf{x}$$

This is the linear approximation:

$$f(\mathbf{a} + \Delta\mathbf{x}) \approx f(\mathbf{a}) + \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \Delta\mathbf{x}$$

This is the **tangent plane** (for $n=2$) generalized to all dimensions.

---

## 8. Existence vs. Differentiability — A Subtle Warning

> **Existence of all partial derivatives at a point does NOT imply differentiability at that point.**

This is one of the most important subtleties in multivariable analysis.

**Example.** Define:

$$f(x,y) = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } y = x^2, x \neq 0 \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Then $f_x(0,0) = 0$ and $f_y(0,0) = 0$ both exist. But $f$ is not even continuous at $(0,0)$ (approach along $y = x^2$).

**Sufficient condition for differentiability:** If $f_x$ and $f_y$ exist in a neighborhood of $(a,b)$ and are _continuous_ there, then $f$ is differentiable at $(a,b)$.

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

This is where the theory becomes extraordinarily powerful in practice.

### 9.1 Loss Minimization — Gradient Descent

In supervised machine learning, we have a **loss function** $\mathcal{L}(\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_n)$ where $\theta_i$ are the model parameters (weights). We want:

$$\theta^* = \arg\min_{\theta} \mathcal{L}(\theta)$$

**Gradient descent** iterates:

$$\theta_i^{(t+1)} = \theta_i^{(t)} - \eta \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i}\bigg|_{\theta^{(t)}}$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the learning rate. Each update uses the **partial derivative** of the loss with respect to each individual weight — moving each weight in the direction that most decreases the loss. This is literally Clairaut-era calculus operating on billion-dimensional spaces.

### 9.2 Backpropagation — Chain Rule at Scale

A neural network is a composition of functions:

$$\hat{y} = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1(\mathbf{x})$$

The loss is $\mathcal{L}(\hat{y}, y)$. To compute $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i^{(k)}}$ (the gradient with respect to a weight in layer $k$), we apply the **multivariable chain rule** recursively:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i^{(k)}} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial a_k} \cdot \frac{\partial a_k}{\partial \theta_i^{(k)}}$$

where $a_k$ is the output of layer $k$. The term $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial a_k}$ is itself computed by chaining through layers $k+1, \ldots, L$. This is **backpropagation** — it is the chain rule for partial derivatives, applied recursively through a computational graph.

```
Loss ← Layer L ← Layer L-1 ← ... ← Layer k ← ... ← Input
           ↑
   ∂L/∂θ computed by chain rule backwards
```

Every modern deep learning framework (PyTorch, JAX, TensorFlow) implements **automatic differentiation** — essentially computing partial derivatives of arbitrarily complex compositions automatically.

### 9.3 The Hessian — Second-Order Methods

The **Hessian matrix** of $\mathcal{L}$ is the matrix of second-order partials:

$$H_{ij} = \frac{\partial^2 \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta_i \partial \theta_j}$$

By Clairaut's Theorem, $H$ is symmetric (provided the partials are continuous — essentially always true for neural networks with smooth activations).

Second-order optimizers (Newton's method, L-BFGS, K-FAC) use $H$ or approximations to it. The eigenvalues of $H$ determine the local geometry of the loss landscape — sharp minima (large eigenvalues) vs. flat minima (small eigenvalues), which has profound implications for generalization.

### 9.4 Partial Derivatives in Probability and Statistics

The **log-likelihood** $\ell(\theta) = \log p(\text{data} \mid \theta)$ is differentiated to find maximum likelihood estimates:

$$\frac{\partial \ell}{\partial \theta_i} = 0 \quad \text{for all } i$$

This is a system of equations in partial derivatives. The **Fisher information matrix** is the expected outer product of the score function — again, partial derivatives everywhere.

### 9.5 Attention Mechanisms and Softmax

In transformer architectures, the softmax function $\sigma : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is:

$$\sigma(\mathbf{z})_i = \frac{e^{z_i}}{\sum_j e^{z_j}}$$

During backpropagation through attention layers, we need $\frac{\partial \sigma_i}{\partial z_j}$ — the Jacobian of softmax — which requires computing all mixed partial derivatives. The result:

$$\frac{\partial \sigma_i}{\partial z_j} = \sigma_i(\delta_{ij} - \sigma_j)$$

where $\delta_{ij}$ is the Kronecker delta. This is a direct application of quotient rule + partial differentiation.

## 10. Worked Examples — From Definition

### Example 10.1 — Verifying with the Limit Definition

Let $f(x,y) = x^2 + xy$. Compute $f_x(1, 2)$ from the definition.

$$f_x(1,2) = \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{f(1+h, 2) - f(1,2)}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{(1+h)^2 + (1+h)(2) - (1 + 2)}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{1 + 2h + h^2 + 2 + 2h - 3}{h}$$

$$= \lim_{h\to 0} \frac{4h + h^2}{h} = \lim_{h\to 0}(4 + h) = 4$$

Using the rule: $f_x = 2x + y$, so $f_x(1,2) = 2 + 2 = 4$. ✓

### Example 10.2 — Implicit Partial Differentiation

Suppose $F(x, y, z) = x^2 + y^2 + z^2 - 1 = 0$ defines $z$ implicitly as a function of $x, y$ near a point. Find $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$.

Differentiate $F(x, y, z(x,y)) = 0$ with respect to $x$:

$$F_x + F_z \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 0 \implies \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{F_x}{F_z} = -\frac{2x}{2z} = -\frac{x}{z}$$

**General implicit function rule:**

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{\partial F/\partial x}{\partial F/\partial z}$$

This is used constantly in thermodynamics and economics (where multiple constraints define surfaces).

### Example 10.3 — Gradient and Level Curves

$f(x,y) = x^2 + 4y^2$. The level curve $f = 4$ is an ellipse $x^2 + 4y^2 = 4$.

$$\nabla f = (2x, 8y)$$

At the point $(2, 0)$: $\nabla f = (4, 0)$ — pointing in the $+x$ direction, perpendicular to the ellipse at that point (the ellipse is vertical there). ✓

### Gradient as Compass on a Topographic Map

Think of level curves as contour lines on a map. The gradient $\nabla f$ at any point is a vector:

- **Perpendicular** to the contour line through that point
- **Pointing uphill** (toward higher $f$ values)
- **Longer** where the terrain is steeper

This is exactly how gradient descent works in machine learning: the loss landscape is a high-dimensional terrain, and the gradient tells the optimizer which direction is "uphill" in loss — so we step the opposite way.

## 13. Common Mistakes to Avoid

1. **Forgetting to freeze variables.** When computing $f_x$, every $y$ is a constant. $\frac{\partial}{\partial x}(y^2) = 0$, not $2y$.
    
2. **Confusing $f_{xy}$ with $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x \partial y}$.** The subscript and fraction notations have _opposite order_ conventions.
    
3. **Assuming continuity from existence.** All partials can exist at a point while $f$ is discontinuous there.
    
4. **Applying Clairaut without checking continuity.** In 99% of practical cases it holds; but know the hypothesis.
    
5. **Confusing gradient with derivative.** The gradient $\nabla f$ is a _vector_; the derivative of $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ is a _row vector_ (the transpose). They contain the same information; the distinction matters in matrix calculus.
