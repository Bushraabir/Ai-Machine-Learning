

> _"Explicit functions are a convenience. Most relationships in nature are implicit — a constraint that variables must satisfy. Implicit differentiation is how we extract rates of change from constraints, without ever solving for one variable in terms of the others."_

---

## §1. Motivation — When You Cannot Solve Explicitly

Consider the curve $x^3 + y^3 = 6xy$ (the **folium of Descartes**). Can you solve for $y$ in terms of $x$? Technically yes — via the cubic formula — but the result is a nightmare. Yet we can ask: what is the slope of this curve at the point $(3, 3)$?

Or consider $e^{xy} + \sin(x + y) = 2$. Solving for $y$ is impossible in closed form. But the curve exists, it has slopes at each point, and we can compute them.

**Implicit differentiation** extracts $\frac{dy}{dx}$ (or $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$) directly from the defining equation $F(x, y) = 0$, without isolating the dependent variable. The theoretical guarantee that such a function even _exists_ is the **Implicit Function Theorem**.

---

## §2. The Core Idea — Differentiate the Constraint

Suppose $y$ is implicitly defined as a function of $x$ by $F(x, y) = 0$. This means there exists (locally) a function $y = g(x)$ such that:

$$F(x,, g(x)) = 0 \quad \text{for all } x \text{ near } a$$

Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$. Left side: chain rule. Right side: zero.

$$\frac{d}{dx}F(x, g(x)) = F_x(x, y) \cdot 1 + F_y(x, y) \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 0$$

Solve:

$$\boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}}$$

**That is the whole algorithm.** The rest of this note is making it rigorous, generalizing it, and understanding when it works.

---

## §3. The Implicit Function Theorem — The Guarantee

Before computing derivatives, we must ask: does $F(x, y) = 0$ actually define $y$ as a function of $x$ near a given point?

**Theorem** _(Implicit Function Theorem, 2D)._ Let $F: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be $C^1$ in a neighborhood of $(a, b)$, with $F(a, b) = 0$. If:

$$F_y(a, b) \neq 0$$

then there exists an open interval $I$ containing $a$ and a unique $C^1$ function $y = g(x)$ on $I$ such that:

- $g(a) = b$
- $F(x, g(x)) = 0$ for all $x \in I$
- $g'(x) = -\dfrac{F_x(x, g(x))}{F_y(x, g(x))}$

**What the theorem says geometrically.** Near $(a, b)$, the level curve $F = 0$ looks like the graph of a function. The condition $F_y \neq 0$ ensures the curve is not "vertical" there — it has a well-defined slope.

**What happens when $F_y = 0$?** The theorem fails. The curve may have a vertical tangent, a self-intersection, a cusp, or a fold. These are exactly the singular points where implicit differentiation breaks down.

**Example.** $F(x, y) = x^2 + y^2 - 1$ (unit circle). $F_y = 2y$. The theorem fails at $y = 0$, i.e., at $(1, 0)$ and $(-1, 0)$ — exactly where the circle has vertical tangents.

---

## §4. Algorithm — One Equation, Two Variables

Given $F(x, y) = 0$, to find $\frac{dy}{dx}$:

**Method 1 — Direct chain rule:** Differentiate $F(x, y) = 0$ with respect to $x$, treating $y$ as a function of $x$. Apply the chain rule to every term involving $y$. Collect $\frac{dy}{dx}$ terms on one side and solve.

**Method 2 — Differential:** Take $dF = 0$, i.e., $F_x,dx + F_y,dy = 0$. Divide by $dx$: $\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}$.

Both methods are equivalent. Method 2 is faster for complex expressions.

### Example 1 — Folium of Descartes

$x^3 + y^3 = 6xy$. Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$.

Set $F = x^3 + y^3 - 6xy$. Then:

$$F_x = 3x^2 - 6y, \quad F_y = 3y^2 - 6x$$

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{3x^2 - 6y}{3y^2 - 6x} = \frac{2y - x^2}{y^2 - 2x}$$

At $(3, 3)$: $\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{6 - 9}{9 - 6} = \frac{-3}{3} = -1$. The tangent line at $(3,3)$ is $y - 3 = -(x - 3)$, i.e., $x + y = 6$.

### Example 2 — Transcendental Equation

$e^{xy} + \sin(x + y) = 2$. Find $\frac{dy}{dx}$ at $(0, 0)$.

$F = e^{xy} + \sin(x+y) - 2$.

$$F_x = ye^{xy} + \cos(x+y), \quad F_y = xe^{xy} + \cos(x+y)$$

At $(0, 0)$: $F_x = 0 + \cos(0) = 1$, $F_y = 0 + \cos(0) = 1$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx}\bigg|_{(0,0)} = -\frac{1}{1} = -1$$

### Example 3 — Second Derivative

$x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ (circle). Find $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.

**First derivative:** $2x + 2y\frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \implies \frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}$.

**Second derivative.** Differentiate $\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{x}{y}$ with respect to $x$:

$$\frac{d^2y}{dx^2} = -\frac{y \cdot 1 - x \cdot \frac{dy}{dx}}{y^2} = -\frac{y - x\left(-\frac{x}{y}\right)}{y^2} = -\frac{y + \frac{x^2}{y}}{y^2} = -\frac{y^2 + x^2}{y^3} = -\frac{r^2}{y^3}$$

Note: substitute $\frac{dy}{dx}$ before simplifying — always.

## §5. Three Variables — Implicit Surface

Now $F(x, y, z) = 0$ defines a surface. Suppose $z = z(x, y)$ implicitly.

Differentiate $F(x, y, z(x,y)) = 0$ with respect to $x$ (holding $y$ constant):

$$F_x + F_z \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 0 \implies \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{F_x}{F_z}$$

Differentiate with respect to $y$:

$$F_y + F_z \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = 0 \implies \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -\frac{F_y}{F_z}$$

**Condition for validity:** $F_z \neq 0$ at the point (Implicit Function Theorem in 3D).

The gradient of $z = g(x,y)$ is:

$$\nabla g = \left(-\frac{F_x}{F_z},, -\frac{F_y}{F_z}\right)$$

and the tangent plane to the surface $F = 0$ at $(a, b, c)$ is:

$$F_x(a,b,c)(x-a) + F_y(a,b,c)(y-b) + F_z(a,b,c)(z-c) = 0$$

### Example — Ellipsoid

$\frac{x^2}{4} + \frac{y^2}{9} + z^2 = 1$. Find $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$ and $\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}$.

$F = \frac{x^2}{4} + \frac{y^2}{9} + z^2 - 1$.

$$F_x = \frac{x}{2}, \quad F_y = \frac{2y}{9}, \quad F_z = 2z$$

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{x/2}{2z} = -\frac{x}{4z}, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -\frac{2y/9}{2z} = -\frac{y}{9z}$$

At $(2, 0, 0)$: $F_z = 0$ — the theorem fails. Indeed, $(2, 0, 0)$ is a point with a vertical tangent plane ($z$ is not differentiable there).

## §6. The General Case — $n$ Variables, $m$ Constraints

Let $\mathbf{F}: \mathbb{R}^{n+m} \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be $C^1$ near $(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^m$ with $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{0}$.

Write the variables as $(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) \in \mathbb{R}^n \times \mathbb{R}^m$. The Jacobian of $\mathbf{F}$ with respect to $\mathbf{y}$:

$$J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F} = \left(\frac{\partial F_i}{\partial y_j}\right)_{i,j=1}^{m}$$

**Theorem** _(Implicit Function Theorem, General)._ If $\det(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})) \neq 0$, then there exists a unique $C^1$ function $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})$ near $\mathbf{a}$ with $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{a}) = \mathbf{b}$ and $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})) = \mathbf{0}$. Moreover:

$$J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{g} = -(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F})^{-1} \cdot J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{F}$$

This is the matrix version of $\frac{dy}{dx} = -F_x/F_y$.

**Proof sketch.** Differentiate $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})) = \mathbf{0}$ with respect to $\mathbf{x}$ via the chain rule:

$$J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{F} + J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F} \cdot J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{g} = 0$$

Since $J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F}$ is invertible (by hypothesis), solve: $J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{g} = -(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F})^{-1} J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{F}$. $\blacksquare$

The existence of $\mathbf{g}$ (not just its derivative) requires a deeper argument — the contraction mapping theorem or inverse function theorem.

## §7. Implicit Differentiation via Differentials — The Slickest Method

For any equation $F(x, y) = c$, take the total differential:

$$dF = F_x,dx + F_y,dy = 0$$

Then $\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}$ immediately, by treating $dF = 0$ as a linear constraint on $dx$ and $dy$.

For $F(x, y, z) = c$:

$$F_x,dx + F_y,dy + F_z,dz = 0$$

- Hold $y$ fixed ($dy = 0$): $\quad\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{F_x}{F_z}$
- Hold $x$ fixed ($dx = 0$): $\quad\frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -\frac{F_y}{F_z}$

No chain rule bookkeeping. Just algebra on differentials.

### The Cyclic Identity (a Beautiful Consequence)

From $F(x, y, z) = 0$, we have three implicit partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\bigg|_y = -\frac{F_x}{F_z}, \quad \frac{\partial x}{\partial y}\bigg|_z = -\frac{F_y}{F_x}, \quad \frac{\partial y}{\partial z}\bigg|_x = -\frac{F_z}{F_y}$$

Multiply all three:

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\bigg|_y \cdot \frac{\partial x}{\partial y}\bigg|_z \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial z}\bigg|_x = \left(-\frac{F_x}{F_z}\right)\left(-\frac{F_y}{F_x}\right)\left(-\frac{F_z}{F_y}\right) = -1$$

$$\boxed{\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\bigg|_y \cdot \frac{\partial x}{\partial y}\bigg|_z \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial z}\bigg|_x = -1}$$

This is the **cyclic identity** (or triple product rule, or Euler's chain rule). It is **not** the chain rule — it produces $-1$, not $+1$. It appears everywhere in thermodynamics: $(∂P/∂V)_T (∂V/∂T)_P (∂T/∂P)_V = -1$ is the same identity for an ideal gas.

**Warning.** This is not $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} \cdot \frac{\partial x}{\partial y} \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial z}$ in the chain rule sense. The subscripts matter — each partial holds a different variable fixed.

## §8. Second-Order Implicit Differentiation

Given $F(x, y) = 0$, compute $\frac{d^2y}{dx^2}$.

**Step 1.** Compute $y' = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}$.

**Step 2.** Differentiate $y' = -\frac{F_x(x,y)}{F_y(x,y)}$ with respect to $x$, using the quotient rule and chain rule (treating $y = y(x)$):

$$y'' = -\frac{(F_{xx} + F_{xy}y')F_y - F_x(F_{yx} + F_{yy}y')}{F_y^2}$$

Substitute $y' = -F_x/F_y$ and simplify:

$$y'' = -\frac{F_{xx}F_y^2 - 2F_{xy}F_xF_y + F_{yy}F_x^2}{F_y^3}$$

This is the standard formula. In practice, substitute $y'$ explicitly rather than memorizing this — the derivation is more reliable.

### Example — Implicitly Defined Curve

$x^2 + xy + y^2 = 3$. Find $y''$ at $(1, 1)$.

**Differential:** $2x,dx + y,dx + x,dy + 2y,dy = 0$ $$\Rightarrow (2x+y),dx + (x+2y),dy = 0$$ $$y' = -\frac{2x+y}{x+2y}$$

At $(1,1)$: $y' = -\frac{3}{3} = -1$.

**Second derivative.** Differentiate $y'(x+2y) = -(2x+y)$:

$$(x + 2y)y'' + (1 + 2y')y' = -(2 + y')$$

At $(1,1)$, $y' = -1$: $$(3)y'' + (1 - 2)(-1) = -(2 - 1)$$ $$3y'' + 1 = -1 \implies y'' = -\frac{2}{3}$$

## §9. Logarithmic Differentiation — Implicit Differentiation's Cousin

For $y = f(x)^{g(x)}$ or products/quotients of many factors, take the log of both sides first, then differentiate implicitly.

$y = f(x)^{g(x)}$. Take $\ln y = g(x)\ln f(x)$.

Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$:

$$\frac{y'}{y} = g'(x)\ln f(x) + g(x)\frac{f'(x)}{f(x)}$$

$$y' = y\left[g'\ln f + \frac{gf'}{f}\right] = f(x)^{g(x)}\left[g'(x)\ln f(x) + \frac{g(x)f'(x)}{f(x)}\right]$$

**Example.** $y = x^{\sin x}$.

$$\ln y = \sin x \cdot \ln x$$ $$\frac{y'}{y} = \cos x \ln x + \frac{\sin x}{x}$$ $$y' = x^{\sin x}\left(\cos x \ln x + \frac{\sin x}{x}\right)$$

## §10. Worked Examples — Gallery

### Example A — Tangent Line to an Implicit Curve

Find the tangent line to $y^2 = x^3(2 - x)$ (lemniscate-type) at $(1, 1)$.

$F = y^2 - x^3(2-x) = y^2 - 2x^3 + x^4$.

$$F_x = -6x^2 + 4x^3, \quad F_y = 2y$$

At $(1, 1)$: $F_x = -6 + 4 = -2$, $F_y = 2$.

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{-2}{2} = 1$$

Tangent line: $y - 1 = 1(x - 1)$, i.e., $y = x$.

### Example B — Horizontal and Vertical Tangents

$x^2 - xy + y^2 = 3$.

$$F_x = 2x - y, \quad F_y = -x + 2y, \quad y' = -\frac{2x-y}{-x+2y} = \frac{2x-y}{x-2y}$$

**Horizontal tangents** ($y' = 0$): $2x - y = 0 \Rightarrow y = 2x$. Substitute into the curve: $$x^2 - x(2x) + (2x)^2 = 3 \Rightarrow 3x^2 = 3 \Rightarrow x = \pm 1$$ Points: $(1, 2)$ and $(-1, -2)$.

**Vertical tangents** ($y' = \infty$, i.e., $F_y = 0$): $x - 2y = 0 \Rightarrow x = 2y$. Substitute: $$(2y)^2 - (2y)y + y^2 = 3 \Rightarrow 3y^2 = 3 \Rightarrow y = \pm 1$$ Points: $(2, 1)$ and $(-2, -1)$.

### Example C — Partial Derivatives from an Implicit Surface

The ideal gas law $PV = nRT$ defines any variable as a function of the others. Take $F(P, V, T) = PV - nRT = 0$.

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\bigg|_P = -\frac{F_T}{F_V} = -\frac{-nR}{P} = \frac{nR}{P}$$

$$\frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\bigg|_T = -\frac{F_V}{F_P} = -\frac{P}{V}$$

$$\frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\bigg|_V = -\frac{F_P}{F_T} = -\frac{V}{-nR} = \frac{V}{nR}$$

**Check the cyclic identity:**

$$\frac{\partial V}{\partial T}\bigg|_P \cdot \frac{\partial T}{\partial P}\bigg|_V \cdot \frac{\partial P}{\partial V}\bigg|_T = \frac{nR}{P} \cdot \frac{V}{nR} \cdot \left(-\frac{P}{V}\right) = -1 \checkmark$$

## §11. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 11.1 — Implicit Layers in Deep Learning

The standard neural network layer is **explicit**: given input $\mathbf{x}$, compute output $\mathbf{y} = f(\mathbf{x}; \theta)$ in one forward pass.

An **implicit layer** is defined by a constraint:

$$F(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x}; \theta) = 0$$

The output $\mathbf{y}$ is the solution to this equation. Examples:

- **Fixed-point layers**: $\mathbf{y} = f(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x})$ (Deep Equilibrium Models)
- **Optimization layers**: $\mathbf{y} = \arg\min_\mathbf{z} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{z}, \mathbf{x}; \theta)$, which satisfies $\nabla_\mathbf{z} \mathcal{L}(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x}; \theta) = 0$

To train these by gradient descent, we need $\frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \theta}$ and $\frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}$ — but $\mathbf{y}$ has no explicit formula. Enter implicit differentiation.

**The Implicit Function Theorem applied.** Differentiate $F(\mathbf{y}(\theta), \mathbf{x}, \theta) = 0$ with respect to $\theta$:

$$\underbrace{\frac{\partial F}{\partial \mathbf{y}}}_{\text{square matrix}} \frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \theta} + \frac{\partial F}{\partial \theta} = 0$$

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \theta} = -\left(\frac{\partial F}{\partial \mathbf{y}}\right)^{-1} \frac{\partial F}{\partial \theta}$$

This is the general IFT formula. We never need to unroll the iterative solver that finds $\mathbf{y}$ — we only need the Jacobian $\frac{\partial F}{\partial \mathbf{y}}$ at the solution. This is called **implicit differentiation through fixed points**.

### 11.2 — Deep Equilibrium Models (DEQ)

A DEQ (Bai et al., 2019) replaces all layers with:

$$\mathbf{z}^\star = f_\theta(\mathbf{z}^\star, \mathbf{x})$$

i.e., $\mathbf{z}^\star$ is a fixed point. Forward pass: iterate $f$ until convergence (Newton's method, Anderson acceleration). Backward pass: never unroll the iterations. Instead, at convergence:

$$F(\mathbf{z}^\star, \mathbf{x}; \theta) = \mathbf{z}^\star - f_\theta(\mathbf{z}^\star, \mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{0}$$

Implicit differentiation gives:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{z}^\star}{\partial \theta} = -\left(I - \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial \mathbf{z}}\bigg|_{\mathbf{z}^\star}\right)^{-1} \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial \theta}\bigg|_{\mathbf{z}^\star}$$

The matrix $\left(I - J_f\right)$ must be invertible — this is the IFT condition. At a stable fixed point (spectral radius of $J_f < 1$), the Neumann series guarantees invertibility:

$$(I - J_f)^{-1} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty J_f^k$$

Convergence of the fixed-point iteration and invertibility of $(I - J_f)$ are two sides of the same contraction condition.

**Memory advantage.** An explicit $L$-layer network stores $O(L)$ activations for backprop. A DEQ stores only the fixed point $\mathbf{z}^\star$ — $O(1)$ memory, regardless of how many iterations the forward pass took.

### 11.3 — Differentiable Optimization Layers (OptNet, cvxpylayers)

An optimization layer solves:

$$\mathbf{y}^\star(\mathbf{x}) = \arg\min_{\mathbf{y}} f(\mathbf{y}; \mathbf{x}) \quad \text{s.t. } g(\mathbf{y}; \mathbf{x}) \leq 0$$

At the optimum, the KKT conditions (necessary optimality conditions) give a system:

$$\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{y}^\star, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^\star, \mathbf{x}) = 0$$

where $\boldsymbol{\lambda}$ are the dual variables. Implicit differentiation on $\mathbf{F} = 0$:

$$\frac{\partial(\mathbf{y}^\star, \boldsymbol{\lambda}^\star)}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = -\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial (\mathbf{y}, \boldsymbol{\lambda})}\right)^{-1} \frac{\partial \mathbf{F}}{\partial \mathbf{x}}$$

This is the foundation of **differentiable convex optimization** (Amos & Kolter, 2017). The optimization layer can be embedded anywhere in a neural network, and gradients flow through it via the IFT, not through the solver iterations.

### 11.4 — Neural ODEs

A Neural ODE (Chen et al., 2018) defines the hidden state dynamics by:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{h}}{dt} = f_\theta(\mathbf{h}(t), t)$$

with $\mathbf{h}(t_0) = \mathbf{x}$. The output $\mathbf{h}(T)$ is the solution of this ODE at time $T$. The **adjoint method** for computing $\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta}$ is implicit differentiation applied to the ODE:

Define the adjoint $\mathbf{a}(t) = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{h}(t)}$. It satisfies the **adjoint ODE**:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{a}}{dt} = -\mathbf{a}(t)^\top \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial \mathbf{h}}$$

The gradient with respect to parameters:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \theta} = -\int_{t_0}^{T} \mathbf{a}(t)^\top \frac{\partial f_\theta}{\partial \theta},dt$$

No backpropagation through the ODE solver. Memory is $O(1)$ in depth. The adjoint ODE is derived by implicit differentiation of the optimality conditions — the IFT, applied continuously.

### 11.5 — The IFT and Meta-Learning

**MAML** (Model-Agnostic Meta-Learning, Finn et al., 2017) seeks parameters $\theta$ such that one gradient step on a new task gives good performance. The inner-loop update:

$$\phi(\theta) = \theta - \alpha \nabla_\theta \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}}(\theta)$$

The meta-objective requires $\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial \theta}$. For one gradient step this is explicit. For many steps, computing $\frac{\partial \phi}{\partial \theta}$ via unrolling is expensive. **iMAML** (Rajeswaran et al., 2019) instead defines $\phi$ as the solution to:

$$\phi^\star(\theta) = \arg\min_\phi \left[\mathcal{L}_{\text{task}}(\phi) + \frac{\lambda}{2}|\phi - \theta|^2\right]$$

which satisfies $\nabla_\phi \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}}(\phi^\star) + \lambda(\phi^\star - \theta) = 0$. This is $F(\phi^\star, \theta) = 0$. Implicit differentiation:

$$\frac{\partial \phi^\star}{\partial \theta} = \left(\nabla^2_\phi \mathcal{L}_{\text{task}} + \lambda I\right)^{-1} \cdot \lambda I$$

The Hessian inverse appears — but it can be approximated by conjugate gradient, making iMAML practical. The IFT removes the need to store the full inner-loop computation graph.

## §12. Summary — The Structure of Implicit Differentiation

$$F(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}) = 0 \xrightarrow{\text{differentiate}} J_\mathbf{x}F + J_\mathbf{y}F \cdot \frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = 0 \xrightarrow{\text{solve}} \frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \mathbf{x}} = -(J_\mathbf{y}F)^{-1} J_\mathbf{x}F$$

|Setting|Formula|Condition|
|---|---|---|
|$F(x,y)=0$, find $y'$|$y' = -F_x/F_y$|$F_y \neq 0$|
|$F(x,y,z)=0$, find $z_x$|$z_x = -F_x/F_z$|$F_z \neq 0$|
|$\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y})=0$, find $J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{y}$|$-(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F})^{-1}J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{F}$|$\det(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F}) \neq 0$|
|Fixed point $\mathbf{z}=f(\mathbf{z},\mathbf{x})$|$-(I-J_\mathbf{z}f)^{-1}J_\mathbf{x}f$|$\rho(J_\mathbf{z}f) < 1$|
|Optimization layer|$-(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F}_{\text{KKT}})^{-1}J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{F}_{\text{KKT}}$|KKT nondegeneracy|

## §13. Theorems to Know Cold

**Theorem 1** _(Implicit Function Theorem, 2D)._ If $F \in C^1$ near $(a,b)$, $F(a,b) = 0$, $F_y(a,b) \neq 0$: then $y = g(x)$ exists locally, is $C^1$, and $g' = -F_x/F_y$.

**Theorem 2** _(IFT, $n$D)._ If $\mathbf{F} \in C^1$ near $(\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b})$, $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{a},\mathbf{b}) = \mathbf{0}$, $\det(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F}) \neq 0$: then $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})$ exists locally, is $C^1$, and $J\mathbf{g} = -(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F})^{-1}J_\mathbf{x}\mathbf{F}$.

**Theorem 3** _(Cyclic Identity)._ If $F(x,y,z)=0$ defines each variable implicitly in terms of the other two: $$\frac{\partial x}{\partial y}\bigg|_z \cdot \frac{\partial y}{\partial z}\bigg|_x \cdot \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}\bigg|_y = -1$$

**Theorem 4** _(Inverse Function Theorem — a Corollary of IFT)._ If $\mathbf{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is $C^1$ near $\mathbf{a}$ and $\det(J_\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{a})) \neq 0$, then $\mathbf{f}$ is locally invertible near $\mathbf{a}$, and $J_{\mathbf{f}^{-1}}(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{a})) = (J_\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{a}))^{-1}$.

_Proof:_ Apply IFT to $F(\mathbf{y}, \mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{y}) - \mathbf{x} = 0$.

## §14. Things That Will Trip You Up

> **$F_y = 0$ kills implicit differentiation** at that point. The tangent may be vertical, or the curve may have a singularity. Always check.

> **The notation $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$ is ambiguous without context.** Is $y$ being held fixed? Is $z$ defined implicitly by one equation or two? Always state what is being held constant.

> **Second derivatives require substituting $y'$ before simplifying.** Never leave $y'$ in the expression for $y''$ — substitute the formula you found and simplify.

> **The cyclic identity gives $-1$, not $+1$.** This surprises everyone the first time. It is not the chain rule.

> **Implicit differentiation gives local information only.** The IFT guarantees a function $y = g(x)$ only near the point where $F_y \neq 0$. The function may not extend globally, and there may be multiple branches.

> **In the ML context**: implicit differentiation through a solver assumes the solver has converged. The formula $\frac{\partial \mathbf{y}}{\partial \theta} = -(J_\mathbf{y}\mathbf{F})^{-1}J_\theta\mathbf{F}$ is only valid at the exact solution $\mathbf{F}(\mathbf{y}^\star, \theta) = 0$. Approximate solutions give biased gradients.
