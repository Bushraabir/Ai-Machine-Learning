

> _"A function of one variable is a curve. A function of two variables is a landscape — it has hills, valleys, ridges, and saddle points. Learning to read that landscape is the entire project of multivariable calculus."_

---

## 0. Motivation

In single-variable calculus, we study $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$. But the real world is rarely one-dimensional:

- Temperature in a room depends on position $(x, y)$ on the floor.
- The pressure of a gas depends on volume $V$ and temperature $T$.
- The loss of a neural network depends on millions of parameters $(\theta_1, \theta_2, \ldots, \theta_p)$.

The step from one variable to two is not merely an added variable — it is a qualitative leap in richness. New phenomena appear: partial derivatives, mixed partials, level curves, saddle points, constrained extrema. Understanding the two-variable case deeply is the prerequisite for everything that follows.

---

## 1. Definition

### Formal Definition

A **function of two variables** is a rule $f$ that assigns to each ordered pair $(x, y)$ in a set $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ a unique real number $z = f(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$f: D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad (x,y) \mapsto f(x,y)$$

- $D$ is the **domain** of $f$ — the set of valid inputs.
- The **range** is ${f(x,y) : (x,y) \in D} \subseteq \mathbb{R}$.
- $z = f(x,y)$ is the **dependent variable**; $x, y$ are **independent variables**.

### Natural Domain

Unless stated otherwise, the domain of $f$ is the **largest subset of $\mathbb{R}^2$ for which the expression $f(x,y)$ makes sense** (real-valued, defined). This requires excluding:

- Points where denominators vanish.
- Points where expressions under even roots are negative.
- Points where logarithms receive non-positive arguments.

**Example:** $f(x,y) = \dfrac{\sqrt{y - x^2}}{1 - x^2 - y^2}$

- Need $y - x^2 \geq 0$: above or on the parabola $y = x^2$.
- Need $1 - x^2 - y^2 \neq 0$: outside the unit circle.

Domain: ${(x,y) : y \geq x^2} \setminus {(x,y): x^2+y^2 = 1}$.

## 2. The Graph

### Definition

The **graph** of $f: D \to \mathbb{R}$ is the set of all points $(x,y,z) \in \mathbb{R}^3$ such that $z = f(x,y)$ and $(x,y) \in D$:

$$\text{graph}(f) = {(x, y, f(x,y)) : (x,y) \in D} \subset \mathbb{R}^3$$

Geometrically: a **surface** in $\mathbb{R}^3$ lying above (or below) the $xy$-plane.

### Visualization

Think of the $xy$-plane as a map. The value $f(x,y)$ is the **elevation** at location $(x,y)$. The graph is the terrain — the topographic surface.

```
        z
        │        *  surface z = f(x,y)
        │      * *  *
        │    *       *
        │  *           *
        │*               *
        └──────────────────── y
       /
      /
     x

For each (x,y) in the domain, there is exactly one height z = f(x,y).
```

### Key Surfaces to Know

|Function|Surface|
|---|---|
|$z = ax + by + c$|Plane|
|$z = x^2 + y^2$|Circular paraboloid (bowl)|
|$z = x^2 - y^2$|Hyperbolic paraboloid (saddle)|
|$z = \sqrt{x^2 + y^2}$|Cone|
|$z = \sqrt{1 - x^2 - y^2}$|Upper hemisphere of unit sphere|
|$z = e^{-(x^2+y^2)}$|Gaussian bell surface|

## 3. Level Curves

### Definition

The **level curve** (or **contour**) of $f$ at height $c \in \mathbb{R}$ is the set:

$$L_c = {(x,y) \in D : f(x,y) = c}$$

It is the **horizontal cross-section** of the graph at height $z = c$, projected down to the $xy$-plane.

**Crucially:** A point $(x,y)$ lies on the level curve $L_c$ if and only if the surface passes through height $c$ directly above it.

### The Contour Map

A **contour map** (contour plot) is the collection of level curves ${L_c}$ for various values of $c$, drawn in the $xy$-plane. Every topographic map you have ever read is a contour map.

Reading a contour map:

- **Closely spaced contours:** Steep terrain (rapid change in $f$).
- **Widely spaced contours:** Gentle terrain (slow change in $f$).
- **Closed contours:** Peaks, valleys, or saddle points.
- **Contours never cross:** If $L_c$ and $L_{c'}$ crossed at a point $(x_0, y_0)$, then $f(x_0,y_0) = c$ and $f(x_0,y_0) = c'$ simultaneously — impossible for $c \neq c'$.

### Examples

**$f(x,y) = x^2 + y^2$:** Level curves $c = x^2 + y^2$ are **circles** of radius $\sqrt{c}$ centered at the origin. The surface is a paraboloid. Contours are evenly spaced circles — but the paraboloid gets steeper as $r$ increases, so equal spacing in $c$ gives circles that pack closer together.

**$f(x,y) = x^2 - y^2$:** Level curves $c = x^2 - y^2$ are **hyperbolas** (for $c \neq 0$) and the two lines $y = \pm x$ (for $c = 0$). This is the **saddle surface** — its contour map looks like a topographic pass between two mountains.

**$f(x,y) = xy$:** Level curves $xy = c$ are **rectangular hyperbolas** with asymptotes along the axes.

```
Contour map of z = x² + y²:       Contour map of z = x² - y² (saddle):

    y                                   y
    │   (3)  (2)  (1)                   │
    │  ╭──╮╭──╮╭──╮                    │  ╲ ╱ ╲ ╱
    │  │  ││  ││  │                     │   ╳   ╳
    │  ╰──╯╰──╯╰──╯                    │  ╱ ╲ ╱ ╲
    └──────────────── x                 └──────────────── x

  circles = level curves               hyperbolas = level curves
  (bowl shape)                         (saddle shape)
```

## 4. Limits and Continuity

### The Core Difficulty

In $\mathbb{R}$, approaching a point $a$ means coming from the left or right — only two paths. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, approaching $(a,b)$ means coming from **infinitely many directions** and along **infinitely many curved paths**. For a limit to exist, the function must approach the same value **along every possible path**.

### Definition

$$\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)} f(x,y) = L$$

means: for every $\varepsilon > 0$, there exists $\delta > 0$ such that:

$$0 < \sqrt{(x-a)^2 + (y-b)^2} < \delta \implies |f(x,y) - L| < \varepsilon$$

### The Two-Path Test (Nonexistence)

To show a limit **does not exist**: find two paths to $(a,b)$ along which $f$ approaches different values.

**Example:** Show $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{x^2 - y^2}{x^2 + y^2}$ does not exist.

- Along $y = 0$: $f(x,0) = x^2/x^2 = 1 \to 1$.
- Along $x = 0$: $f(0,y) = -y^2/y^2 = -1 \to -1$.

Since $1 \neq -1$, the limit does not exist. $\square$

**Example (subtler):** Show $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{xy^2}{x^2+y^4}$ does not exist.

- Along $y = 0$: $f \to 0$.
- Along $x = y^2$: $f = \frac{y^2\cdot y^2}{y^4 + y^4} = \frac{1}{2} \to \frac{1}{2}$.

Limit does not exist. Note: along any **straight** line $y = mx$, the limit is $0$ — only a parabolic path reveals the failure. This shows why the two-path test must consider curves, not just lines.

### Continuity

$f$ is **continuous at $(a,b)$** if:

1. $f(a,b)$ is defined.
2. $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)} f(x,y)$ exists.
3. $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(a,b)} f(x,y) = f(a,b)$.

**Theorem:** Polynomials in $x,y$ are continuous on all of $\mathbb{R}^2$. Rational functions are continuous on their domains. Compositions of continuous functions are continuous.

**Consequence:** For nice functions (polynomials, rationals away from singularities, exponentials, trig), you can evaluate limits by **direct substitution**. The challenge is only at boundary points of the domain or suspected discontinuities.

## 5. Partial Derivatives (First Pass)

Full treatment in the next note. Here, the idea.

The **partial derivative** $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial x}(a,b)$ is the derivative of $f$ in the $x$-direction, holding $y = b$ fixed:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial x}(a,b) = \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{f(a+h,b) - f(a,b)}{h}$$

Geometrically: it is the slope of the curve obtained by slicing the surface $z = f(x,y)$ with the vertical plane $y = b$.

Similarly for $\dfrac{\partial f}{\partial y}$: hold $x = a$ fixed, differentiate in $y$.

**Rule:** To compute $\partial f/\partial x$, treat $y$ as a constant and differentiate in $x$ using all single-variable rules.

## 6. Worked Examples

### Example 1: Domain

Find the domain of $f(x,y) = \ln(x + y - 1)$.

Need $x + y - 1 > 0$, i.e., $x + y > 1$.

Domain: the open half-plane above the line $x + y = 1$. The boundary line is excluded (log of zero is undefined).

---

### Example 2: Level Curves of a Gaussian

Let $f(x,y) = e^{-(x^2 + y^2)}$.

Level curve at height $c$ (where $0 < c \leq 1$): $$e^{-(x^2+y^2)} = c \implies x^2 + y^2 = -\ln c$$

For $0 < c < 1$: $-\ln c > 0$, so these are circles of radius $\sqrt{-\ln c}$. As $c \to 1^-$: radius $\to 0$ (peak at origin). As $c \to 0^+$: radius $\to \infty$ (function decays to zero far from origin).

The contour map is a family of concentric circles — and the circles get farther apart as $c$ decreases (the Gaussian decays rapidly near origin, then flattens out).

---

### Example 3: A Subtle Limit

Show $\displaystyle\lim_{(x,y)\to(0,0)}\frac{x^2 y}{x^2+y^2} = 0$.

Use the Squeeze Theorem. Observe: $$\left|\frac{x^2 y}{x^2+y^2}\right| \leq \frac{x^2 |y|}{x^2+y^2} \leq |y|$$

since $x^2 \leq x^2 + y^2$. As $(x,y) \to (0,0)$, $|y| \to 0$. By squeeze, the limit is $0$.

_Note:_ To confirm, convert to polar: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$: $$\frac{r^2\cos^2\theta \cdot r\sin\theta}{r^2} = r\cos^2\theta\sin\theta \to 0 \text{ as } r\to 0$$

since $|\cos^2\theta\sin\theta| \leq 1$ for all $\theta$. The polar method works when the bound is independent of $\theta$.

---

### Example 4: Discontinuity

Let $f(x,y) = \begin{cases} \dfrac{2xy}{x^2+y^2} & (x,y)\neq(0,0) \ 0 & (x,y) = (0,0)\end{cases}$

Is $f$ continuous at $(0,0)$?

Along $y = mx$: $f(x,mx) = \dfrac{2mx^2}{x^2(1+m^2)} = \dfrac{2m}{1+m^2}$, which depends on $m$.

For $m = 1$: limit is $1$. For $m = 0$: limit is $0$. The limit does not exist, so $f$ is **not continuous** at $(0,0)$, regardless of how we define $f(0,0)$.

### The "Slicing" Interpretation

The surface $z = f(x,y)$ is simultaneously:

- The set of all level curves stacked at their respective heights.
- The set of all "traces" — curves obtained by fixing $x$ or $y$ to a constant.

```
Fix y = b:  curve z = f(x, b) in the plane y = b  →  slope = ∂f/∂x
Fix x = a:  curve z = f(a, y) in the plane x = a  →  slope = ∂f/∂y
```

## 8. Functions of Three or More Variables

The definitions extend naturally:

$$f: D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad (x_1, \ldots, x_n) \mapsto f(x_1,\ldots,x_n)$$

We can no longer visualize the graph (it lives in $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$). Instead, we study **level sets**:

$$L_c = {(x_1,\ldots,x_n) : f(x_1,\ldots,x_n) = c}$$

which are $(n-1)$-dimensional hypersurfaces in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

For $n = 3$: $f(x,y,z) = c$ defines a **level surface** in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — e.g., $x^2+y^2+z^2 = r^2$ (sphere), $x^2/a^2+y^2/b^2+z^2/c^2 = 1$ (ellipsoid).

**All the theory — limits, continuity, partial derivatives, gradients — extends verbatim** to $n$ variables. The geometry is harder to visualize but the algebra is identical.

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

### 9.1 The Loss Function Is a Function of Millions of Variables

The most important function in all of machine learning is the **loss function**:

$$\mathcal{L}: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}, \qquad \boldsymbol{\theta} \mapsto \mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol{\theta})$$

where $p$ is the number of parameters (GPT-4: $p \sim 10^{12}$). This is exactly a function of $p$ variables — the direct generalization of $f(x,y)$ to $p$ dimensions.

Everything we study for $f(x,y)$ has a direct ML analog:

|$f(x,y)$ concept|ML analog|
|---|---|
|Domain $D \subset \mathbb{R}^2$|Parameter space $\mathbb{R}^p$|
|Graph $z = f(x,y)$|Loss landscape (unvisualizable for $p \gg 2$)|
|Level curve $f(x,y) = c$|**Loss contour** $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta) = c$ — iso-loss surface in parameter space|
|Continuity|Smoothness of $\mathcal{L}$ (required for gradient methods to work)|
|Partial derivative $\partial f/\partial x$|Gradient component $\partial\mathcal{L}/\partial\theta_i$ — used in backpropagation|

### 9.2 Level Curves as Decision Boundaries

For a binary classifier $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, the **decision boundary** is the level curve $f(x,y) = 0.5$ (or $f = 0$ for logistic regression). The classifier outputs:

- Class 1 if $f(x,y) > 0.5$
- Class 0 if $f(x,y) < 0.5$

A **linear classifier** produces straight-line decision boundaries (level curves of a linear $f$). A **neural network** can produce arbitrarily complex level curves — this is the Universal Approximation Theorem in geometric form: a neural network can approximate any continuous function $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$, hence any level curve as a decision boundary.

### 9.3 Continuity and the Vanishing Gradient Problem

The **continuity** of the loss function near a region of parameter space is directly connected to gradient flow. At a discontinuity or near a point where the gradient is zero but the function is not at a minimum (a **flat region** or **plateau**), gradient descent stalls — the optimization analogue of a function with zero partial derivatives everywhere in a region.

The Rectified Linear Unit (**ReLU**) activation $\text{ReLU}(x) = \max(0,x)$ makes networks that are continuous but **not differentiable** at $x = 0$. The composed function $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta)$ for a ReLU network is a **piecewise linear** function of $\boldsymbol\theta$ — continuous everywhere, differentiable almost everywhere.

### 9.4 Gaussian Functions and Radial Basis Networks

The function $f(x,y) = e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2\sigma^2}$ (Example 2 above) is the **2D Gaussian**. Its level curves are concentric circles. In ML:

- **Kernel methods** (SVMs with RBF kernel): The kernel $K(\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{x}') = e^{-|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{x}'|^2/2\sigma^2}$ measures similarity as a Gaussian of distance.
- **Radial Basis Function (RBF) networks**: Hidden units compute $\phi(\mathbf{x}) = e^{-|\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{c}_i|^2/2\sigma_i^2}$ — exactly the two-variable Gaussian, with centers $\mathbf{c}_i$.
- **Gaussian Processes**: The covariance function is a Gaussian of the input distance — level curves of this covariance are ellipses in input space.

### 9.5 Latent Space and Feature Maps

In a **Variational Autoencoder (VAE)** or **diffusion model**, data is mapped to a 2D (or low-dimensional) **latent space**. The decoder then defines a function $f: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^d$ (going from 2 latent variables to a high-dimensional output — the reverse direction of what we study here, but the domain is $\mathbb{R}^2$).

The **latent space interpolation** — walking along a straight line or curve in the $(z_1, z_2)$ plane — is exactly choosing a path in the domain $D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$ of the decoder function. The continuity of $f$ ensures nearby latent points decode to nearby outputs.

### 9.6 Two-Variable Functions as Neural Network Layers

A single neuron with two inputs computes:

$$z = \sigma(w_1 x + w_2 y + b)$$

This is a function $f(x,y) = \sigma(w_1 x + w_2 y + b)$. Its level curves are:

$$w_1 x + w_2 y + b = \sigma^{-1}(c) = \text{const}$$

which are **straight lines** (linear decision boundaries). Stacking neurons allows the level curves of the composed function to become arbitrarily curved — the geometric meaning of expressiveness.

### 9.7 Domain and Data Manifold

In practice, input data does not fill all of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Natural images, for instance, occupy a low-dimensional **manifold** $\mathcal{M} \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ (the **manifold hypothesis**). The effective domain of the learned function $f$ is this manifold — points off the manifold are out-of-distribution. This is the geometric basis for why neural networks hallucinate and fail on adversarial examples: the function is evaluated outside its meaningful domain $D$.

## 10. Summary

A function of two variables $f: D \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ is a surface in $\mathbb{R}^3$, studied through:

**Domain:** Largest set where $f(x,y)$ makes sense — exclude division by zero, square roots of negatives, logs of non-positives.

**Graph:** The surface $z = f(x,y)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — the geometric object we analyze.

**Level curves:** $f(x,y) = c$ — horizontal slices of the graph, contour lines on a map. They never cross; dense packing means steep terrain.

**Limits:** Must agree along every path to $(a,b)$, not just along lines. Two-path test proves nonexistence; polar coordinates and squeeze theorem prove existence.

**Continuity:** Defined exactly as in one variable, but the limit condition is harder to verify due to the infinity of approach paths.

The entire theory extends to $n$ variables. In machine learning, the loss landscape $\mathcal{L}(\boldsymbol\theta)$ is a function of millions of variables — but every concept introduced here (domain, level sets, continuity, partial derivatives) applies directly, at scale.
