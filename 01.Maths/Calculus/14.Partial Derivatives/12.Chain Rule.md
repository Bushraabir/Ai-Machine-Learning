

> _"The chain rule is not a formula. It is a theorem about the composition of linear approximations. Everything else — backpropagation, automatic differentiation, implicit differentiation — is a corollary."_

---

## §1. Motivation — Composition and Rate of Change

In single-variable calculus, if $y = f(u)$ and $u = g(x)$, then:

$$\frac{dy}{dx} = \frac{dy}{du} \cdot \frac{du}{dx}$$

This is almost too clean. The fractions "cancel." But in multiple variables, the story is richer: a function can depend on many intermediate variables, each of which depends on many others. The chain rule tells us how to **trace the influence of a change at the source through every path it takes to the output**.

The multivariable chain rule is not one formula — it is a **family of formulas**, all instances of one underlying theorem: the derivative of a composition is the composition of the derivatives.

---

## §2. The One-Variable Chain Rule — The Right Way to Think About It

Let $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ and $g: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, both differentiable. Define $h = f \circ g$, i.e., $h(x) = f(g(x))$.

**Theorem** _(Chain Rule, 1D)._ $h'(x) = f'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x)$.

**Proof sketch.** By differentiability of $g$ at $x$: $$g(x+\Delta x) = g(x) + g'(x)\Delta x + \varepsilon_1 \Delta x, \quad \varepsilon_1 \to 0$$

By differentiability of $f$ at $u = g(x)$: $$f(u + \Delta u) = f(u) + f'(u)\Delta u + \varepsilon_2 \Delta u, \quad \varepsilon_2 \to 0$$

Setting $\Delta u = g(x+\Delta x) - g(x) = g'(x)\Delta x + \varepsilon_1 \Delta x$:

$$h(x+\Delta x) - h(x) = f'(g(x))\bigl[g'(x)\Delta x + \varepsilon_1\Delta x\bigr] + \varepsilon_2\bigl[g'(x)\Delta x + \varepsilon_1\Delta x\bigr]$$

Divide by $\Delta x$ and let $\Delta x \to 0$. The error terms vanish: $h'(x) = f'(g(x))g'(x)$. $\blacksquare$

**The key insight.** The chain rule says: the linear approximation of a composition is the composition of the linear approximations. Derivatives linearize; linear maps compose by multiplication. That is all.

## §3. The Multivariable Chain Rule — General Statement

Let $\mathbf{g}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ be differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$, and $f: \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}$ be differentiable at $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{a})$. Define $h = f \circ \mathbf{g}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$.

**Theorem** _(Chain Rule, General)._ $h$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$, and:

$$dh_\mathbf{a} = df_{\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{a})} \circ d\mathbf{g}_\mathbf{a}$$

In matrix/coordinate form, if $\mathbf{g} = (g_1, \ldots, g_m)$ and $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)$:

$$\frac{\partial h}{\partial x_j} = \sum_{i=1}^{m} \frac{\partial f}{\partial u_i} \cdot \frac{\partial g_i}{\partial x_j}$$

or equivalently: $J_h = J_f \cdot J_\mathbf{g}$ (Jacobian of composition = product of Jacobians).

**In differential notation** (the most elegant form):

$$dh = df = \sum_{i=1}^m \frac{\partial f}{\partial u_i},du_i, \quad du_i = \sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\partial g_i}{\partial x_j},dx_j$$

Substitute $du_i$ into $dh$: you get the chain rule automatically, by pure algebraic substitution.

## §4. Case I — One Independent Variable

$z = f(x, y)$, $x = x(t)$, $y = y(t)$. Then $z$ is a function of $t$ alone:

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{dx}{dt} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{dy}{dt}$$

**Think of it as:** every path from $t$ to $z$ must pass through either $x$ or $y$. Sum the contributions along each path:

```
        t
       / \
      x   y
       \ /
        z
```

Each edge contributes a derivative; each path contributes a product; sum over all paths.

**Example.** $z = x^2 + \sin(xy)$, $x = e^t$, $y = t^2$. Find $\frac{dz}{dt}$.

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 2x + y\cos(xy), \quad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = x\cos(xy)$$ $$\frac{dx}{dt} = e^t, \quad \frac{dy}{dt} = 2t$$

$$\frac{dz}{dt} = (2x + y\cos(xy))e^t + x\cos(xy) \cdot 2t$$

Substituting $x = e^t$, $y = t^2$ gives the explicit form if needed.

## §5. Case II — Two Independent Variables

$z = f(x, y)$, $x = x(s, t)$, $y = y(s, t)$. Now $z$ is a function of $s$ and $t$:

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s}$$

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial f}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial f}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial t}$$

**Dependency diagram:**

```
      s   t         s   t
       \ /           \ /
        x             y
         \           /
          \         /
               z
```

For $\partial z/\partial s$: trace all paths from $s$ to $z$. Path $s \to x \to z$ contributes $\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} \cdot \frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$. Path $s \to y \to z$ contributes $\frac{\partial y}{\partial s} \cdot \frac{\partial z}{\partial y}$. Sum them.

**Example.** $z = e^{xy}$, $x = s^2 - t^2$, $y = s + 2t$.

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial s} = ye^{xy}(2s) + xe^{xy}(1) = (2sy + x)e^{xy}$$ $$\frac{\partial z}{\partial t} = ye^{xy}(-2t) + xe^{xy}(2) = (-2ty + 2x)e^{xy}$$

## §6. The Tree Diagram Rule — A Systematic Algorithm

For any composition, the chain rule is computed by the following algorithm:

> **Draw the dependency diagram.** Each variable is a node. An arrow from $u$ to $v$ means "$v$ depends on $u$."
> 
> **For each path from an independent variable to the target, form the product of derivatives along that path.**
> 
> **Sum over all such paths.**

This is not a heuristic — it is a direct consequence of the general formula $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x_j} = \sum_i \frac{\partial z}{\partial u_i}\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j}$.

**Example — Three layers.** $w = f(x, y, z)$, each of $x, y, z$ depending on $s$ and $t$:

$$\frac{\partial w}{\partial s} = \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}\frac{\partial x}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}\frac{\partial y}{\partial s} + \frac{\partial w}{\partial z}\frac{\partial z}{\partial s}$$

Three paths from $s$ to $w$; each contributes one term.

## §7. Implicit Differentiation — The Chain Rule in Disguise

Suppose $F(x, y) = 0$ defines $y$ implicitly as a function of $x$. Differentiate both sides with respect to $x$, treating $y = y(x)$:

$$\frac{d}{dx}F(x, y(x)) = 0$$

Chain rule on the left:

$$F_x \cdot 1 + F_y \cdot \frac{dy}{dx} = 0 \implies \boxed{\frac{dy}{dx} = -\frac{F_x}{F_y}}$$

**For $F(x, y, z) = 0$ defining $z = z(x,y)$:**

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{F_x}{F_z}, \qquad \frac{\partial z}{\partial y} = -\frac{F_y}{F_z}$$

**Proof.** Differentiate $F(x, y, z(x,y)) = 0$ with respect to $x$, holding $y$ fixed:

$$F_x \cdot 1 + F_y \cdot 0 + F_z \cdot \frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = 0$$

Solve for $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$. The formula follows immediately. $\blacksquare$

**Example.** $x^3 + y^3 + z^3 - 3xyz = 1$. Find $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$.

$F = x^3 + y^3 + z^3 - 3xyz$. Then $F_x = 3x^2 - 3yz$, $F_z = 3z^2 - 3xy$.

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = -\frac{3x^2 - 3yz}{3z^2 - 3xy} = \frac{yz - x^2}{z^2 - xy}$$

## §8. The Chain Rule in Differential Notation

In differentials, the chain rule requires no separate statement — it is automatic substitution.

$z = f(u, v)$, $u = u(x, y)$, $v = v(x,y)$:

$$dz = f_u,du + f_v,dv$$

Now substitute $du = u_x,dx + u_y,dy$ and $dv = v_x,dx + v_y,dy$:

$$dz = f_u(u_x,dx + u_y,dy) + f_v(v_x,dx + v_y,dy)$$ $$= (f_u u_x + f_v v_x),dx + (f_u u_y + f_v v_y),dy$$

Read off $\frac{\partial z}{\partial x}$ and $\frac{\partial z}{\partial y}$ as coefficients. The chain rule is invisible — it's already encoded in the substitution.

## §9. The Jacobian Formulation

For $\mathbf{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ and $\mathbf{g}: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^n$, the Jacobian of $\mathbf{f} \circ \mathbf{g}$ is:

$$J_{\mathbf{f} \circ \mathbf{g}}(\mathbf{x}) = J_\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})) \cdot J_\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})$$

where $J_\mathbf{f}$ is $m \times n$ and $J_\mathbf{g}$ is $n \times p$, so the product is $m \times p$. The chain rule is **matrix multiplication of Jacobians**.

**Coordinate form:**

$$(J_{\mathbf{f}\circ\mathbf{g}})_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^n (J_\mathbf{f})_{ik}(J_\mathbf{g})_{kj} = \sum_k \frac{\partial f_i}{\partial u_k}\frac{\partial g_k}{\partial x_j}$$

This is the master formula from which every special case follows.

## §10. Special Cases Worth Knowing

### 10.1 — Gradient of a Composition

$h(\mathbf{x}) = f(\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}))$, $f: \mathbb{R}^m \to \mathbb{R}$, $\mathbf{g}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$:

$$\nabla h(\mathbf{x}) = J_\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x})^\top \nabla f(\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{x}))$$

The gradient transforms by the **transpose Jacobian**. This is the adjoint, and it is exactly why backpropagation uses transposed weight matrices.

### 10.2 — Directional Derivative via Chain Rule

The directional derivative of $f$ at $\mathbf{a}$ in direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ is obtained by setting $\mathbf{x}(t) = \mathbf{a} + t\hat{\mathbf{u}}$ and differentiating at $t = 0$:

$$D_{\hat{u}} f = \frac{d}{dt}\bigg|_{t=0} f(\mathbf{a} + t\hat{\mathbf{u}}) = \nabla f(\mathbf{a}) \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}$$

The chain rule converts a directional derivative into a dot product with the gradient.

### 10.3 — Tangent Vector to a Curve on a Surface

If $\mathbf{r}(t)$ is a curve on a level surface $f(\mathbf{x}) = c$, then $f(\mathbf{r}(t)) = c$ for all $t$. Differentiate:

$$\nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \cdot \mathbf{r}'(t) = 0$$

The gradient is perpendicular to every tangent vector on the surface — a purely chain-rule proof.

### 10.4 — Change of Variables in PDEs

Suppose $u = u(x,t)$ satisfies a PDE, and we change variables: $\xi = x - ct$, $\tau = t$. Then:

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial \xi}\frac{\partial \xi}{\partial x} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau}\frac{\partial \tau}{\partial x} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial \xi} \cdot 1 + \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau} \cdot 0 = \frac{\partial u}{\partial \xi}$$

$$\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial \xi}\frac{\partial \xi}{\partial t} + \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau}\frac{\partial \tau}{\partial t} = \frac{\partial u}{\partial \xi}(-c) + \frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau}$$

So $\frac{\partial u}{\partial t} + c\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} = 0$ becomes $\frac{\partial u}{\partial \tau} = 0$: the transport equation transforms to a trivial PDE. The chain rule is the engine of every change-of-variables in PDEs.

## §11. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Polar Laplacian

Express $\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial x^2} + \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial y^2}$ in polar coordinates $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$.

**Step 1.** Compute $\frac{\partial f}{\partial r}$ and $\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}$ by chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} = f_x \cos\theta + f_y \sin\theta$$ $$\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta} = -f_x r\sin\theta + f_y r\cos\theta$$

**Step 2.** Invert (solve for $f_x$, $f_y$):

$$f_x = \cos\theta,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} - \frac{\sin\theta}{r}\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}$$ $$f_y = \sin\theta,\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} + \frac{\cos\theta}{r}\frac{\partial f}{\partial \theta}$$

**Step 3.** Differentiate again (apply the operators $\frac{\partial}{\partial x}$ and $\frac{\partial}{\partial y}$ using the chain rule on the result). After careful computation:

$$\nabla^2 f = f_{xx} + f_{yy} = \frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial r^2} + \frac{1}{r}\frac{\partial f}{\partial r} + \frac{1}{r^2}\frac{\partial^2 f}{\partial\theta^2}$$

The Laplacian in polar coordinates, derived entirely by chain rule.

### Example 2 — Sensitivity Analysis

Revenue $R = p \cdot q(p, a)$ where $p$ is price and $a$ is advertising spend. Find $\frac{dR}{dp}$ when $p$ and $a$ are both functions of a budget constraint parameter $\lambda$.

$$\frac{dR}{d\lambda} = \frac{\partial R}{\partial p}\frac{dp}{d\lambda} + \frac{\partial R}{\partial a}\frac{da}{d\lambda}$$

$$\frac{\partial R}{\partial p} = q + p\frac{\partial q}{\partial p}, \quad \frac{\partial R}{\partial a} = p\frac{\partial q}{\partial a}$$

The chain rule decomposes the total effect into direct and indirect channels — a standard tool in economics.

### Example 3 — Second Derivative via Chain Rule

Let $z = f(u)$, $u = x^2 + y^2$. Compute $\frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x^2}$.

**First derivative:**

$$\frac{\partial z}{\partial x} = f'(u) \cdot 2x$$

**Second derivative** (product rule + chain rule):

$$\frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x^2} = \frac{\partial}{\partial x}[f'(u) \cdot 2x] = f''(u)\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} \cdot 2x + f'(u) \cdot 2$$ $$= f''(u)(2x)^2 + 2f'(u) = 4x^2 f''(u) + 2f'(u)$$

Similarly $\frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial y^2} = 4y^2 f''(u) + 2f'(u)$. So:

$$\nabla^2 z = 4(x^2 + y^2)f''(u) + 4f'(u) = 4u,f''(u) + 4f'(u)$$

## §12. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

The chain rule is not merely _related_ to machine learning. It **is** machine learning. The entire discipline of differentiable programming rests on one fact: you can differentiate a composition by multiplying Jacobians.

### 12.1 — Backpropagation Is the Chain Rule

A neural network is a composition:

$$\mathcal{L} = \ell \circ f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots \circ f_1$$

where $f_k(\mathbf{x}) = \sigma_k(W_k \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}_k)$ is the $k$-th layer. Define intermediate activations:

$$\mathbf{a}_0 = \mathbf{x}, \quad \mathbf{a}_k = f_k(\mathbf{a}_{k-1}), \quad \mathcal{L} = \ell(\mathbf{a}_L)$$

By the Jacobian chain rule:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_k} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_{k+1}} \cdot J_{f_{k+1}}(\mathbf{a}_k)$$

Backpropagation is this recursion, run from $k = L$ down to $k = 0$. The "error signal" $\boldsymbol{\delta}_k = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_k}$ propagates backward through transposed Jacobians.

**For a single layer** $\mathbf{z}_k = W_k \mathbf{a}_{k-1} + \mathbf{b}_k$, $\mathbf{a}_k = \sigma(\mathbf{z}_k)$:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial W_k} = \boldsymbol{\delta}_k \cdot \mathbf{a}_{k-1}^\top, \quad \boldsymbol{\delta}_{k-1} = W_k^\top \boldsymbol{\delta}_k \odot \sigma'(\mathbf{z}_{k-1})$$

The transpose $W_k^\top$ is the adjoint of the Jacobian $J = W_k$ (since the layer is linear in $\mathbf{a}_{k-1}$). This is §10.1 above, instantiated for neural networks.

### 12.2 — Forward Mode vs Reverse Mode

The chain rule can be evaluated in two directions:

**Forward mode** (tangent computation). Start with $d\mathbf{a}_0 = d\mathbf{x}$ and propagate forward:

$$d\mathbf{a}_k = J_{f_k}(\mathbf{a}_{k-1}) \cdot d\mathbf{a}_{k-1}$$

Cost per forward pass: $O(n)$ Jacobian-vector products (one per input). Efficient when $n \ll m$ (few inputs).

**Reverse mode** (cotangent computation, backprop). Start with $\bar{\mathcal{L}} = 1$ and propagate backward:

$$\bar{\mathbf{a}}_{k-1} = J_{f_k}(\mathbf{a}_{k-1})^\top \cdot \bar{\mathbf{a}}_k$$

Cost: $O(m)$ vector-Jacobian products (one per output). Efficient when $m \ll n$. For neural networks: $m = 1$ (scalar loss), $n = $ billions of parameters. Reverse mode wins decisively.

This is why backpropagation is efficient: it is reverse-mode automatic differentiation, which is the chain rule evaluated right-to-left on the computation graph.

### 12.3 — Computation Graphs and the Chain Rule

Every computation in a neural network can be represented as a **directed acyclic graph** (DAG) where:

- Nodes are intermediate values
- Edges encode dependencies
- Each edge carries a local Jacobian

The chain rule says: the gradient at any node is the sum over all paths from that node to the output, of the product of edge Jacobians along each path.

```
Input x → [Layer 1] → a₁ → [Layer 2] → a₂ → ... → [Loss] → ℒ
              ↑                  ↑
           ∂a₁/∂x           ∂a₂/∂a₁
```

Backprop traverses this graph in reverse topological order, accumulating products. This is exactly the tree diagram rule from §6, instantiated for computation graphs.

### 12.4 — Vanishing and Exploding Gradients

For a deep network with $L$ layers and activation $\sigma$, the gradient at layer $k$ involves the product:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_k} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_L} \cdot \prod_{j=k}^{L-1} J_{f_{j+1}}$$

Each factor $J_{f_j} = W_j^\top \text{diag}(\sigma'(\mathbf{z}_j))$ has spectral radius (largest singular value) roughly $|W_j| \cdot \max|\sigma'|$.

- If $|J_j| < 1$ for most $j$: the product $\to 0$ exponentially fast. **Vanishing gradients.** Early layers learn nothing.
- If $|J_j| > 1$ for most $j$: the product $\to \infty$ exponentially fast. **Exploding gradients.** Training diverges.

Both pathologies are pure consequences of the chain rule applied to a long product of matrices. Solutions: careful initialization (Xavier/He), batch normalization (controls $|J_j|$), residual connections (add identity to the Jacobian), gradient clipping.

**ReLU** activations have $\sigma'(x) \in {0, 1}$, which helps with vanishing gradients compared to sigmoid ($\sigma' \leq 0.25$). This is a chain rule argument.

### 12.5 — Recurrent Neural Networks and BPTT

In an RNN, the hidden state evolves as $\mathbf{h}_t = f(\mathbf{h}_{t-1}, \mathbf{x}_t; W)$. The gradient of loss at time $T$ with respect to parameters at time $k$ involves:

$$\frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_T}{\partial W}\bigg|_{\text{step } k} = \frac{\partial \mathcal{L}_T}{\partial \mathbf{h}_T}\left(\prod_{j=k}^{T-1} \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{j+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_j}\right)\frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_k}{\partial W}$$

The product of $T - k$ Jacobians $\frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{j+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_j}$ is the chain rule unrolled through time — called **Backpropagation Through Time (BPTT)**. The vanishing/exploding gradient problem is especially severe here because $T$ can be hundreds of steps. LSTMs and GRUs introduce gating mechanisms that approximately maintain $\left|\prod \frac{\partial \mathbf{h}_{j+1}}{\partial \mathbf{h}_j}\right| \approx 1$.

### 12.6 — Residual Networks (ResNets) — The Chain Rule Fix

A residual block computes $\mathbf{a}_{k+1} = \mathbf{a}_k + F(\mathbf{a}_k)$. Its Jacobian:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{a}_{k+1}}{\partial \mathbf{a}_k} = I + J_F(\mathbf{a}_k)$$

The identity $I$ ensures that the product of Jacobians through $L$ residual blocks always contains the identity term:

$$\prod_{k=1}^L \left(I + J_{F_k}\right)$$

This expands (like a polynomial) into a sum of $2^L$ terms, most of which contain fewer than $L$ Jacobian factors. Some paths are short — gradients can flow through them without vanishing. This is the **chain rule explanation** of why ResNets can be trained to depth 1000+.

## §14. Theorems to Know Cold

**Theorem 1** _(Chain Rule)._ If $\mathbf{g}$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$ and $f$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{a})$, then $f \circ \mathbf{g}$ is differentiable at $\mathbf{a}$ and $d(f \circ \mathbf{g})_\mathbf{a} = df_{\mathbf{g}(\mathbf{a})} \circ d\mathbf{g}_\mathbf{a}$.

**Theorem 2** _(Implicit Function Theorem)._ If $F(\mathbf{x}, y)$ is $C^1$ near $(\mathbf{a}, b)$ with $F(\mathbf{a}, b) = 0$ and $F_y(\mathbf{a}, b) \neq 0$, then there exists a $C^1$ function $y = g(\mathbf{x})$ near $\mathbf{a}$ with $F(\mathbf{x}, g(\mathbf{x})) = 0$, and $\frac{\partial g}{\partial x_i} = -\frac{F_{x_i}}{F_y}$.

**Theorem 3** _(Gradient Orthogonality)._ If $\mathbf{r}(t)$ lies on the level set $f = c$, then $\nabla f(\mathbf{r}(t)) \perp \mathbf{r}'(t)$ for all $t$.

**Theorem 4** _(Jacobian of Inverse)._ If $\mathbf{f}: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is invertible and differentiable, then $J_{\mathbf{f}^{-1}}(\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})) = [J_\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{x})]^{-1}$.

## §15. Things That Will Trip You Up

> **$\partial z/\partial x$ means different things depending on context.** If $z = f(x,y)$ and $y$ depends on $x$, you must decide: are you holding $y$ fixed (partial), or letting it vary (total derivative)? Sloppy notation kills you here.

> **The chain rule requires differentiability**, not just existence of partial derivatives. If $\mathbf{g}$ is merely continuous, the composition may not be differentiable.

> **Implicit differentiation is not magic.** It is the chain rule + implicit function theorem. Always verify $F_y \neq 0$ before writing $dy/dx = -F_x/F_y$.

> **Second derivatives require the chain rule twice**, and produce extra terms. $\frac{\partial^2 z}{\partial x^2} \neq \left(f''(u)\right)(u_x)^2$ alone — you must also include $f'(u) u_{xx}$.

> **In backprop**, the gradient of the loss with respect to a parameter $W_k$ is not $\boldsymbol{\delta}_k$ alone — it requires the outer product $\boldsymbol{\delta}_k \mathbf{a}_{k-1}^\top$. Confusing the upstream gradient with the parameter gradient is a common error in manual backprop derivations.
