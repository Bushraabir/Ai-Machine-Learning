## 0. What Is a PDE?

An **ordinary differential equation (ODE)** involves a function of _one_ variable and its derivatives:

$$\frac{d^2 y}{dx^2} + \omega^2 y = 0$$

A **partial differential equation (PDE)** involves a function of _several_ variables and its partial derivatives:

$$\frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 u}{\partial x^2}$$

Here $u = u(x,t)$ depends on both space $x$ and time $t$. The equation relates $u$ to its own partial derivatives — it is a constraint that $u$ must satisfy everywhere on its domain.

**The fundamental shift in difficulty:** In an ODE, specifying $y(0)$ and $y'(0)$ (two numbers) determines the solution. In a PDE, we must specify $u$ on an entire boundary — a function — and the solution fills in the rest of the domain. The space of solutions is infinite-dimensional.

---

## 1. Classification and Taxonomy

### 1.1 Order

The **order** of a PDE is the order of the highest partial derivative appearing.

- $u_t = ku_{xx}$: second order (in $x$), first order (in $t$) — conventionally called second order overall.
- $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$: second order.
- $u_{xxx} + u_{yy} = 0$: third order.

### 1.2 Linearity

A PDE in $u$ is **linear** if it is linear in $u$ and all its derivatives — no products like $u \cdot u_x$, no nonlinear terms like $u^2$.

$$\underbrace{a(x,t)u_{xx} + b(x,t)u_{xt} + c(x,t)u_{tt}}_{\text{linear in } u \text{ and its derivatives}} + d(x,t)u_x + e(x,t)u_t + f(x,t)u = g(x,t)$$

- **Homogeneous:** $g = 0$.
- **Inhomogeneous:** $g \neq 0$ (source/forcing term).

### 1.3 The Classification of Second-Order Linear PDEs

For the general second-order linear PDE in two variables:

$$Au_{xx} + Bu_{xy} + Cu_{yy} + Du_x + Eu_y + Fu = G$$

Define the **discriminant** $\Delta = B^2 - 4AC$ (analogous to a conic section):

|$\Delta < 0$|$\Delta = 0$|$\Delta > 0$|
|---|---|---|
|**Elliptic**|**Parabolic**|**Hyperbolic**|
|Laplace, Poisson|Heat/Diffusion|Wave equation|
|Steady-state|Irreversible diffusion|Wave propagation|
|Boundary value problem|Initial + boundary|Initial + boundary|

**Why this matters:** The type determines the mathematical character of solutions and the correct type of conditions to impose:

- Elliptic: need boundary conditions all around the domain.
- Parabolic: need initial condition + boundary conditions.
- Hyperbolic: need initial conditions ($u$ and $u_t$) + boundary conditions.

```
CLASSIFICATION TRIANGLE

          ELLIPTIC                PARABOLIC               HYPERBOLIC
        (Δ = B²-4AC < 0)       (Δ = B²-4AC = 0)       (Δ = B²-4AC > 0)

       ∇²u = 0                  u_t = k u_xx             u_tt = c² u_xx
        │                          │                          │
        ▼                          ▼                          ▼
   Smooth solutions           Smooth for t > 0          Finite speed
   Max principle              Diffuses singularities    of propagation
   No time evolution          Irreversible              Reversible
```

## 2. The Three Fundamental PDEs

These are the canonical equations of mathematical physics. Every PDE course orbits around them. Every ML application connects back to them.

---

### 2.1 The Heat Equation (Parabolic)

$$\boxed{u_t = k,\nabla^2 u = k(u_{xx} + u_{yy} + \cdots)}$$

**Physical meaning:** $u(\mathbf{x}, t)$ = temperature at position $\mathbf{x}$, time $t$. $k > 0$ = thermal diffusivity.

**Derivation sketch.** Consider a small region $\Omega$. The rate of change of heat energy is:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\int_\Omega u,dV = k \oint_{\partial\Omega} \nabla u \cdot \hat{\mathbf{n}},dS = k\int_\Omega \nabla^2 u,dV$$

(The last step uses the **Divergence Theorem**.) Since $\Omega$ is arbitrary: $u_t = k\nabla^2 u$.

**Key properties:**

- Solutions are immediately $C^\infty$ for $t > 0$, even if initial data is discontinuous. The heat equation has an **infinite speed of propagation** — a local disturbance is felt everywhere instantly (unphysical but useful).
- **Maximum principle:** The maximum of $u$ over a closed domain occurs on the boundary or at $t=0$ — never in the interior. Temperature cannot exceed its boundary values.
- **Irreversibility:** Running time backward ($t \to -t$) gives $u_t = -k\nabla^2 u$ — a backward heat equation that amplifies high-frequency noise catastrophically. Heat diffusion is fundamentally irreversible.

**In 1D on $[0, L]$ with $u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0$, $u(x,0) = f(x)$:**

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}$$

where $B_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx$ — the **Fourier sine coefficients** of $f$.

**Observation:** Each mode $\sin(n\pi x/L)$ decays exponentially in time with rate $k(n\pi/L)^2$. **High-frequency modes decay faster** — the heat equation smooths out detail. This is the mathematics of why hot objects cool and why signals lose high-frequency information over time.

---

### 2.2 The Wave Equation (Hyperbolic)

$$\boxed{u_{tt} = c^2,\nabla^2 u}$$

**Physical meaning:** $u(\mathbf{x},t)$ = displacement of a vibrating medium (string, membrane, sound pressure). $c > 0$ = wave speed.

**Derivation sketch (1D string).** A small segment of a string under tension $T$, with mass density $\rho$, obeys Newton's second law. For small displacements:

$$\rho,\Delta x, u_{tt} = T, u_x(x+\Delta x) - T, u_x(x) \approx T, u_{xx}, \Delta x$$

Dividing: $u_{tt} = \frac{T}{\rho} u_{xx} = c^2 u_{xx}$ with $c = \sqrt{T/\rho}$.

**D'Alembert's Solution (1D, infinite domain):**

Given $u(x,0) = f(x)$ (initial displacement) and $u_t(x,0) = g(x)$ (initial velocity):

$$\boxed{u(x,t) = \frac{f(x+ct) + f(x-ct)}{2} + \frac{1}{2c}\int_{x-ct}^{x+ct} g(s),ds}$$

**Interpretation:** The solution is a superposition of two waves — one traveling right at speed $c$ and one traveling left at speed $c$.

```
t=0:        f(x)
            /\
           /  \
          /    \
---------/------\---------  x

t=T:    left wave    right wave
        \  f(x+cT)   f(x-cT) /
         \          /
---------  --------  --------  x

Both halves travel away from the source at speed c.
```

**Key properties:**

- **Finite speed of propagation:** A disturbance at $x=0$ reaches position $x$ only after time $t = |x|/c$. This is causality.
- **Reversibility:** The wave equation is time-reversible ($t \to -t$ gives the same equation).
- **Conservation:** The total energy (kinetic + potential) is conserved.

**In 1D on $[0,L]$ with fixed endpoints $u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0$:**

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)!\left[A_n \cos!\left(\frac{n\pi c}{L}t\right) + B_n \sin!\left(\frac{n\pi c}{L}t\right)\right]$$

The frequencies $\omega_n = \frac{n\pi c}{L}$ are the **natural frequencies** of the string — overtones of the fundamental $\omega_1 = \pi c/L$. This is the mathematics of musical instruments.

### 2.3 Laplace's Equation and Poisson's Equation (Elliptic)

$$\boxed{\nabla^2 u = 0} \quad \text{(Laplace)} \qquad \boxed{\nabla^2 u = f} \quad \text{(Poisson)}$$

**Physical meaning:** Steady-state temperature distribution (no time evolution), electrostatic potential (where there are no charges — Laplace; where there are charges — Poisson), fluid velocity potential.

Solutions of Laplace's equation are called **harmonic functions**.

**Key properties of harmonic functions:**

- **Mean value property:** $u(P)$ = average of $u$ over any sphere centered at $P$.

$$u(\mathbf{a}) = \frac{1}{|\partial B_r(\mathbf{a})|}\oint_{\partial B_r(\mathbf{a})} u,dS$$

- **Maximum principle:** $u$ attains its maximum and minimum on the _boundary_ of the domain — never in the interior (unless $u$ is constant).
- **Uniqueness:** The Dirichlet problem (specify $u$ on the boundary, find $u$ inside) has at most one solution.
- **Smoothness:** Harmonic functions are $C^\infty$ — in fact, real analytic ($C^\omega$).

**Example — Laplace in polar coordinates:**

For radial problems $u = u(r)$:

$$\nabla^2 u = u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r}u_r = \frac{1}{r}\frac{d}{dr}!\left(r\frac{du}{dr}\right) = 0$$

Solution: $u(r) = A\ln r + B$ (in 2D). This is the **fundamental solution** — the potential of a point charge/source.

## 3. Boundary and Initial Conditions

A PDE alone does not determine a unique solution. We need additional conditions.

### Types of Boundary Conditions

|Type|Form|Physical meaning|
|---|---|---|
|**Dirichlet**|$u = g$ on $\partial\Omega$|Prescribed value (temperature fixed at boundary)|
|**Neumann**|$\nabla u \cdot \hat{n} = h$ on $\partial\Omega$|Prescribed flux (insulated boundary: $h=0$)|
|**Robin**|$\alpha u + \beta \nabla u \cdot \hat{n} = g$|Convective boundary (mixed)|

### Well-Posedness (Hadamard)

A PDE problem is **well-posed** if:

1. A solution **exists**.
2. The solution is **unique**.
3. The solution **depends continuously** on the data (stability).

Ill-posed problems arise frequently in inverse problems and have deep connections to machine learning regularization.

## 4. Method of Separation of Variables

This is the primary analytical method for linear homogeneous PDEs on simple domains. The idea: assume the solution factors as a product of functions, each depending on only one variable.

### 4.1 Heat Equation — Full Solution

**Problem:** $u_t = k,u_{xx}$, $x \in [0,L]$, $t > 0$

**BCs:** $u(0,t) = 0$, $u(L,t) = 0$

**IC:** $u(x,0) = f(x)$

**Step 1 — Assume:** $u(x,t) = X(x),T(t)$

Substituting:

$$X(x),T'(t) = k,X''(x),T(t)$$

$$\frac{T'(t)}{kT(t)} = \frac{X''(x)}{X(x)} = -\lambda$$

Since the left side depends only on $t$ and the right side only on $x$, both must equal the same constant $-\lambda$.

**Step 2 — Solve the spatial ODE:**

$$X'' + \lambda X = 0, \quad X(0) = X(L) = 0$$

This is an **eigenvalue problem** (Sturm-Liouville). Non-trivial solutions exist only for:

$$\lambda_n = \left(\frac{n\pi}{L}\right)^2, \quad X_n(x) = \sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right), \quad n = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

The $\lambda_n$ are the **eigenvalues**; the $X_n$ are the **eigenfunctions**.

**Step 3 — Solve the temporal ODE:**

$$T_n'(t) = -k\lambda_n T_n(t) \implies T_n(t) = e^{-k\lambda_n t} = e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}$$

**Step 4 — Superposition:** (Linearity allows summing all solutions)

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n,X_n(x),T_n(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}$$

**Step 5 — Apply initial condition:**

$$u(x,0) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} B_n \sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) = f(x)$$

By **Fourier series orthogonality** ($\int_0^L \sin\frac{m\pi x}{L}\sin\frac{n\pi x}{L},dx = \frac{L}{2}\delta_{mn}$):

$$B_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx$$

**This is why Fourier series exist:** they are the natural language of the heat equation. Separation of variables _creates_ Fourier series.

### 4.2 Wave Equation — Vibrating String

**Problem:** $u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx}$, $x \in [0,L]$, with $u(0,t) = u(L,t) = 0$

**ICs:** $u(x,0) = f(x)$, $u_t(x,0) = g(x)$

Same spatial eigenfunctions $X_n = \sin(n\pi x/L)$, but now the temporal ODE is:

$$T_n'' + c^2\lambda_n T_n = 0 \implies T_n(t) = A_n\cos(\omega_n t) + B_n\sin(\omega_n t)$$

where $\omega_n = cn\pi/L$ is the $n$-th angular frequency.

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)!\left[A_n\cos(\omega_n t) + B_n\sin(\omega_n t)\right]$$

Apply ICs:

$$A_n = \frac{2}{L}\int_0^L f(x)\sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx, \quad B_n = \frac{2}{L\omega_n}\int_0^L g(x)\sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)dx$$

### 4.3 Laplace's Equation on a Rectangle

**Problem:** $u_{xx} + u_{yy} = 0$ on $[0,a]\times[0,b]$, with three zero BCs and $u(x,b) = f(x)$.

Assume $u = X(x)Y(y)$, leading to:

$$\frac{X''}{X} = -\frac{Y''}{Y} = -\lambda$$

Spatial: $X_n = \sin(n\pi x/a)$ (to satisfy $X(0) = X(a) = 0$).

Vertical: $Y'' - \lambda_n Y = 0 \implies Y_n(y) = \sinh!\left(\frac{n\pi y}{a}\right)$ (choosing the combination that satisfies $Y(0) = 0$).

$$u(x,y) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} C_n \sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{a}\right)\sinh!\left(\frac{n\pi y}{a}\right)$$

The $\sinh$ (rather than exponential) appears because the boundary conditions couple both ends. This reflects the **elliptic character** of Laplace's equation.

## 5. The Fundamental Solution and Green's Functions

### 5.1 Heat Kernel

The **fundamental solution** of the heat equation on $\mathbb{R}^n$ is:

$$\Phi(\mathbf{x}, t) = \frac{1}{(4\pi kt)^{n/2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{|\mathbf{x}|^2}{4kt}\right), \quad t > 0$$

This is a **Gaussian** centered at the origin that spreads out over time. The solution to the heat equation with initial condition $u(\mathbf{x},0) = f(\mathbf{x})$ is:

$$u(\mathbf{x},t) = \int_{\mathbb{R}^n} \Phi(\mathbf{x}-\mathbf{y},t),f(\mathbf{y}),d\mathbf{y} = (\Phi(\cdot,t) * f)(\mathbf{x})$$

**Interpretation:** The solution is a **convolution** of the initial data with the Gaussian kernel. The heat equation performs Gaussian smoothing — this is exactly the operation used in image processing. With variance $\sigma^2 = 2kt$ at time $t$, larger $t$ means more smoothing.

```
f(x) initial data:          Φ(x,t₁):              u(x,t₁) = Φ * f:

  /\/\  sharp features      _____                    /‾‾\
 /    \                    /     \                  /    \  smoothed
/      \                  /       \                /      \
```

### 5.2 Fundamental Solution of Laplace's Equation

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the fundamental solution is the potential of a point source at the origin:

$$\Phi(\mathbf{x}) = \begin{cases} -\dfrac{1}{2\pi}\ln|\mathbf{x}| & n = 2 \ \dfrac{1}{(n-2)\omega_n |\mathbf{x}|^{n-2}} & n \geq 3 \end{cases}$$

where $\omega_n$ = surface area of the unit sphere in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

The Poisson equation $-\nabla^2 u = f$ has solution $u = \Phi * f$.

This is the **Green's function** of $-\nabla^2$ on $\mathbb{R}^n$. It is the inverse of the Laplacian — in the same way that $\frac{1}{k}$ is the inverse of multiplication by $k$.

## 6. Superposition and Linearity

The **superposition principle** is the central tool for linear PDEs.

**Theorem:** If $u_1$ and $u_2$ both satisfy a _linear homogeneous_ PDE $\mathcal{L}[u] = 0$, then $c_1 u_1 + c_2 u_2$ also satisfies it, for any constants $c_1, c_2$.

This is what makes Fourier series work: we build the solution as an infinite superposition of eigenfunctions, each of which individually satisfies the PDE and the boundary conditions. The initial condition then determines the coefficients.

**For inhomogeneous problems $\mathcal{L}[u] = f$:** Decompose into particular + homogeneous:

$$u = u_p + u_h, \quad \mathcal{L}[u_p] = f, \quad \mathcal{L}[u_h] = 0$$

This mirrors the ODE strategy exactly.

## 7. Worked Examples

### Example 7.1 — Verifying a Solution

Verify that $u(x,t) = e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) + 3e^{-\pi^2 t}\sin(2\pi x)$ satisfies $u_t = u_{xx}$ with appropriate BCs.

**Computing $u_t$:**

$$u_t = -4\pi^2 e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) + 3(-\pi^2)e^{-\pi^2 t}\sin(2\pi x)$$

Wait — recalculate. Let $k=1$, $L = 1$. The $n=1$ mode has $\lambda_1 = \pi^2$; the $n=2$ mode has $\lambda_2 = 4\pi^2$.

$$u_t = -4\pi^2 e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) - 3\pi^2 e^{-\pi^2 t}\sin(2\pi x)$$

**Computing $u_{xx}$:**

$$u_{xx} = -\pi^2 e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) \cdot (-1)$$

Actually let us be precise. $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\sin(\pi x) = -\pi^2 \sin(\pi x)$ and $\frac{\partial^2}{\partial x^2}\sin(2\pi x) = -4\pi^2\sin(2\pi x)$.

$$u_{xx} = -\pi^2 e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) - 3\cdot 4\pi^2 e^{-\pi^2 t}\sin(2\pi x)$$

Hmm — $u_t \neq u_{xx}$ with the given formula. Let me use the correct form. With $B_1 = 1$, $B_2 = 3$ and $\lambda_n = (n\pi)^2$:

$$u = e^{-\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) + 3e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(2\pi x)$$

$$u_t = -\pi^2 e^{-\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) - 12\pi^2 e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(2\pi x)$$

$$u_{xx} = -\pi^2 e^{-\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) - 3\cdot 4\pi^2 e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(2\pi x) = -\pi^2 e^{-\pi^2 t}\sin(\pi x) - 12\pi^2 e^{-4\pi^2 t}\sin(2\pi x)$$

$u_t = u_{xx}$ ✓. The boundary conditions $u(0,t) = u(1,t) = 0$ hold since $\sin(0) = \sin(\pi) = 0$ ✓.

### Example 7.2 — Applying D'Alembert's Formula

**Wave equation:** $u_{tt} = 4u_{xx}$ (so $c = 2$) on $\mathbb{R}$

**ICs:** $u(x,0) = \sin x$, $u_t(x,0) = 0$

**D'Alembert:**

$$u(x,t) = \frac{\sin(x+2t) + \sin(x-2t)}{2} + \frac{1}{4}\int_{x-2t}^{x+2t}0,ds$$

$$= \frac{\sin(x+2t) + \sin(x-2t)}{2} = \sin x\cos(2t)$$

(using the sum-to-product identity). This is a **standing wave**: the spatial pattern $\sin x$ oscillates in time with frequency $\omega = 2$. The crests and troughs don't travel — they oscillate in place.

### Example 7.3 — Laplace on a Disk (Polar Coordinates)

**Problem:** $\nabla^2 u = 0$ inside the unit disk $r < 1$, with $u(1,\theta) = \cos\theta$.

In polar coordinates: $\nabla^2 u = u_{rr} + \frac{1}{r}u_r + \frac{1}{r^2}u_{\theta\theta} = 0$.

Separate: $u = R(r)\Theta(\theta)$. The angular equation gives $\Theta = \cos(n\theta)$ or $\sin(n\theta)$, and the radial equation gives $R = r^n$ (regular at $r=0$).

For $n=1$ (matching our BC $\cos\theta$): $u(r,\theta) = r\cos\theta = x$.

**Verification:** $\nabla^2(x) = 0$ ✓. At $r=1$: $u = \cos\theta$ ✓. Beautiful.

The **Poisson integral formula** gives the general solution:

$$u(r,\theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{(1-r^2)}{1 - 2r\cos(\theta - \phi) + r^2},f(\phi),d\phi$$

This is the Green's function solution for the disk — the **Poisson kernel** in the numerator is a sum of all the radial eigenmodes, exhibiting the same structure as the 1D Fourier solution but in polar coordinates.

## 8. Nonlinear PDEs — A Glimpse

Most PDEs arising in nature are nonlinear. Superposition fails. Solutions can develop **shocks** (discontinuities in finite time) even from smooth initial data.

### 8.1 Burgers' Equation

$$u_t + u,u_x = \nu, u_{xx}$$

The $u,u_x$ term is nonlinear (advection speed depends on $u$ itself). With $\nu = 0$ (inviscid Burgers): waves steepen and break into shocks. With $\nu > 0$: diffusion competes with steepening.

Burgers' equation appears in turbulence, traffic flow, and as a test case for numerical PDE solvers.

### 8.2 Navier-Stokes Equations

$$\rho!\left(\frac{\partial \mathbf{v}}{\partial t} + (\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}\right) = -\nabla p + \mu\nabla^2\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{f}$$

The $(\mathbf{v}\cdot\nabla)\mathbf{v}$ is the nonlinear inertia term (convective acceleration). Whether smooth solutions exist for all time in 3D is one of the **Millennium Prize Problems** (Clay Mathematics Institute, $1,000,000).

## 9. Connection to AI, Machine Learning, and Deep Learning

PDEs and deep learning are colliding in one of the most active frontiers of applied mathematics.

### 9.1 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)

**The problem:** Solve a PDE $\mathcal{L}[u] = f$ in domain $\Omega$ with boundary condition $u|_{\partial\Omega} = g$.

**The idea (Raissi, Perdikaris, Karniadakis, 2019):** Approximate $u(\mathbf{x})$ by a neural network $u_\theta(\mathbf{x})$. Define the loss:

$$\mathcal{L}(\theta) = \underbrace{\frac{1}{N_r}\sum_{i=1}^{N_r}\bigl|\mathcal{L}[u_\theta](https://claude.ai/chat/%5Cmathbf%7Bx%7D_i%5Er) - f(\mathbf{x}_i^r)\bigr|^2}_{\text{residual loss}} + \underbrace{\frac{1}{N_b}\sum_{j=1}^{N_b}\bigl|u_\theta(\mathbf{x}_j^b) - g(\mathbf{x}_j^b)\bigr|^2}_{\text{boundary loss}}$$

where $\mathbf{x}_i^r$ are **collocation points** in the interior and $\mathbf{x}_j^b$ are points on the boundary.

Minimizing this loss forces $u_\theta$ to approximately satisfy the PDE **and** the boundary conditions. No mesh required — this is a meshless method.

The terms $\frac{\partial u_\theta}{\partial x}$, $\frac{\partial^2 u_\theta}{\partial x^2}$ are computed via **automatic differentiation** through the network graph. Computing second derivatives requires differentiating through the backward pass — this is why PINNs need $C^2$ activation functions (Tanh, GELU, Swish; ReLU's second derivative is zero almost everywhere).

```
         Collocation points · in interior         Boundary points × on ∂Ω
         
         ×  ×  ×  ×  ×  ×
         ×  ·  ·  ·  ·  ×
         ×  ·  ·  ·  ·  ×      u_θ must satisfy:
         ×  ·  ·  ·  ·  ×      · Lu_θ = f   at interior points
         ×  ·  ·  ·  ·  ×      × u_θ = g   at boundary points
         ×  ×  ×  ×  ×  ×
```

**Strengths:** Handles complex geometries, inverse problems (identify PDE coefficients from data), incorporates noisy observations.

**Weaknesses:** Training is slow; fails on problems with sharp gradients or shocks; hard to enforce causality in time-dependent PDEs.

### 9.2 Neural Operators — Learning Solution Maps

**Key insight:** A PDE defines a map from initial/boundary conditions to solutions. Instead of solving one PDE, can we _learn this map_?

**Fourier Neural Operator (FNO)** (Li et al., 2020): Learn the solution operator $\mathcal{G}: f \mapsto u$ where $u$ solves $\mathcal{L}[u] = f$. The network operates in Fourier space — literally using the Fourier modes that appear in separation of variables. Once trained, FNO evaluates new solutions in milliseconds vs. hours for traditional solvers.

**DeepONet** (Lu et al., 2021): A neural network that learns operators using the _universal approximation theorem for operators_, which is itself a generalization of the standard UAT.

### 9.3 Diffusion Models — The Heat Equation in Reverse

**Score-based generative models** (DDPM, score matching) are literally built on the heat equation.

**Forward process:** Starting from data $\mathbf{x}_0 \sim p_{\text{data}}$, add Gaussian noise incrementally:

$$\mathbf{x}_t = \sqrt{1 - \beta t},\mathbf{x}_0 + \sqrt{\beta t},\boldsymbol{\epsilon}, \quad \boldsymbol{\epsilon} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I)$$

In the continuous limit, this follows the **forward SDE:**

$$d\mathbf{x} = -\frac{\beta}{2}\mathbf{x},dt + \sqrt{\beta},d\mathbf{W}$$

The marginal density $p_t(\mathbf{x})$ satisfies the **Fokker-Planck equation** — a PDE of the form:

$$\frac{\partial p_t}{\partial t} = \frac{\beta}{2}\nabla\cdot(\mathbf{x}p_t) + \frac{\beta}{2}\nabla^2 p_t$$

This is a convection-diffusion equation. As $t \to \infty$, $p_t \to \mathcal{N}(0,I)$ — pure noise. The data distribution has been smoothed into a Gaussian by running the heat equation.

**Reverse process (generation):** Run the PDE backwards using the **reverse-time SDE** (Anderson, 1982):

$$d\mathbf{x} = \left[-\frac{\beta}{2}\mathbf{x} - \beta,\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p_t(\mathbf{x})\right]dt + \sqrt{\beta},d\overline{\mathbf{W}}$$

The term $\nabla_\mathbf{x}\log p_t(\mathbf{x})$ is the **score function** — the neural network learns to estimate it. Once known, we can reverse the diffusion and generate data from noise.

**DALL-E, Stable Diffusion, Sora** — all of these are learned reverse diffusions. They are the heat equation run backward, guided by a neural approximation to the score function.

```
  DATA                    →   Heat Equation   →              NOISE
  p_data(x)               →   (forward SDE)   →          N(0, I)

  GENERATED SAMPLES       ←   Neural Network  ←          NOISE
  p_data(x)               ←  (reverse SDE)    ←          N(0, I)
                               learns score ∇ log p_t
```

### 9.4 The Connection Between Optimization and PDEs

**Hamilton-Jacobi equation:** The optimal value function $V(\mathbf{x}, t)$ in continuous-time optimal control satisfies:

$$-V_t = \min_u \bigl[L(\mathbf{x}, u) + \nabla V \cdot f(\mathbf{x}, u)\bigr]$$

This is a **first-order nonlinear PDE** (Hamilton-Jacobi-Bellman). Reinforcement learning is the discrete, approximate version of solving this PDE. Deep RL methods (SAC, PPO, TD3) implicitly approximate the solution to an HJB equation.

### 9.5 Graph Neural Networks and Discrete Laplacians

The **graph Laplacian** $L = D - A$ (where $D$ is degree matrix, $A$ is adjacency matrix) is the discrete analogue of $-\nabla^2$. Graph Convolutional Networks (GCNs) perform operations that are discrete approximations of:

$$\frac{\partial \mathbf{H}}{\partial t} = -L\mathbf{H}$$

— the heat equation on graphs. Running the forward pass of a GCN is running discrete diffusion on the graph. The deeper the network, the more the node features diffuse — the discrete heat equation "over-smoothing" problem.

## 11. Key Formulas — Reference

$$\textbf{Heat: } u_t = k u_{xx} \implies u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty B_n \sin!\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right)e^{-k(n\pi/L)^2 t}$$

$$\textbf{Wave: } u_{tt} = c^2 u_{xx} \implies u(x,t) = \frac{f(x+ct)+f(x-ct)}{2} + \frac{1}{2c}\int_{x-ct}^{x+ct}g(s),ds$$

$$\textbf{Fundamental solution (heat): } \Phi(x,t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi kt}}e^{-x^2/4kt}$$

$$\textbf{Laplace (polar, disk): } u(r,\theta) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty r^n(A_n\cos n\theta + B_n\sin n\theta)$$

$$\textbf{Poisson kernel: } u(r,\theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\frac{1-r^2}{1-2r\cos(\theta-\phi)+r^2}f(\phi),d\phi$$

$$\textbf{Classification: } \Delta = B^2 - 4AC \begin{cases} < 0 & \text{elliptic} \ = 0 & \text{parabolic} \ > 0 & \text{hyperbolic} \end{cases}$$
