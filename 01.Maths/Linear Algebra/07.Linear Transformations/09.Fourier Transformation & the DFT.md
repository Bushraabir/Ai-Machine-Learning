
## Why this note exists

Every time I think I understand Fourier analysis, I realize I was thinking about it too narrowly. The first time it was a formula for coefficients. The second time it was an inner product. The third time it was a change of basis. The fourth — and I think this is the right one — it is a statement about _which basis diagonalizes the convolution operator_.

These are not four different facts. They are the same fact, seen from different distances. This note is my attempt to hold all four views at once.

---

## The Continuous Fourier Transform

Let $f \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$. The Fourier transform is the map $\mathcal{F}: L^2(\mathbb{R}) \to L^2(\mathbb{R})$ defined by

$$\hat{f}(\xi) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(t), e^{-2\pi i \xi t}, dt$$

The inversion formula recovers $f$ from $\hat{f}$:

$$f(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \hat{f}(\xi), e^{2\pi i \xi t}, d\xi$$

These two formulas are nearly symmetric — the only asymmetry is the sign in the exponent. There are different conventions (some put the $2\pi$ in the exponent, some put $\frac{1}{2\pi}$ in front of the inverse). The convention above makes Parseval's theorem clean:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |f(t)|^2, dt = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\hat{f}(\xi)|^2, d\xi$$

In other words, $\mathcal{F}$ is a **unitary operator** on $L^2(\mathbb{R})$: it preserves the norm. In finite dimensions, unitary = orthogonal (over $\mathbb{C}$). That is the 18.06 analog.

## The Fourier Series (Periodic Case)

Before the DFT, it is worth being clear about what happens for periodic functions, since the DFT is really a discretization of this.

Let $f: [0, 1] \to \mathbb{C}$ be square-integrable and $1$-periodic. The Fourier series is

$$f(t) = \sum_{k=-\infty}^{\infty} c_k, e^{2\pi i k t}$$

where the coefficients are

$$c_k = \int_0^1 f(t), e^{-2\pi i k t}, dt = \langle f, e_k \rangle$$

with $e_k(t) = e^{2\pi i k t}$.

The key fact: ${e_k}_{k \in \mathbb{Z}}$ is an **orthonormal basis** for $L^2([0,1])$:

$$\langle e_j, e_k \rangle = \int_0^1 e^{2\pi i j t} \overline{e^{2\pi i k t}}, dt = \int_0^1 e^{2\pi i (j-k) t}, dt = \delta_{jk}$$

So $c_k = \langle f, e_k \rangle$ is just a **coordinate** in an orthonormal basis. The reconstruction $f = \sum c_k e_k$ is the standard expansion in an ONB. There is nothing mysterious here — it is 18.06 projection, but into an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space.

## The Discrete Fourier Transform: Setup

Now discretize. Sample $f$ at $n$ equally spaced points: $f_j = f(j/n)$ for $j = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$. We have a vector $\mathbf{f} = (f_0, f_1, \ldots, f_{n-1}) \in \mathbb{C}^n$.

Define the primitive $n$-th root of unity:

$$\omega_n = e^{2\pi i / n}$$

The **Discrete Fourier Transform** of $\mathbf{f}$ is the vector $\hat{\mathbf{f}} \in \mathbb{C}^n$ with

$$\hat{f}_k = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} f_j, \omega_n^{-jk}, \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$$

The inverse DFT is

$$f_j = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \hat{f}_k, \omega_n^{jk}, \qquad j = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$$

Note the $\frac{1}{n}$ normalization in the inverse — this is one convention. Another places $\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$ symmetrically in both directions (making the DFT matrix unitary). I will keep this convention for now because it matches most implementations.

---

## The DFT as a Matrix: The Fourier Matrix $F_n$

This is the 18.06 heart of it. The DFT is a matrix-vector multiplication:

$$\hat{\mathbf{f}} = F_n \mathbf{f}$$

where $F_n$ is the $n \times n$ **Fourier matrix** with entries

$$(F_n)_{kj} = \omega_n^{-jk} = e^{-2\pi i jk/n}, \qquad j, k = 0, 1, \ldots, n-1$$

Let me write $F_n$ explicitly for $n = 4$, using $\omega = \omega_4 = e^{2\pi i/4} = i$:

$$F_4 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & \omega^{-1} & \omega^{-2} & \omega^{-3} \ 1 & \omega^{-2} & \omega^{-4} & \omega^{-6} \ 1 & \omega^{-3} & \omega^{-6} & \omega^{-9} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & -i & -1 & i \ 1 & -1 & 1 & -1 \ 1 & i & -1 & -i \end{pmatrix}$$

(using $\omega^{-1} = e^{-2\pi i/4} = e^{-i\pi/2} = -i$, $\omega^{-2} = -1$, $\omega^{-3} = i$, and periodicity $\omega^{-4} = 1$.)

The key algebraic fact: the _columns_ of $F_n$ are orthogonal. Specifically, if $\tilde{F}_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} F_n$, then $\tilde{F}_n$ is **unitary**: $\tilde{F}_n^* \tilde{F}_n = I$.

**Proof.** The $(j,k)$ entry of $F_n^* F_n$ is the inner product of column $j$ and column $k$ of $F_n$:

$$(F_n^* F_n)_{jk} = \sum_{m=0}^{n-1} \overline{(F_n)_{mj}}, (F_n)_{mk} = \sum_{m=0}^{n-1} \omega_n^{mj} \cdot \omega_n^{-mk} = \sum_{m=0}^{n-1} \omega_n^{m(j-k)}$$

If $j = k$, each term is $1$, so the sum is $n$.

If $j \neq k$, this is a geometric series with ratio $r = \omega_n^{j-k} \neq 1$ (since $j - k \not\equiv 0 \pmod{n}$):

$$\sum_{m=0}^{n-1} r^m = \frac{r^n - 1}{r - 1} = \frac{(\omega_n^{j-k})^n - 1}{\omega_n^{j-k} - 1} = \frac{e^{2\pi i(j-k)} - 1}{\omega_n^{j-k} - 1} = \frac{1 - 1}{\omega_n^{j-k} - 1} = 0$$

So $(F_n^* F_n)_{jk} = n \delta_{jk}$, which gives $F_n^* F_n = n I$. Thus $F_n^{-1} = \frac{1}{n} F_n^*$, and the inverse DFT formula follows immediately. $\blacksquare$

The columns of $F_n$ form an **orthogonal basis** for $\mathbb{C}^n$ (not orthonormal, but that is just a scalar factor). The DFT is a change of basis from the standard basis ${e_j}$ to the Fourier basis $\left{ \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \text{col}_k(F_n) \right}$.

## The General $n$ Fourier Matrix

For completeness, here is $F_n$ in full generality. Let $\omega = e^{2\pi i/n}$. Row index $k$, column index $j$, both from $0$ to $n-1$:

$$F_n = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \ 1 & \omega^{-1} & \omega^{-2} & \omega^{-3} & \cdots & \omega^{-(n-1)} \ 1 & \omega^{-2} & \omega^{-4} & \omega^{-6} & \cdots & \omega^{-2(n-1)} \ 1 & \omega^{-3} & \omega^{-6} & \omega^{-9} & \cdots & \omega^{-3(n-1)} \ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ 1 & \omega^{-(n-1)} & \omega^{-2(n-1)} & \omega^{-3(n-1)} & \cdots & \omega^{-(n-1)^2} \end{pmatrix}$$

Entry $(k,j)$ is $\omega^{-jk}$. The matrix is symmetric: $(F_n)_{kj} = (F_n)_{jk}$. It is not Hermitian — it is complex symmetric.

For real signals $\mathbf{f} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the DFT satisfies the **conjugate symmetry**: $\hat{f}_{n-k} = \overline{\hat{f}_k}$. So only the first $\lfloor n/2 \rfloor + 1$ coefficients are independent. This is why FFT routines return $n/2 + 1$ complex numbers for a real input of length $n$.

## Convolution and the Convolution Theorem

This is, to me, the deepest reason Fourier analysis is important.

**Definition.** The circular convolution of $\mathbf{f}, \mathbf{g} \in \mathbb{C}^n$ is

$$(\mathbf{f} * \mathbf{g})_k = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} f_j, g_{k-j \bmod n}$$

**Theorem (Convolution Theorem).**

$$\widehat{\mathbf{f} * \mathbf{g}} = \hat{\mathbf{f}} \odot \hat{\mathbf{g}}$$

where $\odot$ denotes pointwise multiplication.

**Proof.** Direct computation:

$$\widehat{(\mathbf{f}_\mathbf{g})}_k = \sum_{m=0}^{n-1} (\mathbf{f}_\mathbf{g})_m \omega^{-mk} = \sum_{m=0}^{n-1} \left(\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} f_j g_{m-j}\right) \omega^{-mk}$$

Substitute $\ell = m - j$:

$$= \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} f_j \sum_{\ell=0}^{n-1} g_\ell, \omega^{-(j+\ell)k} = \left(\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} f_j \omega^{-jk}\right)!\left(\sum_{\ell=0}^{n-1} g_\ell \omega^{-\ell k}\right) = \hat{f}_k \cdot \hat{g}_k \qquad \blacksquare$$

The linear algebra statement: **the Fourier matrix $F_n$ diagonalizes every circulant matrix**. A circulant matrix $C$ has the form

$$C = \begin{pmatrix} c_0 & c_{n-1} & c_{n-2} & \cdots & c_1 \ c_1 & c_0 & c_{n-1} & \cdots & c_2 \ c_2 & c_1 & c_0 & \cdots & c_3 \ \vdots & & & \ddots & \vdots \ c_{n-1} & c_{n-2} & c_{n-3} & \cdots & c_0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Then $C = F_n^{-1} \operatorname{diag}(\hat{\mathbf{c}}), F_n$, where $\hat{\mathbf{c}} = F_n \mathbf{c}$ and $\mathbf{c} = (c_0, c_1, \ldots, c_{n-1})^T$.

In other words: **the eigenvectors of every circulant matrix are exactly the columns of $F_n$**. Convolution with $\mathbf{c}$ is multiplication by a circulant, and the Fourier modes $\omega^{jk}$ are the eigenvectors. This is the algebraic reason the Fourier transform is the right tool for shift-invariant systems.

## The Fast Fourier Transform (FFT)

Naive DFT costs $O(n^2)$ — for each of $n$ output frequencies, sum $n$ terms. The FFT reduces this to $O(n \log n)$ by exploiting the structure of $F_n$.

**Cooley-Tukey decomposition** (for $n = 2^N$):

Split $\mathbf{f}$ into even-indexed and odd-indexed samples:

$$\hat{f}_k = \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} f_j \omega_n^{-jk} = \sum_{j=0}^{n/2-1} f_{2j} \omega_n^{-2jk} + \sum_{j=0}^{n/2-1} f_{2j+1} \omega_n^{-(2j+1)k}$$

$$= \sum_{j=0}^{n/2-1} f_{2j} \omega_{n/2}^{-jk} + \omega_n^{-k} \sum_{j=0}^{n/2-1} f_{2j+1} \omega_{n/2}^{-jk}$$

(using $\omega_n^{-2jk} = e^{-2\pi i \cdot 2jk/n} = e^{-2\pi i jk/(n/2)} = \omega_{n/2}^{-jk}$.)

Let $E_k = \text{DFT}_{n/2}(\mathbf{f}_\text{even})_k$ and $O_k = \text{DFT}_{n/2}(\mathbf{f}_\text{odd})_k$. Then:

$$\hat{f}_k = E_k + \omega_n^{-k} O_k$$ $$\hat{f}_{k + n/2} = E_k - \omega_n^{-k} O_k$$

(using periodicity of $E_k$ and $O_k$ with period $n/2$.) This is the **butterfly operation**. Each level of the recursion does $O(n)$ work; there are $\log_2 n$ levels, giving $O(n \log n)$ total.

In matrix language: $F_n$ factors as

$$F_n = \begin{pmatrix} I & D \ I & -D \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} F_{n/2} & 0 \ 0 & F_{n/2} \end{pmatrix} P$$

where $D = \operatorname{diag}(1, \omega_n^{-1}, \omega_n^{-2}, \ldots, \omega_n^{-(n/2-1)})$ (the twiddle factors) and $P$ is the **bit-reversal permutation** (separates even and odd indices).

Apply recursively: $F_{n/2}$ factors similarly. After $\log_2 n$ levels, $F_n$ is expressed as a product of $\log_2 n$ sparse matrices, each with only $2$ nonzeros per row. This is the factored view from Strang.

For $n = 8$, the full factorization is:

$$F_8 = \begin{pmatrix} I_4 & D_4 \ I_4 & -D_4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} F_4 & 0 \ 0 & F_4 \end{pmatrix} P_8$$

Then each $F_4$ factors similarly. The total operation count: $\frac{n}{2} \log_2 n = 4 \cdot 3 = 12$ complex multiplications (vs. $64$ for naive $n^2$).

## Parseval / Plancherel for the DFT

For the DFT with the normalization $\hat{\mathbf{f}} = F_n \mathbf{f}$:

$$\sum_{j=0}^{n-1} |f_j|^2 = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} |\hat{f}_k|^2$$

Or equivalently, with the unitary normalization $\tilde{F}_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} F_n$:

$$|\mathbf{f}|^2 = |\tilde{F}_n \mathbf{f}|^2$$

This is just the isometry property of a unitary matrix: $|U\mathbf{x}| = |\mathbf{x}|$ for all $\mathbf{x}$. In 18.06 language: orthogonal (unitary) transformations preserve lengths. Parseval is not a special theorem — it is the trivial fact that $Q^T Q = I$ preserves $|\mathbf{x}|$.

## Spectral Interpretation

The DFT coefficient $\hat{f}_k$ measures "how much of frequency $k/n$ is in $\mathbf{f}$." More precisely:

- $k = 0$: DC component, $\hat{f}_0 = \sum_j f_j$ (proportional to mean)
- $k = 1, \ldots, \lfloor n/2 \rfloor$: positive frequencies, cycles $1$ through $n/2$ per period
- $k = n/2 + 1, \ldots, n-1$: these are aliases of negative frequencies due to sampling; $k = n - m$ corresponds to frequency $-m/n$

The **Nyquist frequency** is $k = n/2$, corresponding to the highest resolvable oscillation: alternating $+1, -1, +1, -1, \ldots$. Any frequency above this aliases back down — this is the Nyquist-Shannon sampling theorem, which follows directly from the periodicity of $\omega_n$.

## Eigenstructure Revisited

The eigenvalues of the circulant matrix $C$ (with first row $\mathbf{c}$) are precisely $\hat{c}_0, \hat{c}_1, \ldots, \hat{c}_{n-1}$ — the DFT of the first row. The eigenvectors are the columns of $\frac{1}{n} F_n^*$ (or equivalently rows of $F_n$).

This gives an extremely fast way to solve circulant linear systems $C\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$:

1. Compute $\hat{\mathbf{b}} = F_n \mathbf{b}$ — one FFT, $O(n \log n)$
2. Divide: $\hat{x}_k = \hat{b}_k / \hat{c}_k$ for each $k$
3. Invert: $\mathbf{x} = F_n^{-1} \hat{\mathbf{x}}$ — one IFFT, $O(n \log n)$

Compare to Gaussian elimination for a general $n \times n$ system: $O(n^3)$. For a circulant system of size $n = 10^6$, this is the difference between $10^{18}$ operations and $2 \times 10^7$. This is why PDEs with periodic boundary conditions on uniform grids are solved spectrally.

## The 2D DFT

For images (or any function on a 2D grid), the DFT extends naturally as a **separable** transform. For an $M \times N$ image $f[m, n]$:

$$\hat{f}[k, \ell] = \sum_{m=0}^{M-1} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} f[m, n], e^{-2\pi i (mk/M + n\ell/N)}$$

In matrix form: $\hat{F} = F_M \cdot F \cdot F_N^T$ (applying the 1D DFT to rows, then columns). This works because $e^{-2\pi i (mk/M + n\ell/N)} = e^{-2\pi i mk/M} \cdot e^{-2\pi i n\ell/N}$ — the kernel factors.

JPEG (original, not JPEG 2000) computes the 2D DCT (Discrete Cosine Transform, a real-valued variant of the DFT) on $8 \times 8$ blocks, quantizes the high-frequency coefficients, and discards the small ones. The reason it works: natural images have energy concentrated in low frequencies. The DFT basis is the right one to exploit this.

## Relation to the Wavelet Note

The DFT uses globally supported basis functions — each Fourier mode $e^{2\pi i kt/n}$ involves all $n$ samples. A single spike at position $j$ has nonzero DFT coefficients everywhere (since $\hat{f}_k = \omega_n^{-jk}$ with $|\omega_n^{-jk}| = 1$). So localized features are maximally delocalized in the Fourier domain.

Wavelets trade global frequency precision for local time-frequency information. The DFT is the right tool when:

- The signal is stationary (statistics don't change over time)
- Global frequency content is what matters
- The system is shift-invariant (circulant)

Wavelets are the right tool when:

- The signal has transients, discontinuities, or nonstationarity
- We need to know both _when_ and _at what frequency_ something happens
- We want sparse representations of piecewise smooth functions

The two are not in competition. They are optimal for different signal classes.

## Things I Want to Come Back To

- Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT) — real-valued, used in JPEG and MP3; arises naturally from considering symmetric extensions of real signals
- Chirp-Z transform — computing the DFT on a spiral contour rather than the unit circle; generalizes FFT to arbitrary sampling grids
- Non-uniform DFT (NUDFT/NFFT) — when samples are not equally spaced; loses the FFT structure but can be approximated
- Number-theoretic transform — DFT over $\mathbb{Z}/p\mathbb{Z}$ instead of $\mathbb{C}$; used in fast polynomial multiplication for exact arithmetic
- Multidimensional FFT and tensor structure — the $F_M \cdot F \cdot F_N^T$ factorization is a Kronecker product, and Kronecker products appear everywhere in numerical linear algebra

## Summary

The DFT is the matrix $F_n$ with entries $\omega_n^{-jk}$. Its columns are orthogonal — this is the geometric series identity. Because $F_n$ is (up to normalization) unitary, it is an orthonormal change of basis. Because its columns are the eigenvectors of every circulant matrix, the DFT diagonalizes convolution. The FFT is not a different transform — it is the same $F_n$, factored into $\log_2 n$ sparse matrices by recursively exploiting the even-odd structure of $\omega_n^2 = \omega_{n/2}$.

Everything reduces to: columns of $F_n$ form an orthogonal basis, and that basis happens to diagonalize the convolution operator.
