# Pseudoinverse (Moore-Penrose Inverse)

## 0. Why This Exists — The Motivating Problem

> **Core tension:** $A^{-1}$ exists only when $A$ is square *and* full rank.  
> But in practice, systems $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ are almost always:
> - **Overdetermined** ($m > n$): more equations than unknowns → *no exact solution*
> - **Underdetermined** ($m < n$): more unknowns than equations → *infinitely many solutions*
> - **Rank-deficient**: even if square, $A$ may be singular

The pseudoinverse $A^+$ is the *unique* generalized inverse that handles all three cases optimally.

---

## 1. Definition

For any matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, the **Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse** $A^+ \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$ is the unique matrix satisfying all four **Penrose conditions**:

| # | Condition | Meaning |
|---|-----------|---------|
| 1 | $A A^+ A = A$ | $A^+$ is a generalized inverse |
| 2 | $A^+ A A^+ = A^+$ | $A^+$ is a *reflexive* generalized inverse |
| 3 | $(A A^+)^T = A A^+$ | $AA^+$ is symmetric (orthogonal projector onto $\text{col}(A)$) |
| 4 | $(A^+ A)^T = A^+ A$ | $A^+A$ is symmetric (orthogonal projector onto $\text{row}(A)$) |

> **Uniqueness:** There is exactly one matrix satisfying all four conditions. This is a theorem — not a definition choice.

---

## 2. Construction via SVD (The Right Way to Think About It)

### 2.1 SVD Decomposition

$$A = U \Sigma V^T$$

where:
- $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$: orthogonal, columns are **left singular vectors** (basis for $\text{col}(A)$ and $\text{null}(A^T)$)
- $\Sigma \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$: diagonal with singular values $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$, rest zero
- $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$: orthogonal, columns are **right singular vectors** (basis for $\text{row}(A)$ and $\text{null}(A)$)
- $r = \text{rank}(A)$

### 2.2 The Pseudoinverse via SVD

$$\boxed{A^+ = V \Sigma^+ U^T}$$

where $\Sigma^+$ inverts only the **nonzero** singular values:

$$\Sigma^+ = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1^{-1} & & \\ & \ddots & \\ & & \sigma_r^{-1} \\ & 0 & \end{pmatrix}^T \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$$
