
> _Two transformations, one after the other. The matrix of the composition is the product of the matrices. This sounds obvious. It isn't, until you see it._

---

## The Setup

Let $T_A : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ and $T_B : \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be linear transformations with associated matrices $A$ ($m \times n$) and $B$ ($n \times p$).

The composition $T_A \circ T_B$ sends $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^p$ first through $B$, then through $A$:

$$ \mathbf{x} \mapsto B\mathbf{x} \mapsto A(B\mathbf{x}) = (AB)\mathbf{x} $$

The key claim: **the matrix of the composition is exactly $AB$**.

This is not a definition to be swallowed — it's a theorem, and the proof is a one-liner that you should be able to reproduce from memory.

---

## Why the Dimensions Must Match

$B$ is $n \times p$, so it eats vectors from $\mathbb{R}^p$ and spits out vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$. $A$ is $m \times n$, so it needs to eat from $\mathbb{R}^n$ — exactly what $B$ produces.

If the "inner" dimensions don't match, the composition isn't defined. The matrix product $AB$ enforces this automatically: you can only multiply an $m \times n$ matrix by an $n \times p$ matrix. The dimension check on the matrices _is_ the check that the transformations can be composed.

The result $AB$ is $m \times p$: a direct transformation from $\mathbb{R}^p$ to $\mathbb{R}^m$.

---

## The Column Picture — Where it Becomes Concrete

Each column of $AB$ is $A$ times the corresponding column of $B$.

Let $B = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 & \mathbf{b}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{b}_p \end{bmatrix}$, where each $\mathbf{b}_j \in \mathbb{R}^n$. Then:

$$ AB = \begin{bmatrix} A\mathbf{b}_1 & A\mathbf{b}_2 & \cdots & A\mathbf{b}_p \end{bmatrix} $$

Each column of the output is: take a column of $B$, push it through $A$.

This is the column picture. The $j$-th column of $AB$ tells you where the $j$-th standard basis vector of $\mathbb{R}^p$ ends up after passing through both transformations in sequence.

---

## The Entry Formula

The $(i,j)$ entry of $AB$ is the dot product of row $i$ of $A$ with column $j$ of $B$:

$$ (AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{ik} , b_{kj} $$

Stare at the indices: $k$ runs over the shared inner dimension. It's the index being "summed out" — the one that was the output dimension of $B$ and the input dimension of $A$.

---

## A Worked Example — Full Matrix Written Out

Let $A$ be $2 \times 3$ and $B$ be $3 \times 2$:

$$ A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{bmatrix} 7 & 8 \ 9 & 10 \ 11 & 12 \end{bmatrix} $$

The product $AB$ is $2 \times 2$. Compute entry by entry:

$$ (AB)_{11} = (1)(7) + (2)(9) + (3)(11) = 7 + 18 + 33 = 58 $$

$$ (AB)_{12} = (1)(8) + (2)(10) + (3)(12) = 8 + 20 + 36 = 64 $$

$$ (AB)_{21} = (4)(7) + (5)(9) + (6)(11) = 28 + 45 + 66 = 139 $$

$$ (AB)_{22} = (4)(8) + (5)(10) + (6)(12) = 32 + 50 + 72 = 154 $$

So:

$$ AB = \begin{bmatrix} 58 & 64 \ 139 & 154 \end{bmatrix} $$

Now verify with the column picture. First column of $B$ is $\begin{bmatrix} 7 \ 9 \ 11 \end{bmatrix}$:

$$ A \begin{bmatrix} 7 \ 9 \ 11 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 7 + 18 + 33 \ 28 + 45 + 66 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 58 \ 139 \end{bmatrix} \checkmark $$

---

## The Proof That the Composition Has Matrix $AB$

**Claim.** If $T_A(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$ and $T_B(\mathbf{x}) = B\mathbf{x}$, then $(T_A \circ T_B)(\mathbf{x}) = (AB)\mathbf{x}$.

**Proof.** By definition of composition and linearity of matrix multiplication:

$$ (T_A \circ T_B)(\mathbf{x}) = T_A(T_B(\mathbf{x})) = T_A(B\mathbf{x}) = A(B\mathbf{x}) = (AB)\mathbf{x} $$

The last equality is associativity of matrix multiplication, which follows from the definition of the product. $\blacksquare$

That's it. The proof is clean because associativity does all the work. Associativity of matrix multiplication isn't obvious (prove it once from the entry formula — it's a good exercise in index manipulation), but once you have it, the correspondence between composition and product is immediate.

---

## Noncommutativity — The Essential Warning

In general, $AB \neq BA$.

Even when both products are defined (which requires $A$ and $B$ to be square of the same size), they are almost never equal. The composition "first $B$, then $A$" is a fundamentally different operation from "first $A$, then $B$".

Geometric intuition: rotate 90 degrees then reflect is not the same as reflect then rotate 90 degrees. Try it with your hand.

The commutator $[A, B] = AB - BA$ measures the failure of commutativity. In most of applied mathematics and physics, how big $[A, B]$ is matters enormously (see: quantum mechanics).

---

## Associativity — What Does Hold

$$(AB)C = A(BC)$$

whenever the dimensions are compatible. This means the order in which you perform multiplications does not matter — only the order of the matrices in the product matters. You can parenthesize however you like.

This is useful computationally: if $A$ is $m \times n$, $B$ is $n \times p$, $C$ is $p \times q$, computing $(AB)C$ costs $O(mnp + mpq)$ operations whereas $A(BC)$ costs $O(npq + mnq)$. The answer is the same; the cost differs. For rectangular matrices of very different sizes, the choice of parenthesization can make a dramatic difference.

---

## The Rank Inequality

$$ \operatorname{rank}(AB) \leq \min(\operatorname{rank}(A), \operatorname{rank}(B)) $$

The column space of $AB$ lies inside the column space of $A$ (since every column of $AB$ is a linear combination of columns of $A$). The column space of $AB$ is the image of the column space of $B$ under $A$, so rank can only decrease or stay the same when you compose.

Informally: you can't create new independence by composing. Composition can only lose information, never gain it.

---

## Connection to Strang and 18.06

Strang introduces matrix multiplication via the row-column rule, but the deeper point — made repeatedly in 18.06 — is that matrices _are_ transformations, and multiplication _is_ composition. The entry formula is just bookkeeping. The real content is the geometric/algebraic fact that applying $B$ then $A$ is the same as applying $AB$.

Lecture 3 in 18.06 makes this explicit: the reason we define multiplication the way we do (rather than entry-wise, which would be simpler) is precisely to make $A(B\mathbf{x}) = (AB)\mathbf{x}$ true. The definition is reverse-engineered from what we need transformations to do.

---

## Things to Actually Think About

- What happens to the null space? $\operatorname{null}(B) \subseteq \operatorname{null}(AB)$. Why? If $B\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$, then $A(B\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$.
- Can $AB = 0$ with neither $A$ nor $B$ zero? Yes. Example: $A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$, $B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$... actually, $AB = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. Check it.
- If $A$ is invertible, then $\operatorname{rank}(AB) = \operatorname{rank}(B)$. An invertible map doesn't lose rank.
- $\operatorname{rank}(AB) \geq \operatorname{rank}(A) + \operatorname{rank}(B) - n$ (Sylvester's inequality — prove this once).
