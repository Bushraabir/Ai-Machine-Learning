## The geometric soul of linearity

After the definition sinks in, the next question is: what does a linear transformation _look like_? What does it do to space?

The answer is cleaner than you might expect. A linear transformation cannot do anything wild. It cannot curve things. It cannot fold a line into a parabola. It cannot map a triangle to a circle. The two axioms — additivity and homogeneity — together enforce a rigid geometric discipline:

**Linear transformations map lines to lines (or to the origin) and preserve the ratios of points along those lines.**

Everything else — triangles mapping to triangles, parallelograms to parallelograms, grids to grids — falls out of this one fact.

---

## Lines map to lines

**Proposition.** If $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is linear, then the image of any line is either a line or a single point.

_Proof._ A line in $\mathbb{R}^n$ through the point $\mathbf{p}$ in direction $\mathbf{d} \neq \mathbf{0}$ is parametrized as:

$$\ell(t) = \mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{d}, \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

Apply $T$:

$$T(\ell(t)) = T(\mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{d}) = T(\mathbf{p}) + t, T(\mathbf{d})$$

This is a parametric line through $T(\mathbf{p})$ in direction $T(\mathbf{d})$ — unless $T(\mathbf{d}) = \mathbf{0}$, in which case every point on $\ell$ maps to the single point $T(\mathbf{p})$. $\square$

That is the whole proof. The linearity does all the work. No assumptions on $n$, $m$, or the specific map.

Notice what is happening: the parameter $t$ is preserved _linearly_. The point at parameter $t = 1/3$ on the original line maps to the point at parameter $t = 1/3$ on the image line. This is not a coincidence — it is homogeneity in action.

## Ratios are preserved — the real content

This deserves its own statement.

**Proposition.** If $T$ is linear and $\mathbf{q}$ lies a fraction $\lambda$ of the way from $\mathbf{p}$ to $\mathbf{r}$ — that is, $\mathbf{q} = (1 - \lambda)\mathbf{p} + \lambda\mathbf{r}$ — then $T(\mathbf{q})$ lies the same fraction $\lambda$ of the way from $T(\mathbf{p})$ to $T(\mathbf{r})$.

_Proof._

$$T(\mathbf{q}) = T((1-\lambda)\mathbf{p} + \lambda\mathbf{r}) = (1-\lambda)T(\mathbf{p}) + \lambda, T(\mathbf{r})$$

Done. $\square$

The midpoint of a segment maps to the midpoint of the image segment. The point one-third of the way along maps one-third of the way along. This is what it means to preserve ratios.

## Triangles map to triangles

A triangle is determined by three vertices $\mathbf{p}$, $\mathbf{q}$, $\mathbf{r}$ and all the points in between. Formally, it is the _convex hull_ of those three points:

$$\Delta(\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}) = {(1-s-t)\mathbf{p} + s\mathbf{q} + t\mathbf{r} : s, t \ge 0,\ s + t \le 1}$$

Apply $T$:

$$T((1-s-t)\mathbf{p} + s\mathbf{q} + t\mathbf{r}) = (1-s-t)T(\mathbf{p}) + s, T(\mathbf{q}) + t, T(\mathbf{r})$$

This is exactly the triangle with vertices $T(\mathbf{p})$, $T(\mathbf{q})$, $T(\mathbf{r})$ — the same parameters $s, t$ range over the same region.

So: **the image of the triangle with vertices $\mathbf{p}, \mathbf{q}, \mathbf{r}$ is the triangle with vertices $T(\mathbf{p}), T(\mathbf{q}), T(\mathbf{r})$.** To transform the triangle, I only need to transform its vertices. The interior comes along for free.

This generalizes immediately. Any convex polytope — tetrahedra, parallelepipeds, simplices in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — is determined by its vertices, and its image under $T$ is the polytope with the transformed vertices.

## Parallelograms map to parallelograms

This one is especially useful for understanding determinants.

Take the parallelogram spanned by vectors $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$:

$$\Pi = {s\mathbf{u} + t\mathbf{v} : 0 \le s, t \le 1}$$

Its image:

$$T(s\mathbf{u} + t\mathbf{v}) = s, T(\mathbf{u}) + t, T(\mathbf{v})$$

The image is the parallelogram spanned by $T(\mathbf{u})$ and $T(\mathbf{v})$.

Now, the area of the original parallelogram is $|\det[\mathbf{u}\ \mathbf{v}]|$ (in $\mathbb{R}^2$). The area of the image is $|\det[T(\mathbf{u})\ T(\mathbf{v})]|$. If $T$ has matrix $A$, then $T(\mathbf{u}) = A\mathbf{u}$ and $T(\mathbf{v}) = A\mathbf{v}$, so:

$$|\det[A\mathbf{u}\ A\mathbf{v}]| = |\det(A)| \cdot |\det[\mathbf{u}\ \mathbf{v}]|$$

**$|\det(A)|$ is exactly the factor by which $T$ scales all areas.** This is the geometric meaning of the determinant. It is not just a number computed from a matrix — it is the area scaling factor of the corresponding linear map.

In $\mathbb{R}^3$, the same holds for volumes. In $\mathbb{R}^n$, for $n$-dimensional volume. The determinant unifies all of these.

## What a linear transformation can and cannot do

It helps to be precise about the constraints.

**Can do:**

- Stretch and compress along any directions
- Rotate
- Reflect
- Project (collapse one or more dimensions to zero)
- Shear (shift one coordinate proportionally to another)
- Any composition of the above

**Cannot do:**

- Curve a line into anything nonlinear
- Map a triangle to a circle, ellipse, or parabola
- Translate (shift) the origin — $T(\mathbf{0}) = \mathbf{0}$ always
- Map two distinct lines through the origin to the same image, _unless they both collapse into that image_ (i.e., they both lie in $\ker T$)

That last point is worth sitting with. Distinct lines through the origin are the key objects. If $T$ is invertible, it sends distinct lines through the origin to distinct lines through the origin, bijectively. If $T$ is singular, some of those lines collapse — they pile up in the kernel.

## Why translation is not linear — and affine maps

A pure translation $T(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}$ with $\mathbf{b} \neq \mathbf{0}$ is _not_ linear. It fails immediately: $T(\mathbf{0}) = \mathbf{b} \neq \mathbf{0}$.

Does translation map lines to lines? Yes. Does it map triangles to triangles? Yes — it just slides them rigidly. So it _feels_ linear geometrically, but it violates the algebraic definition.

The resolution: translation is an _affine_ map, not a linear one. An affine map has the form $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b}$ — a linear part plus a translation. Affine maps also preserve lines, ratios, and the shapes of triangles.

The standard trick to handle affine maps is _homogeneous coordinates_: embed $\mathbb{R}^n$ into $\mathbb{R}^{n+1}$ by appending a $1$, so $\mathbf{x} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x} \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$. Then:

$$\begin{bmatrix} A & \mathbf{b} \ \mathbf{0}^T & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x} \ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} A\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{b} \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

The affine map becomes a _linear_ map in one higher dimension. This is why computer graphics, robotics, and projective geometry all work in homogeneous coordinates — it lets you compose rotations, scalings, and translations uniformly as matrix multiplication. Every rigid motion of a 3D object in a game engine is a $4 \times 4$ matrix for exactly this reason.

## A concrete example — shear in $\mathbb{R}^2$

The shear matrix:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

maps $\begin{bmatrix} x \ y \end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} x + y \ y \end{bmatrix}$.

Take the triangle with vertices:

$$\mathbf{p} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{q} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{r} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

The images are:

$$A\mathbf{p} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad A\mathbf{q} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad A\mathbf{r} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

So the right triangle with legs along the axes is sheared into a triangle with the same base but a leaned-over top vertex. Its area: $\det(A) = 1$, so the area is preserved. The shape changed; the area did not. This is the hallmark of a shear.

## The grid picture — Strang's way of seeing it

Strang's most useful geometric observation: to see what a linear transformation does to all of $\mathbb{R}^2$, just watch what it does to the unit square grid. Since lines map to lines and the grid is made of lines, the transformed grid tells the whole story.

The columns of the matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{a}_1 & \mathbf{a}_2 \end{bmatrix}$ are exactly the images of the two standard basis vectors. The unit square spanned by $\mathbf{e}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$ maps to the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{a}_1$ and $\mathbf{a}_2$. The entire grid maps to the lattice of parallelograms built from these two vectors.

This is why the columns of a matrix are the most important thing to look at. They are not just abstract numbers — they are the vectors that the transformation _places_ the coordinate axes onto. Everything else in the image is a linear combination of those.

## The projection example — when a triangle collapses

Let $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be projection onto the $x$-axis:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Take the triangle with vertices $\begin{bmatrix} 0\0 \end{bmatrix}$, $\begin{bmatrix} 1\0 \end{bmatrix}$, $\begin{bmatrix} 1\1 \end{bmatrix}$.

The images are $\begin{bmatrix} 0\0 \end{bmatrix}$, $\begin{bmatrix} 1\0 \end{bmatrix}$, $\begin{bmatrix} 1\0 \end{bmatrix}$.

Two vertices land on the same point. The triangle collapses to a line segment. Technically the image is still a "degenerate triangle" — a triangle where two vertices coincide, which is a line segment. Lines map to lines (or points), and a segment is a line. Nothing breaks.

$\det(A) = 0$. Area scales by zero. This is exactly what the determinant is telling us.

## Summary — what to carry forward

The geometric content of linearity is this: **$T$ is completely determined on all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ by its values on a basis, and it respects all linear combinations exactly.** This has three geometric consequences:

1. Lines map to lines (or collapse to points).
2. Ratios along lines are preserved — midpoints stay midpoints, etc.
3. Any shape that is defined by linear combinations of its vertices — triangles, parallelograms, polytopes — maps to the same type of shape with transformed vertices.

What $T$ cannot do is introduce any curvature or nonlinearity. The two axioms forbid it completely.

The determinant measures the one thing that _can_ change: the scale of volumes. A transformation can rotate space, shear it, project it down — but the factor by which it changes $n$-dimensional volume is always $|\det(A)|$, everywhere uniformly.

## Things to think about

- A circle is not a polytope — it is defined by $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$, a nonlinear equation. A linear transformation maps it to an _ellipse_ in general (or a line segment, or a point). This is worth proving: substitute $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{y}$ into the circle equation and see what you get.
- The set of all linear transformations from $V$ to $W$ is itself a vector space, denoted $\mathcal{L}(V, W)$. Its dimension is $mn$ when $\dim V = n$ and $\dim W = m$. This matches the number of entries in an $m \times n$ matrix.
- What happens to the image of a curve that is _not_ a line or a polytope? The image of a smooth curve $\gamma(t)$ under $T$ is $T(\gamma(t))$, which is smooth. But a circle maps to an ellipse, not a circle — shapes that depend on nonlinear equations change type.
