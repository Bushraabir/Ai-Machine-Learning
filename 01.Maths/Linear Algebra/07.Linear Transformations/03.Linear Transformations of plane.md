# Linear Transformations of the Plane


The previous note established the general geometry: lines map to lines, ratios are preserved, polytopes map to polytopes. Now I want to get very concrete. I want to sit down with $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ and catalogue every qualitatively distinct thing it can do to the plane. Not abstractly — with actual matrices, actual pictures in my head, and actual proofs for the claims I make.

The plane is the right place to do this. Two dimensions is low enough that I can draw everything, and high enough that every phenomenon that matters already shows up.

---

## The matrix encodes everything

Every linear map $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ is represented by a $2 \times 2$ matrix once I fix a basis. The standard choice:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{bmatrix}$$

acts as

$$T\begin{bmatrix} x \ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x \ y \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} ax + by \ cx + dy \end{bmatrix}$$

The columns are the images of the basis vectors:

$$T(\mathbf{e}_1) = \begin{bmatrix} a \ c \end{bmatrix}, \qquad T(\mathbf{e}_2) = \begin{bmatrix} b \ d \end{bmatrix}$$

This is the key. To understand what $T$ does to all of $\mathbb{R}^2$, I only need to track where the two basis vectors land. Every other vector is a linear combination of $\mathbf{e}_1$ and $\mathbf{e}_2$, and linearity does the rest. The whole map is determined by two vectors in the output.

Strang says: look at the columns. He is right.

---

## The unit square picture

The most useful mental image: what does $T$ do to the unit square? The unit square has vertices $\mathbf{0}$, $\mathbf{e}_1$, $\mathbf{e}_2$, $\mathbf{e}_1 + \mathbf{e}_2$. Their images are:

$$\mathbf{0} \mapsto \mathbf{0}, \qquad \mathbf{e}_1 \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} a \ c \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{e}_2 \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} b \ d \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{e}_1 + \mathbf{e}_2 \mapsto \begin{bmatrix} a+b \ c+d \end{bmatrix}$$

The square maps to the parallelogram spanned by the two columns. Its (signed) area is $\det(A) = ad - bc$. If I know the columns, I know the image of the unit square, and therefore — because the whole plane tiles as copies of the unit square — I know the image of the entire grid.

This is the grid picture that Strang uses throughout 18.06. I find it genuinely clarifying. When I look at a matrix, I immediately try to see the two column vectors and imagine the parallelogram they span.

## Catalogue of transformations

I want to go through each type carefully, with explicit matrices and full proofs where there is something to prove.

### Scaling

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} s_1 & 0 \ 0 & s_2 \end{bmatrix}$$

This stretches the $x$-axis by $s_1$ and the $y$-axis by $s_2$, independently. The column vectors are $s_1 \mathbf{e}_1$ and $s_2 \mathbf{e}_2$ — still along the axes, just rescaled.

The unit square maps to a rectangle of dimensions $|s_1| \times |s_2|$. Area scales by $|s_1 s_2| = |\det(A)|$. Correct.

If $s_1 = s_2 = s$, this is a uniform scaling — it scales everything by $s$ and preserves all angles and shapes. Circles stay circles.

If one of the $s_i$ is negative, the corresponding axis is reflected. If both are negative, there is a net rotation by $\pi$ (since a reflection in both axes equals a $180°$ rotation — worth verifying directly).

### Rotation

Rotating counterclockwise by angle $\theta$:

$$R_\theta = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

**Derivation.** I need $R_\theta(\mathbf{e}_1)$ and $R_\theta(\mathbf{e}_2)$. The vector $\mathbf{e}_1 = \begin{bmatrix}1\0\end{bmatrix}$ at angle $0$ rotates to angle $\theta$:

$$R_\theta(\mathbf{e}_1) = \begin{bmatrix}\cos\theta \ \sin\theta\end{bmatrix}$$

The vector $\mathbf{e}_2 = \begin{bmatrix}0\1\end{bmatrix}$ at angle $\pi/2$ rotates to angle $\theta + \pi/2$:

$$R_\theta(\mathbf{e}_2) = \begin{bmatrix}\cos(\theta + \pi/2) \ \sin(\theta + \pi/2)\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}-\sin\theta \ \cos\theta\end{bmatrix}$$

These become the columns of $R_\theta$.

Note: $\det(R_\theta) = \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$. Area is preserved — of course, a rotation does not stretch space. Also $R_\theta^{-1} = R_{-\theta} = R_\theta^T$, which is why orthogonal matrices satisfy $Q^T Q = I$.

### Reflection

Reflection across the line making angle $\alpha$ with the $x$-axis:

$$M_\alpha = \begin{bmatrix} \cos 2\alpha & \sin 2\alpha \ \sin 2\alpha & -\cos 2\alpha \end{bmatrix}$$

The two most common cases:

- Reflection across the $x$-axis ($\alpha = 0$): $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$
- Reflection across the $y$-axis ($\alpha = \pi/2$): $\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
- Reflection across $y = x$ ($\alpha = \pi/4$): $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$

In all cases, $\det(M_\alpha) = -1$. The sign flip is the signature of orientation reversal. A reflection flips the handedness of the plane — a counterclockwise triangle becomes clockwise after reflection. The area is preserved ($|\det| = 1$), but orientation is reversed ($\det < 0$).

This is the geometric meaning of the sign of the determinant. Positive: orientation preserved. Negative: orientation reversed.

### Shear

Horizontal shear by factor $k$:

$$S_k = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & k \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

maps $\begin{bmatrix}x \ y\end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix}x + ky \ y\end{bmatrix}$.

The $y$-coordinate is unchanged. The $x$-coordinate is shifted proportionally to $y$. Geometrically: take the plane and slide each horizontal strip sideways in proportion to its height. The unit square becomes a parallelogram:

$$\begin{bmatrix}0\0\end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix}0\0\end{bmatrix}, \quad \begin{bmatrix}1\0\end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix}1\0\end{bmatrix}, \quad \begin{bmatrix}0\1\end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix}k\1\end{bmatrix}, \quad \begin{bmatrix}1\1\end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix}1+k\1\end{bmatrix}$$

$\det(S_k) = 1$. Area is unchanged. The shape deforms — right angles can become acute or obtuse — but the area of every region is exactly preserved. This is a hallmark of the shear.

A shear is closely related to Gaussian elimination: adding $k$ times one row to another is a shear. The elimination matrices in $A = LU$ are shear matrices.

### Projection

Orthogonal projection onto the $x$-axis:

$$P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

maps $\begin{bmatrix}x\y\end{bmatrix} \mapsto \begin{bmatrix}x\0\end{bmatrix}$. The $y$-component is annihilated.

$\det(P) = 0$. The transformation is singular. The kernel is the entire $y$-axis. The image is the $x$-axis. Dimension collapses from $2$ to $1$.

Projection onto an arbitrary line through the origin with unit direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \begin{bmatrix}u_1 \ u_2\end{bmatrix}$:

$$P_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}} = \hat{\mathbf{u}}\hat{\mathbf{u}}^T = \begin{bmatrix} u_1^2 & u_1 u_2 \ u_1 u_2 & u_2^2 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is a rank-1 matrix. Its column space is the span of $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$. It satisfies $P^2 = P$ — projecting twice is the same as projecting once, which is the defining property of a projector.

Proof that $P^2 = P$:

$$(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\hat{\mathbf{u}}^T)(\hat{\mathbf{u}}\hat{\mathbf{u}}^T) = \hat{\mathbf{u}}(\hat{\mathbf{u}}^T\hat{\mathbf{u}})\hat{\mathbf{u}}^T = \hat{\mathbf{u}} \cdot 1 \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}^T = \hat{\mathbf{u}}\hat{\mathbf{u}}^T$$

since $\hat{\mathbf{u}}^T\hat{\mathbf{u}} = 1$ for a unit vector. Clean.

## The determinant organizes everything

Looking at the catalogue, the determinant is doing a lot of classification work:

|Type|$\det(A)$|Rank|Orientation|
|---|---|---|---|
|Rotation|$+1$|$2$|preserved|
|Reflection|$-1$|$2$|reversed|
|Uniform scaling by $s$|$s^2$|$2$|preserved if $s>0$|
|Shear|$+1$|$2$|preserved|
|Projection|$0$|$1$|collapsed|
|Squish to a line|$0$|$1$|collapsed|
|Squish to a point|$0$|$0$|collapsed|

For invertible maps ($\det \neq 0$): $|\det(A)|$ is the area scaling factor, and $\text{sgn}(\det(A))$ tells me whether orientation is preserved or reversed.

For singular maps ($\det = 0$): the plane is crushed down to a lower-dimensional subspace.

The rotation group $SO(2)$ is exactly the set of $2 \times 2$ matrices with $\det = 1$ and $A^T A = I$. Adding reflections gives $O(2)$, the matrices with $|\det| = 1$ and $A^T A = I$.

## What a circle becomes

The previous note said: a linear transformation maps a circle to an ellipse (or a line segment, or a point). Let me actually prove this.

**Claim.** Let $A$ be an invertible $2 \times 2$ matrix. Then $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$ maps the unit circle $|\mathbf{x}|^2 = 1$ to an ellipse.

**Proof.** A point $\mathbf{y}$ is in the image of the unit circle if and only if $\mathbf{y} = A\mathbf{x}$ for some $\mathbf{x}$ with $|\mathbf{x}| = 1$. Since $A$ is invertible, $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{y}$. The condition $|\mathbf{x}| = 1$ becomes:

$$|A^{-1}\mathbf{y}|^2 = 1 \implies \mathbf{y}^T (A^{-1})^T A^{-1} \mathbf{y} = 1 \implies \mathbf{y}^T (AA^T)^{-1} \mathbf{y} = 1$$

This is the equation $\mathbf{y}^T M \mathbf{y} = 1$ where $M = (AA^T)^{-1}$ is symmetric positive definite (since $AA^T$ is). Every such equation defines an ellipse in $\mathbb{R}^2$. $\square$

The axes of the ellipse are the eigenvectors of $AA^T$, and the semi-axis lengths are the square roots of the eigenvalues of $AA^T$ — equivalently, the singular values of $A$. This is the geometric meaning of the SVD: the singular values of $A$ are the lengths of the semi-axes of the ellipse that $A$ maps the unit circle to.

This is worth remembering: $A$ takes the unit circle to an ellipse whose semi-axes are the singular values of $A$.

## The trace is the infinitesimal scaling rate

For a matrix $A = I + \varepsilon B$ with $\varepsilon$ small:

$$\det(I + \varepsilon B) \approx 1 + \varepsilon \operatorname{tr}(B)$$

This is a first-order Taylor expansion of the determinant. The trace is the infinitesimal volume change rate. In dynamical systems, if $\dot{\mathbf{x}} = B\mathbf{x}$, then the flow contracts area if $\operatorname{tr}(B) < 0$ and expands area if $\operatorname{tr}(B) > 0$. Area is preserved (as in Hamiltonian mechanics) if and only if $\operatorname{tr}(B) = 0$.

I will not prove this in full here, but it is worth knowing. The determinant is the finite scaling; the trace is the infinitesimal scaling. Liouville's theorem in classical mechanics is exactly the statement that the Hamiltonian flow preserves volume, which follows from $\operatorname{tr}(B) = 0$ for the Hamiltonian vector field's Jacobian.

## Composition and the product formula

If I compose two transformations $S$ then $T$, the resulting matrix is $BA$ (applying $A$ first, then $B$). What happens to areas?

$$|\det(BA)| = |\det(B)| \cdot |\det(A)|$$

So areas scale by $|\det(B)| \cdot |\det(A)|$ under the composition. The area scaling factors multiply. This is the multiplicativity of the determinant, which is algebraically proved via the Leibniz formula or by row operations, but geometrically it is obvious: scale by $|\det(A)|$ first, then by $|\det(B)|$, total scaling is the product.

This also means: $A$ is invertible iff $|\det(A)| \neq 0$, since $\det(A^{-1}) = 1/\det(A)$ — the inverse scales areas by the reciprocal factor.

## Eigenvectors are the lines fixed by the transformation

One last geometric fact I want to record here. An eigenvector $\mathbf{v}$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$ satisfies $A\mathbf{v} = \lambda\mathbf{v}$. Geometrically: the line through $\mathbf{0}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ is mapped to itself (if $\lambda \neq 0$) or collapsed to $\mathbf{0}$ (if $\lambda = 0$).

For a rotation $R_\theta$ with $\theta \neq 0, \pi$: no real eigenvectors. Every line through the origin is moved, not fixed. The only fixed point is the origin. (There are complex eigenvectors $e^{\pm i\theta}$, which encode the rotation algebraically.)

For a shear $S_k$ with $k \neq 0$: the $x$-axis is the only eigenvector direction (eigenvalue $1$). The shear fixes the $x$-axis and displaces everything else.

For a projection $P$: the image line is fixed (eigenvalue $1$) and the kernel is collapsed (eigenvalue $0$). Two distinct eigenspaces.

For a reflection $M_\alpha$: the mirror line has eigenvalue $+1$, and the perpendicular direction has eigenvalue $-1$. The mirror is fixed; the perpendicular is flipped.

This is the beginning of spectral theory. The eigenvectors of $A$ are the directions in which $A$ acts as a pure scaling. They are the "natural axes" of the transformation. Everything else is built from these.

## What to carry from this note

The five essential transformations of the plane are: scaling, rotation, reflection, shear, projection. Every $2 \times 2$ linear map is some composition of these, and the determinant classifies which regime we are in: area-expanding, area-contracting, area-preserving, or area-destroying.

The determinant's sign tells us about orientation. Its magnitude tells us about area scaling. Zero means collapse. The trace measures the infinitesimal version of the determinant, and shows up in dynamics.

Eigenvectors are the lines the transformation does not tilt — only the ones it may scale or flip.

And the circle-to-ellipse fact deserves to be on a poster: the unit circle maps to an ellipse, and the semi-axes of that ellipse are the singular values of $A$. That is what the SVD is saying, geometrically.
