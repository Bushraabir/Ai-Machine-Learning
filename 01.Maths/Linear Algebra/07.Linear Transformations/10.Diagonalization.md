## Why Diagonalization Matters

The central question of linear algebra, once you get past solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, is: _what does a matrix actually do?_ Most matrices mix coordinates together in a way that is hard to see. Diagonalization is the project of finding a basis in which $A$ acts by pure scaling — where the geometry is transparent.

If $A$ is diagonalizable, then in the right basis it is nothing more than a list of scale factors. Matrix powers, exponentials, differential equations — all become trivial. The work is entirely in finding that basis.

---

## Eigenvalues and Eigenvectors: The Right Language

A nonzero vector $\mathbf{x}$ is an **eigenvector** of $A$ if

$$A\mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$$

for some scalar $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$, called the **eigenvalue**. The direction of $\mathbf{x}$ is unchanged by $A$ — it is only stretched (or flipped) by $\lambda$.

To find eigenvalues, rearrange: $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has a nonzero solution iff $A - \lambda I$ is singular:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$

This is the **characteristic equation**. For an $n \times n$ matrix it gives a degree-$n$ polynomial in $\lambda$ — the **characteristic polynomial** $p(\lambda)$. Over $\mathbb{C}$, it always has $n$ roots (counted with multiplicity, by the fundamental theorem of algebra). Over $\mathbb{R}$, complex roots come in conjugate pairs.

Once $\lambda$ is known, the eigenvectors are $\text{null}(A - \lambda I)$ — the null space of a singular matrix, which is always nontrivial.

---

## The Diagonalization Theorem

**Theorem.** An $n \times n$ matrix $A$ is diagonalizable if and only if it has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors.

If $\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_n$ are those eigenvectors with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, form the **eigenvector matrix** $S$ with these as columns:

$$S = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \ \mathbf{x}_1 & \mathbf{x}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{x}_n \ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix}$$

Then the identity $AS = S\Lambda$ holds column by column ($A\mathbf{x}_k = \lambda_k \mathbf{x}_k$ for each $k$), where

$$\Lambda = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & & & \ & \lambda_2 & & \ & & \ddots & \ & & & \lambda_n \end{pmatrix}$$

Since $S$ has linearly independent columns, $S$ is invertible. So:

$$\boxed{A = S \Lambda S^{-1}}$$

This is the **eigendecomposition** of $A$.

**Proof of the column-by-column identity.** Look at the $k$-th column of $AS$:

$$[AS]_k = A\mathbf{x}_k = \lambda_k \mathbf{x}_k$$

The $k$-th column of $S\Lambda$ is $\lambda_k \mathbf{x}_k$ (multiplying column $k$ of $S$ by the $k$-th diagonal entry of $\Lambda$). So $AS = S\Lambda$ column by column. Since $S$ is invertible, $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$. $\square$

---

## Change of Basis: The Real Meaning

The formula $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$ is a **change of basis** statement. To see this, think about what each factor does to a vector $\mathbf{v}$:

1. $S^{-1}\mathbf{v}$: express $\mathbf{v}$ in the eigenbasis (find its coordinates $\mathbf{c}$ such that $\mathbf{v} = S\mathbf{c}$)
2. $\Lambda(S^{-1}\mathbf{v})$: scale each coordinate by the corresponding eigenvalue
3. $S(\Lambda S^{-1}\mathbf{v})$: convert back to the standard basis

So $A$ is, in its own natural basis, nothing but diagonal scaling. The complexity of $A$ in standard coordinates is an artifact of using the wrong basis.

This is Strang's core point: **every diagonalizable matrix is simple in the right coordinates.** The eigenvectors _are_ the right coordinates.

## A Worked Example

Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 1: Characteristic polynomial.**

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{pmatrix} 3-\lambda & 1 \ 0 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix} = (3-\lambda)(2-\lambda) = 0$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = 2$.

**Step 2: Eigenvectors.**

For $\lambda_1 = 3$:

$$A - 3I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{rref}} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Free variable $x_1$, pivot forces $x_2 = 0$. Eigenvector: $\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

For $\lambda_2 = 2$:

$$A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Free variable $x_2$, pivot: $x_1 = -x_2$. Eigenvector: $\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

**Step 3: Form $S$ and $\Lambda$.**

$$S = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \Lambda = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 4: Compute $S^{-1}$.**

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{pmatrix}$, the inverse is $\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$. Here $\det(S) = 1$, so:

$$S^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Verification:** $S\Lambda S^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$

First, $S\Lambda = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -2 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

Then $(S\Lambda)S^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -2 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = A$. $\checkmark$

## When Does Diagonalization Fail?

Two distinct failure modes.

**Failure mode 1: Repeated eigenvalues with deficient eigenspaces.**

A repeated eigenvalue $\lambda$ has _algebraic multiplicity_ $m_a$ (its multiplicity as a root of $\det(A - \lambda I)$) and _geometric multiplicity_ $m_g = \dim,\text{null}(A - \lambda I)$. Always $m_g \le m_a$. When $m_g < m_a$ for any eigenvalue, $A$ does not have $n$ independent eigenvectors and is **not diagonalizable**.

Example: $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$. The only eigenvalue is $\lambda = 2$ with $m_a = 2$.

$$A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\text{null}(A - 2I) = \text{span}\left{\begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}\right}$, so $m_g = 1 < 2 = m_a$. Only one independent eigenvector. $A$ is a **Jordan block** and is not diagonalizable — but it is the canonical "almost diagonal" form (Jordan normal form).

**Failure mode 2: No real eigenvalues.**

The matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$ (rotation by $90°$) has characteristic polynomial $\lambda^2 + 1 = 0$ — no real roots. Over $\mathbb{C}$ it diagonalizes with eigenvalues $\pm i$. Over $\mathbb{R}$ it does not.

**Sufficient conditions for diagonalizability (no computation needed):**

- $A$ has $n$ _distinct_ eigenvalues $\Rightarrow$ automatically $n$ independent eigenvectors
- $A$ is real symmetric ($A = A^T$) $\Rightarrow$ the Spectral Theorem guarantees real eigenvalues and an orthonormal eigenbasis (see below)
- $A$ is normal ($A^_A = AA^_$) over $\mathbb{C}$ $\Rightarrow$ unitarily diagonalizable

## The Spectral Theorem (Symmetric Matrices)

The most important special case, and the one that comes up everywhere.

**Theorem.** If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is symmetric ($A = A^T$), then:

1. All eigenvalues of $A$ are real.
2. Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
3. There is an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$ consisting entirely of eigenvectors of $A$.

In other words, $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ where $Q$ is orthogonal ($Q^{-1} = Q^T$):

$$A = Q\Lambda Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \lambda_i \mathbf{q}_i \mathbf{q}_i^T$$

The last form is the **spectral decomposition**: $A$ is a sum of rank-1 projection matrices, each scaled by an eigenvalue. Each $\mathbf{q}_i \mathbf{q}_i^T$ projects onto the $i$-th eigenspace. This is the cleanest possible picture.

**Proof that eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.** Let $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$ and $A\mathbf{y} = \mu\mathbf{y}$ with $\lambda \ne \mu$. Compute:

$$\lambda, \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{y} = (A\mathbf{x})^T \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}^T A^T \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}^T A\mathbf{y} = \mu, \mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{y}$$

So $(\lambda - \mu),\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y} = 0$. Since $\lambda \ne \mu$, we get $\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y} = 0$. $\square$

The symmetry of $A$ was used exactly once: $A^T = A$ in the middle step.

## Powers of a Matrix

Diagonalization makes matrix powers effortless. From $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$:

$$A^k = (S\Lambda S^{-1})^k = S\Lambda^k S^{-1}$$

since the $S^{-1}S$ terms in the middle all cancel. And

$$\Lambda^k = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1^k & & \ & \ddots & \ & & \lambda_n^k \end{pmatrix}$$

So $A^{100}$ costs no more work than $A^1$, once you have the eigendecomposition. In contrast, brute-force matrix multiplication costs $O(n^3)$ per step — catastrophic for large $k$.

**Application: Fibonacci numbers.** The recurrence $F_{k+2} = F_{k+1} + F_k$ is a linear recurrence. Write:

$$\begin{pmatrix} F_{k+1} \ F_k \end{pmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}}_{A} \begin{pmatrix} F_k \ F_{k-1} \end{pmatrix}$$

So $\begin{pmatrix} F_{k+1} \ F_k \end{pmatrix} = A^k \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

The eigenvalues of $A$ are $\lambda_{1,2} = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{5}}{2}$ — the golden ratio $\phi$ and its conjugate $\hat{\phi} = \frac{1-\sqrt{5}}{2}$. Since $|\hat\phi| < 1$, its contribution decays and $F_k \sim \frac{\phi^k}{\sqrt{5}}$ for large $k$. The eigenvalues _are_ the growth rate of the sequence.

## The Matrix Exponential

For differential equations $\frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dt} = A\mathbf{u}$, the solution is $\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}(0)$, where

$$e^{At} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(At)^k}{k!}$$

With $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$:

$$e^{At} = S, e^{\Lambda t}, S^{-1}, \qquad e^{\Lambda t} = \begin{pmatrix} e^{\lambda_1 t} & & \ & \ddots & \ & & e^{\lambda_n t} \end{pmatrix}$$

The eigenvalues of $A$ directly control the behavior of solutions:

- $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0$ for all $i$: solution decays to $\mathbf{0}$ (stable equilibrium)
- $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) > 0$ for some $i$: solution grows (instability)
- $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) = 0$ for all $i$, $A$ diagonalizable: oscillation, no growth

This is the entire stability theory of linear ODEs in one sentence.

## Trace, Determinant, and Eigenvalues

Two invariants connect $A$ and its eigenvalues without any computation of $S$:

$$\text{tr}(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i, \qquad \det(A) = \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda_i$$

These follow from expanding the characteristic polynomial:

$$p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I) = (-1)^n\left(\lambda^n - (\text{tr}, A)\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + (-1)^n \det A\right)$$

The trace is the sum of diagonal entries (obviously), and also the sum of eigenvalues (less obviously). The determinant is the product of eigenvalues. In particular: $A$ is singular iff at least one eigenvalue is zero. Good internal consistency check.

**Similarity invariance.** If $B = M^{-1}AM$, then $B$ and $A$ have the same characteristic polynomial — hence the same eigenvalues, trace, and determinant. The eigenvalues are properties of the linear transformation, not of the particular matrix representing it.

## What Diagonalization Is Not

**Not all matrices are diagonalizable.** As above: defective matrices (insufficient eigenvectors for a repeated eigenvalue) are not. Their canonical form is the Jordan normal form — block diagonal with Jordan blocks. The Jordan form exists for every matrix over $\mathbb{C}$.

**Not the same as SVD.** The eigendecomposition $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$ requires $A$ to be square and uses the _same_ basis $S$ for domain and codomain. The SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$ works for any $m \times n$ matrix, uses _different_ orthonormal bases $U$ and $V$, and always exists. For symmetric positive definite matrices, $S = Q = U = V$ and the two coincide. In general they are different.

**Not always numerically stable.** If $S$ is nearly singular (nearly dependent eigenvectors), computing $S^{-1}$ is ill-conditioned. In practice, eigenvalue algorithms (QR algorithm) do not form $S^{-1}$ explicitly.

## The Geometry in Two Dimensions

For a $2 \times 2$ diagonalizable matrix with real eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ and eigenvectors $\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2$:

- Any vector $\mathbf{v} = c_1 \mathbf{x}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{x}_2$ maps to $A\mathbf{v} = c_1 \lambda_1 \mathbf{x}_1 + c_2 \lambda_2 \mathbf{x}_2$
- The eigenvector directions are the **principal axes**: they don't rotate, only scale
- If $|\lambda_1| > |\lambda_2|$, repeated application of $A$ collapses everything toward $\mathbf{x}_1$ (the dominant eigenvector)
- If $\lambda_1 > 0 > \lambda_2$, the matrix is a saddle: stretching along $\mathbf{x}_1$, compressing along $\mathbf{x}_2$

This geometric picture extends to $n$ dimensions — eigenvectors span the directions of pure scaling, everything else is a linear combination.

## Connections to the Rest of Linear Algebra

**Null space and zero eigenvalues.** $\lambda = 0$ is an eigenvalue iff $A\mathbf{x} = 0$ has a nontrivial solution, i.e., $A$ is singular. The zero eigenspace _is_ the null space.

**Rank.** For a diagonalizable matrix, $\text{rank}(A) = $ number of nonzero eigenvalues (counted with multiplicity).

**Positive definiteness.** $A$ is positive definite iff all eigenvalues are positive (for symmetric $A$). The eigenvalues are the principal curvatures of the quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$.

**Projections.** A projection matrix $P^2 = P$ has eigenvalues only $0$ and $1$. Eigenvalue $1$: vectors already in the column space (fixed by projection). Eigenvalue $0$: vectors in the null space (annihilated by projection). Diagonalizable with $\Lambda = \text{diag}(1,\ldots,1,0,\ldots,0)$.

**Orthogonal matrices.** An orthogonal matrix $Q$ has $|{\lambda}| = 1$ for all eigenvalues. Over $\mathbb{C}$, the eigenvalues are on the unit circle. Real eigenvalues are $\pm 1$; complex ones come in pairs $e^{\pm i\theta}$ (corresponding to rotation by $\theta$ in some 2D plane).

## Things I Want to Come Back To

- Jordan normal form — the full story of what happens when diagonalization fails; every matrix is similar to a Jordan matrix over $\mathbb{C}$
- Cayley-Hamilton theorem: $p(A) = 0$ where $p$ is the characteristic polynomial — every matrix satisfies its own characteristic equation; proof using Jordan form or direct computation
- Schur decomposition: every matrix is unitarily similar to upper triangular over $\mathbb{C}$ (weaker than diagonalization, but always exists and is numerically friendly)
- Generalized eigenvectors and the exponential of a Jordan block: $e^{Jt}$ where $J$ is a Jordan block has polynomial factors in $t$ multiplying $e^{\lambda t}$
- Simultaneously diagonalizable matrices: $AB = BA$ iff $A$ and $B$ can be diagonalized in the same basis (assuming both are diagonalizable)

## Summary

Diagonalization is the factorization $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$, valid when $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. It turns a complicated matrix into a transparent one — in the eigenbasis, $A$ is just multiplication by scalars. The symmetric case ($A = Q\Lambda Q^T$, orthonormal basis, real eigenvalues) is the cleanest version and underlies the spectral theorem, PCA, and the theory of quadratic forms. When diagonalization fails, Jordan form takes over. Powers, exponentials, stability, and projection all reduce to eigenvalue reading once the decomposition is in hand.
