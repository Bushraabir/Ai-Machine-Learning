
> _The identity matrix does nothing — and that makes it the most useful matrix in linear algebra. Change of basis is just the identity transformation written in two different languages simultaneously._

---

## The Identity Transformation

The identity transformation $I : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ sends every vector to itself:

$$ I(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x} \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n $$

Its matrix — relative to the standard basis — is the $n \times n$ identity matrix:

$$ I_n = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Columns are the standard basis vectors $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_n$. This is always the pattern: the $j$-th column of any transformation matrix is the image of $\mathbf{e}_j$. Since the identity fixes every vector, the $j$-th column is just $\mathbf{e}_j$.

**Properties** (all immediate from definition):

- $IA = A$ and $AI = A$ for any compatible $A$
- $I^{-1} = I$
- $\det(I) = 1$
- $I$ is symmetric, orthogonal, positive definite — it satisfies every niceness condition in the book

None of these are interesting on their own. The identity earns its place through change of basis.

---

## Coordinates are a Choice

This is the point that doesn't get made loudly enough in a first course.

A vector $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is a geometric object — an arrow. Its _coordinates_ are not intrinsic. They depend entirely on which basis you use to measure it.

Given a basis $\mathcal{B} = {\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n}$ for $\mathbb{R}^n$, every vector $\mathbf{v}$ has a unique representation:

$$ \mathbf{v} = c_1 \mathbf{b}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{b}_2 + \cdots + c_n \mathbf{b}_n $$

The coordinate vector of $\mathbf{v}$ relative to $\mathcal{B}$ is $[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}} = \begin{bmatrix} c_1 \ c_2 \ \vdots \ c_n \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

When we write $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$ we are implicitly using the standard basis $\mathcal{E} = {\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2}$. The numbers $3$ and $1$ are not the vector — they are instructions: "go $3$ units in the $\mathbf{e}_1$ direction, $1$ unit in the $\mathbf{e}_2$ direction." A different basis gives different instructions for the same arrow.

---

## The Change of Basis Matrix

Let $\mathcal{B} = {\mathbf{b}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n}$ be a basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$, with the $\mathbf{b}_j$ expressed in standard coordinates.

**Definition.** The change of basis matrix from $\mathcal{B}$-coordinates to standard coordinates is:

$$ P = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{b}_1 & \mathbf{b}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{b}_n \end{bmatrix} $$

That is, the columns of $P$ are the basis vectors of $\mathcal{B}$, written in standard coordinates.

**The conversion formulas are then:**

$$ \mathbf{v} = P [\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}} \qquad \text{(}\mathcal{B}\text{-coords to standard)} $$

$$ [\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}} = P^{-1} \mathbf{v} \qquad \text{(standard to }\mathcal{B}\text{-coords)} $$

$P$ is invertible because the $\mathbf{b}_j$ are linearly independent (they form a basis). So $P^{-1}$ always exists.

---

## Why $P$ is the Identity in Disguise

Here is the conceptual heart.

The identity transformation $I : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ sends every vector to itself. But if the input is measured in $\mathcal{B}$-coordinates and the output is measured in standard coordinates, then the matrix of this "do-nothing" transformation is $P$.

More precisely: $P$ represents the identity map

$$ I : (\mathbb{R}^n, \text{basis } \mathcal{B}) \longrightarrow (\mathbb{R}^n, \text{basis } \mathcal{E}) $$

The same vector, described first in one language, then in another. The transformation does nothing to the geometric object. Only the description changes.

This is why change of basis matrices are always invertible: the inverse map is $I$ in the other direction, represented by $P^{-1}$.

## A Worked Example — Full Matrices Written Out

Let $\mathcal{B} = \left{ \mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2 \right}$ where:

$$ \mathbf{b}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{b}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 3 \ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**Step 1.** Write down $P$:

$$ P = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

**Step 2.** Find $P^{-1}$.

$$ \det(P) = (1)(1) - (3)(2) = 1 - 6 = -5 $$

$$ P^{-1} = \frac{1}{-5} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -3 \ -2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -\tfrac{1}{5} & \tfrac{3}{5} \[4pt] \tfrac{2}{5} & -\tfrac{1}{5} \end{bmatrix} $$

**Step 3.** Convert a vector. Let $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \ 3 \end{bmatrix}$ in standard coordinates. Find $[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}}$:

$$ [\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}} = P^{-1} \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} -\tfrac{1}{5} & \tfrac{3}{5} \[4pt] \tfrac{2}{5} & -\tfrac{1}{5} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 5 \ 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 + \tfrac{9}{5} \[4pt] 2 - \tfrac{3}{5} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{4}{5} \[4pt] \tfrac{7}{5} \end{bmatrix} $$

**Verification.** Check $P[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}} = \mathbf{v}$:

$$ \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{4}{5} \[4pt] \tfrac{7}{5} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{4}{5} + \tfrac{21}{5} \[4pt] \tfrac{8}{5} + \tfrac{7}{5} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 \ 3 \end{bmatrix} \checkmark $$

So $\mathbf{v} = \frac{4}{5}\mathbf{b}_1 + \frac{7}{5}\mathbf{b}_2$. Same vector, two sets of coordinates.

## Changing Between Two Non-Standard Bases

Suppose you have two bases $\mathcal{B}$ and $\mathcal{C}$, neither of which is the standard basis. How do you convert $[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}}$ to $[\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{C}}$?

Route through the standard basis:

$$ [\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{C}} = P_{\mathcal{C}}^{-1} , P_{\mathcal{B}} , [\mathbf{v}]_{\mathcal{B}} $$

where $P_{\mathcal{B}}$ has columns $\mathbf{b}_j$ in standard coordinates, and $P_{\mathcal{C}}$ has columns $\mathbf{c}_j$ in standard coordinates.

The matrix $P_{\mathcal{C}}^{-1} P_{\mathcal{B}}$ is the change of basis matrix from $\mathcal{B}$ to $\mathcal{C}$. It is a product of two "identity transformations" composed: first decode from $\mathcal{B}$ into standard, then encode into $\mathcal{C}$.

---

## Transformations in a New Basis — The Similarity Formula

This is where change of basis becomes genuinely powerful.

Suppose $T : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ has matrix $A$ in the standard basis. What is the matrix of $T$ in basis $\mathcal{B}$?

The matrix of $T$ in $\mathcal{B}$-coordinates is:

$$ [T]_{\mathcal{B}} = P^{-1} A P $$

**Why.** To apply $T$ in $\mathcal{B}$-coordinates: first convert from $\mathcal{B}$ to standard ($P$), then apply $T$ ($A$), then convert back from standard to $\mathcal{B}$ ($P^{-1}$). The composition is $P^{-1} A P$.

Two matrices $A$ and $B$ are called **similar** if $B = P^{-1}AP$ for some invertible $P$. Similar matrices represent the same linear transformation — just in different coordinate systems. They have the same eigenvalues, the same determinant, the same rank, the same trace. The coordinate system is not intrinsic to the map.

The entire theory of diagonalization is the statement: find a basis $\mathcal{B}$ — the eigenvectors — in which $[T]_{\mathcal{B}}$ is diagonal. That is, find $P$ such that $P^{-1}AP = \Lambda$, a diagonal matrix of eigenvalues.

---

## The Orthogonal Case

When $\mathcal{B}$ is an orthonormal basis — each $\mathbf{b}_j$ has unit length, all $\mathbf{b}_j$ are mutually perpendicular — the change of basis matrix $P$ satisfies $P^T P = I$, so $P^{-1} = P^T$.

This makes everything cheaper:

- No matrix inversion needed; just transpose
- $P$ is an isometry: it preserves lengths and angles
- The similarity formula becomes $P^T A P$

This is the case that appears throughout applied math. The spectral theorem says symmetric matrices can always be diagonalized with an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors — meaning $A = P \Lambda P^T$ with $P$ orthogonal. Computationally and theoretically, orthonormal bases are the "right" choice whenever you can arrange it.

---

## The $3 \times 3$ Orthonormal Example

Let $\mathcal{B} = {\mathbf{b}_1, \mathbf{b}_2, \mathbf{b}_3}$ with:

$$ \mathbf{b}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\1\0\end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\-1\0\end{bmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b}_3 = \begin{bmatrix}0\0\1\end{bmatrix} $$

Then:

$$ P = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \[4pt] \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \[4pt] 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

Check: $P^T P = I_3$. (Verify by multiplying — the columns are orthonormal so the dot products land on $\delta_{ij}$.)

Therefore $P^{-1} = P^T$:

$$ P^{-1} = P^T = \begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \[4pt] \tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -\tfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & 0 \[4pt] 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

In this case $P = P^T$, which happens because the basis vectors happen to be symmetric. In general $P \neq P^T$ even when $P$ is orthogonal.

---

## What Changes and What Doesn't

Under a change of basis $A \mapsto P^{-1}AP$, these are **preserved** (they are intrinsic to the transformation):

|Invariant|Why|
|---|---|
|Eigenvalues|$\det(P^{-1}AP - \lambda I) = \det(P^{-1}(A - \lambda I)P) = \det(A - \lambda I)$|
|Determinant|$\det(P^{-1}AP) = \det(P^{-1})\det(A)\det(P) = \det(A)$|
|Trace|$\text{tr}(P^{-1}AP) = \text{tr}(APP^{-1}) = \text{tr}(A)$ (cyclic property)|
|Rank|Same column space dimension, just re-expressed|
|Null space dimension|Rank-nullity forces it|

These are **not** preserved in general:

- The eigenvectors (they change — that's the whole point of choosing a new basis)
- Individual matrix entries
- Whether the matrix is diagonal (unless you chose $P$ correctly)

## Connection to Strang and 18.06

In 18.06, this material lives in Chapter 6 alongside eigenvalues. The message Strang drives home: the "right" basis for a linear transformation is the eigenbasis. In that basis, the transformation is diagonal — each direction gets scaled independently, no mixing. The change of basis matrix $P$ that diagonalizes $A$ has the eigenvectors as its columns.

The identity transformation appears earlier (Chapter 2) when Strang discusses elimination — the elimination matrices $E_{ij}$ can be thought of as "almost identity" matrices, the identity with one off-diagonal entry. Recognizing $I$ as the baseline from which all other matrices deviate is a recurring theme.

## Things to Sit With

- Prove that similar matrices have the same characteristic polynomial. The computation is short but worth doing once with your own hand.
- Why does the trace equal the sum of eigenvalues? Connect the cyclic property of trace to the fact that the trace of a diagonal matrix is the sum of its diagonal entries.
- What goes wrong if $P$ is not invertible? The "basis" $\mathcal{B}$ would be linearly dependent — not actually a basis. The formula $P^{-1}AP$ requires $P$ to be invertible. There is no change of basis to a non-basis.
- Convince yourself geometrically: in $\mathbb{R}^2$, pick a basis of two non-perpendicular vectors. Draw a vector. Write its $\mathcal{B}$-coordinates. The numbers are not the projections onto the basis vectors — they are the coefficients in the combination. These are different things (unless the basis is orthonormal).
