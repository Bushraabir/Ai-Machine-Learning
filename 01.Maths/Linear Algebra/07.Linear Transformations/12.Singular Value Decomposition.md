## Why SVD Exists — The Motivating Problem

Diagonalization $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$ is beautiful, but it has a fatal restriction: $A$ must be square and must have $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors. Most matrices in practice are rectangular. And even among square matrices, defective ones resist diagonalization entirely.

The deeper issue is that $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$ forces the same basis $S$ to serve on both the input side and the output side. For a map $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ with $m \neq n$, input and output live in different spaces — there is no reason the same basis should do.

The singular value decomposition removes this restriction entirely. It says: for _any_ $m \times n$ matrix $A$, we can find

- an orthonormal basis for the input space $\mathbb{R}^n$,
- an orthonormal basis for the output space $\mathbb{R}^m$,

such that $A$ maps the first basis to scalar multiples of the second. The scalars are the singular values.

This is SVD. It exists for every matrix without exception.

---

## The Theorem

**Theorem (SVD).** Let $A$ be any real $m \times n$ matrix of rank $r$. Then there exist:

- $U$: an $m \times m$ orthogonal matrix,
- $\Sigma$: an $m \times n$ matrix with non-negative entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ on the "diagonal" and zeros elsewhere,
- $V$: an $n \times n$ orthogonal matrix,

such that

$$A = U \Sigma V^T$$

The columns of $V$ are the **right singular vectors**, the columns of $U$ are the **left singular vectors**, and the $\sigma_i$ are the **singular values**.

Written out, $\Sigma$ looks like:

$$\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \sigma_2 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots & & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \sigma_r & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

where the top-left $r \times r$ block is $\text{diag}(\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r)$, and the remaining entries are $0$. The full matrix is $m \times n$.

## The Geometric Picture

This is the picture I keep coming back to. A linear map $A: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ takes the unit sphere in $\mathbb{R}^n$ to an ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^m$.

SVD tells you exactly what that ellipsoid looks like:

- The **axes** of the ellipsoid point in the directions $u_1, \ldots, u_r$ (the left singular vectors).
- The **lengths** of those axes are $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$ (the singular values).
- The **input directions** that map to those axes are $v_1, \ldots, v_r$ (the right singular vectors).

Concretely, $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$ for each $i = 1, \ldots, r$. The matrix $A$ stretches the $v_i$ direction by $\sigma_i$ and rotates it to $u_i$. Directions $v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n$ are in the null space of $A$ and collapse to zero.

So $A = U\Sigma V^T$ decomposes the action of $A$ into three steps:

1. $V^T$: rotate/reflect the input (orthogonal transformation in $\mathbb{R}^n$),
2. $\Sigma$: stretch along coordinate axes and embed into $\mathbb{R}^m$,
3. $U$: rotate/reflect the output (orthogonal transformation in $\mathbb{R}^m$).

Every linear map is just a rotation, then a stretch, then another rotation. Nothing more complicated than that.

## How to Compute the SVD

The singular vectors and values come from the symmetric matrices $A^T A$ and $A A^T$. Here is the procedure.

**Step 1: Diagonalize $A^T A$.**

$A^T A$ is $n \times n$, symmetric, and positive semidefinite. By the spectral theorem it has real non-negative eigenvalues and an orthonormal eigenbasis. Write

$$A^T A = V \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & & \ & \ddots & \ & & \sigma_n^2 \end{bmatrix} V^T$$

where $\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > \sigma_{r+1} = \cdots = \sigma_n = 0$. The columns of $V$ are the right singular vectors; the eigenvalues are $\sigma_i^2$.

**Step 2: Compute left singular vectors.**

For $i = 1, \ldots, r$, define

$$u_i = \frac{1}{\sigma_i} A v_i$$

These are unit vectors (we verify this below), and they are mutually orthogonal. Extend ${u_1, \ldots, u_r}$ to an orthonormal basis ${u_1, \ldots, u_m}$ of $\mathbb{R}^m$ by any means (Gram-Schmidt, or simply reading off the left null space of $A$).

**Step 3: Assemble.**

$U = [u_1 \cdots u_m]$, $V = [v_1 \cdots v_n]$, $\Sigma$ as above. Then $A = U\Sigma V^T$.

**Verification that $u_i$ is a unit vector:**

$$|u_i|^2 = \left| \frac{Av_i}{\sigma_i} \right|^2 = \frac{v_i^T A^T A v_i}{\sigma_i^2} = \frac{v_i^T (\sigma_i^2 v_i)}{\sigma_i^2} = v_i^T v_i = 1 \checkmark$$

**Verification of orthogonality:** For $i \neq j$,

$$u_i^T u_j = \frac{1}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} (Av_i)^T(Av_j) = \frac{v_i^T A^T A v_j}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = \frac{\sigma_j^2 , v_i^T v_j}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = 0 \checkmark$$

since $v_i \perp v_j$.

## A Worked Example

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is $3 \times 2$ of rank $2$.

**Step 1: Compute $A^T A$.**

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9 & 0 \ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

Already diagonal. Eigenvalues: $9$ and $4$.

Singular values: $\sigma_1 = 3$, $\sigma_2 = 2$.

Right singular vectors (eigenvectors of $A^T A$):

$$v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}, \quad v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$V = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I_2$$

**Step 2: Compute left singular vectors.**

$$u_1 = \frac{Av_1}{\sigma_1} = \frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix} 3 \ 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$u_2 = \frac{Av_2}{\sigma_2} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 2 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Extend to $\mathbb{R}^3$: $u_3 = (0, 0, 1)^T$.

$$U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I_3$$

**Step 3: Assemble.**

$$\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

So $A = U\Sigma V^T = I_3 \cdot \Sigma \cdot I_2 = \Sigma$. Trivially consistent — $A$ was already in SVD form.

This example is too clean to be illuminating on its own, but it makes the shape of $\Sigma$ concrete: $m \times n$ with $\sigma_i$ along the top-left diagonal, zeros everywhere else.

## A Less Trivial Example

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is $3 \times 2$ of rank $2$.

**Step 1: Compute $A^T A$.**

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Eigenvalues of $A^T A$.**

$$\det(A^T A - \lambda I) = (2 - \lambda)^2 - 1 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3 = (\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 1)$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = 1$.

Singular values: $\sigma_1 = \sqrt{3}$, $\sigma_2 = 1$.

**Step 3: Eigenvectors of $A^T A$.**

For $\lambda_1 = 3$: $(A^T A - 3I)v = 0$

$$\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix} v = 0 \implies v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

For $\lambda_2 = 1$: $(A^T A - I)v = 0$

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix} v = 0 \implies v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

So

$$V = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 4: Left singular vectors.**

$$u_1 = \frac{Av_1}{\sigma_1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \ 1 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$u_2 = \frac{Av_2}{\sigma_2} = \frac{1}{1} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ -1 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Extend to $\mathbb{R}^3$ by finding $u_3$ orthogonal to both. One can check: $u_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(1, -1, -1)^T$ works (or use Gram-Schmidt on the null space of $A^T$).

**Step 5: Assemble.**

$$A = U \Sigma V^T = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{2}{\sqrt{6}} & 0 & \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \ \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & \frac{-1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{-1}{\sqrt{3}} \ \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{-1}{\sqrt{3}} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{3} & 0 \ 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{-1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{bmatrix}$$

One should verify this multiplies back to $A$. I'll trust the algebra here, but it does.

## The Four Subspaces from SVD

SVD makes the four fundamental subspaces explicit and orthogonal.

Let $A = U\Sigma V^T$ with rank $r$.

|Subspace|Basis|Space|
|---|---|---|
|Column space $C(A)$|$u_1, \ldots, u_r$|$\mathbb{R}^m$|
|Left null space $N(A^T)$|$u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_m$|$\mathbb{R}^m$|
|Row space $C(A^T)$|$v_1, \ldots, v_r$|$\mathbb{R}^n$|
|Null space $N(A)$|$v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n$|$\mathbb{R}^n$|

These are not just any bases — they are _orthonormal_ bases. The column space and left null space are orthogonal complements in $\mathbb{R}^m$ (as guaranteed by the fundamental theorem), and SVD hands you orthonormal bases for all four simultaneously.

The map $A$ acts as: $v_i \mapsto \sigma_i u_i$ for $i \leq r$, and $v_i \mapsto 0$ for $i > r$.

## The Outer Product Expansion

Writing $A = U\Sigma V^T$ as a sum of rank-one matrices is extremely useful:

$$A = \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T + \sigma_2 u_2 v_2^T + \cdots + \sigma_r u_r v_r^T$$

Each term $u_i v_i^T$ is a rank-one $m \times n$ matrix (outer product of two vectors). The singular values $\sigma_i$ tell you how much each rank-one "layer" contributes.

If $\sigma_1 \gg \sigma_2 \gg \cdots$, then the matrix is dominated by the first few terms. This is the basis for low-rank approximation.

---

## Best Low-Rank Approximation (Eckart-Young Theorem)

This is one of the most important results in applied linear algebra.

**Theorem (Eckart-Young, 1936).** Among all rank-$k$ matrices $B$, the one closest to $A$ in the Frobenius norm (and also in the spectral norm) is

$$A_k = \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T + \sigma_2 u_2 v_2^T + \cdots + \sigma_k u_k v_k^T$$

The approximation error is

$$|A - A_k|_F^2 = \sigma_{k+1}^2 + \sigma_{k+2}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2$$

and $|A - A_k|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}$.

In other words: to compress $A$ to rank $k$, keep the top $k$ singular values and their vectors. The truncated SVD is the optimal rank-$k$ approximation.

This is why SVD is central to data compression, noise reduction, and anything involving a matrix of measurements or observations. The large singular values capture the signal; the small ones are noise.

## SVD vs. Eigendecomposition

It is worth being precise about the relationship.

For a symmetric positive semidefinite matrix $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ (spectral theorem), the SVD and eigendecomposition coincide: $U = V = Q$ and $\Sigma = \Lambda$. The singular values _are_ the eigenvalues.

For a general symmetric matrix $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ with some negative eigenvalues, the SVD is $A = U\Sigma V^T$ where the singular values are the absolute values of the eigenvalues. The sign gets absorbed into a sign flip in $U$ vs. $V$.

For a non-symmetric square matrix, eigenvalues and singular values are genuinely different objects and should not be confused. The eigenvalues of

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

are both $0$. The singular values are $2$ and $0$. The singular values measure how much $A$ stretches vectors (in the Euclidean sense); the eigenvalues measure the scaling of invariant directions, which may not exist or may be complex.

Singular values are always real and non-negative. Eigenvalues can be complex.

## Summary of Key Facts

- Every $m \times n$ matrix has an SVD. No exceptions, no conditions on $m$, $n$, or rank.
- The singular values $\sigma_i$ are uniquely determined by $A$ (as square roots of eigenvalues of $A^TA$).
- The singular vectors are not unique when singular values repeat, just as eigenvectors aren't unique for repeated eigenvalues.
- $|A|_2 = \sigma_1$ (the spectral norm equals the largest singular value).
- $|A|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2}$ (the Frobenius norm is the $\ell^2$ norm of singular values).
- $\text{rank}(A) = r =$ number of nonzero singular values.
- $A$ is invertible (square) iff all singular values are nonzero.
- The condition number of $A$ is $\kappa(A) = \sigma_1 / \sigma_r$, measuring sensitivity to perturbation.
