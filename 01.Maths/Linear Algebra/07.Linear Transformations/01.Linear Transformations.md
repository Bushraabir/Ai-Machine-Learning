
## Why this matters — before any definition

Every matrix $A$ is secretly a function. When I write $A\mathbf{x}$, I am not just doing arithmetic — I am _applying a rule_ that sends every vector $\mathbf{x}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$ to some vector in $\mathbb{R}^m$. The whole machinery of linear algebra — null spaces, eigenvalues, diagonalization — is really the study of what these functions _do_ to space.

The punchline I want to internalize: **linear transformations are the morphisms of linear algebra.** They are the maps that respect the structure. Everything else is a consequence of that.

---

## The Definition

Let $V$ and $W$ be vector spaces over a field $F$ (think $\mathbb{R}$ for now, but nothing stops us from working over $\mathbb{C}$ later).

**Definition.** A function $T: V \to W$ is a _linear transformation_ if it satisfies two axioms for all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in V$ and all scalars $c \in F$:

$$T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v}) \qquad \text{(additivity)}$$

$$T(c\mathbf{u}) = c, T(\mathbf{u}) \qquad \text{(homogeneity)}$$

These two can be collapsed into a single condition:

$$T(c\mathbf{u} + d\mathbf{v}) = c, T(\mathbf{u}) + d, T(\mathbf{v})$$

and by induction, $T$ preserves _all_ linear combinations:

$$T!\left(\sum_{i=1}^k c_i \mathbf{v}_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^k c_i, T(\mathbf{v}_i)$$

This is the essential point. A linear transformation is completely determined by what it does to a _basis_. Once I know $T(\mathbf{e}_1), T(\mathbf{e}_2), \ldots, T(\mathbf{e}_n)$, I know everything.

## Immediate consequences — things to prove, not memorize

**Proposition 1.** $T(\mathbf{0}) = \mathbf{0}$.

_Proof._ $T(\mathbf{0}) = T(0 \cdot \mathbf{0}) = 0 \cdot T(\mathbf{0}) = \mathbf{0}$. $\square$

This is a quick check: if a candidate map doesn't send zero to zero, it is not linear. Full stop.

**Proposition 2.** $T(-\mathbf{v}) = -T(\mathbf{v})$.

_Proof._ $T(-\mathbf{v}) = T((-1)\mathbf{v}) = (-1)T(\mathbf{v}) = -T(\mathbf{v})$. $\square$

## The matrix representation — the big theorem

This is where abstraction meets computation.

**Theorem.** Let $V = \mathbb{R}^n$ with standard basis ${\mathbf{e}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{e}_n}$ and $W = \mathbb{R}^m$. Every linear transformation $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is of the form $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$ for a unique $m \times n$ matrix $A$.

_Proof._ Write any $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ as $\mathbf{x} = x_1 \mathbf{e}_1 + \cdots + x_n \mathbf{e}_n$. By linearity:

$$T(\mathbf{x}) = x_1 T(\mathbf{e}_1) + x_2 T(\mathbf{e}_2) + \cdots + x_n T(\mathbf{e}_n)$$

Now define $A$ to be the matrix whose $j$-th column is $T(\mathbf{e}_j)$:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & & | \ T(\mathbf{e}_1) & T(\mathbf{e}_2) & \cdots & T(\mathbf{e}_n) \ | & | & & | \end{bmatrix}$$

Then the expression above is exactly matrix-vector multiplication $A\mathbf{x}$. Uniqueness: if $B\mathbf{x} = A\mathbf{x}$ for all $\mathbf{x}$, plug in $\mathbf{e}_j$ to get $B$'s columns equal $A$'s columns, so $B = A$. $\square$

**The moral.** To find the matrix of any linear transformation from $\mathbb{R}^n$, just feed in the standard basis vectors one at a time and record the outputs as columns.

## A worked example — rotation in $\mathbb{R}^2$

Let $T: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be counterclockwise rotation by angle $\theta$.

Feed in $\mathbf{e}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$. After rotation:

$$T(\mathbf{e}_1) = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta \ \sin\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

Feed in $\mathbf{e}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$. After rotation:

$$T(\mathbf{e}_2) = \begin{bmatrix} -\sin\theta \ \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

So the rotation matrix is:

$$R_\theta = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

Notice: $\det(R_\theta) = \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$. Rotations preserve area and orientation. That is not a coincidence — it is encoded in the determinant.

## Kernel and Image

These are the two fundamental subspaces of a linear map.

**Definition.** The _kernel_ (or null space) of $T: V \to W$ is:

$$\ker(T) = {\mathbf{v} \in V : T(\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}}$$

**Definition.** The _image_ (or range) of $T$ is:

$$\operatorname{im}(T) = {T(\mathbf{v}) : \mathbf{v} \in V} \subseteq W$$

**Proposition.** $\ker(T)$ is a subspace of $V$ and $\operatorname{im}(T)$ is a subspace of $W$.

_Proof (kernel)._ If $T(\mathbf{u}) = T(\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}$, then $T(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = T(\mathbf{u}) + T(\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}$, and $T(c\mathbf{u}) = cT(\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{0}$. Closed under addition and scalar multiplication, and contains $\mathbf{0}$. $\square$

In matrix language: $\ker(T) = N(A)$ (the null space) and $\operatorname{im}(T) = C(A)$ (the column space).

## The Rank-Nullity Theorem

This is one of the most elegant facts in linear algebra. I should know this cold.

**Theorem (Rank-Nullity).** Let $T: V \to W$ be a linear transformation with $\dim V = n < \infty$. Then:

$$\dim(\ker T) + \dim(\operatorname{im} T) = n$$

or equivalently, $\operatorname{nullity}(T) + \operatorname{rank}(T) = n$.

_Proof._ Let ${\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k}$ be a basis for $\ker(T)$. Extend it to a basis of $V$:

$${\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k, \mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_{n-k}}$$

I claim ${T(\mathbf{u}_1), \ldots, T(\mathbf{u}_{n-k})}$ is a basis for $\operatorname{im}(T)$.

_Spanning:_ Any $T(\mathbf{x})$ with $\mathbf{x} = \sum c_i \mathbf{v}_i + \sum d_j \mathbf{u}_j$ gives

$$T(\mathbf{x}) = \sum c_i \underbrace{T(\mathbf{v}_i)}_{=\mathbf{0}} + \sum d_j T(\mathbf{u}_j) = \sum d_j T(\mathbf{u}_j)$$

so the $T(\mathbf{u}_j)$ span $\operatorname{im}(T)$.

_Independence:_ Suppose $\sum d_j T(\mathbf{u}_j) = \mathbf{0}$. Then $T!\left(\sum d_j \mathbf{u}_j\right) = \mathbf{0}$, so $\sum d_j \mathbf{u}_j \in \ker(T)$. Write it as a linear combination of the $\mathbf{v}_i$'s. But ${\mathbf{v}_i, \mathbf{u}_j}$ is a basis, so all coefficients are zero — in particular all $d_j = 0$.

Therefore $\dim(\operatorname{im} T) = n - k$ and $\dim(\ker T) = k$, giving $k + (n-k) = n$. $\square$

**What this says concretely.** For an $m \times n$ matrix $A$:

$$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n$$

Every "dimension lost" into the null space is a "dimension lost" from the output. The theorem is a conservation law.

## Composition and matrix multiplication — the real reason $AB$ is defined the way it is

If $T: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^n$ has matrix $B$ and $S: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ has matrix $A$, then the composition $S \circ T: \mathbb{R}^p \to \mathbb{R}^m$ is also linear (easy to check), and its matrix is $AB$.

This is _why_ matrix multiplication is defined the way it is. It is not an arbitrary rule. It is the formula that makes the correspondence between linear maps and matrices _preserve composition_.

$$\underbrace{(S \circ T)(\mathbf{x})}_{\text{composition of maps}} = \underbrace{A(B\mathbf{x})} = \underbrace{(AB)\mathbf{x}}_{\text{product of matrices}}$$

The non-commutativity of matrix multiplication ($AB \neq BA$ in general) reflects the non-commutativity of function composition — rotate then reflect is different from reflect then rotate.

## Invertible transformations

$T: V \to W$ is _invertible_ if there exists $T^{-1}: W \to V$ with $T^{-1} \circ T = \operatorname{id}_V$ and $T \circ T^{-1} = \operatorname{id}_W$.

**Proposition.** $T$ is invertible if and only if it is bijective (injective and surjective).

Injective $\iff$ $\ker(T) = {\mathbf{0}}$.  
Surjective $\iff$ $\operatorname{im}(T) = W$.

For a square matrix $A$ ($n \times n$): $A$ is invertible $\iff$ $\ker(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$ $\iff$ $\operatorname{rank}(A) = n$ $\iff$ $\det(A) \neq 0$. These are all equivalent. I should be able to go between them without thinking.

## General vector spaces — the abstract version

Nothing above required $V = \mathbb{R}^n$. Here are the examples I should keep in mind:

|Domain $V$|Target $W$|Map $T$|Kernel|
|---|---|---|---|
|$\mathcal{P}_n$ (polynomials deg $\le n$)|$\mathcal{P}_{n-1}$|$T(p) = p'$ (differentiation)|constant polynomials|
|$C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$|$C^\infty(\mathbb{R})$|$T(f) = f'$|constant functions|
|$\mathbb{R}^n$|$\mathbb{R}$|$T(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{c}^T \mathbf{x}$|a hyperplane|
|$M_{n \times n}$|$\mathbb{R}$|$T(A) = \operatorname{tr}(A)$|traceless matrices|

Differentiation is linear. Integration (as a map from $C[a,b]$ to $\mathbb{R}$) is linear. The transpose map $A \mapsto A^T$ is linear. These are not exotic — they are everywhere.

## The matrix of $T$ in a general basis

Suppose $V$ has basis $\mathcal{B} = {\mathbf{b}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{b}_n}$ and $W$ has basis $\mathcal{C} = {\mathbf{c}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{c}_m}$.

To find the matrix $[T]_{\mathcal{C}}^{\mathcal{B}}$ representing $T$ with respect to these bases:

1. Compute $T(\mathbf{b}_j)$ for each $j$.
2. Express $T(\mathbf{b}_j)$ as a linear combination of the $\mathbf{c}_i$'s: $T(\mathbf{b}_j) = \sum_i a_{ij} \mathbf{c}_i$.
3. The column $j$ of the matrix is $(a_{1j}, a_{2j}, \ldots, a_{mj})^T$.

The standard basis case is just this with all basis vectors being $\mathbf{e}_i$, which is why the columns are exactly $T(\mathbf{e}_j)$.

This setup leads directly to the change of basis formula: if $P$ is the change-of-basis matrix, then for a transformation $T: V \to V$:

$$[T]_{\mathcal{B}} = P^{-1} [T]_{\text{std}} P$$

Two matrices related this way are called _similar_. Similar matrices represent the _same linear transformation_ in different bases. This is the right way to think about similarity — it is a statement about the underlying map, not just the matrix.

## Things I keep confusing — honest notes to self

- $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ has an $m \times n$ matrix. The _output_ dimension determines the _rows_. I mix this up when I'm tired.
- The image lives in $W$, not $V$. The kernel lives in $V$. They are subspaces of different spaces.
- $T$ injective means $\ker(T)$ is trivial. $T$ surjective means $\operatorname{im}(T) = W$. For square transformations on finite-dimensional spaces, one implies the other. Not true in infinite dimensions — worth remembering.
- Differentiation $D: \mathcal{P}_n \to \mathcal{P}_{n-1}$ is surjective but not injective (kernel = constants). Integration in the other direction is injective but not surjective. The rank-nullity theorem works out in both cases.
