## The Core Idea

Two matrices $A$ and $B$ are **similar** if there exists an invertible matrix $M$ such that

$$B = M^{-1} A M$$

This is the definition. Everything else in this note is unpacking what it _means_, why it matters, and what it tells us about the deep structure of a linear map.

The way I think about it: $A$ and $B$ are different _representations_ of the same linear transformation — just written in different bases. The matrix $M$ is the change-of-basis matrix that moves you between those two coordinate systems. $A$ is how the transformation looks in one basis; $B$ is how it looks in another. Same map, different clothes.

---

## The Change-of-Basis Interpretation

Fix a linear transformation $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$. In the standard basis ${e_1, \ldots, e_n}$, the matrix of $T$ is some $A$. Now suppose I want to re-express the same transformation in a new basis formed by the columns of $M$ — call them ${v_1, \ldots, v_n}$.

The recipe is:

1. Take a vector expressed in the new basis: multiply by $M$ to convert it to standard coordinates.
2. Apply $A$ (the transformation, in standard coordinates).
3. Convert back to the new basis: multiply by $M^{-1}$.

The composite is $M^{-1}AM$. That is $B$.

So $B = M^{-1}AM$ does _not_ define a new transformation. It defines the _same_ transformation, viewed from a different vantage point. This is the whole story, really. Everything downstream follows from this.

---

## Eigenvalues Are Preserved

This is the first big theorem and the reason similarity matters so much.

**Theorem.** If $B = M^{-1}AM$, then $A$ and $B$ have the same eigenvalues.

**Proof.** We look at the characteristic polynomial.

$$\det(B - \lambda I) = \det(M^{-1}AM - \lambda I)$$

Since $\lambda I = \lambda M^{-1} I M = M^{-1}(\lambda I)M$, we can write

$$\det(B - \lambda I) = \det(M^{-1}(A - \lambda I)M)$$

Using multiplicativity of the determinant:

$$= \det(M^{-1}) \cdot \det(A - \lambda I) \cdot \det(M)$$

$$= \det(M^{-1}) \det(M) \cdot \det(A - \lambda I)$$

$$= \det(M^{-1}M) \cdot \det(A - \lambda I)$$

$$= \det(I) \cdot \det(A - \lambda I) = \det(A - \lambda I)$$

So the characteristic polynomials are identical, hence the eigenvalues are the same. $\blacksquare$

This should feel obvious from the change-of-basis story: eigenvalues of a linear transformation are intrinsic to the transformation itself, not to the coordinate system you use to describe it. Of course they're preserved.

What about eigenvectors? They change. If $Ax = \lambda x$, then in the new basis the eigenvector is $M^{-1}x$. The direction changes with the coordinates, but the eigenvalue — the scaling factor — does not.

## Diagonalization as a Special Case

The most important application of similarity: we want $B$ to be diagonal.

If $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, then we can take $M = S$, the matrix whose columns are those eigenvectors:

$$S = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & & | \ x_1 & x_2 & \cdots & x_n \ | & | & & | \end{bmatrix}$$

Then:

$$S^{-1}AS = \Lambda = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \lambda_2 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix}$$

This is the diagonalization $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$, which I've written before. But seen through the similarity lens it's cleaner: we are choosing the eigenvector basis precisely because the transformation _looks_ diagonal in that basis. The eigenvectors are the natural coordinate axes.

The change-of-basis matrix $M = S$ takes standard coordinates into eigenvector coordinates. In those coordinates, the map just scales each axis independently. That's all diagonalization is.

## A Worked Example

Let me do this with an explicit $2 \times 2$ matrix so the algebra is honest.

Take

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 1: Find eigenvalues.**

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \det \begin{bmatrix} 2 - \lambda & 1 \ 0 & 3 - \lambda \end{bmatrix} = (2-\lambda)(3-\lambda)$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 2$, $\lambda_2 = 3$.

**Step 2: Find eigenvectors.**

For $\lambda_1 = 2$:

$$(A - 2I)x = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} x = 0 \implies x_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

For $\lambda_2 = 3$:

$$(A - 3I)x = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} x = 0 \implies x_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Build $S$ and invert it.**

$$S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad S^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 4: Verify $S^{-1}AS = \Lambda$.**

$$S^{-1}AS = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

First compute $AS$:

$$AS = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

Then $S^{-1}(AS)$:

$$S^{-1}AS = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \Lambda$$

The diagonal entries are exactly the eigenvalues, in the order we stacked the eigenvectors. Good.

## When Does Diagonalization Fail?

Not every matrix is diagonalizable. The obstruction is a shortage of eigenvectors. Two cases:

**Case 1: Repeated eigenvalues with full eigenspace.** A scalar matrix like $cI$ is already diagonal and has all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ as its eigenspace. No problem.

**Case 2: Repeated eigenvalues with deficient eigenspace (defective matrices).** The classic example:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Both eigenvalues are $0$. The eigenspace for $\lambda = 0$ is spanned only by $e_1 = (1,0)^T$ — it is one-dimensional, not two. There is only one linearly independent eigenvector. You cannot fill the columns of $S$.

Such a matrix is not diagonalizable. It is similar to a _Jordan block_, which is the closest diagonal-like form available:

$$J = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

In this case $A$ already _is_ a Jordan block. More generally, every matrix is similar to its Jordan normal form — but that story is for a later note.

## Similar Does Not Mean Equal Entries

A common confusion worth stamping out. $A$ and $B$ being similar does not mean they are equal, or that they have the same entries, or even the same trace/determinant... wait, actually:

**Trace and determinant are preserved under similarity.**

Since similar matrices have the same eigenvalues (with multiplicity), and:

- $\text{tr}(A) = \sum \lambda_i$
- $\det(A) = \prod \lambda_i$

Both are determined by the eigenvalues, so they are similarity invariants.

Conversely, having the same trace and determinant does _not_ imply similarity. Take

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Both have $\text{tr} = 4$ and $\det = 4$ and both have $\lambda = 2$ (twice). But $A = 2I$ is similar only to itself (since $M^{-1}(2I)M = 2I$ for any $M$), while $B$ is a nontrivial Jordan block. They are not similar.

The _complete_ set of similarity invariants is the Jordan normal form — eigenvalues together with the sizes of Jordan blocks.

---

## Summary of Invariants Under Similarity

If $B = M^{-1}AM$, then $A$ and $B$ have the same:

- Eigenvalues (with multiplicity)
- Characteristic polynomial $\det(A - \lambda I)$
- Trace $= \sum \lambda_i$
- Determinant $= \prod \lambda_i$
- Rank
- Nullity
- Jordan normal form (up to reordering of blocks)

They do _not_ in general have the same eigenvectors, the same entries, or the same singular values.

---

## Why This Matters

The philosophical point: matrices are representations of linear maps with respect to a chosen basis. The choice of basis is arbitrary — it is a feature of how we coordinatize space, not a feature of the map. Similar matrices are just the same map in different coordinates.

This means any intrinsic property of the linear map — eigenvalues, rank, whether it's invertible, what its Jordan form looks like — must be a similarity invariant. Basis-dependent quantities (entries, singular values, norms) are not intrinsic and need not be preserved.

When I want to understand a linear map, I should always ask: can I find a basis in which the matrix looks simpler? Diagonalization is the answer when it's possible. Jordan form is the answer when it's not.
