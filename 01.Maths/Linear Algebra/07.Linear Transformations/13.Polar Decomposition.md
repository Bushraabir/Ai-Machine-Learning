## The Motivating Analogy

Every nonzero complex number $z \in \mathbb{C}$ can be written as

$$z = r e^{i\theta}$$

where $r = |z| \geq 0$ is the magnitude and $e^{i\theta}$ is a number of modulus $1$ — a pure rotation in the complex plane. This is the polar form of a complex number. It separates the "how much does $z$ scale things" part from the "how much does $z$ rotate things" part.

The polar decomposition of a matrix is exactly this, promoted to linear maps. Every square matrix $A$ can be written as

$$A = QS$$

where $Q$ is orthogonal (pure rotation/reflection) and $S$ is symmetric positive semidefinite (pure stretching). Alternatively,

$$A = S'Q$$

with $S'$ symmetric positive semidefinite and $Q$ the same orthogonal factor.

Left polar decomposition and right polar decomposition. Both exist. Both are unique when $A$ is invertible.

The analogy is not merely aesthetic. For $1 \times 1$ matrices (scalars), the polar decomposition literally reduces to $a = \text{sgn}(a) \cdot |a|$, which is the polar form $r e^{i\theta}$ restricted to the real line.

---

## The Theorem

**Theorem (Polar Decomposition).** Let $A$ be a real $n \times n$ matrix. Then:

1. There exist an orthogonal matrix $Q$ and a symmetric positive semidefinite matrix $S$ such that $$A = QS$$
2. There exist an orthogonal matrix $Q$ and a symmetric positive semidefinite matrix $S'$ such that $$A = S'Q$$
3. If $A$ is invertible, both decompositions are unique, and $S, S'$ are symmetric positive definite.

The two symmetric factors are related by $S' = QSQ^T$, so they are similar via an orthogonal conjugation — same eigenvalues, rotated eigenvectors.

---

## Construction from SVD

The polar decomposition is not an independent theorem. It follows directly from SVD, and the proof is constructive.

Let $A = U\Sigma V^T$ be the SVD of $A$, with $U, V$ orthogonal and $\Sigma$ diagonal with non-negative entries.

**Right polar decomposition $A = QS$:**

Write

$$A = U\Sigma V^T = (UV^T)(V\Sigma V^T)$$

Set $Q = UV^T$ and $S = V\Sigma V^T$.

- $Q = UV^T$ is orthogonal: $(UV^T)^T(UV^T) = VU^TUV^T = VV^T = I$. $\checkmark$
- $S = V\Sigma V^T$ is symmetric: $S^T = (V\Sigma V^T)^T = V\Sigma^T V^T = V\Sigma V^T = S$. $\checkmark$
- $S$ is positive semidefinite: for any $x$, $x^TSx = x^TV\Sigma V^Tx = |{\Sigma^{1/2}V^Tx}|^2 \geq 0$. $\checkmark$
- The eigenvalues of $S = V\Sigma V^T$ are exactly $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n$ — the singular values of $A$.

**Left polar decomposition $A = S'Q$:**

Write

$$A = U\Sigma V^T = (U\Sigma U^T)(UV^T)$$

Set $S' = U\Sigma U^T$ and $Q = UV^T$ (same $Q$).

- Same verification applies. Eigenvalues of $S'$ are also $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n$.

The symmetric factors $S$ and $S'$ have the same singular values as $A$ as their eigenvalues. The orthogonal factor $Q = UV^T$ is the same in both decompositions — it captures the rotational part of $A$.

---

## What $S$ and $Q$ Actually Are

It helps to have explicit formulas rather than just "follows from SVD."

Since $S = V\Sigma V^T$ and $S^2 = V\Sigma^2 V^T = V(A^TA \text{ diagonal part})V^T$... let me be more careful.

From $A = QS$ we get $A^T = S^TQ^T = SQ^{-1}$, so

$$A^TA = (QS)^T(QS) = S^TQ^TQS = S^2$$

Therefore $S = \sqrt{A^TA}$, the symmetric positive semidefinite square root of $A^TA$.

This is well-defined: $A^TA$ is symmetric positive semidefinite, so it has a unique symmetric positive semidefinite square root (diagonalize, take positive square roots of eigenvalues, conjugate back).

Similarly, $AA^T = S'^2$, so $S' = \sqrt{AA^T}$.

Once $S$ is known, $Q = AS^{-1}$ when $A$ is invertible. When $A$ is singular, $Q$ is not unique — the construction via SVD still works, but one must choose how to extend $Q$ on the null space.

---

## Uniqueness When $A$ Is Invertible

**Proof of uniqueness.** Suppose $A = Q_1 S_1 = Q_2 S_2$ with $Q_i$ orthogonal and $S_i$ symmetric positive definite. Then

$$A^TA = S_1^TQ_1^TQ_1S_1 = S_1^2$$ $$A^TA = S_2^TQ_2^TQ_2S_2 = S_2^2$$

So $S_1^2 = S_2^2$. Since $S_1, S_2$ are symmetric positive definite, their square roots are unique, so $S_1 = S_2$. Calling this $S$, we then have $Q_1 S = Q_2 S$, and since $S$ is invertible, $Q_1 = Q_2$. $\blacksquare$

If $A$ is singular, $S$ is positive semidefinite (not definite), $S$ is still unique (the square root of a PSD matrix is unique), but $Q$ is not — $Q$ can be arbitrary on the null space of $S$.

## A Worked Example

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 1: Compute $A^TA$.**

$$A^TA = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9 & 3 \ 3 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Find $S = \sqrt{A^TA}$.**

Eigenvalues of $A^TA$:

$$\det(A^TA - \lambda I) = (9-\lambda)(5-\lambda) - 9 = \lambda^2 - 14\lambda + 36$$

$$\lambda = \frac{14 \pm \sqrt{196 - 144}}{2} = \frac{14 \pm \sqrt{52}}{2} = 7 \pm \sqrt{13}$$

So $\lambda_1 = 7 + \sqrt{13}$, $\lambda_2 = 7 - \sqrt{13}$, and the singular values are $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$.

The symmetric factor is $S = V\text{diag}(\sigma_1, \sigma_2)V^T$ where $V$ diagonalizes $A^TA$.

For a $2\times 2$ matrix there is a closed-form expression for the square root of a PSD matrix. If $A^TA = P$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ and $\text{tr}(P) = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$, $\det(P) = \lambda_1\lambda_2$, then

$$\sqrt{P} = \frac{P + \sqrt{\det(P)}, I}{\sqrt{\text{tr}(P) + 2\sqrt{\det(P)}}}$$

Here $\det(A^TA) = 45 - 9 = 36$, so $\sqrt{\det} = 6$.

$$\text{tr}(A^TA) = 14, \quad \text{tr}(A^TA) + 2\sqrt{\det} = 14 + 12 = 26$$

$$S = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\left(\begin{bmatrix} 9 & 3 \ 3 & 5 \end{bmatrix} + 6I\right) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\begin{bmatrix} 15 & 3 \ 3 & 11 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Recover $Q = AS^{-1}$.**

First invert $S$. Since $S = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\begin{bmatrix}15 & 3 \ 3 & 11\end{bmatrix}$, we have

$$\det(S) = \frac{1}{26}(165 - 9) = \frac{156}{26} = 6$$

$$S^{-1} = \frac{1}{6} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{26}}{1} \begin{bmatrix} 11 & -3 \ -3 & 15 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{bmatrix} 11 & -3 \ -3 & 15 \end{bmatrix}$$

Wait — let me redo. For $S = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}M$ where $M = \begin{bmatrix}15 & 3 \ 3 & 11\end{bmatrix}$:

$$S^{-1} = \sqrt{26} \cdot M^{-1} = \sqrt{26} \cdot \frac{1}{\det M}\begin{bmatrix}11 & -3 \ -3 & 15\end{bmatrix}$$

$\det M = 165 - 9 = 156$.

$$S^{-1} = \frac{\sqrt{26}}{156}\begin{bmatrix}11 & -3 \ -3 & 15\end{bmatrix}$$

Then

$$Q = AS^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix}3 & 1 \ 0 & 2\end{bmatrix} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{26}}{156}\begin{bmatrix}11 & -3 \ -3 & 15\end{bmatrix} = \frac{\sqrt{26}}{156}\begin{bmatrix}30 & 6 \ -6 & 30\end{bmatrix} = \frac{\sqrt{26}}{156} \cdot 6\begin{bmatrix}5 & 1 \ -1 & 5\end{bmatrix}$$

$$Q = \frac{\sqrt{26}}{26}\begin{bmatrix}5 & 1 \ -1 & 5\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\begin{bmatrix}5 & 1 \ -1 & 5\end{bmatrix}$$

**Verification:** $Q^TQ = \frac{1}{26}\begin{bmatrix}5 & -1 \ 1 & 5\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}5 & 1 \ -1 & 5\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{26}\begin{bmatrix}26 & 0 \ 0 & 26\end{bmatrix} = I$. $\checkmark$

And $QS = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\begin{bmatrix}5 & 1 \ -1 & 5\end{bmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\begin{bmatrix}15 & 3 \ 3 & 11\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{26}\begin{bmatrix}78 & 26 \ 0 & 52\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}3 & 1 \ 0 & 2\end{bmatrix} = A$. $\checkmark$

## The Geometric Interpretation

This is the real payoff. What does $A = QS$ say about the geometry of the linear map $A$?

$S$ is symmetric positive semidefinite. Its eigenvectors form an orthonormal basis, and it scales space along those axes by its eigenvalues $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n$ (the singular values of $A$). There is no rotation in $S$ — it is a pure stretch along its principal axes.

$Q$ is orthogonal. It rotates (and possibly reflects) space. It preserves all lengths and angles. There is no stretching in $Q$.

So $A = QS$ says: first stretch $(\text{by } S)$, then rotate $(\text{by } Q)$. Every invertible linear map is a stretch followed by a rotation.

Alternatively $A = S'Q$: first rotate, then stretch.

Compare with the SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$: first rotate by $V^T$, then stretch by $\Sigma$, then rotate again by $U$. The polar decomposition collapses two of those steps into one symmetric matrix. The SVD tells you the principal stretching directions explicitly (the singular vectors); the polar decomposition tells you how to cleanly separate the rotational from the stretching parts.

For the example above: $Q = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\begin{bmatrix}5 & 1 \ -1 & 5\end{bmatrix}$ is a rotation by angle $\theta = \arctan(1/5)$, and $S = \frac{1}{\sqrt{26}}\begin{bmatrix}15 & 3 \ 3 & 11\end{bmatrix}$ does the stretching along its eigendirections.

## Connection to the Complex Polar Form, Made Precise

The analogy deserves to be made rigorous.

In $\mathbb{C}$, multiplication by $z = re^{i\theta}$ is a linear map $\mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ (identifying $\mathbb{C}$ with $\mathbb{R}^2$). The matrix of this map is

$$M_z = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix} \cdot \begin{bmatrix} r & 0 \ 0 & r \end{bmatrix} = r\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

That is $M_z = QS$ where $Q$ is a rotation matrix and $S = rI$ is a scalar multiple of the identity (trivially symmetric PSD). The polar decomposition of the $2\times 2$ matrix of complex multiplication is exactly the polar form of the complex number.

The generalization to $n \times n$ matrices replaces the single modulus $r$ with a full symmetric positive definite matrix $S$ (which may stretch by different amounts in different directions) and replaces the scalar phase $e^{i\theta}$ with a general orthogonal matrix $Q$.

---

## Polar Decomposition vs. SVD — When to Use Which

Both factorizations come from SVD. The choice depends on what you want to see.

SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$ is best when you want the principal stretching directions explicitly — $V$ tells you which input directions get stretched, $U$ tells you where they go, $\Sigma$ tells you by how much. It works for rectangular matrices.

Polar decomposition $A = QS$ is best when you want to isolate the rotational part of $A$ from the stretching part — for instance in continuum mechanics, where $A$ is the deformation gradient and you genuinely want to separate rigid rotation from strain. It requires $A$ to be square.

The symmetric factor $S = \sqrt{A^TA}$ is sometimes called the **right stretch tensor** in mechanics; $S' = \sqrt{AA^T}$ is the **left stretch tensor**; $Q = UV^T$ is the **rotation tensor**.

---

## When $A$ Is Symmetric Positive Definite

If $A$ itself is symmetric positive definite, then $A^TA = A^2$, so $S = \sqrt{A^2} = A$, and $Q = AS^{-1} = AA^{-1} = I$.

The polar decomposition of a symmetric positive definite matrix is $A = I \cdot A$. There is no rotational part — the map is a pure stretch along its eigenvectors. This makes sense: symmetric positive definite matrices have an orthonormal eigenbasis with positive eigenvalues, so they are precisely the "pure stretch" matrices.

---

## When $A$ Is Orthogonal

If $A$ is orthogonal, then $A^TA = I$, so $S = \sqrt{I} = I$, and $Q = AI^{-1} = A$.

The polar decomposition of an orthogonal matrix is $A = A \cdot I$. There is no stretching — the map is a pure rotation (or reflection). Singular values are all $1$, as expected.

## Summary

|Object|Role|Properties|
|---|---|---|
|$Q = UV^T$|Rotation/reflection|Orthogonal: $Q^TQ = I$|
|$S = V\Sigma V^T = \sqrt{A^TA}$|Right stretch|Symmetric PSD; eigenvalues $= \sigma_i(A)$|
|$S' = U\Sigma U^T = \sqrt{AA^T}$|Left stretch|Symmetric PSD; eigenvalues $= \sigma_i(A)$|

Key facts:

- $A = QS$ (right) $= S'Q$ (left). Both use the same $Q$.
- Uniqueness: $Q$ and $S$ are unique iff $A$ is invertible.
- $S$ and $S'$ have the same eigenvalues (the singular values of $A$), but different eigenvectors.
- When $A$ is symmetric PSD: $Q = I$, $S = A$.
- When $A$ is orthogonal: $S = I$, $Q = A$.
- Everything flows from the SVD. Polar decomposition is SVD with the two orthogonal factors partially recombined.
