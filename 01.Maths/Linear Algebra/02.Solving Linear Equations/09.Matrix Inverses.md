

## Why This Is Not Just Division

The first instinct is to think of $A^{-1}$ as "dividing by $A$." That instinct is wrong in an important way. Division of numbers works because every nonzero number has a multiplicative inverse, and multiplication of numbers commutes. Neither of these is true in general for matrices. The inverse, when it exists, is a separate object that needs to be constructed — it is not automatic, and it is not symmetric in left vs. right multiplication.

This note builds the theory carefully: definition, existence conditions, the product rule, and what breaks when things go singular.

---

## Definition

> **Definition.** A square matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is **invertible** (or **nonsingular**) if there exists a matrix $B \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ such that: $$AB = BA = I_n$$ In that case, $B$ is unique and is called the **inverse** of $A$, written $A^{-1}$.

Two things to note immediately:

**Uniqueness.** Suppose $AB = BA = I$ and $AC = CA = I$. Then: $$B = BI = B(AC) = (BA)C = IC = C$$ So the inverse is unique whenever it exists.

**Left inverse = right inverse.** If $AB = I$ and $A$ is square, then in fact $BA = I$ as well. This is not obvious — it follows from the rank-nullity theorem or from the theory of determinants. I will prove it below using the determinant characterization. For non-square matrices this fails entirely: a left inverse need not be a right inverse.

---

## The Inverse via Elimination: Gauss-Jordan

The computational method from 18.06: augment $A$ with $I$ and row-reduce.

$$\left( A \mid I \right) \xrightarrow{\text{row ops}} \left( I \mid A^{-1} \right)$$

**Why this works.** Each row operation is left-multiplication by an elementary matrix $E_i$. After all operations: $$E_k \cdots E_2 E_1 A = I \implies E_k \cdots E_1 = A^{-1}$$ The same product of elementary matrices, applied to the right side (which started as $I$), produces $A^{-1}$.

If at any point during elimination we get a zero row on the left side, then $A$ is singular and no inverse exists.

## Characterizations of Invertibility

The following are all equivalent for $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$:

1. $A$ is invertible
2. $\det(A) \ne 0$
3. $\text{rank}(A) = n$ (full rank)
4. The columns of $A$ are linearly independent
5. The rows of $A$ are linearly independent
6. The null space of $A$ is ${0}$: $Ax = 0 \implies x = 0$
7. $Ax = b$ has a unique solution for every $b \in \mathbb{R}^n$
8. $0$ is not an eigenvalue of $A$
9. $A^T$ is invertible

These are not seven separate facts — they are one fact stated seven ways. In practice, (2) is the easiest to check for small matrices, (3) is what elimination reveals, and (6) is the most useful conceptually.

## Singular vs. Invertible: The Divide

Let me be precise about what singular means, because "not invertible" can feel vague.

> **Definition.** $A$ is **singular** if it is square and $\det(A) = 0$, equivalently, if $\text{rank}(A) < n$.

### What Singular Looks Like Geometrically

The map $x \mapsto Ax$ takes $\mathbb{R}^n$ to the column space of $A$. If $A$ is invertible, this map is a bijection on $\mathbb{R}^n$ — it stretches, rotates, reflects, but is reversible. If $A$ is singular, the image is a proper subspace — the map collapses an entire subspace (the null space) to zero. Once you collapse a direction, you cannot reconstruct the original vector.

This is why $Ax = b$ may have no solution or infinitely many when $A$ is singular: the column space doesn't fill all of $\mathbb{R}^n$, so some $b$ are unreachable, and the null space gives free variables.

### Examples of Singular Matrices

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix} $$

Row 2 is twice row 1. $\det = 4 - 4 = 0$. The null space contains $\begin{pmatrix} 2 \ -1 \end{pmatrix}$.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Rank 2 matrix in $\mathbb{R}^{3 \times 3}$. Maps everything to the $xy$-plane. The $z$-direction is annihilated.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{pmatrix} \text{ is singular} \iff ad - bc = 0 \iff \text{columns are proportional} $$

### A Crucial Non-Example

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

$\det(A) = 1 \ne 0$, so invertible. Indeed:

$$ A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Verify: $AA^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$. Good.

## Inverse of a $2 \times 2$ Matrix

For the $2 \times 2$ case there is an explicit formula worth knowing cold:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{pmatrix} \implies A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \ -c & a \end{pmatrix} $$

provided $\det(A) = ad - bc \ne 0$.

**Proof.** Directly verify:

# $$ \begin{pmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{pmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \ -c & a \end{pmatrix}

# \frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{pmatrix} ad - bc & -ab + ba \ cd - dc & -cb + da \end{pmatrix}

\frac{1}{ad-bc} \begin{pmatrix} ad-bc & 0 \ 0 & ad-bc \end{pmatrix} = I $$

The pattern: swap the diagonal, negate the off-diagonal, divide by the determinant. Nothing analogous is so clean in dimension $\ge 3$.

## Inverse of a Product

> **Theorem (Reversal Law).** If $A$ and $B$ are both invertible $n \times n$ matrices, then $AB$ is invertible and: $$(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$$

This is one of the most important formulas in linear algebra. The order reverses.

**Proof.** We verify both $AB \cdot (B^{-1}A^{-1}) = I$ and $(B^{-1}A^{-1}) \cdot AB = I$.

$$ (AB)(B^{-1}A^{-1}) = A(BB^{-1})A^{-1} = A \cdot I \cdot A^{-1} = AA^{-1} = I $$

$$ (B^{-1}A^{-1})(AB) = B^{-1}(A^{-1}A)B = B^{-1} \cdot I \cdot B = B^{-1}B = I $$

Both sides give $I$, so by definition $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$. $\blacksquare$

### Why the Order Reverses

The right analogy is dressing and undressing. If you put on socks then shoes (operation $A$ then $B$), to undo this you take off shoes first, then socks. The last operation applied must be the first to be undone.

More formally: $AB$ means "first apply $B$, then apply $A$" (acting on column vectors on the right). To invert $AB$, you must first undo $A$ (apply $A^{-1}$), then undo $B$ (apply $B^{-1}$). So reading right to left: $(AB)^{-1}$ acts as $B^{-1}$ first, then $A^{-1}$, which is $B^{-1}A^{-1}$.

### Extension to Products of $k$ Matrices

By induction:

$$(A_1 A_2 \cdots A_k)^{-1} = A_k^{-1} \cdots A_2^{-1} A_1^{-1}$$

**Proof by induction.** Base case $k = 2$ is the theorem above. For the inductive step, write $A_1 \cdots A_k = (A_1 \cdots A_{k-1}) A_k$ and apply the $k = 2$ case:

$$ (A_1 \cdots A_k)^{-1} = A_k^{-1}(A_1 \cdots A_{k-1})^{-1} = A_k^{-1} A_{k-1}^{-1} \cdots A_1^{-1} $$

where the last step uses the inductive hypothesis. $\blacksquare$

### Determinant Consequence

From $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$ and the multiplicativity of the determinant:

$$\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$$

This is independent but consistent: $\det((AB)^{-1}) = \frac{1}{\det(A)\det(B)} = \det(B^{-1})\det(A^{-1})$, as expected.

## Other Inverse Rules

These follow from the definition and are worth having in one place.

**Inverse of transpose:** $$(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$$

**Proof.** We need to show $(A^{-1})^T \cdot A^T = I$: $$(A^{-1})^T A^T = (A A^{-1})^T = I^T = I$$ using the identity $(PQ)^T = Q^T P^T$. $\blacksquare$

So $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$, and one often writes this as $A^{-T}$.

**Inverse of a scalar multiple:** $$(\alpha A)^{-1} = \frac{1}{\alpha} A^{-1}, \quad \alpha \ne 0$$

**Proof.** $(\alpha A)\left(\frac{1}{\alpha}A^{-1}\right) = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha} A A^{-1} = I$. $\blacksquare$

**Inverse of inverse:** $$(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$$

This is immediate from the definition: if $AB = BA = I$, then $B^{-1} = A$.

**Power rule:** $$A^{-k} := (A^{-1})^k = (A^k)^{-1}, \quad k \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}$$

## Inverse via the Adjugate (Theoretical Formula)

For general $n \times n$ matrices, there is a closed-form formula:

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \text{adj}(A)$$

where $\text{adj}(A)$ is the **classical adjugate** (or adjoint) of $A$: the transpose of the cofactor matrix.

The $(i,j)$ entry of $\text{adj}(A)$ is $C_{ji}$, where $C_{ji} = (-1)^{j+i} M_{ji}$ and $M_{ji}$ is the $(j,i)$ minor (determinant of $A$ with row $j$ and column $i$ deleted).

**This formula is almost never used computationally** for $n \ge 3$ — it requires computing $n^2$ determinants of $(n-1) \times (n-1)$ matrices. Elimination is $O(n^3)$; the adjugate formula is $O(n! \cdot n^2)$ or worse. Its value is theoretical: it proves that $A^{-1}$ exists whenever $\det(A) \ne 0$, and it gives explicit rational functions of the entries of $A$.

For $2 \times 2$, the adjugate formula is exactly the swap-and-negate formula from above.

## Worked Example: Inverse of a $3 \times 3$ via Gauss-Jordan

Let:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 3 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Augment with the identity:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 2 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 3 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) $$

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \tfrac{1}{2}R_1$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 2 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & \tfrac{5}{2} & 1 & -\tfrac{1}{2} & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - \tfrac{2}{5}R_2$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 2 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & \tfrac{5}{2} & 1 & -\tfrac{1}{2} & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & \tfrac{8}{5} & \tfrac{1}{5} & -\tfrac{2}{5} & 1 \end{array}\right) $$

Now back-substitute (Jordan step — eliminate upward):

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \tfrac{5}{8}R_3$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 2 & 1 & 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & \tfrac{5}{2} & 0 & -\tfrac{5}{8} & \tfrac{6}{8} & -\tfrac{5}{8} \ 0 & 0 & \tfrac{8}{5} & \tfrac{1}{5} & -\tfrac{2}{5} & 1 \end{array}\right) $$

$R_1 \leftarrow R_1 - \tfrac{2}{5}R_2$ (to clear the $(1,2)$ entry):

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|ccc} 2 & 0 & 0 & \tfrac{5}{4} & -\tfrac{3}{4} & \tfrac{1}{2} \ 0 & \tfrac{5}{2} & 0 & -\tfrac{5}{8} & \tfrac{3}{4} & -\tfrac{5}{8} \ 0 & 0 & \tfrac{8}{5} & \tfrac{1}{5} & -\tfrac{2}{5} & 1 \end{array}\right) $$

Normalize each row:

$$ A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac{5}{8} & -\tfrac{3}{8} & \tfrac{1}{4} \[4pt] -\tfrac{1}{4} & \tfrac{3}{10} \cdot \tfrac{4}{1}? & ? \end{pmatrix} $$

Let me redo the normalization cleanly. $\det(A) = 2(6-1) - 1(2-0) = 10 - 2 = 8$.

Using the adjugate for verification:

$$ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{8}\begin{pmatrix} 5 & -2 & 1 \ -2 & 4 & -2 \ 1 & -2 & 5 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Verification** — check $(A^{-1}A)_{11}$:

$$ \frac{1}{8}(5 \cdot 2 + (-2) \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 0) = \frac{1}{8}(10 - 2) = 1 \checkmark $$

Check $(A^{-1}A)_{12}$:

$$ \frac{1}{8}(5 \cdot 1 + (-2) \cdot 3 + 1 \cdot 1) = \frac{1}{8}(5 - 6 + 1) = 0 \checkmark $$

The full product does give $I$ — I will trust the adjugate formula here since $\det = 8$ was verified independently.

## Worked Example: Inverse of a Product

Let:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Both have determinant $1 \cdot 1 - 0 = 1$ and $3 \cdot 2 - 0 = 6$ respectively, both nonzero.

$$ A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B^{-1} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

Compute $AB$:

$$ AB = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 4 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Direct inverse of $AB$: $\det(AB) = 10 - 4 = 6$.

$$ (AB)^{-1} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -4 \ -1 & 5 \end{pmatrix} $$

Now compute $B^{-1}A^{-1}$:

$$ B^{-1}A^{-1} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -4 \ -1 & 5 \end{pmatrix} $$

They agree, as the theorem guarantees. Note that $A^{-1}B^{-1}$ gives something different:

$$ A^{-1}B^{-1} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 4 & -6 \ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

which is not $(AB)^{-1}$. The order matters.

## What Breaks When $A$ Is Singular

If $A$ is singular, then:

- $Ax = 0$ has nontrivial solutions. Any candidate inverse $B$ would need $ABx = 0$ for all $x$, but $AB = I$ would require $x = 0$ for all $x$. Contradiction.
- More precisely: if $AB = I$ for some $B$, then $\det(A)\det(B) = 1$, so $\det(A) \ne 0$. Singular matrices can never have a right inverse (for square matrices).
- The system $Ax = b$ either has no solution (if $b \notin \text{col}(A)$) or infinitely many (if it has one — because you can add any element of the null space).

This is the core of the Fredholm alternative, which I should revisit when doing inner products.

## Left vs. Right Inverse for Non-Square Matrices

This is a clarification I keep needing to make:

- If $A$ is $m \times n$ with $m > n$ (tall matrix, overdetermined): $A$ can have a **left inverse** $L$ such that $LA = I_n$, but not a right inverse. The Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse gives the least-squares left inverse: $A^+ = (A^T A)^{-1}A^T$.
- If $A$ is $m \times n$ with $m < n$ (wide matrix, underdetermined): $A$ can have a **right inverse** $R$ such that $AR = I_m$, but not a left inverse.
- For square matrices: left inverse = right inverse = the inverse (as I proved at the top).

The pseudoinverse $A^+$ extends the concept of inverse to all matrices, including singular and non-square ones, but that is a separate note.

## Summary

|Concept|Key Formula / Condition|
|---|---|
|Invertible|$AB = BA = I$, equivalently $\det A \ne 0$|
|Singular|$\det A = 0$, null space nontrivial, rank $< n$|
|$2 \times 2$ inverse|$\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix}d&-b\-c&a\end{pmatrix}$|
|Inverse of product|$(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$ (order reverses)|
|Inverse of $k$ factors|$(A_1 \cdots A_k)^{-1} = A_k^{-1} \cdots A_1^{-1}$|
|Inverse of transpose|$(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$|
|General formula|$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A}\text{adj}(A)$ (theoretical)|
|Computation|Gauss-Jordan on $(A \mid I)$|

## Things to Come Back To

- The connection between invertibility and change of basis: $A^{-1}$ in the context of similar matrices $B = P^{-1}AP$ is the reason $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$ appears everywhere in the similarity transformation formula.
- Invertibility over other fields or rings: over $\mathbb{Z}$, the only invertible matrices have $\det = \pm 1$. Over $\mathbb{F}_2$, strange things happen. These come up in coding theory.
- The condition number $\kappa(A) = |A||A^{-1}|$: a matrix can be invertible but so nearly singular that $A^{-1}$ is numerically useless. Strang does not emphasize this but it matters in practice.
