## What Elimination Actually Is

Elimination is the systematic algorithm for solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$. Every student has seen it under the name "Gaussian elimination" or "row reduction." But I want to understand it as more than an algorithm — I want to understand what it is _doing_ to the matrix.

The core idea: apply a sequence of row operations to $A$ that preserve the solution set of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, and drive the system into upper triangular form. Once the system is triangular, back-substitution solves it immediately.

The deeper idea: elimination is actually computing the **$LU$ factorization** of $A$. The row operations I apply are being recorded as elementary matrices, and their product is the lower triangular matrix $L$. The end result of elimination is $U$ — the upper triangular matrix. So $A = LU$. Every step of elimination is a step toward this factorization.

---

## The Three Legal Row Operations

These are the only operations I am allowed to perform. Each one preserves the solution set of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.

**1. Row swap:** interchange two rows. $$R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$$

**2. Scalar multiplication:** multiply a row by a nonzero constant $c \neq 0$. $$R_i \leftarrow c \cdot R_i$$

**3. Row replacement (the workhorse):** replace a row by itself plus a multiple of another row. $$R_i \leftarrow R_i - \ell \cdot R_j, \quad j < i$$

Gaussian elimination uses almost exclusively the third operation. The multiplier $\ell$ is chosen to kill an entry below a pivot.

Why do these preserve the solution set? Each operation corresponds to multiplying both sides of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ by an invertible elementary matrix $E$ on the left: $EA\mathbf{x} = E\mathbf{b}$. Since $E$ is invertible, the new system has exactly the same solutions.

---

## The Pivot — The Central Object

The **pivot** is the first nonzero entry in a row after elimination has cleared everything below and to the left of it. Concretely: after I have eliminated all entries in column $j$ below row $i$, the entry $a_{ii}$ that was used to do the eliminating is the pivot for that step.

Pivots must be **nonzero**. This is not optional — the multiplier $\ell_{ij} = a_{ij} / a_{jj}$ requires dividing by the pivot. A zero pivot means I need to swap rows (a row exchange) to bring a nonzero entry into the pivot position. If no nonzero entry exists in that column, the matrix is singular and I cannot get a pivot there.

The pivots are the diagonal entries of $U$ after all row operations are complete. Their product is the determinant:

$$ \det(A) = \prod_i u_{ii} \cdot (\pm 1) $$

where the $\pm 1$ accounts for any row swaps.

---

## The Algorithm, Step by Step

I will run through elimination methodically. The input is $A$ and $\mathbf{b}$; I work on the augmented matrix $[A \mid \mathbf{b}]$.

**Step 1.** Use the $(1,1)$ entry as the first pivot. For each row $i > 1$, compute the multiplier: $$\ell_{i1} = \frac{a_{i1}}{a_{11}}$$ and replace $R_i \leftarrow R_i - \ell_{i1} \cdot R_1$. This kills every entry in column 1 below the pivot.

**Step 2.** Move to the $(2,2)$ entry (or whatever is now in the second pivot position). Repeat: compute multipliers, eliminate below.

**Step $k$.** At stage $k$, the pivot is the $(k,k)$ entry. Eliminate all entries in column $k$ below row $k$ using multipliers $\ell_{ik} = a_{ik}^{(k)} / a_{kk}^{(k)}$, where the superscript $(k)$ denotes the matrix after $k-1$ stages of elimination.

Continue until the matrix is upper triangular.

## A Full $3 \times 3$ Example

Solve the system:

$$ 2x + y - z = 8 $$ $$ -3x - y + 2z = -11 $$ $$ -2x + y + 2z = -3 $$

Write the augmented matrix:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \ -3 & -1 & 2 & -11 \ -2 & 1 & 2 & -3 \end{array}\right) $$

**Stage 1:** Pivot is $a_{11} = 2$.

Multipliers: $$\ell_{21} = \frac{-3}{2}, \qquad \ell_{31} = \frac{-2}{2} = -1$$

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \left(-\tfrac{3}{2}\right) R_1 = R_2 + \tfrac{3}{2} R_1$:

$$ -3 + \tfrac{3}{2}(2) = 0, \quad -1 + \tfrac{3}{2}(1) = \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad 2 + \tfrac{3}{2}(-1) = \tfrac{1}{2}, \quad -11 + \tfrac{3}{2}(8) = 1 $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - (-1) R_1 = R_3 + R_1$:

$$ -2 + 2 = 0, \quad 1 + 1 = 2, \quad 2 + (-1) = 1, \quad -3 + 8 = 5 $$

After Stage 1:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \ 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{1}{2} & 1 \ 0 & 2 & 1 & 5 \end{array}\right) $$

**Stage 2:** Pivot is $a_{22} = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

Multiplier: $$\ell_{32} = \frac{2}{1/2} = 4$$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 4 R_2$:

$$ 0, \quad 2 - 4\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = 0, \quad 1 - 4\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right) = -1, \quad 5 - 4(1) = 1 $$

After Stage 2 — the upper triangular system $U$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \ 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{1}{2} & 1 \ 0 & 0 & -1 & 1 \end{array}\right) $$

Three pivots: $2$, $\tfrac{1}{2}$, $-1$. All nonzero — $A$ is invertible.

**Back-substitution:**

From row 3: $-z = 1 \Rightarrow z = -1$.

From row 2: $\tfrac{1}{2}y + \tfrac{1}{2}(-1) = 1 \Rightarrow \tfrac{1}{2}y = \tfrac{3}{2} \Rightarrow y = 3$.

From row 1: $2x + 3 - (-1) = 8 \Rightarrow 2x = 4 \Rightarrow x = 2$.

Solution: $(x, y, z) = (2, 3, -1)$.

## Elimination as Matrix Multiplication — The $E$ Matrices

Each row operation "replace $R_i$ by $R_i - \ell R_j$" corresponds to left-multiplying by an elementary matrix $E_{ij}$. For a $3 \times 3$ system, the matrix that subtracts $\ell$ times row 1 from row 2 is:

$$ E_{21} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ -\ell_{21} & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Left-multiplying $A$ by $E_{21}$ does exactly the row operation. The full elimination is:

$$ E_{32} E_{31} E_{21} A = U $$

(applying operations right to left). So:

$$ A = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1} U = LU $$

The matrix $L = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1}$ is lower triangular. And crucially, inverting each $E_{ij}$ is trivial: just flip the sign of the multiplier.

$$ E_{21}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ \ell_{21} & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

The multipliers $\ell_{ij}$ drop directly into $L$ without any additional computation.

---

## The $LU$ Factorization

This is the payoff of the elimination viewpoint. Once I have run elimination (with no row swaps), the result is:

$$ A = LU $$

where:

$$ L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ \ell_{21} & 1 & 0 \ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad U = \begin{pmatrix} u_{11} & u_{12} & u_{13} \ 0 & u_{22} & u_{23} \ 0 & 0 & u_{33} \end{pmatrix} $$

$L$ is **unit lower triangular** (ones on the diagonal; the multipliers fill the lower triangle).

$U$ is **upper triangular** (the result of elimination; the pivots are on the diagonal).

For the example above, reading off the multipliers $\ell_{21} = -3/2$, $\ell_{31} = -1$, $\ell_{32} = 4$:

$$ L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ -\tfrac{3}{2} & 1 & 0 \ -1 & 4 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad U = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1 \ 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{1}{2} \ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Verification:** $LU$ should recover $A$. I trust the algebra but should check this at least once for a worked example.

---

## Why $LU$ and Not Just Elimination?

Once I have $A = LU$, solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ splits into two triangular systems:

$$ L\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{b} \quad \text{(forward substitution)} $$ $$ U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{c} \quad \text{(back substitution)} $$

Both are $O(n^2)$ to solve. The factorization itself costs $O(n^3)$ — and I only do it once. If I need to solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ for many different right-hand sides $\mathbf{b}$, I factor $A$ once and then solve each new $\mathbf{b}$ in $O(n^2)$ time. This is the practical reason $LU$ matters.

## When Elimination Breaks Down — Row Exchanges

If during elimination a zero appears in the pivot position, but nonzero entries exist below it in the same column, I can fix this with a **row swap**. The corrected algorithm that incorporates row swaps is **$PA = LU$ factorization**, where $P$ is a permutation matrix.

$$ PA = LU $$

$P$ records which row swaps were performed. It is an orthogonal matrix ($P^T = P^{-1}$) with exactly one $1$ per row and per column.

If no nonzero entry exists below a zero pivot position, then the matrix is singular — no row swap can save it. Elimination produces a row of zeros, and the rank is less than $n$.

**A zero pivot is a warning. No possible pivot is a verdict.**

---

## Failure — The Singular Case

Suppose after all row operations, the final matrix looks like:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \ 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{1}{2} & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & c \end{array}\right) $$

If $c \neq 0$: the third equation says $0 = c$. **No solution.** The right-hand side $\mathbf{b}$ is not in the column space of $A$.

If $c = 0$: the third equation says $0 = 0$. **Infinitely many solutions.** The variable corresponding to the missing third pivot is free — it can take any value, and the other variables adjust accordingly.

The number of pivots equals the rank $r$. The number of free variables is $n - r$.

---

## The Cost of Elimination

For an $n \times n$ matrix, how many operations does elimination require?

At stage $k$, I eliminate entries in column $k$ below the pivot. There are $n - k$ rows to update, and each row requires $n - k + 1$ multiplications and subtractions (updating the remaining $n - k + 1$ entries in that row, including the augmented column).

Total operations:

$$ \sum_{k=1}^{n-1} (n-k)(n-k+1) \approx \sum_{k=1}^{n} k^2 \approx \frac{n^3}{3} $$

So elimination costs $O(n^3)$ — roughly $n^3/3$ multiplications. For $n = 1000$, that is roughly $3 \times 10^8$ operations. For $n = 10^6$, elimination becomes impractical — this is why iterative methods exist.

Back-substitution costs $O(n^2)$, which is negligible compared to $n^3/3$.

## Summary — The Breakdown of Elimination

|Stage|What happens|Mathematical object|
|---|---|---|
|Write augmented matrix|Combine $A$ and $\mathbf{b}$|$[A \mid \mathbf{b}]$|
|Forward elimination|Apply row operations to reach triangular form|$E_{32}E_{31}E_{21}A = U$|
|Record multipliers|Fill lower triangle of $L$|$A = LU$|
|Handle zero pivots|Row swaps if needed|$PA = LU$|
|Back-substitution|Solve $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{c}$ from bottom up|Solution $\mathbf{x}$|
|Check for failure|Zero row in $U$: singular $A$|Rank $< n$|

The pivots are the irreducible core. Their count is the rank. Their product is the determinant. Their positions determine which variables are free.
