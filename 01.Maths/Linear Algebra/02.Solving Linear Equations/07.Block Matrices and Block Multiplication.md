## Partitioning a Matrix
Take a matrix $A$ of size $m \times n$. Choose a partition: split the rows into groups of sizes $m_1, m_2, \ldots, m_r$ with $m_1 + m_2 + \cdots + m_r = m$, and split the columns into groups of sizes $n_1, n_2, \ldots, n_s$ with $n_1 + \cdots + n_s = n$.

This produces a block matrix with $r \times s$ blocks. The block in position $(i,j)$ has size $m_i \times n_j$.
For a $4 \times 4$ matrix partitioned $2|2$ horizontally and $2|2$ vertically:
$$
A =
\left(
\begin{array}{cc|cc}
a_{11} & a_{12} & a_{13} & a_{14} \\
a_{21} & a_{22} & a_{23} & a_{24} \\
\hline
a_{31} & a_{32} & a_{33} & a_{34} \\
a_{41} & a_{42} & a_{43} & a_{44}
\end{array}
\right)
=
\begin{pmatrix}
A_{11} & A_{12} \\
A_{21} & A_{22}
\end{pmatrix}
$$
where each $A_{ij}$ is a $2 \times 2$ submatrix:
$$ A_{11} = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \\ a_{21} & a_{22} \end{pmatrix}, \quad A_{12} = \begin{pmatrix} a_{13} & a_{14} \\ a_{23} & a_{24} \end{pmatrix}, \quad A_{21} = \begin{pmatrix} a_{31} & a_{32} \\ a_{41} & a_{42} \end{pmatrix}, \quad A_{22} = \begin{pmatrix} a_{33} & a_{34} \\ a_{43} & a_{44} \end{pmatrix} $$
The notation is deliberately parallel to ordinary matrix entries. I will write $A = [A_{ij}]$ just as I write a scalar matrix as $A = [a_{ij}]$.

## Block Multiplication

Let $A$ be $m \times n$ and $B$ be $n \times p$. Partition both with a **compatible** column/row split: the column partition of $A$ must match the row partition of $B$.

If I partition $A$ as $r \times s$ blocks and $B$ as $s \times t$ blocks (so the inner block dimension is $s$ for both), then:

$$ (AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{s} A_{ik} B_{kj} $$

This is identical in form to the scalar rule $(AB)_{ij} = \sum_k a_{ik} b_{kj}$, with scalar entries replaced by block submatrices.

**The compatibility condition:** the number of columns in block $A_{ik}$ must equal the number of rows in block $B_{kj}$. This is guaranteed if the column partition of $A$ matches the row partition of $B$ — the column sizes of $A$'s blocks equal the row sizes of $B$'s corresponding blocks.

## A Concrete $4 \times 4$ Example

Let:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \ A_{21} & A_{22} \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{pmatrix} B_{11} & B_{12} \ B_{21} & B_{22} \end{pmatrix} $$

where each block is $2 \times 2$. Then:

$$ AB = \begin{pmatrix} A_{11}B_{11} + A_{12}B_{21} & A_{11}B_{12} + A_{12}B_{22} \ A_{21}B_{11} + A_{22}B_{21} & A_{21}B_{12} + A_{22}B_{22} \end{pmatrix} $$

This looks exactly like $2 \times 2$ matrix multiplication, but each "entry" is itself a $2 \times 2$ matrix product.

**Explicit numerical example.** Let:

$$ A = \left(\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 0 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 3 & 0 \ \hline 1 & 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 1 & 2 \end{array}\right), \qquad B = \left(\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 0 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 & 0 \ \hline 2 & 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) $$

Blocks of $A$:

$$ A_{11} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = I, \quad A_{12} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 3 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad A_{21} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad A_{22} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Blocks of $B$:

$$ B_{11} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = I, \quad B_{12} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B_{21} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B_{22} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = I $$

Now compute $AB$ block by block.

**Top-left block:** $A_{11}B_{11} + A_{12}B_{21}$

$$ I \cdot I + \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 3 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \ 6 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 1 \ 6 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Top-right block:** $A_{11}B_{12} + A_{12}B_{22}$

$$ I \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 3 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \cdot I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 3 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 \ 4 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Bottom-left block:** $A_{21}B_{11} + A_{22}B_{21}$

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \cdot I + \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Bottom-right block:** $A_{21}B_{12} + A_{22}B_{22}$

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \cdot I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 2 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

So:

$$ AB = \left(\begin{array}{cc|cc} 5 & 1 & 2 & 2 \ 6 & 1 & 4 & 0 \ \hline 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 2 & 3 & 2 & 2 \end{array}\right) $$

## Block Transpose and Block Inverse

**Transpose of a block matrix:**

$$ \begin{pmatrix} A_{11} & A_{12} \ A_{21} & A_{22} \end{pmatrix}^T = \begin{pmatrix} A_{11}^T & A_{21}^T \ A_{12}^T & A_{22}^T \end{pmatrix} $$

Two things happen simultaneously: the blocks swap positions across the diagonal (just as scalar entries do), and each block is individually transposed. Both operations are necessary.

**Inverse of a $2 \times 2$ block matrix:**

This is harder. For a block matrix:

$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & D \end{pmatrix} $$

the inverse is not simply the matrix of inverses of blocks. The correct formula involves the **Schur complement**.

---

## The Schur Complement

Assume $A$ is invertible. The **Schur complement** of $A$ in $M$ is:

$$ S = D - C A^{-1} B $$

Then, if $S$ is also invertible:

$$ M^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} + A^{-1} B S^{-1} C A^{-1} & -A^{-1} B S^{-1} \ -S^{-1} C A^{-1} & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix} $$

**Deriving this via block elimination.** Rather than memorize the formula, I want to understand where it comes from. Apply block elimination to $M$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & D \end{pmatrix} $$

Eliminate the $C$ block: $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - CA^{-1} R_1$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & D - CA^{-1}B \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix} $$

This is block upper triangular. The elimination matrix is:

$$ E = \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \ -CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix} $$

and $EM = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix}$.

So $M = E^{-1} \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix}$, which is the block $LU$ factorization of $M$:

$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \ CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix} $$

$\det(M) = \det(A) \cdot \det(S)$ follows immediately from the block triangular structure.

The Schur complement $S = D - CA^{-1}B$ is exactly the block pivot. The parallel to scalar elimination is perfect: just as $a_{22} - (a_{21}/a_{11}) \cdot a_{12}$ is the $(2,2)$ pivot after eliminating the $(2,1)$ entry, $S = D - CA^{-1}B$ is the block pivot after eliminating the $C$ block.

## Important Special Cases

**Block diagonal matrix:**

$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \ 0 & D \end{pmatrix} $$

Multiplication: $\begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \ 0 & D \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} B & 0 \ 0 & E \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} AB & 0 \ 0 & DE \end{pmatrix}$

Inverse: $\begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \ 0 & D \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} & 0 \ 0 & D^{-1} \end{pmatrix}$

Determinant: $\det(M) = \det(A) \det(D)$

Block diagonal matrices are the simplest block structure — they decouple completely. The blocks act independently.

**Block upper triangular:**

$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & D \end{pmatrix} $$

Determinant: $\det(M) = \det(A) \det(D)$. Same as the block diagonal case — the off-diagonal block $B$ does not affect the determinant.

Inverse: $\begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & D \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} & -A^{-1}BD^{-1} \ 0 & D^{-1} \end{pmatrix}$

The off-diagonal block of the inverse is $-A^{-1}BD^{-1}$ — not zero. Upper triangular structure is preserved, but the inverse has a nontrivial off-diagonal block.

**Block symmetric:**

$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ B^T & D \end{pmatrix} $$

where $A$ and $D$ are symmetric. The Schur complement $S = D - B^T A^{-1} B$ is also symmetric (easy to verify). Positive definiteness of $M$ is equivalent to positive definiteness of both $A$ and $S$ — this is a useful criterion.


## Why Block Structure Matters

**Computational efficiency.** If $A$ has a block structure, I can exploit it. Block diagonal systems decompose into independent smaller systems. Block triangular systems reduce to a sequence of smaller solves. In numerical linear algebra, exploiting sparsity and block structure is the difference between a feasible and an infeasible computation.

**Revealing structure.** Many matrices that arise in applications are naturally block-structured. Discretizations of PDEs on grids, graph Laplacians, covariance matrices in statistics — all of these often have block structure that reflects the underlying geometry or independence structure of the problem.

**Theoretical proofs.** Block arguments often simplify proofs that would be messy at the entry level. The Schur complement derivation of $\det(M) = \det(A)\det(S)$ is cleaner than expanding the determinant directly. The block $LU$ factorization is cleaner than tracking individual entries.

**The key principle:** whenever the matrix has natural substructure — groups of variables that interact with each other but not with others — block notation captures that substructure cleanly.
