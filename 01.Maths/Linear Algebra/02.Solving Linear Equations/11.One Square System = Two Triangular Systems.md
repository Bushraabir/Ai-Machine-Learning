# One Square System = Two Triangular Systems

> Personal note. This is the computational heart of 18.06. Everything Strang does in Chapter 2 is secretly about this factorization. Once I understood that $A = LU$ is just Gaussian elimination written as a matrix equation, the whole course clicked into place.

---

## The Core Idea

Solving $Ax = b$ directly is hard. But there is a class of systems that are trivially solvable: triangular systems. If the coefficient matrix is upper triangular, solve by back substitution from the bottom up. If lower triangular, solve by forward substitution from the top down. Each step is one division and some subtractions — $O(n^2)$ total.

The key insight: Gaussian elimination on a square system $Ax = b$ does not just solve the system. It secretly factors $A$ into a product of two triangular matrices:

$$A = LU$$

where $L$ is lower triangular with ones on the diagonal, and $U$ is upper triangular. Once you have this factorization, solving $Ax = b$ becomes:

$$Ax = b \implies LUx = b \implies \begin{cases} Lc = b & \text{(forward substitution)} \ Ux = c & \text{(back substitution)} \end{cases}$$

One square system has been split into two triangular ones. This is not a trick — it is a structural decomposition of $A$ itself, independent of $b$. The same $L$ and $U$ serve for any right-hand side.

---

## Gaussian Elimination as Matrix Multiplication

Let me make the factorization explicit. Suppose we have $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ and we perform Gaussian elimination without row exchanges.

Each elimination step — subtracting a multiple of one row from another — is left-multiplication by an elementary matrix. The step "subtract $\ell_{ij}$ times row $j$ from row $i$" (with $i > j$) corresponds to:

# $$ E_{ij} = I - \ell_{ij} e_i e_j^T

\begin{pmatrix} 1 & & & & \ & \ddots & & & \ & & 1 & & \ & & -\ell_{ij} & \ddots & \ & & & & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

where the $-\ell_{ij}$ appears in position $(i,j)$ with $i > j$. This matrix is lower triangular with ones on the diagonal — a unit lower triangular matrix.

After all elimination steps, we obtain:

$$E_{n-1,n-2} \cdots E_{21} \cdot A = U$$

where $U$ is upper triangular. Therefore:

$$A = E_{21}^{-1} \cdots E_{n-1,n-2}^{-1} \cdot U$$

The key observation: the inverse of $E_{ij} = I - \ell_{ij}e_ie_j^T$ is $E_{ij}^{-1} = I + \ell_{ij}e_ie_j^T$ — just flip the sign of the multiplier. And products of such matrices compose beautifully (no cancellation between different positions) to give:

$$L = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1} \cdots = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & & & \ \ell_{21} & 1 & & \ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 & \ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots \end{pmatrix}$$

The multipliers $\ell_{ij}$ (the numbers we computed during elimination) drop directly into position $(i,j)$ of $L$. No additional computation needed.

---

## Why the Multipliers Drop In Without Interaction

This is the part that surprised me the first time. When you multiply $E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1}$, you might expect the off-diagonal entries to interfere with each other. They do not, as long as there are no row exchanges.

**Claim:** For $j < k$, the matrix product $\left(I + \ell_{i_1 j} e_{i_1} e_j^T\right)\left(I + \ell_{i_2 k} e_{i_2} e_k^T\right) = I + \ell_{i_1 j} e_{i_1} e_j^T + \ell_{i_2 k} e_{i_2} e_k^T$ when $j \ne k$.

**Proof.** The cross term is $\ell_{i_1 j} \ell_{i_2 k} (e_{i_1} e_j^T)(e_{i_2} e_k^T) = \ell_{i_1 j} \ell_{i_2 k} e_{i_1} (e_j^T e_{i_2}) e_k^T$. Since $j < k \le i_2$, we have $j \ne i_2$, so $e_j^T e_{i_2} = 0$. The cross term vanishes. $\blacksquare$

This is why the multipliers simply fill in their slots in $L$. The structure is cleaner than it has any right to be.

## The $LU$ Factorization: Statement and Algorithm

> **Theorem.** If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ can be reduced to upper triangular form by Gaussian elimination without row exchanges (i.e., no zero pivots are encountered), then $A = LU$ where:
> 
> - $U$ is the upper triangular matrix produced by elimination (its diagonal entries are the pivots)
> - $L$ is unit lower triangular, with $L_{ij} = \ell_{ij}$ (the multiplier used to eliminate entry $(i,j)$) for $i > j$

The factorization is unique under these conditions.

**Uniqueness proof.** Suppose $A = L_1 U_1 = L_2 U_2$ with $L_1, L_2$ unit lower triangular and $U_1, U_2$ upper triangular. Then $L_2^{-1} L_1 = U_2 U_1^{-1}$. The left side is unit lower triangular; the right side is upper triangular. A matrix that is both unit lower triangular and upper triangular must be $I$. So $L_1 = L_2$ and $U_1 = U_2$. $\blacksquare$

## Solving $Ax = b$ via $LU$

Once $A = LU$ is computed (cost: $O(n^3)$), any system $Ax = b$ is solved in two passes (cost: $O(n^2)$ per right-hand side):

**Step 1 — Forward substitution:** Solve $Lc = b$.

Since $L$ is unit lower triangular:

$$c_1 = b_1$$ $$c_i = b_i - \sum_{j=1}^{i-1} \ell_{ij} c_j, \quad i = 2, 3, \ldots, n$$

**Step 2 — Back substitution:** Solve $Ux = c$.

Since $U$ is upper triangular with pivots $u_{ii}$ on the diagonal:

$$x_n = \frac{c_n}{u_{nn}}$$ $$x_i = \frac{1}{u_{ii}}\left(c_i - \sum_{j=i+1}^{n} u_{ij} x_j\right), \quad i = n-1, \ldots, 1$$

The power of this decomposition: if you need to solve $Ax = b_1, Ax = b_2, \ldots, Ax = b_k$ for the same $A$, you factor $A = LU$ once and then perform $2k$ triangular solves. Compared to $k$ separate Gaussian eliminations, this saves a factor of $k$ in the dominant $O(n^3)$ work.

## Worked Example: $3 \times 3$ Full Decomposition

Let:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \ 4 & 3 & 3 \ 8 & 7 & 9 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Step 1: Eliminate column 1.**

Multipliers: $\ell_{21} = \tfrac{4}{2} = 2$, $\ell_{31} = \tfrac{8}{2} = 4$.

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 4R_1$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Step 2: Eliminate column 2.**

Multiplier: $\ell_{32} = \tfrac{3}{1} = 3$.

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_2$:

$$ U = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Assemble $L$:** Drop the multipliers into their positions.

$$ L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 2 & 1 & 0 \ 4 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Verify $A = LU$:**

# $$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 2 & 1 & 0 \ 4 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}

# \begin{pmatrix} 1{\cdot}2 & 1{\cdot}1 & 1{\cdot}1 \ 2{\cdot}2+1{\cdot}0 & 2{\cdot}1+1{\cdot}1 & 2{\cdot}1+1{\cdot}1 \ 4{\cdot}2+3{\cdot}0+0 & 4{\cdot}1+3{\cdot}1+0 & 4{\cdot}1+3{\cdot}1+1{\cdot}2 \end{pmatrix}

\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \ 4 & 3 & 3 \ 8 & 7 & 9 \end{pmatrix} = A \checkmark $$

**Now solve $Ax = b$ for $b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 5 \ 14 \end{pmatrix}$.**

_Forward: $Lc = b$._

$$ c_1 = 1 $$ $$ c_2 = 5 - 2(1) = 3 $$ $$ c_3 = 14 - 4(1) - 3(3) = 14 - 4 - 9 = 1 $$

So $c = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 3 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}$.

_Back: $Ux = c$._

$$ x_3 = \frac{1}{2} $$ $$ x_2 = 3 - 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{5}{2} $$ $$ x_1 = \frac{1}{2}!\left(1 - 1 \cdot \frac{5}{2} - 1 \cdot \frac{1}{2}\right) = \frac{1}{2}(1 - 3) = -1 $$

So $x = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \ \tfrac{5}{2} \ \tfrac{1}{2} \end{pmatrix}$.

**Check:** $2(-1) + 1(\tfrac{5}{2}) + 1(\tfrac{1}{2}) = -2 + 3 = 1$ $\checkmark$. $4(-1) + 3(\tfrac{5}{2}) + 3(\tfrac{1}{2}) = -4 + \tfrac{15}{2} + \tfrac{3}{2} = -4 + 9 = 5$ $\checkmark$.

## When Row Exchanges Are Needed: $PA = LU$

Gaussian elimination fails if a pivot is zero — we cannot use a zero entry to eliminate below it. The fix is **partial pivoting**: before eliminating in column $k$, swap row $k$ with the row below it that has the largest absolute value in column $k$. This is captured by a permutation matrix $P$.

> **Theorem.** For any $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ (not necessarily invertible), there exists a permutation matrix $P$ such that $PA = LU$, where $L$ is unit lower triangular and $U$ is upper triangular.

The factorization $PA = LU$ means: reorder the rows of $A$ (according to $P$), and the result factors as $LU$.

To solve $Ax = b$ using $PA = LU$:

$$ Ax = b \implies PAx = Pb \implies LUx = Pb $$

Then proceed with two triangular solves using right-hand side $Pb$ (just a reordering of $b$'s entries).

In practice, MATLAB's `[L,U,P] = lu(A)` always uses partial pivoting.

## $LDU$ Factorization

Extract the diagonal from $U$: write $U = D\tilde{U}$ where $D = \text{diag}(u_{11}, u_{22}, \ldots, u_{nn})$ and $\tilde{U}$ is unit upper triangular (ones on the diagonal). Then:

$$A = LU = L D \tilde{U}$$

This is the $LDU$ factorization: unit lower triangular, diagonal, unit upper triangular. It is the block version I used in the Schur complement note. The diagonal entries of $D$ are the pivots of $A$.

For **symmetric** matrices $A = A^T$, the $LDU$ factorization satisfies $\tilde{U} = L^T$, giving:

$$A = LDL^T$$

This is the symmetric factorization. For positive definite $A$, the pivots (diagonal of $D$) are all positive, and one can write $D = \sqrt{D}\sqrt{D}$ to obtain the **Cholesky factorization** $A = \hat{L}\hat{L}^T$ where $\hat{L} = L\sqrt{D}$.

## Operation Count

This is worth knowing. For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$:

|Operation|Cost|
|---|---|
|Factor $A = LU$|$\sim \tfrac{2}{3}n^3$ flops|
|Solve $Lc = b$ (forward)|$\sim n^2$ flops|
|Solve $Ux = c$ (back)|$\sim n^2$ flops|
|Total for one solve|$\sim \tfrac{2}{3}n^3$ (dominated by factorization)|
|Each additional right-hand side|$\sim 2n^2$ flops|

The $\tfrac{2}{3}n^3$ for the factorization comes from: at step $k$, we perform $(n-k)^2$ multiplications and subtractions. Summing: $\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}(n-k)^2 \approx \int_0^n t^2,dt = \tfrac{n^3}{3}$, but accounting for both multiplications and additions gives $\tfrac{2}{3}n^3$.

---

## When Does $LU$ Fail? (No Row Exchanges)

$LU$ without pivoting fails when a zero pivot is encountered — but this does not necessarily mean $A$ is singular.

Example:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

$\det(A) = -1 \ne 0$, so $A$ is invertible, but the $(1,1)$ entry is zero — we cannot begin elimination. We need to swap rows first: $PA = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$, which factors immediately with $L = I$ and $U = PA$.

So: "no $LU$ factorization without pivoting" does not mean singular. It means the natural order of elimination breaks down. $PA = LU$ always exists.

What does genuine singularity look like in $LU$ terms? A zero on the diagonal of $U$ — a zero pivot with nothing below it to swap in. Then $Ux = c$ has no unique solution.

---

## The $LU$ Factorization and $A^{-1}$

Once $A = LU$ is known, $A^{-1} = U^{-1}L^{-1}$ (reversal law from the inverse note). Computing $A^{-1}$ explicitly means solving $n$ systems $Ax_k = e_k$ for $k = 1, \ldots, n$ where $e_k$ are the standard basis vectors. Each solve is $O(n^2)$ once $LU$ is known, so the full inverse costs $O(n^3)$.

But in practice: never compute $A^{-1}$ explicitly to solve $Ax = b$. Solve $Ax = b$ directly using $LU$. Computing and storing $A^{-1}$ is both slower and numerically less stable than triangular solves.

---

## Connection to the Determinant

$$\det(A) = \det(L)\det(U) = 1 \cdot \prod_{i=1}^n u_{ii} = \prod_{i=1}^n \text{(pivots of } A\text{)}$$

since $\det(L) = 1$ (unit lower triangular) and $\det(U) = \prod u_{ii}$ (upper triangular). This is the most efficient way to compute $\det(A)$ in general: factor $A = LU$ (or $PA = LU$, in which case $\det(A) = \text{sign}(P) \prod u_{ii}$), read off the product of pivots.

---

## Summary

The central chain of reasoning:

$$ Ax = b \xrightarrow{\text{factor}} LUx = b \xrightarrow{\text{substitute } c = Ux} \begin{cases} Lc = b & \text{forward solve, } O(n^2) \ Ux = c & \text{back solve, } O(n^2) \end{cases} $$

|Object|Description|
|---|---|
|$L$|Unit lower triangular; $L_{ij} = \ell_{ij}$ = multiplier to eliminate $(i,j)$|
|$U$|Upper triangular; diagonal entries are the pivots|
|$D$|Diagonal matrix of pivots ($LDU$ variant)|
|$P$|Permutation matrix for row swaps ($PA = LU$ variant)|
|Factorization cost|$\sim \tfrac{2}{3}n^3$|
|Per-solve cost (after factoring)|$\sim 2n^2$|

## Things I Want to Think About More

- The relationship between $LU$ and the Schur complement: the Schur complement of $A_{11}$ in a block matrix is exactly what the $(2,2)$ block becomes after block elimination, which is block $LU$. The two notes connect directly here.
- Banded matrices: if $A$ has bandwidth $w$ (nonzero only in $w$ diagonals), then $L$ and $U$ inherit the band structure and the factorization costs $O(w^2 n)$ instead of $O(n^3)$. Huge savings for sparse systems from PDEs.
- Stability of pivoting: partial pivoting bounds the growth of entries in $U$ by a factor of $2^{n-1}$ in the worst case, but in practice growth is negligible. Complete pivoting is theoretically tighter but rarely used.
- The $LDL^T$ factorization for symmetric matrices: pivots can be negative (indefinite matrices), in which case $D$ has negative diagonal entries and Cholesky fails. Bunch-Kaufman uses $2 \times 2$ pivot blocks to handle this.
