>[!note] You already have $LDL^T$. So why Cholesky?
>
Because it is **the** numerical workhorse for positive definite systems. It is:
-Half the work of $LU$ ($n^3/6$ vs $n^3/3$ operations)
-Numerically the most stable factorization for PD matrices
-The standard solver in statistics (covariance matrices), ML (Gaussian processes, Kalman filters), and physics simulations
The one tradeoff: it requires positive definiteness. If any pivot is $\leq 0$, the square root breaks.

## Derivation from $LDL^T$
Start from what you already know.
- **Step 1:** For symmetric positive definite $A$: $A = LDL^T \quad \text{where all } d_i > 0$
- **Step 2:** Since all $d_i > 0$, define the diagonal square root matrix: $D^{1/2} = \text{diag}(\sqrt{d_1}, \sqrt{d_2}, \ldots, \sqrt{d_n})$
- **Step 3:** Factor $D = D^{1/2} D^{1/2}$ and regroup: $A = L \underbrace{D^{1/2} D^{1/2}}_D L^T = \underbrace{(LD^{1/2})}_{\tilde{L}} \underbrace{(D^{1/2}L^T)}_{\tilde{L}^T}$
- **Step 4:** Define $\tilde{L} = LD^{1/2}$. Then: $\boxed{A = \tilde{L}\tilde{L}^T}$ ->where $\tilde{L}$ is lower triangular (not unit — diagonal entries are $\sqrt{d_i}$, not 1).

> [!note] What Changes vs $LDL^T$
> In $LDL^T$: $L$ has 1s on diagonal, $D$ holds the pivot magnitudes separately.
> In Cholesky: $\tilde{L}$ absorbs $\sqrt{d_i}$ into its diagonal. The factorization collapses from three matrices to two.

> [!theorem] Cholesky Factorization
> A symmetric matrix $A$ is **positive definite** if and only if there exists a unique lower triangular matrix $L$ with **strictly positive diagonal entries** such that:
> $$A = LL^T$$

The uniqueness is easy to verify: the diagonal of $L$ is forced by $d_i = \ell_{ii}^2 > 0$.

## Example
$A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & 2 & -1 \\ -2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$
From [[15.Symmetric Matrices, Their Products, and LDL^T|LDL^T Decomposition]], we already know: $D = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad L_{LDL} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1/2 & 1 & 0 \\ -1/2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$

- **Step 1:Compute $D^{1/2}$**
	$D^{1/2} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix}$

- Step 2: Compute $L = L_{LDL} \cdot D^{1/2}$
	$L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1/2 & 1 & 0 \\ -1/2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & \sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix}$

### Verification: $LL^T$
$LL^T = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & \sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & \sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & 2 & -1 \\ -2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = A \checkmark$

## Direct Algorithm (No $LDL^T$ Intermediate)

You can compute $L$ directly without first doing $LDL^T$.

For $j = 1, 2, \ldots, n$:

$$\ell_{jj} = \sqrt{a_{jj} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} \ell_{jk}^2}$$

For $i > j$:

$$\ell_{ij} = \frac{1}{\ell_{jj}}\left(a_{ij} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} \ell_{ik}\ell_{jk}\right)$$

> [!example] Trace Through for Our $3 \times 3$
>
> $\ell_{11} = \sqrt{4} = 2$
>
> $\ell_{21} = (2)/2 = 1$, $\quad \ell_{31} = (-2)/2 = -1$
>
> $\ell_{22} = \sqrt{2 - 1^2} = \sqrt{1} = 1$
>
> $\ell_{32} = (-1 - (-1)(1))/1 = 0$
>
> $\ell_{33} = \sqrt{3 - (-1)^2 - 0^2} = \sqrt{2}$
>
> Result: $L = \begin{bmatrix}2 & 0 & 0 \\ 1 & 1 & 0 \\ -1 & 0 & \sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix}$ ✓

## Why Positive Definiteness is Required:

> [!warning] The Square Root Requirement
> The formula $\ell_{jj} = \sqrt{(\ldots)}$ requires the argument to be strictly positive at every step.
> If any pivot $d_j \leq 0$, you get a complex or undefined $\ell_{jj}$.
> This is not a computational failure — it is telling you that $A$ is **not** positive definite.

Conversely, if the Cholesky algorithm runs to completion (all square roots of positive numbers), you have simultaneously proven that $A$ is positive definite. Cholesky is therefore also the most efficient **positive definiteness test**: just attempt the factorization.

##  Comparison Table — $LU$ vs $LDL^T$ vs Cholesky

| Property | $LU$ | $LDL^T$ | Cholesky $LL^T$ |
|---|---|---|---|
| Matrix type | Any square | Symmetric | Symmetric **positive definite** |
| Factors | $L$, $U$ (unrelated) | $L$, $D$, $L^T$ | $L$, $L^T$ |
| Diagonal of $L$ | All 1s | All 1s | $\sqrt{\text{pivots}}$ |
| Operations | $n^3/3$ | $n^3/6$ | $n^3/6$ |
| Storage | Full $L$ + $U$ | $L$ + $D$ vector | $L$ only |
| Numerical stability | Good | Good | **Best** for SPD |
| PD test? | Indirect | Yes (check $d_i > 0$) | Yes (attempt factorization) |

> [!tip] Decision Rule
> - Non-symmetric? → Use $LU$
> - Symmetric, PD uncertain? → Use $LDL^T$ (no square roots, check sign of pivots)
> - Symmetric, know it's PD? → Use Cholesky (cleanest, fastest in practice)




## Numerical Stability: Why Cholesky Wins

Standard $LU$ with partial pivoting is stable in practice but requires row interchanges, which destroys any structure. Cholesky for SPD matrices is **unconditionally stable** without any pivoting:

> [!theorem] Stability of Cholesky For a symmetric positive definite matrix $A$, the computed Cholesky factor $\hat{L}$ satisfies: $$\hat{L}\hat{L}^T = A + E, \quad |E|_2 \leq c_n \epsilon_{\text{machine}} |A|_2$$ where $c_n$ is a modest constant depending on $n$.

No amplification of rounding errors. No need for pivoting. This is why numerical libraries (LAPACK's `dpotrf`, NumPy's `linalg.cholesky`, PyTorch's `torch.linalg.cholesky`) all default to Cholesky for positive definite systems.
