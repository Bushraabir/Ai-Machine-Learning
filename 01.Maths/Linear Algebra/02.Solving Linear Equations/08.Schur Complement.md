Let $M$ be an $n \times n$ matrix partitioned into blocks:
$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix} $$
where:
- $A$ is $p \times p$
- $B$ is $p \times q$
- $C$ is $q \times p$
- $D$ is $q \times q$
- $p + q = n$
We want to understand the invertibility of $M$ and find $M^{-1}$ in terms of the blocks. The key move is block Gaussian elimination.


## Block Gaussian Elimination
Suppose $A$ is invertible. We eliminate the $C$ block by left-multiplying $M$ with the block lower triangular matrix:
$$ L = \begin{pmatrix} I_p & 0 \\ -CA^{-1} & I_q \end{pmatrix} $$
Computing $LM$:

$$
\begin{pmatrix}
I & 0 \\
-CA^{-1} & I
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix}
A & B \\
C & D
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
A & B \\
-CA^{-1}A + C & -CA^{-1}B + D
\end{pmatrix}
=
\begin{pmatrix}
A & B \\
0 & D - CA^{-1}B
\end{pmatrix}
$$

The $(2,1)$ block is gone. The object that appeared in the $(2,2)$ position is the **Schur complement of $A$ in $M$**.

---

## Definition

> **Definition.** Let $M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & D \end{pmatrix}$ with $A$ invertible. The **Schur complement of $A$ in $M$** is: $$M/A := D - CA^{-1}B$$

Similarly, if $D$ is invertible, the **Schur complement of $D$ in $M$** is: $$M/D := A - BD^{-1}C$$

This is the object left over after you've eliminated from the other direction. Both can exist simultaneously if both $A$ and $D$ are invertible, and there's a determinant identity that ties them together (see below).

---

## The Block LDU Factorization

Going further, we can factor $M$ completely. Write:

# $$ M

# \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & D \end{pmatrix}

\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \ CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix}}_{L} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \ 0 & D - CA^{-1}B \end{pmatrix}}_{D\text{-block}} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} I & A^{-1}B \ 0 & I \end{pmatrix}}_{U} $$

Let me verify the middle term multiplied by $U$:

# $$ \begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} I & A^{-1}B \ 0 & I \end{pmatrix}

\begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix} $$

where I wrote $S = D - CA^{-1}B$. Then:

# $$ \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \ CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix}

# \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ CA^{-1}A & CA^{-1}B + S \end{pmatrix}

# \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & CA^{-1}B + D - CA^{-1}B \end{pmatrix}

\begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & D \end{pmatrix} $$

Good, the factorization checks out. This is exactly the block analogue of $LDU$ decomposition from Chapter 2 of Strang â€” the pivots are $A$ and $S = M/A$.

---

## Determinant Formula

From the $LDU$ factorization, since $\det(L) = \det(U) = 1$:

$$ \boxed{\det(M) = \det(A) \cdot \det(D - CA^{-1}B) = \det(A) \cdot \det(M/A)} $$

By symmetry (eliminate in the other direction):

$$ \det(M) = \det(D) \cdot \det(A - BD^{-1}C) = \det(D) \cdot \det(M/D) $$

These two together give the **Jacobi identity**:

$$ \det(M/A) \cdot \det(A) = \det(M/D) \cdot \det(D) $$

i.e., $\det(D - CA^{-1}B)\det(A) = \det(A - BD^{-1}C)\det(D)$.

This is a highly non-obvious algebraic fact if you stare at it without the block factorization â€” but it falls out immediately from the two ways of doing $LDU$.

---

## Inverting a Block Matrix

If $A$ and $S = M/A$ are both invertible, then $M$ is invertible and:

# $$ M^{-1}

# \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & D \end{pmatrix}^{-1}

\begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} + A^{-1}B S^{-1} C A^{-1} & -A^{-1}B S^{-1} \ -S^{-1}CA^{-1} & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix} $$

**Proof.** Invert the $LDU$ factorization:

$$ M^{-1} = U^{-1} \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} & 0 \ 0 & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix} L^{-1} $$

where:

$$ U^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} I & -A^{-1}B \ 0 & I \end{pmatrix}, \quad L^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \ -CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix} $$

Computing $U^{-1} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} & 0 \ 0 & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix}$:

# $$ \begin{pmatrix} I & -A^{-1}B \ 0 & I \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} & 0 \ 0 & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix}

\begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} & -A^{-1}BS^{-1} \ 0 & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix} $$

Then multiply on the right by $L^{-1}$:

# $$ \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} & -A^{-1}BS^{-1} \ 0 & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \ -CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix}

\begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} + A^{-1}BS^{-1}CA^{-1} & -A^{-1}BS^{-1} \ -S^{-1}CA^{-1} & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix} $$

Done. Notice that the $(2,2)$ block of $M^{-1}$ is exactly $S^{-1} = (M/A)^{-1}$. The Schur complement is literally the pivot for block inversion.

---

## Invertibility Criterion

> **Theorem.** $M$ is invertible if and only if $A$ is invertible and $M/A = D - CA^{-1}B$ is invertible.

This is immediate from $\det(M) = \det(A)\det(M/A)$ â€” $M$ is nonsingular iff both factors are nonsingular.

There's a symmetric version: $M$ is invertible iff $D$ is invertible and $M/D$ is invertible. Both conditions are equivalent (they both just say $\det M \ne 0$), but they give different computational paths.

---

## Positive Definiteness and the Schur Complement

This is one of the most important applications. Let $M$ be symmetric:

$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ B^T & D \end{pmatrix} $$

> **Theorem.** $M \succ 0$ (positive definite) if and only if $A \succ 0$ and $M/A = D - B^T A^{-1} B \succ 0$.

**Proof ($\Rightarrow$):** If $M \succ 0$, take any $x \ne 0$ and set $y = \begin{pmatrix} x \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$. Then $y^T M y = x^T A x > 0$, so $A \succ 0$.

For the Schur complement, note from the $LDU$ factorization:

$$ M = L \begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix} L^T $$

(here $L = \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \ B^T A^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix}$ and $S = D - B^T A^{-1} B$). Since $L$ is invertible, $M \succ 0$ iff $\begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix} \succ 0$ iff $A \succ 0$ and $S \succ 0$.

This is used constantly in optimization and statistics. A block covariance matrix is positive semidefinite; its Schur complement is the conditional covariance. See below.

---

## The Woodbury Identity (Connection)

The block inverse formula implies the **Sherman-Morrison-Woodbury identity**. From the $(1,1)$ block of $M^{-1}$:

$$ (A - BD^{-1}C)^{-1} = A^{-1} + A^{-1}B(D - CA^{-1}B)^{-1}CA^{-1} $$

Setting $B \to U$, $C \to V$, this gives:

$$ (A - UV^T)^{-1} = A^{-1} + A^{-1}U(I - V^T A^{-1} U)^{-1}V^T A^{-1} $$

which is the standard Woodbury formula. So Woodbury is just the $(1,1)$ block of block matrix inversion, which is just the Schur complement in disguise. I find it much easier to re-derive Woodbury this way whenever I forget the exact form.

## Worked Example: $4 \times 4$ Block Matrix

Let:

$$ M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 & 1 \ 1 & 3 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 4 & 2 \ 1 & 0 & 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix} $$

Partition as $p = q = 2$:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad C = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad D = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \ 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix} $$

First compute $A^{-1}$. We have $\det(A) = 6 - 1 = 5$, so:

$$ A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Now compute $CA^{-1}$:

$$ CA^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 2 \ 3 & -1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Then $CA^{-1}B$:

$$ CA^{-1}B = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} -1 & 2 \ 3 & -1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

The Schur complement:

$$ S = D - CA^{-1}B = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \ 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}

- \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 - \tfrac{2}{5} & 2 + \tfrac{1}{5} \ 2 + \tfrac{1}{5} & 5 - \tfrac{3}{5} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac{18}{5} & \tfrac{11}{5} \ \tfrac{11}{5} & \tfrac{22}{5} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 18 & 11 \ 11 & 22 \end{pmatrix} $$

And so:

$$ \det(M) = \det(A) \cdot \det(S) = 5 \cdot \det!\left(\frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 18 & 11 \ 11 & 22 \end{pmatrix}\right) = 5 \cdot \frac{1}{25}(18 \cdot 22 - 121) = \frac{1}{5}(396 - 121) = \frac{275}{5} = 55 $$

Notice $A \succ 0$ (pivots $2$ and $5/2$) and $S \succ 0$ ($\det(S) = 275/25 = 11 > 0$ and trace positive), confirming $M \succ 0$ as expected from an exercise like this.

## Application: Conditional Gaussian Distribution

This is where the Schur complement shows its probabilistic face. Let:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} X \ Y \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}\left( \begin{pmatrix} \mu_X \ \mu_Y \end{pmatrix},, \begin{pmatrix} \Sigma_{XX} & \Sigma_{XY} \ \Sigma_{YX} & \Sigma_{YY} \end{pmatrix} \right) $$

The conditional distribution of $X$ given $Y = y$ is Gaussian with:

$$ X \mid Y = y \sim \mathcal{N}!\left(\mu_X + \Sigma_{XY}\Sigma_{YY}^{-1}(y - \mu_Y),; \Sigma_{XX} - \Sigma_{XY}\Sigma_{YY}^{-1}\Sigma_{YX}\right) $$

The conditional covariance is $\Sigma_{XX} - \Sigma_{XY}\Sigma_{YY}^{-1}\Sigma_{YX}$, which is exactly the **Schur complement of $\Sigma_{YY}$ in the joint covariance matrix**.

This isn't a coincidence â€” the derivation uses the block matrix inverse formula directly. When you condition on $Y$, you're performing algebraic elimination in the exponent of the Gaussian density, and the pivot that appears is the Schur complement.

## Summary of Key Facts

Let $M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \ C & D \end{pmatrix}$ with $A$ invertible and $S = D - CA^{-1}B$.

|Fact|Formula|
|---|---|
|Schur complement|$M/A = D - CA^{-1}B$|
|Determinant|$\det M = \det A \cdot \det S$|
|Invertibility|$M$ invertible $\iff$ $A$, $S$ both invertible|
|$(2,2)$ block of $M^{-1}$|$(M/A)^{-1} = S^{-1}$|
|PD criterion|$M \succ 0 \iff A \succ 0$ and $S \succ 0$|
|Block LDU|$M = L \begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \ 0 & S \end{pmatrix} U$|
