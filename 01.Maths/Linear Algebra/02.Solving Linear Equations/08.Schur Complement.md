Before any definition, ground the entire theory in a concrete linear system.
Suppose we have a block matrix equation:
$$\begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} f \\ g \end{pmatrix}$$
This is simply two simultaneous block equations:
$$\begin{cases} Ax + By = f \\ Cx + Dy = g \end{cases}$$
where $x \in \mathbb{R}^p$, $y \in \mathbb{R}^q$, and the blocks have compatible dimensions.

**The entire theory of Schur complements is the story of eliminating $x$ from this system.**

## Step-by-Step Elimination:
**Step 1.** From the first equation, assuming $A$ is invertible:
$Ax + By = f$
$\implies x = A^{-1}(f - By)$

**Step 2.** Substitute into the second equation:
$C\bigl(A^{-1}(f - By)\bigr) + Dy = g$

**Step 3.** Expand:
$CA^{-1}f - CA^{-1}By + Dy = g$

**Step 4.** Group terms involving $y$:
$\underbrace{(D - CA^{-1}B)}_{\text{Schur complement}} y = g - CA^{-1}f$ -> **This is the key moment.** 
The matrix multiplying $y$ after eliminating $x$ is called the Schur complement of $A$ in $M$.

## Intuition: What the Schur Complement Means
Think of the four blocks as follows:
- $A$: the part you already understand and can solve directly
- $B, C$: the coupling terms connecting the two sub-systems
- $D$: the remaining part, which you still need to handle
When you eliminate $x$, the connections $B$ and $C$ distort $D$. That distortion is $-CA^{-1}B$.

So the Schur complement is:
$$M/A = \underbrace{D}_{\text{original remaining}} - \underbrace{CA^{-1}B}_{\text{influence of } A \text{ on } D \text{ via the coupling}}$$

**Mental model in one sentence:** The Schur complement is the effective matrix for $y$ after the influence of the $A$-block has been subtracted out.

**Decoupled case check.** If $B = 0$ and $C = 0$ (no coupling between blocks):
$$M/A = D - C \cdot A^{-1} \cdot B = D - 0 = D$$

No interaction means nothing changes. The Schur complement reduces to $D$ exactly. This is the correct sanity check.

## Block Gaussian Elimination: The Matrix Formulation
The elimination above is encoded by left-multiplying $M$ with a block lower triangular matrix:
$$L = \begin{pmatrix} I_p & 0 \\ -CA^{-1} & I_q \end{pmatrix}$$

This matrix literally says: subtract $C \cdot A^{-1} \cdot (\text{row 1})$ from row 2. That is standard Gaussian elimination, generalized to blocks.
Computing $LM$:
$$\begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \\ -CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ 0 & D - CA^{-1}B \end{pmatrix}$$

The $(2,1)$ block is eliminated. The $(2,2)$ block is the Schur complement.

## Numerical Example: 2x2 Blocks
Let:
$$M = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \\ 2 & 0 & 4 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$
Partition as $p = q = 2$:
$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad D = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$
**Compute $A^{-1}$:** $\det(A) = 6 - 1 = 5$, so:
$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Compute $CA^{-1}$:**
$$CA^{-1} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Compute $CA^{-1}B$:** Since $B = I$:
$$CA^{-1}B = CA^{-1} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Schur complement:**
$$S = D - CA^{-1}B = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 4 & -2 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 16 & 7 \\ 6 & 12 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Determinant:**
$$\det(M) = \det(A) \cdot \det(S) = 5 \cdot \frac{1}{25}(192 - 42) = \frac{150}{5} = 30$$

## Formal Definition:

>  Let $M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix}$ with $A$ invertible. The **Schur complement of $A$ in $M$** is: $$M/A := D - CA^{-1}B$$

Similarly, if $D$ is invertible, the **Schur complement of $D$ in $M$** is:
$$M/D := A - BD^{-1}C$$

## The Block LDU Factorization

Write $S = D - CA^{-1}B = M/A$. The full factorization is:
$$M = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \\ CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix}}_{L} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \\ 0 & S \end{pmatrix}}_{\Delta} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} I & A^{-1}B \\ 0 & I \end{pmatrix}}_{U}$$

This is the block analogue of $LDU$ decomposition. The pivots are $A$ (the top-left block pivot) and $S = M/A$ (the Schur complement pivot). This decomposition is the source of all other facts below.

---

## Determinant Formula

Since $\det(L) = \det(U) = 1$ (both are block triangular with identity diagonal blocks):

$$\boxed{\det(M) = \det(A) \cdot \det(M/A)}$$
By eliminating in the other direction:
$$\det(M) = \det(D) \cdot \det(M/D)$$

These two together give the **Jacobi identity**:
$$\det(D - CA^{-1}B) \cdot \det(A) = \det(A - BD^{-1}C) \cdot \det(D)$$

This is algebraically non-obvious if you stare at it cold. It falls out immediately from the two LDU directions.

---

## Invertibility Criterion

> **Theorem.** $M$ is invertible if and only if $A$ is invertible and $M/A = D - CA^{-1}B$ is invertible.

**Proof.** From $\det(M) = \det(A) \cdot \det(M/A)$, $M$ is nonsingular if and only if both factors are nonsingular.

Symmetric version: $M$ is invertible if and only if $D$ and $M/D$ are both invertible.

---

## Block Matrix Inverse

If $A$ and $S = M/A$ are both invertible:
$$M^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} + A^{-1}BS^{-1}CA^{-1} & -A^{-1}BS^{-1} \\ -S^{-1}CA^{-1} & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix}$$
**Derivation.** Invert the LDU factorization $M^{-1} = U^{-1} \Delta^{-1} L^{-1}$, where:
$$U^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} I & -A^{-1}B \\ 0 & I \end{pmatrix}, \quad \Delta^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} & 0 \\ 0 & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix}, \quad L^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \\ -CA^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix}$$

Multiply in sequence to obtain the formula above.

**Key observation:** The $(2,2)$ block of $M^{-1}$ is exactly $S^{-1} = (M/A)^{-1}$. The Schur complement is the pivot for block inversion.

## Positive Definiteness and the Schur Complement

Let $M$ be symmetric:
$$M = \begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ B^T & D \end{pmatrix}$$

> **Theorem.** $M \succ 0$ if and only if $A \succ 0$ and $M/A = D - B^T A^{-1} B \succ 0$.

**Proof.** The LDU factorization takes the symmetric form:
$$M = L \begin{pmatrix} A & 0 \\ 0 & S \end{pmatrix} L^T, \quad L = \begin{pmatrix} I & 0 \\ B^T A^{-1} & I \end{pmatrix}$$

Since $L$ is invertible, $M \succ 0$ if and only if the block diagonal factor is positive definite, which requires $A \succ 0$ and $S \succ 0$.

---

## The Woodbury Identity (Derived from Block Inversion)

The block inverse formula implies the Sherman-Morrison-Woodbury identity. Reading the $(1,1)$ block of $M^{-1}$:
$$(A - BD^{-1}C)^{-1} = A^{-1} + A^{-1}B(D - CA^{-1}B)^{-1}CA^{-1}$$
Setting $B \to U$, $C \to V$, $D \to I$:
$$(A - UV^T)^{-1} = A^{-1} + A^{-1}U(I - V^T A^{-1} U)^{-1}V^T A^{-1}$$

This is the standard Woodbury identity. It is not a separate theorem — it is the $(1,1)$ block of block matrix inversion, which is the Schur complement in disguise.

## Example: 4x4 Block Matrix

$$M = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 4 & 2 \\ 1 & 0 & 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}, \quad A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B = C = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad D = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \\ 2 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$$
$\det(A) = 5$, so $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$.

$$CA^{-1}B = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ -1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$S = D - CA^{-1}B = \frac{1}{5}\begin{pmatrix} 18 & 11 \\ 11 & 22 \end{pmatrix}$$
$$\det(M) = \det(A) \cdot \det(S) = 5 \cdot \frac{1}{25}(396 - 121) = \frac{275}{5} = 55$$

Since $A \succ 0$ (pivots $2$ and $5/2$) and $\det(S) = 11 > 0$ with positive trace, $S \succ 0$, confirming $M \succ 0$.

## Application: Conditional Gaussian Distribution
Let:
$$\begin{pmatrix} X \ Y \end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}!\left( \begin{pmatrix} \mu_X \ \mu_Y \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} \Sigma_{XX} & \Sigma_{XY} \ \Sigma_{YX} & \Sigma_{YY} \end{pmatrix} \right)$$
The conditional distribution $X \mid Y = y$ is Gaussian with:
$$\mu_{X|Y} = \mu_X + \Sigma_{XY}\Sigma_{YY}^{-1}(y - \mu_Y)$$
$$\Sigma_{X|Y} = \Sigma_{XX} - \Sigma_{XY}\Sigma_{YY}^{-1}\Sigma_{YX}$$

The conditional covariance $\Sigma_{X|Y}$ is exactly the **Schur complement of $\Sigma_{YY}$ in the joint covariance matrix**.

This is not a coincidence. Conditioning on $Y$ is algebraically equivalent to block Gaussian elimination in the exponent of the Gaussian density. The pivot that appears in the reduced system is the Schur complement.
