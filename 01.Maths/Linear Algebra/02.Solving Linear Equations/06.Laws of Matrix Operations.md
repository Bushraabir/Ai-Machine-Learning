
## Why This Deserves Its Own Note

Matrix algebra looks like ordinary algebra at first. Addition works exactly as expected. But multiplication breaks one of the most basic rules of arithmetic — commutativity — and this failure is not an edge case. It is the defining feature of matrix algebra, and it has consequences everywhere: in the transpose laws, the inverse laws, and the factorization theorems.

I want to record all the laws carefully, with proofs for the nontrivial ones and explicit counterexamples for the rules that fail.

---

## Matrix Addition

Let $A, B, C$ be $m \times n$ matrices. Addition is defined componentwise: $(A + B)_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}$.

**Commutativity:** $$A + B = B + A$$

**Associativity:** $$(A + B) + C = A + (B + C)$$

**Additive identity:** The zero matrix $0$ (all entries zero) satisfies $A + 0 = A$.

**Additive inverse:** $A + (-A) = 0$ where $(-A)_{ij} = -a_{ij}$.

These all hold componentwise and carry over directly from $\mathbb{R}$. Nothing interesting happens with addition — it is multiplication where the structure changes.

---

## Scalar Multiplication

For scalar $c \in \mathbb{R}$ and matrices $A$, $B$ of compatible sizes:

$$c(A + B) = cA + cB$$ $$(c + d)A = cA + dA$$ $$(cd)A = c(dA)$$ $$1 \cdot A = A$$

Again, nothing surprising. The interesting laws all involve matrix-matrix multiplication.

---

## Matrix Multiplication — The Setup

For $A$ of size $m \times n$ and $B$ of size $n \times p$, the product $AB$ is $m \times p$ with entries:

$$(AB)_{ij} = \sum_{k=1}^{n} a_{ik} b_{kj}$$

Row $i$ of $A$ dotted with column $j$ of $B$. The inner dimensions must match: $A$ is $m \times \mathbf{n}$ and $B$ is $\mathbf{n} \times p$.

There are four ways to think about this product, and each illuminates something different:

**1. Entry by entry:** $(AB)_{ij} = \mathbf{a}_i^T \mathbf{b}_j$ — dot product of row $i$ of $A$ with column $j$ of $B$.

**2. Column by column:** each column of $AB$ is $A$ times the corresponding column of $B$. $$AB = A \begin{pmatrix} | & & | \ \mathbf{b}_1 & \cdots & \mathbf{b}_p \ | & & | \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} | & & | \ A\mathbf{b}_1 & \cdots & A\mathbf{b}_p \ | & & | \end{pmatrix}$$

**3. Row by row:** each row of $AB$ is the corresponding row of $A$ times $B$.

**4. Sum of outer products:** $AB = \sum_{k=1}^{n} \mathbf{a}_k \mathbf{b}_k^T$, where $\mathbf{a}_k$ is the $k$-th column of $A$ and $\mathbf{b}_k^T$ is the $k$-th row of $B$. Each term is a rank-1 matrix.

The outer product view (4) is underrated. It shows that $AB$ is a sum of $n$ rank-1 matrices — useful for understanding rank and for thinking about low-rank approximations.

## The Laws That Hold

**Associativity:** $$A(BC) = (AB)C$$

**Proof:** Both sides have the same size. The $(i,j)$ entry of $A(BC)$ is:

$$\sum_k a_{ik} (BC)_{kj} = \sum_k a_{ik} \sum_\ell b_{k\ell} c_{\ell j} = \sum_k \sum_\ell a_{ik} b_{k\ell} c_{\ell j}$$

The $(i,j)$ entry of $(AB)C$ is:

$$\sum_\ell (AB)_{i\ell} c_{\ell j} = \sum_\ell \left(\sum_k a_{ik} b_{k\ell}\right) c_{\ell j} = \sum_k \sum_\ell a_{ik} b_{k\ell} c_{\ell j}$$

Identical. So associativity holds. The proof is just rearranging a finite sum — no trick needed.

Consequence: I can write $ABC$ without parentheses and it is unambiguous.

---

**Left distributivity:** $$A(B + C) = AB + AC$$

**Right distributivity:** $$(A + B)C = AC + BC$$

Both follow from expanding the sums entry by entry. Straightforward.

---

**Powers of a square matrix:** $$A^p = \underbrace{A \cdot A \cdots A}_{p \text{ times}}$$

Well-defined by associativity. $A^0 = I$ by convention.

---

## The Law That Fails — Commutativity

$$AB \neq BA \quad \text{in general}$$

This is not just "sometimes false" — it is almost always false. Let me be explicit.

**Dimension obstruction:** if $A$ is $m \times n$ and $B$ is $n \times p$, then $AB$ is $m \times p$ but $BA$ requires $B$ to be $p \times m$ — a completely different size condition. So $BA$ may not even be defined when $AB$ is.

**Even when both are defined:** take $A$ and $B$ both $2 \times 2$:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

$$ AB = \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot1 + 1\cdot1 & 1\cdot0 + 1\cdot0 \ 0\cdot1 + 0\cdot1 & 0\cdot0 + 0\cdot0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

$$ BA = \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot1 + 0\cdot0 & 1\cdot1 + 0\cdot0 \ 1\cdot1 + 0\cdot0 & 1\cdot1 + 0\cdot0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

$AB \neq BA$. Not even close.

**The cancellation law also fails.** In ordinary algebra, $AB = AC$ and $A \neq 0$ implies $B = C$. Not so for matrices:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 5 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad C = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 7 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

$$AB = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = AC$$

but $B \neq C$. Left-cancellation fails because $A$ is singular — it has a nontrivial nullspace.

**Also:** $AB = 0$ does not imply $A = 0$ or $B = 0$.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Two nonzero matrices multiply to zero. This is impossible for nonzero real numbers.

## Transpose Laws

The transpose of $A$ is the matrix $A^T$ with $(A^T)_{ij} = a_{ji}$ — rows and columns are swapped.

For $A$ of size $m \times n$: $A^T$ is $n \times m$.

**Law 1:** $$(A^T)^T = A$$

Transposing twice returns the original. Obvious from the definition.

**Law 2:** $$(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T$$

Componentwise: $(A + B)_{ij} = a_{ij} + b_{ij}$, so $(A+B)^T_{ij} = a_{ji} + b_{ji} = (A^T)_{ij} + (B^T)_{ij}$. Done.

**Law 3 — the reversal law:** $$(AB)^T = B^T A^T$$

This is the nontrivial one. The order reverses.

**Proof:** The $(i,j)$ entry of $(AB)^T$ is the $(j,i)$ entry of $AB$: $$(AB)^T_{ij} = (AB)_{ji} = \sum_k a_{jk} b_{ki}$$

The $(i,j)$ entry of $B^T A^T$ is: $$(B^T A^T)_{ij} = \sum_k (B^T)_{ik} (A^T)_{kj} = \sum_k b_{ki} a_{jk}$$

These are equal (multiplication of scalars commutes). So $(AB)^T = B^T A^T$.

**Generalizing:** for a product of any number of matrices: $$(ABC \cdots Z)^T = Z^T \cdots C^T B^T A^T$$

The entire product reverses. I think of this as: "transpose reverses the order." The same reversal appears in inverse laws.

**Symmetric matrices:** $A$ is symmetric if $A^T = A$. For any matrix $A$, the product $A^T A$ is always symmetric: $$(A^T A)^T = A^T (A^T)^T = A^T A \checkmark$$

This is used constantly in least squares and in the definition of positive definiteness.

## Inverse Laws

For a square invertible matrix $A$:

$$A^{-1} A = I \qquad \text{and} \qquad A A^{-1} = I$$

Both must hold — a one-sided inverse of a square matrix is automatically a two-sided inverse.

**Law 1:** $$(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$$

The inverse of $A^{-1}$ is $A$ itself.

**Law 2:** $$(cA)^{-1} = \frac{1}{c} A^{-1}$$

for scalar $c \neq 0$.

**Law 3 — the reversal law:** $$(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1} A^{-1}$$

Order reverses, just like for the transpose.

**Proof:** I need to check that $B^{-1} A^{-1}$ is indeed the inverse of $AB$: $$(AB)(B^{-1} A^{-1}) = A(B B^{-1}) A^{-1} = A I A^{-1} = A A^{-1} = I$$ $$(B^{-1} A^{-1})(AB) = B^{-1}(A^{-1} A)B = B^{-1} I B = B^{-1} B = I$$

Both sides give $I$, so $B^{-1} A^{-1}$ is the inverse of $AB$.

**The mnemonic:** put on socks then shoes; take off shoes then socks. The reversal is forced by the order of multiplication.

**Generalizing:** $$(ABC)^{-1} = C^{-1} B^{-1} A^{-1}$$

**Law 4 — transpose of inverse:** $$(A^{-1})^T = (A^T)^{-1}$$

**Proof:** Start from $AA^{-1} = I$. Transpose both sides: $$(AA^{-1})^T = I^T = I$$ $$(A^{-1})^T A^T = I$$

So $(A^{-1})^T$ is the left inverse of $A^T$. Similarly from $A^{-1} A = I$: $$A^T (A^{-1})^T = I$$

So $(A^{-1})^T$ is also the right inverse of $A^T$, meaning $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$.

Consequence: for orthogonal matrices where $A^{-1} = A^T$, this law says $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T = (A^T)^T = A$. Consistent.

## What Commutes With Everything — Scalar Matrices

The only $n \times n$ matrices that commute with every $n \times n$ matrix are the **scalar matrices** $cI$ for $c \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$ (cI)A = cA = A(cI) $$

This is a theorem, not just an observation. The center of the matrix algebra is ${cI : c \in \mathbb{R}}$.

So if someone hands me a matrix $B$ and tells me $BA = AB$ for all $A$, I can immediately conclude $B = cI$ for some scalar $c$. That is a strong constraint.

|---|---|---|
|Addition commutativity|$A + B = B + A$|Yes|
|Addition associativity|$(A+B)+C = A+(B+C)$|Yes|
|Multiplication associativity|$(AB)C = A(BC)$|Yes|

|Transpose of inverse|$(A^{-1})^T = (A^T)^{-1}$|Yes (if invertible)|

## The Pattern — Reversal Under Two Operations

Both transpose and inverse satisfy a reversal law for products:

$$(AB)^T = B^T A^T \qquad \text{and} \qquad (AB)^{-1} = B^{-1} A^{-1}$$

This is not a coincidence. Both operations are **anti-homomorphisms** of the matrix algebra — they reverse the order of multiplication. The underlying reason is the same: the operations "undo" the matrix product, and undoing a composition of operations requires going in reverse order.

Combined: if $A$ is orthogonal ($A^{-1} = A^T$), then:

$$(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1} A^{-1} = B^T A^T = (AB)^T$$

So the product of two orthogonal matrices is orthogonal. The reversal laws interact cleanly.
