The $2 \times 2$ case is easy to draw. The $n \times n$ case is where the full theory lives but is impossible to visualize. The $3 \times 3$ case is the sweet spot — it is the last system I can see completely, in three dimensions, with the row and column pictures fully geometric. After this, I have to trust the algebra.

For $n = 3$, I am _incredibly lucky_:

```
n = 3   →   vectors live in R^3   →   I can draw them
            planes exist in R^3   →   I can draw them
            transformations act on R^3 → I can see them

n = 4   →   vectors live in R^4   →   impossible to draw
            hyperplanes in R^4    →   impossible to draw
            transformations       →   algebra only
```

The mathematics works perfectly in any dimension. It is _us_ who cannot keep up past 3D. This is why I want to understand the $3 \times 3$ case as deeply as possible — the geometric intuition built here carries into higher dimensions, even when I can no longer see them.

The system I will carry through every picture:
$2x - y + z = 3$ 
$x + 3y - z = 5$
$-x + y + 4z = 2$


> A $3 \times 3$ matrix is a machine that acts on all of $\mathbb{R}^3$ at once. It grabs the entire coordinate system and warps it — stretching, squishing, rotating, collapsing.

$$A : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$$

There are three lenses through which to read the same matrix. Each one reveals something different. The same equation $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ looks completely different depending on which lens I use.


## Matrix Form
Always write the system as $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ first. This is the compact form that makes structure visible.
$$ A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 & -1 \\ -1 & 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix}=
\begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} $$
$A$ is $3 \times 3$. The unknown is a vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^3$. The right-hand side $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^3$. Everything that follows is just two ways of reading this one equation.

### 1) The Transformation View
_What does this matrix actually do to space?_
A matrix is a function. It eats a vector and spits out a new one: $A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{w}$. But the magic is that it does this consistently to every point in space at once — the whole coordinate grid moves together, following a single rule.

The transformation is _linear_ — straight lines stay straight, parallel lines stay parallel, and the origin stays fixed. The grid warps but never curves.

| Transformation | What it does to space                                |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| Rotation       | Spins everything around an axis                      |
| Scaling        | Stretches or squishes in some direction              |
| Reflection     | Flips space like a mirror                            |
| Shearing       | Tilts space, like leaning a stack of papers sideways |
| Projection     | Collapses a dimension — squashes 3D into 2D          |
The left grid is your original space. After multiplying by $A$, it becomes the warped grid on the right. Every point moved — but the transformation followed a _consistent rule_.

### 2) The Column Picture
_Where do the axes go after the transformation?_
Regroup the same system by columns:
$$ x \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}-y \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}
- z \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} $$
Let me name these:
$$ \mathbf{a}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \\ -1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{a}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 3 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{a}_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ -1 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 5 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} $$
The question is now purely geometric in $\mathbb{R}^3$: can I find scalars $x, y, z$ such that
$$x\mathbf{a}_1 + y\mathbf{a}_2 + z\mathbf{a}_3 = \mathbf{b}$$
Think of it as: I am standing at the origin with three moves available — I can go $x$ units in the direction of $\mathbf{a}_1$, $y$ units in the direction of $\mathbf{a}_2$, $z$ units in the direction of $\mathbf{a}_3$. Can I reach $\mathbf{b}$?

Write the matrix as three column vectors:
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & | \\ \mathbf{a}_1 & \mathbf{a}_2 & \mathbf{a}_3 \\ | & | & | \end{bmatrix}$$
These columns tell me exactly where the standard basis vectors land:
- $\mathbf{a}_1$ = where $(1,0,0)$ — the $x$-axis — lands after the transformation
- $\mathbf{a}_2$ = where $(0,1,0)$ — the $y$-axis — lands after the transformation
- $\mathbf{a}_3$ = where $(0,0,1)$ — the $z$-axis — lands after the transformation
The three columns together form a **parallelepiped** — the image of the unit cube under $A$.

>  Are the columns **linearly independent**?
> - **Yes** → parallelepiped has real 3D volume → transformation is invertible
> - **No** → columns are coplanar → space **collapses** into a plane or line → not invertible

If the three columns span all of $\mathbb{R}^3$ (linearly independent, $A$ invertible), then I can reach every $\mathbf{b}$. If the columns are coplanar (linearly dependent), I can only reach vectors in that plane — most $\mathbf{b}$ are unreachable. The column picture makes the existence question about $\mathbf{b}$ and the column space completely transparent.

### 3) The Row Picture

_What constraints does the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ impose?_

Each row of $A$ gives one equation. Each equation $\mathbf{a}_i^T \mathbf{x} = b_i$ defines a **plane** in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — the set of all points $(x, y, z)$ whose dot product with the normal vector $\mathbf{a}_i$ equals $b_i$.

When I solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, I am asking: where do these three planes intersect?

For my system:

**Plane 1:** $2x - y + z = 3$, normal vector $\mathbf{n}_1 = (2, -1, 1)$

**Plane 2:** $x + 3y - z = 5$, normal vector $\mathbf{n}_2 = (1, 3, -1)$

**Plane 3:** $-x + y + 4z = 2$, normal vector $\mathbf{n}_3 = (-1, 1, 4)$

The plane is perpendicular to its normal vector, sitting at signed distance $b_i / |\mathbf{n}_i|$ from the origin.

**The solution is the point where all three planes intersect.** Generically in $\mathbb{R}^3$: two planes intersect in a line; three planes intersect in a point.

```
   Case 1 — unique solution       Case 2 — no solution       Case 3 — infinite solutions

        pi_1                        pi_1  ===                    pi_1 \
       / \                          pi_2  ===                    pi_2 /   <- shared line
      * <- point                    pi_3  ===                    (coincide)
       \ /
        pi_2, pi_3

   planes meet at 1 point        planes never meet            planes share a line
   -> unique solution            -> no solution               -> infinite solutions
```

When things go wrong:

- Two planes parallel → no intersection → no solution
- Three planes sharing a common line → infinitely many solutions
- All three planes parallel → no solution
- Three planes reducing to the same plane → infinitely many solutions

The row picture makes the geometry of failure completely clear. But it tells me nothing efficient about how to _find_ the solution.

## The Determinant — The Volume Story

The determinant is the single most important scalar attached to a matrix. It answers:

> By what factor does this transformation scale volume?

$$\det(A) = \text{signed volume of the parallelepiped formed by the columns}$$

```
   det(A) > 0                  det(A) = 0                  det(A) < 0
   
   [solid box]                 [flat sheet]                 [flipped box]
   
   3D → 3D                     3D → 2D plane               3D → 3D
   volume preserved             COLLAPSED                   orientation flipped
   orientation kept             not invertible              like a mirror image
```

| $\det(A)$ | Volume | Orientation | Invertible? |
| --------- | ------ | ----------- | ----------- |
| $> 0$     | Scaled | Preserved   | Yes         |
| $= 0$     | Zero   | Lost        | No          |
| $< 0$     | Scaled | Flipped     | Yes         |

If $\det(A) = 0$, the matrix is not invertible. 3D space has been squashed into a 2D plane (or worse, a line or a point). Information is irreversibly lost. There is no way to undo the transformation.

## Elimination — Solving the System

The workhorse. I augment the matrix and apply row operations to reach upper triangular form.

$$ [A \mid \mathbf{b}] = \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \ 1 & 3 & -1 & 5 \ -1 & 1 & 4 & 2 \end{array}\right) $$

**Step 1.** Use row 1 as the pivot row. Eliminate the first column below the pivot $a_{11} = 2$.

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \dfrac{1}{2} R_1$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \ 0 & \tfrac{7}{2} & -\tfrac{3}{2} & \tfrac{7}{2} \ -1 & 1 & 4 & 2 \end{array}\right) $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 + \dfrac{1}{2} R_1$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \ 0 & \tfrac{7}{2} & -\tfrac{3}{2} & \tfrac{7}{2} \ 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{9}{2} & \tfrac{7}{2} \end{array}\right) $$

**Step 2.** Use row 2 as the pivot row. Eliminate column 2 below pivot $a_{22} = \tfrac{7}{2}$.

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - \dfrac{1/2}{7/2} R_2 = R_3 - \dfrac{1}{7} R_2$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & -1 & 1 & 3 \ 0 & \tfrac{7}{2} & -\tfrac{3}{2} & \tfrac{7}{2} \ 0 & 0 & \tfrac{33}{7} & 3 \end{array}\right) $$

Three pivots: $2$, $\dfrac{7}{2}$, $\dfrac{33}{7}$. All nonzero. $A$ is invertible.

**Back substitution:**

From row 3: $\dfrac{33}{7} z = 3 \Rightarrow z = \dfrac{7}{11}$

From row 2: $\dfrac{7}{2} y - \dfrac{3}{2} \cdot \dfrac{7}{11} = \dfrac{7}{2} \Rightarrow \dfrac{7}{2} y = \dfrac{7}{2} + \dfrac{21}{22} = \dfrac{98}{22} \Rightarrow y = \dfrac{14}{11}$

From row 1: $2x = 3 + y - z = 3 + \dfrac{14}{11} - \dfrac{7}{11} = \dfrac{40}{11} \Rightarrow x = \dfrac{20}{11}$

$$ \boxed{\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 20/11 \ 14/11 \ 7/11 \end{pmatrix}} $$

**Verification (column picture):**

$$ \frac{20}{11} \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 1 \ -1 \end{pmatrix}

- \frac{14}{11} \begin{pmatrix} -1 \ 3 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}
- \frac{7}{11} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ -1 \ 4 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{11} \begin{pmatrix} 40 - 14 + 7 \ 20 + 42 - 7 \ -20 + 14 + 28 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{11} \begin{pmatrix} 33 \ 55 \ 22 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \ 5 \ 2 \end{pmatrix} \checkmark $$

---

## What the Pivots Are Telling Me

The three pivots $2,, \tfrac{7}{2},, \tfrac{33}{7}$ are the diagonal entries of $U$ (upper triangular form). Their signs determine the signs of the eigenvalues (by Sylvester's law). Their product gives $\det(A)$:

$$\det(A) = 2 \cdot \frac{7}{2} \cdot \frac{33}{7} = 33$$

All pivots nonzero $\Rightarrow$ $\det(A) \neq 0$ $\Rightarrow$ $A$ is invertible $\Rightarrow$ unique solution for every $\mathbf{b}$ $\Rightarrow$ the three planes always meet at exactly one point $\Rightarrow$ the three columns span all of $\mathbb{R}^3$.

Everything is equivalent. The pivots are the computational witness to all of it.

## Singular Cases in 3x3 — The Geometry

When $A$ is singular, the row and column pictures both degenerate.

### Singular Case 1 — No Solution

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 3 \ 2 & 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \ y \ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \ 5 \ 9 \end{pmatrix} $$

After elimination:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 3 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) $$

Row 3 says $0 = 1$. No solution.

**Row picture:** planes 2 and 3 are parallel (same left-hand side coefficients after subtracting row 1) but have different right-hand sides. Parallel planes do not intersect.

**Column picture:** $\mathbf{b} = (3,5,9)^T$ is not in $\mathcal{C}(A)$ — the columns only span a 2-dimensional subspace (a plane through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$), and $(3,5,9)^T$ misses that plane.

### Singular Case 2 — Infinitely Many Solutions

Change the right-hand side to $\mathbf{b} = (3, 5, 8)^T$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 1 & 1 & 3 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) $$

Row 3 says $0 = 0$. Two pivots, one free variable ($z$). Infinitely many solutions.

**Row picture:** planes 2 and 3 are the same plane (after adjusting for row 1). Two distinct planes meet in a line; the third coincides with one of them. The solution set is that entire line.

**Column picture:** $\mathbf{b}$ lies in the column space (a 2-dimensional plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$), and because the columns are dependent, multiple combinations reach $\mathbf{b}$. The solution set is a 1-dimensional affine subspace — a line in $\mathbb{R}^3$, not through the origin.

---

## The Complete Solution in the Singular Case

For the infinitely-many-solutions case, set $z = t$ (free parameter):

From row 2: $y = 2 - 2t$

From row 1: $x = 3 - y - z = 3 - (2 - 2t) - t = 1 + t$

So:

$$ \mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} + t \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ -2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R} $$

The particular solution $\mathbf{x}_p = (1, 2, 0)^T$ satisfies $A\mathbf{x}_p = \mathbf{b}$.

The null vector $(1, -2, 1)^T$ satisfies $A\mathbf{x}_n = \mathbf{0}$ — it lives in the nullspace of $A$.

This is the structure $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_p + \mathbf{x}_n$ from the general theory. Here the nullspace is 1-dimensional (one free variable), so the solution set is a line through $\mathbf{x}_p$ in the direction of $\mathbf{x}_n$.

## A Note on the Classic Misconception

It is tempting to say "a $3 \times 3$ matrix represents a 3D plane." This is wrong in an important way.

- A single _row_ of the matrix defines a plane (one equation: $ax + by + cz = d$)
- The matrix itself is not a plane — it is an operator that acts on all of 3D space
- A plane is a 2D surface _living inside_ 3D space; the matrix is a _transformation of_ that space

## LU Decomposition — What Elimination Is Actually Doing

Gaussian elimination on $A$ is really factoring $A = LU$ where:

- $U$ is upper triangular — the result after forward elimination
- $L$ is lower triangular — records the multipliers used

For my original example:

$$ L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ \tfrac{1}{2} & 1 & 0 \ -\tfrac{1}{2} & \tfrac{1}{7} & 1 \end{pmatrix} \qquad U = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 1 \ 0 & \tfrac{7}{2} & -\tfrac{3}{2} \ 0 & 0 & \tfrac{33}{7} \end{pmatrix} $$

So $A = LU$. Solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ becomes:

1. Forward substitution: solve $L\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{b}$ for $\mathbf{c}$
2. Back substitution: solve $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{c}$ for $\mathbf{x}$

Both steps are $O(n^2)$ once $L$ and $U$ are known. The factorization itself is $O(n^3)$. The value of $LU$: if I need to solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ for many different $\mathbf{b}$, I only pay the $O(n^3)$ cost once.

## Invertibility — What It Means Geometrically

If $A$ is invertible, the unique solution is $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$.

**Row picture:** the three planes are in general position — no two parallel, no three sharing a line. They carve out a unique point.

**Column picture:** the three columns $\mathbf{a}_1, \mathbf{a}_2, \mathbf{a}_3$ are linearly independent — they do not all lie in a common plane through the origin. They form a basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$, so every $\mathbf{b}$ can be reached, and reached in exactly one way.

Invertibility is a global statement about $A$: it works for every $\mathbf{b}$, not just a particular one. A matrix is either always invertible or never invertible — it does not depend on which $\mathbf{b}$ I choose.

---

## The Mental Checklist

When I encounter any $3 \times 3$ matrix, I ask these questions in order:

1. **Transformation** — what does this do to a vector? Can I visualize it (rotation? scaling? shear)?
2. **Columns** — are the three column vectors independent? Do they span all of $\mathbb{R}^3$, or collapse into a plane?
3. **Rows** — what three planes does this define? Where do they intersect?
4. **Determinant** — is it zero? Non-zero? Positive or negative? What is the volume scaling factor?

# 🧠 Understanding 3×3 Matrices Geometrically

> _"A matrix is not just a box of numbers. It's a story about what happens to space."_

If you've ever stared at a grid of 9 numbers and wondered what they actually _mean_ — this note is for you. We're going to make 3×3 matrices feel alive, geometric, and intuitive.

---

## The Big Idea First

Before any formulas, here's the punchline:

> **A 3×3 matrix is a machine that warps 3D space.**

Think of it like this: you have a room full of points floating in 3D space. The matrix grabs that _entire room_ — every point, every direction, every distance — and transforms it. Maybe it stretches it. Maybe it squishes it flat. Maybe it flips it like a pancake. That's what a matrix does.

$$A : \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$$

There are **three lenses** you can use to see the same matrix. Each one reveals something different. Master all three, and linear algebra becomes transparent.

---

## Lens 1 — The Transformation View 🔄

_"What does this matrix actually **do** to space?"_

A matrix is a function. It eats a vector and spits out a new one:

$$A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{w}$$

But the magic is that it does this _consistently to every point in space at once_ — not just one vector. The whole coordinate grid moves together.

### Types of Transformations

| Transformation | What it does to space                                |
| -------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- |
| **Rotation**   | Spins everything around an axis                      |
| **Scaling**    | Stretches or squishes in some direction              |
| **Reflection** | Flips space like a mirror                            |
| **Shearing**   | Tilts space, like leaning a stack of papers sideways |
| **Projection** | Collapses a dimension — squashes 3D into 2D          |

## Lens 2 — The Column Picture 📦

_"Where do the axes go after the transformation?"_

Write the matrix as three column vectors side by side:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & | \ \mathbf{c_1} & \mathbf{c_2} & \mathbf{c_3} \ | & | & | \end{bmatrix}$$

These columns tell you exactly where the standard basis vectors land:

- $\mathbf{c_1}$ = where $(1,0,0)$ — the $x$-axis — lands after the transformation
- $\mathbf{c_2}$ = where $(0,1,0)$ — the $y$-axis — lands after the transformation
- $\mathbf{c_3}$ = where $(0,0,1)$ — the $z$-axis — lands after the transformation

The three columns together form a shape called a **parallelepiped** — like a tilted, stretched box. This is the _image of the unit cube_ under the transformation.

## Lens 3 — The Row Picture ✂️

_"What constraints does the system $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ impose?"_

Flip your view entirely. Each **row** of the matrix defines a **plane** in 3D space:

$$\text{row}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x} = b_1 \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \text{a plane in } \mathbb{R}^3$$

When you solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, you are asking:

> _"Where do these three planes intersect?"_

## Why 3×3 is the Last Fully Visualizable Case 🌌

Here's a humbling thought. For $n = 3$, we are _incredibly lucky_:

```
n = 3   →   vectors live in ℝ³   →   we can draw them   ✅
            planes exist in ℝ³   →   we can draw them   ✅
            transformations act on ℝ³ → we can see them ✅

n = 4   →   vectors live in ℝ⁴   →   impossible to draw ❌
            hyperplanes in ℝ⁴    →   impossible to draw ❌
            transformations       →   algebra only       ❌
```

The mathematics works perfectly in any dimension. It's _us_ who can't keep up past 3D. This is why mastering the 3D case so deeply matters — the geometric intuition you build here carries you into higher dimensions, even when you can no longer see.

## ❌ The Classic Misconception

> ❌ _"A 3×3 matrix represents a 3D plane"_

Here's the correction:

- A single **row** of the matrix defines a plane (one equation: $ax + by + cz = d$)
- The **matrix itself** is not a plane — it is an operator that acts on all of 3D space
- A plane is a 2D surface _living inside_ 3D space; the matrix is a _transformation of_ that space

## 📋 The Mental Checklist

When you encounter any 3×3 matrix, ask these questions in order:

1. **Transformation** — What does this do to a vector? Can I visualize it (rotation? scaling? shear)?
2. **Columns** — Are the three column vectors independent? Do they span all of 3D space, or collapse into a plane?
3. **Rows** — What three planes does this define? Where do they intersect?
4. **Determinant** — Is it zero? Non-zero? Positive or negative? What's the volume scaling factor?

---

## 💬 Day-to-Day Intuition

When you see a 3×3 matrix, don't see 9 numbers in a grid.

See a **machine** that:

- **Grabs** the entire 3D coordinate system
- **Moves** each axis to a new position (the columns tell you where)
- **Slices** space with three constraint planes (the rows define these)
- **Scales** volume by the determinant — zero means total collapse

The numbers are just the _description_ of the machine. The machine itself is a living, geometric action on space.
