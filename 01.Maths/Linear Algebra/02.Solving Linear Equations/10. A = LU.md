The first move in [[02.Gaussian Elimination]]: subtract $\ell_{21}$ times row 1 from row 2 to kill the $(2,1)$ entry. This is the same as left-multiplying by:
$E_{21} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ -\ell_{21} & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$ where $\ell_{21} = \dfrac{a_{21}}{a_{11}}$ is the **multiplier**.
More generally, the elementary matrix that subtracts $\ell_{ij}$ times row $j$ from row $i$ (for $i > j$) is: $E_{ij} = I - \ell_{ij} e_i e_j^T$
where $e_i, e_j$ are the standard basis vectors. This matrix differs from $I$ in exactly one off-diagonal entry: position $(i,j)$ contains $-\ell_{ij}$.

**Key properties of $E_{ij}$:**
- $\det(E_{ij}) = 1$ (determinant is unchanged by row operations — this is why elimination preserves the determinant up to sign)
- $E_{ij}^{-1} = I + \ell_{ij} e_i e_j^T$ — just flip the sign. The inverse of "subtract $\ell_{ij}$ times row $j$ from row $i$" is "add it back."
$$ E_{ij}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & \cdots & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & & \ell_{ij} & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & \cdots & 0 & \cdots & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

The inverse just puts $+\ell_{ij}$ where $E_{ij}$ had $-\ell_{ij}$.


## Elimination as a Sequence of Left-Multiplications
Suppose $A$ is $3 \times 3$ and no row exchanges are needed. Elimination proceeds in two steps:
**Step 1: Eliminate column 1.**
	$E_{21}E_{31} A = \begin{pmatrix} * & * & * \\ 0 & * & * \\ 0 & * & * \end{pmatrix}$ ->where $\ell_{21} = a_{21}/a_{11}$ and $\ell_{31} = a_{31}/a_{11}$.
**Step 2: Eliminate column 2.**
	$E_{32}(E_{21}E_{31}A) = \begin{pmatrix} * & * & * \\ 0 & * & * \\ 0 & 0 & * \end{pmatrix} = U$ ->where $\ell_{32}$ uses the already-updated $(3,2)$ entry, i.e. the second pivot.

So the full statement is:
$$ E_{32} E_{31} E_{21} A = U $$
which gives:
$$ A = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1} U = LU $$

where $L = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1}$.

## $L$ Is Lower Triangular with the Multipliers
Here is the remarkable fact that makes $LU$ so clean. When the $E_{ij}^{-1}$ are multiplied together **without row exchanges**, the multipliers simply drop into their natural positions in $L$. There is no interference between them.
For $3 \times 3$: $$ L = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1}

\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \ell_{21} & 1 & 0 \\ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$
The multiplier $\ell_{ij}$ sits in position $(i,j)$ — exactly where it "came from" during elimination. The diagonal of $L$ is all ones (**unit lower triangular**). There is no need to multiply out the three matrices; just read off the multipliers.

**Why does this work?** When $j < k$, the matrices $E_{ij}^{-1}$ and $E_{ik}^{-1}$ act on different rows and do not interact:
$$ (I + \ell_{31} e_3 e_1^T)(I + \ell_{21} e_2 e_1^T) = I + \ell_{21} e_2 e_1^T + \ell_{31} e_3 e_1^T $$
because the cross term $e_3 e_1^T \cdot e_2 e_1^T = e_3 (e_1^T e_2) e_1^T = 0$ (since $e_1^T e_2 = 0$). The multipliers from different columns do not interact. This is the algebraic reason the $L$ matrix has such a clean form.

**This argument breaks the moment you do a row exchange.** If rows are swapped between elimination steps, the multipliers no longer sit cleanly in $L$. This is precisely why row exchanges force us to introduce the permutation matrix $P$ and write $PA = LU$ instead.

## The $LU$ Factorization:

> [!Theorem] Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$. If all leading principal submatrices $A_k$ (the top-left $k \times k$ submatrices, for $k = 1, \ldots, n-1$) are nonsingular, then $A$ has a unique $LU$ factorization: $$A = LU$$ where $L$ is unit lower triangular (ones on the diagonal, zeros above) and $U$ is upper triangular. The diagonal entries of $U$ are the **pivots**.

**Proof sketch.** By induction on $n$. Write: $A = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12}^T \\ a_{21} & A_{22} \end{pmatrix}$
where $a_{11}$ is a scalar (the first pivot, nonzero by assumption), $a_{12}^T$ is the first row (remaining), $a_{21}$ is the first column (remaining), and $A_{22}$ is the $(n-1) \times (n-1)$ trailing submatrix.

Define $\ell = a_{21}/a_{11}$, the column of multipliers. Then: $$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ -\ell & I \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12}^T \\ a_{21} & A_{22} \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12}^T \\ 0 & A_{22} - \ell a_{12}^T \end{pmatrix} $$
The trailing matrix $A_{22} - \ell a_{12}^T$ is the **[[08.Schur Complement]] of $a_{11}$ in $A$** — there it is again. By the inductive hypothesis, this Schur complement has an $LU$ factorization (its leading submatrices are nonsingular because they are leading submatrices of $A$, which are nonsingular by assumption). Assembling the factors gives the result. $\blacksquare$


## Example: $3 \times 3$ Full Elimination
Let: $A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 4 & 3 & 3 \\ 8 & 7 & 9 \end{pmatrix}$
**Step 1: Eliminate column 1.**
	Multipliers: $\ell_{21} = 4/2 = 2$, $\ell_{31} = 8/2 = 4$.
	$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 4R_1$: $\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$

**Step 2: Eliminate column 2.**
	Multiplier: $\ell_{32} = 3/1 = 3$.
	$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_2$: $U = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$
	**Read off $L$:** just place the multipliers in their positions.
	$L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 4 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$

$$
\textbf{Verify } A = LU
$$
$L =\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & 0 \\2 & 1 & 0 \\4 & 3 & 1\end{pmatrix},\quad U =\begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 & 1 \\0 & 1 & 1 \\0 & 0 & 2\end{pmatrix}$
$LU =\begin{pmatrix}1 & 0 & 0 \\2 & 1 & 0 \\4 & 3 & 1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 & 1 \\0 & 1 & 1 \\0 & 0 & 2\end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix}1\cdot2 & 1\cdot1 & 1\cdot1 \\2\cdot2 + 0 & 2\cdot1 + 1 & 2\cdot1 + 1 \\4\cdot2 + 0 + 0 & 4\cdot1 + 3\cdot1 + 0 & 4\cdot1 + 3\cdot1 + 2\end{pmatrix}$
$=\begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 & 1 \\4 & 3 & 3 \\8 & 7 & 9\end{pmatrix}= A$

$$
\text{Pivots: } 2,\,1,\,2 \quad (\text{diagonal entries of } U)
$$


## Using $A = LU$ to Solve $Ax = b$

This is the point of the factorization. $Ax = b$ becomes $LUx = b$. Split into two triangular systems:

$$ Lc = b \quad \text{(forward substitution)} $$ $$ Ux = c \quad \text{(back substitution)} $$

Each triangular system is $O(n^2)$. If you already have $L$ and $U$, solving a new right-hand side $b$ costs $2 \times O(n^2)$ instead of the full $O(n^3)$ of Gaussian elimination from scratch.

**This is the key point for applications.** In practice — FEM solvers, structural analysis, any problem where you solve $Ax = b_1, Ax = b_2, \ldots$ for many right-hand sides with the same $A$ — you factor once ($O(n^3)$) and solve many times ($O(n^2)$ each). The factorization is the expensive investment; each solve is cheap.

**Forward substitution on the example above.** Take $b = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \ 10 \ 26 \end{pmatrix}$.

Solve $Lc = b$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 4 & 3 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c_1 \\ c_2 \\ c_3 \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 10 \\ 26 \end{pmatrix} $$
$c_1 = 4$, 
$c_2 = 10 - 2(4) = 2$, 
$c_3 = 26 - 4(4) - 3(2) = 26 - 16 - 6 = 4$.
Solve $Ux = c$: $$ \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \\ x_3 \end{pmatrix}=\begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 2 \\ 4 \end{pmatrix} $$
$x_3 = 2$,
$x_2 = 2 - 1(2) = 0$,
$x_1 = (4 - 1(0) - 1(2))/2 = 1$.

So $x = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix}$. 
Check: $Ax = \begin{pmatrix} 2+0+2 \\ 4+0+6 \\ 8+0+18 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \\ 10 \\ 26 \end{pmatrix}$. $\checkmark$


## The $LDU$ Factorization
It is sometimes cleaner to separate the pivots from $U$ into a diagonal matrix $D$. Write $U = DU'$ where $D = \text{diag}(u_{11}, u_{22}, \ldots, u_{nn})$ and $U'$ is **unit** upper triangular (ones on the diagonal). Then:
$$A = LDU'$$
For the example above:
$$ D = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, \quad U' = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1/2 & 1/2 \\ 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$
**For symmetric matrices:** if $A = A^T$, then the $LDU$ factorization satisfies $U' = L^T$ (because the factorization is unique and $A = LDU' = (LDU')^T = (U')^T D^T L^T = (U')^T D L^T$, and by uniqueness $L = (U')^T$). So for symmetric $A$:

$$A = LDL^T$$

This is cheaper to compute than a full $LU$ (only need $L$ and $D$) and is the basis for the **Cholesky factorization** when $A \succ 0$: in that case all pivots in $D$ are positive, so $D = D^{1/2}D^{1/2}$, and:

$$A = LDL^T = (LD^{1/2})(LD^{1/2})^T = R^T R$$

where $R = D^{1/2}L^T$ is upper triangular. That is Cholesky: $A = R^T R$.


## When Row Exchanges Are Needed: $PA = LU$
If a zero pivot is encountered during elimination, we must swap rows. Even if the pivot is not zero but is very small, we should swap for numerical stability (partial pivoting). Let $P$ be the permutation matrix encoding all the row swaps. Then:
$$PA = LU$$
$P$ reorders the rows of $A$ so that the reordered matrix can be factored without pivoting. Every nonsingular matrix has a $PA = LU$ factorization for some permutation $P$.
**Example.** Consider:
$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$
The $(1,1)$ entry is $0$ — we cannot pivot. Swap rows:
$$ P = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad PA = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$
Now $PA$ is already upper triangular, so $L = I$ and $U = PA$:
$$ PA = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = LU $$
In practice, LAPACK's `dgetrf` always computes $PA = LU$ with partial pivoting.
see more[[16.PA = LU]]

## Counting Operations

For an $n \times n$ matrix, the operation count for $LU$ factorization is:
$$\text{flops} \approx \frac{2}{3}n^3$$

Each elimination step on column $k$ involves $(n-k)$ rows, each requiring $(n-k+1)$ multiplications and additions. Summing:

$$\sum_{k=1}^{n-1}(n-k)^2 \approx \int_0^n x^2, dx = \frac{n^3}{3}$$

and with the multiplications and additions together it becomes $\frac{2}{3}n^3$. For large $n$ this is the dominant cost. Forward and back substitution each cost $O(n^2)$, which is negligible.

This is why the factorization is done once and reused: each subsequent solve for a new $b$ costs $O(n^2)$ instead of $O(n^3)$.
see more[[12.The Cost of Elimination]]

## Connection to the Determinant

From $A = LU$:

$$\det(A) = \det(L)\det(U)$$

Since $L$ is unit lower triangular, $\det(L) = 1$. Since $U$ is upper triangular:

$$\det(U) = u_{11} \cdot u_{22} \cdots u_{nn} = \prod_{k=1}^n p_k$$

where $p_k$ are the pivots. Therefore:

$$\det(A) = \prod_{k=1}^n p_k$$

**The determinant is the product of the pivots.** This is one of the cleanest consequences of $LU$ and one Strang emphasizes repeatedly in 18.06. If any pivot is zero, $\det(A) = 0$ — consistent with singularity. If we did row swaps, each swap introduces a factor of $-1$, so:

$$\det(A) = (-1)^s \prod_{k=1}^n p_k$$

where $s$ is the number of row swaps.


## Uniqueness of $LU$

> **Theorem.** If $A$ has an $LU$ factorization ($L$ unit lower triangular, $U$ upper triangular), it is unique.

**Proof.** Suppose $A = L_1 U_1 = L_2 U_2$. Then:

$$L_2^{-1} L_1 = U_2 U_1^{-1}$$

The left side is unit lower triangular (product of unit lower triangular matrices). The right side is upper triangular. A matrix that is simultaneously unit lower triangular and upper triangular must be $I$ (look at it: below-diagonal entries are $0$ from the right side, and $0$ from the left side; diagonal entries are $1$ from the unit lower triangular condition; above-diagonal entries are $0$ from the left side). So $L_2^{-1}L_1 = I$ and $U_2 U_1^{-1} = I$, giving $L_1 = L_2$ and $U_1 = U_2$. $\blacksquare$
