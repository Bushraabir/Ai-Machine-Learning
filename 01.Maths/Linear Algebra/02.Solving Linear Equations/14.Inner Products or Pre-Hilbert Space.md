An **inner product** on a real vector space \( V \) is a function:
$$
\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle : V \times V \to \mathbb{R}
$$
that takes two vectors and returns a real number.
#### Axioms:For all  $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in V$ and $\alpha \in \mathbb{R}$:
### 1. Symmetry
	$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u} \rangle$
### 2. Linearity (First Argument)
	$\langle \alpha \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{v} \rangle=\alpha \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle+\langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{v} \rangle$

### 3. Positive Definiteness
	$\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \ge 0$
	$\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0 \iff \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$
Using **symmetry + linearity**, you also get:
$$
\langle \mathbf{u}, \alpha \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w} \rangle
=
\alpha \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle
+
\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{w} \rangle
$$
So the inner product is **linear in both arguments** (over real numbers).

>[!note] Inner product = generalized **dot product**
>
It measures:
-length → $\langle v, v \rangle$
-angle → via cosine
-orthogonality → $\langle u, v \rangle = 0$


## The Standard Dot Product in $\mathbb{R}^n$
The canonical example. For $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:
$$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^{n} u_i v_i$$
Written out as a matrix product for $n = 4$:
$$\mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 & u_2 & u_3 & u_4 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \\ v_4 \end{bmatrix} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + u_3 v_3 + u_4 v_4$$

Verification of axioms is immediate. The interesting question is not whether this satisfies (IP1)–(IP3) but _why this particular operation tells us something geometric_. see more[[11.Length and dot product]]

## The Geometric Meaning — Where It Gets Interesting
The inner product is a machine for measuring **angles and lengths** in a vector space. Here is the precise connection.
**Definition (norm induced by the inner product):** $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}$
This is the "length" that the inner product defines. For the standard dot product in $\mathbb{R}^n$, this is just the Euclidean norm.

**Theorem (Cauchy-Schwarz):** $|\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle| \leq |\mathbf{u}| \cdot |\mathbf{v}|$
with equality if and only if $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly dependent (one is a scalar multiple of the other).

_Proof._ Consider the function $f(t) = \langle \mathbf{u} + t\mathbf{v},\ \mathbf{u} + t\mathbf{v} \rangle \geq 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Expanding by linearity and symmetry:
$$f(t) = \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u} \rangle + 2t\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle + t^2 \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \geq 0$$
This is a non-negative quadratic in $t$, so its discriminant must be non-positive:
$$4\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle^2 - 4\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{u} \rangle \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle \leq 0$$
Dividing by 4:
$$\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2 |\mathbf{v}|^2$$
Taking square roots (both sides non-negative): $|\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle| \leq |\mathbf{u}| |\mathbf{v}|$. Equality holds when the discriminant is zero, i.e., the quadratic $f(t)$ has a double root, meaning $\mathbf{u} + t_0 \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ for some $t_0$, i.e., linear dependence. $\square$

**Corollary:** Because of Cauchy-Schwarz, the quantity $\frac{\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}{|\mathbf{u}| |\mathbf{v}|}$ always lies in $[-1, 1]$, so it can be interpreted as a cosine. We _define_ the angle $\theta$ between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ in the inner product space by:
$\cos \theta = \frac{\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}{|\mathbf{u}| |\mathbf{v}|}$

The inner product encodes angle. That is the geometric meaning.

## Orthogonality
$\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are **orthogonal** if $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$ This corresponds to $\theta = 90°$, i.e., the two vectors point in "perpendicular directions" in whatever geometry the inner product defines.

**Pythagorean theorem in inner product spaces:** If $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$, then $|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2$

_Proof._
$$|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}|^2 = \langle \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v},\ \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \rangle = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle + |\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2 \quad \square$$

The middle term vanishes precisely because of orthogonality.

## The Weighted Inner Product — A Non-Standard Example
Not all inner products on $\mathbb{R}^n$ are the standard dot product. Given a symmetric positive definite matrix $M$ (all eigenvalues $> 0$), define: $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle_M = \mathbf{u}^T M \mathbf{v}$

For $n = 3$ with diagonal weight matrix $M = \begin{bmatrix} w_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & w_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & w_3 \end{bmatrix}, \quad w_i > 0$
this gives $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle_M = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} w_1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & w_2 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & w_3 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ v_3 \end{bmatrix} = w_1 u_1 v_1 + w_2 u_2 v_2 + w_3 u_3 v_3$

This is a valid inner product. The weights change which vectors are orthogonal and what "length" means. The geometry is different from Euclidean geometry.

**Positive definiteness check:** $\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle_M = \mathbf{v}^T M \mathbf{v} > 0$ for $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$ exactly because $M$ is positive definite. This is why the positive definiteness of $M$ is required — it is the condition ensuring (IP3) holds.


## Inner Products on Function Spaces — $L^2[a,b ]$
This is where the abstraction earns its keep. Let $V$ be the space of continuous functions on $[a,b]$. Define:
$$\langle f, g \rangle = \int_a^b f(x), g(x), dx$$
Verification of axioms:
- **(IP1):** $\int f g = \int g f$. Trivially symmetric.
- **(IP2):** $\int (\alpha f + h) g = \alpha \int fg + \int hg$. Linearity of the integral.
- **(IP3):** $\int_a^b [f(x)]^2 dx \geq 0$ since the integrand is non-negative. And $\int_a^b [f(x)]^2 dx = 0$ implies $f(x) = 0$ for all $x \in [a,b]$ (by continuity). So $\langle f, f \rangle = 0 \Rightarrow f = 0$.

The induced norm is
$$|f| = \sqrt{\int_a^b [f(x)]^2, dx}$$

and "orthogonality" of two functions means
$$\int_a^b f(x) g(x), dx = 0$$

**This is exactly what Fourier analysis is about.** The sine and cosine functions are orthogonal in the $L^2[0, 2\pi]$ inner product:
$$\int_0^{2\pi} \sin(mx) \sin(nx), dx = 0 \quad \text{for } m \neq n$$

The Fourier series is a decomposition of a function into an orthogonal basis — the same idea as decomposing a vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ into orthogonal basis vectors, but in an infinite-dimensional inner product space. Everything I know about projections and orthogonal bases in $\mathbb{R}^n$ extends here.

## Projection as a Consequence of the Inner Product

Given a subspace $W \subseteq V$, the **orthogonal projection** of $\mathbf{v}$ onto $W$ is the vector $\hat{\mathbf{v}} \in W$ minimizing $|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{w}|$ over all $\mathbf{w} \in W$. The inner product structure is what makes this minimizer unique and computable.

For the simplest case, projecting onto the line spanned by $\mathbf{a}$ $\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{a} \rangle}{\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle} \mathbf{a}$

The coefficient $\frac{\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{a} \rangle}{\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle}$ is the "shadow" of $\mathbf{v}$ along $\mathbf{a}$. The error $\mathbf{v} - \hat{\mathbf{v}}$ is orthogonal to $\mathbf{a}$:
$\langle \mathbf{v} - \hat{\mathbf{v}},\ \mathbf{a} \rangle$
$= \langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{a} \rangle - \frac{\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{a} \rangle}{\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle} \langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{a} \rangle$
$= 0$

This is the fundamental **orthogonality condition** of projections. It says: the best approximation from a subspace is the one whose error is perpendicular to the subspace. Inner product = the only tool that can express perpendicularity.

### What an Inner Product Actually Does:
An inner product on a vector space gives you three things simultaneously:
1. **A notion of length:** $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{v} \rangle}$
2. **A notion of angle:** $\cos\theta = \langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle / (|\mathbf{u}| |\mathbf{v}|)$, justified by Cauchy-Schwarz
3. **A notion of perpendicularity:** $\langle \mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \rangle = 0$

Without an inner product, a vector space has no geometry — just linear combinations. The inner product is precisely the additional structure that turns algebra into geometry. The dot product in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the weighted dot product with matrix $M$, and the $L^2$ integral pairing are all different choices of geometry on different spaces. Every geometric concept in linear algebra — orthogonal complement, projection, least squares, Gram-Schmidt, eigenvectors of symmetric matrices — is secretly a consequence of having an inner product.
