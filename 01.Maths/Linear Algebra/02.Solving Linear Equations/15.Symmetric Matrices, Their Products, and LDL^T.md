
## Why I'm Writing This

Symmetric matrices keep appearing everywhere — in the normal equations $A^T A$, in quadratic forms, in the covariance matrix of data. For a while I was treating $A^T = A$ as just a nice property that makes computation easier. What I missed is that symmetry is a structural condition that forces the entire eigenvalue/eigenvector theory to behave perfectly. This note also serves as the bridge between LU factorization (which I know) and $LDL^T$ (which I am learning now).

---

## Definition and First Examples

A matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is **symmetric** if

$$A^T = A$$

equivalently, $a_{ij} = a_{ji}$ for all $i, j$. The entries are mirrored across the main diagonal.

**Example: $3 \times 3$ symmetric matrix.**

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -1 \ 2 & 5 & 3 \ -1 & 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$$

Reading the matrix: $a_{12} = a_{21} = 2$, $a_{13} = a_{31} = -1$, $a_{23} = a_{32} = 3$. The diagonal $4, 5, 6$ can be anything.

**Non-example.** The matrix

$$B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \ 3 & 1 & -1 \ 0 & 4 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

has $b_{12} = 2 \neq 3 = b_{21}$. Not symmetric.

## When Is a Matrix Product Symmetric?

This is the question that comes up constantly in practice, because most symmetric matrices I encounter are not written down directly — they arise as products.

**Claim:** $AB$ is generally _not_ symmetric, even if both $A$ and $B$ are symmetric.

**Proof by counterexample.** Take

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

Both are symmetric. Their product:

$$AB = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1\cdot0+2\cdot1 & 1\cdot1+2\cdot4 \ 2\cdot0+3\cdot1 & 2\cdot1+3\cdot4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 9 \ 3 & 14 \end{bmatrix}$$

Since $9 \neq 3$, $AB$ is not symmetric. The product of two symmetric matrices is symmetric **if and only if they commute**: $AB = BA$.

**The natural way to produce a symmetric matrix from $A$: form $A^T A$.**

**Theorem.** For any matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, the product $A^T A$ is symmetric.

_Proof._ We need to check $(A^T A)^T = A^T A$.

$$(A^T A)^T = A^T (A^T)^T = A^T A \qquad \square$$

using the rule $(BC)^T = C^T B^T$ and $(A^T)^T = A$.

Similarly, $A A^T$ is symmetric (it is $m \times m$, while $A^T A$ is $n \times n$).

**Example.** Take

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 3 & 4 \ 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$$

Then

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 & 5 \ 2 & 4 & 6 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 3 & 4 \ 5 & 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1+9+25 & 2+12+30 \ 2+12+30 & 4+16+36 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 35 & 44 \ 44 & 56 \end{bmatrix}$$

Symmetric, as guaranteed. $A^T A$ is the matrix that appears in the normal equations for least squares, and now I see why Strang spends so much time on it — it inherits all the nice properties of symmetric matrices.

**General rule.** If $S$ is symmetric and $A$ is any matrix, then $A^T S A$ is symmetric:

$$(A^T S A)^T = A^T S^T (A^T)^T = A^T S A$$

using $S^T = S$. This construction appears everywhere: in weighted least squares, in quadratic forms, in finite element methods.

## The Spectral Theorem — Why Symmetry Really Matters

I want to state this carefully because it is the reason symmetric matrices dominate applied linear algebra.

**Spectral Theorem.** Every real symmetric matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ has the following properties:

1. All eigenvalues of $A$ are **real**.
2. Eigenvectors corresponding to **distinct** eigenvalues are **orthogonal**.
3. $A$ is **orthogonally diagonalizable**: there exists an orthogonal matrix $Q$ (so $Q^T Q = I$) and a diagonal matrix $\Lambda$ such that

$$A = Q \Lambda Q^T$$

where the columns of $Q$ are unit eigenvectors of $A$ and $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$.

_Proof of (2) — distinct eigenvalues give orthogonal eigenvectors._ Suppose $A\mathbf{q}_1 = \lambda_1 \mathbf{q}_1$ and $A\mathbf{q}_2 = \lambda_2 \mathbf{q}_2$ with $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$.

Compute $\mathbf{q}_2^T A \mathbf{q}_1$ in two ways. First:

$$\mathbf{q}_2^T (A \mathbf{q}_1) = \mathbf{q}_2^T (\lambda_1 \mathbf{q}_1) = \lambda_1 \mathbf{q}_2^T \mathbf{q}_1$$

Second, using symmetry $A = A^T$:

$$\mathbf{q}_2^T A \mathbf{q}_1 = (A^T \mathbf{q}_2)^T \mathbf{q}_1 = (A \mathbf{q}_2)^T \mathbf{q}_1 = (\lambda_2 \mathbf{q}_2)^T \mathbf{q}_1 = \lambda_2 \mathbf{q}_2^T \mathbf{q}_1$$

So $\lambda_1 \mathbf{q}_2^T \mathbf{q}_1 = \lambda_2 \mathbf{q}_2^T \mathbf{q}_1$, i.e., $(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2)\mathbf{q}_2^T \mathbf{q}_1 = 0$. Since $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$, we must have $\mathbf{q}_2^T \mathbf{q}_1 = 0$. $\square$

**Remark (personal):** The proof uses symmetry in the most direct way possible — $A = A^T$ lets you "transpose the whole expression" and trade which eigenvector gets hit by $A$. Without symmetry this trick fails immediately.

## The $LDL^T$ Decomposition

### Motivation

I know $LU$ factorization: $A = LU$ where $L$ is unit lower triangular and $U$ is upper triangular. For a symmetric matrix, there is a more natural factorization that exploits the symmetry: since $A = A^T$, we expect the factorization to look the same from both sides.

$LDL^T$ is the symmetric version of $LU$. Instead of $L$ and a separate $U$, you get $L$, a diagonal $D$, and $L^T$ — the upper factor is just the transpose of the lower factor.

### The Factorization

**Theorem.** If $A$ is a symmetric matrix that admits $LU$ factorization without row exchanges (all leading principal submatrices are non-singular), then

$$A = LDL^T$$

where $L$ is unit lower triangular and $D$ is diagonal.

### Derivation from $LU$

Start from $A = LU$. Since $A$ is symmetric:

$$LU = A = A^T = (LU)^T = U^T L^T$$

Now factor $D = \text{diag}(u_{11}, u_{22}, \ldots, u_{nn})$ (the diagonal of $U$) out of $U$:

$$U = D \tilde{U}$$

where $\tilde{U}$ is unit upper triangular (diagonal entries all 1). Substituting:

$$A = L D \tilde{U}$$

From $LU = U^T L^T$ and the uniqueness of $LU$ factorization, one can show $\tilde{U} = L^T$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{A = L D L^T}$$

### Working Through a $3 \times 3$ Example

Take

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \ 2 & 2 & -1 \ -2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

First verify $A$ is symmetric: $a_{12} = a_{21} = 2$, $a_{13} = a_{31} = -2$, $a_{23} = a_{32} = -1$. Good.

**Step 1: Eliminate column 1.** Pivot is $a_{11} = 4$.

Multipliers: $\ell_{21} = 2/4 = 1/2$, $\ell_{31} = -2/4 = -1/2$.

Row 2 $\leftarrow$ Row 2 $- \frac{1}{2}$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix} - \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 - 1 & -1+1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 3 $\leftarrow$ Row 3 $- (-\frac{1}{2})$ Row 1 = Row 3 $+ \frac{1}{2}$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} + \frac{1}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1+1 & 3-1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

After first elimination:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Eliminate column 2.** Pivot is $1$. Multiplier: $\ell_{32} = 0/1 = 0$.

No change to row 3. The matrix after full elimination is:

$$U = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Extract $D$ and $L^T$.**

$$D = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$L^T = D^{-1} U = \begin{bmatrix} 1/4 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1/2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1/2 & -1/2 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

So

$$L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 1/2 & 1 & 0 \ -1/2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Verification: $L D L^T$.**

$$L D = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 1/2 & 1 & 0 \ -1/2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 & 0 \ 2 & 1 & 0 \ -2 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$(LD) L^T = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 & 0 \ 2 & 1 & 0 \ -2 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1/2 & -1/2 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \ 2 & 2 & -1 \ -2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = A \qquad \checkmark$$

## $LDL^T$ and the Positive Definiteness Test

This is the payoff. Recall $A$ is **positive definite** if $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$. There are several equivalent conditions; $LDL^T$ gives one of the most computationally natural.

**Theorem.** A symmetric matrix $A$ is positive definite if and only if all diagonal entries of $D$ in the $LDL^T$ factorization are positive.

_Why._ Write $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T L D L^T \mathbf{x} = (L^T \mathbf{x})^T D (L^T \mathbf{x})$.

Set $\mathbf{y} = L^T \mathbf{x}$. Since $L^T$ is unit upper triangular it is invertible, so as $\mathbf{x}$ ranges over all nonzero vectors, so does $\mathbf{y}$. Therefore:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0 \text{ for all } \mathbf{x} \neq 0 \iff \mathbf{y}^T D \mathbf{y} > 0 \text{ for all } \mathbf{y} \neq 0$$

But $\mathbf{y}^T D \mathbf{y} = \sum_i d_i y_i^2$. This is positive for all nonzero $\mathbf{y}$ if and only if all $d_i > 0$.

In the example above: $d_1 = 4 > 0$, $d_2 = 1 > 0$, $d_3 = 2 > 0$, so $A$ is positive definite.

**Connection to $LU$ pivots.** The diagonal entries of $D$ are exactly the pivots in Gaussian elimination (before any row scaling). Strang's pivot test for positive definiteness — "all pivots positive" — and the $LDL^T$ diagonal test are the same test, stated differently.

## $LDL^T$ vs $LU$ vs Cholesky — When to Use What

**$LU$:** General (non-symmetric) square matrices. Two separate triangular factors, no relation between $L$ and $U$. Requires $n^3/3$ operations.

**$LDL^T$:** Symmetric matrices. Exploits symmetry: only compute $L$ and $D$, since $U = DL^T$ is determined. Roughly half the work of $LU$ for the same size matrix — $n^3/6$ operations. Does not require positive definiteness.

**Cholesky ($LL^T$):** Symmetric positive definite matrices. Write $D = D^{1/2} D^{1/2}$ and absorb $D^{1/2}$ into $L$: set $\tilde{L} = L D^{1/2}$, giving $A = \tilde{L} \tilde{L}^T$. The entries of $D^{1/2}$ are $\sqrt{d_i}$, which requires $d_i > 0$, hence the positive definiteness requirement. Cholesky is numerically the most stable option when positive definiteness is guaranteed.

**Personal note:** I find $LDL^T$ cleaner than Cholesky conceptually because the $D$ sits visibly between $L$ and $L^T$ — the symmetry of the factorization literally mirrors the symmetry of the matrix. Cholesky hides the diagonal information inside $\tilde{L}$.

## The Full Picture in One Place

$$\underbrace{A}_{= A^T} = \underbrace{L}_{\text{unit lower triangular}} \underbrace{D}_{\text{diagonal}} \underbrace{L^T}_{\text{unit upper triangular}}$$

The $D$ diagonal entries are the pivots. The entries of $L$ below the diagonal are the Gaussian elimination multipliers $\ell_{ij} = a_{ij}^{(k)} / a_{jj}^{(k)}$ (where superscript $(k)$ denotes the matrix at step $k$ of elimination) — exactly the same multipliers as in $LU$, just read off the same elimination process.

Nothing new needs to be computed. $LDL^T$ is $LU$ factorization with the observation that for a symmetric matrix, $U = DL^T$ holds automatically.

## Open Questions

- Indefinite symmetric matrices (some $d_i > 0$, some $d_i < 0$): $LDL^T$ still exists, but the matrix is not positive definite. What does the sign pattern of $D$'s diagonal entries tell us? This is Sylvester's law of inertia — the number of positive, negative, and zero diagonal entries of $D$ is an invariant of $A$, independent of the factorization. I want to come back to this.
- When does the $LDL^T$ factorization fail to exist without row exchanges? Exactly when a zero pivot is encountered, i.e., a leading principal submatrix is singular. In that case a modified $LDL^T$ with $2 \times 2$ diagonal blocks in $D$ can handle indefinite matrices. This is the Bunch-Kaufman algorithm.
- The connection between $LDL^T$ and the spectral decomposition $Q\Lambda Q^T$: both diagonalize $A$, but $L$ is triangular (not orthogonal) and the two $D$'s are different objects. How do the pivots relate to the eigenvalues? Answer: they don't have a simple relationship in general, but for positive definite $A$ they have the same sign, and by the determinant formula $\det A = \prod d_i = \prod \lambda_i$.
