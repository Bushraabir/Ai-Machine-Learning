You already know [[10. A = LU]]. For a **symmetric** matrix ($A = A^T$), there is hidden structure: $U$ is not free — it is completely determined by $L$ and the pivots. $LDL^T$ makes this explicit.

| Factorization   | Applies To                  | Cost        | Requires                         |
| --------------- | --------------------------- | ----------- | -------------------------------- |
| $LU$            | Any square matrix           | $n^3/3$ ops | Non-singular leading submatrices |
| $LDL^T$         | Symmetric                   | $n^3/6$ ops | Same as LU                       |
| Cholesky $LL^T$ | Symmetric Positive Definite | $n^3/6$ ops | All pivots $> 0$                 |

> [!theorem] $LDL^T$ Factorization
> If $A$ is symmetric and all leading principal submatrices are non-singular (no row exchanges needed in Gaussian elimination), then:
> $$A = LDL^T$$
> where $L$ is **unit lower triangular** (1s on diagonal) and $D$ is **diagonal**.

## Derivation from LU 
Start from what you know: $A = LU$.
- **Step 1:** Use symmetry. $LU = A = A^T = (LU)^T = U^T L^T$
- **Step 2:** Factor the diagonal out of $U$. Let $D = \text{diag}(u_{11}, u_{22}, \ldots, u_{nn})$, and write:
	$U = D\tilde{U}$
	where $\tilde{U}$ is unit upper triangular (all diagonal entries = 1).
- **Step 3:** Substitute back: $A = LD\tilde{U}$
- **Step 4:** By the **uniqueness of LU factorization**, matching $LD\tilde{U} = U^T L^T$ forces: $\tilde{U} = L^T$

Therefore: $\boxed{A = LDL^T}$

> [!note] Key Insight
> The uniqueness of LU factorization does the heavy lifting. You don't need a separate proof — the algebra forces $\tilde{U} = L^T$.

## 4. Example: $3 \times 3$ Matrix
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & 2 & -1 \\ -2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$
**Symmetry check:** $a_{12}=a_{21}=2$, $a_{13}=a_{31}=-2$, $a_{23}=a_{32}=-1$ ✓
- **Step 1 : Eliminate Column 1**
	Pivot: $a_{11} = 4$
	Multipliers: $\ell_{21} = \frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}, \qquad \ell_{31} = \frac{-2}{4} = -\frac{1}{2}$
	After row operations: $\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

> [!check] Symmetry at Work
> Notice: after eliminating column 1, the submatrix in rows/cols 2–3 is **also symmetric**. This is always true — eliminating from a symmetric matrix leaves a symmetric Schur complement.

- **Step 2: Eliminate Column 2**
	Pivot: $a_{22}^{(2)} = 1$. Multiplier: $\ell_{32} = 0/1 = 0$. No change.
	$U = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$

-  Step 3: Extract $D$ and $L^T$
	$D = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$
	$L^T = D^{-1}U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1/2 & -1/2 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$
	$L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 1/2 & 1 & 0 \\ -1/2 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$

> [!tip] Shortcut
> The entries of $L$ below the diagonal **are exactly the Gaussian elimination multipliers** $\ell_{ij}$. You read them off automatically — no extra computation.
### Verification:
$LD = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 & 0 \\ 2 & 1 & 0 \\ -2 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$
$(LD)L^T = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 & -2 \\ 2 & 2 & -1 \\ -2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = A \checkmark$

## 5. $LDL^T$ and the Positive Definiteness Test:
> $A$ is **positive definite** (PD) if $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$.

> [!theorem] Pivot Test via $LDL^T$
> A symmetric matrix $A$ is positive definite **if and only if** all diagonal entries of $D$ are positive.

### Proof:
$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T L D L^T \mathbf{x} = \underbrace{(L^T\mathbf{x})^T}_{\mathbf{y}^T} D \underbrace{(L^T\mathbf{x})}_{\mathbf{y}} = \mathbf{y}^T D \mathbf{y}$
Let $\mathbf{y} = L^T \mathbf{x}$. Since $L^T$ is unit upper triangular, it is **invertible**, so as $\mathbf{x}$ ranges over all nonzero vectors, so does $\mathbf{y}$.
$\mathbf{y}^T D \mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=1}^n d_i y_i^2$ -> This is $> 0$ for all $\mathbf{y} \neq \mathbf{0}$ **if and only if** every $d_i > 0$.
$$\boxed{A \text{ is PD} \iff d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n > 0}$$

### Applied to Our Example
$D = \text{diag}(4, 1, 2)$ — all positive → $A$ **is positive definite** ✓

|---|---|---|---|

| Cholesky | **Yes** | **Yes** | Numerically optimal for PD systems |

> [!note] Cholesky from $LDL^T$
> When all $d_i > 0$, write $D = D^{1/2} D^{1/2}$ and absorb:
> $$A = L D L^T = (LD^{1/2})(D^{1/2}L^T) = \tilde{L}\tilde{L}^T$$
> The square roots $\sqrt{d_i}$ require $d_i > 0$, hence the PD requirement.

## The Formula for $LDL^T$ Entries
You don't need to run full LU and then extract; you can compute $L$ and $D$ directly:
For $j = 1, 2, \ldots, n$: $d_j = a_{jj} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} \ell_{jk}^2 \, d_k$
For $i > j$: $\ell_{ij} = \frac{1}{d_j}\left(a_{ij} - \sum_{k=1}^{j-1} \ell_{ik} \, \ell_{jk} \, d_k\right)$

> [!example] Trace Through for Our 3×3
> 
> $j=1$: $d_1 = a_{11} = 4$
> 
> $i=2, j=1$: $\ell_{21} = a_{21}/d_1 = 2/4 = 1/2$
> 
> $i=3, j=1$: $\ell_{31} = a_{31}/d_1 = -2/4 = -1/2$
> 
> $j=2$: $d_2 = a_{22} - \ell_{21}^2 d_1 = 2 - (1/4)(4) = 1$
> 
> $i=3, j=2$: $\ell_{32} = (a_{32} - \ell_{31}\ell_{21}d_1)/d_2 = (-1 - (-1/2)(1/2)(4))/1 = (-1+1)/1 = 0$
> 
> $j=3$: $d_3 = a_{33} - \ell_{31}^2 d_1 - \ell_{32}^2 d_2 = 3 - (1/4)(4) - 0 = 2$

Results match perfectly: $D = \text{diag}(4,1,2)$, $L$ as before ✓
