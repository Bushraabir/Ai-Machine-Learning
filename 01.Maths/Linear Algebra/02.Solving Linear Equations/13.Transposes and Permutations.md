
## Transposes

### Definition

> **Definition.** The **transpose** of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is the matrix $A^T \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times m}$ defined by: $$(A^T)_{ij} = A_{ji}$$

Rows become columns and columns become rows. If:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix} \quad \in \mathbb{R}^{2 \times 3} $$

then:

$$ A^T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 4 \ 2 & 5 \ 3 & 6 \end{pmatrix} \quad \in \mathbb{R}^{3 \times 2} $$

Nothing deep here — it is a reflection across the main diagonal. What makes transposes important is how they interact with everything else.

---

### Algebraic Properties

> **Theorem.** For matrices $A$, $B$ of compatible dimensions and scalar $\alpha$:
> 
> 1. $(A^T)^T = A$
> 2. $(\alpha A)^T = \alpha A^T$
> 3. $(A + B)^T = A^T + B^T$
> 4. $(AB)^T = B^T A^T$
> 5. If $A$ is invertible: $(A^{-1})^T = (A^T)^{-1}$

**Proof of (4) — the reversal rule.** Let $C = AB$, so $C_{ij} = \sum_k A_{ik} B_{kj}$.

Then $(C^T)_{ij} = C_{ji} = \sum_k A_{jk}B_{ki} = \sum_k (B^T)_{ik}(A^T)_{kj} = (B^T A^T)_{ij}$.

Therefore $(AB)^T = B^T A^T$. $\blacksquare$

Note the reversal: same pattern as $(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1}A^{-1}$. Both the inverse and the transpose reverse the order of products. This is not a coincidence — it reflects the categorical duality between a linear map and its adjoint/dual.

**Proof of (5).** We want to show $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$, i.e., $(A^{-1})^T \cdot A^T = I$.

$$(A^{-1})^T A^T = (AA^{-1})^T = I^T = I$$

using property (4). $\blacksquare$

So $(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$, which is often written $A^{-T}$.

---

### The Transpose and Inner Products

The transpose has a clean characterization in terms of the standard inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$. For $x \in \mathbb{R}^m$, $y \in \mathbb{R}^n$, $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$:

$$\langle Ax, y \rangle_{\mathbb{R}^m} = \langle x, A^T y \rangle_{\mathbb{R}^n}$$

**Proof.**

$$ \langle Ax, y \rangle = (Ax)^T y = x^T A^T y = \langle x, A^T y \rangle $$

This identity is fundamental. It says that $A^T$ is the **adjoint** of $A$ with respect to the standard inner product. In the abstract language of linear maps between inner product spaces, the adjoint is defined by exactly this property. The transpose is just what the adjoint looks like when you choose the standard basis.

This identity is also why the normal equations for least squares are $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$: the "best" $x$ is the one for which the residual $b - Ax$ is orthogonal to the column space of $A$, which means $A^T(b - Ax) = 0$.

---

### Symmetric Matrices

> **Definition.** $A$ is **symmetric** if $A^T = A$, i.e., $A_{ij} = A_{ji}$ for all $i, j$.

Symmetric matrices are always square. Examples:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \ 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 4 \ 0 & 2 & -1 \ 4 & -1 & 7 \end{pmatrix} $$

The most important source of symmetric matrices: if $A$ is any rectangular matrix, then $A^T A$ and $A A^T$ are both symmetric and positive semidefinite.

**Proof of symmetry.** $(A^T A)^T = A^T (A^T)^T = A^T A$. $\blacksquare$

Products of symmetric matrices are generally not symmetric unless they commute: $(AB)^T = B^T A^T = BA \ne AB$ in general.

---

### Skew-Symmetric Matrices

> **Definition.** $A$ is **skew-symmetric** (or antisymmetric) if $A^T = -A$.

Every square matrix decomposes uniquely into a symmetric and a skew-symmetric part:

$$ A = \underbrace{\frac{A + A^T}{2}}_{\text{symmetric}} + \underbrace{\frac{A - A^T}{2}}_{\text{skew-symmetric}} $$

The diagonal entries of a skew-symmetric matrix are all zero (since $a_{ii} = -a_{ii}$ implies $a_{ii} = 0$).

## Permutations

### Definition

> **Definition.** A **permutation matrix** $P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is a matrix obtained by reordering the rows of the identity matrix $I_n$.

Equivalently, $P$ has exactly one $1$ in each row and each column, and $0$s everywhere else.

For $n = 3$, there are $3! = 6$ permutation matrices:

$$ I = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\0&1&0\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}, \quad P_{12} = \begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\1&0&0\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}, \quad P_{13} = \begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\0&1&0\1&0&0\end{pmatrix} $$

$$ P_{23} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\0&0&1\0&1&0\end{pmatrix}, \quad P_{123} = \begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\0&0&1\1&0&0\end{pmatrix}, \quad P_{132} = \begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\1&0&0\0&1&0\end{pmatrix} $$

where $P_\sigma$ denotes the permutation matrix for the permutation $\sigma$.

---

### Action of a Permutation Matrix

Left multiplication by $P$: **permutes the rows** of whatever follows.

Right multiplication by $P$: **permutes the columns** of whatever precedes.

Concretely, if $P$ is the permutation matrix for $\sigma \in S_n$, then:

$$(PA)_{ij} = A_{\sigma^{-1}(i),, j}$$

That is, row $i$ of $PA$ is row $\sigma^{-1}(i)$ of $A$. (The inverse appears because $P$ puts row $\sigma(i)$ of the identity into row $i$ of $P$, so multiplying by $P$ sends row $\sigma^{-1}(i)$ of $A$ to row $i$.)

In practice, when we do row swaps during elimination, we are left-multiplying by elementary permutation matrices (transpositions).

---

### Inverse and Transpose of a Permutation Matrix

> **Theorem.** For any permutation matrix $P$: $$P^{-1} = P^T$$ That is, $P$ is an **orthogonal matrix**: $P^T P = P P^T = I$.

**Proof.** The columns of $P$ are distinct standard basis vectors $e_{\sigma(1)}, e_{\sigma(2)}, \ldots, e_{\sigma(n)}$. Since these are orthonormal, $P^T P = I$. $\blacksquare$

Alternatively: $(P^T P)_{ij} = \sum_k (P^T)_{ik}P_{kj} = \sum_k P_{ki}P_{kj}$. Since $P$ has exactly one $1$ per row and column, $P_{ki}P_{kj} = 1$ only when $k = \sigma^{-1}(i) = \sigma^{-1}(j)$, which forces $i = j$. So $P^T P = I$.

This means: **to undo a row permutation, just transpose the permutation matrix.** This is used all the time in $PA = LU$ factorization — to solve $Ax = b$, first compute $PA = LU$, then solve $LUx = Pb$.

---

### The Group Structure

The set of all $n \times n$ permutation matrices forms a group under matrix multiplication, isomorphic to the symmetric group $S_n$. In particular:

- The product of two permutation matrices is a permutation matrix.
- The identity $I$ is the identity permutation.
- Every permutation matrix has a unique inverse, which is its transpose.

The number of $n \times n$ permutation matrices is $n!$.

---

### Permutations and Elimination: $PA = LU$

In Gaussian elimination without pivoting, we compute $A = LU$. But this requires nonzero pivots to appear in the right positions, which is not always the case even for invertible $A$.

**Partial pivoting** selects, at each step, the largest entry in the current column as the pivot (for numerical stability). This requires row swaps, which are exactly left-multiplication by permutation matrices.

The result is:

$$PA = LU$$

where $P$ is the permutation matrix that records all the row swaps made during elimination, $L$ is unit lower triangular, and $U$ is upper triangular.

> **Theorem.** For any invertible matrix $A$, there exists a permutation matrix $P$ such that $PA = LU$ with $L$ unit lower triangular and $U$ upper triangular with nonzero diagonal.

The proof is constructive: run elimination with partial pivoting. At each step, swap the current row with the row below it that has the largest pivot, and record the swap in $P$. The process completes because $A$ is invertible.

**Solving $Ax = b$ with $PA = LU$:**

1. Factor: $PA = LU$ (cost $\frac{n^3}{3}$)
2. Form $Pb$: just reorder the entries of $b$ according to $P$ (cost $O(n)$)
3. Solve $Lc = Pb$ by forward substitution (cost $\frac{n^2}{2}$)
4. Solve $Ux = c$ by back substitution (cost $\frac{n^2}{2}$)

---

### Determinant of a Permutation Matrix

The determinant of a permutation matrix $P_\sigma$ is the **sign of the permutation** $\sigma$:

$$\det(P_\sigma) = \text{sgn}(\sigma) = \begin{cases} +1 & \text{if } \sigma \text{ is an even permutation} \ -1 & \text{if } \sigma \text{ is an odd permutation} \end{cases}$$

**Even/odd.** A permutation is even if it can be written as a product of an even number of transpositions (adjacent swaps), and odd otherwise. The parity is well-defined (this is a non-trivial fact from group theory).

This connects to the full determinant formula:

$$\det(A) = \sum_{\sigma \in S_n} \text{sgn}(\sigma) \prod_{i=1}^{n} A_{i,\sigma(i)}$$

This sum is over all $n!$ permutations. Each term is a product of $n$ entries, one from each row and column, with a sign determined by the parity of the permutation. For $n = 3$ this gives $6$ terms; for $n = 4$ it gives $24$.

---

### Worked Example: $3 \times 3$ Determinant via Permutations

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b & c \ d & e & f \ g & h & k \end{pmatrix} $$

The six permutations of ${1,2,3}$ with their signs:

$$ \begin{array}{c|c|c|c} \sigma & \text{Cycle type} & \text{sgn}(\sigma) & \text{Term} \ \hline (1)(2)(3) & \text{identity} & +1 & aek \ (12)(3) & \text{transposition} & -1 & bdk \ (13)(2) & \text{transposition} & -1 & cfg \  
(1)(23) & \text{transposition} & -1 & afh \ (123) & \text{3-cycle} & +1 & bfg \ (132) & \text{3-cycle} & +1 & cdh \ \end{array} $$

Therefore:

$$ \det(A) = aek - bdk - cfg - afh + bfg + cdh $$

Wait — let me recompute more carefully. The term for permutation $\sigma$ is $\prod_i A_{i,\sigma(i)}$, i.e., entry $(1, \sigma(1))$ times entry $(2, \sigma(2))$ times entry $(3,\sigma(3))$.

$$ \begin{array}{c|c|c} \sigma & \text{Picks} & \text{Term} \ \hline (1,2,3) \to (1,2,3) & A_{11}A_{22}A_{33} = a \cdot e \cdot k & +aek \ (1,2,3) \to (2,1,3) & A_{12}A_{21}A_{33} = b \cdot d \cdot k & -bdk \ (1,2,3) \to (3,2,1) & A_{13}A_{22}A_{31} = c \cdot e \cdot g & -ceg \ (1,2,3) \to (1,3,2) & A_{11}A_{23}A_{32} = a \cdot f \cdot h & -afh \ (1,2,3) \to (2,3,1) & A_{12}A_{23}A_{31} = b \cdot f \cdot g & +bfg \ (1,2,3) \to (3,1,2) & A_{13}A_{21}A_{32} = c \cdot d \cdot h & +cdh \ \end{array} $$

$$\det(A) = aek - bdk - ceg - afh + bfg + cdh$$

This is the standard $3 \times 3$ determinant formula (Sarrus' rule written algebraically).

### $LDU$ Decomposition and Symmetric Matrices

For a symmetric matrix $A = A^T$, the $LDU$ factorization has a special structure:

$$A = LDL^T$$

where $D$ is diagonal and $L$ is unit lower triangular. This halves the storage and computation relative to general $LU$ (Cholesky goes further, writing $A = LL^T$ when $A \succ 0$).

The key point: $U = L^T$ because $A^T = (LDL^T)^T = LDL^T = A$. Symmetry of $A$ propagates to the factorization.

---

## Putting It Together: $PA = LDU$

For a general square invertible matrix, the full factorization is:

$$PA = LDU$$

where:

- $P$ is a permutation matrix (records row swaps from partial pivoting)
- $L$ is unit lower triangular (stores the multipliers $\ell_{ij}$)
- $D$ is diagonal (the pivots)
- $U$ is unit upper triangular

Often one writes $PA = LU$ by absorbing $D$ into $U$, producing upper triangular $U$ with the pivots on the diagonal (not necessarily $1$s).

The transpose gives:

$$(PA)^T = (LU)^T = U^T L^T$$

So $A^T P^T = U^T L^T$ — the $LU$ factorization of $A^T$ (with column permutation $P^T$) involves $U^T$ as the lower triangular factor and $L^T$ as the upper triangular factor. This is how Cholesky arises: if $A = A^T$ and $A \succ 0$, then $A = LL^T$.

---

## Worked Example: $PA = LU$ for a Matrix Requiring a Swap

Let:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 2 & 1 \ 3 & 1 & 2 \ 6 & 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix} $$

The $(1,1)$ entry is $0$ — we cannot use it as a pivot. Partial pivoting: the largest entry in column 1 is $6$ (row 3). Swap rows 1 and 3:

$$ P_1 A = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 2 & 4 \ 3 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad P_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 0&0&1\0&1&0\1&0&0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Eliminate column 1. Multiplier for row 2: $\ell_{21} = 3/6 = 1/2$. Row 2 $\leftarrow$ Row 2 $- \frac{1}{2}$ Row 1:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 2 & 4 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

The $(2,2)$ entry is now $0$ — need another pivot swap. Swap rows 2 and 3:

$$ P_2 \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 2 & 4 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 & 2 & 4 \ 0 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} = U, \quad P_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\0&0&1\0&1&0\end{pmatrix} $$

$U$ has a zero on the diagonal — this tells us $A$ is singular. Indeed, $\det(A) = 0$ (row 3 of the original is twice row 2). But let us read off the factorization anyway.

The overall permutation is $P = P_2 P_1$:

$$ P = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\0&0&1\0&1&0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\0&1&0\1&0&0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0&0&1\1&0&0\0&1&0\end{pmatrix} $$

And $L$ collects the multipliers appropriately adjusted for the row swaps. The factorization is $PA = LU$ with:

$$ L = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\frac{1}{2}&1&0\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad U = \begin{pmatrix}6&2&4\0&2&1\0&0&0\end{pmatrix} $$

(For a singular matrix, $U$ has a zero row, confirming $\det(A) = 0$.)

## Things to Come Back To

- The connection between $A^T$ and the dual/adjoint map. If $A: V \to W$ is a linear map, the adjoint $A^_: W^_ \to V^_$ is the transpose in coordinates. Over $\mathbb{C}$, this becomes the conjugate transpose $A^_$ (Hermitian adjoint), not just $A^T$.
- Permutation matrices as the symmetric group: the product of two permutation matrices corresponds to composition of permutations. This is why there are $n!$ permutation matrices and why they form a non-abelian group for $n \ge 3$.
- The sign of a permutation in the determinant formula connects to the theory of the exterior algebra: $\det$ is the unique alternating multilinear form on rows (or columns). Swapping two rows changes the sign, which is exactly what transpositions do to $\text{sgn}(\sigma)$.
- $LDL^T$ vs. Cholesky: for $A \succ 0$, both factor $A$ into triangular factors. $LDL^T$ avoids square roots; Cholesky computes $\sqrt{d_{ii}}$ to produce $LL^T$ directly. Both cost $\frac{n^3}{6}$.
