
Plain $LU$ factorization breaks the moment a zero pivot appears. I kept treating row exchanges as a nuisance — "just swap the rows and continue." But that mental model breaks down when I need to think about the factorization as a whole object, not just as a calculation procedure. The point of this note is to understand the permutation matrix $P$ as a first-class mathematical object, prove that $PA = LU$ always exists for invertible $A$, and see what the factorization actually says.

---

## What Goes Wrong Without Row Exchanges

Consider

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

The $(1,1)$ entry is zero. We cannot use it as a pivot. Gaussian elimination fails at the very first step — not because $A$ is singular (it isn't, $\det A = -2$), but because the algorithm encounters a zero in the pivot position.

Fix: swap rows 1 and 2 to get

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Now elimination proceeds normally with pivots $2$ and $1$. The matrix we actually factored is not $A$ — it is the row-permuted matrix. The $PA = LU$ framework is the correct way to account for this.

## Permutation Matrices

### Definition

A **permutation matrix** $P \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is a matrix obtained by reordering the rows of the identity matrix $I_n$.

Equivalently, $P$ has exactly one entry equal to $1$ in each row and each column, with all other entries $0$.

**Example: all $3 \times 3$ permutations.**

There are $3! = 6$ permutation matrices of size $3 \times 3$. Written out fully:

$$I = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \qquad P_{12} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \qquad P_{13} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$P_{23} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad P_{123} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \qquad P_{132} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

The subscript notation: $P_{ij}$ swaps rows $i$ and $j$. $P_{123}$ sends row 1 $\to$ row 2 $\to$ row 3 $\to$ row 1 (a cyclic permutation).

### Left vs Right Multiplication

Left-multiplying by $P$ permutes the **rows** of a matrix. Right-multiplying by $P$ permutes the **columns**.

**Example.** Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} a_1 \ a_2 \ a_3 \end{bmatrix}$$

where $a_i$ denotes the $i$-th row. Then

$$P_{12} A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} a_1 \ a_2 \ a_3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a_2 \ a_1 \ a_3 \end{bmatrix}$$

Rows 1 and 2 are swapped. This is the operation we want: rearrange rows of $A$ before factoring.

### Key Properties

**Theorem.** Every permutation matrix $P$ satisfies:

$$P^{-1} = P^T \qquad \text{i.e.,} \qquad P^T P = P P^T = I$$

In other words, permutation matrices are **orthogonal matrices**.

_Proof._ The $(i,j)$ entry of $P^T P$ is the dot product of column $i$ and column $j$ of $P$. Column $i$ of $P$ is a standard basis vector $\mathbf{e}_{\sigma(i)}$ for some permutation $\sigma$. The dot product $\mathbf{e}_{\sigma(i)} \cdot \mathbf{e}_{\sigma(j)} = 1$ if $\sigma(i) = \sigma(j)$, and $0$ otherwise. Since $\sigma$ is a bijection, $\sigma(i) = \sigma(j)$ iff $i = j$. So $(P^T P)_{ij} = \delta_{ij}$, i.e., $P^T P = I$. $\square$

**Consequence:** $P^{-1} = P^T$ means undoing a row permutation costs nothing — just transpose. No matrix inversion required.

**Determinant of a permutation matrix:** $\det P = \pm 1$. The sign is $+1$ if $P$ corresponds to an even permutation (even number of row swaps) and $-1$ if odd. For $P_{12}$ above, $\det P_{12} = -1$ since it is a single transposition.

**Product of permutation matrices:** The product of two permutation matrices is again a permutation matrix. The set of all $n \times n$ permutation matrices forms a group under multiplication — isomorphic to $S_n$, the symmetric group on $n$ elements. For $n = 3$, this is a group of order $6$.

## The $PA = LU$ Theorem

### Statement

**Theorem.** For any invertible matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, there exists a permutation matrix $P$, a unit lower triangular matrix $L$, and an upper triangular matrix $U$ with nonzero diagonal entries, such that

$$PA = LU$$

Equivalently, $A = P^{-1} L U = P^T L U$.

### Why It Always Exists

The proof is constructive — it is Gaussian elimination with **partial pivoting**.

At each step $k$ of elimination (choosing the pivot in column $k$), instead of taking whatever is currently in position $(k,k)$, we search rows $k$ through $n$ for the largest absolute value entry in column $k$ (partial pivoting) or any nonzero entry (sufficient for existence). If the entire column below position $(k,k)$ is zero, then $A$ is singular — but we assumed $A$ is invertible, so this cannot happen. We swap the current row with the row containing the chosen pivot, record the swap in $P$, then eliminate as usual.

The claim is that collecting all these row swaps into a single permutation matrix $P$ applied at the start is equivalent to applying individual swap matrices throughout the process. More precisely:

Let $P_1, P_2, \ldots, P_{n-1}$ be the individual permutation matrices applied at each step. Then $P = P_{n-1} \cdots P_2 P_1$ (applied left to right in elimination order, but composed right to left as matrices). The full elimination gives

$$L_{n-1} P_{n-1} \cdots L_1 P_1 A = U$$

where each $L_k$ is an elementary lower triangular matrix (one nonzero off-diagonal column). The key technical step — which I will not prove in full here — is that the individual $P_k$ and $L_k$ matrices can be rearranged so that all permutations accumulate into a single $P$ on the left and all lower triangular factors accumulate into a single $L$:

$$P_{n-1} \cdots P_1 A = LU \quad \Rightarrow \quad PA = LU \qquad \square$$

## A Full $3 \times 3$ Example

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 3 & 2 & 1 \ 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

This has a nonzero $(1,1)$ entry, but I will force a row exchange anyway by using partial pivoting — choosing the largest pivot at each step — to illustrate the full machinery.

### Step 1: Choose the pivot in column 1.

Entries in column 1: $|1|, |3|, |2|$. Largest is $3$ in row 2. Swap rows 1 and 2.

$$P_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$P_1 A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 3 & 2 & 1 \ 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 1 \ 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

Eliminate column 1. Multipliers: $\ell_{21} = 1/3$, $\ell_{31} = 2/3$.

Row 2 $\leftarrow$ Row 2 $- \frac{1}{3}$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} - \frac{1}{3}\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & \frac{4}{3} & \frac{2}{3} \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 3 $\leftarrow$ Row 3 $- \frac{2}{3}$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} - \frac{2}{3}\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\frac{1}{3} & \frac{7}{3} \end{bmatrix}$$

After step 1:

$$A^{(1)} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & \tfrac{4}{3} & \tfrac{2}{3} \ 0 & -\tfrac{1}{3} & \tfrac{7}{3} \end{bmatrix}$$

### Step 2: Choose the pivot in column 2 (rows 2 and 3 only).

Entries: $|4/3|$ and $|-1/3|$. Largest is $4/3$ in row 2. No swap needed here.

$$P_2 = I$$

Multiplier: $\ell_{32} = \frac{-1/3}{4/3} = -\frac{1}{4}$.

Row 3 $\leftarrow$ Row 3 $- (-\frac{1}{4})$ Row 2 = Row 3 $+ \frac{1}{4}$ Row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 & -\tfrac{1}{3} & \tfrac{7}{3} \end{bmatrix} + \frac{1}{4}\begin{bmatrix} 0 & \tfrac{4}{3} & \tfrac{2}{3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & \tfrac{7}{3} + \tfrac{1}{6} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & \tfrac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix}$$

After step 2:

$$U = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & \tfrac{4}{3} & \tfrac{2}{3} \ 0 & 0 & \tfrac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix}$$

### Assembling $P$, $L$, $U$.

Only one row swap occurred (step 1), so

$$P = P_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

The multipliers go directly into $L$:

$$L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ \ell_{21} & 1 & 0 \ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ \tfrac{1}{3} & 1 & 0 \ \tfrac{2}{3} & -\tfrac{1}{4} & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Important subtlety:** The multipliers in $L$ are the multipliers used _after_ the row permutation at each step — they go into $L$ without modification, in the positions determined by the reordered rows. This is the key thing that confused me initially: you do not permute the multipliers when you form $L$. They just fill in below the diagonal in elimination order.

### Verification: $PA = LU$.

$$PA = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 3 & 2 & 1 \ 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 1 \ 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$LU = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ \tfrac{1}{3} & 1 & 0 \ \tfrac{2}{3} & -\tfrac{1}{4} & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & \tfrac{4}{3} & \tfrac{2}{3} \ 0 & 0 & \tfrac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 1 of $LU$: $\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$ $\checkmark$

Row 2 of $LU$: $\begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{1}{3}\cdot3 + 0 & \tfrac{1}{3}\cdot2 + \tfrac{4}{3} & \tfrac{1}{3}\cdot1 + \tfrac{2}{3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & \tfrac{2}{3}+\tfrac{4}{3} & \tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{2}{3} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$ $\checkmark$

Row 3 of $LU$: $\begin{bmatrix} \tfrac{2}{3}\cdot3 + 0 & \tfrac{2}{3}\cdot2 + (-\tfrac{1}{4})\cdot\tfrac{4}{3} & \tfrac{2}{3}\cdot1 + (-\tfrac{1}{4})\cdot\tfrac{2}{3} + \tfrac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix}$

$= \begin{bmatrix} 2 & \tfrac{4}{3} - \tfrac{1}{3} & \tfrac{2}{3} - \tfrac{1}{6} + \tfrac{5}{2} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & \tfrac{4}{6}-\tfrac{1}{6}+\tfrac{15}{6} \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$ $\checkmark$

## Solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ Using $PA = LU$

Once we have $PA = LU$, solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ proceeds in three steps.

Starting from $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, multiply both sides on the left by $P$:

$$PA\mathbf{x} = P\mathbf{b} \implies LU\mathbf{x} = P\mathbf{b}$$

Let $\mathbf{c} = P\mathbf{b}$ (just permute the entries of $\mathbf{b}$ — free), $\mathbf{y} = U\mathbf{x}$. Then:

**Step 1:** Compute $\mathbf{c} = P\mathbf{b}$ by permuting $\mathbf{b}$.

**Step 2:** Solve $L\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{c}$ by **forward substitution** (top to bottom, since $L$ is lower triangular).

**Step 3:** Solve $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}$ by **backward substitution** (bottom to top, since $U$ is upper triangular).

Each of steps 2 and 3 costs $O(n^2)$. The expensive part — $O(n^3)$ — was computing $P$, $L$, $U$ in the first place. Once the factorization is done, each new right-hand side $\mathbf{b}$ can be solved in $O(n^2)$. This is the whole point of factorization when solving multiple systems with the same $A$.

## Why Partial Pivoting Matters Numerically

The existence theorem says we need any nonzero pivot, not necessarily the largest one. But in practice, small pivots cause catastrophic cancellation in floating-point arithmetic. Dividing by a very small number amplifies rounding errors.

**Partial pivoting** (choosing the largest entry in the column at each step) is the standard numerical strategy. It bounds the growth of entries in $U$ relative to $A$, keeping the factorization numerically stable.

There is a quantity called the **growth factor**:

$$\rho = \frac{\max_{i,j} |u_{ij}|}{\max_{i,j} |a_{ij}|}$$

For partial pivoting, theory guarantees $\rho \leq 2^{n-1}$. In practice, $\rho$ is almost always much smaller — the worst case almost never occurs on real matrices. This empirical stability is why partial pivoting is the standard choice in LAPACK and virtually every numerical linear algebra library.

**Complete pivoting** (searching the entire remaining submatrix for the largest entry) gives a tighter theoretical bound but requires both row and column permutations, leading to $PA Q = LU$. It is rarely used in practice because it is slower and partial pivoting is already excellent.

## The Deeper Structure — $P$, $L$, $U$ as a Group Decomposition

There is a theorem in the theory of algebraic groups — the **Bruhat decomposition** — which says that for an invertible matrix, the factorization $PA = LU$ is essentially unique up to a choice of $P$. More precisely, for each permutation matrix $P$, there is at most one pair $(L, U)$ such that $PA = LU$ with $L$ unit lower triangular and $U$ upper triangular with nonzero diagonal.

I am not ready to prove this carefully. But it tells me that $P$ is not just a computational convenience — it is a canonical invariant of the matrix $A$. The choice of permutation is determined by the zero/nonzero structure of $A$ in a precise way.

## The Subtlety I Always Get Wrong

When multiple row swaps happen at different stages of elimination, the question is: where do the multipliers go in $L$?

The rule: after a row swap at step $k$, the multipliers computed in step $k$ go into row positions as they are _after_ the swap. The rows of $L$ below the diagonal reflect the ordering of rows in the already-permuted matrix.

A common mistake is to compute the multipliers for the original row order, then try to "un-permute" them. That is wrong. The multipliers are always computed from the current state of the matrix — rows already permuted by all previous swaps. They go directly into $L$ in the position they occupy at the time of computation.

I spent an embarrassing amount of time getting this wrong. The worked example above is clean because only one swap occurs. In practice, with two or more swaps, drawing the matrix at each step and tracking which row is which is the only reliable method.

## Summary

$$PA = LU$$

$P$: permutation matrix ($P^{-1} = P^T$, records all row swaps)  
$A$: the original matrix  
$L$: unit lower triangular (multipliers from elimination, in post-swap order)  
$U$: upper triangular with nonzero diagonal (pivots on the diagonal)

Exists for every invertible $A$. The factorization is the correct way to do Gaussian elimination — not $A = LU$ with an asterisk for "assuming no zero pivots appear."

To solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$: permute $\mathbf{b}$ to get $P\mathbf{b}$, forward-substitute through $L$, back-substitute through $U$.

## Open Questions

- If $A$ is symmetric, can $PA = LU$ be refined to $PAP^T = LDL^T$? Yes — applying the same permutation to rows and columns preserves symmetry. This is the symmetric pivoting strategy, and it gives $PAP^T = LDL^T$ with $P$ a permutation and $D$ block diagonal. Strang does not cover this in 18.06 but it appears in numerical methods courses.
- What happens to the determinant? $\det(PA) = \det(P)\det(A)$ and $\det(LU) = \det(L)\det(U) = 1 \cdot \prod u_{ii}$. So $\det(A) = \det(P^T) \prod u_{ii} = (\pm 1) \prod u_{ii}$. The sign of $\det P$ — which is $+1$ for an even number of swaps and $-1$ for odd — comes directly out of the $PA = LU$ factorization.
- The connection between $P$ in $PA = LU$ and the pivot columns of $A$: partial pivoting chooses the largest entry, but does the resulting $P$ tell us anything about the column space or null space? Not directly — $P$ is an artifact of the algorithm, not a canonical property of $A$. Two different partial pivoting implementations could produce different $P$'s for the same $A$.
