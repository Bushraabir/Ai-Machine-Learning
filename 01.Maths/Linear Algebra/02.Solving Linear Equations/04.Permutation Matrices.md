A permutation matrix $P$ is a square matrix obtained by reordering the rows of the identity matrix $I$.

Equivalently: $P$ has exactly one entry equal to $1$ in each row and exactly one entry equal to $1$ in each column, with all other entries $0$.

For $n = 3$, the identity permutation is $I$ itself:
$$ I = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$
The permutation matrix that swaps rows 1 and 2:
$$ P_{12} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$
Left-multiplying any $3 \times n$ matrix $A$ by $P_{12}$ swaps its first and second rows.

The permutation matrix that cycles rows $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 1$:
$$ P_{\text{cyc}} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$


## All Permutation Matrices for $n = 3$

>There are $3! = 6$ permutations of three rows, so exactly 6 permutation matrices of size $3 \times 3$. Let me write them all out explicitly.

The six permutations of $(1, 2, 3)$ — written as: row 1 goes to position $\sigma(1)$, row 2 to $\sigma(2)$, row 3 to $\sigma(3)$:

**$\sigma = (1,2,3)$ — identity:** $$ P_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$
**$\sigma = (2,1,3)$ — swap rows 1 and 2:** $$ P_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$
**$\sigma = (1,3,2)$ — swap rows 2 and 3:** $$ P_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$
**$\sigma = (3,2,1)$ — swap rows 1 and 3:** $$ P_4 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$
**$\sigma = (2,3,1)$ — cycle $1 \to 3 \to 2 \to 1$:** $$ P_5 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$
**$\sigma = (3,1,2)$ — cycle $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 1$:** $$ P_6 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

For $n \times n$ matrices there are $n!$ permutation matrices total: $1, 2, 6, 24, 120, 720, \ldots$

## What $P$ Does: Left vs. Right Multiplication

This is worth being precise about because the two directions do completely different things.

**Left multiplication $PA$:** permutes the **rows** of $A$.Row $i$ of $PA$ is row $\sigma(i)$ of $A$, where $\sigma$ is the permutation encoded by $P$.

**Right multiplication $AP$:** permutes the **columns** of $A$.Column $j$ of $AP$ is column $\sigma(j)$ of $A$.

In elimination I always left-multiply — I am rearranging rows, not columns. Right-multiplying $A$ by $P$ would rearrange the unknowns, which is a different operation entirely.

# Algebraic Properties

**1. Orthogonality**:
$$ P^T P = I \qquad$$$$ \Longrightarrow \qquad P^{-1} = P^T $$
This is the most important property. The inverse of a permutation matrix is its transpose. 
**Proof**: the $(i,j)$ entry of $P^T P$ is the dot product of column $i$ of $P^T$ (which is row $i$ of $P$) with column $j$ of $P$. Row $i$ of $P$ has a single $1$ in some position $k$; column $j$ of $P$ has a single $1$ in some position $\ell$. The dot product is $1$ iff $k = \ell$, which happens iff $i = j$ (since each column has exactly one $1$). So $P^T P = I$.


**2. The inverse permutation:**
If $P$ sends row $i$ to position $\sigma(i)$, then $P^{-1} = P^T$ sends row $\sigma(i)$ back to position $i$. The transpose undoes the permutation.
For the swap matrix $P_{12}$:
$$ P_{12}^T = P_{12}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = P_{12} $$
Swapping rows 1 and 2 twice returns to the original. So $P_{12}$ is its own inverse: $P_{12}^2 = I$. This is true of any single-swap permutation matrix.

**3. Determinant:**
$$ \det(P) = +1 \text{ or } -1 $$

>[!important] 
>$\det(P) = +1$ if $P$ is an **even** permutation (achievable by an even number of swaps).  
$\det(P) = -1$ if $P$ is an **odd** permutation (achievable by an odd number of swaps).

The identity has $\det = +1$. Each row swap flips the sign. So:
- $P_1$: $\det = +1$ (zero swaps)
- $P_2, P_3, P_4$: $\det = -1$ (one swap each)
- $P_5, P_6$: $\det = +1$ (two swaps each, or equivalently they are 3-cycles)

**4. Closure under multiplication.**

The product of two permutation matrices is a permutation matrix. The composition of two row rearrangements is another row rearrangement. The set of all $n \times n$ permutation matrices forms a **group** under multiplication — the symmetric group $S_n$.

## Permutation Matrices Are Orthogonal Matrices

$P^{-1} = P^T$ is exactly the definition of an orthogonal matrix. So every permutation matrix is orthogonal.

What does this mean geometrically? Orthogonal matrices preserve lengths and angles:

$$ |P\mathbf{x}| = |\mathbf{x}|, \qquad (P\mathbf{x}) \cdot (P\mathbf{y}) = \mathbf{x} \cdot \mathbf{y} $$

Permuting the components of a vector does not change its length — this is obvious, since I am just reordering the squared terms in $|\mathbf{x}|^2 = x_1^2 + x_2^2 + \cdots + x_n^2$.

Permutation matrices are a special subclass of orthogonal matrices. In general, orthogonal matrices can rotate and reflect; permutation matrices can only permute coordinates. They are the "discrete" orthogonal matrices.
