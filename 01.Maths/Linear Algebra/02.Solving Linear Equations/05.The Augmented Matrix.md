When I solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, I am performing row operations on $A$ — but every row operation on $A$ must be simultaneously applied to $\mathbf{b}$. If I manipulate $A$ and forget to carry $\mathbf{b}$ along, the right-hand side no longer corresponds to the transformed system and the solution is wrong.

The augmented matrix is the bookkeeping device that prevents this mistake. I attach $\mathbf{b}$ as an extra column to $A$, separated by a vertical bar to remind me it plays a different role:

$$ [A \mid \mathbf{b}] = \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} a_{11} & a_{12} & \cdots & a_{1n} & b_1 \\ a_{21} & a_{22} & \cdots & a_{2n} & b_2 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots & \vdots \\ a_{m1} & a_{m2} & \cdots & a_{mn} & b_m \end{array}\right) $$
Now every row operation I perform on the left automatically carries through to the right. The vertical bar is a reminder — not a mathematical boundary — that the last column is $\mathbf{b}$, not a column of $A$.

> row operations on $[A \mid \mathbf{b}]$ produce $[A' \mid \mathbf{b}']$ where $A'\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}'$ has exactly the same solution set as $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.


## Row Operations
Each legal row operation corresponds to left-multiplying by an elementary matrix $E$:
$$ E[A \mid \mathbf{b}] = [EA \mid E\mathbf{b}] $$
Since $E$ is invertible, the systems $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ and $(EA)\mathbf{x} = E\mathbf{b}$ have identical solution sets. The augmented matrix packages both sides so this simultaneous transformation is automatic.

After the full sequence of elimination steps $E_k \cdots E_2 E_1$, I arrive at:
$$ [U \mid \mathbf{c}] $$
where $U$ is upper triangular and $\mathbf{c} = E_k \cdots E_1 \mathbf{b}$ is the transformed right-hand side. Then back-substitution on $U\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{c}$ gives the solution.

## Anatomy of the Augmented Matrix After Elimination

After row reduction to row echelon form, the augmented matrix $[U \mid \mathbf{c}]$ for an $m \times n$ system of rank $r$ looks like this:
$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccccccc|c} \mathbf{p}_1 & * & * & * & * & * & * & c_1 \\ 0 & 0 & \mathbf{p}_2 & * & * & * & * & c_2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \mathbf{p}_3 & * & * & c_3 \\ \vdots & & & & & \ddots & & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & \mathbf{p}_r & c_r \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & c_{r+1} \\ \vdots & & & & & & & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & c_m \end{array}\right) $$
The bold entries $\mathbf{p}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{p}_r$ are the pivots. Everything below each pivot in its column is zero.

The rows $r+1$ through $m$ (if any) are all-zero rows on the left side. Whether the system is consistent or inconsistent is determined entirely by the corresponding entries $c_{r+1}, \ldots, c_m$ on the right.

## Reading the Three Cases Directly from $[U \mid \mathbf{c}]$

**Case 1 : Unique solution:** $r = n = m$.
$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} p_1 & * & * & c_1 \\ 0 & p_2 & * & c_2 \\ 0 & 0 & p_3 & c_3 \end{array}\right) $$
Every column has a pivot, every row has a pivot. No free variables, no zero rows. Back-substitution gives a unique $(x_1, x_2, x_3)$.

**Case 2 — No solution:** $r < m$ and some $c_i \neq 0$ for $i > r$.
$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} p_1 & * & * & c_1 \\ 0 & p_2 & * & c_2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & c_3 \end{array}\right) \quad \text{with } c_3 \neq 0 $$
The third row says $0 \cdot x_1 + 0 \cdot x_2 + 0 \cdot x_3 = c_3 \neq 0$. This is $0 = c_3$, a contradiction. The system is inconsistent. No solution exists. Geometrically: $\mathbf{b}$ is not in the column space of $A$.

**Case 3 — Infinitely many solutions:** $r < n$ and all $c_i = 0$ for $i > r$.
$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} p_1 & * & * & c_1 \\ 0 & 0 & p_2 & c_2 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) $$

Column 2 has no pivot — it is a free column. The variable $x_2$ is free: it can take any value $t \in \mathbb{R}$, and $x_1, x_3$ are expressed in terms of $t$ via back-substitution. The solution set is a line (or affine subspace of dimension $n - r$) in $\mathbb{R}^n$.


## Example: $3 \times 4$ System
I want to see an underdetermined system explicitly. For example:
$x_1 + 2x_2 + 3x_3 + x_4 = 7$ 
$2x_1 + 4x_2 + 7x_3 - x_4 = 9$ 
$x_1 + 2x_2 + 5x_3 - 3x_4 = 6$

Augmented matrix:
$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 & 7 \\ 2 & 4 & 7 & -1 & 9 \\ 1 & 2 & 5 & -3 & 6 \end{array}\right) $$
**Stage 1:** Pivot $= 1$ in position $(1,1)$.
$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$: $(2-2,\\ 4-4,\\ 7-6,\\ -1-2,\\ 9-14) = (0,\\ 0,\\ 1,\\ -3,\\ -5)$
$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_1$: $(1-1,\ 2-2,\ 5-3,\ -3-1,\ 6-7) = (0,\ 0,\ 2,\ -4,\ -1)$

After Stage 1:
$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 & 7 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -3 & -5 \\ 0 & 0 & 2 & -4 & -1 \end{array}\right) $$
Column 2 has no pivot below row 1 — $x_2$ is a free variable.

**Stage 2:** Pivot $= 1$ in position $(2,3)$.
$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 2R_2$: $(0,\ 0,\ 2-2,\ -4+6,\ -1+10) = (0,\ 0,\ 0,\ 2,\ 9)$
After Stage 2:
$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 1 & 7 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -3 & -5 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 & 9 \end{array}\right) $$

**Stage 3:** Pivot $= 2$ in position $(3,4)$.
No rows below. Elimination complete.

Three pivots: $1, 1, 2$ in columns 1, 3, 4. Column 2 is free. Rank $r = 3$, free variables $= n - r = 4 - 3 = 1$.

**Back-substitution:**
From row 3: $2x_4 = 9 \Rightarrow x_4 = \tfrac{9}{2}$.
From row 2: $x_3 - 3x_4 = -5 \Rightarrow x_3 = -5 + 3\cdot\tfrac{9}{2} = -5 + \tfrac{27}{2} = \tfrac{17}{2}$.
From row 1: $x_1 + 2x_2 + 3x_3 + x_4 = 7$.

Let $x_2 = t$ (free). Then: $$x_1 = 7 - 2t - 3\cdot\tfrac{17}{2} - \tfrac{9}{2} = 7 - 2t - \tfrac{51}{2} - \tfrac{9}{2} = 7 - 2t - 30 = -23 - 2t$$

Complete solution:
$$ \mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix} -23 \\ 0 \\ \tfrac{17}{2} \\ \tfrac{9}{2} \end{pmatrix} + t \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R} $$

The first vector is the particular solution $\mathbf{x}_p$ (setting $t = 0$). The second vector is the special solution in the nullspace, corresponding to the free variable $x_2 = 1$.

This is precisely the structure $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_p + \mathbf{x}_n$ from before. The augmented matrix made every step of this visible and automatic.

## The Augmented Matrix for Finding $A^{-1}$
There is a second major use of the augmented matrix: computing the inverse. If $A$ is square and invertible, I augment with the identity:
$$ [A \mid I] $$
Then I row reduce all the way to **[[04.Reduced Row Echelon Form (RREF)]]** — not just echelon form, but with zeros above the pivots as well, and pivots scaled to 1. The result is:
$$ [I \mid A^{-1}] $$

**Why this works:** I am applying the same sequence of row operations to both $A$ and $I$. The operations turn $A$ into $I$, meaning I applied $E_k \cdots E_1 A = I$, so $E_k \cdots E_1 = A^{-1}$. The same operations applied to $I$ give $E_k \cdots E_1 I = A^{-1}$.

This is Gauss-Jordan elimination. The cost is $O(n^3)$, same as $LU$ factorization, but now I get the full inverse rather than just a factorization.

**When to use $A^{-1}$ vs $LU$:** If I need to solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ for one or a few $\mathbf{b}$, use $LU$. If I genuinely need $A^{-1}$ itself (e.g., to form the matrix $A^{-1}B$ for some other matrix $B$), use Gauss-Jordan.

see more: [[06.Gauss–Jordan Elimination]]

## A $2 \times 2$ Inverse by Gauss-Jordan
$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 5 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$
Augmented with identity:
$$ \left(\begin{array}{cc|cc} 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 5 & 3 & 0 & 1 \end{array}\right) $$
- $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \tfrac{5}{2} R_1$: $\left(\begin{array}{cc|cc} 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & \tfrac{1}{2} & -\tfrac{5}{2} & 1 \end{array}\right)$
- $R_2 \leftarrow 2 R_2$ (scale pivot to 1):$\left(\begin{array}{cc|cc} 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 & -5 & 2 \end{array}\right)$

- $R_1 \leftarrow R_1 - R_2$ (eliminate above pivot in column 2): $\left(\begin{array}{cc|cc} 2 & 0 & 6 & -2 \\ 0 & 1 & -5 & 2 \end{array}\right)$
- $R_1 \leftarrow \tfrac{1}{2} R_1$ (scale pivot to 1): $\left(\begin{array}{cc|cc} 1 & 0 & 3 & -1 \\ 0 & 1 & -5 & 2 \end{array}\right)$

Therefore: $A^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -5 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$

**Check:** $AA^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 5 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \ -5 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6-5 & -2+2 \ 15-15 & -5+6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \checkmark$

## Reduced Row Echelon Form

The echelon form $U$ has pivots in staircase position with zeros below each pivot. The **reduced** row echelon form (RREF) goes further:

- Zeros **above** each pivot as well as below.
- Each pivot scaled to exactly $1$.

RREF is unique — every matrix has exactly one RREF. Echelon form is not unique (the operations used to reach it can vary). RREF is the canonical form.

For solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$: either echelon form followed by back-substitution, or RREF with the answer readable directly. For finding $A^{-1}$: RREF is required (Gauss-Jordan).

The RREF of $A$ is often written $R$:

$$ R = \text{rref}(A) $$

For an invertible $n \times n$ matrix: $R = I$.

For a singular matrix: $R$ has at least one all-zero row, and the pivot columns of $R$ are standard basis vectors.

## What the Augmented Matrix Encodes — A Summary

|Feature|Where it appears in $[U \mid \mathbf{c}]$|
|---|---|
|Rank $r$|Number of nonzero rows in $U$|
|Pivot columns|Columns containing pivots|
|Free variables|Columns of $U$ with no pivot|
|Consistency|$c_i = 0$ for all $i > r$?|
|Unique solution|$r = n = m$, consistent|
|Infinitely many|$r < n$, consistent|
|No solution|$c_i \neq 0$ for some $i > r$|
|Nullspace|Read from free columns after solving $[U \mid \mathbf{0}]$|
|Particular solution|Back-substitute with free variables set to zero|
