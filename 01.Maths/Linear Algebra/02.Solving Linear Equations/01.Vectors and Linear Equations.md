## Two Ways to See the Same System

This is where everything starts. A system of linear equations can be read in two fundamentally different ways — and both readings are correct, but they reveal different geometry. 
Take a simple $2 \times 2$ system:
$$ 2x - y = 1 $$$$ x + y = 5 $$
Most people have seen this before and think of it as: two equations, find $x$ and $y$. That is fine. But there are two geometric pictures hiding here, and they are not the same picture. These are:
1) the row picture
2) the column picture

## The Row Picture:
Each equation defines a line in $\mathbb{R}^2$. Solving the system means finding the point where the two lines intersect.
- $2x - y = 1$ is a line with slope 2, $y$-intercept $-1$.
- $x + y = 5$ is a line with slope $-1$, $y$-intercept $5$.
They intersect at $(x, y) = (2, 3)$.

The row picture generalizes to $\mathbb{R}^n$ as hyperplanes (codimension-1 affine subspaces). $n$ equations in $n$ unknowns means $n$ hyperplanes; a unique solution means they all meet at a single point.
![[Pasted image 20260412225502.png]]
This is the picture most people grow up with. It is geometrically correct but computationally inconvenient — picturing $n$ hyperplanes intersecting in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is hard for large $n$.

## The Column Pictur:

Rewrite the same system by collecting coefficients into column vectors:
$$ x \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + y \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} $$
Now the question is: what linear combination of the two column vectors produces the right-hand side?
The answer $(x, y) = (2, 3)$ says: take 2 copies of the first column and 3 copies of the second:
$$ 2 \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + 3 \begin{pmatrix} -1 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 - 3 \\ 2 + 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} $$
Correct. The column picture is a statement about linear combinations of vectors. The solution is the pair of coefficients that combines the columns into the target $\mathbf{b}$.
![[Pasted image 20260413061520.png]]
This is the picture that scales. It connects directly to the concepts of span, independence, and basis. The row picture is local (intersection of hyperplanes); the column picture is global (what vectors can be reached by linear combination).


## Matrix Form:

Both pictures are unified in the matrix equation $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.
$$ A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 5 \end{pmatrix} $$
- **Row picture:** each row of $A$ defines a hyperplane in $\mathbf{x}$-space. The rows of $A$ dotted with $\mathbf{x}$ give the components of $\mathbf{b}$.
- **Column picture:** $A\mathbf{x}$ is a linear combination of columns of $A$ with weights from $\mathbf{x}$. The question is whether $\mathbf{b}$ lies in the column space of $A$.
$$ A\mathbf{x} = x_1 \mathbf{a}_1 + x_2 \mathbf{a}_2 + \cdots + x_n \mathbf{a}_n $$
where $\mathbf{a}_j$ is the $j$-th column of $A$. I should internalize this. Matrix-vector multiplication is a linear combination of columns. Full stop.


## Vector Form:
A vector $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is an ordered $n$-tuple of real numbers. I think of it as a point in $\mathbb{R}^n$, or equivalently as an arrow from the origin to that point. Both views are useful.
**Addition:** component-wise.
$$ \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} u_1 + v_1 \\ u_2 + v_2 \\ \vdots \\ u_n + v_n \end{pmatrix} $$
Geometrically: parallelogram law, or tip-to-tail addition.

**Scalar multiplication:** stretch or flip.
$$ c\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} cv_1 \\ cv_2 \\ \vdots \\ cv_n \end{pmatrix} $$
**Linear combination:** the fundamental operation.
$$ c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k \mathbf{v}_k $$
>[!important] 
>Everything in linear algebra — absolutely everything — comes back to linear combinations.

## The Dot Product
For $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$: $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i $
Key facts I use constantly:
- $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}| |\mathbf{v}| \cos\theta$ where $\theta$ is the angle between them.
- $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0 \iff \mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$.
- $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{u} = |\mathbf{u}|^2$.
The dot product is how the row picture works. Each equation $\mathbf{a}_i \cdot \mathbf{x} = b_i$ says: the dot product of row $i$ of $A$ with $\mathbf{x}$ equals $b_i$. The set of all $\mathbf{x}$ satisfying this is a hyperplane perpendicular to $\mathbf{a}_i$.seem more: [[11.Length and dot product]]

>[!quote] ## When Does $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ Have a Solution?

This is the central existence question.

#### $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ has a solution $\iff$ $\mathbf{b}$ lies in the column space $\mathcal{C}(A)$
The column space is the set of all possible linear combinations of the columns of $A$ — all reachable right-hand sides. If $\mathbf{b}$ is not in $\mathcal{C}(A)$, no combination of the columns can produce it, so no solution exists.

For an $n \times n$ system:
- If $A$ is invertible (rank $n$): $\mathcal{C}(A) = \mathbb{R}^n$, so $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ has a unique solution for every $\mathbf{b}$.
- If $A$ is singular: $\mathcal{C}(A)$ is a proper subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Some $\mathbf{b}$ have no solution; others may have infinitely many. [[07.Singular Matrix]]

## $3 \times 3$Example:
Consider the following equations:
$x + 2y + 3z = 6$ 
$2x + 5y + 2z = 4$
$6x - 3y + z = 2$
**Matrix form:**
$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 5 & 2 \\ 6 & -3 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} $$
**==Row picture==:** three planes in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The solution is the point (if it exists and is unique) where all three planes meet.
==**Column picture==:**
$$ x \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} + y \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} + z \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} $$
Find the weights $x, y, z$.
**Elimination** (row reducing the augmented matrix $[A \mid \mathbf{b}]$):
$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\ 2 & 5 & 2 & 4 \\ 6 & -3 & 1 & 2 \end{array}\right) $$
1) $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, 
2) $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 6R_1$:
$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & -4 & -8 \\ 0 & -15 & -17 & -34 \end{array}\right) $$
3) $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 + 15R_2$:
$$ \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\ 0 & 1 & -4 & -8 \\ 0 & 0 & -77 & -154 \end{array}\right) $$
Third pivot: $-77$. 
So, Back-substitute: $z = 2$, then $y = -8 + 4(2) = 0$, then $x = 6 - 0 - 6 = 0$.
Solution: $(x, y, z) = (0, 0, 2)$.

**Check (column picture):**
$$ 0 \begin{pmatrix} 1 \\ 2 \\ 6 \end{pmatrix} + 0 \begin{pmatrix} 2 \\ 5 \\ -3 \end{pmatrix} + 2 \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 2 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 4 \\ 2 \end{pmatrix} \checkmark $$


## Some Exceptions

### **Case 1: No solution.**

After elimination, a row of the form $(0 ; 0 ; \cdots ; 0 \mid c)$ with $c \neq 0$ appears. This says $0 = c$, a contradiction. The planes in the row picture do not have a common intersection. The right-hand side $\mathbf{b}$ is not in the column space.

Consider the following matrix:
$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 5 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
Row reduce the augmented matrix. Start:
$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 & 5 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\right]$$
1) $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$:
$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & -7 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\right]$$
Row 2 now reads $0x + 0y + 0z = -7$, i.e., $0 = -7$. **No solution.**

Note what happened: rows 1 and 2 of $A$ were identical (row 2 is exactly $2 \times$ row 1), so they define the same plane in the row picture. But $b_2 \neq 2b_1$, so those two planes are parallel — they never meet.

### **Case 2: Infinitely many solutions.**

After elimination, a row of the form $(0 ; 0 ; \cdots ; 0 \mid 0)$ appears. There are fewer pivots than unknowns — free variables exist. The solution set is an affine subspace (a line, plane, etc.) in $\mathbb{R}^n$, not a single point. The column picture: $\mathbf{b}$ is in the column space, but the columns are dependent, so multiple combinations of them produce $\mathbf{b}$.

Consider the following matrix:
$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 3 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \\ 12 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$
Augmented matrix:
$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\ 2 & 4 & 6 & 12 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\right]$$
1) $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$:
$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \end{array}\right]$$
2) Swap $R_2 \leftrightarrow R_3$:
$$\left[\begin{array}{ccc|c} 1 & 2 & 3 & 6 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right]$$
Two pivots (columns 1 and 3), one free variable ($y$). From row 2: $z = 1$. From row 1: $x = 6 - 3z - 2y = 3 - 2y$. The solution set is:
$$\begin{pmatrix} x \\ y \\ z \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \\ 0 \\ 1 \end{pmatrix} + y \begin{pmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \\ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad y \in \mathbb{R}$$
This is a **line** in $\mathbb{R}^3$ — an affine subspace (particular solution + null space direction).
In both singular cases: the three planes (row picture) are not in "general position." Either parallel planes (no solution) or planes sharing a common line (infinitely many).

## The Structure of the Solution Set

If $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is consistent, let $\mathbf{x}_p$ be any particular solution. Then the complete solution is:
$\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}_p + \mathbf{x}_n$ , where $\mathbf{x}_n$ ranges over all vectors in $\mathcal{N}(A)$ (the nullspace of $A$).

**Proof:** If $A\mathbf{x}_p = \mathbf{b}$ and $A\mathbf{x}_n = \mathbf{0}$, then $A(\mathbf{x}_p + \mathbf{x}_n) = \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{b}$. Conversely, if $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, then $A(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_p) = \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_p \in \mathcal{N}(A)$.

This decomposition is fundamental. The particular solution shifts me to the right affine subspace; the nullspace directions are all the freedom I have once I am there.

When $A$ is invertible: $\mathcal{N}(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$, so the unique solution is $\mathbf{x} = A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$.



For $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ with $A$ an $m \times n$ matrix of rank $r$:

| Condition                       | Solutions                                                        |
| ------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $r = n = m$ (square, full rank) | Unique solution for every $\mathbf{b}$                           |
| $r = n < m$ (full column rank)  | At most one solution; exists iff $\mathbf{b} \in \mathcal{C}(A)$ |
| $r = m < n$ (full row rank)     | Infinitely many solutions for every $\mathbf{b}$                 |
| $r < m$, $r < n$                | Zero or infinitely many solutions depending on $\mathbf{b}$      |

The rank $r$ controls everything: how much of $\mathbb{R}^m$ the columns can reach, and how much freedom is left in the solution.

## the Column Picture Is the Right Picture

The row picture is intuitive but it obscures what matters. When I am doing elimination, I am manipulating rows — but what I care about is the column space. Elimination finds the solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, which is a question about columns.

The column picture makes it obvious:
- Why $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ might have no solution: $\mathbf{b}$ is not reachable.
- Why it might have infinitely many: the columns are dependent, so multiple combinations hit $\mathbf{b}$.
- What the solution set looks like: a particular combination plus any null combination.

Everything from here — subspaces, rank, projections, orthogonality — is built on the column picture.
