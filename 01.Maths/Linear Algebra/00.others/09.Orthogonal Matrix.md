A square matrix  $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is **orthogonal** if:
$Q^T Q = I \quad \text{and} \quad QQ^T = I$
therefore,
$Q^{-1} = Q^T$
The transpose is the inverse — extremely strong structural constraint.

## 1. Geometric Meaning

An orthogonal matrix represents a **rigid transformation** in space:
- Rotation
- Reflection
- Combination of both
### it preserves:
- Lengths (norms)
- Angles
- Dot products

$$
\|Qx\| = \|x\|, \quad (Qx) \cdot (Qy) = x \cdot y
$$

-  **No distortion** — only reorientation.

## 2. Column Interpretation

Let:
$$
Q = [q_1 \ q_2 \ \dots \ q_n]
$$

### Properties:
- $q_i \cdot q_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$
- $\|q_i\| = 1$
 
- Columns form an **orthonormal basis**

## 3. Row Interpretation
Rows of \( Q \) are also orthonormal:
- Each row has unit length
- Rows are mutually perpendicular
- Orthogonality exists in both row and column spaces


## Example (2×2 Rotation Matrix)
$$Q =
\begin{bmatrix}
\cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\
\sin\theta & \cos\theta
\end{bmatrix}
$$
##### Check orthogonality:
$$Q^T Q = I$$
#### Interpretation:
- Rotates vectors by angle \( \theta \)
- No change in length or angle

## Example (3×3 Orthogonal Matrix)
$$
Q =
\begin{bmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & -1 \\
0 & 1 & 0
\end{bmatrix}
$$
### Interpretation:
- Rotates vectors in the $yz$-plane
- Leaves $x$-axis unchanged


## Determinant
$$
\det(Q) = \pm 1
$$
### Meaning:
- \( +1 \) → pure rotation
- \( -1 \) → reflection (orientation flip)

##### Orthogonal matrices preserve volume (no scaling)


## Stability and Numerical Importance
Orthogonal matrices are **numerically stable** because:
$$
\|Qx\| = \|x\|
$$
### Implications:
- No amplification of errors
- Used in:
  - QR decomposition
  - Eigenvalue algorithms
  - Least squares problems


## Inverse is Cheap
Instead of computing inverse:
$$
Q^{-1} = Q^T
$$
- Computationally efficient and exact

## Connection to QR Decomposition

Any matrix \( A \) can be decomposed:
$$
A = QR
$$
- \( Q \) → orthogonal matrix  
- \( R \) → upper triangular  

#### Key tool in solving linear systems and least squares
