A square matrix $A$ is **singular** if it has no inverse. That is, there is no matrix $B$ such that:

$$AB = BA = I$$

A matrix that does have an inverse is called **invertible** or **non-singular**.

This definition is clean but not illuminating. The real content is in the equivalent characterisations below — each one is a different face of the same geometric reality.

## Five Equivalent Characterisations

The following are all equivalent for a square $n \times n$ matrix $A$:

|#|Statement|
|---|---|
|1|$A$ has no inverse|
|2|$\det(A) = 0$|
|3|The columns of $A$ are linearly dependent|
|4|$Ax = \mathbf{0}$ has a non-trivial solution (some $x \neq \mathbf{0}$)|
|5|$Ax = b$ has either no solution or infinitely many solutions for some $b$|

These are not five separate definitions. They are the same fact viewed from algebra, geometry, and the theory of linear systems. Any one of them can be taken as the definition; the others follow.

## The Determinant View

The most common test for singularity in practice:

$$A \text{ is singular} \iff \det(A) = 0$$

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix, this is immediate:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{bmatrix}$$$$
 \implies \det(A) = ad - bc$$

If $ad - bc = 0$, the matrix is singular.

**Example :**
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$\det(A) = (2)(2) - (4)(1) = 4 - 4 = 0 \quad \checkmark \text{ singular}$$

$$B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 3 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$
$$\det(B) = (2)(2) - (3)(1) = 4 - 3 = 1 \neq 0 \quad \checkmark \text{ invertible}$$
For $3 \times 3$ and larger matrices, computing the determinant is more involved, but the criterion is the same: zero determinant means singular.

## The Column Picture

This is the view Strang insists on, and it is the most geometric.

Write $Ax = b$ as a linear combination of columns:

$$x_1 \mathbf{c}_1 + x_2 \mathbf{c}_2 + \cdots + x_n \mathbf{c}_n = b$$

where $\mathbf{c}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{c}_n$ are the columns of $A$.

The question is: can every $b \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be written as such a combination?

- If the columns **span** $\mathbb{R}^n$ — if they are linearly independent — yes. The matrix is invertible.
- If the columns are **linearly dependent** — if some column lies in the span of the others — the columns do not fill $\mathbb{R}^n$. They span a lower-dimensional subspace. Many $b$ vectors are simply unreachable. The matrix is singular.

**The $2 \times 2$ example above:**
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Column 1: $\begin{bmatrix} 2 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$, Column 2: $\begin{bmatrix} 4 \ 2 \end{bmatrix} = 2 \cdot \begin{bmatrix} 2 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$

The second column is exactly twice the first. Both columns lie on the same line through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

## The Null Space View

$A$ is singular if and only if $Ax = \mathbf{0}$ has a **non-trivial solution**; a solution $x \neq \mathbf{0}$.

>[!definition] The set of all solutions to $Ax = \mathbf{0}$ is called the **null space** (or kernel) of $A$:
>
$$\mathcal{N}(A) = {x \in \mathbb{R}^n : Ax = \mathbf{0}}$$
>
-If $A$ is invertible: $\mathcal{N}(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$. The only solution is the trivial one.
-If $A$ is singular: $\mathcal{N}(A)$ contains non-zero vectors. The null space has **positive dimension**.

**For the singular example above:**
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \quad Ax = \mathbf{0}$$
$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \\ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 1: $2x_1 + 4x_2 = 0$
	$\implies x_1 = -2x_2$
Row 2: $x_1 + 2x_2 = 0$$
	$\implies x_1 = -2x_2$

Both rows say the same thing. The solution is:
$$x = t\begin{bmatrix} -2 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{for any } t \in \mathbb{R}$$
The null space is a whole **line** through the origin. The matrix collapses this entire line to $\mathbf{0}$.

## What Happens During Elimination

[[02.Gaussian Elimination|Gaussian Elimination]] reveals singularity directly. Attempt to row-reduce $A$:
$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$
Step 1: Subtract $\frac{1}{2}$ times row 1 from row 2:
$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 1 - \frac{1}{2}(2) & 2 - \frac{1}{2}(4) \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 \\ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$
A **row of zeros** appears. There is no pivot in the second row. Elimination has broken down.

In general: **a singular matrix produces at least one zero row** during elimination (assuming no row exchanges save it). The number of non-zero rows after full elimination is the **rank** of $A$.
- $n \times n$ matrix with full rank $r = n$: invertible.
- $n \times n$ matrix with rank $r < n$: singular.

A zero row in the reduced form means the corresponding equation contributes no information. The system is either inconsistent or underdetermined.

## The $3 \times 3$ Case

Consider a $3 \times 3$ system. In the row picture, each equation is a **plane** in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Three planes generically meet at a point — that is the invertible case. Singularity means they fail to meet at a unique point. This can happen in three distinct ways:

In all three cases, the matrix formed by the coefficients is singular. But the consequences for $Ax = b$ differ: Case 1 and 3 give infinitely many solutions (for the right $b$), while Case 2 gives no solution.

This is why singularity splits into two sub-cases: **no solution** vs **infinitely many solutions** — the matrix $A$ is singular in both, but the behaviour depends on whether $b$ lies in the column space.


## Rank and the Dimension of the Null Space

For an $n \times n$ matrix $A$, define:
$$\text{rank}(A) = \text{number of pivots} = \dim(\text{column space})$$
The **rank-nullity theorem** says:
$$\text{rank}(A) + \dim(\mathcal{N}(A)) = n$$
So:
- Full rank ($r = n$): null space is ${\mathbf{0}}$, matrix is invertible.
- Rank deficient ($r < n$): null space has dimension $n - r > 0$, matrix is singular.
The null space dimension $n - r$ is called the **nullity** of $A$. It counts the number of "free directions" — the dimensions along which $A$ collapses information.

# Why Singularity Means Non-Invertibility

This is worth proving directly, not just asserting.

**Claim.** If $Ax = \mathbf{0}$ has a non-trivial solution, then $A$ cannot have an inverse.

**Proof.** Suppose $x_0 \neq \mathbf{0}$ satisfies $Ax_0 = \mathbf{0}$. Suppose for contradiction that $A^{-1}$ exists. Then:

$$x_0 = I x_0 = (A^{-1}A) x_0 = A^{-1}(Ax_0) = A^{-1}\mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$$

This contradicts $x_0 \neq \mathbf{0}$. So $A^{-1}$ cannot exist. $\blacksquare$

**The geometric reading.** If $A$ collapses a whole subspace to $\mathbf{0}$, then knowing $Ax = b$ does not tell you which $x$ produced $b$ — many $x$ values give the same output. You cannot reverse a map that loses information.

## Singular vs Near-Singular

A matrix can be non-singular and still behave badly — if its columns are nearly linearly dependent, small errors in $b$ produce large errors in the solution $x = A^{-1}b$. This is measured by the **condition number**:
$$\kappa(A) = |A|,|A^{-1}|$$
A singular matrix has $\kappa(A) = \infty$ (since $A^{-1}$ does not exist). A matrix with very large condition number is **ill-conditioned** — it is technically invertible but numerically unreliable. The columns are nearly dependent.

This is not just a computational concern. It reflects genuine geometric near-singularity: the column space is nearly lower-dimensional, and the matrix is close to losing a dimension.

## The Determinant Connection — Why $\det = 0$

The determinant measures the **volume scaling factor** of the linear transformation $A$. Specifically, if $A$ maps the unit cube, its image has volume $|\det(A)|$.

- Invertible matrix: $|\det(A)| > 0$. The transformation preserves volume (up to scaling). No dimension is collapsed.
- Singular matrix: $|\det(A)| = 0$. The transformation squashes some dimension — the image of the unit cube has zero volume. The output lives in a subspace of dimension less than $n$.

```
Determinant as volume — 2×2 case

Non-singular (det ≠ 0):          Singular (det = 0):

  col2   /|                         col2
   |    / |                          |
   |   /  |   parallelogram          |  col1 and col2
   |  /   |   has nonzero area       |  are parallel
   | /    |                          |
   |/     |                          +-----------> col1
   O------+---> col1
   
   Area = |det(A)| > 0             Area = |det(A)| = 0
   (a 2D region)                   (a line, 1D)
```

A singular matrix flattens the space. The image of $\mathbb{R}^n$ under $A$ is a strict subspace — a lower-dimensional object with zero $n$-dimensional volume.

## Summary

Singularity is not one property — it is a cluster of equivalent statements:

$$\det(A) = 0$$ $$\iff \text{columns are linearly dependent}$$ $$\iff \text{column space} \subsetneq \mathbb{R}^n$$ $$\iff Ax = \mathbf{0} \text{ has non-trivial solutions}$$ $$\iff A \text{ has no inverse}$$ $$\iff A \text{ collapses at least one dimension}$$ $$\iff \text{rank}(A) < n$$

Every one of these is a rephrasing of the same geometric fact: **the transformation $A$ loses information**. It maps a higher-dimensional object into a lower-dimensional one. Once information is lost, it cannot be recovered — and that is why no inverse exists.
