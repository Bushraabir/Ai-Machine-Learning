The **Fourier matrix** is the matrix representation of the **Discrete Fourier Transform (DFT)**.  
It converts a vector from the **time / spatial domain** into the **frequency domain**.

For an integer \(n\), the Fourier matrix \(F \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}\) is defined as

$$
F =
\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \\
1 & \omega & \omega^2 & \cdots & \omega^{n-1} \\
1 & \omega^2 & \omega^4 & \cdots & \omega^{2(n-1)} \\
\vdots & \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \\
1 & \omega^{n-1} & \omega^{2(n-1)} & \cdots & \omega^{(n-1)^2}
\end{pmatrix}
$$

where

$$
\omega = e^{\frac{2\pi i}{n}}
$$

is a **primitive \(n\)-th root of unity**.

# Compact Formula

The entry of the Fourier matrix can be written compactly as

$$
F_{jk} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \omega^{jk}
$$
for $j,k = 0,1,2,\ldots,n-1$
Thus each entry is a **power of the complex root of unity**.

# Interpretation

Multiplying a vector by the Fourier matrix performs the **Discrete Fourier Transform**.

If

$$
x =
\begin{pmatrix}
x_0 \\
x_1 \\
\vdots \\
x_{n-1}
\end{pmatrix}
$$

then

$$
y = Fx
$$

produces the **frequency representation** of \(x\).

Each component $y_k$ measures the strength of the **frequency \(k\)** inside the signal.

# Key Properties

## 1. Unitary Matrix

The Fourier matrix is **unitary**, meaning
$$
F^*F = FF^* = I
$$
where \(F^*\) is the **conjugate transpose**.

Thus $F^{-1} = F^*$
This property preserves vector length:
$$
|Fx| = |x|
$$

## 2. Orthogonal Basis (in Complex Space)

The columns of $F$ form an **orthonormal basis** of $\mathbb{C}^n$. Each column represents a **complex sinusoidal wave** with a different frequency.

## 3. Diagonalizes Circulant Matrices

A major theoretical result:
If \(C\) is a **circulant matrix**, then
$$
C = F^* \Lambda F
$$where $\Lambda$ is a **diagonal matrix of eigenvalues**.
This means the Fourier matrix **diagonalizes all circulant matrices simultaneously**.

### Example: $n = 3$

For $n=3$
$$
\omega = e^{2\pi i/3}
$$
The Fourier matrix becomes
$$
F =
\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 & 1 \\
1 & \omega & \omega^2 \\
1 & \omega^2 & \omega
\end{pmatrix}
$$


### Geometric Meaning
The numbers $1, \omega, \omega^2, \ldots, \omega^{n-1}$
lie **evenly spaced on the unit circle** in the complex plane.
Thus the Fourier matrix is built from **complex rotations**, which encode periodic patterns.

## Why the Fourier Matrix Matters

The Fourier matrix is foundational in many areas:
- **Signal processing**
- **Image compression**
- **Fast Fourier Transform (FFT) algorithms**
- **Solving convolution problems**
- **Diagonalizing circulant matrices**
- **Quantum computing**
It provides the mathematical bridge between **time-domain structure and frequency-domain structure**.
