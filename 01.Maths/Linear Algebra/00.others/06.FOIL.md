FOIL comes up in the proof that elimination matrices from the same column compose without a cross term. It is worth writing down what FOIL actually is — precisely — before using it, because the matrix version requires one extra piece of care that the scalar version does not.

- ## FOIL in the Scalar Case
FOIL is a mnemonic for expanding the product of two binomials. Given $(a + b)(c + d)$:
$$ (a + b)(c + d) = \underbrace{ac}_{\text{First}} + \underbrace{ad}_{\text{Outer}} + \underbrace{bc}_{\text{Inner}} + \underbrace{bd}_{\text{Last}} $$
That is all it is. It is just the distributive law applied twice:
$$ (a + b)(c + d) = a(c + d) + b(c + d) = ac + ad + bc + bd $$
In the scalar case, order does not matter. $ac = ca$. The four terms can be rearranged freely.

- ## FOIL for Matrices — The Critical Difference
For matrices, the same distributive law holds:
$$ (A + B)(C + D) = AC + AD + BC + BD $$
This is still valid. But **order of multiplication must be preserved**. Matrix multiplication is not commutative in general, so $AC \neq CA$. When applying FOIL to a matrix product, every term must keep its left-right order.
 **So the rule is: expand by distribution, but never swap the order of factors within a term.**

## The Application: Composing Inverse Elimination Matrices
This is where FOIL earns its place in the 18.06 notes. The setting is Gaussian elimination, where the inverse of an elementary elimination matrix has the form:
$$ E_{ij}^{-1} = I + \ell_{ij}, \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T $$
Here $\mathbf{e}_i$ and $\mathbf{e}_j$ are standard basis vectors (columns of the identity), and $\ell_{ij}$ is the multiplier used to eliminate the $(i,j)$ entry. The outer product $\mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T$ is a matrix that is zero everywhere except for a single $1$ in position $(i, j)$.
- ### Lemma 1 : Same-Column Elimination Matrices Compose Cleanly
Take two inverse elimination matrices from the **same column** $j$, operating on **different rows** $i \neq k$, both with $i, k > j$:
$$ E_{ij}^{-1} \cdot E_{kj}^{-1} = \left(I + \ell_{ij}, \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T\right)\left(I + \ell_{kj}, \mathbf{e}_k \mathbf{e}_j^T\right) $$
Apply FOIL — distribute the left factor across the right, preserving order:
$$ = I \cdot I + I \cdot \ell_{kj}, \mathbf{e}_k \mathbf{e}_j^T + \ell_{ij}, \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T \cdot I + \ell_{ij}, \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T \cdot \ell_{kj}, \mathbf{e}_k \mathbf{e}_j^T $$
Simplify the first three terms (multiplying by $I$ changes nothing):
$$ = I + \ell_{kj}, \mathbf{e}_k \mathbf{e}_j^T + \ell_{ij}, \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T + \underbrace{\ell_{ij},\ell_{kj}; \mathbf{e}_i \left(\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k\right) \mathbf{e}_j^T}_{\text{the cross term}} $$
Now examine the cross term. The key factor inside it is: $\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k$
This is the **dot product** of two standard basis vectors. Explicitly: $\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } j = k \ 0 & \text{if } j \neq k \end{cases}$
Since both matrices eliminate in **column $j$**, and since $i \neq k$ with $i, k > j$, we have $j \neq k$. Therefore: $\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k = 0$
The cross term vanishes entirely. What remains is: $E_{ij}^{-1} \cdot E_{kj}^{-1} = I + \ell_{ij}, \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T + \ell_{kj}, \mathbf{e}_k \mathbf{e}_j^T$

This is exactly the matrix that would result from placing **both multipliers** $\ell_{ij}$ and $\ell_{kj}$ into the identity simultaneously, with no interaction between them. The composition is clean.

## Why This Matters

This lemma is the engine behind the clean form of the $L$ matrix in $LU$ decomposition.When we run elimination and collect all the inverse elimination matrices, we need:
$$ L = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} \cdots E_{n,n-1}^{-1} $$
If any two of these came from **different columns**, the cross term would survive and the product would be messier. But because elimination proceeds column by column — all column-1 operations before column-2, and so on — matrices from the same column appear together in the product, and the lemma guarantees their cross terms vanish.

The result: $L$ ends up being the lower triangular matrix with the multipliers $\ell_{ij}$ sitting directly in their natural positions $(i,j)$, no mixing, no extra terms. The proof that $L$ has this form is FOIL applied repeatedly.

## The Dot Product Fact, Written Out
Because it is easy to invoke without thinking about it, write it out once carefully. The standard basis vector $\mathbf{e}_k \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is the vector that is $1$ in position $k$ and $0$ elsewhere: $\mathbf{e}_k = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ \vdots \ 1 \ \vdots \ 0 \end{bmatrix} \leftarrow \text{position } k$
The dot product $\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k$ is:
$$ \mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k = \sum_{m=1}^{n} (\mathbf{e}_j)_m (\mathbf{e}_k)_m $$
In the sum, $(\mathbf{e}_j)_m = 1$ only when $m = j$, and $(\mathbf{e}_k)_m = 1$ only when $m = k$. Both are $1$ simultaneously only if $j = k$. So the sum is $1$ if $j = k$ and $0$ otherwise. This is the **Kronecker delta**: $\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k = \delta_{jk}$.
