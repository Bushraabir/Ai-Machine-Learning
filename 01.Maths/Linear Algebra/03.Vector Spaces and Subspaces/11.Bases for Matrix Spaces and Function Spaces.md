## Why This Note Exists

Everything so far has lived in $\mathbb{R}^n$: column vectors, subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^n$, matrices acting on $\mathbb{R}^n$. But the definition of a vector space is more general than that. A vector space is any set equipped with addition and scalar multiplication satisfying the eight axioms — and $\mathbb{R}^n$ is just the most familiar example.

Two other natural vector spaces are:

- The space of $m \times n$ matrices, $M_{m \times n}$
- Spaces of functions, like the space of polynomials of degree $\leq n$

These spaces have bases and dimensions, computed by the same logic as before. Working through them forces me to see that "basis" and "dimension" are properties of the algebraic structure, not of the geometric picture of arrows in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

---

## The Space of $m \times n$ Matrices

Fix $m$ and $n$. The set $M_{m \times n}$ of all $m \times n$ matrices with real entries is a vector space under:

- Addition: $(A + B)_{ij} = A_{ij} + B_{ij}$
- Scalar multiplication: $(cA)_{ij} = c A_{ij}$

The zero vector is the zero matrix.

**What is the dimension of $M_{m \times n}$?**

A basis: the **elementary matrices** $E^{(i,j)}$, defined as the matrix with a 1 in position $(i,j)$ and 0 everywhere else, for $1 \leq i \leq m$ and $1 \leq j \leq n$.

For example, in $M_{2 \times 3}$:

$$ E^{(1,1)} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad E^{(1,2)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad E^{(1,3)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

$$ E^{(2,1)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad E^{(2,2)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad E^{(2,3)} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

There are $mn$ such matrices.

**Independence:** Suppose $\sum_{i,j} c_{ij} E^{(i,j)} = 0$. The $(i,j)$ entry of the left side is $c_{ij}$. For this to equal the zero matrix, every $c_{ij} = 0$. So the $E^{(i,j)}$ are independent.

**Spanning:** Any matrix $A \in M_{m \times n}$ satisfies $A = \sum_{i,j} A_{ij} E^{(i,j)}$. So the $E^{(i,j)}$ span $M_{m \times n}$.

Therefore:

$$ \dim M_{m \times n} = mn $$

For $M_{2 \times 3}$: dimension 6. For $M_{n \times n}$: dimension $n^2$.

This is the right answer and the right proof, but I want to absorb what it means. The dimension is $mn$ because there are $mn$ independent entries in the matrix — each one can be chosen freely. The elementary matrices are the analogue of the standard basis vectors $e_i$; they isolate a single entry, just as $e_i$ isolates a single coordinate.

## Important Subspaces of $M_{n \times n}$

Inside the space $M_{n \times n}$ of $n \times n$ matrices (dimension $n^2$) there are natural subspaces:

**Symmetric matrices:** $S = {A \in M_{n \times n} : A^T = A}$.

A symmetric matrix is determined by its entries on and above the diagonal: $A_{ij}$ for $i \leq j$. That is $\frac{n(n+1)}{2}$ free entries.

$$ \dim S = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} $$

A basis for $S$: the matrices $E^{(i,i)}$ for $1 \leq i \leq n$ (diagonal elementary matrices), plus the matrices $E^{(i,j)} + E^{(j,i)}$ for $i < j$ (symmetrized off-diagonal pairs).

For $n = 2$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Three basis matrices. $\dim S = 3 = \frac{2 \cdot 3}{2}$. Correct.

**Skew-symmetric matrices:** $K = {A \in M_{n \times n} : A^T = -A}$.

The diagonal must be zero (since $A_{ii} = -A_{ii}$ forces $A_{ii} = 0$). The above-diagonal entries determine everything: $A_{ij}$ for $i < j$, with $A_{ji} = -A_{ij}$.

$$ \dim K = \frac{n(n-1)}{2} $$

A basis: the matrices $E^{(i,j)} - E^{(j,i)}$ for $i < j$.

For $n = 2$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -1 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

One basis matrix. $\dim K = 1 = \frac{2 \cdot 1}{2}$. Correct.

**The decomposition:** Every square matrix splits uniquely into a symmetric part and a skew-symmetric part:

$$ A = \underbrace{\frac{A + A^T}{2}}_{\text{symmetric}} + \underbrace{\frac{A - A^T}{2}}_{\text{skew-symmetric}} $$

This means $M_{n \times n} = S + K$ and $S \cap K = {0}$ (the only matrix that is both symmetric and skew-symmetric is the zero matrix). So

$$ \dim M_{n \times n} = \dim S + \dim K = \frac{n(n+1)}{2} + \frac{n(n-1)}{2} = \frac{n^2 + n + n^2 - n}{2} = n^2 $$

which is consistent with $\dim M_{n \times n} = n^2$. The decomposition is a direct sum: $M_{n \times n} = S \oplus K$.

## The Space of Polynomials of Degree $\leq n$

Let $\mathcal{P}_n$ denote the set of all polynomials with real coefficients and degree at most $n$:

$$ \mathcal{P}_n = {a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots + a_n x^n : a_0, \ldots, a_n \in \mathbb{R}} $$

This is a vector space: the sum of two such polynomials has degree at most $n$, scalar multiples have degree at most $n$, and the zero polynomial is in the set.

**The standard basis:** ${1, x, x^2, \ldots, x^n}$.

**Independence:** Suppose $c_0 + c_1 x + c_2 x^2 + \cdots + c_n x^n = 0$ as a polynomial (meaning it is zero for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$). Then all coefficients are zero: $c_0 = c_1 = \cdots = c_n = 0$. This is a fact about polynomials — a nonzero polynomial of degree $n$ has at most $n$ roots, so it cannot be identically zero unless all coefficients vanish.

**Spanning:** Every polynomial $p(x) = a_0 + a_1 x + \cdots + a_n x^n$ is immediately a combination of $1, x, \ldots, x^n$.

Therefore:

$$ \dim \mathcal{P}_n = n + 1 $$

Not $n$ — there are $n+1$ monomials from $x^0$ through $x^n$. This is a small count that I have gotten wrong before by forgetting the constant term.

---

## Other Bases for $\mathcal{P}_n$

The monomial basis ${1, x, x^2, \ldots, x^n}$ is the most obvious, but not always the most useful. Since $\dim \mathcal{P}_n = n+1$, any $n+1$ independent polynomials in $\mathcal{P}_n$ form a basis.

**Lagrange basis:** Given $n+1$ distinct points $x_0, x_1, \ldots, x_n \in \mathbb{R}$, define the Lagrange basis polynomials

$$ \ell_j(x) = \prod_{\substack{k=0 \ k \neq j}}^{n} \frac{x - x_k}{x_j - x_k} $$

Each $\ell_j$ has degree $n$, satisfies $\ell_j(x_j) = 1$ and $\ell_j(x_k) = 0$ for $k \neq j$.

**Independence:** Suppose $\sum_j c_j \ell_j(x) = 0$ for all $x$. Evaluate at $x = x_j$: $c_j \ell_j(x_j) = c_j = 0$. So all $c_j = 0$.

**Spanning:** Given any $p \in \mathcal{P}_n$, the polynomial $q(x) = \sum_j p(x_j) \ell_j(x)$ has degree $\leq n$ and agrees with $p$ at all $n+1$ points $x_0, \ldots, x_n$. Since $p - q$ is a polynomial of degree $\leq n$ with $n+1$ roots, $p - q = 0$, so $p = q$.

The Lagrange basis is useful for polynomial interpolation: given values $y_0, \ldots, y_n$ at the nodes, the unique polynomial of degree $\leq n$ passing through all of them is $p(x) = \sum_j y_j \ell_j(x)$. The coordinates of $p$ in the Lagrange basis are simply the values $(y_0, \ldots, y_n)$.

## The Infinite-Dimensional Case: A Preview

The space $\mathcal{P}$ of all polynomials (no degree bound) is also a vector space. The set ${1, x, x^2, x^3, \ldots}$ is linearly independent — no finite combination of monomials can be identically zero unless all coefficients are zero. And this set spans all polynomials by definition.

So ${1, x, x^2, \ldots}$ is a basis for $\mathcal{P}$, but it is an infinite basis. The dimension of $\mathcal{P}$ is infinite — or more precisely, countably infinite.

The space $C[0,1]$ of continuous functions on $[0,1]$ is also a vector space. It is infinite-dimensional, and in fact its dimension is uncountably infinite — you cannot even list a basis. The Fourier basis ${1, \cos x, \sin x, \cos 2x, \sin 2x, \ldots}$ is a basis in a weaker sense (convergence in $L^2$, not finite combinations), which is the starting point for Fourier analysis.

I am not going to prove anything about infinite-dimensional spaces here. The point is to record that the definitions of basis and dimension extend naturally — or at least, they suggest how to extend. The finite-dimensional theory developed in this course is the foundation for all of it.

## The Pattern Across All These Spaces

What all these examples have in common is the following template:

1. Identify what determines an element of the space uniquely (entries $A_{ij}$, coefficients $a_k$, values at nodes $y_j$)
2. Build a basis element for each free parameter — one basis element that is 1 in that parameter and 0 in all others
3. The number of free parameters is the dimension

This is the same logic as the standard basis in $\mathbb{R}^n$: $e_i$ is 1 in position $i$ and 0 elsewhere, and the number of positions is $n = \dim \mathbb{R}^n$.

Every finite-dimensional vector space with a natural set of parameters admits a canonical basis built this way. It is always the "put a 1 here and 0 everywhere else" basis.
