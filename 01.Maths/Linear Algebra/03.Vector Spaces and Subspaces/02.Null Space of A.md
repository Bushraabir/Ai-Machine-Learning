
## Why I'm Writing This

Solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ feels, at first, like a trivial problem — the answer is always $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$, right? It is not, unless $A$ has full column rank. The null space is the entire solution set of the homogeneous equation, and understanding its structure — how many free variables there are, how to find a basis for it, why it is a subspace — is the foundation for understanding the solution set of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ in general. You cannot understand the particular solution without first understanding the homogeneous one.

---

## Definition

The **null space** of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is

$$N(A) = {, \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n : A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} ,}$$

It is a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ — the domain of $A$, not the codomain. I established this in [[Vector Spaces and Subspaces]]. The proof is two lines: if $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ and $A\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0}$, then $A(c\mathbf{x} + d\mathbf{y}) = cA\mathbf{x} + dA\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0}$.

What I want here is not the proof that $N(A)$ is a subspace — that is done — but the algorithm for finding it, and the structural understanding of what it looks like.

---

## The Algorithm: Elimination to Reduced Row Echelon Form

The tool for finding $N(A)$ is Gaussian elimination, carried all the way to **reduced row echelon form (rref)**, not just row echelon form. Let me be precise about what these mean.

**Row echelon form (ref):** After forward elimination. Upper triangular structure. The first nonzero entry in each row (the **pivot**) is strictly to the right of the pivot in the row above. All zero rows are at the bottom.

**Reduced row echelon form (rref):** Row echelon form, plus:

- Every pivot is $1$ (scaled).
- Every entry above a pivot is $0$ (eliminated upward as well as downward).

The rref of a matrix is unique — there is only one rref for a given matrix, regardless of the order of elimination steps. That uniqueness is what makes rref the right canonical form for understanding the null space.

---

## Pivot Columns and Free Columns

After reduction to rref, columns of $A$ fall into two categories:

**Pivot columns:** Columns that contain a leading $1$ (a pivot). There are $r$ of them, where $r = \text{rank}(A)$.

**Free columns:** Columns with no pivot. There are $n - r$ of them.

The variables $x_j$ corresponding to free columns are called **free variables**. They can be set to any value. Once the free variables are chosen, the pivot variables are completely determined by back substitution.

This is the structural fact I want to internalize: $N(A)$ has dimension $n - r$, and I find a basis for it by setting each free variable to $1$ (and all others to $0$) in turn, then solving for the pivot variables.

## A Full Example: $3 \times 4$ Matrix

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{bmatrix}$$

I want to find all $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^4$ with $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.

### Step 1: Forward Elimination

**Pivot in column 1: value $1$, row 1.**

Multipliers: $\ell_{21} = 2$, $\ell_{31} = 3$.

Row 2 $\leftarrow$ Row 2 $- 2 \cdot$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \end{bmatrix} - 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 3 $\leftarrow$ Row 3 $- 3 \cdot$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{bmatrix} - 3\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

After step 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Column 2 has no pivot** — it is all zeros below row 1 (and row 1's entry in column 2 is above the current elimination front). Column 2 is a free column.

**Pivot in column 3: value $2$, row 2.**

Row 3 $\leftarrow$ Row 3 $- 1 \cdot$ Row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

After forward elimination (row echelon form):

$$U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Rank $r = 2$. Pivots in columns 1 and 3. Free columns: 2 and 4. Free variables: $x_2$ and $x_4$.

### Step 2: Reduce to rref

Scale row 2 by $1/2$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Eliminate above pivot in column 3: Row 1 $\leftarrow$ Row 1 $- 2 \cdot$ Row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} - 2\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Reduced row echelon form:

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

### Step 3: Read Off the Solution

The system $R\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ is:

$$x_1 + 2x_2 - 2x_4 = 0$$ $$x_3 + 2x_4 = 0$$

Free variables: $x_2$ and $x_4$. Solve for the pivot variables $x_1$ and $x_3$:

$$x_1 = -2x_2 + 2x_4$$ $$x_3 = -2x_4$$

### Step 4: Find the Special Solutions (Basis for $N(A)$)

Set $(x_2, x_4) = (1, 0)$: pivot variables give $x_1 = -2$, $x_3 = 0$.

$$\mathbf{s}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Set $(x_2, x_4) = (0, 1)$: pivot variables give $x_1 = 2$, $x_3 = -2$.

$$\mathbf{s}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

The null space is

$$N(A) = \left{ x_2 \begin{bmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} + x_4 \begin{bmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{bmatrix} : x_2, x_4 \in \mathbb{R} \right} = \text{span}(\mathbf{s}_1, \mathbf{s}_2)$$

$\dim N(A) = 2 = n - r = 4 - 2$. The rank-nullity theorem is satisfied.

### Verification

Check $A\mathbf{s}_1 = \mathbf{0}$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -2 + 2 \ -4 + 4 \ -6 + 6 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} \checkmark$$

Check $A\mathbf{s}_2 = \mathbf{0}$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 + 0 - 4 + 2 \ 4 + 0 - 12 + 8 \ 6 + 0 - 16 + 10 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} \checkmark$$

## The rref and the Null Space: The General Pattern

Let me make the structure explicit for the general case. Suppose $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ has rank $r$. After reduction to rref, reorder the columns so that pivot columns come first. The rref looks like:

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & F \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

where $I_r$ is $r \times r$ identity (in the pivot columns), $F$ is $r \times (n-r)$ (the free column entries in the pivot rows), and the zero blocks are padding.

**Why this structure gives the null space immediately.** The equation $R\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ with pivot variables $\mathbf{x}_p \in \mathbb{R}^r$ and free variables $\mathbf{x}_f \in \mathbb{R}^{n-r}$ reads:

$$I_r \mathbf{x}_p + F \mathbf{x}_f = \mathbf{0} \implies \mathbf{x}_p = -F \mathbf{x}_f$$

So the full solution is

$$\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_p \ \mathbf{x}_f \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -F \ I_{n-r} \end{bmatrix} \mathbf{x}_f$$

The **null space matrix** is

$$N = \begin{bmatrix} -F \ I_{n-r} \end{bmatrix} \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times (n-r)}$$

The columns of $N$ are exactly the special solutions. The identity block $I_{n-r}$ in the bottom half is what you get by setting each free variable to $1$ and all others to $0$ — which is exactly the procedure I followed above.

**In the worked example:** $r = 2$, $n - r = 2$. The free columns of $R$ (columns 2 and 4) give

$$F = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -2 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

So $-F = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 2 \ 0 & -2 \end{bmatrix}$ and

$$N = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & 2 \ 1 & 0 \ 0 & -2 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Reading off the columns: $\mathbf{s}_1 = (-2, 1, 0, 0)^T$ and $\mathbf{s}_2 = (2, 0, -2, 1)^T$. Matches what I found above. $\checkmark$

## The Rank-Nullity Theorem

**Theorem.** For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$:

$$\text{rank}(A) + \dim N(A) = n$$

equivalently $r + (n - r) = n$.

_Proof._ The rref has $r$ pivot columns and $n - r$ free columns. Each free column produces exactly one special solution, and these solutions are linearly independent (the $i$-th special solution has a $1$ in the $i$-th free variable position and $0$ in all other free variable positions — the $I_{n-r}$ block). So $\dim N(A) = n - r$. $\square$

The proof is fully constructive: not only does it tell you the dimension, it gives you the basis explicitly.

---

## Distinct Cases by Rank

The structure of $N(A)$ depends entirely on the rank $r$ relative to $n$.

**$r = n$ (full column rank):** No free variables. The only solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ is $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$. $N(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$, dimension $0$.

This happens exactly when the columns of $A$ are linearly independent. The map $\mathbf{x} \mapsto A\mathbf{x}$ is injective — different inputs give different outputs.

**$r < n$ (rank deficient in columns):** There are $n - r \geq 1$ free variables. $N(A)$ is nontrivial — it contains infinitely many vectors. $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ has infinitely many solutions.

**Square matrix, $m = n$:** $A$ is invertible if and only if $r = n$, i.e., $N(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$. If $A$ is singular, $r < n$ and $N(A)$ is nontrivial.

## What the Null Space Really Means

Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ and think of $A$ as a linear map $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$, $T(\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{x}$.

$N(A)$ is the **kernel** of this map — the set of vectors that get sent to zero. The map collapses the entire subspace $N(A)$ to the single point $\mathbf{0}$.

**Why this matters for $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$.** Suppose $\mathbf{x}_p$ is one particular solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ (if one exists). Then the complete solution set is:

$${\mathbf{x} : A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}} = \mathbf{x}_p + N(A) = {\mathbf{x}_p + \mathbf{v} : \mathbf{v} \in N(A)}$$

_Proof._ If $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$, then $A(\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_p) = A\mathbf{x} - A\mathbf{x}_p = \mathbf{b} - \mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}$, so $\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{x}_p \in N(A)$. Conversely, if $\mathbf{v} \in N(A)$, then $A(\mathbf{x}_p + \mathbf{v}) = A\mathbf{x}_p + A\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{b} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{b}$. $\square$

The solution set of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is a **coset** of $N(A)$ — a translate of the null space by the particular solution. It is not a subspace (unless $\mathbf{b} = \mathbf{0}$), but it is a flat (an affine subspace) of dimension $n - r$.

**Remark (personal).** This structure — particular solution plus null space — is the linear algebra analogue of the general solution to a linear ODE: $y = y_{\text{particular}} + y_{\text{homogeneous}}$. The homogeneous solution is always present; the particular solution shifts you to the right affine flat. I find it genuinely helpful that both settings have identical structure.

## A Second Example: When There Are No Free Variables

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \ 2 & 7 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is $3 \times 2$, so $n = 2$. I expect at most $2$ pivots.

Forward elimination. Multiplier $\ell_{21} = 2$:

Row 2 $\leftarrow$ Row 2 $- 2 \cdot$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 7 \end{bmatrix} - 2\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 3 has $\ell_{31} = 0$, so no change. After step 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \ 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 3 $\leftarrow$ Row 3 $- 1 \cdot$ Row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} - \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Reduce to rref: Row 1 $\leftarrow$ Row 1 $- 3 \cdot$ Row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 3 \end{bmatrix} - 3\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Rank $r = 2 = n$. Both columns are pivot columns. No free variables.

$R\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ gives $x_1 = 0$ and $x_2 = 0$. So $N(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$, dimension $0 = n - r = 2 - 2$.

The columns of $A$ are linearly independent.

---

## Linear Independence via the Null Space

This gives a clean definition:

**The columns of $A$ are linearly independent if and only if $N(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$.**

_Proof._ The columns are linearly independent iff the only solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ is $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$, by definition of linear independence ($x_1\mathbf{a}_1 + \cdots + x_n\mathbf{a}_n = \mathbf{0}$ only when all $x_i = 0$). This is exactly the statement $N(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$. $\square$

So computing the null space and testing linear independence are the same computation.

## Summary of the Algorithm

Given $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, to find $N(A)$:

1. Row reduce $A$ to rref $R$.
2. Identify pivot columns and free columns. Let $r$ = number of pivots.
3. For each free variable $x_j$: set $x_j = 1$, all other free variables $= 0$, and solve $R\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ for the pivot variables.
4. Each choice gives one special solution. Collect them: ${\mathbf{s}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_{n-r}}$.
5. $N(A) = \text{span}(\mathbf{s}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_{n-r})$, and ${\mathbf{s}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{s}_{n-r}}$ is a basis for $N(A)$.

$\dim N(A) = n - r$. Check: $r + (n-r) = n$. ✓

## Open Questions

- The rref is unique: the same matrix always produces the same rref regardless of the sequence of row operations. I have used this but not proved it. The proof goes through the uniqueness of pivot positions. Worth a short proof sketch.
- The null space of $A^T$ — the left null space $N(A^T)$ — lives in $\mathbb{R}^m$ and has dimension $m - r$. The same rref algorithm applies to $A^T$. The left null space is orthogonal to the column space: $N(A^T) \perp C(A)$. I will establish this in [[Projections and Orthogonality]].
- For the complete picture: once I have $N(A)$ and a particular solution $\mathbf{x}_p$, the full solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ is $\mathbf{x}_p + N(A)$. The question of when $\mathbf{x}_p$ exists is the question of when $\mathbf{b} \in C(A)$. These two questions — solvability and solution structure — are the subject of [[Complete Solution to Ax=b]].
