
## The Setup I Keep Coming Back To

Everything here lives inside one question: given $Ax = 0$, what is the solution set?

The answer comes in two layers. First, identify _which columns of $A$ are pivots and which are free_. Second, construct _one special solution per free variable_ by setting that free variable to 1 and the rest to 0, then back-substituting. The nullspace is then the span of those special solutions. That is the whole story — but let me be careful about each step.

---

## Row Reduction and the Pivot Columns

Take a concrete matrix and reduce it. I want to see every entry.

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Step 1:** Eliminate below the $(1,1)$ pivot. Subtract $2\cdot R_1$ from $R_2$, then $3\cdot R_1$ from $R_3$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Step 2:** The $(2,2)$ entry is 0 — no pivot there. Column 2 is a _free column_. Move right: column 3 has entry 2. That is the second pivot. Subtract $R_2$ from $R_3$:

$$ U = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

This is the row echelon form $U$. Read off immediately:

- **Pivot columns:** 1 and 3 (columns where a leading nonzero entry appeared)
- **Free columns:** 2 and 4 (columns 2 and 4 never produced a pivot)
- **Rank:** $r = 2$ (number of pivots)

The pivots themselves are 1 and 2.

---

## Why "Free Column" Is the Right Name

A free column is one that is a _linear combination of the pivot columns to its left_. Here column 2 is exactly $2 \cdot (\text{col 1})$, and column 4 is $2\cdot(\text{col 3}) + 2\cdot(\text{col 1}) / \ldots$ — I should check this more carefully by row-reducing further to reduced row echelon form.

**Reduced row echelon form (rref):** Divide $R_2$ by 2, then eliminate _upward_ using the pivots.

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 / 2$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_1 \leftarrow R_1 - 2R_2$:

$$ R = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Now the pivot columns (1 and 3) contain the identity's columns: $e_1$ and $e_2$ embedded in $\mathbb{R}^3$. The free columns reveal their dependence explicitly:

- Column 2 of $R$ is $\begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$, meaning $\text{col}_2 = 2\cdot\text{col}_1$
- Column 4 of $R$ is $\begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 2 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$, meaning $\text{col}_4 = -2\cdot\text{col}_1 + 2\cdot\text{col}_3$

This is the information that gets encoded directly into the special solutions.

---

## Constructing the Special Solutions

The nullspace of $A$ equals the nullspace of $R$ (row operations do not change the solution set of $Ax = 0$). So I solve $Rx = 0$.

Write out $Rx = 0$ with $x = (x_1, x_2, x_3, x_4)^T$:

$$ x_1 + 2x_2 - 2x_4 = 0 $$ $$ x_3 + 2x_4 = 0 $$

The pivot variables are $x_1$ and $x_3$. The free variables are $x_2$ and $x_4$. I get to assign free variables anything I want; the pivot variables are then forced.

**Special solution 1:** Set $x_2 = 1$, $x_4 = 0$.

From equation 2: $x_3 = -2(0) = 0$.  
From equation 1: $x_1 = -2(1) + 2(0) = -2$.

$$ s_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Special solution 2:** Set $x_2 = 0$, $x_4 = 1$.

From equation 2: $x_3 = -2(1) = -2$.  
From equation 1: $x_1 = -2(0) + 2(1) = 2$.

$$ s_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

**The nullspace:**

$$ N(A) = c_1 \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} + c_2 \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad c_1, c_2 \in \mathbb{R} $$

This is a 2-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^4$, consistent with the rank-nullity theorem: $\dim N(A) = n - r = 4 - 2 = 2$.

---

## The Pattern Worth Memorizing (But Also Understanding)

From the rref $R$, the free columns have entries $\begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 2 \end{pmatrix}$ in the pivot rows. The special solutions are built by negating those entries and placing them in the pivot-variable slots, with $0$s and $1$s in the free-variable slots.

More explicitly: if the free columns of $R$ (restricted to pivot rows) form a matrix $F$, and the pivot columns form $I_r$, then the nullspace matrix is

$$ N = \begin{pmatrix} -F \ I_{n-r} \end{pmatrix} \quad \text{(rows ordered: pivot rows, then free rows)} $$

Check: for our example, $F = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -2 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$, so

$$ N = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 2 \ 0 & -2 \ 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

The columns of $N$ are exactly $s_1$ and $s_2$. This is the formula Strang calls the "complete solution structure."

---

## The Rank-Nullity Theorem as a Sanity Check

Every time I compute a nullspace, I check:

$$ \dim N(A) = n - \text{rank}(A) $$

Here $n = 4$, $\text{rank}(A) = 2$, so $\dim N(A) = 2$. I got two special solutions. Good.

If I ever get a different count, I made an arithmetic error in elimination — usually a sign error in the upward elimination step, or a missed pivot swap.

---

## What the Pivot Columns Actually Span

The pivot columns of $A$ (not $R$, but $A$ itself) form a basis for the column space $C(A)$. Here:

$$ \text{col}_1(A) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \text{col}_3(A) = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 6 \ 8 \end{pmatrix} $$

These two vectors are linearly independent (neither is a multiple of the other), and every other column of $A$ is a combination of them. So $C(A) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3$ is a 2-dimensional plane through the origin.

This is the link between the pivot column concept in elimination and the column space concept in geometry.

---

## A Note I Want to Remember

The choice of "which free variable gets the 1" in each special solution is a convention, not a theorem. Setting $x_2 = 1, x_4 = 0$ for $s_1$ and $x_2 = 0, x_4 = 1$ for $s_2$ gives me vectors that are a _basis_ for $N(A)$ — they are linearly independent by construction, because $s_1$ has a 1 in position 2 and a 0 in position 4, while $s_2$ has a 0 in position 2 and a 1 in position 4.

This is the same trick used to build the standard basis: distinguishing vectors by where their 1 sits.
