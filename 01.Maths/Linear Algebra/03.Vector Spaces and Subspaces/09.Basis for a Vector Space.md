
## The Definition

A **basis** for a vector space $V$ is a set of vectors $v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_k$ that satisfies two conditions simultaneously:

1. $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ are **linearly independent**
2. $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ **span** $V$

Neither condition alone is enough. Independence without spanning means the set is too small — it misses some directions in $V$. Spanning without independence means the set is too large — it carries redundant vectors. A basis is exactly the right size.

Every vector in $V$ can be written as a linear combination of the basis vectors, and this representation is **unique**. That uniqueness is what makes a basis useful: it is a coordinate system.

**Proof of uniqueness:** Suppose $v = c_1 v_1 + \cdots + c_k v_k$ and also $v = d_1 v_1 + \cdots + d_k v_k$. Subtract: $0 = (c_1 - d_1)v_1 + \cdots + (c_k - d_k)v_k$. Independence forces $c_i - d_i = 0$ for all $i$. So $c_i = d_i$. $\blacksquare$

This is the theorem I find most clarifying about what a basis actually is: it is a unique coordinate system, not just a spanning set. Every point in $V$ has exactly one address.

## The Standard Basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$

The most immediate example: in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the standard basis is

$$ e_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 0 \ \vdots \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad e_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ 0 \ \vdots \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \ldots, \quad e_n = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ \vdots \ 0 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Independence: the only solution to $c_1 e_1 + \cdots + c_n e_n = 0$ is $c_i = 0$ for all $i$ — read off component by component.

Spanning: any $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)^T = x_1 e_1 + \cdots + x_n e_n$.

The standard basis is the identity matrix's columns. This is not a coincidence — it is the definition of the identity.

## A Non-Standard Basis for $\mathbb{R}^2$

Bases are not unique. In $\mathbb{R}^2$:

$$ v_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ -1 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Independence:** $c_1(1,1)^T + c_2(1,-1)^T = 0$ gives the system

$$ c_1 + c_2 = 0 $$ $$ c_1 - c_2 = 0 $$

Adding: $2c_1 = 0$ so $c_1 = 0$, then $c_2 = 0$. Independent.

**Spanning:** Given any $(a, b)^T \in \mathbb{R}^2$, solve $c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 = (a,b)^T$:

$$ c_1 + c_2 = a $$ $$ c_1 - c_2 = b $$

Adding: $c_1 = \frac{a+b}{2}$. Subtracting: $c_2 = \frac{a-b}{2}$. A solution exists for every $(a,b)$. Spans $\mathbb{R}^2$.

So ${v_1, v_2}$ is a basis. Every vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ has a unique representation in this basis. For example:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 3 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} = 2 \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} + 1 \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ -1 \end{pmatrix} $$

The coordinates $(2, 1)$ are in the ${v_1, v_2}$ basis, not the standard basis. This is what "change of basis" means — the vector is the same; the address depends on which coordinate system is in use.

## The Fundamental Theorem: All Bases Have the Same Size

This is the theorem that makes "dimension" well-defined.

**Theorem:** If $v_1, \ldots, v_m$ and $w_1, \ldots, w_k$ are both bases for the same vector space $V$, then $m = k$.

**Proof:** I will show $m \leq k$ and $k \leq m$, which forces $m = k$.

Since $v_1, \ldots, v_m$ span $V$ and $w_1, \ldots, w_k$ are in $V$ and are independent, I claim $k \leq m$.

Form the matrix $A$ with columns $v_1, \ldots, v_m$. Since the $v_i$ span $V$, each $w_j$ is a combination of them: $w_j = Ac_j$ for some $c_j \in \mathbb{R}^m$. Form the matrix $C = [c_1 \mid \cdots \mid c_k]$, so the $w_j$ are the columns of $AC$.

The $w_j$ are independent, so $N(AC) = {0}$, meaning $\text{rank}(AC) = k$. But $\text{rank}(AC) \leq \text{rank}(A) \leq m$. So $k \leq m$.

By symmetry (swapping the roles of the two bases), $m \leq k$. Hence $m = k$. $\blacksquare$

This proof is worth understanding slowly. The key move is: independence of the $w_j$ forces the rank of $AC$ to be $k$, but rank cannot exceed the number of columns of $A$, which is $m$. So $k \leq m$.

## Dimension

The **dimension** of a vector space $V$, written $\dim V$, is the number of vectors in any basis for $V$.

This is well-defined by the theorem above.

Some standard dimensions:

- $\dim \mathbb{R}^n = n$
- $\dim {0} = 0$ (the zero space has the empty set as its only basis — zero vectors, spanning nothing, with vacuous independence)
- $\dim C(A) = \text{rank}(A) = r$
- $\dim N(A) = n - r$
- $\dim C(A^T) = r$
- $\dim N(A^T) = m - r$

The last four are the dimensions of the four fundamental subspaces of an $m \times n$ matrix of rank $r$. They all follow from the rank.

## Bases from Row Reduction

Given a matrix $A$, row reduction produces bases for all four fundamental subspaces. Let me be precise about which matrix to use for each.

**Basis for $C(A)$:** Take the **pivot columns of $A$** (the original matrix, before any row operations). These are linearly independent (by definition of pivot columns) and span $C(A)$ (every free column is a combination of them).

Do not take pivot columns of $U$ or $R$ — those span a different space.

**Basis for $N(A)$:** The special solutions — one per free variable, built from the $-F/I$ recipe in the rref. These are independent by construction (each has a 1 in one free-variable position and 0 in all others) and span $N(A)$.

**Basis for $C(A^T)$ (row space):** The **nonzero rows of $R$** (the rref). Row operations do not change the row space, so the rows of $R$ span the same row space as the rows of $A$. And the nonzero rows of $R$ are independent (each has a leading 1 in a column where all other rows have 0).

**Basis for $N(A^T)$ (left nullspace):** The rows of $A$ that became zero rows during elimination — more precisely, the combinations of rows of $A$ that produce zero. These come from the $E$ matrix in $EA = R$; the rows of $E$ corresponding to zero rows of $R$ are a basis for $N(A^T)$.

## A Full Example

Take

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{pmatrix} $$

with rref

$$ R = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Pivot columns: 1 and 3. Free columns: 2 and 4. Rank $r = 2$.

**Basis for $C(A)$:** pivot columns of $A$:

$$ \left{ \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 6 \ 8 \end{pmatrix} \right} $$

Dimension: 2.

**Basis for $N(A)$:** special solutions from $-F/I$ recipe:

$$ \left{ \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} \right} $$

Dimension: $4 - 2 = 2$.

**Basis for $C(A^T)$:** nonzero rows of $R$:

$$ \left{ \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 0 \ -2 \end{pmatrix}, \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 1 \ 2 \end{pmatrix} \right} $$

Dimension: 2.

**Check:** $\dim C(A) + \dim N(A^T) = r + (m - r) = 2 + 1 = 3 = m$. And $\dim C(A^T) + \dim N(A) = r + (n - r) = 2 + 2 = 4 = n$. Both check out.

## The Basis Completion Theorem

Two useful facts I want to record:

**Reducing a spanning set to a basis:** Given vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ that span $V$, remove the ones corresponding to free columns (they are combinations of earlier vectors). What remains are the pivot columns — a basis for $V$.

**Extending an independent set to a basis:** Given independent vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_j$ in $V$ with $j < \dim V$, there exist vectors $v_{j+1}, \ldots, v_k$ in $V$ such that $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ is a basis for $V$.

Both facts are practically important. The first is what row reduction does. The second is used in proofs — you often need to extend a partial basis to a full one to apply some argument.

## Three Equivalent Conditions for a Basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$

For $n$ vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the following are equivalent:

1. $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ are a basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$
2. $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ are linearly independent
3. $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ span $\mathbb{R}^n$

The equivalence holds only when the count is exactly right — exactly $n$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$. In that case, independence implies spanning and spanning implies independence, because either condition forces the matrix $A = [v_1 \mid \cdots \mid v_n]$ to have rank $n$, which implies both.

For fewer than $n$ vectors, you can have independence without spanning. For more than $n$ vectors, you can have spanning without independence. The equivalence is a special gift that only appears at the exact right count.

## Why This Matters Beyond Solving $Ax = b$

The basis concept is where linear algebra becomes geometry and analysis, not just computation.

A basis gives a vector space a coordinate system. Once a basis is fixed, every vector has a unique tuple of coordinates. Linear maps become matrices. The change-of-basis formula describes how coordinates transform when the coordinate system changes. Eigendecomposition is the search for a basis in which a linear map becomes diagonal.

Everything downstream — orthogonal projections, least squares, principal component analysis, the spectral theorem, Fourier series (an infinite-dimensional version) — is built on the idea that the right basis simplifies the problem.

The choice of basis is not fixed by the mathematics; it is a choice made by the person solving the problem. Choosing well is one of the main skills of linear algebra.
