
## The Definition

A set of vectors $v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_k \in \mathbb{R}^n$ **spans** a subspace $V$ if every vector in $V$ can be written as a linear combination of $v_1, \ldots, v_k$.

That is: for every $v \in V$, there exist scalars $c_1, \ldots, c_k$ such that

$$ v = c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 + \cdots + c_k v_k $$

The subspace $V$ itself is then called the **span** of $v_1, \ldots, v_k$, written

$$ V = \text{span}(v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_k) $$

The span is always a subspace — it contains $0$ (take all $c_i = 0$), is closed under addition, and is closed under scalar multiplication. These are immediate from the definition of linear combination. The span of any set of vectors is the smallest subspace containing all of them.

---

## Spanning as a Column Space Statement

If I arrange $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ as columns of a matrix $A$:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \ v_1 & v_2 & \cdots & v_k \ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix} $$

then $\text{span}(v_1, \ldots, v_k) = C(A)$, the column space of $A$.

The question "does $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ span $V$?" becomes: "is $V \subseteq C(A)$?" And if $V = C(A)$, they span exactly $V$.

This reframing is important. All the machinery of row reduction — rank, pivot columns, the rref — now applies directly to spanning questions.

## A Worked Example

Do the following three vectors span $\mathbb{R}^3$?

$$ v_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 2 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 1 \ 7 \end{pmatrix} $$

Form $A = [v_1 \mid v_2 \mid v_3]$ and row reduce:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 2 & 1 & 7 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 2R_1$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Three pivots. Rank $r = 3 = m$. The column space $C(A) = \mathbb{R}^3$.

So yes — $v_1, v_2, v_3$ span $\mathbb{R}^3$.

But notice: $v_3 = 2v_1 + v_2$ from the linear dependence (column 3 is a free column... wait, there are three pivots here, so actually all columns are pivot columns and the vectors are independent). Let me verify: $2(1,0,2)^T + 1(0,1,3)^T = (2,1,7)^T = v_3$. Yes — $v_3 = 2v_1 + v_2$.

This is a contradiction: three pivots means full rank means the columns are independent. But I just showed $v_3 = 2v_1 + v_2$.

Let me recheck. $A$ has three columns and three rows. The echelon form has three nonzero rows. Three pivots. So rank is 3. But if rank is 3 and there are 3 columns, the nullspace is ${0}$, meaning the columns are independent. But $2v_1 + v_2 - v_3 = (2,0,4)^T + (0,1,3)^T - (2,1,7)^T = (0,0,0)^T$. That is a nontrivial combination equaling zero. So the columns are dependent. There is an error somewhere in my elimination.

Recheck: $R_3 - 2R_1 = (2,1,7) - 2(1,0,2) = (2-2, 1-0, 7-4) = (0,1,3)$. Correct.  
$R_3 - R_2 = (0,1,3) - (0,1,1) = (0,0,2)$.

So the $(3,3)$ entry is 2, not zero. Third pivot exists. But the combination $2v_1 + v_2 - v_3 = 0$ should mean the nullspace is nontrivial. Let me recheck that combination:

$2v_1 = (2,0,4)^T$, $v_2 = (0,1,3)^T$, sum $= (2,1,7)^T$. And $v_3 = (2,1,7)^T$. So $2v_1 + v_2 = v_3$ means $2v_1 + v_2 - v_3 = 0$. So $c = (2,1,-1)^T$ should be in $N(A)$.

Check: $Ac = 1\cdot(2) + 0\cdot(1) + 2\cdot(-1) = 0$. Row 1: $2 + 0 - 2 = 0$. Row 2: $0 + 1 - 1 = 0$. Row 3: $4 + 3 - 7 = 0$. Yes. So $(2,1,-1)^T \in N(A)$.

The error: the echelon form I computed is wrong. Let me redo carefully.

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 2 & 1 & 7 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 2R_1$: $(2-2\cdot1, 1-2\cdot0, 7-2\cdot2) = (0, 1, 3)$.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2$: $(0-0, 1-1, 3-1) = (0, 0, 2)$.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Three pivots. Rank 3. Nullspace $= {0}$. But the arithmetic above shows $(2,1,-1) \in N(A)$. Contradiction.

There is an error in my combination check. Recompute $Ac$ for $c = (2,1,-1)^T$:

Row 1: $1\cdot2 + 0\cdot1 + 2\cdot(-1) = 2 + 0 - 2 = 0$.  
Row 2: $0\cdot2 + 1\cdot1 + 1\cdot(-1) = 0 + 1 - 1 = 0$.  
Row 3: $2\cdot2 + 1\cdot1 + 7\cdot(-1) = 4 + 1 - 7 = -2 \neq 0$.

Row 3 is $-2$, not $0$. So $(2,1,-1)^T \notin N(A)$. My earlier combination check was wrong. $2v_1 + v_2 \neq v_3$: the third component gives $2\cdot2 + 3 = 7 = v_3$'s third component. Wait: $2v_1 + v_2 = 2(1,0,2)^T + (0,1,3)^T = (2,0,4)^T + (0,1,3)^T = (2,1,7)^T = v_3$. That is correct.

The issue is in my row 3 matrix-vector product. The matrix $A$ has rows $(1,0,2)$, $(0,1,1)$, $(2,1,7)$ — these are the rows, not the columns. The columns are $v_1, v_2, v_3$. Row 3 of $Ac$: $2\cdot2 + 1\cdot1 + 7\cdot(-1) = 4 + 1 - 7 = -2$. But I want $A\cdot(2,1,-1)^T$ where the columns of $A$ are $v_1, v_2, v_3$. That means $Ac = 2v_1 + 1\cdot v_2 + (-1)v_3 = (2,1,7)^T - (2,1,7)^T = 0$. The row-by-row computation must give the same answer.

Row 3 of $A$ is $(2,1,7)$ (the third entries of $v_1, v_2, v_3$, i.e., row 3 of $A = [v_1|v_2|v_3]$). So row 3 of $Ac = 2\cdot2 + 1\cdot1 + 7\cdot(-1)$... but $A_{3,1} = 2$ (third entry of $v_1$), $A_{3,2} = 1$ (third entry of $v_2$), $A_{3,3} = 7$ (third entry of $v_3$). Then $(Ac)_3 = 2\cdot2 + 1\cdot1 + 7\cdot(-1) = 4 + 1 - 7 = -2$.

But $2v_1 + v_2 - v_3$ has third component $2\cdot2 + 1\cdot3 + (-1)\cdot7 = 4 + 3 - 7 = 0$.

The discrepancy: row 3 of $Ac$ uses row 3 of the matrix $A$, which is $(A_{3,1}, A_{3,2}, A_{3,3}) = (v_1[3], v_2[3], v_3[3]) = (2, 3, 7)$, not $(2, 1, 7)$. I wrote the matrix $A$ incorrectly above. The correct matrix is:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 2 & 3 & 7 \end{pmatrix} $$

because $v_3 = (2,1,7)^T$ has second entry 1, not matching — wait. $v_1 = (1,0,2)^T$, $v_2 = (0,1,3)^T$, $v_3 = (2,1,7)^T$. So:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 2 & 3 & 7 \end{pmatrix} $$

Row 3 is $(2, 3, 7)$, not $(2, 1, 7)$. I misread $v_2$'s third entry earlier — I wrote $v_2 = (0,1,3)^T$ but then used 1 as its third entry. The third entry of $v_2$ is 3. I will restart with the corrected matrix.

**Corrected reduction:**

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 2 & 3 & 7 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 2R_1$: $(2-2, 3-0, 7-4) = (0, 3, 3)$.

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 3 & 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_2$: $(0, 3-3, 3-3) = (0,0,0)$.

$$ U = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Rank $r = 2$. Column 3 is a free column. The vectors do **not** span $\mathbb{R}^3$. Their span is a 2-dimensional plane — consistent with $v_3 = 2v_1 + v_2$.

This is a useful lesson in its own right: always carry all entries carefully. A single wrong entry produces a completely wrong conclusion about spanning.

## The Spanning Test

The question "do $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ span $\mathbb{R}^m$?" reduces to:

$$ \text{rank}(A) = m $$

where $A$ is the $m \times k$ matrix with $v_i$ as columns.

This requires $k \geq m$ — you need at least $m$ vectors to have any chance of spanning $\mathbb{R}^m$. But having $k \geq m$ vectors is not sufficient; they must have $m$ independent ones among them.

More generally, $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ span a subspace $V$ of dimension $d$ if and only if the column space of $A$ equals $V$, equivalently if $\text{rank}(A) = d$ and $C(A) = V$.

---

## Too Many Vectors: Spanning with Redundancy

Consider four vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$:

$$ v_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v_4 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 3 \end{pmatrix} $$

These four vectors span $\mathbb{R}^2$ — in fact $v_1$ and $v_2$ alone already span $\mathbb{R}^2$, and $v_3, v_4$ are redundant. The matrix $A = [v_1 \mid v_2 \mid v_3 \mid v_4]$ has rank 2 (since $\mathbb{R}^2$ has dimension 2), and the null space has dimension $4 - 2 = 2$, reflecting the two redundancies.

Spanning with more vectors than necessary is fine — it just means the spanning set is not a basis. The dependence relations live in the nullspace.

---

## Too Few Vectors: Cannot Span

If $k < m$, then $k$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^m$ cannot span $\mathbb{R}^m$, because $\text{rank}(A) \leq k < m$. The column space $C(A)$ is a proper subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$ — a lower-dimensional flat through the origin.

One vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$: spans a line.  
Two independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$: span a plane.  
Three independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$: span all of $\mathbb{R}^3$.

The dimension of the span equals the rank of the matrix formed by the vectors.

---

## The Subspace Spanned Is the Column Space

This is the point I want to engrave. Given any set of vectors, their span is the column space of the matrix they form. Every question about spanning is a question about the column space. Every question about the column space is a question about rank.

The column space $C(A)$ is spanned by the columns of $A$. But more usefully, it is spanned by the **pivot columns of $A$** alone — the free columns are linear combinations of the pivot columns and contribute nothing new to the span. The pivot columns form a basis for $C(A)$.

From the corrected example:

$$ U = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Pivot columns: 1 and 2. So $v_1 = (1,0,2)^T$ and $v_2 = (0,1,3)^T$ form a basis for $\text{span}(v_1, v_2, v_3)$. The span is the plane

$$ \left{ a\begin{pmatrix}1\0\2\end{pmatrix} + b\begin{pmatrix}0\1\3\end{pmatrix} : a, b \in \mathbb{R} \right} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^3 $$

Note carefully: the pivot columns of $A$ (the original matrix, not $U$) are the basis. Pivot columns of $U$ are $e_1$ and $e_2$, which span a different plane. The row operations change the columns.

---

## Spanning vs. Independence: The Inverse Pressures

These two properties pull in opposite directions, and recognizing this tension is fundamental:

- **To span $\mathbb{R}^m$:** need rank $r = m$, which requires $k \geq m$ vectors
- **To be independent:** need rank $r = k$, which requires $r \leq n$ and no redundancy

A spanning set can always be **reduced** to a basis by removing redundant vectors (the ones corresponding to free columns).

An independent set can always be **extended** to a basis by adding vectors that cover new directions.

A **basis** is a set that is simultaneously spanning and independent. It is the minimal spanning set and the maximal independent set.
