
## Why I'm Writing This

I spent the first part of this course treating "vector space" as a synonym for $\mathbb{R}^n$. That works for computation, but it obscures what is actually going on. The axioms of a vector space are an abstraction that captures exactly the structure needed for linear algebra to work — and once I see which axioms are load-bearing, the whole theory becomes more transparent. This note is about understanding the definition, not just using it.

---

## The Definition of a Vector Space

A **vector space** over $\mathbb{R}$ is a set $V$ together with two operations:

- **Addition:** $V \times V \to V$, written $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}$
- **Scalar multiplication:** $\mathbb{R} \times V \to V$, written $c\mathbf{v}$

satisfying the following eight axioms for all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in V$ and all $c, d \in \mathbb{R}$:

**(VS1) Commutativity of addition:** $$\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u}$$

**(VS2) Associativity of addition:** $$(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) + \mathbf{w} = \mathbf{u} + (\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w})$$

**(VS3) Additive identity:** There exists $\mathbf{0} \in V$ such that $$\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{v} \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{v} \in V$$

**(VS4) Additive inverse:** For every $\mathbf{v} \in V$, there exists $-\mathbf{v} \in V$ such that $$\mathbf{v} + (-\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{0}$$

**(VS5) Multiplicative identity:** $$1 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}$$

**(VS6) Associativity of scalar multiplication:** $$(cd)\mathbf{v} = c(d\mathbf{v})$$

**(VS7) Distributivity over vector addition:** $$c(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}) = c\mathbf{u} + c\mathbf{v}$$

**(VS8) Distributivity over scalar addition:** $$(c + d)\mathbf{v} = c\mathbf{v} + d\mathbf{v}$$

The elements of $V$ are called **vectors** — but that word now means any object satisfying these eight rules, not necessarily an arrow in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

**Remark (personal).** The first time I read these axioms I thought: this is just saying the obvious things about $\mathbb{R}^n$. But the point is that _other_ sets satisfy these axioms too — sets of functions, sets of matrices, sets of polynomials. The axioms are exactly what you need to do linear combinations. Nothing more, nothing less.

## Examples of Vector Spaces

### $\mathbb{R}^n$

The standard example. Vectors are $n$-tuples of real numbers, addition and scalar multiplication are componentwise. All eight axioms hold by properties of $\mathbb{R}$. The zero vector is $(0, 0, \ldots, 0)$.

Explicit for $\mathbb{R}^3$: the vectors

$$\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 \ u_2 \ u_3 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} v_1 \ v_2 \ v_3 \end{bmatrix}$$

satisfy $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} u_1 + v_1 \ u_2 + v_2 \ u_3 + v_3 \end{bmatrix}$ and $c\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} cu_1 \ cu_2 \ cu_3 \end{bmatrix}$. The axioms are immediate.

### $M_{m \times n}$ — Matrices

The set of all $m \times n$ real matrices is a vector space. Addition is matrix addition (componentwise), scalar multiplication scales every entry. The zero vector is the $m \times n$ zero matrix.

For $2 \times 2$ matrices:

$$\begin{bmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{bmatrix} + \begin{bmatrix} e & f \ g & h \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} a+e & b+f \ c+g & d+h \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \lambda \begin{bmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda a & \lambda b \ \lambda c & \lambda d \end{bmatrix}$$

This is a vector space of dimension $mn$. Concretely, $M_{2\times 2}$ has dimension 4 — four free entries, four basis "matrices."

### $\mathcal{P}_n$ — Polynomials of Degree $\leq n$

The set of all polynomials of degree at most $n$ with real coefficients:

$$\mathcal{P}_n = {a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 + \cdots + a_n x^n : a_i \in \mathbb{R}}$$

Addition: add coefficients of like terms. Scalar multiplication: scale all coefficients. The zero vector is the zero polynomial $p(x) = 0$.

$\mathcal{P}_n$ has dimension $n+1$ with basis ${1, x, x^2, \ldots, x^n}$.

### $C[a,b]$ — Continuous Functions

The set of all continuous functions $f: [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$. Addition: $(f+g)(x) = f(x) + g(x)$. Scalar multiplication: $(cf)(x) = c \cdot f(x)$. Zero vector: the constant function $f(x) = 0$.

This is an _infinite-dimensional_ vector space. No finite set of functions spans it. This is the setting where Fourier analysis lives — see [[Inner Products]].

### A Non-Example: The First Quadrant of $\mathbb{R}^2$

Let $V = {(x, y) : x \geq 0, y \geq 0}$. Addition is fine. But scalar multiplication fails: take $\mathbf{v} = (1, 1) \in V$ and $c = -1$. Then $c\mathbf{v} = (-1, -1) \notin V$.

Axiom (VS4) also fails: the additive inverse of $(1,1)$ is $(-1,-1)$, which is not in $V$.

The first quadrant is closed under addition but not under scalar multiplication. It is not a vector space.

## Subspaces

### Definition

A nonempty subset $W \subseteq V$ is a **subspace** of $V$ if $W$ is itself a vector space under the same addition and scalar multiplication inherited from $V$.

Since $W$ inherits its operations from $V$, most axioms (commutativity, associativity, distributivity) are automatic. The only things that need to be checked are the **closure conditions**:

**Theorem (Subspace Test).** A nonempty subset $W \subseteq V$ is a subspace of $V$ if and only if:

**(S1) Closed under addition:** $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W \Rightarrow \mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} \in W$

**(S2) Closed under scalar multiplication:** $\mathbf{v} \in W, c \in \mathbb{R} \Rightarrow c\mathbf{v} \in W$

_Proof._ If $W$ is a subspace, (S1) and (S2) hold by the vector space axioms applied in $W$. Conversely, suppose (S1) and (S2) hold and $W$ is nonempty. Pick any $\mathbf{v} \in W$. By (S2), $0 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \in W$, so the zero vector is present. By (S2), $(-1)\mathbf{v} = -\mathbf{v} \in W$, so additive inverses exist. The remaining axioms (commutativity, associativity, distributivity) are inherited from $V$. $\square$

**Consequence:** Conditions (S1) and (S2) can be combined into a single condition:

$$\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W \text{ and } c, d \in \mathbb{R} \implies c\mathbf{u} + d\mathbf{v} \in W$$

i.e., $W$ is closed under all **linear combinations**. This is equivalent to (S1) + (S2).

**Every subspace must contain $\mathbf{0}$.** This is the fastest way to rule out a subset. If $\mathbf{0} \notin W$, then $W$ is not a subspace, full stop.

## The Four Fundamental Subspaces

For a matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$, there are four subspaces attached to it. These are the subspaces that actually appear in linear algebra — understanding them is the main goal of the first half of 18.06. I will define them here and develop them further in [[Column Space and Null Space]].

### Column Space $C(A)$

$$C(A) = {, A\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n ,} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$$

The set of all linear combinations of the columns of $A$. It lives in $\mathbb{R}^m$.

**Example.** Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 2 & 1 \ 3 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

The columns of $A$ are $\mathbf{a}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{bmatrix}$ and $\mathbf{a}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$.

$C(A) = \left{ x_1 \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{bmatrix} + x_2 \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} : x_1, x_2 \in \mathbb{R} \right}$

This is a 2-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ — a plane through the origin.

**Why it is a subspace.** Take $\mathbf{b} = A\mathbf{x}$ and $\mathbf{c} = A\mathbf{y}$ in $C(A)$. Then $\mathbf{b} + \mathbf{c} = A\mathbf{x} + A\mathbf{y} = A(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}) \in C(A)$. Similarly $\lambda \mathbf{b} = A(\lambda \mathbf{x}) \in C(A)$. Closed under both operations. $\checkmark$

**Connection to solvability.** $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ has a solution if and only if $\mathbf{b} \in C(A)$. The column space is exactly the set of right-hand sides for which the system is consistent.

### Null Space $N(A)$

$$N(A) = {, \mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n : A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} ,} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$$

The set of all solutions to the homogeneous equation. It lives in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

**Example.** Same $A$ as above.

$A\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 2 & 1 \ 3 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \ 2x_1 + x_2 \ 3x_1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$

From row 1: $x_1 = 0$. From row 3: $x_1 = 0$ (consistent). From row 2: $2(0) + x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_2 = 0$.

So $N(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$. The only solution to $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ is the zero vector — $A$ has trivial null space, meaning $A$ has full column rank ($r = n = 2$).

**Why it is a subspace.** If $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ and $A\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0}$, then $A(\mathbf{x} + \mathbf{y}) = A\mathbf{x} + A\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0} + \mathbf{0} = \mathbf{0}$. Similarly $A(c\mathbf{x}) = cA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$. $\checkmark$

**Remark (personal).** The null space lives in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (the domain), while the column space lives in $\mathbb{R}^m$ (the codomain). They are subspaces of _different_ spaces. This caused me genuine confusion early on — I kept thinking they should be comparable objects somehow. They are not. The rank-nullity theorem relates their _dimensions_, but the spaces themselves live in completely different ambient spaces.

### Row Space $C(A^T)$

$$C(A^T) = {, A^T \mathbf{y} : \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^m ,} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$$

The column space of $A^T$, equivalently the span of the rows of $A$. Lives in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

### Left Null Space $N(A^T)$

$$N(A^T) = {, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^m : A^T \mathbf{y} = \mathbf{0} ,} \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$$

The null space of $A^T$. Lives in $\mathbb{R}^m$.

## The Fundamental Theorem of Linear Algebra (Part 1)

If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ has rank $r$, then:

$$\dim C(A) = r$$ $$\dim N(A) = n - r$$ $$\dim C(A^T) = r$$ $$\dim N(A^T) = m - r$$

These four numbers must add up correctly: $r + (n-r) = n$ and $r + (m-r) = m$.

**The rank-nullity theorem** is the $n$-side: $\dim C(A) + \dim N(A) = n$.

The deeper Part 2 of the fundamental theorem — which says $C(A)$ and $N(A^T)$ are orthogonal complements in $\mathbb{R}^m$, and $C(A^T)$ and $N(A)$ are orthogonal complements in $\mathbb{R}^n$ — requires inner products and is developed in [[Projections and Orthogonality]].

## Subspace Arithmetic

Two operations on subspaces produce new subspaces:

**Intersection:** If $W_1$ and $W_2$ are subspaces of $V$, then $W_1 \cap W_2$ is a subspace.

_Proof._ If $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W_1 \cap W_2$, then $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W_1$ and $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in W_2$. By closure in each, $c\mathbf{u} + d\mathbf{v} \in W_1$ and $c\mathbf{u} + d\mathbf{v} \in W_2$, so $c\mathbf{u} + d\mathbf{v} \in W_1 \cap W_2$. $\square$

**Sum:** The **sum** of two subspaces is defined as

$$W_1 + W_2 = {, \mathbf{w}_1 + \mathbf{w}_2 : \mathbf{w}_1 \in W_1, \mathbf{w}_2 \in W_2 ,}$$

This is also a subspace (the smallest subspace containing both $W_1$ and $W_2$).

**Union is not a subspace** in general. $W_1 \cup W_2$ fails closure under addition unless one contains the other. Standard example: in $\mathbb{R}^2$, let $W_1$ be the $x$-axis and $W_2$ be the $y$-axis. Both are subspaces. But $(1, 0) \in W_1$ and $(0, 1) \in W_2$, while $(1,0) + (0,1) = (1,1) \notin W_1 \cup W_2$.

## Every Subspace Must Pass Through the Origin

This deserves emphasis. Let $W$ be a subspace and pick any $\mathbf{v} \in W$. By (S2), $0 \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \in W$.

**Consequence:** Lines, planes, and hyperplanes that do not pass through the origin are _not_ subspaces.

In $\mathbb{R}^2$: the line $x + y = 1$ is not a subspace — it does not contain $(0,0)$. The line $x + y = 0$ is a subspace — it is the null space of the matrix $\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

In $\mathbb{R}^3$: the plane $2x - y + 3z = 0$ is a subspace (null space of $\begin{bmatrix} 2 & -1 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$). The plane $2x - y + 3z = 5$ is not — it misses the origin, and the sum of two points on it lies on $2x - y + 3z = 10$, not on the original plane.

## Subspaces of $\mathbb{R}^3$ — A Complete Classification

Every subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ is one of exactly four types:

**Dimension 0:** ${\mathbf{0}}$. The trivial subspace.

**Dimension 1:** Any line through the origin. Parametrically, ${t\mathbf{v} : t \in \mathbb{R}}$ for some nonzero $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$.

**Dimension 2:** Any plane through the origin. The set of solutions to $ax + by + cz = 0$ for some nonzero $(a,b,c)$. This is the null space of the $1 \times 3$ matrix $\begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \end{bmatrix}$.

**Dimension 3:** All of $\mathbb{R}^3$ itself.

Nothing else exists. A subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ cannot have dimension $1.5$ or $2.7$ — dimension is always a non-negative integer, and it is bounded above by $3$.

This classification is worth internalizing. When I stare at a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$, I should immediately ask: what is its dimension? That single number tells me its geometric type.

## Span as the Smallest Subspace

Given vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k \in V$, the **span** is

$$\text{span}(\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k) = \left{ \sum_{i=1}^k c_i \mathbf{v}_i : c_i \in \mathbb{R} \right}$$

**Theorem.** $\text{span}(\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k)$ is a subspace of $V$.

_Proof._ Let $\mathbf{u} = \sum c_i \mathbf{v}_i$ and $\mathbf{w} = \sum d_i \mathbf{v}_i$ be two elements of the span. Then $\alpha\mathbf{u} + \beta\mathbf{w} = \sum (\alpha c_i + \beta d_i)\mathbf{v}_i$ is again in the span. $\square$

Moreover, $\text{span}(\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k)$ is the _smallest_ subspace containing all $\mathbf{v}_i$: any subspace that contains all $\mathbf{v}_i$ must contain all their linear combinations, hence must contain the span.

**Connection to column space.** $C(A) = \text{span}(\mathbf{a}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{a}_n)$ where $\mathbf{a}_j$ are the columns of $A$. The column space is the span of the columns.
