
## The Central Theorem

If $Ax = b$ is consistent, then every solution has the form

$$ x = x_p + x_n $$

where $x_p$ is _any one particular solution_ and $x_n$ is _any element of the nullspace_ $N(A)$.

This is worth pausing on. The solution set is not a subspace — it does not contain the zero vector (unless $b = 0$). It is an _affine subspace_: a translate of $N(A)$ by $x_p$. The nullspace gives the shape; the particular solution gives the location.

**Proof (the clean version):**  
Suppose $Ax_p = b$ and $Ax_n = 0$. Then $A(x_p + x_n) = Ax_p + Ax_n = b + 0 = b$. So $x_p + x_n$ is a solution.  
Conversely, if $Ax = b$ and $Ax_p = b$, then $A(x - x_p) = 0$, so $x - x_p \in N(A)$, meaning $x = x_p + (x - x_p)$ with $x - x_p \in N(A)$.  
These two directions together say: solution set $= {x_p + x_n : x_n \in N(A)}$. $\blacksquare$

## The Full Worked Example

I want to see this concretely. Take

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \ b_2 \ b_3 \end{pmatrix} $$

I will first find conditions on $b$ for consistency, then find $x_p$, then write the complete solution.

### Step 1: Augmented Matrix and Row Reduction

Form $[A \mid b]$ and reduce:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 & b_1 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 & b_2 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 & b_3 \end{array}\right) $$

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_1$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 & b_1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 & b_2 - 2b_1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 & b_3 - 3b_1 \end{array}\right) $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 & b_1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 & b_2 - 2b_1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & b_3 - b_2 - b_1 \end{array}\right) $$

### Step 2: Consistency Condition

The last row reads $0 = b_3 - b_2 - b_1$. This must hold for $Ax = b$ to have any solution at all. The condition is:

$$ b_1 + b_2 - b_3 = 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad b_3 = b_1 + b_2 $$

Geometric reading: $b$ must lie in the column space $C(A)$. Row 3 of $A$ was a linear combination of rows 1 and 2 ($R_3 = R_1 + R_2$ up to coefficients — actually $R_3 = R_1 + R_2$ exactly for our $A$: $(3,6,8,10) = (1,2,2,2) + (2,4,6,8)$). Whenever rows of $A$ are dependent, the _same combination_ of entries of $b$ must also vanish.

For the rest of the computation, take a specific $b$ satisfying this: let $b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 5 \ 6 \end{pmatrix}$. Check: $1 + 5 = 6$. Good.

### Step 3: Reduced Row Echelon Form of the Augmented Matrix

Continue from the echelon form with $b_1 = 1$, $b_2 = 5$, $b_3 = 6$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 & 3 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) $$

Divide $R_2$ by 2:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3/2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) $$

$R_1 \leftarrow R_1 - 2R_2$:

$$ \left(\begin{array}{cccc|c} 1 & 2 & 0 & -2 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 & 3/2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{array}\right) $$

This is the rref of $[A \mid b]$. Read the system:

$$ x_1 + 2x_2 - 2x_4 = -2 $$ $$ x_3 + 2x_4 = \tfrac{3}{2} $$

Pivot variables: $x_1, x_3$. Free variables: $x_2, x_4$.

### Step 4: The Particular Solution

The cleanest particular solution: set all free variables to zero.

$x_2 = 0$, $x_4 = 0$:

$$ x_1 = -2, \quad x_3 = \tfrac{3}{2} $$

$$ x_p = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 0 \ 3/2 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Verify:

$$ Ax_p = \begin{pmatrix} 1(−2)+2(0)+2(3/2)+2(0) \ 2(−2)+4(0)+6(3/2)+8(0) \ 3(−2)+6(0)+8(3/2)+10(0) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2+3 \ -4+9 \ -6+12 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 5 \ 6 \end{pmatrix} = b \checkmark $$

### Step 5: The Nullspace (from the Previous Note)

The special solutions of $Ax = 0$ were computed in [[Pivot Columns and Special Solutions]]:

$$ s_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad s_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

### Step 6: The Complete Solution

$$ \boxed{x = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 0 \ 3/2 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} + c_1 \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} + c_2 \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad c_1, c_2 \in \mathbb{R}} $$

This is a 2-dimensional affine subspace (a plane) inside $\mathbb{R}^4$, passing through $x_p$ and parallel to $N(A)$.

## The Three Possible Solution Counts

Given $A$ is $m \times n$ with rank $r$:

|Condition|Solution count|Geometric picture|
|---|---|---|
|$r < m$ and $b \notin C(A)$|0|The plane $C(A)$ misses $b$|
|$r = m$ (full row rank)|always at least 1|$C(A) = \mathbb{R}^m$, so $b$ always in $C(A)$|
|$r = n$ (full column rank)|at most 1|$N(A) = {0}$, so $x_p$ is unique if it exists|
|$r = m = n$ (square, invertible)|exactly 1|$x = A^{-1}b$|

The case $r < n$ always gives infinitely many solutions (when consistent), because $\dim N(A) = n - r \geq 1$.

These four cases are not arbitrary — they are the skeleton of the whole theory. Everything in Chapters 3 and 4 of Strang is a refinement of this table.

## Why Setting Free Variables to Zero Gives $x_p$

This is the thing that confused me at first. When I set $x_2 = x_4 = 0$, I am not doing anything special geometrically — I am just picking _one_ representative from the affine solution set. The point $x_p$ lives in a specific coset of $N(A)$. Any other choice of free variables would shift $x_p$ by a nullspace vector, landing on a different point in the same coset.

The choice $x_{\text{free}} = 0$ is convenient because it makes back-substitution mechanical: only the pivot variables appear, and they are read off directly from the right-hand side of the rref system.

## The Failure of Consistency: What the Row Operations Tell You

When I formed $[A \mid b]$ and reduced, the third row became

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 & b_3 - b_2 - b_1 \end{pmatrix} $$

If $b_3 - b_2 - b_1 \neq 0$, this row reads $0 = \text{nonzero}$, which is impossible. The system is inconsistent.

The row operation that produced this row was: start with $R_3$, subtract $R_2$, subtract $R_1$. So the combination $R_3 - R_2 - R_1$ kills every row of $A$ (producing the zero row), but the _same combination applied to $b$_ does not give zero. This is exactly the statement that $b$ has a nonzero component in the _left nullspace_ of $A$ — the space of row combinations that annihilate $A$.

This is a preview of the left nullspace $N(A^T)$ and why it is orthogonal to $C(A)$: $b \in C(A)$ if and only if $y^T b = 0$ for every $y$ in $N(A^T)$.

## A Cleaner Way to State the Complete Solution

Let me write this in a notation I find cleaner than Strang's for my own use:

The solution set of $Ax = b$ (when nonempty) is

$$ x_p + N(A) := {x_p + v : v \in N(A)} $$

This is a _coset_ of $N(A)$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Different choices of $x_p$ (there are infinitely many when $N(A) \neq {0}$) give the same coset. There is no "canonical" particular solution except by a choice of convention (e.g., minimum-norm $x_p$, which requires least squares and the pseudoinverse — come back to this later).
