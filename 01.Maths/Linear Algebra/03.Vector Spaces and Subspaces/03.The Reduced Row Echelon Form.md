
## Why I'm Writing This

Row reduction is the computational engine behind almost everything in the first half of 18.06. I know how to do it mechanically. What I did not understand clearly until recently is what the rref _is_ as a mathematical object — why it is unique, what information it compresses, and why going all the way to rref (rather than stopping at row echelon form) is the right canonical form for reading off the null space and the rank. This note is about understanding rref as a theorem, not just as an algorithm.

---

## The Three Forms: Triangular, Echelon, Reduced

There is a hierarchy of how far you can simplify a matrix by row operations.

**Row echelon form (ref):** The result of forward elimination only. The shape is staircase-upper-triangular:

- All zero rows at the bottom.
- In each nonzero row, the first nonzero entry (the **leading entry** or **pivot**) is strictly to the right of the pivot in the row above.

The pivots need not be $1$, and there may be nonzero entries above the pivots.

**Reduced row echelon form (rref):** Row echelon form, plus:

- Every pivot equals $1$.
- Every entry in a pivot column is $0$ except the pivot itself — both above and below.

The rref goes further: it eliminates upward as well as downward, and scales pivots to $1$. This extra work is what produces a unique canonical form.

**Example: the same matrix at each stage.**

Starting matrix:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 & 4 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 10 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 15 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$$

After forward elimination (ref):

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 10 & 8 \ 0 & 2 & 4 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

(I swapped rows 1 and 2 first since $(1,1)$ is zero.) The pivots are $2$ and $2$, not $1$. Entries above the second pivot have not been cleared.

After rref (scale pivots to $1$, clear above):

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 & 3 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

I will derive this step by step below.

## Row Operations and Row Equivalence

Three **elementary row operations** are allowed:

**(R1) Row scaling:** Multiply a row by a nonzero scalar $c \neq 0$.

**(R2) Row replacement:** Replace row $i$ by row $i$ plus $c$ times row $j$ (for $i \neq j$, any $c$).

**(R3) Row swap:** Exchange rows $i$ and $j$.

Each operation is invertible: scaling by $c$ is undone by scaling by $1/c$; row replacement by $c$ is undone by replacement by $-c$; a swap undoes itself. So row operations never change the solution set of $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ — they are equivalences.

**Definition.** Two matrices are **row equivalent** if one can be obtained from the other by a finite sequence of elementary row operations.

Row equivalence is an equivalence relation (reflexive, symmetric, transitive). The rref is the canonical representative of each equivalence class.

## Deriving the rref Step by Step

Return to the example. Starting matrix:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 & 4 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 10 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 15 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Swap rows 1 and 2** (column 1, rows 1 and 3, have entries $0, 2, 3$ — pick the nonzero entry in row 2):

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 10 & 8 \ 0 & 2 & 4 & 2 \ 3 & 6 & 15 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Eliminate column 1 below pivot.** Multiplier $\ell_{31} = 3/2$.

Row 3 $\leftarrow$ Row 3 $- \frac{3}{2} \cdot$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 & 15 & 12 \end{bmatrix} - \frac{3}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 10 & 8 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 10 & 8 \ 0 & 2 & 4 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Column 2 has a nonzero entry in row 2.** Pivot is $2$. Nothing to eliminate below (row 3 is zero). This is ref.

**Scale row 1 by $1/2$:**

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 5 & 4 \ 0 & 2 & 4 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Scale row 2 by $1/2$:**

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 5 & 4 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Eliminate above pivot in column 2.** Row 1 $\leftarrow$ Row 1 $- 2 \cdot$ Row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 5 & 4 \end{bmatrix} - 2\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is the rref. (I get slightly different entries here than my initial claim — let me recheck from the start more carefully.)

Actually let me redo the original elimination cleanly.

Row 3 $\leftarrow$ Row 3 $- \frac{3}{2}$ Row 1:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 3 & 6 & 15 & 12 \end{bmatrix} - \frac{3}{2}\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 4 & 10 & 8 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3-3 & 6-6 & 15-15 & 12-12 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Good, row 3 is zero throughout. Scale rows: row 1 by $1/2$, row 2 by $1/2$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 & 5 & 4 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Upward elimination in column 2: Row 1 $\leftarrow$ Row 1 $- 2 \cdot$ Row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1-0 & 2-2 & 5-4 & 4-2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$\boxed{R = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}}$$

Rank $r = 2$. Pivot columns: 1 and 2. Free columns: 3 and 4. Free variables: $x_3, x_4$.

## Reading Information Directly from the rref

The rref is a data-compression device. Here is everything it tells you at a glance.

### The Rank

$r = $ number of pivot rows = number of nonzero rows in $R$. In the example: $r = 2$.

### Pivot and Free Columns

Pivot columns are the columns containing leading $1$'s. Free columns are all others.

In the example:

- Pivot columns: 1, 2
- Free columns: 3, 4
- Pivot variables: $x_1, x_2$
- Free variables: $x_3, x_4$

### The Null Space

From the rref rows (nonzero rows only):

$$x_1 + x_3 + 2x_4 = 0 \implies x_1 = -x_3 - 2x_4$$ $$x_2 + 2x_3 + x_4 = 0 \implies x_2 = -2x_3 - x_4$$

Special solution 1: set $(x_3, x_4) = (1, 0)$:

$$\mathbf{s}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \ -2 \ 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Special solution 2: set $(x_3, x_4) = (0, 1)$:

$$\mathbf{s}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -2 \ -1 \ 0 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$N(A) = \text{span}!\left(\begin{bmatrix} -1 \ -2 \ 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix},\ \begin{bmatrix} -2 \ -1 \ 0 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}\right)$$

Dimension $= n - r = 4 - 2 = 2$. ✓

### The Column Space (Which $\mathbf{b}$ Make $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ Consistent)

The pivot columns of $A$ (columns 1 and 2 of the _original_ $A$, not of $R$) form a basis for $C(A)$. From $A$:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 2 & 4 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 10 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 15 & 12 \end{bmatrix}$$

Pivot columns of $A$ are columns 1 and 2:

$$\mathbf{a}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 2 \ 3 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{a}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 2 \ 4 \ 6 \end{bmatrix}$$

$C(A) = \text{span}(\mathbf{a}_1, \mathbf{a}_2)$, a 2-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$.

**Warning.** The pivot columns of $R$ are the standard basis vectors $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2$ — they are not the same as the pivot columns of $A$. The rref tells you _which_ columns of $A$ are pivot columns, but the actual basis vectors for $C(A)$ must be taken from $A$ itself, not from $R$.

This is a point I got wrong more than once. Row operations change the column space. They preserve the row space and the null space, but not the column space.

## Uniqueness of the rref

**Theorem.** Every matrix has a unique reduced row echelon form.

I will not give the full proof (it requires induction on the number of pivot columns and careful case analysis), but the key idea is this: the pivot positions are determined by the matrix — specifically, the $k$-th pivot position is the leftmost column of $A$ that is not in the span of the previous pivot columns. Once pivot positions are fixed, the rref entries are completely forced by the requirements that:

- Each pivot is $1$.
- Each pivot column has zeros everywhere else.

Since there is no choice at any step, the result is unique.

**Consequence.** Two matrices have the same rref if and only if they are row equivalent. The rref is the canonical representative of a row equivalence class.

**Contrast with $LU$.** The $LU$ factorization is not unique in this sense — different orderings of row operations (with partial pivoting choices) give different $L$ and $U$ factors, and $PA = LU$ depends on the chosen permutation. The rref, by contrast, is a property of the matrix itself, not of the algorithm used to compute it.

## The rref and Row Operations as Matrix Multiplication

Every elementary row operation is left-multiplication by an elementary matrix.

**(R1) Scale row $i$ by $c$:** Left-multiply by the matrix $E$ which is $I$ except with $c$ in position $(i,i)$.

**(R2) Add $c$ times row $j$ to row $i$:** Left-multiply by the matrix $E$ which is $I$ except with $c$ in position $(i,j)$.

**(R3) Swap rows $i$ and $j$:** Left-multiply by the corresponding permutation matrix $P_{ij}$.

Since each elementary matrix is invertible, the entire sequence of row operations corresponds to left-multiplication by an invertible matrix. Specifically:

$$R = E_k E_{k-1} \cdots E_2 E_1 A$$

for some sequence of elementary matrices $E_1, \ldots, E_k$. Setting $M = E_k \cdots E_1$ (invertible), we have

$$R = MA$$

This means $R$ and $A$ are related by an invertible left factor — they have the same row space and null space, but generally different column spaces.

**Connecting rref to $PA = LU$.** Forward elimination corresponds to the $E_i$'s that do row replacements and swaps, producing $U$. Going further to rref corresponds to additionally doing upward elimination (more row replacements) and row scaling. The rref is $LU$ taken to completion.

## The $\begin{bmatrix} I & F \end{bmatrix}$ Block Structure

When the matrix has full row rank ($r = m$) and the pivot columns are the first $r$ columns (or after reordering columns so they are), the rref has the clean block form:

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & F \end{bmatrix}$$

where $I_r$ is $r \times r$ identity and $F$ is $r \times (n - r)$.

For a general $m \times n$ matrix with rank $r$:

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} I_r & F \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

The $F$ block encodes all the information about the free columns — how they are expressed as linear combinations of the pivot columns (after row reduction). The null space matrix is $N = \begin{bmatrix} -F \ I_{n-r} \end{bmatrix}$, as I developed in [[Null Space of A — Solving Ax=0]].

In the worked example:

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Here $r = 2$, and in block form (pivot columns 1, 2; free columns 3, 4):

$$I_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad F = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

The null space matrix:

$$N = \begin{bmatrix} -F \ I_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & -2 \ -2 & -1 \ 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Columns are $\mathbf{s}_1$ and $\mathbf{s}_2$ from above. ✓

## What Row Reduction Preserves and What It Destroys

This deserves an explicit table, because getting it wrong is responsible for most errors at this stage.

**Preserved by row operations:**

- The **null space** $N(A)$. Since $R = MA$ for invertible $M$, $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} \iff MA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} \iff R\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$.
- The **row space** $C(A^T)$. Row operations replace rows by linear combinations of rows — the span of the rows does not change.
- The **rank** $r$. Both row and column rank equal $r$ and are invariant under row operations.
- Linear (in)dependence relations among the **columns**. If columns $j_1, \ldots, j_k$ of $A$ are linearly dependent, the same columns of $R$ are linearly dependent with the same coefficients.

**Destroyed by row operations:**

- The **column space** $C(A)$. The columns of $R$ are generally different vectors than the columns of $A$.
- The **eigenvalues** (for square matrices). Row operations have no reason to preserve $\det(\lambda I - A)$.
- The **left null space** $N(A^T)$. Since rows change, $A^T$ changes, and its null space changes.

**Remark (personal).** The fact that the null space is preserved is what makes rref work for solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ — solving $R\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ is the same problem. The fact that the column space is destroyed is why I must take basis vectors for $C(A)$ from the original $A$, not from $R$. Both facts follow immediately from $R = MA$.

## rref of a Square Invertible Matrix

If $A$ is $n \times n$ and invertible (rank $r = n$), then:

- All $n$ columns are pivot columns.
- No free variables.
- The rref is exactly $I_n$.

$$R = I_n$$

_Proof._ An invertible matrix has $n$ linearly independent columns, so all $n$ columns become pivot columns after reduction. With $r = n$ pivots and the rref conditions (each pivot is $1$, each pivot column cleared), the only possibility is $I_n$. $\square$

**Consequence: the matrix inverse via rref.** The augmented system $[A \mid I]$ reduced to rref gives $[I \mid A^{-1}]$. This is the Gauss-Jordan method for computing $A^{-1}$: row-reduce $A$ to $I$, applying the same operations to $I$, and $I$ becomes $A^{-1}$.

_Why it works._ The row operations applied to $[A \mid I]$ correspond to left-multiplication by $M = E_k \cdots E_1$. On the left block: $MA = I$, so $M = A^{-1}$. On the right block: $MI = M = A^{-1}$. So the right block of the reduced augmented matrix is $A^{-1}$.

## A Note on Computation

In exact arithmetic (rational or symbolic), computing the rref is straightforward. In floating-point arithmetic, the situation is more delicate:

- Small pivots cause large multipliers and amplify rounding errors.
- The rref is theoretically unique but numerically unstable — small perturbations in $A$ can change which columns are pivot columns (if a small pivot is barely nonzero vs. zero, numerical errors determine the answer).
- For numerical computation, $PA = LU$ with partial pivoting is preferred over rref. The rref is a theoretical tool; for actual computation with floating-point matrices, one uses rank-revealing $QR$ decomposition or the SVD.

This does not diminish the theoretical importance of rref — it just means I should know when I am doing algebra and when I am doing numerics.

## Summary

The rref of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ is the unique matrix $R$ row equivalent to $A$ satisfying:

- Zero rows at the bottom.
- Each nonzero row's first entry is $1$ (the pivot).
- Pivots strictly step right as rows go down.
- Each pivot column has zeros everywhere except the pivot.

From $R$ I read directly:

- $r = $ rank = number of pivot rows.
- Pivot and free columns, pivot and free variables.
- Basis for $N(A)$: one special solution per free variable.
- Which columns of $A$ form a basis for $C(A)$: the pivot columns (taken from $A$, not from $R$).

The rref is $R = MA$ for some invertible $M$. It preserves $N(A)$ and $C(A^T)$. It destroys $C(A)$.
