## The Definition, Stated Carefully

Vectors $v_1, v_2, \ldots, v_k \in \mathbb{R}^n$ are **linearly independent** if the only solution to

$$ c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 + \cdots + c_k v_k = 0 $$

is $c_1 = c_2 = \cdots = c_k = 0$.

They are **linearly dependent** if there exist scalars $c_1, \ldots, c_k$, _not all zero_, such that the same equation holds.

The definition is an equation. That is the first thing to absorb. Independence is not a geometric intuition — it is a statement about the nullspace of a matrix.

---

## Rewriting as a Nullspace Problem

Form the matrix $A$ whose columns are $v_1, \ldots, v_k$:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \ v_1 & v_2 & \cdots & v_k \ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix} $$

Then the equation $c_1 v_1 + \cdots + c_k v_k = 0$ is exactly $Ac = 0$ where $c = (c_1, \ldots, c_k)^T$.

So:

- $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ are **linearly independent** $\iff$ $N(A) = {0}$ $\iff$ $\text{rank}(A) = k$
- $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ are **linearly dependent** $\iff$ $N(A) \neq {0}$ $\iff$ $\text{rank}(A) < k$

This is why the pivot column story from [[Pivot Columns and Special Solutions]] is directly relevant here. The pivot columns of any matrix are always linearly independent — they are precisely the columns that do not end up in the nullspace construction.

---

## A Worked Example: Dependent Vectors

Take three vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$:

$$ v_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 4 \ 6 \end{pmatrix}, \quad v_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Form $A = [v_1 \mid v_2 \mid v_3]$ and row reduce:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 2 & 4 & 0 \ 3 & 6 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_1$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & -2 \end{pmatrix} $$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Rank is 2, not 3. Column 2 is a free column. So $v_1, v_2, v_3$ are linearly **dependent**.

The dependence relation comes from the nullspace. From the echelon form, with $x_2$ free:

$-2x_3 = 0 \Rightarrow x_3 = 0$.  
$x_1 + 2x_2 + x_3 = 0 \Rightarrow x_1 = -2x_2$.

Set $x_2 = 1$: the special solution is $c = (-2, 1, 0)^T$.

So the dependence relation is: $-2v_1 + v_2 + 0 \cdot v_3 = 0$, i.e., $v_2 = 2v_1$.

Indeed: $v_2 = (2,4,6)^T = 2(1,2,3)^T = 2v_1$. The row reduction found it automatically.

## A Worked Example: Independent Vectors

Take:

$$ w_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad w_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad w_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Form $A = [w_1 \mid w_2 \mid w_3]$ and reduce:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

This is already upper triangular with three nonzero diagonal entries. Three pivots, rank 3. The nullspace is ${0}$. The vectors are linearly **independent**.

No row operation needed. The triangular structure makes independence obvious — this is worth noticing. Triangular matrices with nonzero diagonal are always invertible, always full rank, always have independent columns. Upper triangular with nonzero diagonal is a fast independence check.

---

## The Critical Counting Constraint

If $v_1, \ldots, v_k \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $k > n$, then the vectors are **always dependent**, no exceptions.

The proof is immediate from the matrix picture. $A$ is $n \times k$. Its rank satisfies $\text{rank}(A) \leq n < k$. So $\text{rank}(A) < k$, meaning the nullspace is nontrivial, meaning dependence.

You cannot have more than $n$ independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$. This is one of those facts that sounds obvious once stated but is the key to everything: it is why bases have a fixed size, why dimension is well-defined, why you cannot cram more independent directions into a space than the space has room for.

The contrapositive is equally important: if you have $k$ independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, then $k \leq n$.

---

## Two Vectors: The Geometric Special Case

Two vectors $v_1, v_2$ are dependent if and only if one is a scalar multiple of the other — they point in the same (or opposite) direction.

For two vectors the condition $c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 = 0$ with $(c_1, c_2) \neq (0,0)$ means $v_1 = -\frac{c_2}{c_1} v_2$ (assuming $c_1 \neq 0$). Geometrically: they are collinear through the origin.

This is the only case where independence has a clean picture. For three or more vectors, geometric intuition becomes unreliable — you need the algebra.

---

## What Dependence Says About the Matrix

If $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ are the columns of $A$ and they are dependent, then:

- $\text{rank}(A) < k$
- $A$ has a nontrivial nullspace
- If $A$ is square ($k = n$), then $A$ is **singular**: not invertible, $\det(A) = 0$, the system $Ax = b$ either has no solution or infinitely many

The singular case is worth pausing on. A square matrix is invertible if and only if its columns are linearly independent. This is the algebraic content of invertibility. The determinant being nonzero is just another way of stating the same fact — but the independence framing is more fundamental, because it works for non-square matrices too.

---

## Dependence Among Rows

Everything above was stated for columns. But row independence is equally valid and symmetric.

$v_1, \ldots, v_k$ as **rows** of $A$ are independent $\iff$ $\text{rank}(A) = k \iff$ the row space has dimension $k$.

For a square matrix, row independence and column independence are equivalent — both reduce to $\det(A) \neq 0$.

For a non-square $m \times n$ matrix with $m \neq n$, they diverge. You can have independent columns with dependent rows and vice versa. The rank handles both simultaneously: $\text{rank}(A)$ is simultaneously the dimension of the row space and the dimension of the column space.

---

## The Span-Independence Duality

These two concepts are always in tension:

- **Span:** adding more vectors gives more coverage — more vectors, larger or equal span
- **Independence:** adding more vectors risks creating redundancy

A set of vectors achieves both simultaneously when it is a **basis** for its span: large enough to cover everything in the span, small enough that no vector is redundant.

Formally: $v_1, \ldots, v_k$ are a basis for a subspace $V$ if:

1. They span $V$: every element of $V$ is a combination of $v_1, \ldots, v_k$
2. They are independent: no vector is redundant

The number $k$ is then the **dimension** of $V$. This is well-defined because any two bases of $V$ have the same number of elements — a fact that needs proof (see [[Dimension and Basis]]).

---

## Testing Independence in Practice

The mechanical test is always the same: form the matrix, row reduce, count pivots.

- Count of pivots $=$ count of independent columns among those in the matrix
- If pivots $=$ number of columns: independent
- If pivots $<$ number of columns: dependent, and each free variable gives one dependence relation

There is no shortcut that avoids this (except for special structures like triangular matrices or orthogonal matrices). Geometric intuition is fine for two or three vectors in low dimensions; for anything else, reduce.

---

## A Subtlety Worth Recording

The zero vector is always dependent with any other set of vectors. If $v_k = 0$, then $c_k v_k = 0$ for any $c_k$, so take $c_k = 1$ and all other $c_i = 0$: that gives a nontrivial combination equaling zero.

Equivalently, a matrix with a zero column always has a nontrivial nullspace.

So: no basis ever contains the zero vector. Any set containing $0$ is automatically dependent.
