
## The Setup

Every $m \times n$ matrix $A$ of rank $r$ gives rise to four subspaces. Two live in $\mathbb{R}^n$ (the input space), two live in $\mathbb{R}^m$ (the output space). They come in two orthogonal pairs. Their dimensions add up correctly in each ambient space. None of this is accidental — it is the complete picture of what $A$ does as a linear map.

The four subspaces:

|Subspace|Notation|Definition|Lives in|
|---|---|---|---|
|Column space|$C(A)$|All vectors $Ax$, $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$|$\mathbb{R}^m$|
|Nullspace|$N(A)$|All $x$ with $Ax = 0$|$\mathbb{R}^n$|
|Row space|$C(A^T)$|All vectors $A^T y$, $y \in \mathbb{R}^m$|$\mathbb{R}^n$|
|Left nullspace|$N(A^T)$|All $y$ with $A^T y = 0$|$\mathbb{R}^m$|

The names "column space" and "row space" are self-explanatory. The "left nullspace" earns its name because $A^T y = 0$ can be written $y^T A = 0^T$ — the vector $y$ multiplies $A$ on the left.

---

## The Four Dimensions

For an $m \times n$ matrix of rank $r$:

$$ \dim C(A) = r $$ $$ \dim N(A) = n - r $$ $$ \dim C(A^T) = r $$ $$ \dim N(A^T) = m - r $$

Two dimension equations, one for each ambient space:

$$ \underbrace{r}_{\dim C(A)} + \underbrace{(m-r)}_{\dim N(A^T)} = m \qquad \text{(dimensions in } \mathbb{R}^m \text{)} $$

$$ \underbrace{r}_{\dim C(A^T)} + \underbrace{(n-r)}_{\dim N(A)} = n \qquad \text{(dimensions in } \mathbb{R}^n \text{)} $$

The column space and left nullspace together fill $\mathbb{R}^m$. The row space and nullspace together fill $\mathbb{R}^n$. And the pairs are not merely complementary in dimension — they are orthogonal complements. But I am recording the orthogonality separately in [[Orthogonality and the Fundamental Subspaces]].

The non-obvious fact here is $\dim C(A) = \dim C(A^T) = r$. The column space lives in $\mathbb{R}^m$ and the row space lives in $\mathbb{R}^n$, and when $m \neq n$ these are genuinely different ambient spaces. Yet they have the same dimension. This equality — row rank equals column rank — is a theorem, not a definition, and the proof goes through the rref.

---

## Why Row Rank Equals Column Rank

**Claim:** The number of independent rows of $A$ equals the number of independent columns of $A$.

**Proof via rref:** Row reduce $A$ to its rref $R$. The nonzero rows of $R$ are independent (each has a leading 1 in a column where all others have 0), and they span the same row space as $A$ (row operations preserve the row space). So $\dim C(A^T) = $ number of nonzero rows of $R$ $= r$.

The pivot columns of $A$ are independent (by definition of pivots — no pivot column is a combination of earlier pivot columns) and span $C(A)$. So $\dim C(A) = $ number of pivot columns $= r$.

Both equal the number of pivots. Hence $\dim C(A) = \dim C(A^T)$. $\blacksquare$

The proof is elementary but the conclusion is striking. Transpose a matrix: the rows become columns, the columns become rows, $m$ and $n$ swap. Yet the rank does not change:

$$ \text{rank}(A^T) = \text{rank}(A) $$

## A Full Worked Example

Let

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{pmatrix} $$

This is the matrix from [[Pivot Columns and Special Solutions]]. The rref is

$$ R = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Here $m = 3$, $n = 4$, $r = 2$.

---

### Subspace 1: The Column Space $C(A)$

**Dimension:** $r = 2$.

**Basis:** The pivot columns of $A$ — columns 1 and 3:

$$ \left{ \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, ; \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 6 \ 8 \end{pmatrix} \right} $$

**What it is geometrically:** A 2-dimensional plane through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

**What it means for $Ax = b$:** The system $Ax = b$ is consistent if and only if $b \in C(A)$, i.e., $b$ lies on this plane. Since $C(A)$ is a proper subspace of $\mathbb{R}^3$ (dimension 2, not 3), most vectors $b \in \mathbb{R}^3$ make $Ax = b$ inconsistent.

**Warning I keep needing to repeat to myself:** Take pivot columns of $A$, not of $R$. The pivot columns of $R$ are $e_1$ and $e_2$, which span a completely different plane.

---

### Subspace 2: The Nullspace $N(A)$

**Dimension:** $n - r = 4 - 2 = 2$.

**Basis:** The two special solutions, one per free variable ($x_2$ and $x_4$):

$$ \left{ \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, ; \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} \right} $$

**What it is geometrically:** A 2-dimensional plane through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^4$.

**What it means:** These are all the vectors $x$ that $A$ maps to $0$. The matrix $A$ cannot distinguish between $x$ and $x + x_n$ for any $x_n \in N(A)$ — they produce the same output. The nullspace measures the non-injectivity of $A$.

---

### Subspace 3: The Row Space $C(A^T)$

**Dimension:** $r = 2$.

**Basis:** The nonzero rows of $R$, written as column vectors:

$$ \left{ \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 0 \ -2 \end{pmatrix}, ; \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 1 \ 2 \end{pmatrix} \right} $$

**What it is geometrically:** A 2-dimensional plane through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^4$.

**Note:** The row space and nullspace both live in $\mathbb{R}^4$. Their dimensions are $2$ and $2$. They are orthogonal complements: $C(A^T) \perp N(A)$ and $C(A^T) + N(A) = \mathbb{R}^4$. Together they fill all of $\mathbb{R}^4$ with no overlap except ${0}$.

**Why use rows of $R$ and not rows of $A$?** Both span the same row space — row operations preserve the row space. But the rows of $R$ are in a canonical form that makes independence obvious. Either works; the rows of $R$ are cleaner.

---

### Subspace 4: The Left Nullspace $N(A^T)$

**Dimension:** $m - r = 3 - 2 = 1$.

**How to find a basis:** I need $y \in \mathbb{R}^3$ with $A^T y = 0$, equivalently $y^T A = 0^T$.

The row operations that reduce $A$ to $R$ can be captured in a matrix $E$ (the product of all the elementary matrices) such that $EA = R$. The rows of $E$ corresponding to zero rows of $R$ are exactly the combinations of rows of $A$ that produce $0$ — these are the elements of $N(A^T)$.

For this matrix, the third row of $R$ is zero. The elimination steps were:

- $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$
- $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_1$
- $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2$ (which by this point is already $R_3 - 3R_1 - (R_2 - 2R_1) = R_3 - R_2 - R_1$)

The combination that produced the zero row: $R_3 - R_2 - R_1 = 0$ (as a row of $A$). So

$$ y = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \ -1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

is the basis vector for $N(A^T)$.

**Verify:** $A^T y$: row 1 of $A^T$ is $(1,2,3)$, dot with $(-1,-1,1)$: $-1 - 2 + 3 = 0$. Row 2: $(2,4,6) \cdot (-1,-1,1) = -2-4+6 = 0$. Row 3: $(2,6,8)\cdot(-1,-1,1) = -2-6+8=0$. Row 4: $(2,8,10)\cdot(-1,-1,1) = -2-8+10=0$. All zero. $\checkmark$

**What it means geometrically:** $N(A^T)$ is a line through the origin in $\mathbb{R}^3$. It is orthogonal to the column space $C(A)$, which is a plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$. A line and a plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$ that are orthogonal and together span $\mathbb{R}^3$: $1 + 2 = 3 = m$. Consistent.

**What it means for consistency:** $Ax = b$ is solvable if and only if $y^T b = 0$ for every $y \in N(A^T)$. Here: $Ax = b$ is solvable iff $(-1,-1,1) \cdot b = 0$, i.e., $b_3 - b_2 - b_1 = 0$. This is exactly the consistency condition derived in [[All Solutions to Ax=b]] from the zero row of the augmented matrix. The left nullspace is the algebraic reason that condition exists.

## The Big Picture: One Diagram

Everything about an $m \times n$ matrix $A$ of rank $r$:

```
        R^n (input)                    R^m (output)
  ┌─────────────────────┐        ┌─────────────────────┐
  │                     │        │                     │
  │   Row space C(A^T)  │──────▶ │   Column space C(A) │
  │     dim = r         │   A    │     dim = r          │
  │                     │        │                     │
  ├─────────────────────┤        ├─────────────────────┤
  │                     │        │                     │
  │   Nullspace N(A)    │──────▶ │  Left nullspace     │
  │     dim = n - r     │   A    │    N(A^T)           │
  │                     │  (→0)  │    dim = m - r       │
  └─────────────────────┘        └─────────────────────┘
```

The row space maps to the column space — $A$ restricted to $C(A^T)$ is an invertible map onto $C(A)$. The nullspace maps to ${0}$ — $A$ collapses it entirely. The two pairs are orthogonal within their respective ambient spaces.

This is the complete action of $A$. Every $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ splits uniquely as $x = x_r + x_n$ with $x_r \in C(A^T)$ and $x_n \in N(A)$. Then $Ax = Ax_r + Ax_n = Ax_r + 0 = Ax_r$. The only part of $x$ that $A$ sees is $x_r$; the nullspace component is invisible.

## Three Special Cases Worth Memorizing

**Full column rank ($r = n$):** $N(A) = {0}$. Every $x$ is uniquely determined by $Ax$ (if a solution exists). The system $Ax = b$ has at most one solution. The row space fills all of $\mathbb{R}^n$.

**Full row rank ($r = m$):** $N(A^T) = {0}$. The system $Ax = b$ is consistent for every $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$ — the column space fills $\mathbb{R}^m$. The left nullspace is trivial so there are no consistency conditions.

**Full rank, square ($r = m = n$):** All four subspaces reduce to their extremes: $C(A) = \mathbb{R}^m$, $N(A) = {0}$, $C(A^T) = \mathbb{R}^n$, $N(A^T) = {0}$. The matrix $A$ is invertible. Every $Ax = b$ has exactly one solution.
