
## Why This Topic Exists

Most linear systems $Ax = b$ have no solution. This is not an edge case — it is the generic situation whenever you have more equations than unknowns, which happens constantly in data fitting, measurement, and science. You have $m$ observations and $n$ parameters with $m \gg n$, and you want to find the $x$ that comes _closest_ to satisfying $Ax = b$ even though exact equality is impossible.

The question is: what does "closest" mean, and how do we compute it?

The answer lives entirely in the geometry of projections onto column spaces. Once I see that, the normal equations stop looking like an algebraic trick and start looking inevitable.

---

## The Geometry First

$Ax = b$ has a solution exactly when $b \in C(A)$, the column space of $A$. When $b \notin C(A)$, no $x$ satisfies it. But there is a unique point in $C(A)$ that is closest to $b$: its orthogonal projection $\hat{b} = \text{proj}_{C(A)} b$.

The vector $\hat{x}$ we want satisfies

$$A\hat{x} = \hat{b}$$

where $\hat{b}$ is the projection of $b$ onto $C(A)$. This system _does_ have a solution because $\hat{b} \in C(A)$ by construction.

The error vector is $e = b - \hat{b} = b - A\hat{x}$.

The key geometric fact: $e$ is orthogonal to every vector in $C(A)$. That means $e \perp A\hat{x}$ for all $\hat{x}$, which means $e$ is orthogonal to every column of $A$, which means

$$A^T e = 0$$

Substituting $e = b - A\hat{x}$:

$$A^T(b - A\hat{x}) = 0$$

$$\boxed{A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b}$$

These are the **normal equations**. They always have a solution. If $A$ has independent columns, the solution is unique.

---

## Deriving the Same Thing Algebraically

It is worth seeing the calculus derivation too, if only to confirm that the geometry is not cheating.

We want to minimize the squared error:

$$|e|^2 = |b - Ax|^2 = (b - Ax)^T(b - Ax)$$

Expand:

$$|b - Ax|^2 = b^T b - 2x^T A^T b + x^T A^T A x$$

Take the gradient with respect to $x$ and set it to zero:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial x}|b - Ax|^2 = -2A^T b + 2A^T A x = 0$$

$$A^T A x = A^T b$$

Same equations. The matrix $A^T A$ is symmetric and positive semidefinite. It is positive definite — hence invertible — precisely when the columns of $A$ are linearly independent.

---

## The Normal Equations in Full

When $A$ has linearly independent columns ($\text{rank}(A) = n$), $A^T A$ is invertible and

$$\hat{x} = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T b$$

The projection of $b$ onto $C(A)$ is

$$\hat{b} = A\hat{x} = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T b$$

So the **projection matrix** onto $C(A)$ is

$$P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$$

This $P$ is $m \times m$, symmetric ($P^T = P$), and idempotent ($P^2 = P$). Any matrix with those two properties is a projection matrix onto its own column space.

---

## Properties of $A^T A$

Let $A$ be $m \times n$ with $m \geq n$.

**Symmetry:** $(A^T A)^T = A^T (A^T)^T = A^T A$. Always symmetric.

**Null space:** $N(A^T A) = N(A)$.

_Proof._ If $Ax = 0$ then $A^T A x = 0$, so $N(A) \subseteq N(A^T A)$. Conversely, if $A^T A x = 0$, then $x^T A^T A x = 0$, so $|Ax|^2 = 0$, so $Ax = 0$. Hence $N(A^T A) \subseteq N(A)$. $\square$

**Invertibility:** $A^T A$ is invertible if and only if $A$ has linearly independent columns (i.e., $N(A) = {0}$).

**Rank:** $\text{rank}(A^T A) = \text{rank}(A)$.

## A Worked Example: Fitting a Line

Three data points: $(1, 1)$, $(2, 2)$, $(3, 4)$. Fit a line $y = c + dx$.

The system $Ax = b$ asks for:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c \ d \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

Three equations, two unknowns — overdetermined. Compute $A^T A$ and $A^T b$:

$$A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \ 6 & 14 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$A^T b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 \ 17 \end{pmatrix}$$

Solve the $2 \times 2$ normal equations:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 6 \ 6 & 14 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} c \ d \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 7 \ 17 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\det(A^T A) = 3 \cdot 14 - 6 \cdot 6 = 42 - 36 = 6$.

$$(A^T A)^{-1} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 14 & -6 \ -6 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\hat{x} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 14 & -6 \ -6 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 7 \ 17 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} 98 - 102 \ -42 + 51 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix} -4 \ 9 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2/3 \ 3/2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Best fit line: $y = -\tfrac{2}{3} + \tfrac{3}{2}x$.

The projection $\hat{b} = A\hat{x}$:

$$\hat{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} -2/3 \ 3/2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -2/3 + 3/2 \ -2/3 + 3 \ -2/3 + 9/2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 5/6 \ 7/3 \ 23/6 \end{pmatrix}$$

Error vector $e = b - \hat{b}$:

$$e = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 4 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 5/6 \ 7/3 \ 23/6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/6 \ -1/3 \ 1/6 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Verify orthogonality:** $A^T e$ should be zero.

$$A^T e = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1/6 \ -1/3 \ 1/6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/6 - 1/3 + 1/6 \ 1/6 - 2/3 + 1/2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} \checkmark$$

The error is perpendicular to the column space, as it must be.

## What Goes Wrong When Columns Are Dependent

If the columns of $A$ are linearly dependent, then $N(A) \neq {0}$, so $A^T A$ is singular. The normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$ are still consistent (they always are — $A^T b \in C(A^T A)$ follows from $C(A^T) = C(A^T A)$, which itself follows from $N(A) = N(A^T A)$). But the solution $\hat{x}$ is not unique.

The projection $\hat{b}$ is still unique — there is only one closest point in $C(A)$ to $b$. What is not unique is the _combination of columns_ that produces it. In statistics this is called multicollinearity; in linear algebra it is the statement that $\hat{x}$ is determined only up to elements of $N(A)$.

The right tool here is the pseudoinverse $A^+$, which picks the unique minimum-norm solution $\hat{x} = A^+ b$. But that is a separate note.

## The Projection Matrix $P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$

It is worth sitting with what $P$ does. It projects any vector $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$ onto $C(A)$.

- $Pb = \hat{b}$, the closest point in $C(A)$ to $b$.
- $(I - P)b = e$, the error, which lies in $C(A)^\perp = N(A^T)$.
- $P^2 = P$: projecting twice does nothing after the first time.
- $P^T = P$: orthogonal projections are symmetric.

**Special cases:**

- If $b \in C(A)$ already, then $Pb = b$.
- If $b \perp C(A)$, then $Pb = 0$.

When $A = q$ is a single unit vector, $P = qq^T$ is the rank-1 projection onto the line spanned by $q$. The general formula is the natural extension of this to higher-dimensional subspaces.

---

## Why the Error $e$ Lives in $N(A^T)$

This deserves to be said explicitly because it ties directly to the four fundamental subspaces.

The fundamental theorem says $\mathbb{R}^m = C(A) \oplus N(A^T)$ (orthogonal direct sum). Every $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$ decomposes uniquely as

$$b = \hat{b} + e, \qquad \hat{b} \in C(A),\ e \in N(A^T)$$

Least squares is precisely this decomposition. $\hat{b} = Pb$ is the component in the column space, and $e = (I-P)b$ is the component in the left nullspace. The normal equations enforce $A^T e = 0$, which is exactly the statement $e \in N(A^T)$.

I think this is the cleanest way to understand least squares: it is the fundamental subspace decomposition of $b$, made explicit.

## Fitting More General Functions

The same machinery applies to fitting any linear combination of prescribed functions $\phi_1(t), \phi_2(t), \ldots, \phi_n(t)$ to data $(t_1, b_1), \ldots, (t_m, b_m)$.

Set

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} \phi_1(t_1) & \phi_2(t_1) & \cdots & \phi_n(t_1) \ \phi_1(t_2) & \phi_2(t_2) & \cdots & \phi_n(t_2) \ \vdots & & & \vdots \ \phi_1(t_m) & \phi_2(t_m) & \cdots & \phi_n(t_m) \end{pmatrix}$$

The columns of $A$ are the functions $\phi_j$ evaluated at the data points. The normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$ give the best-fit coefficients in exactly the same way. For polynomial fitting, $\phi_j(t) = t^{j-1}$; for Fourier fitting, $\phi_j$ are sines and cosines. The linear algebra is identical in every case.

## Things to Remember (for Myself)

- The normal equations come from enforcing $e \perp C(A)$, i.e., $A^T(b - A\hat{x}) = 0$. Never forget the geometry behind the algebra.
- $A^T A$ invertible $\iff$ columns of $A$ are independent. Check this before blindly inverting.
- The projection $\hat{b}$ is always unique. Only the representation $\hat{x}$ is ambiguous when columns are dependent.
- $P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$ is the projection matrix. It is $m \times m$, symmetric, idempotent. Its eigenvalues are all 0 or 1.
- Verify any computation by checking $A^T e = 0$. If it fails, something went wrong.
- The whole story is: $b$ decomposes into $\hat{b} \in C(A)$ and $e \in N(A^T)$. Least squares finds that decomposition.
