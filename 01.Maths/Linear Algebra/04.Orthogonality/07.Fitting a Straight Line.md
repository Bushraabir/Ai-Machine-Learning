## The Setup

This is the least squares story made completely concrete. We have $m$ data points

$$(t_1, b_1),\ (t_2, b_2),\ \ldots,\ (t_m, b_m)$$

and we want to fit a line

$$b = C + Dt$$

The two unknowns are $C$ (intercept) and $D$ (slope). If the line passed exactly through every point, we would need

$$C + D t_1 = b_1$$ $$C + D t_2 = b_2$$ $$\vdots$$ $$C + D t_m = b_m$$

In matrix form, this is $Ax = b$ where

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & t_1 \ 1 & t_2 \ \vdots & \vdots \ 1 & t_m \end{pmatrix}, \qquad x = \begin{pmatrix} C \ D \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \ b_2 \ \vdots \ b_m \end{pmatrix}$$

For $m > 2$ data points not all on a common line, this system is inconsistent. No exact solution exists. We want the least squares solution $\hat{x} = \begin{pmatrix} \hat{C} \ \hat{D} \end{pmatrix}$.

---

## The Normal Equations for a Line

Recall from the general theory: $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$.

Compute $A^T A$:

$$A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \ t_1 & t_2 & \cdots & t_m \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & t_1 \ 1 & t_2 \ \vdots & \vdots \ 1 & t_m \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} m & \sum t_i \ \sum t_i & \sum t_i^2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Compute $A^T b$:

$$A^T b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \ t_1 & t_2 & \cdots & t_m \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \ b_2 \ \vdots \ b_m \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \sum b_i \ \sum t_i b_i \end{pmatrix}$$

The normal equations are therefore the $2 \times 2$ system:

$$\begin{pmatrix} m & \sum t_i \ \sum t_i & \sum t_i^2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \hat{C} \ \hat{D} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \sum b_i \ \sum t_i b_i \end{pmatrix}$$

This is what statisticians write down as the "least squares formulas for simple linear regression." It is just the normal equations for this specific $A$.

---

## Solving the $2 \times 2$ System

The determinant of $A^T A$ is

$$\det(A^T A) = m \sum t_i^2 - \left(\sum t_i\right)^2$$

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality (or just by recognizing this as $m$ times the variance of the $t_i$'s), this is strictly positive as long as the $t_i$ are not all equal. So $A^T A$ is invertible whenever the horizontal coordinates are not all the same — which is the natural condition for fitting a line.

By Cramer's rule or direct inversion:

$$\hat{D} = \frac{m \sum t_i b_i - \sum t_i \sum b_i}{m \sum t_i^2 - \left(\sum t_i\right)^2}$$

$$\hat{C} = \frac{\sum b_i - \hat{D} \sum t_i}{m} = \bar{b} - \hat{D},\bar{t}$$

where $\bar{t} = \frac{1}{m}\sum t_i$ and $\bar{b} = \frac{1}{m}\sum b_i$ are the sample means. The best fit line always passes through the point of means $(\bar{t}, \bar{b})$. This is a clean fact worth noting.

## A Fully Worked Example

Four data points: $(1, 1)$, $(2, 2)$, $(3, 2)$, $(4, 4)$.

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 2 \ 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 1: Compute $A^T A$.**

$$m = 4, \quad \sum t_i = 1+2+3+4 = 10, \quad \sum t_i^2 = 1+4+9+16 = 30$$

$$A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 10 \ 10 & 30 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Compute $A^T b$.**

$$\sum b_i = 1+2+2+4 = 9, \quad \sum t_i b_i = 1\cdot1 + 2\cdot2 + 3\cdot2 + 4\cdot4 = 1+4+6+16 = 27$$

$$A^T b = \begin{pmatrix} 9 \ 27 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Write and solve the normal equations.**

$$\begin{pmatrix} 4 & 10 \ 10 & 30 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \hat{C} \ \hat{D} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 9 \ 27 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\det = 4 \cdot 30 - 10 \cdot 10 = 120 - 100 = 20$.

$$(A^T A)^{-1} = \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 30 & -10 \ -10 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\begin{pmatrix} \hat{C} \ \hat{D} \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 30 & -10 \ -10 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 9 \ 27 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 270 - 270 \ -90 + 108 \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 18 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 9/10 \end{pmatrix}$$

Best fit line: $\hat{b} = 0 + \tfrac{9}{10}t$, i.e., $b = 0.9t$.

**Step 4: Find the projection $\hat{b} = A\hat{x}$.**

$$\hat{b} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 9/10 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 9/10 \ 18/10 \ 27/10 \ 36/10 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.9 \ 1.8 \ 2.7 \ 3.6 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 5: Compute the error $e = b - \hat{b}$.**

$$e = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 2 \ 4 \end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix} 0.9 \ 1.8 \ 2.7 \ 3.6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.1 \ 0.2 \ -0.7 \ 0.4 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 6: Verify $A^T e = 0$.**

$$A^T e = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 0.1 \ 0.2 \ -0.7 \ 0.4 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.1 + 0.2 - 0.7 + 0.4 \ 0.1 + 0.4 - 2.1 + 1.6 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} \checkmark$$

The squared error is $|e|^2 = 0.01 + 0.04 + 0.49 + 0.16 = 0.70$.

---

## Verify: The Line Passes Through the Point of Means

$$\bar{t} = \frac{10}{4} = 2.5, \qquad \bar{b} = \frac{9}{4} = 2.25$$

Check: $\hat{C} + \hat{D}\bar{t} = 0 + 0.9 \times 2.5 = 2.25 = \bar{b}$. $\checkmark$

## Simplification When $\sum t_i = 0$

If the $t_i$ are chosen symmetrically around zero — i.e., $\sum t_i = 0$ — then $A^T A$ is diagonal:

$$A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} m & 0 \ 0 & \sum t_i^2 \end{pmatrix}$$

The normal equations decouple completely:

$$\hat{C} = \frac{\sum b_i}{m} = \bar{b}, \qquad \hat{D} = \frac{\sum t_i b_i}{\sum t_i^2}$$

The slope and intercept are determined independently. This is not just a computational convenience — it reflects the fact that when the columns of $A$ are orthogonal, the projection onto each component is independent of the other. Centering the data (replacing $t_i$ by $t_i - \bar{t}$) always achieves this.

This is the first hint of why orthogonal bases are so valuable computationally. When $A^T A$ is diagonal, there is nothing to solve — each coefficient is just a dot product divided by a norm squared.

---

## The Projection Matrix Explicitly

For this $A$, the projection matrix $P = A(A^T A)^{-1}A^T$ projects any $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$ onto the two-dimensional column space of $A$ — the subspace of all vectors of the form $C \mathbf{1} + D \mathbf{t}$, where $\mathbf{1} = (1,\ldots,1)^T$ and $\mathbf{t} = (t_1,\ldots,t_m)^T$.

For the worked example:

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{20}\begin{pmatrix} 30 & -10 \ -10 & 4 \end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

I will not expand this fully — it is a $4 \times 4$ matrix — but $P$ satisfies $P^2 = P$ and $P^T = P$, and applying it to any $b$ gives the closest point to $b$ that lies on some line through the data.

---

## Connecting Back to Statistics

The least squares line is the same as the ordinary linear regression line. The formulas for $\hat{C}$ and $\hat{D}$ are classical. The statistical interpretation adds noise models and confidence intervals on top, but the computation is purely linear algebra: project $b$ onto $C(A)$.

The residuals $e_i = b_i - (\hat{C} + \hat{D}t_i)$ are the vertical distances from each data point to the fitted line. Least squares minimizes the sum of _squared_ vertical distances, not absolute distances or horizontal distances or perpendicular distances. That choice is what makes the problem linear.

---

## Things to Remember (for Myself)

- The matrix $A$ always has a column of ones (for the intercept) and a column of $t_i$'s (for the slope). Do not forget the column of ones.
- The normal equations collapse to a $2 \times 2$ system. Always write them out explicitly before inverting.
- Centering the $t_i$ around zero decouples slope and intercept and makes computation clean. Worth doing in practice.
- The best fit line passes through $(\bar{t}, \bar{b})$ without exception.
- Always verify $A^T e = 0$ at the end. It is a two-component check and takes ten seconds.
- The error $e$ lies in $N(A^T)$, the left nullspace of $A$. Every residual vector from a least squares fit has this property.
