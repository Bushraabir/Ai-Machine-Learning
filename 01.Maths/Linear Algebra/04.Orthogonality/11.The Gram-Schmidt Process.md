## Why We Need This

The normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$ are the right answer to least squares, but they are not always the right _computation_. Forming $A^T A$ explicitly squares the condition number of $A$, which can be catastrophic for nearly-dependent columns.

The deeper issue is this: if the columns of $A$ were orthonormal to begin with, the normal equations would collapse to $\hat{x} = Q^T b$ — no matrix to invert, no system to solve. Each coefficient is just a dot product.

Gram-Schmidt is the algorithm that takes any linearly independent set of vectors and produces an orthonormal set spanning the same space. It is how we manufacture that nice situation from any starting basis.

---

## The Idea in One Sentence

At each step, take the next vector, subtract off all its components in the directions already handled, and normalize what remains.

---

## The Process: Two Vectors First

Start with two linearly independent vectors $a$ and $b$ in $\mathbb{R}^m$.

**First vector.** Just normalize:

$$q_1 = \frac{a}{|a|}$$

**Second vector.** Take $b$ and subtract its projection onto $q_1$:

$$b' = b - (q_1^T b), q_1$$

The vector $b'$ is orthogonal to $q_1$ by construction:

$$q_1^T b' = q_1^T b - (q_1^T b)(q_1^T q_1) = q_1^T b - q_1^T b = 0$$

Then normalize:

$$q_2 = \frac{b'}{|b'|}$$

The pair ${q_1, q_2}$ is an orthonormal basis for $\text{span}{a, b}$.

---

## The Process: Three Vectors

Add a third vector $c$, linearly independent of $a$ and $b$.

Subtract off the projections onto both $q_1$ and $q_2$:

$$c' = c - (q_1^T c), q_1 - (q_2^T c), q_2$$

Verify: $q_1^T c' = q_1^T c - (q_1^T c)(1) - (q_2^T c)(q_1^T q_2) = 0$ since $q_1^T q_2 = 0$. Similarly $q_2^T c' = 0$.

Then normalize:

$$q_3 = \frac{c'}{|c'|}$$

The set ${q_1, q_2, q_3}$ is an orthonormal basis for $\text{span}{a, b, c}$.

---

## The General Step

Given vectors $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n$ linearly independent. After producing $q_1, \ldots, q_{k-1}$, the $k$-th step is:

$$a_k' = a_k - \sum_{j=1}^{k-1} (q_j^T a_k), q_j$$

$$q_k = \frac{a_k'}{|a_k'|}$$

The scalar $q_j^T a_k$ is the component of $a_k$ in the direction $q_j$. Subtracting removes that component. The result $a_k'$ is orthogonal to all of $q_1, \ldots, q_{k-1}$.

Note: if $a_k'= 0$ at any step, it means $a_k$ was already in the span of $a_1, \ldots, a_{k-1}$ — the original vectors were linearly _dependent_. Gram-Schmidt detects dependence by producing a zero vector.

---

## A Fully Worked Example

Take three vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$:

$$a_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad a_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \quad a_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 1: Orthogonalize and normalize $a_1$.**

$$|a_1| = \sqrt{1+1+0} = \sqrt{2}$$

$$q_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Orthogonalize $a_2$ against $q_1$.**

$$q_1^T a_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1\cdot1 + 1\cdot0 + 0\cdot1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$

$$a_2' = a_2 - (q_1^T a_2),q_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1/2\-1/2\1\end{pmatrix}$$

$$|a_2'| = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{4} + \tfrac{1}{4} + 1} = \sqrt{\tfrac{3}{2}} = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}$$

$$q_2 = \frac{2}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1/2\-1/2\1\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix}$$

**Check:** $q_1^T q_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(1 - 1 + 0) = 0$. $\checkmark$

**Step 3: Orthogonalize $a_3$ against $q_1$ and $q_2$.**

$$q_1^T a_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(0 + 1 + 0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$

$$q_2^T a_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(0 - 1 + 2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}$$

$$a_3' = a_3 - (q_1^T a_3),q_1 - (q_2^T a_3),q_2$$

$$= \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix}$$

$$= \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix}$$

$$= \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1/2\1/2\0\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1/6\-1/6\1/3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0 - 1/2 - 1/6\ 1 - 1/2 + 1/6\ 1 - 0 - 1/3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-2/3\2/3\2/3\end{pmatrix}$$

$$|a_3'| = \sqrt{\tfrac{4}{9}+\tfrac{4}{9}+\tfrac{4}{9}} = \sqrt{\tfrac{12}{9}} = \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$$

$$q_3 = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \cdot \begin{pmatrix}-2/3\2/3\2/3\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\begin{pmatrix}-1\1\1\end{pmatrix}$$

**Check:** $q_1^T q_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}\sqrt{3}}(-1+1+0) = 0$. $\checkmark$

$q_2^T q_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}\sqrt{3}}(-1\cdot1 + (-1)\cdot1 + 2\cdot1)\cdot\frac{1}{1}$

Wait, more carefully: $q_2^T q_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(1\cdot(-1) + (-1)\cdot 1 + 2 \cdot 1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{18}}(-1-1+2) = 0$. $\checkmark$

The orthonormal basis for $\mathbb{R}^3$ is:

$$q_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad q_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix}, \quad q_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\begin{pmatrix}-1\1\1\end{pmatrix}$$

## The Connection to QR Factorization

This is the payoff. Trace what Gram-Schmidt actually says about the relationship between $a_k$ and the $q_j$'s.

At step $k$, we wrote:

$$a_k = a_k' + \sum_{j=1}^{k-1}(q_j^T a_k),q_j = |a_k'|, q_k + \sum_{j=1}^{k-1}(q_j^T a_k),q_j$$

This expresses $a_k$ as a linear combination of $q_1, \ldots, q_k$ with _no_ $q_{k+1}, \ldots, q_n$ appearing. The coefficients are:

$$r_{jk} = q_j^T a_k \quad (j < k), \qquad r_{kk} = |a_k'|$$

In matrix form, if $A = [a_1 \mid a_2 \mid \cdots \mid a_n]$ and $Q = [q_1 \mid q_2 \mid \cdots \mid q_n]$, then:

$$A = QR$$

where $R$ is **upper triangular** with positive diagonal entries $r_{kk} = |a_k'| > 0$.

Gram-Schmidt is not just an orthogonalization algorithm. It _is_ the QR factorization, column by column.

For the worked example:

$$R = \begin{pmatrix} |a_1| & q_1^T a_2 & q_1^T a_3 \ 0 & |a_2'| & q_2^T a_3 \ 0 & 0 & |a_3'| \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ 0 & \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \ 0 & 0 & \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \end{pmatrix}$$

---

## Why Orthonormal Columns Make Everything Easy

If $A = QR$ with $Q$ having orthonormal columns ($Q^T Q = I$), then the normal equations

$$A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$$

become

$$(QR)^T(QR)\hat{x} = (QR)^T b$$

$$R^T Q^T Q R \hat{x} = R^T Q^T b$$

$$R^T R \hat{x} = R^T Q^T b$$

Since $R$ is invertible (positive diagonal), $R^T$ is invertible, so:

$$R \hat{x} = Q^T b$$

A triangular system. No $A^T A$ to form. No squaring of the condition number. This is why QR is the numerically preferred method for least squares.

The solution is:

$$\hat{x} = R^{-1} Q^T b$$

and the projection of $b$ onto $C(A)$ is:

$$\hat{b} = A\hat{x} = QR \cdot R^{-1} Q^T b = Q Q^T b$$

When $Q$ is square (full orthonormal basis), $QQ^T = I$. When $Q$ is $m \times n$ with $m > n$ (thin QR), $QQ^T \neq I$ but it is still the projection matrix onto $C(A)$.

---

## Projections With an Orthonormal Basis

The special case where Gram-Schmidt pays off most visibly: if $q_1, \ldots, q_n$ is an orthonormal basis for a subspace $S$, then the projection of any $b$ onto $S$ is:

$$\hat{b} = (q_1^T b),q_1 + (q_2^T b),q_2 + \cdots + (q_n^T b),q_n$$

Each coefficient $(q_j^T b)$ is independent of all the others. The normal equations have become diagonal — $A^T A = I$ when $A = Q$. This is the orthonormal basis advantage: projections decompose into independent one-dimensional projections.

In the language of matrices: $P = QQ^T = \sum_{j=1}^n q_j q_j^T$, a sum of rank-one projections.

---

## What Gram-Schmidt Does Not Do

Gram-Schmidt is numerically unstable in its classical form. The issue is error accumulation: later vectors subtract projections onto $q_1, \ldots, q_{k-1}$, and rounding errors in those projections propagate. For large $n$, the vectors $q_j$ produced by classical Gram-Schmidt can lose orthogonality badly.

The fix is **modified Gram-Schmidt**: instead of subtracting all projections from the original $a_k$ at once, subtract one projection at a time, updating the running vector after each subtraction. This gives the same result in exact arithmetic but far better numerical behavior.

In practice, Householder reflections give an even more stable QR factorization than either form of Gram-Schmidt. But that is a numerical linear algebra story.

## Things to Remember (for Myself)

- The formula at each step: $a_k' = a_k - \sum_{j < k}(q_j^T a_k),q_j$. Then $q_k = a_k' / |a_k'|$.
- If $a_k' = 0$: the original vectors are dependent. Stop — or discard $a_k$ and continue.
- The coefficients $r_{jk} = q_j^T a_k$ are exactly the entries of $R$ in $A = QR$.
- $Q^T Q = I$ always (orthonormal columns). $QQ^T = I$ only when $Q$ is square.
- With QR, the normal equations become $R\hat{x} = Q^T b$. Back-substitution, done.
- Projection onto $S = \text{span}{q_1,\ldots,q_n}$: just sum up the rank-one projections $(q_j^T b)q_j$.
- Classical Gram-Schmidt is unstable for large systems. Modified Gram-Schmidt is better. Householder is best.
