## The Natural Extension

In the previous note we fit a line $b = C + Dt$ to data. The machinery was: write down the overdetermined system $Ax = b$, then solve the normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$.

Nothing in that argument depended on the line being degree one. The moment we decide to fit a parabola

$$b = C + Dt + Et^2$$

the unknowns become $x = \begin{pmatrix} C \ D \ E \end{pmatrix}$ and $A$ gains a third column. Everything else is identical.

---

## The Matrix $A$

Given $m$ data points $(t_1, b_1), \ldots, (t_m, b_m)$ with $m > 3$, asking for an exact parabola through all of them gives the overdetermined system:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & t_1 & t_1^2 \ 1 & t_2 & t_2^2 \ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \ 1 & t_m & t_m^2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} C \ D \ E \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \ b_2 \ \vdots \ b_m \end{pmatrix}$$

The matrix $A$ is $m \times 3$. Its columns are the constant function $\mathbf{1}$, the linear function $\mathbf{t}$, and the quadratic function $\mathbf{t^2}$, all evaluated at the data points.

This is a **Vandermonde-type** matrix. Its columns are linearly independent as long as at least three of the $t_i$ are distinct — which is the natural condition for fitting a parabola to have meaning.

---

## The Normal Equations

$A^T A$ is now $3 \times 3$:

$$A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & \cdots & 1 \ t_1 & t_2 & \cdots & t_m \ t_1^2 & t_2^2 & \cdots & t_m^2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & t_1 & t_1^2 \ 1 & t_2 & t_2^2 \ \vdots & \vdots & \vdots \ 1 & t_m & t_m^2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} m & \sum t_i & \sum t_i^2 \ \sum t_i & \sum t_i^2 & \sum t_i^3 \ \sum t_i^2 & \sum t_i^3 & \sum t_i^4 \end{pmatrix}$$

And $A^T b$ is:

$$A^T b = \begin{pmatrix} \sum b_i \ \sum t_i b_i \ \sum t_i^2 b_i \end{pmatrix}$$

The normal equations are:

$$\begin{pmatrix} m & \sum t_i & \sum t_i^2 \ \sum t_i & \sum t_i^2 & \sum t_i^3 \ \sum t_i^2 & \sum t_i^3 & \sum t_i^4 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \hat{C} \ \hat{D} \ \hat{E} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \sum b_i \ \sum t_i b_i \ \sum t_i^2 b_i \end{pmatrix}$$

Notice the pattern: entry $(i,j)$ of $A^T A$ is $\sum_k t_k^{i+j-2}$. The sums of powers of $t_i$ fill the matrix, and they march upward in degree as you move right or downward.

---

## A Fully Worked Example

Five data points: $(0, 0)$, $(1, 1)$, $(2, 5)$, $(3, 10)$, $(4, 16)$.

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 4 \ 1 & 3 & 9 \ 1 & 4 & 16 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ 5 \ 10 \ 16 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 1: Compute the sums.**

$$\sum t_i = 0+1+2+3+4 = 10$$ $$\sum t_i^2 = 0+1+4+9+16 = 30$$ $$\sum t_i^3 = 0+1+8+27+64 = 100$$ $$\sum t_i^4 = 0+1+16+81+256 = 354$$ $$\sum b_i = 0+1+5+10+16 = 32$$ $$\sum t_i b_i = 0+1+10+30+64 = 105$$ $$\sum t_i^2 b_i = 0+1+20+90+256 = 367$$

**Step 2: Write $A^T A$ and $A^T b$.**

$$A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 10 & 30 \ 10 & 30 & 100 \ 30 & 100 & 354 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad A^T b = \begin{pmatrix} 32 \ 105 \ 367 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Solve the normal equations.**

$$\begin{pmatrix} 5 & 10 & 30 \ 10 & 30 & 100 \ 30 & 100 & 354 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} \hat{C} \ \hat{D} \ \hat{E} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 32 \ 105 \ 367 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row reduce the augmented matrix:

$$\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 5 & 10 & 30 & 32 \ 10 & 30 & 100 & 105 \ 30 & 100 & 354 & 367 \end{array}\right)$$

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 6R_1$:

$$\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 5 & 10 & 30 & 32 \ 0 & 10 & 40 & 41 \ 0 & 40 & 174 & 175 \end{array}\right)$$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 4R_2$:

$$\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 5 & 10 & 30 & 32 \ 0 & 10 & 40 & 41 \ 0 & 0 & -26 & -164+175 \end{array}\right) = \left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 5 & 10 & 30 & 32 \ 0 & 10 & 40 & 41 \ 0 & 0 & -26 & 11 \end{array}\right)$$

Wait — recompute $R_3 - 4R_2$: the right-hand side is $175 - 4 \cdot 41 = 175 - 164 = 11$. The $(3,3)$ entry is $174 - 4 \cdot 40 = 174 - 160 = 14$. Let me redo:

$$R_3 - 4R_2: \quad (0,\ 40-40,\ 174-160\ |\ 175-164) = (0,\ 0,\ 14\ |\ 11)$$

So:

$$\left(\begin{array}{ccc|c} 5 & 10 & 30 & 32 \ 0 & 10 & 40 & 41 \ 0 & 0 & 14 & 11 \end{array}\right)$$

Back-substitution:

$$\hat{E} = \frac{11}{14}$$

$$10\hat{D} + 40 \cdot \frac{11}{14} = 41 \implies 10\hat{D} = 41 - \frac{440}{14} = 41 - \frac{220}{7} = \frac{287 - 220}{7} = \frac{67}{7} \implies \hat{D} = \frac{67}{70}$$

$$5\hat{C} + 10 \cdot \frac{67}{70} + 30 \cdot \frac{11}{14} = 32 \implies 5\hat{C} = 32 - \frac{670}{70} - \frac{330}{14} = 32 - \frac{67}{7} - \frac{165}{7} = 32 - \frac{232}{7} = \frac{224 - 232}{7} = -\frac{8}{7}$$

$$\hat{C} = -\frac{8}{35}$$

**Best fit parabola:**

$$\hat{b}(t) = -\frac{8}{35} + \frac{67}{70}t + \frac{11}{14}t^2$$

**Step 4: Compute the error vector $e = b - A\hat{x}$.**

$$\hat{b}_1 = -\frac{8}{35} + 0 + 0 = -\frac{8}{35} \approx -0.229$$ $$\hat{b}_2 = -\frac{8}{35} + \frac{67}{70} + \frac{11}{14} = -\frac{16}{70} + \frac{67}{70} + \frac{55}{70} = \frac{106}{70} = \frac{53}{35} \approx 1.514$$ $$\hat{b}_3 = -\frac{8}{35} + \frac{134}{70} + \frac{44}{14} = -\frac{16}{70} + \frac{134}{70} + \frac{220}{70} = \frac{338}{70} = \frac{169}{35} \approx 4.829$$ $$\hat{b}_4 = -\frac{8}{35} + \frac{201}{70} + \frac{99}{14} = -\frac{16}{70} + \frac{201}{70} + \frac{495}{70} = \frac{680}{70} = \frac{68}{7} \approx 9.714$$ $$\hat{b}_5 = -\frac{8}{35} + \frac{268}{70} + \frac{176}{14} = -\frac{16}{70} + \frac{268}{70} + \frac{880}{70} = \frac{1132}{70} = \frac{566}{35} \approx 16.171$$

$$e \approx \begin{pmatrix} 0.229 \ -0.514 \ 0.171 \ 0.286 \ -0.171 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 5: Verify $A^T e = 0$** (numerically, checking the three components):

Row 1 of $A^T$: $\sum e_i \approx 0.229 - 0.514 + 0.171 + 0.286 - 0.171 = 0.001 \approx 0$ (rounding error only).

This is close enough given the fractions involved; the exact computation yields zero identically.

## What Happens When $m = 3$?

With exactly three data points and three unknowns, $A$ is $3 \times 3$ square. If the three $t_i$ are distinct, $A$ is a full Vandermonde matrix and is invertible. The system $Ax = b$ has an exact solution — the unique parabola through three points. There is no least squares approximation needed and no error: $e = 0$.

This is the boundary case. The moment we have four or more points, we are genuinely in least squares territory.

---

## The General Pattern: Fitting a Degree $n$ Polynomial

For fitting $b = c_0 + c_1 t + c_2 t^2 + \cdots + c_n t^n$ to $m > n+1$ points, $A$ is $m \times (n+1)$ with

$$A_{ij} = t_i^{j-1}, \qquad i = 1,\ldots,m,\quad j = 1,\ldots,n+1$$

The normal equations are $(n+1) \times (n+1)$. The entry in row $i$, column $j$ of $A^T A$ is $\sum_{k=1}^m t_k^{i+j-2}$. As $n$ grows, $A^T A$ becomes increasingly ill-conditioned — the columns of $A$ become nearly dependent because high powers of $t$ look similar on a grid. This is why in practice one uses orthogonal polynomials (Legendre, Chebyshev) or QR factorization rather than forming $A^T A$ directly.

---

## The Column Space Picture

The column space $C(A)$ is the set of all vectors in $\mathbb{R}^m$ of the form

$$C \cdot \mathbf{1} + D \cdot \mathbf{t} + E \cdot \mathbf{t^2}$$

where $\mathbf{1}, \mathbf{t}, \mathbf{t^2}$ are the three columns of $A$ (evaluated at the $t_i$'s). This is a three-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$. The least squares problem asks: which point in this subspace is closest to $b$? The answer is $\hat{b} = Pb$, the orthogonal projection of $b$ onto $C(A)$.

The error $e = b - \hat{b}$ is orthogonal to every vector in $C(A)$, which is exactly the statement $A^T e = 0$ — three scalar conditions, one for each column of $A$.

The geometry is the same as always. Only the dimension of the subspace has changed.

---

## Conditioning and a Warning

The matrix $A^T A$ for polynomial fitting is related to the **Hilbert matrix** when the $t_i$ are uniformly spaced. Hilbert matrices are notoriously ill-conditioned. For degree $n = 2$ this is rarely a problem, but for higher degrees the normal equations can be numerically catastrophic.

The right approach for higher-degree polynomial fitting is to work with an orthogonal basis for the polynomial space — this is what Gram-Schmidt on the columns of $A$ produces. The QR factorization of $A$ gives $A = QR$, and the normal equations become $R\hat{x} = Q^T b$, which are triangular and well-conditioned. That is the next note.

---

## Things to Remember (for Myself)

- $A$ is $m \times 3$ for a parabola. Columns are $\mathbf{1}$, $\mathbf{t}$, $\mathbf{t^2}$. Do not forget the column of ones.
- $A^T A$ is $3 \times 3$ and symmetric. Its entries are sums of powers of $t_i$ up to $t_i^4$.
- Three distinct $t_i$ values guarantee $A$ has independent columns. Fewer than three and the parabola is not determined.
- With exactly $m = 3$ points and $3$ unknowns: exact interpolation, no error. The least squares problem only bites when $m > 3$.
- The error $e$ must satisfy $A^T e = 0$ — here that is three orthogonality conditions. Always check at least one of them.
- Forming $A^T A$ explicitly is fine for $n = 2$ but dangerous for high-degree polynomials. QR is the numerically stable alternative.
