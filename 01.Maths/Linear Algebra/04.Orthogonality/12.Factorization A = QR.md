
## Where This Comes From

In the previous note, I established that projecting onto $C(A)$ is effortless when the columns of $A$ are orthonormal — the normal equations become $I\hat{x} = Q^T b$ and solve themselves. The natural question is: given a matrix $A$ with linearly independent but otherwise arbitrary columns, can I always find such an orthonormal basis for $C(A)$?

The answer is yes, and the process is **Gram-Schmidt**. The output of Gram-Schmidt is not just the orthonormal basis — it is a factorization $A = QR$ that encodes exactly how the original columns of $A$ relate to the new orthonormal ones. That factorization is what this note is about.

---

## The Gram-Schmidt Process

Let $A$ have linearly independent columns $a_1, a_2, \ldots, a_n \in \mathbb{R}^m$. We construct orthonormal vectors $q_1, q_2, \ldots, q_n$ such that

$$\text{span}(q_1, \ldots, q_j) = \text{span}(a_1, \ldots, a_j) \quad \text{for each } j = 1, \ldots, n$$

The column spaces are preserved at every step.

**Step 1.** Take $a_1$ as the first direction. Normalize:

$$q_1 = \frac{a_1}{|a_1|}$$

**Step 2.** Subtract from $a_2$ its projection onto $q_1$. What remains is orthogonal to $q_1$:

$$z_2 = a_2 - (q_1^T a_2), q_1$$

Normalize:

$$q_2 = \frac{z_2}{|z_2|}$$

**Step 3.** Subtract from $a_3$ its projections onto $q_1$ and $q_2$:

$$z_3 = a_3 - (q_1^T a_3), q_1 - (q_2^T a_3), q_2$$

Normalize:

$$q_3 = \frac{z_3}{|z_3|}$$

**Step $j$ (general).** Remove all components in the directions already found:

$$z_j = a_j - \sum_{i=1}^{j-1} (q_i^T a_j), q_i$$

$$q_j = \frac{z_j}{|z_j|}$$

At each step, $z_j \neq 0$ because $a_j \notin \text{span}(a_1, \ldots, a_{j-1})$ — guaranteed by linear independence of the columns of $A$.

---

## Reading Off the $R$ Matrix

Rearrange the Gram-Schmidt equations to express each $a_j$ in terms of the $q_i$'s.

From Step 1: $a_1 = |a_1| q_1$.

From Step 2: $a_2 = (q_1^T a_2) q_1 + |z_2| q_2$.

From Step 3: $a_3 = (q_1^T a_3) q_1 + (q_2^T a_3) q_2 + |z_3| q_3$.

In general:

$$a_j = \sum_{i=1}^{j-1}(q_i^T a_j), q_i + |z_j|, q_j = \sum_{i=1}^{j} r_{ij}, q_i$$

where $r_{ij} = q_i^T a_j$ for $i < j$ and $r_{jj} = |z_j| > 0$.

Writing all $n$ columns at once, in matrix form:

$$\underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \ a_1 & a_2 & \cdots & a_n \ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix}}_{A} = \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \ q_1 & q_2 & \cdots & q_n \ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix}}_{Q} \underbrace{\begin{pmatrix} r_{11} & r_{12} & r_{13} & \cdots & r_{1n} \ 0 & r_{22} & r_{23} & \cdots & r_{2n} \ 0 & 0 & r_{33} & \cdots & r_{3n} \ \vdots & & & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & r_{nn} \end{pmatrix}}_{R}$$

$R$ is **upper triangular** with **positive diagonal entries** $r_{jj} = |z_j|$. This is the QR factorization.

Explicitly, for three columns:

$$R = \begin{pmatrix} |a_1| & q_1^T a_2 & q_1^T a_3 \ 0 & |z_2| & q_2^T a_3 \ 0 & 0 & |z_3| \end{pmatrix}$$

The diagonal entries are the norms of the residuals at each Gram-Schmidt step. The upper off-diagonal entries are the projection coefficients subtracted during orthogonalization.

## A Fully Worked Example

Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Columns: $a_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix}$, $a_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\1\end{pmatrix}$, $a_3 = \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix}$.

---

**Step 1: Orthogonalize $a_1$.**

$$|a_1| = \sqrt{1+1+0} = \sqrt{2}$$

$$q_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix}$$

$$r_{11} = |a_1| = \sqrt{2}$$

---

**Step 2: Orthogonalize $a_2$ against $q_1$.**

$$r_{12} = q_1^T a_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot 0 + 0 \cdot 1) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$

$$z_2 = a_2 - r_{12}, q_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1/2\-1/2\1\end{pmatrix}$$

$$|z_2| = \sqrt{1/4 + 1/4 + 1} = \sqrt{3/2} = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}$$

$$q_2 = \frac{z_2}{|z_2|} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1/2\-1/2\1\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix}$$

$$r_{22} = |z_2| = \frac{\sqrt{6}}{2}$$

---

**Step 3: Orthogonalize $a_3$ against $q_1$ and $q_2$.**

$$r_{13} = q_1^T a_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(0 + 1 + 0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$

$$r_{23} = q_2^T a_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(0 - 1 + 2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}$$

$$z_3 = a_3 - r_{13}, q_1 - r_{23}, q_2$$

$$= \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix}$$

$$= \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix}$$

$$= \begin{pmatrix}0 - 1/2 - 1/6\ 1 - 1/2 + 1/6\ 1 - 0 - 2/6\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-2/3\2/3\2/3\end{pmatrix}$$

$$|z_3| = \sqrt{4/9 + 4/9 + 4/9} = \sqrt{12/9} = \frac{2\sqrt{3}}{3} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$$

$$q_3 = \frac{z_3}{|z_3|} = \frac{\sqrt{3}}{2} \begin{pmatrix}-2/3\2/3\2/3\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\begin{pmatrix}-1\1\1\end{pmatrix}$$

$$r_{33} = |z_3| = \frac{2}{\sqrt{3}}$$

---

**The factorization $A = QR$:**

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & -\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \[10pt] \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \[10pt] 0 & \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{6}} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \end{pmatrix}$$

$$R = \begin{pmatrix} \sqrt{2} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \[10pt] 0 & \dfrac{\sqrt{6}}{2} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{6}} \[10pt] 0 & 0 & \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{3}} \end{pmatrix}$$

**Verify $Q^T Q = I$:** Check that columns of $Q$ are orthonormal — they were built that way, so this is guaranteed. Check one: $q_1^T q_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{12}}(1 \cdot 1 + 1 \cdot(-1) + 0) = 0$. $\checkmark$

**Verify $A = QR$:** First column of $QR$ should be $a_1$:

$$QR,e_1 = Q \begin{pmatrix}\sqrt{2}\0\0\end{pmatrix} = \sqrt{2}, q_1 = \sqrt{2} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} = a_1 \checkmark$$

## Why $R$ is Upper Triangular

The triangularity of $R$ is not an accident of the algebra — it has a direct geometric meaning.

At step $j$, the new orthonormal vector $q_j$ is built from $a_1, \ldots, a_j$ only. It has no component in the directions $a_{j+1}, \ldots, a_n$ (those have not been processed yet). Therefore $q_j^T a_i = 0$ for $i < j$ — wait, the other direction: $q_i^T a_j = r_{ij}$ appears in column $j$ of $R$ for $i \leq j$, but $r_{ij} = 0$ for $i > j$ because $a_j$ has already been projected onto $q_1, \ldots, q_{j-1}$ before $q_j$ is even constructed, so $q_j$ carries no information from $a_j$...

More cleanly: $R$ is upper triangular because $a_j \in \text{span}(q_1, \ldots, q_j)$ but $a_j \notin \text{span}(q_{j+1}, \ldots, q_n)$ — column $j$ of $A$ is expressed only using the first $j$ columns of $Q$. That is exactly upper triangularity.

---

## QR and Least Squares

This is the payoff. Given $A = QR$, the least squares solution to $Ax = b$ satisfies the normal equations:

$$A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$$ $$(QR)^T(QR)\hat{x} = (QR)^T b$$ $$R^T Q^T Q R \hat{x} = R^T Q^T b$$ $$R^T R \hat{x} = R^T Q^T b$$

Since $R$ is invertible (positive diagonal), cancel $R^T$:

$$\boxed{R\hat{x} = Q^T b}$$

This is an upper triangular system — solved by back-substitution in $O(n^2)$ operations. No $A^T A$ is ever formed. The condition number of $R$ equals the condition number of $A$, not its square. This is the numerically correct approach to least squares.

The projection of $b$ onto $C(A)$ is $\hat{b} = A\hat{x} = QR\hat{x} = Q(Q^T b) = QQ^T b$ — confirming the formula from the previous note.

---

## Uniqueness of QR

If we require $R$ to have positive diagonal entries, the factorization $A = QR$ is unique. The positivity of the diagonal fixes the sign of each $q_j$ (Gram-Schmidt produces $q_j$ up to sign; requiring $r_{jj} = |z_j| > 0$ pins it down).

If $A$ is $m \times n$ with $m > n$ (the usual overdetermined case), $Q$ is $m \times n$ with orthonormal columns and $R$ is $n \times n$ upper triangular. This is the **thin QR** or **economy QR** factorization. One can extend $Q$ to a full $m \times m$ orthogonal matrix by appending $m - n$ additional orthonormal vectors, but for least squares the thin version is what matters.

---

## Connection to $LU$

Both $LU$ and $QR$ are factorizations that triangularize $A$, but in fundamentally different ways:

- $LU$: $A = LU$ uses elimination — subtracts multiples of rows. Preserves the column space of leading submatrices, but destroys orthogonality.
- $QR$: $A = QR$ uses orthogonalization — subtracts projections. Preserves the column space of leading submatrices _and_ produces orthonormal columns.

$LU$ is faster to compute ($O(\frac{2}{3}n^3)$ vs $O(2mn^2 - \frac{2}{3}n^3)$ for Gram-Schmidt) but numerically less stable for least squares. $QR$ is the right tool when stability matters.

## Things to Remember (for Myself)

- Gram-Schmidt at step $j$: subtract projections onto all previous $q_i$'s, then normalize. The residual $z_j$ cannot be zero if the columns are independent.
- $r_{jj} = |z_j|$ is always positive. If it comes out zero, $a_j$ was dependent on the previous columns — stop.
- $R$ is upper triangular because $a_j$ only involves $q_1, \ldots, q_j$. Lower entries are zero by construction.
- The key formula for least squares via QR: $R\hat{x} = Q^T b$. Solve by back-substitution. Never form $A^T A$.
- $Q^T Q = I$ always (thin $Q$). $QQ^T = I$ only when $Q$ is square.
- Gram-Schmidt is numerically unstable in its basic form — modified Gram-Schmidt (subtracting projections one at a time rather than all at once) is the practical version. Householder reflections are even better. But conceptually, they all produce the same $QR$.
