
## The Geometric Idea

Take a vector $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$ and a line through the origin spanned by some nonzero vector $a \in \mathbb{R}^m$. I want to find the point on that line _closest_ to $b$.

That closest point must be some scalar multiple of $a$. Call it $\hat{x} a$. The error vector — the piece of $b$ left unaccounted for — is

$$e = b - \hat{x} a$$

The geometric condition for "closest" is that $e$ is **perpendicular to $a$**. If $e$ had any component along $a$, I could reduce the distance by sliding further along the line. So the condition is:

$$a \cdot e = 0 \implies a^T(b - \hat{x} a) = 0$$

Everything flows from that single equation.

---

## Deriving the Projection

Expand:

$$a^T b - \hat{x}, a^T a = 0$$

$$\hat{x} = \frac{a^T b}{a^T a}$$

The **projection of $b$ onto the line spanned by $a$** is:

$$p = \hat{x}, a = \frac{a^T b}{a^T a}, a$$

The scalar $\hat{x}$ is well-defined as long as $a \neq 0$, since $a^T a = |a|^2 > 0$.

---

## The Projection Matrix

I want to write the projection as a matrix acting on $b$. Factor out $b$:

$$p = a \frac{a^T b}{a^T a} = \frac{a, a^T}{a^T a}, b$$

So the **projection matrix onto the line spanned by $a$** is:

$$\boxed{P = \frac{a, a^T}{a^T a}}$$

Note the order carefully. $a^T a$ is a scalar (inner product). $a, a^T$ is an $m \times m$ matrix (outer product). $P$ is $m \times m$, rank 1.

---

## A Full Example

Let $a = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 2 \end{pmatrix}$, $b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

**Step 1.** Compute the scalar:

$$\hat{x} = \frac{a^T b}{a^T a} = \frac{(1)(1)+(2)(0)+(2)(0)}{1^2+2^2+2^2} = \frac{1}{9}$$

**Step 2.** The projection:

$$p = \frac{1}{9}\begin{pmatrix}1\2\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1/9\2/9\2/9\end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 3.** The error:

$$e = b - p = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\0\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1/9\2/9\2/9\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}8/9\-2/9\-2/9\end{pmatrix}$$

**Check perpendicularity:**

$$a^T e = (1)(8/9) + (2)(-2/9) + (2)(-2/9) = \frac{8 - 4 - 4}{9} = 0 \checkmark$$

**Step 4.** The projection matrix:

$$P = \frac{a a^T}{a^T a} = \frac{1}{9} \begin{pmatrix}1\2\2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 & 2\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{9}\begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 & 2\2 & 4 & 4\2 & 4 & 4\end{pmatrix}$$

$$= \begin{pmatrix}1/9 & 2/9 & 2/9\2/9 & 4/9 & 4/9\2/9 & 4/9 & 4/9\end{pmatrix}$$

Verify: $Pb = \begin{pmatrix}1/9\2/9\2/9\end{pmatrix}$. Correct.

---

## Properties of $P$

These are not just facts to memorize — each one has a clean reason.

**1. $P$ is symmetric: $P^T = P$**

$$P^T = \left(\frac{a a^T}{a^T a}\right)^T = \frac{(a a^T)^T}{a^T a} = \frac{a a^T}{a^T a} = P$$

because $(aa^T)^T = (a^T)^T a^T = a a^T$.

**2. $P$ is idempotent: $P^2 = P$**

$$P^2 = \frac{a a^T}{a^T a} \cdot \frac{a a^T}{a^T a} = \frac{a(a^T a)a^T}{(a^T a)^2} = \frac{a a^T}{a^T a} = P$$

The middle step uses $a^T a$ as a scalar that factors out. Geometrically: projecting twice gives the same result as projecting once. Once $b$ lands on the line, it stays there.

**3. $P$ has rank 1**

The column space of $P$ is exactly the line spanned by $a$. Every column of $a a^T$ is a scalar multiple of $a$.

**4. Eigenvalues of $P$ are $1$ and $0$**

- Any vector along $a$ satisfies $Pa = a$, so $\lambda = 1$ with eigenvector $a$.
- Any vector $v$ perpendicular to $a$ satisfies $Pv = 0$, so $\lambda = 0$ with eigenspace $a^\perp$.

The eigenvalue $1$ has multiplicity $1$ (the line), and $\lambda = 0$ has multiplicity $m-1$ (the orthogonal complement). This matches $\text{rank}(P) = 1$ and $\text{nullity}(P) = m-1$.

**5. $I - P$ is also a projection**

$I - P$ projects onto the orthogonal complement $a^\perp$. It has the same symmetry and idempotency:

$$(I-P)^2 = I - 2P + P^2 = I - 2P + P = I - P$$

$$(I-P)^T = I^T - P^T = I - P$$

And for any $b$: $b = Pb + (I-P)b = p + e$. This is the orthogonal decomposition of $b$ into its component along $a$ and its component perpendicular to $a$.

---

## Why $P$ is Rank 1 (and Why That Matters)

$P = \frac{aa^T}{a^Ta}$ is an outer product divided by a scalar. The column space of $P$ is $\text{span}(a)$ and the row space is $\text{span}(a^T)$ — both 1-dimensional. So $\text{rank}(P) = 1$.

This means $P$ collapses all of $\mathbb{R}^m$ onto a single line. The null space of $P$ is the entire orthogonal complement $a^\perp$, which has dimension $m-1$. The rank-nullity theorem is satisfied: $1 + (m-1) = m$.

---

## The Decomposition $b = p + e$

Every vector $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$ decomposes uniquely as:

$$b = \underbrace{Pb}_{p \in \text{span}(a)} + \underbrace{(I-P)b}_{e \in a^\perp}$$

This is precisely the direct sum decomposition

$$\mathbb{R}^m = \text{span}(a) \oplus a^\perp$$

from the previous note. The two subspaces $\text{span}(a)$ and $a^\perp$ have trivial intersection (one is 1-dimensional, the other is $(m-1)$-dimensional, and they are orthogonal), so their bases combine to form a basis for all of $\mathbb{R}^m$. The projection $P$ is the map that extracts the $\text{span}(a)$ component.

---

## Connection to Least Squares

Projecting onto a line is the simplest instance of least squares. If I have the system

$$ax = b, \quad a \in \mathbb{R}^m,\ x \in \mathbb{R}$$

with $m > 1$, this is overdetermined — generically no solution. The least squares solution $\hat{x}$ minimizes $|ax - b|^2$, and it is exactly

$$\hat{x} = \frac{a^T b}{a^T a}$$

the same formula derived above. The projection $p = a\hat{x}$ is the closest point in $\text{col}(A) = \text{span}(a)$ to $b$. When we move to projecting onto higher-dimensional subspaces (the columns of a full matrix $A$), the formula generalizes to the normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$, and the projection matrix becomes $P = A(A^TA)^{-1}A^T$ — but the geometry is identical.

---

## Things to Remember (for Myself)

- The key equation is $a^T e = 0$. Perpendicularity of the error to the subspace is the definition of projection, not just a consequence.
- $P = aa^T / a^Ta$: outer product on top, inner product on the bottom. Easy to confuse — keep the dimensions straight. $a$ is $m \times 1$, $a^T$ is $1 \times m$, so $aa^T$ is $m \times m$ and $a^Ta$ is $1 \times 1$.
- $P^2 = P$ and $P^T = P$ are the two characterizing properties of an orthogonal projection matrix. Any matrix satisfying both is an orthogonal projection onto its own column space.
- The complement $I - P$ is just as important as $P$ itself. The decomposition $b = Pb + (I-P)b$ is always orthogonal.
