
## Why Projection Exists at All

The problem that forces projection into existence: given a subspace $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$ and a vector $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$, find the vector in $S$ closest to $b$.

This comes up immediately in systems $Ax = b$ that have no solution. The column space $C(A)$ does not contain $b$. We cannot solve exactly — so we ask instead: which vector in $C(A)$ is nearest to $b$? That nearest vector is the projection of $b$ onto $C(A)$.

Everything in this note flows from that one geometric question.

---

## Projection onto a Line First

Before subspaces, do the one-dimensional case. Let $S = \text{span}{a}$ for some nonzero $a \in \mathbb{R}^m$. We want $p = \hat{x} a$ (some scalar multiple of $a$) such that the error $e = b - p$ is perpendicular to $a$.

The condition $a \perp e$:

$$a^T(b - \hat{x} a) = 0$$ $$a^T b - \hat{x} a^T a = 0$$ $$\hat{x} = \frac{a^T b}{a^T a}$$

So the projection is

$$p = a \hat{x} = a \frac{a^T b}{a^T a} = \frac{a a^T}{a^T a} b$$

The matrix $P = \dfrac{a a^T}{a^T a}$ is the **projection matrix** onto $\text{span}{a}$.

Notice: $a a^T$ is an $m \times m$ matrix (outer product), while $a^T a$ is a scalar. The ratio is well-defined.

---

## Projection onto a Subspace $S = C(A)$

Now let $S$ be a $k$-dimensional subspace, with basis vectors as the columns of $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times k}$ (so $A$ has full column rank, $\text{rank}(A) = k$).

We want $p \in C(A)$, so $p = A\hat{x}$ for some $\hat{x} \in \mathbb{R}^k$. The error $e = b - A\hat{x}$ must be perpendicular to every vector in $C(A)$, i.e., perpendicular to each column of $A$:

$$A^T(b - A\hat{x}) = 0$$

This is the **normal equation**:

$$\boxed{A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b}$$

When $A$ has full column rank, $A^T A$ is invertible (I will prove this below). So:

$$\hat{x} = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T b$$

$$p = A\hat{x} = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T b$$

The **projection matrix** onto $C(A)$ is:

$$\boxed{P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T}$$

---

## Why $A^T A$ is Invertible When $A$ Has Full Column Rank

This is worth proving carefully because it is used everywhere.

**Claim:** If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times k}$ has full column rank (columns are linearly independent), then $A^T A$ is invertible.

**Proof.** It suffices to show $N(A^T A) = {0}$. Suppose $A^T A x = 0$. Then:

$$x^T A^T A x = 0 \implies (Ax)^T(Ax) = 0 \implies |Ax|^2 = 0 \implies Ax = 0$$

Since $A$ has full column rank, $N(A) = {0}$, so $x = 0$. $\square$

This argument is clean and worth memorizing: $A^T A x = 0 \Rightarrow Ax = 0 \Rightarrow x = 0$.

---

## Properties of the Projection Matrix

Let $P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$. Then:

**1. $P$ is symmetric:** $$P^T = \left(A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T\right)^T = A \left((A^T A)^{-1}\right)^T A^T = A(A^T A)^{-T} A^T$$ Since $A^T A$ is symmetric, $(A^T A)^{-T} = (A^T A)^{-1}$, so $P^T = P$.

**2. $P$ is idempotent ($P^2 = P$):** $$P^2 = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T \cdot A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T = A(A^T A)^{-1} \underbrace{(A^T A)(A^T A)^{-1}}_{I} A^T = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T = P$$

Idempotency is the algebraic expression of the geometric fact that projecting twice does nothing: if $b$ is already in $S$, its projection is itself.

**3. $Pb = b$ if and only if $b \in C(A)$:** If $b = Ax$ for some $x$, then $Pb = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T A x = A x = b$.

**4. $Pb = 0$ if and only if $b \in N(A^T)$:** If $A^T b = 0$, then $Pb = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T b = 0$.

Properties 3 and 4 confirm that $P$ projects onto $C(A)$ and kills $N(A^T)$.

---

## The Complementary Projection

Define $P^\perp = I - P$. This is the projection onto the orthogonal complement of $C(A)$, which is $N(A^T)$.

Check: $(I - P)^2 = I - 2P + P^2 = I - 2P + P = I - P$. Idempotent. And $(I-P)^T = I - P^T = I - P$. Symmetric.

Every $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$ decomposes uniquely:

$$b = \underbrace{Pb}_{p \in C(A)} + \underbrace{(I-P)b}_{e \in N(A^T)}$$

This is exactly the direct sum decomposition $\mathbb{R}^m = C(A) \oplus N(A^T)$ from the fundamental theorem of linear algebra. The projection is the mechanism that makes this decomposition _constructive_ — given $b$, it tells you what $p$ and $e$ are.

## A Full Worked Example

Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$A$ has 3 rows, 2 columns, rank 2. We want to project $b$ onto $C(A)$.

**Step 1: Compute $A^T A$.**

$$A^T A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 1 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1\cdot1+1\cdot1+1\cdot1 & 1\cdot0+1\cdot1+1\cdot2 \ 0\cdot1+1\cdot1+2\cdot1 & 0\cdot0+1\cdot1+2\cdot2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 3 \ 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Compute $A^T b$.**

$$A^T b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \begin{pmatrix} 6 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Solve the normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$.**

$$\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 3 \ 3 & 5 \end{pmatrix} \hat{x} = \begin{pmatrix} 6 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$(A^T A)^{-1} = \dfrac{1}{15-9}\begin{pmatrix}5 & -3 \ -3 & 3\end{pmatrix} = \dfrac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}5 & -3 \ -3 & 3\end{pmatrix}$

$$\hat{x} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}5 & -3 \ -3 & 3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}6\0\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}30\-18\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}5\-3\end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 4: The projection.**

$$p = A\hat{x} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\1&1\1&2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}5\-3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}5\2\-1\end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 5: The error.**

$$e = b - p = \begin{pmatrix}6\0\0\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}5\2\-1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\-2\1\end{pmatrix}$$

**Verify $e \perp C(A)$:** check $A^T e = 0$:

$$A^T e = \begin{pmatrix}1&1&1\0&1&2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1\-2\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1-2+1\0-2+2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\0\end{pmatrix} \checkmark$$

---

## Connection to Least Squares

The system $Ax = b$ with $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times k}$, $m > k$ is overdetermined — more equations than unknowns, generically no solution. Least squares asks: minimize $|Ax - b|^2$.

The minimizer $\hat{x}$ satisfies the normal equations $A^T A \hat{x} = A^T b$ — exactly what we derived above. The geometric reason: $|Ax - b|^2$ is minimized when $Ax$ is as close to $b$ as possible, i.e., when $Ax$ is the projection of $b$ onto $C(A)$.

So least squares _is_ projection. The normal equations are not some separate algebraic trick; they are the condition $e \perp C(A)$ written in coordinates.

---

## Orthonormal Columns Make Everything Simpler

If the columns of $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times k}$ are orthonormal ($Q^T Q = I_k$), the projection formula collapses beautifully:

$$P = Q(Q^T Q)^{-1} Q^T = Q I_k Q^T = QQ^T$$

The normal equations become $\hat{x} = Q^T b$: the coefficients are just dot products of $b$ with each basis vector. This is why orthonormal bases are so desirable computationally — no matrix inversion, no solving a system.

When $Q$ is square ($k = m$, so $Q$ is an orthogonal matrix), $P = QQ^T = I$. Projecting onto all of $\mathbb{R}^m$ is the identity. This makes sense.

## The Projection Matrix Explicitly in $\mathbb{R}^3$

For the example above, let me write out $P$ fully to see what it looks like.

$$P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\1&1\1&2\end{pmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}5&-3\-3&3\end{pmatrix} \cdot \begin{pmatrix}1&1&1\0&1&2\end{pmatrix}$$

First compute $A(A^T A)^{-1}$:

$$\frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}1&0\1&1\1&2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}5&-3\-3&3\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}5&-3\2&0\-1&3\end{pmatrix}$$

Then multiply by $A^T$:

$$P = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}5&-3\2&0\-1&3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&1&1\0&1&2\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}5&2&-1\2&2&2\-1&2&5\end{pmatrix}$$

Verify $P$ is symmetric: yes. Verify $P^2 = P$: left as an exercise to myself the first time I actually doubt it.

Check $Pb = p$:

$$\frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}5&2&-1\2&2&2\-1&2&5\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}6\0\0\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{6}\begin{pmatrix}30\12\-6\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}5\2\-1\end{pmatrix} = p \checkmark$$

## What I Need to Never Confuse

**The formula $P = A(A^T A)^{-1} A^T$ requires $A$ to have full column rank.** If the columns are dependent, $A^T A$ is singular and this formula breaks down. In that case, either reduce to a linearly independent spanning set first, or use the pseudoinverse.

**$P$ is not $A^{-1}$ or anything like it.** $A$ is not even square in the typical projection setup. $P$ is $m \times m$, projecting vectors in $\mathbb{R}^m$ onto a subspace of $\mathbb{R}^m$.

**The normal equations are not an approximation.** They give the _exact_ minimizer of $|Ax - b|^2$. "Least squares" sometimes sounds like an approximation method; it is actually an exact solution to a different (and well-posed) problem.

---

## Characterization of Projection Matrices

Conversely: if $P \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times m}$ satisfies $P^2 = P$ and $P^T = P$, then $P$ is the orthogonal projection onto $C(P)$.

Any idempotent matrix ($P^2 = P$) is a projection onto $C(P)$ along $N(P)$, but without symmetry the subspaces $C(P)$ and $N(P)$ need not be orthogonal. The symmetry condition $P^T = P$ is precisely what forces the projection to be _orthogonal_.

So the two conditions $P^2 = P$ and $P = P^T$ together are equivalent to "$P$ is an orthogonal projection." This is a clean characterization I want to remember.
