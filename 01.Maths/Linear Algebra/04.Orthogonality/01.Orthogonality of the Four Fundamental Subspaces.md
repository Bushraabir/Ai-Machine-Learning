
## The Core Theorem

For any $m \times n$ matrix $A$:

$$ C(A^T) \perp N(A) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{R}^n $$

$$ C(A) \perp N(A^T) \quad \text{in } \mathbb{R}^m $$

And more than just orthogonality — they are **orthogonal complements**:

$$ C(A^T) \oplus N(A) = \mathbb{R}^n $$

$$ C(A) \oplus N(A^T) = \mathbb{R}^m $$

The symbol $\oplus$ means direct sum: every vector in the ambient space decomposes uniquely as a sum of one vector from each subspace, and the two components are orthogonal. The row space and nullspace do not merely fail to overlap — they are perpendicular and together they fill $\mathbb{R}^n$ completely.

---

## Proof: Row Space is Orthogonal to Nullspace

**Claim:** If $x \in N(A)$ and $v \in C(A^T)$, then $x \perp v$, meaning $v^T x = 0$.

**Proof:** Since $v \in C(A^T)$, we can write $v = A^T y$ for some $y \in \mathbb{R}^m$.

Since $x \in N(A)$, we have $Ax = 0$.

Compute:

$$ v^T x = (A^T y)^T x = y^T A x = y^T \cdot 0 = 0 $$

Done. $\blacksquare$

This proof is three lines and uses only the definition of the two subspaces and one transpose identity. The key step is $(A^T y)^T = y^T A$. The rest is definitions.

The same argument with $A$ replaced by $A^T$ gives $C(A) \perp N(A^T)$.

---

## Orthogonal Complement: The Precise Statement

Two subspaces $V$ and $W$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$ are **orthogonal complements** if:

1. Every vector in $V$ is orthogonal to every vector in $W$: $v \perp w$ for all $v \in V$, $w \in W$
2. Their dimensions add to $n$: $\dim V + \dim W = n$

Condition 1 alone is orthogonality. Condition 2 is what makes them complements — together they fill the whole space. Both conditions are needed.

For our four subspaces:

- $C(A^T)$ and $N(A)$ satisfy condition 1 (proved above) and condition 2 ($r + (n-r) = n$). So they are orthogonal complements in $\mathbb{R}^n$.
- $C(A)$ and $N(A^T)$ satisfy condition 1 (same argument) and condition 2 ($r + (m-r) = m$). So they are orthogonal complements in $\mathbb{R}^m$.

The dimension count is where [[Dimension of a Vector Space]] and [[The Four Fundamental Subspaces]] are doing their work silently in the background.

---

## What Orthogonal Complement Means Concretely

If $V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is any subspace, its orthogonal complement is

$$ V^\perp = { w \in \mathbb{R}^n : w \cdot v = 0 \text{ for all } v \in V } $$

$V^\perp$ is always a subspace (check: if $w_1, w_2 \perp V$ then $w_1 + w_2 \perp V$; if $w \perp V$ then $cw \perp V$). And:

$$ \dim V + \dim V^\perp = n $$

$$ (V^\perp)^\perp = V $$

$$ V \cap V^\perp = {0} $$

The last fact: if $v \in V$ and $v \in V^\perp$, then $v \perp v$, so $v \cdot v = |v|^2 = 0$, so $v = 0$. The only vector orthogonal to itself (in real vector spaces) is zero.

Our theorem says:

$$ N(A) = C(A^T)^\perp \qquad \text{and} \qquad C(A^T) = N(A)^\perp $$

$$ N(A^T) = C(A)^\perp \qquad \text{and} \qquad C(A) = N(A^T)^\perp $$

## A Worked Verification

Take the running matrix:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 2 & 2 \ 2 & 4 & 6 & 8 \ 3 & 6 & 8 & 10 \end{pmatrix} $$

From [[The Four Fundamental Subspaces]], the four bases are:

**Row space $C(A^T)$** (nonzero rows of $R$):

$$ r_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 0 \ -2 \end{pmatrix}, \quad r_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 1 \ 2 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Nullspace $N(A)$** (special solutions):

$$ n_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -2 \ 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad n_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ -2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Check all four dot products — every row space vector against every nullspace vector:

$$ r_1 \cdot n_1 = (1)(-2) + (2)(1) + (0)(0) + (-2)(0) = -2 + 2 + 0 + 0 = 0 \checkmark $$

$$ r_1 \cdot n_2 = (1)(2) + (2)(0) + (0)(-2) + (-2)(1) = 2 + 0 + 0 - 2 = 0 \checkmark $$

$$ r_2 \cdot n_1 = (0)(-2) + (0)(1) + (1)(0) + (2)(0) = 0 \checkmark $$

$$ r_2 \cdot n_2 = (0)(2) + (0)(0) + (1)(-2) + (2)(1) = 0 - 2 + 2 = 0 \checkmark $$

All four are zero. The orthogonality is verified concretely.

**Column space $C(A)$** (pivot columns of $A$):

$$ c_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad c_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 6 \ 8 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Left nullspace $N(A^T)$**:

$$ l_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 \ -1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Check:

$$ c_1 \cdot l_1 = (1)(-1) + (2)(-1) + (3)(1) = -1 - 2 + 3 = 0 \checkmark $$

$$ c_2 \cdot l_1 = (2)(-1) + (6)(-1) + (8)(1) = -2 - 6 + 8 = 0 \checkmark $$

All zero. The column space is orthogonal to the left nullspace.

## The Decomposition of Any Vector

Since $C(A^T)$ and $N(A)$ are orthogonal complements in $\mathbb{R}^n$, every vector $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ decomposes uniquely as:

$$ x = x_r + x_n, \qquad x_r \in C(A^T), \quad x_n \in N(A), \quad x_r \perp x_n $$

This decomposition is unique and the components are perpendicular. The map $x \mapsto x_r$ is the orthogonal projection onto $C(A^T)$; the map $x \mapsto x_n$ is the orthogonal projection onto $N(A)$.

When $A$ acts on $x$:

$$ Ax = A(x_r + x_n) = Ax_r + Ax_n = Ax_r + 0 = Ax_r $$

The nullspace component $x_n$ is invisible to $A$ — it is annihilated. Only the row space component $x_r$ contributes to $Ax$. Furthermore, the map $x_r \mapsto Ax_r$ is a bijection from $C(A^T)$ to $C(A)$ — this restriction of $A$ to the row space is invertible. This is the precise sense in which $A$ acts nicely: it is invertible on the row space and kills the nullspace, and these two actions are completely orthogonal.

Similarly, every $b \in \mathbb{R}^m$ decomposes as $b = b_c + b_l$ with $b_c \in C(A)$ and $b_l \in N(A^T)$. The system $Ax = b$ is solvable if and only if $b_l = 0$, i.e., $b$ has no component in the left nullspace.

---

## Why This Is Not Obvious

It might seem like any two subspaces whose dimensions add to $n$ should be orthogonal complements. This is wrong.

Take $\mathbb{R}^2$. Let $V = \text{span}{(1,0)^T}$ and $W = \text{span}{(1,1)^T}$. Then $\dim V + \dim W = 1 + 1 = 2$. But $(1,0) \cdot (1,1) = 1 \neq 0$. They are not orthogonal. They still span $\mathbb{R}^2$ (any vector can be written as a combination), but not as an orthogonal decomposition.

What makes $C(A^T)$ and $N(A)$ special is that the orthogonality is forced by the matrix equation $Ax = 0$ — the algebraic definition of the nullspace creates the geometric orthogonality. That is the content of the three-line proof above. The dimensions then confirm they are complements, not just orthogonal subspaces.

---

## The Consistency Condition Revisited

The condition for $Ax = b$ to be solvable: $b \in C(A)$.

Since $C(A) = N(A^T)^\perp$, this is equivalent to: $b \perp y$ for every $y \in N(A^T)$.

In other words: $Ax = b$ is consistent if and only if $y^T b = 0$ for every $y$ satisfying $A^T y = 0$.

For our matrix, the only basis vector of $N(A^T)$ is $l_1 = (-1,-1,1)^T$. So the condition is:

$$ l_1^T b = -b_1 - b_2 + b_3 = 0 \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad b_3 = b_1 + b_2 $$

This is the same condition that appeared as a zero row in the augmented matrix during elimination in [[All Solutions to Ax=b]]. Now it has a geometric meaning: $b$ must be orthogonal to the left nullspace, i.e., $b$ must lie in the column space.

The elimination found this condition mechanically. The four subspaces explain why it exists.

## The Full Orthogonality Picture

$$ \mathbb{R}^n: \quad C(A^T) \perp N(A), \quad \dim C(A^T) = r, \quad \dim N(A) = n - r $$

$$ \mathbb{R}^m: \quad C(A) \perp N(A^T), \quad \dim C(A) = r, \quad \dim N(A^T) = m - r $$

```
        R^n                              R^m
  ┌──────────────────┐            ┌──────────────────┐
  │                  │            │                  │
  │  C(A^T)          │            │  C(A)            │
  │  dim = r         │──── A ────▶│  dim = r         │
  │                  │            │                  │
  │  ⊥               │            │  ⊥               │
  │                  │            │                  │
  │  N(A)            │            │  N(A^T)          │
  │  dim = n - r     │──── A ────▶│  dim = m - r     │
  │                  │    (→ 0)   │  (= {0} output)  │
  └──────────────────┘            └──────────────────┘
```

$A$ maps the row space bijectively onto the column space. $A$ collapses the nullspace to zero. The left nullspace receives nothing from $A$ — it is the part of $\mathbb{R}^m$ that no input can reach.
