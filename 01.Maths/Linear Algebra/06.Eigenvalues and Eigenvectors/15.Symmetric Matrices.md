## The Object

A matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ is **symmetric** if $A^T = A$, meaning $a_{ij} = a_{ji}$ for all $i, j$. Written out for $n = 3$:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} & a_{13} \ a_{12} & a_{22} & a_{23} \ a_{13} & a_{23} & a_{33} \end{pmatrix}$$

The entries below the diagonal are the mirror image of the entries above it. There are $\frac{n(n+1)}{2}$ free parameters, not $n^2$.

Symmetric matrices are everywhere: covariance matrices in statistics, stiffness matrices in mechanics, Laplacians in graph theory, Hessians in optimization, and the kernel of every least-squares problem $A^TA$. The reason they dominate applications is the spectral theorem: symmetric matrices are the matrices that are perfectly diagonalizable, with orthogonal eigenvectors and real eigenvalues. No defective eigenvalues, no complex numbers, no Jordan blocks. Everything is clean.

---

## The Spectral Theorem

**Theorem (Spectral Theorem for Real Symmetric Matrices).** If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ is symmetric, then:

1. All eigenvalues of $A$ are real.
2. Eigenvectors corresponding to distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal.
3. There exists an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$ consisting entirely of eigenvectors of $A$.

Equivalently: $A$ can be orthogonally diagonalized,

$$A = Q\Lambda Q^T$$

where $Q$ is orthogonal ($Q^TQ = I$, columns are orthonormal eigenvectors) and $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$ with all $\lambda_i \in \mathbb{R}$.

This is the most important theorem in applied linear algebra. Every structured matrix decomposition in data science --- PCA, SVD, spectral clustering --- runs through this theorem.

## Proof of the Spectral Theorem

I want to prove all three parts carefully. This is worth doing once, completely.

### Part 1: Eigenvalues are real

Let $\lambda \in \mathbb{C}$ be an eigenvalue with eigenvector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{C}^n$, $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$.

Take the inner product of $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$ with $\mathbf{x}$ over $\mathbb{C}$:

$$\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A\mathbf{x} = \lambda \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T\mathbf{x} = \lambda|\mathbf{x}|^2$$

Now take the conjugate transpose of the left side. Since $A$ is real and symmetric, $A^T = A$ and $\bar{A} = A$, so $A^* = \bar{A}^T = A$:

$$\overline{\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^T A^* \bar{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^T A\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \overline{\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A\mathbf{x}}$$

More directly: $\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A\mathbf{x}$ is a $1\times 1$ matrix (a scalar). Its conjugate is $(\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A\mathbf{x})^* = \mathbf{x}^T A^T \bar{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^T A\bar{\mathbf{x}}$.

But also $\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A\mathbf{x} = \overline{\mathbf{x}^T A\bar{\mathbf{x}}}$... let me be cleaner.

Write $s = \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{C}$. Take its complex conjugate:

$$\bar{s} = \overline{\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^T\overline{A\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^T A\bar{\mathbf{x}} = (A\mathbf{x})^T\bar{\mathbf{x}} \cdot \text{[wrong direction]}$$

Let me use the cleaner route. Since $A = A^T$ and $A$ is real:

$$\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T(A\mathbf{x}) = \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T(\lambda\mathbf{x}) = \lambda|\mathbf{x}|^2$$

$$(A\mathbf{x})^T\bar{\mathbf{x}} = (A^T\mathbf{x})^T\bar{\mathbf{x}} = (A\mathbf{x})^T\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \lambda(\mathbf{x})^T\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \lambda|\mathbf{x}|^2$$

But $(A\mathbf{x})^T\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^TA^T\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^TA\bar{\mathbf{x}}$. And $\overline{\bar{\mathbf{x}}^TA\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^T\overline{A},\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \mathbf{x}^TA\bar{\mathbf{x}}$ (since $A$ is real). So $\bar{s} = s$, meaning $s$ is real. But $s = \lambda|\mathbf{x}|^2$ and $|\mathbf{x}|^2 > 0$, so $\lambda$ is real. $\square$

### Part 2: Eigenvectors for distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal

Let $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$ and $A\mathbf{y} = \mu\mathbf{y}$ with $\lambda \neq \mu$, both real.

$$\lambda(\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{y}) = (A\mathbf{x})\cdot\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}^TA^T\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}\cdot(A\mathbf{y}) = \mu(\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{y})$$

So $(\lambda - \mu)(\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{y}) = 0$. Since $\lambda \neq \mu$, we get $\mathbf{x}\cdot\mathbf{y} = 0$. $\square$

The key step was the symmetry of $A$: it allows us to move $A$ from acting on $\mathbf{x}$ to acting on $\mathbf{y}$, picking up the other eigenvalue. Symmetry is the algebraic reason eigenvectors are orthogonal.

### Part 3: Orthonormal basis of eigenvectors

This requires showing that even repeated eigenvalues have a full set of orthonormal eigenvectors, i.e., no Jordan blocks occur. The proof is by induction on $n$.

**Base case:** $n = 1$. Trivial.

**Inductive step:** Assume every $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ symmetric matrix has an orthonormal eigenbasis. Let $\lambda_1$ be any eigenvalue of $A$ (real by Part 1) and $\mathbf{q}_1$ a unit eigenvector. Extend to an orthonormal basis ${\mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_n}$ of $\mathbb{R}^n$.

In this basis, $A$ becomes $Q_1^TAQ_1$ where $Q_1 = [\mathbf{q}_1;\mathbf{v}_2;\cdots;\mathbf{v}_n]$. Compute the first column:

$$Q_1^TAQ_1\mathbf{e}_1 = Q_1^TA\mathbf{q}_1 = Q_1^T(\lambda_1\mathbf{q}_1) = \lambda_1\mathbf{e}_1$$

So the first column of $Q_1^TAQ_1$ is $\lambda_1\mathbf{e}_1$. Since $Q_1^TAQ_1$ is still symmetric ($(Q_1^TAQ_1)^T = Q_1^TA^TQ_1 = Q_1^TAQ_1$), the first row must also be $\lambda_1\mathbf{e}_1^T$. Therefore:

$$Q_1^TAQ_1 = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_1 & \mathbf{0}^T \ \mathbf{0} & B\end{pmatrix}$$

where $B$ is an $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ symmetric matrix. By induction, $B$ has an orthonormal eigenbasis. Lifting back to $\mathbb{R}^n$ gives the full orthonormal eigenbasis of $A$. $\square$

The crucial point: symmetric matrices are **never defective**. A repeated eigenvalue of a symmetric matrix always has a full geometric eigenspace equal to its algebraic multiplicity. Jordan blocks of size $\geq 2$ cannot occur. This is what makes symmetric matrices the nicest matrices in existence.

## The Spectral Decomposition

From $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ with $Q = [\mathbf{q}_1 \cdots \mathbf{q}_n]$:

$$A = \lambda_1\mathbf{q}_1\mathbf{q}_1^T + \lambda_2\mathbf{q}_2\mathbf{q}_2^T + \cdots + \lambda_n\mathbf{q}_n\mathbf{q}_n^T = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$$

Each term $\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$ is a rank-1 orthogonal projection onto the span of $\mathbf{q}_i$. The identity $\sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T = QQ^T = I$ says these projections partition the identity: every vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is decomposed into $n$ orthogonal components, each scaled by the corresponding eigenvalue.

This is the spectral decomposition. It is the most explicit possible statement of what $A$ does: it stretches along $\mathbf{q}_1$ by $\lambda_1$, along $\mathbf{q}_2$ by $\lambda_2$, and so on, in $n$ mutually orthogonal directions.

**Applying a function to $A$.** Since $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$:

$$f(A) = Qf(\Lambda)Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^n f(\lambda_i)\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$$

for any function $f$ applied to the eigenvalues. For example:

$$A^k = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i^k,\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T, \qquad e^{At} = \sum_{i=1}^n e^{\lambda_i t},\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$$

$$A^{-1} = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\lambda_i},\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T \quad (\text{if all } \lambda_i \neq 0), \qquad A^{1/2} = \sum_{i=1}^n \sqrt{\lambda_i},\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T \quad (\text{if all } \lambda_i \geq 0)$$

The last formula is the square root of a positive semidefinite matrix, well-defined and unique. The spectral decomposition makes every matrix function trivial to write down for symmetric matrices.

## Quadratic Forms

Every symmetric matrix defines a quadratic form

$$Q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = \sum_{i,j} a_{ij}x_ix_j$$

Since $a_{ij} = a_{ji}$, the cross terms $a_{ij}x_ix_j + a_{ji}x_jx_i = 2a_{ij}x_ix_j$ combine cleanly. The quadratic form is a real-valued function on $\mathbb{R}^n$ that is homogeneous of degree 2.

In the eigenvector basis $\mathbf{x} = \sum_i c_i\mathbf{q}_i$:

$$Q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = \left(\sum_i c_i\mathbf{q}_i\right)^T A\left(\sum_j c_j\mathbf{q}_j\right) = \sum_{i,j}c_ic_j\mathbf{q}_i^TA\mathbf{q}_j = \sum_{i,j}c_ic_j\lambda_j\mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{q}_j = \sum_i \lambda_i c_i^2$$

The quadratic form is a sum of squares $\sum\lambda_i c_i^2$ in the eigenvector basis. The eigenvalues are the coefficients. This is the **principal axis theorem**: every quadratic form can be written as a weighted sum of squares by rotating to the eigenvector basis.

The sign pattern of the eigenvalues determines the shape of the level sets ${\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = 1}$:

- All $\lambda_i > 0$: ellipsoid (stretched in eigenvector directions by $1/\sqrt{\lambda_i}$)
- All $\lambda_i < 0$: imaginary ellipsoid (no real solutions)
- Mixed signs: hyperboloid (saddle surface)

## Positive Definite Matrices

A symmetric matrix $A$ is **positive definite** (written $A \succ 0$) if

$$\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} > 0 \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \neq 0$$

From the spectral decomposition $Q(\mathbf{x}) = \sum\lambda_i c_i^2$, this holds if and only if all $\lambda_i > 0$.

**Five equivalent conditions** for a symmetric matrix $A$ to be positive definite:

1. All eigenvalues are positive: $\lambda_i > 0$ for all $i$.
2. $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$.
3. All $n$ leading principal minors are positive (Sylvester's criterion):

$$a_{11} > 0, \quad \det\begin{pmatrix}a_{11}&a_{12}\a_{12}&a_{22}\end{pmatrix} > 0, \quad \ldots, \quad \det(A) > 0$$

4. $A = R^TR$ for some matrix $R$ with independent columns (Cholesky factorization: $R$ can be taken upper triangular).
5. All pivots in Gaussian elimination are positive.

These five conditions are all equivalent. Condition 2 is the definition. Condition 1 is the spectral characterization. Condition 3 is the computational test. Condition 4 is the factorization. Condition 5 is the algorithm.

**The Cholesky factorization** $A = R^TR$ is the symmetric analogue of $LU$ decomposition, and it is twice as efficient: since $A$ is symmetric, only the upper triangular part needs to be stored, and the factorization exploits this to halve the work. It is the standard method for solving $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ when $A$ is known to be positive definite.

**Positive semidefinite** ($A \succeq 0$): replace strict inequalities with $\geq 0$. Allows zero eigenvalues.

## The $2\times 2$ Case in Full Detail

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}a & b \ b & d\end{pmatrix}$$

Characteristic polynomial: $\lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad - b^2) = 0$.

Eigenvalues:

$$\lambda = \frac{(a+d) \pm \sqrt{(a+d)^2 - 4(ad-b^2)}}{2} = \frac{(a+d) \pm \sqrt{(a-d)^2 + 4b^2}}{2}$$

The discriminant $(a-d)^2 + 4b^2 \geq 0$ always. So the eigenvalues are always real, as the spectral theorem guarantees. They are equal if and only if $a = d$ and $b = 0$, i.e., $A = aI$.

**Eigenvectors.** For eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = \frac{(a+d) + \sqrt{(a-d)^2+4b^2}}{2}$:

$$(A - \lambda_1 I)\mathbf{x} = \begin{pmatrix}a-\lambda_1 & b \ b & d - \lambda_1\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{x} = 0$$

The eigenvector is $\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}b \ \lambda_1 - a\end{pmatrix}$ (or any scalar multiple). Similarly for $\lambda_2$.

These eigenvectors are automatically orthogonal: $\mathbf{x}_1\cdot\mathbf{x}_2 = b\cdot b + (\lambda_1 - a)(\lambda_2 - a) = b^2 + \lambda_1\lambda_2 - a(\lambda_1+\lambda_2) + a^2$.

Using Vieta: $\lambda_1\lambda_2 = ad - b^2$ and $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = a + d$:

$$\mathbf{x}_1\cdot\mathbf{x}_2 = b^2 + (ad-b^2) - a(a+d) + a^2 = ad - a^2 - ad + a^2 = 0 \checkmark$$

**Positive definiteness test.** $A \succ 0$ iff $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 > 0$ iff $T = a + d > 0$ and $D = ad - b^2 > 0$.

Compare with [[Stability of 2x2 Systems]]: the stability condition $T < 0$, $D > 0$ is the negative definite condition $A \prec 0$ (all eigenvalues negative). A symmetric $A$ gives a stable system iff $A$ is negative definite.

## Worked Example: Full Diagonalization

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}3 & 1 \ 1 & 3\end{pmatrix}$$

$T = 6$, $D = 9 - 1 = 8$. Positive definite (both positive). Eigenvalues:

$$\lambda = \frac{6 \pm \sqrt{36 - 32}}{2} = \frac{6\pm 2}{2} = 4 \text{ or } 2$$

For $\lambda_1 = 4$: $(A - 4I) = \begin{pmatrix}-1&1\1&-1\end{pmatrix}$. Null space: $\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\1\end{pmatrix}$. Unit vector: $\mathbf{q}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\end{pmatrix}$.

For $\lambda_2 = 2$: $(A - 2I) = \begin{pmatrix}1&1\1&1\end{pmatrix}$. Null space: $\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\-1\end{pmatrix}$. Unit vector: $\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\end{pmatrix}$.

Check orthogonality: $\mathbf{q}_1\cdot\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{2}(1\cdot1 + 1\cdot(-1)) = 0$. Good.

$$Q = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\1&-1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \Lambda = \begin{pmatrix}4&0\0&2\end{pmatrix}$$

Verify $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$:

$$Q\Lambda Q^T = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\1&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}4&0\0&2\end{pmatrix}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\1&-1\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}4&2\4&-2\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\1&-1\end{pmatrix}$$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}4+2&4-2\4-2&4+2\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}6&2\2&6\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3&1\1&3\end{pmatrix} = A \checkmark$$

Spectral decomposition:

$$A = 4\cdot\frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\end{pmatrix} + 2\cdot\frac{1}{2}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&-1\end{pmatrix} = 2\begin{pmatrix}1&1\1&1\end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix}1&-1\-1&1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3&1\1&3\end{pmatrix} \checkmark$$

The matrix stretches by $4$ along the diagonal direction $(1,1)/\sqrt{2}$ and by $2$ along the anti-diagonal direction $(1,-1)/\sqrt{2}$. The level curves of $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = 1$ are an ellipse with semi-axes $1/\sqrt{4} = 1/2$ and $1/\sqrt{2}$ along these directions.

## Rayleigh Quotient

The **Rayleigh quotient** of a symmetric matrix $A$ at $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$ is

$$R(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}}$$

From the spectral decomposition with $\mathbf{x} = \sum c_i\mathbf{q}_i$:

$$R(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{\sum\lambda_i c_i^2}{\sum c_i^2}$$

This is a weighted average of eigenvalues, with weights $c_i^2/\sum c_j^2 \geq 0$ summing to $1$. Therefore:

$$\lambda_{\min} \leq R(\mathbf{x}) \leq \lambda_{\max} \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x} \neq 0$$

with equality: $R(\mathbf{q}_{\min}) = \lambda_{\min}$ and $R(\mathbf{q}_{\max}) = \lambda_{\max}$.

This gives a variational characterization of eigenvalues:

$$\lambda_{\max} = \max_{\mathbf{x}\neq 0} \frac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}}, \qquad \lambda_{\min} = \min_{\mathbf{x}\neq 0}\frac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}}$$

This is the **min-max theorem** (Courant-Fischer). It is foundational in numerical linear algebra: eigenvalues of symmetric matrices are extrema of the Rayleigh quotient, which means gradient-based methods can find them.

**Practical implication.** For any unit vector $\mathbf{x}$, $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}$ is sandwiched between the smallest and largest eigenvalues. If you want a lower bound on $\lambda_{\min}$, exhibit any $\mathbf{x}$ and compute $R(\mathbf{x})$. If you want to know $\lambda_{\max}$, the power method converges to it because $A^k\mathbf{x} = \sum \lambda_i^k c_i\mathbf{q}_i$ is dominated by the largest $|\lambda_i|$.

## The Matrix Exponential of a Symmetric Matrix

From [[The Matrix Exponential]] and the spectral decomposition:

$$e^{At} = \sum_{i=1}^n e^{\lambda_i t},\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$$

Since all $\lambda_i$ are real:

- If $A \prec 0$ (negative definite, all $\lambda_i < 0$): $e^{At} \to 0$ exponentially. The system $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ is asymptotically stable.
- If $A \succ 0$ (positive definite, all $\lambda_i > 0$): $e^{At} \to \infty$. Unstable.
- If $A$ is indefinite: mixed growth and decay.

No Jordan blocks, no $te^{\lambda t}$ terms. The matrix exponential of a symmetric matrix is always $\sum e^{\lambda_i t}\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$, clean and diagonal in the eigenvector basis.

The norm satisfies $|e^{At}| = e^{\lambda_{\max}(A)t}$ (operator norm), since $e^{At}$ stretches most in the direction of the largest eigenvalue.

## $A^TA$ Is Always Positive Semidefinite

For any matrix $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m\times n}$, the product $A^TA$ is an $n\times n$ symmetric, positive semidefinite matrix.

**Proof.** Symmetric: $(A^TA)^T = A^T(A^T)^T = A^TA$. Positive semidefinite: $\mathbf{x}^T(A^TA)\mathbf{x} = (A\mathbf{x})^T(A\mathbf{x}) = |A\mathbf{x}|^2 \geq 0$. $\square$

The eigenvalues of $A^TA$ are non-negative. Their square roots are the singular values of $A$. This is the connection between the spectral theorem and the singular value decomposition: SVD is the spectral theorem applied to $A^TA$ and $AA^T$.

The null space of $A^TA$ equals the null space of $A$: $A^TA\mathbf{x} = 0 \iff |A\mathbf{x}|^2 = 0 \iff A\mathbf{x} = 0$. So $A^TA$ is positive definite iff $A$ has independent columns.

This is why $A^TA$ appears in the normal equations for least squares: $A^TA\hat{\mathbf{x}} = A^T\mathbf{b}$. The matrix $A^TA$ is always symmetric, and positive definite when $A$ has independent columns, so the normal equations have a unique solution in that case.

## Summary

A real symmetric matrix $A = A^T$ has:

$$\boxed{A = Q\Lambda Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i,\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T}$$

with $Q$ orthogonal, all $\lambda_i \in \mathbb{R}$, eigenvectors $\mathbf{q}_i$ mutually orthogonal.

|Property|Condition|
|---|---|
|All $\lambda_i > 0$|Positive definite: $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$|
|All $\lambda_i \geq 0$|Positive semidefinite|
|All $\lambda_i < 0$|Negative definite: stable ODE|
|Mixed signs|Indefinite: saddle surface, unstable ODE|
|Smallest $\lambda$|$\min_{\mathbf{x}\neq 0} R(\mathbf{x})$, Rayleigh quotient|
|Largest $\lambda$|$\max_{\mathbf{x}\neq 0} R(\mathbf{x})$, power method converges to this|

No Jordan blocks ever. No complex eigenvalues ever. No defective eigenvalues ever. The spectral theorem is the guarantee that symmetric matrices are always perfectly diagonalizable in an orthonormal basis.
