`eigshow` is a MATLAB demonstration written by Strang (and used throughout 18.06) that makes the geometry of eigenvalues and singular values visible. The idea is simple: draw the unit circle in $\mathbb{R}^2$, apply a $2 \times 2$ matrix $A$ to every point on it, and watch what happens to the image vector $Ax$ as $x$ rotates around the circle.

Two separate things are being shown, and it is worth being precise about which is which.

---

## What eigshow Actually Does

Let $x$ be a unit vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$. As $x$ sweeps around the unit circle, eigshow plots both $x$ and $Ax$ simultaneously. Two vectors rotate together on screen — one tracing the circle, the other tracing the image ellipse.

The demonstration has two modes:

**Eigenvalue mode.** Look for the moments when $x$ and $Ax$ are parallel — pointing in the same direction or exactly opposite directions. At those moments, $Ax = \lambda x$ for some scalar $\lambda$. You have found an eigenvector. The ratio $|Ax| / |x|$ at that moment is $|\lambda|$.

**Singular value mode.** Look for the moments when $x$ and $Ax$ are perpendicular to each other in a specific sense — not $x \perp Ax$, but rather when $Ax$ reaches its maximum and minimum length. At those moments, $x = v_i$ and $Ax = \sigma_i u_i$. The right singular vectors $v_1, v_2$ are the input positions where $|Ax|$ is maximized and minimized. The singular values $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$ are those maximum and minimum lengths.

---

## The Geometry of the Two Modes

### Eigenvalue Mode

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix with real eigenvalues, there are exactly two directions where $Ax \parallel x$. You see them in eigshow as the two positions where both vectors align along the same line through the origin.

If $\lambda > 0$: $x$ and $Ax$ point the same direction. The matrix stretches along $x$.

If $\lambda < 0$: $x$ and $Ax$ point opposite directions. The matrix stretches and flips.

If the eigenvalues are complex (which happens for rotation-like matrices), there are no such positions — $Ax$ is never parallel to $x$ for any real unit vector $x$, and you see the two vectors perpetually chasing each other around without aligning.

This is eigshow making complex eigenvalues visceral: you watch and watch for the alignment and it never comes.

### Singular Value Mode

The image of the unit circle under $A$ is always an ellipse (or a degenerate ellipse — a line segment — if $A$ is rank 1). The semi-axes of that ellipse are $\sigma_1 u_1$ and $\sigma_2 u_2$.

In singular value mode, eigshow finds the two input positions $v_1$ and $v_2$ where:

- $|Av|$ is maximized: this is $v_1$, and the maximum value is $\sigma_1$.
- $|Av|$ is minimized: this is $v_2$, and the minimum value is $\sigma_2$.

And it shows that $Av_1 \perp Av_2$ — the images of the right singular vectors are perpendicular. The input directions $v_1, v_2$ need not be perpendicular to each other in general — but they are, because the $v_i$ are eigenvectors of the symmetric matrix $A^T A$, which always has orthogonal eigenvectors.

So eigshow is making the following visible all at once:

$$v_1 \perp v_2, \quad Av_1 \perp Av_2, \quad |Av_1| = \sigma_1, \quad |Av_2| = \sigma_2$$

This is the SVD in $2 \times 2$, drawn on screen.

## A Specific Example to Run in Your Head

Take

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

**What you see in eigenvalue mode.**

Compute the eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = 2$. There are two positions where $Ax \parallel x$. As $x$ rotates around the circle, at two moments the two vectors on screen lock into alignment — one when $x$ points near the $e_1$ direction (eigenvector for $\lambda = 3$), once when $x$ is near $(1, -1)/\sqrt{2}$ direction (eigenvector for $\lambda = 2$, found by solving $(A - 2I)v = 0$: $v = (1, -1)^T / \sqrt{2}$). At the first alignment, $Ax$ is 3 times as long as $x$. At the second, 2 times.

**What you see in singular value mode.**

Compute $A^T A$:

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9 & 3 \ 3 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues of $A^T A$: characteristic polynomial $\lambda^2 - 14\lambda + 36 = 0$, so $\lambda = 7 \pm \sqrt{13}$.

$$\sigma_1 = \sqrt{7 + \sqrt{13}} \approx 3.30, \qquad \sigma_2 = \sqrt{7 - \sqrt{13}} \approx 1.82$$

In eigshow singular value mode, the two vectors align orthogonally (in the right sense) twice as $x$ rotates. The image ellipse has semi-axes of length $\approx 3.30$ and $\approx 1.82$. The eigenvectors of $A^T A$ give the directions $v_1$ and $v_2$ where these extremes are achieved.

Notice: the eigenvalues of $A$ are $3$ and $2$, but the singular values are approximately $3.30$ and $1.82$. They are not the same — the eigenvalues are the scaling factors for the eigenvectors, while the singular values are the maximum and minimum stretching over all unit vectors. For a symmetric positive definite matrix, the two would coincide. For a non-symmetric matrix like this one, they differ.

## What eigshow Teaches About SVD vs Eigenvalues

The clearest thing eigshow does is force you to see that eigenvalues and singular values are answers to different questions.

**Eigenvalue question:** for which input directions $x$ is the output $Ax$ parallel to $x$?

**Singular value question:** for which input directions $x$ is the output $Ax$ as long as possible (or as short as possible)?

These are genuinely different geometric questions. They happen to have the same answer for symmetric matrices — but for non-symmetric matrices they diverge, and eigshow lets you see the divergence by running both modes on the same matrix.

A rotation matrix $R_\theta = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$ has:

- Eigenvalues $e^{i\theta}$ and $e^{-i\theta}$ — complex, no real eigenvectors. In eigenvalue mode: the two vectors never align. They perpetually rotate relative to each other.
- Singular values both equal to 1 — a rotation maps the unit circle to itself. In singular value mode: $|Ax| = 1$ for every $x$, so every direction is simultaneously a maximizer and minimizer. Singular values are 1 in every direction.

eigshow makes both of these facts immediately visible without computation.

---

## The Key Observation for SVD

Here is the observation that eigshow is really set up to deliver, stated precisely:

As $x$ rotates around the unit circle, $Ax$ traces an ellipse. At the moment $|Ax|$ is maximized, the output $Ax$ is perpendicular to the direction of motion of $Ax$ — that is, $Ax$ is perpendicular to $\frac{d}{d\theta}(Ax)$.

$\frac{d}{d\theta}(Ax) = A\frac{dx}{d\theta}$. If $x = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)^T$, then $dx/d\theta = (-\sin\theta, \cos\theta)^T$, which is perpendicular to $x$.

So the condition "$Ax$ is perpendicular to $A(dx/d\theta)$" becomes $(Ax)^T A(dx/d\theta) = 0$, i.e., $x^T A^T A (dx/d\theta) = 0$.

This says $x$ and $dx/d\theta$ are orthogonal with respect to the inner product defined by $A^T A$. Since $dx/d\theta \perp x$ in the standard sense, the condition reduces to $x$ being an eigenvector of $A^T A$.

**Conclusion.** The input directions that maximize or minimize $|Ax|$ are exactly the eigenvectors of $A^T A$ — the right singular vectors. This is not just a computation; it is a calculus argument that eigshow visualizes. The extremes of the output length, as you sweep through all input directions, occur precisely at the right singular vectors. The SVD is the answer to an optimization problem, and eigshow shows you the optimization happening in real time.

## Running eigshow

In MATLAB:

```matlab
eigshow          % opens the demo with a default matrix
eigshow(A)       % runs with your own 2x2 matrix A
```

Click to toggle between eigenvalue mode and singular value mode. Use the arrow keys or drag to rotate $x$ around the circle. Watch when the vectors align (eigenvalue mode) or when the output reaches its extreme length (singular value mode).

If you do not have MATLAB: the same demonstration can be reproduced in Python with `matplotlib` animations. The key is to plot $x(\theta) = (\cos\theta, \sin\theta)^T$ and $Ax(\theta)$ simultaneously as $\theta$ varies, and mark the special angles.

A minimal Python version:

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

A = np.array([[3, 1], [0, 2]])
theta = np.linspace(0, 2*np.pi, 500)
x = np.array([np.cos(theta), np.sin(theta)])   # unit circle, shape (2, 500)
Ax = A @ x                                      # image ellipse, shape (2, 500)

fig, ax = plt.subplots(figsize=(6, 6))
ax.plot(x[0], x[1], 'b--', label='unit circle')
ax.plot(Ax[0], Ax[1], 'r-', label='image ellipse $Ax$')
ax.set_aspect('equal')
ax.axhline(0, color='k', lw=0.5)
ax.axvline(0, color='k', lw=0.5)
ax.legend()
ax.set_title('eigshow: image of unit circle under $A$')
plt.show()
```

To find and mark the singular vectors:

```python
# Right singular vectors: eigenvectors of A^T A
AtA = A.T @ A
eigvals, eigvecs = np.linalg.eigh(AtA)       # sorted ascending
sigma = np.sqrt(eigvals[::-1])               # descending singular values
V = eigvecs[:, ::-1]                         # right singular vectors as columns

for i in range(2):
    vi = V[:, i]
    Avi = A @ vi
    ax.annotate('', xy=vi, xytext=(0,0),
                arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle='->', color='blue', lw=2))
    ax.annotate('', xy=Avi, xytext=(0,0),
                arrowprops=dict(arrowstyle='->', color='red', lw=2))
```

## What to Take Away

eigshow is not a gimmick. It is a way of building geometric intuition that the algebra alone does not give you. After running it on a few matrices, the following become obvious rather than just true:

- Eigenvectors are the input directions where $A$ does not rotate — only stretches or flips. They are special and rare (only $n$ of them for a diagonalizable $n \times n$ matrix).
- Right singular vectors are the input directions where the output is extremal in length. They always exist, they are always orthogonal, and there are always $n$ of them.
- The image of the unit circle is always an ellipse. The semi-axes of that ellipse are the singular values.
- For a symmetric positive definite matrix, the eigenvectors and right singular vectors coincide, and the eigenvalues equal the singular values. eigshow looks the same in both modes.
- For a rotation matrix, eigenvalue mode shows no alignment ever; singular value mode shows all directions are equivalent.

These are the pictures that should be in your head when you read a theorem about eigenvalues or singular values. eigshow puts them there.
