## When Diagonalization Fails

The diagonalization $A = PDP^{-1}$ requires $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors to form the columns of $P$. The theorem is:

**$A$ is diagonalizable if and only if for every eigenvalue $\lambda$, its geometric multiplicity equals its algebraic multiplicity.**

When this fails for even one eigenvalue, $P$ cannot be assembled, and $A = PDP^{-1}$ does not exist. The matrix is called **defective**.

This is not an exotic edge case. The matrix

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

has $\lambda = 0$ as a double eigenvalue (algebraic multiplicity 2) but only one eigenvector (geometric multiplicity 1). It is defective. It cannot be diagonalized over any field.

---

## Why Defectiveness Happens

The characteristic polynomial $\det(A - \lambda I)$ factors as $(\lambda - \lambda_0)^m \cdots$ when $\lambda_0$ has algebraic multiplicity $m$. The eigenspace $\ker(A - \lambda_0 I)$ has dimension at most $m$, but it can be strictly less.

The deficit arises because $(A - \lambda_0 I)$ is singular but not the zero matrix. It has a nontrivial null space (at least dimension 1, guaranteed by $\lambda_0$ being an eigenvalue) but also a nontrivial image. Vectors in the image of $(A - \lambda_0 I)$ are the ones that cannot be reached as genuine eigenvectors.

The key quantity is the **rank-nullity** applied to $(A - \lambda_0 I)$:

$$\dim\ker(A - \lambda_0 I) + \text{rank}(A - \lambda_0 I) = n$$

Geometric multiplicity $= \dim\ker(A - \lambda_0 I) = n - \text{rank}(A - \lambda_0 I)$.

If $\text{rank}(A - \lambda_0 I)$ is larger than it needs to be, the eigenspace is smaller than algebraic multiplicity, and the matrix is defective.

---

## Generalized Eigenvectors

When $(A - \lambda_0 I)\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$ but $(A - \lambda_0 I)^2\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$, the vector $\mathbf{x}$ is called a **generalized eigenvector of rank 2**.

More generally, $\mathbf{x}$ is a generalized eigenvector of rank $k$ for eigenvalue $\lambda_0$ if:

$$(A - \lambda_0 I)^k\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0} \quad \text{but} \quad (A - \lambda_0 I)^{k-1}\mathbf{x} \neq \mathbf{0}$$

Genuine eigenvectors are the rank-1 case. The **generalized eigenspace** of $\lambda_0$ is:

$$K_{\lambda_0} = \ker(A - \lambda_0 I)^n$$

where $n$ is the size of the matrix. Its dimension equals the algebraic multiplicity of $\lambda_0$, always. This is where the missing dimensions live.

---

## Jordan Chains

Generalized eigenvectors organize into **Jordan chains**. Start with a genuine eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_1$:

$$(A - \lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v}_1 = \mathbf{0}$$

Find $\mathbf{v}_2$ such that:

$$(A - \lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{v}_1$$

Find $\mathbf{v}_3$ such that:

$$(A - \lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v}_3 = \mathbf{v}_2$$

and so on. The chain $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{v}_3, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$ is a Jordan chain of length $k$. Each vector is a generalized eigenvector of increasing rank.

Notice what $A$ does to these vectors:

$$A\mathbf{v}_1 = \lambda_0\mathbf{v}_1$$ $$A\mathbf{v}_2 = \lambda_0\mathbf{v}_2 + \mathbf{v}_1$$ $$A\mathbf{v}_3 = \lambda_0\mathbf{v}_3 + \mathbf{v}_2$$

In the basis ${\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \mathbf{v}_3}$, the matrix $A$ acts as:

$$\begin{pmatrix} \lambda_0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & \lambda_0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & \lambda_0 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is a **Jordan block**.

---

## The Jordan Block

A Jordan block of size $k$ for eigenvalue $\lambda_0$ is the $k \times k$ matrix:

$$J_k(\lambda_0) = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \lambda_0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & 0 & \lambda_0 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & & \ddots & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\lambda_0$ on the diagonal, $1$s on the superdiagonal, $0$s everywhere else.

A Jordan block of size 1 is just $(\lambda_0)$ -- a $1\times 1$ matrix, no superdiagonal. This is the non-defective case.

A Jordan block of size $k \geq 2$ has exactly one eigenvector (the first standard basis vector $\mathbf{e}_1$ in the block's coordinates), so geometric multiplicity 1 against algebraic multiplicity $k$.

---

## Jordan Normal Form

**Theorem (Jordan).** Every square matrix $A$ over $\mathbb{C}$ is similar to a block diagonal matrix:

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} J_{k_1}(\lambda_1) & & \ & J_{k_2}(\lambda_2) & \ & & \ddots \end{pmatrix}$$

where each block $J_{k_i}(\lambda_i)$ is a Jordan block. That is, there exists an invertible $P$ such that:

$$A = PJP^{-1}$$

The matrix $J$ is the **Jordan normal form** of $A$. It is unique up to reordering the blocks.

Over $\mathbb{R}$, this requires working in $\mathbb{C}$ if $A$ has non-real eigenvalues. Over $\mathbb{C}$, it always exists.

---

## How to Find the Jordan Form

Given $A$:

**Step 1.** Find all eigenvalues from $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$. Note algebraic multiplicities.

**Step 2.** For each eigenvalue $\lambda_0$, compute:

$$d_k = \dim\ker(A - \lambda_0 I)^k \quad \text{for } k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

until $d_k$ stabilizes at the algebraic multiplicity $m_a(\lambda_0)$.

**Step 3.** The number of Jordan blocks for $\lambda_0$ equals $d_1 = m_g(\lambda_0)$.

The sizes of the blocks are determined by the sequence $d_1, d_2, \ldots$: the number of blocks of size $\geq k$ equals $d_k - d_{k-1}$ (with $d_0 = 0$).

**Step 4.** For the columns of $P$: for each Jordan chain of length $k$, start from the deepest generalized eigenvector $\mathbf{v}_k$ (solving $(A-\lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{v}_{k-1}$) and walk back down the chain. The chain gives $k$ columns of $P$ in order $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$.

## Worked Example: $2 \times 2$ Defective

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 1 \ 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Eigenvalue:** $\det(A - \lambda I) = (5-\lambda)^2 = 0$, so $\lambda_0 = 5$ with algebraic multiplicity 2.

**Geometric multiplicity:**

$$A - 5I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\ker(A - 5I) = \text{span}\left{\begin{pmatrix}1\0\end{pmatrix}\right}$. Geometric multiplicity 1. Defective.

**Jordan chain:**

The genuine eigenvector is $\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\end{pmatrix}$.

Find $\mathbf{v}_2$ such that $(A - 5I)\mathbf{v}_2 = \mathbf{v}_1$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}v \ w\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\end{pmatrix}$$

The first row gives $w = 1$. The second row gives $0 = 0$. So $v$ is free; take $v = 0$:

$$\mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}0\1\end{pmatrix}$$

**Form $P$:**

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = I, \qquad J = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 1 \ 0 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

In this case $P = I$ and $J = A$ itself -- $A$ is already in Jordan form. This makes sense: $A$ is upper triangular with the $1$ in the superdiagonal.

## Worked Example: $3 \times 3$ with Mixed Structure

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Eigenvalues:** $\lambda_1 = 2$ (algebraic multiplicity 2), $\lambda_2 = 3$ (algebraic multiplicity 1).

**For $\lambda_2 = 3$:** Geometric multiplicity 1, equals algebraic. One $1\times 1$ Jordan block: $J_1(3) = (3)$.

**For $\lambda_1 = 2$:**

$$A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\ker(A - 2I)$: row reduce. Row 3 gives $x_3 = 0$. Row 1 gives $x_2 = 0$. Variable $x_1$ is free.

$$\ker(A - 2I) = \text{span}\left{\begin{pmatrix}1\0\0\end{pmatrix}\right}$$

Geometric multiplicity 1, algebraic multiplicity 2. Defective.

Now check $(A - 2I)^2$:

$$(A-2I)^2 = \begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\0&0&0\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}^2 = \begin{pmatrix}0&0&0\0&0&0\0&0&1\end{pmatrix}$$

$\ker(A-2I)^2$: only constraint is $x_3 = 0$. Dimension 2. So $d_2 = 2 = m_a(2)$. Stabilized.

**Number of blocks for $\lambda = 2$:** $d_1 = 1$, so one Jordan block. Since $d_2 - d_1 = 1$, one block of size $\geq 2$. So one block of size exactly 2: $J_2(2)$.

**Jordan form:**

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

And $A$ is already in this form. (Again triangular with the appropriate superdiagonal.)

## Powers of a Jordan Block

This is why Jordan form matters for [[Matrix Powers]]. Let $J = J_k(\lambda_0)$ be a $k\times k$ Jordan block. Write $J = \lambda_0 I + N$ where:

$$N = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots \ 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots \ \vdots & & \ddots & \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$N$ is the **nilpotent** part: $N^k = 0$ (the superdiagonal shifts upward by one with each power, and after $k$ steps everything is gone).

Since $\lambda_0 I$ and $N$ commute (both are functions of $J$), the binomial theorem applies:

$$J^m = (\lambda_0 I + N)^m = \sum_{j=0}^{k-1}\binom{m}{j}\lambda_0^{m-j}N^j$$

(The sum stops at $k-1$ because $N^k = 0$.) Explicitly:

$$J_k(\lambda_0)^m = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_0^m & \binom{m}{1}\lambda_0^{m-1} & \binom{m}{2}\lambda_0^{m-2} & \cdots & \binom{m}{k-1}\lambda_0^{m-k+1} \ 0 & \lambda_0^m & \binom{m}{1}\lambda_0^{m-1} & \cdots & \vdots \ \vdots & & \ddots & & \vdots \ 0 & \cdots & 0 & & \lambda_0^m \end{pmatrix}$$

For a $2\times 2$ Jordan block:

$$\begin{pmatrix}\lambda_0 & 1 \ 0 & \lambda_0\end{pmatrix}^m = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_0^m & m\lambda_0^{m-1} \ 0 & \lambda_0^m\end{pmatrix}$$

The off-diagonal entry grows like $m\lambda_0^{m-1}$, a polynomial factor multiplying the exponential. This is the signature of a defective eigenvalue in a discrete dynamical system. Solutions grow faster than $\lambda_0^m$ alone would suggest -- there is a polynomial correction.

## Cayley-Hamilton Theorem

As a byproduct of Jordan form:

**Theorem.** Every matrix satisfies its own characteristic polynomial. If $p(\lambda) = \det(\lambda I - A)$, then $p(A) = 0$.

_Proof via Jordan form._ In Jordan coordinates, $A = PJP^{-1}$ and $p(A) = Pp(J)P^{-1}$. Each Jordan block $J_k(\lambda_0)$ satisfies $p(J_k(\lambda_0)) = 0$ because $\lambda_0$ is a root of $p$ with multiplicity $\geq k$, and $(J_k(\lambda_0) - \lambda_0 I)^k = N^k = 0$.

For a $2\times 2$ matrix: if $p(\lambda) = \lambda^2 - (\text{tr},A)\lambda + \det(A)$, then $A^2 - (\text{tr},A)A + (\det A)I = 0$. This gives a way to express $A^2$, and all higher powers, as linear combinations of $I$ and $A$.

## What Non-Diagonalizability Means Physically

For a differential equation $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$:

- A diagonalizable $A$ gives solutions that are pure exponentials $e^{\lambda_i t}$ in each mode.
- A defective $A$ gives solutions of the form $t^j e^{\lambda_0 t}$ for $j = 0, 1, \ldots, k-1$ from each Jordan block of size $k$.

The polynomial factor $t^j$ is the hallmark of resonance. In a second-order ODE, this is the repeated-root case $e^{\lambda t}$ and $te^{\lambda t}$. Jordan blocks are the matrix version of repeated roots requiring polynomial multiplication.

If $|\lambda_0| < 1$ (discrete) or $\text{Re}(\lambda_0) < 0$ (continuous), the polynomial factor eventually loses to the decaying exponential, and the system is still stable. But the transient behavior is qualitatively different from the non-defective case.

---

## Summary

A matrix is non-diagonalizable when some eigenvalue has geometric multiplicity strictly less than algebraic multiplicity. The correct decomposition is:

$$\boxed{A = PJP^{-1}}$$

where $J$ is block diagonal with Jordan blocks. Each Jordan block $J_k(\lambda_0)$ has $\lambda_0$ on the diagonal and $1$s on the superdiagonal.

The columns of $P$ are Jordan chains -- genuine eigenvector first, then generalized eigenvectors found by solving $(A - \lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v}_{j+1} = \mathbf{v}_j$ at each step.

Powers of $J$ produce polynomial-times-exponential factors. The Cayley-Hamilton theorem is a corollary.

The number and sizes of Jordan blocks are determined by the sequence $d_k = \dim\ker(A - \lambda_0 I)^k$.
