
For a $2 \times 2$ or $3 \times 3$ matrix, computing eigenvalues by hand via $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$ is painful but feasible. For a $100 \times 100$ matrix it is completely out of the question. The characteristic polynomial has degree 100, and there is no general algebraic formula for roots of polynomials of degree 5 or higher (Abel-Ruffini). Even numerically, forming the characteristic polynomial and then finding its roots is a terrible algorithm — the polynomial's coefficients are catastrophically sensitive to perturbations.

This note is about what you do instead: how to locate, estimate, and compute eigenvalues of large matrices without touching the characteristic polynomial.

---

## What We Can and Cannot Do

**Cannot do (for large $n$):**

- Compute $\det(A - \lambda I)$ symbolically
- Find all eigenvalues exactly in finitely many arithmetic steps (Abel-Ruffini)
- Diagonalize explicitly by solving $n$ coupled equations

**Can do:**

- Locate eigenvalues in regions of the complex plane (Gershgorin)
- Bound the spectral radius
- Compute the largest eigenvalue iteratively (Power Method)
- Compute all eigenvalues numerically to machine precision (QR Algorithm)
- Exploit structure (symmetry, sparsity, tridiagonal form) heavily

The shift from "solve" to "iterate" is the fundamental move in large-scale linear algebra.

---

## Gershgorin Circle Theorem

This is the first and most elegant tool: a completely non-iterative way to locate all eigenvalues using only the matrix entries.

**Theorem** (Gershgorin, 1931). Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ complex matrix. For each $i = 1, \ldots, n$, define the **Gershgorin disc**

$$D_i = \left{ z \in \mathbb{C} : |z - a_{ii}| \leq R_i \right}, \qquad R_i = \sum_{j \neq i} |a_{ij}|$$

That is: center at the diagonal entry $a_{ii}$, radius equal to the sum of absolute values of the off-diagonal entries in row $i$.

Then every eigenvalue of $A$ lies in the union $\bigcup_{i=1}^n D_i$.

**Proof.** Let $\lambda$ be an eigenvalue with eigenvector $x \neq 0$. Let $k$ be the index where $|x_k| = |x|_\infty$ (the component of largest absolute value). From $Ax = \lambda x$, the $k$-th row gives

$$\sum_{j=1}^n a_{kj} x_j = \lambda x_k$$

$$(\lambda - a_{kk}) x_k = \sum_{j \neq k} a_{kj} x_j$$

Taking absolute values:

$$|\lambda - a_{kk}| |x_k| \leq \sum_{j \neq k} |a_{kj}| |x_j| \leq |x_k| \sum_{j \neq k} |a_{kj}| = R_k |x_k|$$

Since $|x_k| > 0$, divide through:

$$|\lambda - a_{kk}| \leq R_k$$

So $\lambda \in D_k \subseteq \bigcup_i D_i$. $\square$

**Example.** Take

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 10 & 1 & 0 \ 2 & 8 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

Gershgorin discs:

- Row 1: center $10$, radius $|1| + |0| = 1$. Disc: $|z - 10| \leq 1$, so $\lambda \in [9, 11]$.
- Row 2: center $8$, radius $|2| + |1| = 3$. Disc: $|z - 8| \leq 3$, so $\lambda \in [5, 11]$.
- Row 3: center $5$, radius $|1| + |0| = 1$. Disc: $|z - 5| \leq 1$, so $\lambda \in [4, 6]$.

All eigenvalues lie in $[4, 6] \cup [5, 11]$. Since the matrix is symmetric with positive diagonal, they are all real and positive — so the matrix is positive definite, confirmed without computing a single eigenvalue.

**Refinement.** If a connected component of $\bigcup_i D_i$ consists of exactly $k$ discs, then it contains exactly $k$ eigenvalues (counted with multiplicity). This follows from a continuity argument — perturb $A$ to a diagonal matrix and track where eigenvalues go.

## Diagonal Dominance and the Gershgorin Corollary

A matrix is **strictly diagonally dominant** if $|a_{ii}| > R_i$ for every $i$. By Gershgorin, all eigenvalues lie in discs that do not contain 0, so $0$ is not an eigenvalue, so $A$ is invertible.

Moreover, if $A$ is strictly diagonally dominant with positive diagonal entries, then every Gershgorin disc lies in the right half-plane, so all eigenvalues have positive real part. If $A$ is also symmetric, all eigenvalues are real and positive: $A$ is positive definite.

This is a completely elementary sufficient condition for positive definiteness that requires no computation.

## The Power Method

The Power Method computes the **dominant eigenvalue** — the eigenvalue of largest absolute value, assuming it is unique.

**Setup.** Assume $A$ has eigenvalues $|\lambda_1| > |\lambda_2| \geq \cdots \geq |\lambda_n|$. We want $\lambda_1$.

**Algorithm.** Start with an arbitrary vector $b_0$ (not orthogonal to the eigenvector $v_1$ — almost any random vector works). Iterate:

$$b_{k+1} = \frac{Ab_k}{|Ab_k|}$$

At each step, multiply by $A$ and renormalize. The Rayleigh quotient

$$\mu_k = \frac{b_k^T A b_k}{b_k^T b_k}$$

converges to $\lambda_1$.

**Why it works.** Expand $b_0$ in the eigenvector basis:

$$b_0 = c_1 v_1 + c_2 v_2 + \cdots + c_n v_n$$

Then (ignoring renormalization for clarity):

$$A^k b_0 = c_1 \lambda_1^k v_1 + c_2 \lambda_2^k v_2 + \cdots + c_n \lambda_n^k v_n = \lambda_1^k \left( c_1 v_1 + c_2 \left(\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}\right)^k v_2 + \cdots \right)$$

Since $|\lambda_2 / \lambda_1| < 1$, all terms except the first decay geometrically. The direction of $A^k b_0$ converges to $v_1$ at rate $|\lambda_2 / \lambda_1|^k$.

**Rate of convergence.** The error after $k$ steps is $O\left(\left|\dfrac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_1}\right|^k\right)$. If $\lambda_2$ is close to $\lambda_1$, convergence is slow. This is the key weakness.

**Shift to accelerate.** If you know $\lambda_1 \approx \mu$, apply the power method to $(A - \mu I)^{-1}$ instead. Its dominant eigenvalue is $(\lambda_j - \mu)^{-1}$ where $\lambda_j$ is the eigenvalue closest to $\mu$. By shifting close to a target eigenvalue, you can make the ratio arbitrarily favorable. This is **inverse iteration**.

## Inverse Iteration and the Rayleigh Quotient Iteration

**Inverse iteration.** Fix a shift $\sigma$ near a target eigenvalue $\lambda_j$. Solve

$$(A - \sigma I) b_{k+1} = b_k, \qquad \text{then normalize}$$

at each step. This converges to the eigenvector for the eigenvalue closest to $\sigma$, at rate $\left|\dfrac{\lambda_j - \sigma}{\lambda_\ell - \sigma}\right|^k$ where $\lambda_\ell$ is the second-closest eigenvalue to $\sigma$.

**Rayleigh quotient iteration.** Update the shift at each step using the current Rayleigh quotient:

$$\sigma_k = \frac{b_k^T A b_k}{b_k^T b_k}$$

then solve $(A - \sigma_k I)b_{k+1} = b_k$. For symmetric matrices, this converges **cubically** once close to an eigenvector. Practically instantaneous near convergence.

The cost per step is solving a linear system with the shifted matrix — for dense matrices this is $O(n^3)$ once (via LU factorization) then $O(n^2)$ per step.

## Reduction to Hessenberg Form

Before running any iterative algorithm on a large dense matrix, reduce it to **upper Hessenberg form** (for general matrices) or **tridiagonal form** (for symmetric matrices). A Hessenberg matrix has zeros below the first subdiagonal:

$$H = \begin{bmatrix} * & * & * & * & * \ * & * & * & * & * \ 0 & * & * & * & * \ 0 & 0 & * & * & * \ 0 & 0 & 0 & * & * \end{bmatrix}$$

For symmetric $A$, the Hessenberg reduction produces a symmetric tridiagonal matrix:

$$T = \begin{bmatrix} \alpha_1 & \beta_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ \beta_1 & \alpha_2 & \beta_2 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \beta_2 & \alpha_3 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \beta_{n-1} & \alpha_n \end{bmatrix}$$

This reduction is exact (not iterative), accomplished via $n-2$ Householder reflections in $O(n^3)$ time. The resulting $H$ (or $T$) is similar to $A$, so has the same eigenvalues. Every subsequent step of the QR algorithm costs only $O(n^2)$ for Hessenberg matrices (versus $O(n^3)$ for full matrices), making the reduction a one-time investment that pays off over many iterations.

## The QR Algorithm

This is the algorithm actually used to compute all eigenvalues of a dense matrix to machine precision. It is one of the ten greatest algorithms of the 20th century.

**Basic QR iteration.** Start with $A_0 = A$.

At step $k$:

1. Factor $A_k = Q_k R_k$ (QR decomposition: $Q_k$ orthogonal, $R_k$ upper triangular)
2. Set $A_{k+1} = R_k Q_k$

Note: $A_{k+1} = R_k Q_k = Q_k^T (Q_k R_k) Q_k = Q_k^T A_k Q_k$, so each $A_{k+1}$ is **orthogonally similar** to $A_k$, hence similar to $A$. All eigenvalues are preserved exactly.

**Convergence.** Under mild conditions, $A_k$ converges to a block upper triangular matrix (Schur form) whose diagonal blocks are $1 \times 1$ (real eigenvalues) or $2 \times 2$ (complex conjugate pairs). The subdiagonal entries decay to zero.

The convergence rate of the $(i,i+1)$ subdiagonal entry is $O\left(\left|\dfrac{\lambda_{i+1}}{\lambda_i}\right|^k\right)$ — the same ratio as the power method, which makes sense: QR iteration is essentially running the power method simultaneously for all eigenvalues.

**Practical QR algorithm** (what LAPACK actually does):

1. Reduce $A$ to Hessenberg form $H = Q_0^T A Q_0$ via Householder reflections. One-time cost $O(n^3)$.
2. Apply QR iteration with **shifts**: at each step, choose a shift $\sigma_k$ (e.g., the bottom-right entry of $A_k$, or a Wilkinson shift) and factor $A_k - \sigma_k I = Q_k R_k$, then set $A_{k+1} = R_k Q_k + \sigma_k I$.
3. Deflate: once a subdiagonal entry is negligible, split the matrix and work on smaller pieces.

With Wilkinson shifts, the convergence is typically cubic for the last subdiagonal entry. In practice, for a symmetric tridiagonal $n \times n$ matrix, the full eigendecomposition takes $O(n^2)$ iterations total.

## Special Structure: Sparse and Symmetric Matrices

For very large matrices (think $n = 10^6$) arising in PDE discretizations, finite element methods, or graph problems, even storing the full matrix is impossible. These matrices are sparse: most entries are zero. The algorithms of choice are **Krylov subspace methods**.

**Lanczos algorithm** (symmetric case). The key observation: $A$, $Ab_0$, $A^2 b_0$, $\ldots$ span a Krylov subspace. Instead of iterating randomly, build an orthonormal basis for $\mathcal{K}_k = \text{span}{b_0, Ab_0, \ldots, A^{k-1}b_0}$ via a three-term recurrence (for symmetric $A$):

$$\beta_{j+1} q_{j+1} = Aq_j - \alpha_j q_j - \beta_j q_{j-1}$$

where $\alpha_j = q_j^T A q_j$ and $\beta_{j+1} = |Aq_j - \alpha_j q_j - \beta_j q_{j-1}|$.

After $k$ steps, you have a $k \times k$ tridiagonal matrix $T_k$ with diagonal $\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k$ and off-diagonal $\beta_2, \ldots, \beta_k$. Its eigenvalues (**Ritz values**) approximate the extreme eigenvalues of $A$.

**Key property.** The Lanczos algorithm only requires matrix-vector products $Aq$. For a sparse matrix stored in compressed format, each product costs $O(\text{nnz})$ where nnz is the number of nonzero entries. You never form $A$ explicitly or do anything that costs more than a matrix-vector multiply per step.

For a matrix with $n = 10^6$ and nnz $= 10^7$, you might need $k = 100$ Lanczos steps to get good approximations of the 10 largest eigenvalues — a total cost of $10^9$ operations, entirely feasible.

**Arnoldi algorithm** (non-symmetric case). Generalization of Lanczos to non-symmetric matrices. Produces an upper Hessenberg projection of $A$ onto the Krylov subspace. Eigenvalues of the Hessenberg projection approximate eigenvalues of $A$.

## Bounding the Spectral Radius

The **spectral radius** of $A$ is $\rho(A) = \max_i |\lambda_i|$. Several elementary bounds:

**From matrix norms.** For any induced matrix norm $|\cdot|$:

$$\rho(A) \leq |A|$$

The spectral radius is the infimum of $|A|$ over all consistent matrix norms. In particular:

$$\rho(A) \leq |A|_1 = \max_j \sum_i |a_{ij}| \qquad \text{(max column sum)}$$ $$\rho(A) \leq |A|_\infty = \max_i \sum_j |a_{ij}| \qquad \text{(max row sum)}$$ $$\rho(A) \leq |A|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i,j} a_{ij}^2} \qquad \text{(Frobenius norm)}$$

**From Gershgorin.** $\rho(A) \leq \max_i (|a_{ii}| + R_i)$, the radius of the outermost Gershgorin disc.

**Perron-Frobenius** (for non-negative matrices). If $A$ has all non-negative entries and is irreducible, then the spectral radius is a simple eigenvalue with a positive eigenvector. This is fundamental in Markov chains, PageRank, and network analysis — the dominant eigenvalue of a transition matrix is 1, and the corresponding eigenvector is the stationary distribution.

## Why the Characteristic Polynomial is a Bad Algorithm

Worth making this explicit. Suppose you form $p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$ for a $20 \times 20$ matrix and try to find its roots numerically. The coefficients of $p$ involve sums of products of up to 20 entries of $A$. These coefficients can be astronomically large or small. A tiny perturbation in a coefficient (say, due to floating point) can shift a root by a huge amount.

The classic demonstration: Wilkinson's polynomial $\prod_{k=1}^{20}(\lambda - k)$ has roots $1, 2, \ldots, 20$. Perturbing the coefficient of $\lambda^{19}$ by $2^{-23}$ moves one root from the real axis to a complex value far from any integer. The roots are wildly sensitive to the coefficients.

The QR algorithm avoids this entirely by working with the matrix directly, using orthogonal transformations that are perfectly conditioned (orthogonal matrices have condition number 1).

## Summary: Which Algorithm for Which Problem

|Problem|Matrix Type|Algorithm|
|---|---|---|
|All eigenvalues, dense, small ($n \lesssim 10^3$)|General|QR algorithm (after Hessenberg reduction)|
|All eigenvalues, dense, symmetric|Symmetric|Tridiagonalize, then QR or divide-and-conquer|
|Largest eigenvalue|Any|Power Method|
|Eigenvalue near a target $\sigma$|Any|Inverse iteration with shift|
|Few extreme eigenvalues, large sparse|Symmetric|Lanczos algorithm|
|Few eigenvalues, large sparse, non-symmetric|General|Arnoldi / GMRES|
|Locate eigenvalues without computing|Any|Gershgorin circles|
|Positive definiteness check|Symmetric|Gershgorin + diagonal dominance|

## A Personal Remark

The gap between "find eigenvalues of a $3 \times 3$ matrix" and "find eigenvalues of a $1000 \times 1000$ matrix" is not just computational — it is conceptual. The characteristic polynomial approach, which feels so clean theoretically, becomes completely useless. You are forced to think differently: iteratively, approximately, exploiting structure.

The QR algorithm is the right way to think about this. It does not solve an equation; it deforms the matrix continuously, step by step, toward a form where eigenvalues are readable. The eigenvalues were always there in the diagonal entries — you just needed to conjugate away the off-diagonal noise. That is a very different philosophy from "find roots of a polynomial."

Strang's 18.06 touches on the Power Method and mentions QR, but the real depth here is in numerical linear algebra (Trefethen and Bau is the reference to eventually read).
