
## The Setup

A general quadratic form in two variables looks like:

$$Q(x, y) = ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$$

The coefficient on $xy$ is written $2b$ rather than $b$ — this is deliberate, and it will make the matrix symmetric in the cleanest possible way. If someone writes $ax^2 + Bxy + cy^2$, just set $b = B/2$.

This expression hides a matrix. Write $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}x \ y\end{bmatrix}$ and observe:

$$Q(x,y) = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}, \qquad A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b \ b & c \end{bmatrix}$$

Check: $\begin{bmatrix}x & y\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}a & b \ b & c\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}x \ y\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}x & y\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}ax + by \ bx + cy\end{bmatrix} = ax^2 + bxy + bxy + cy^2 = ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$. Yes.

The matrix $A$ is symmetric. The level set $Q(x,y) = 1$, i.e. $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 1$, is a conic section. The question is: what kind, and what are its axes?

---

## The Three Cases

The type of conic depends entirely on the eigenvalues of $A$:

- Both $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 > 0$: $A$ is positive definite. Level set is an **ellipse**.
- Both $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 < 0$: $A$ is negative definite. The set $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 1$ is **empty** (the form is always negative, never equals $+1$).
- $\lambda_1 > 0$, $\lambda_2 < 0$: $A$ is indefinite. Level set is a **hyperbola**.
- One eigenvalue is zero: level set is a **parabola** or pair of lines (degenerate).

I care about the ellipse case. The rest follows the same logic with sign changes.

---

## The Principal Axis Theorem in Two Dimensions

The spectral theorem gives $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ with $Q$ orthogonal. Introduce rotated coordinates:

$$\begin{bmatrix} u \ v \end{bmatrix} = Q^T \begin{bmatrix} x \ y \end{bmatrix}$$

Since $Q$ is orthogonal, this is a rotation (possibly with a reflection) of the $xy$-plane. In these new coordinates:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{u}^T \Lambda \mathbf{u} = \lambda_1 u^2 + \lambda_2 v^2$$

No cross term. The equation $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 1$ becomes:

$$\lambda_1 u^2 + \lambda_2 v^2 = 1 \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \frac{u^2}{(1/\sqrt{\lambda_1})^2} + \frac{v^2}{(1/\sqrt{\lambda_2})^2} = 1$$

This is a standard axis-aligned ellipse in the $(u,v)$ coordinate system. The semi-axes are $1/\sqrt{\lambda_1}$ and $1/\sqrt{\lambda_2}$, pointing in the directions of the eigenvectors of $A$.

The cross term $2bxy$ in the original expression was a symptom of the axes being tilted relative to the coordinate axes. The spectral theorem finds the tilt and removes it.

## A Fully Worked Example

$$Q(x,y) = 5x^2 - 4xy + 5y^2 = 1$$

**Step 1: Identify the matrix.**

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & -2 \ -2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

(The off-diagonal entry is $b = -2$ since the coefficient on $xy$ is $2b = -4$.)

**Step 2: Find the eigenvalues.**

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (5 - \lambda)^2 - 4 = \lambda^2 - 10\lambda + 21 = (\lambda - 3)(\lambda - 7)$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = 7$. Both positive — this is an ellipse.

**Step 3: Find the eigenvectors.**

For $\lambda_1 = 3$:

$$A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & -2 \ -2 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{row reduce}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

So $x = y$. Eigenvector: $\begin{bmatrix}1\1\end{bmatrix}$. Normalized: $\mathbf{q}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\1\end{bmatrix}$.

For $\lambda_2 = 7$:

$$A - 7I = \begin{bmatrix} -2 & -2 \ -2 & -2 \end{bmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{row reduce}} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

So $x = -y$. Eigenvector: $\begin{bmatrix}1\-1\end{bmatrix}$. Normalized: $\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\-1\end{bmatrix}$.

**Step 4: Write out $Q$.**

$$Q = \begin{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \ \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & -\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \end{bmatrix}$$

This is a rotation by $-45°$ (equivalently, the new axes are the lines $y = x$ and $y = -x$).

**Step 5: The ellipse in the rotated frame.**

In coordinates $u, v$ aligned with the eigenvectors:

$$3u^2 + 7v^2 = 1 \qquad \Longleftrightarrow \qquad \frac{u^2}{(1/\sqrt{3})^2} + \frac{v^2}{(1/\sqrt{7})^2} = 1$$

Semi-axis along $\mathbf{q}_1$ direction ($u$-axis, i.e. $y = x$): $\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \approx 0.577$.

Semi-axis along $\mathbf{q}_2$ direction ($v$-axis, i.e. $y = -x$): $\frac{1}{\sqrt{7}} \approx 0.378$.

The larger eigenvalue ($\lambda_2 = 7$) gives the shorter axis. The smaller eigenvalue ($\lambda_1 = 3$) gives the longer axis. This makes sense: where the quadratic form grows quickly (large $\lambda$), the ellipse is tight; where it grows slowly (small $\lambda$), the ellipse is elongated.

---

## The Area of the Ellipse

The area of the ellipse $\frac{u^2}{a^2} + \frac{v^2}{b^2} = 1$ is $\pi ab$. For us, $a = 1/\sqrt{\lambda_1}$ and $b = 1/\sqrt{\lambda_2}$:

$$\text{Area} = \pi \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_1}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_2}} = \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{\lambda_1 \lambda_2}} = \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{\det(A)}}$$

For the example: $\det(A) = 25 - 4 = 21$, so area $= \pi/\sqrt{21}$.

The area is determined entirely by $\det(A)$. Two different matrices with the same determinant give ellipses with the same area, even if the shapes (aspect ratios) are completely different. The shape is determined by the ratio $\lambda_1/\lambda_2$, which is the condition number — a poorly conditioned matrix gives a very elongated ellipse.

---

## Reading Off the Axes Without Full Diagonalization

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix, the eigenvalues come from:

$$\lambda = \frac{\text{tr}(A)}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{\text{tr}(A)}{2}\right)^2 - \det(A)} = \frac{a+c}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(\frac{a-c}{2}\right)^2 + b^2}$$

And the angle of rotation $\theta$ of the principal axes satisfies:

$$\tan(2\theta) = \frac{2b}{a - c}$$

(This comes from the condition that the off-diagonal entry of $Q^T A Q$ vanishes.) So without solving the full eigenvalue problem, one can identify the tilt of the axes from the ratio of the cross-term coefficient to the difference of diagonal coefficients.

For the example: $a = c = 5$, $b = -2$. The formula gives $\tan(2\theta) = \frac{-4}{0}$, which is undefined — meaning $2\theta = 90°$, so $\theta = 45°$. The principal axes are at $45°$ to the coordinate axes, which matches $\mathbf{q}_1 = (1,1)/\sqrt{2}$. $\checkmark$

---

## What the Cross Term $2bxy$ Means Geometrically

When $b = 0$, the matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix}a & 0 \ 0 & c\end{bmatrix}$ is already diagonal — the eigenvectors are $(1,0)$ and $(0,1)$. The ellipse $ax^2 + cy^2 = 1$ is axis-aligned. Clean.

When $b \neq 0$, the matrix is not diagonal and the eigenvectors are rotated versions of the coordinate axes. The cross term $2bxy$ is entirely a consequence of describing a tilted ellipse in an untilted coordinate system. It is not intrinsic to the ellipse — it is an artifact of the coordinate choice.

This is precisely what Strang means when he says: "The eigenvectors give the right axes." The spectral theorem finds the coordinate system in which there is no cross term.

---

## The General Conic: Adding Linear Terms

The full general conic is $ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2 + dx + ey + f = 0$. The linear terms $dx + ey$ shift the center away from the origin. One handles them by completing the square (in the rotated frame), which translates the center without changing the shape.

The shape (ellipse, hyperbola, parabola) is determined entirely by the $2 \times 2$ matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix}a&b\b&c\end{bmatrix}$ and its eigenvalue signs. The center and orientation are secondary.

For the pure quadratic form $ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2 = 1$ (centered at the origin), no translation is needed — the principal axis theorem gives the complete picture directly.

## Higher Dimensions: Ellipsoids

In $n$ dimensions, a positive definite quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 1$ defines an ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^n$. The same logic applies: the spectral theorem gives $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$, the substitution $\mathbf{y} = Q^T \mathbf{x}$ removes all cross terms, and the ellipsoid becomes:

$$\frac{y_1^2}{(1/\sqrt{\lambda_1})^2} + \frac{y_2^2}{(1/\sqrt{\lambda_2})^2} + \cdots + \frac{y_n^2}{(1/\sqrt{\lambda_n})^2} = 1$$

Semi-axes: $1/\sqrt{\lambda_i}$, aligned with eigenvectors $\mathbf{q}_i$.

The $n$-dimensional volume of this ellipsoid is:

$$V_n \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda_1 \lambda_2 \cdots \lambda_n}} = \frac{V_n}{\sqrt{\det(A)}}$$

where $V_n = \pi^{n/2}/\Gamma(n/2 + 1)$ is the volume of the unit $n$-ball. Same pattern as the $2D$ case: $\det(A)$ controls the volume, the eigenvalue ratios control the shape.

## Connection to Statistics: Covariance Ellipses

The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ of a random vector is always symmetric positive semidefinite. The **confidence ellipse** for a bivariate normal distribution is exactly the level set $\mathbf{x}^T \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{x} = c^2$ (for some constant $c$ depending on confidence level). The eigenvectors of $\Sigma$ give the principal directions of spread; the eigenvalues give the variances along those directions.

Large $\lambda_i$ of $\Sigma$: large spread in direction $\mathbf{q}_i$ $\to$ long axis of confidence ellipse. Large $\lambda_i$ of $\Sigma^{-1} = Q \Lambda^{-1} Q^T$: small $1/\lambda_i$ $\to$ short axis of $\mathbf{x}^T \Sigma^{-1} \mathbf{x} = c^2$.

The geometry is the same. The ellipse for $\Sigma$ and for $\Sigma^{-1}$ are inverses of each other in axis length: where $\Sigma$ is elongated, $\Sigma^{-1}$ is compressed.

---

## Summary

The quadratic form $ax^2 + 2bxy + cy^2$ is $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ for $A = \begin{bmatrix}a&b\b&c\end{bmatrix}$. The level set equals 1 is an ellipse when $A$ is positive definite, a hyperbola when $A$ is indefinite, and empty when $A$ is negative definite. The spectral theorem removes the cross term by rotating coordinates to align with the eigenvectors: in that frame the ellipse is $\lambda_1 u^2 + \lambda_2 v^2 = 1$, with semi-axes $1/\sqrt{\lambda_1}$ and $1/\sqrt{\lambda_2}$. The cross term $2bxy$ is not a property of the ellipse itself — it is an artifact of describing a tilted ellipse in an axis-aligned coordinate system. The eigenvectors find the tilt; the eigenvalues find the lengths.
