
Two square matrices $A$ and $B$ are **similar** if there exists an invertible matrix $M$ such that

$$B = M^{-1}AM$$

We write $A \sim B$. The matrix $M$ is called the **change of basis matrix** (or similarity transformation). Note carefully: $M$ is not unique. There are generally infinitely many matrices that witness the similarity between $A$ and $B$.

The mental image I keep coming back to: $A$ and $B$ are the _same linear transformation_, just described in different bases. $M$ is the dictionary that translates between those bases.

---

## The Geometric Picture

Let $T: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ be a linear transformation. Once we fix a basis, $T$ gets represented as a matrix. If I use the standard basis ${e_1, \ldots, e_n}$, I get some matrix $A$. If I use a different basis ${v_1, \ldots, v_n}$ — the columns of $M$ — I get a different matrix $B$.

The formula $B = M^{-1}AM$ is exactly this change of coordinates:

$$\underbrace{M^{-1}}_{\text{back to new basis}} \underbrace{A}_{\text{apply } T \text{ in standard coords}} \underbrace{M}_{\text{go to standard coords}}$$

So $B$ applies $T$ entirely within the new coordinate system. Same transformation, different description.

---

## Similarity is an Equivalence Relation

**Claim.** The relation $\sim$ is an equivalence relation on $M_n(\mathbb{R})$.

**Proof.**

_Reflexivity._ $A = I^{-1}AI$, so $A \sim A$.

_Symmetry._ Suppose $B = M^{-1}AM$. Then $A = MBM^{-1} = (M^{-1})^{-1}B(M^{-1})$, so $A \sim B$.

_Transitivity._ Suppose $B = M^{-1}AM$ and $C = N^{-1}BN$. Then $$C = N^{-1}(M^{-1}AM)N = (MN)^{-1}A(MN)$$ so $A \sim C$. $\square$

This partitions $M_n(\mathbb{R})$ into **similarity classes**. Every matrix in a class represents the same linear transformation (in possibly different bases). Diagonalization, when possible, is just finding the nicest representative of the class.

## Invariants Under Similarity

Here is the key question: what properties of a matrix are preserved when you pass to a similar matrix? These are called **similarity invariants**, and they are the intrinsic properties of the underlying transformation, independent of any choice of basis.

### Determinant

$$\det(M^{-1}AM) = \det(M^{-1})\det(A)\det(M) = \frac{1}{\det M} \cdot \det A \cdot \det M = \det A$$

### Trace

$$\text{tr}(M^{-1}AM) = \text{tr}(A M M^{-1}) = \text{tr}(A)$$

using the cyclic property of trace: $\text{tr}(XY) = \text{tr}(YX)$.

### Rank

$B = M^{-1}AM$ and $M$ is invertible, so multiplication by $M$ and $M^{-1}$ are bijections. They cannot change the rank. Formally: $\text{rank}(M^{-1}AM) = \text{rank}(A)$.

### Characteristic Polynomial

$$\det(\lambda I - M^{-1}AM) = \det(M^{-1}(\lambda I - A)M) = \det(\lambda I - A)$$

So similar matrices have the **same characteristic polynomial**, hence the **same eigenvalues with the same algebraic multiplicities**.

This is the big one. The characteristic polynomial is a complete invariant of the similarity class in many situations.

### Eigenvalues

Immediate from the characteristic polynomial. But it is worth checking directly: if $Ax = \lambda x$, then $$B(M^{-1}x) = M^{-1}AM(M^{-1}x) = M^{-1}Ax = \lambda(M^{-1}x)$$

So $M^{-1}x$ is an eigenvector of $B$ with eigenvalue $\lambda$. Similarity transforms eigenvectors by $M^{-1}$.

### What is NOT Preserved

The **eigenvectors themselves** change (they transform by $M^{-1}$). The individual matrix entries obviously change. The null space vectors change (though the dimension of the null space, i.e. the nullity, is preserved since it equals $n - \text{rank}$).

## Diagonalization as a Special Case

The most important application of similarity: $A$ is **diagonalizable** if and only if $A$ is similar to a diagonal matrix.

If $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, form the matrix

$$S = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & & | \ v_1 & v_2 & \cdots & v_n \ | & | & & | \end{bmatrix}$$

Then $AS = S\Lambda$ where

$$\Lambda = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \lambda_2 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix}$$

Since $S$ is invertible (columns are linearly independent),

$$A = S\Lambda S^{-1} \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \Lambda = S^{-1}AS$$

So $A \sim \Lambda$. The eigenvector matrix $S$ is the change of basis: in the eigenvector basis, $T$ is just scaling by $\lambda_i$ along the $i$-th coordinate axis.

**When does this fail?** When $A$ does not have $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors — i.e., when some eigenvalue has geometric multiplicity strictly less than its algebraic multiplicity. In that case $A$ is not diagonalizable but it still has a Jordan normal form (see the note on Jordan form).

---

## A Concrete Example

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Both have characteristic polynomial $(\lambda - 2)^2$ and the same trace $4$, same determinant $4$. Are they similar?

$B = 2I$, and $2I$ commutes with every matrix: $M^{-1}(2I)M = 2I$ for any invertible $M$. So the only matrix similar to $2I$ is $2I$ itself.

$A \neq 2I$, so $A \not\sim B$.

This shows that **same characteristic polynomial does not imply similar**. The characteristic polynomial does not distinguish between $A$ and $B$ here. For $2 \times 2$ matrices with repeated eigenvalues, you need to check the Jordan structure.

Now take a case where diagonalization works. Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = 2$.

For $\lambda_1 = 3$: solve $(A - 3I)x = 0$:

$$A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & -1 \end{bmatrix} \longrightarrow v_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

For $\lambda_2 = 2$: solve $(A - 2I)x = 0$:

$$A - 2I = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \longrightarrow v_2 = \begin{bmatrix} -1 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

So

$$S = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad S^{-1} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Check: $S^{-1}AS$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & -1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & -2 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \Lambda$$

Good. $A$ is similar to $\Lambda = \text{diag}(3, 2)$.

## Powers of a Matrix via Similarity

One payoff of diagonalization: computing $A^k$ becomes easy.

$$A = S\Lambda S^{-1} \implies A^k = S\Lambda^k S^{-1}$$

where

$$\Lambda^k = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1^k & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \lambda_2^k & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_n^k \end{bmatrix}$$

This is the core reason physicists and engineers care so much about eigenvalues. Instead of multiplying $A$ by itself $k$ times, you just raise scalars to powers. The matrix machinery collapses into arithmetic.

---

## The Converse Question: When Are Two Matrices Similar?

Completely classifying when $A \sim B$ over $\mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$) is the content of **Jordan canonical form theory**. Over $\mathbb{C}$, the answer is:

$A \sim B$ if and only if they have the same Jordan normal form (up to reordering of blocks).

The Jordan form is the canonical representative of each similarity class. Every matrix is similar to a unique Jordan form (up to permutation of blocks). For diagonalizable matrices, the Jordan form is just $\Lambda$, which is why two diagonalizable matrices over $\mathbb{C}$ are similar if and only if they have the same eigenvalues with the same multiplicities.

---

## Remarks Worth Remembering

- Similarity is a statement about a linear transformation, not about the matrix itself. The matrix is just a coordinate representation.
- The set of matrices similar to a given $A$ is its **similarity orbit** under the conjugation action of $GL_n$.
- Trace and determinant being preserved is not a coincidence: both are the coefficient of $\lambda^{n-1}$ and the constant term of the characteristic polynomial, which we already know is invariant.
- Strang's 18.06 lectures treat diagonalization as the central goal of the course, and rightly so — similar matrices are the framework that makes that goal precise.
