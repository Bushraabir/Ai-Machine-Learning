## The Idea

A diagonal matrix is trivial to work with. Powers, exponentials, inverses -- everything reduces to scalar operations on the diagonal entries. The hope is that for a given matrix $A$, there exists a basis of eigenvectors in which $A$ acts as a diagonal matrix. When that basis exists, we can change coordinates, do the computation diagonally, and change back.

This is the entire point of diagonalization. It is a change of basis, not a change of the matrix itself.

---

## The Factorization

Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ matrix with $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors $\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_n$ with corresponding eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$. Form the matrices:

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \ \mathbf{x}_1 & \mathbf{x}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{x}_n \ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix}, \qquad D = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1 & & & \ & \lambda_2 & & \ & & \ddots & \ & & & \lambda_n \end{pmatrix}$$

Then:

$$\boxed{A = PDP^{-1}}$$

The columns of $P$ are the eigenvectors. The diagonal of $D$ holds the eigenvalues in the matching order.

### Why This Works

Look at $AP$. The $k$-th column of $AP$ is $A\mathbf{x}_k = \lambda_k \mathbf{x}_k$. On the other side, $PD$ has $k$-th column $\lambda_k \mathbf{x}_k$ (since $D$ just scales each column of $P$ by the corresponding diagonal entry). So $AP = PD$ column by column. Since the eigenvectors are linearly independent, $P$ is invertible, and right-multiplying by $P^{-1}$ gives $A = PDP^{-1}$.

This is not magic. The equation $AP = PD$ is just the $n$ eigenvalue equations $A\mathbf{x}_k = \lambda_k\mathbf{x}_k$ stacked side by side as columns.

---

## When Is $A$ Diagonalizable?

$A$ is diagonalizable if and only if it has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors.

Sufficient conditions:

- $A$ has $n$ **distinct** eigenvalues. Then the eigenvectors are automatically linearly independent (by the theorem in the eigenvectors note), so $A$ is diagonalizable.
- $A$ is **real symmetric** ($A = A^T$). The spectral theorem guarantees $n$ orthogonal eigenvectors regardless of repeated eigenvalues.

When diagonalization fails:

- A repeated eigenvalue $\lambda$ with algebraic multiplicity $m_a > m_g$ geometric multiplicity is defective. We cannot fill out the full basis. $A$ is not diagonalizable. This is where Jordan form enters.

The key check is always: for each eigenvalue, does $\dim\ker(A - \lambda I)$ equal the algebraic multiplicity of $\lambda$? If yes for all eigenvalues, diagonalizable. If no for any one, not.

## Worked Example: $2 \times 2$

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

### Step 1: Eigenvalues

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 2 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = (\lambda-5)(\lambda-2) = 0$$

$$\lambda_1 = 5, \qquad \lambda_2 = 2$$

Two distinct eigenvalues. Diagonalizable guaranteed.

### Step 2: Eigenvectors

**For $\lambda_1 = 5$:**

$$A - 5I = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 2 \ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{row reduce}} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Free variable $x_2$. Set $x_2 = 1$, then $x_1 = 2$:

$$\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**For $\lambda_2 = 2$:**

$$A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 2 \ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{\text{row reduce}} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Free variable $x_2$. Set $x_2 = -1$, then $x_1 = 1$:

$$\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

### Step 3: Form $P$ and $D$

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad D = \begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

### Step 4: Compute $P^{-1}$

For a $2\times 2$ matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{pmatrix}$, the inverse is $\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$.

$$\det(P) = (2)(-1) - (1)(1) = -3$$

$$P^{-1} = \frac{1}{-3}\begin{pmatrix} -1 & -1 \ -1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/3 & 1/3 \ 1/3 & -2/3 \end{pmatrix}$$

### Verification: $PDP^{-1} = A$

$$PD = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 5 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 & 2 \ 5 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$(PD)P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 10 & 2 \ 5 & -2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} 1/3 & 1/3 \ 1/3 & -2/3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 10/3 + 2/3 & 10/3 - 4/3 \ 5/3 - 2/3 & 5/3 + 4/3 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = A$$

## Worked Example: $3 \times 3$

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 3 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

### Step 1: Eigenvalues

Lower triangular, so eigenvalues are the diagonal entries:

$$\lambda_1 = 2, \qquad \lambda_2 = 3, \qquad \lambda_3 = 4$$

All distinct. Diagonalizable.

### Step 2: Eigenvectors

**For $\lambda_1 = 2$:**

$$A - 2I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2} \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Reorder rows:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row 2: $x_3 = 0$. Row 1: $x_1 + x_2 = 0$, so $x_2$ is free. Set $x_2 = -1$, $x_1 = 1$:

$$\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ -1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

**For $\lambda_2 = 3$:**

$$A - 3I = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 \leftarrow R_2 + R_1} \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_3 \leftarrow R_3 + R_1 \cdot (-1)} \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row 1: $x_1 = 0$. Row 3: $x_2 + x_3 = 0$, so $x_3$ is free. Set $x_3 = -1$, $x_2 = 1$:

$$\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**For $\lambda_3 = 4$:**

$$A - 4I = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 0 & 0 \ 1 & -1 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row 1: $x_1 = 0$. Then Row 2: $-x_2 = 0 \Rightarrow x_2 = 0$. Variable $x_3$ is free. Set $x_3 = 1$:

$$\mathbf{x}_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

### Step 3: Form $P$ and $D$

$$P = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ -1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & -1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad D = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 3 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

## The Power Formula

The reason to diagonalize is computation. Once $A = PDP^{-1}$:

$$A^k = (PDP^{-1})^k = PD^kP^{-1}$$

because the $P^{-1}P$ in the middle collapse:

$$(PDP^{-1})(PDP^{-1}) = PD(P^{-1}P)DP^{-1} = PD^2P^{-1}$$

and by induction $A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$. Since $D$ is diagonal:

$$D^k = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_1^k & & \ & \ddots & \ & & \lambda_n^k \end{pmatrix}$$

What would require multiplying $n \times n$ matrices $k$ times is reduced to raising $n$ scalars to the $k$-th power.

### Example

With the $2 \times 2$ matrix above, $A = PDP^{-1}$ with $\lambda_1 = 5$, $\lambda_2 = 2$:

$$A^{10} = PD^{10}P^{-1}, \qquad D^{10} = \begin{pmatrix} 5^{10} & 0 \ 0 & 2^{10} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 9765625 & 0 \ 0 & 1024 \end{pmatrix}$$

Computing $A^{10}$ by repeated multiplication would be painful. Diagonalization makes it immediate.

## The Expansion in Eigenvectors

The decomposition $A = PDP^{-1}$ has another reading. Write any vector $\mathbf{b}$ in the eigenvector basis:

$$\mathbf{b} = c_1\mathbf{x}_1 + c_2\mathbf{x}_2 + \cdots + c_n\mathbf{x}_n$$

where the coefficients are given by $\mathbf{c} = P^{-1}\mathbf{b}$. Then:

$$A\mathbf{b} = c_1\lambda_1\mathbf{x}_1 + c_2\lambda_2\mathbf{x}_2 + \cdots + c_n\lambda_n\mathbf{x}_n$$

$$A^k\mathbf{b} = c_1\lambda_1^k\mathbf{x}_1 + c_2\lambda_2^k\mathbf{x}_2 + \cdots + c_n\lambda_n^k\mathbf{x}_n$$

Each eigenvector component evolves independently, scaled by its own eigenvalue to the $k$-th power. The eigenvalues decouple the dynamics. This is why eigenvalues govern long-term behavior: whichever $|\lambda_i|$ is largest dominates $A^k\mathbf{b}$ as $k \to \infty$.

## A Non-Diagonalizable Example

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda = 3$ (algebraic multiplicity 2).

$$A - 3I = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Null space: $x_2 = 0$, $x_1$ free. Only one linearly independent eigenvector: $\begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}$.

Geometric multiplicity 1, algebraic multiplicity 2. We cannot form a $2 \times 2$ invertible $P$ from one eigenvector. $A$ is not diagonalizable.

This is the Jordan block $J_2(3)$. Its behavior is fundamentally different from a diagonal matrix -- $A^k$ grows like $k\lambda^{k-1}$ due to the off-diagonal term, not purely like $\lambda^k$.

---

## Diagonalization of Symmetric Matrices

For real symmetric matrices ($A = A^T$), the spectral theorem gives something stronger:

$$A = Q\Lambda Q^T = Q\Lambda Q^{-1}$$

where $Q$ is **orthogonal** ($Q^T = Q^{-1}$, columns are orthonormal) and $\Lambda$ is diagonal with real eigenvalues. This is not just diagonalizability -- it is orthogonal diagonalizability, a much cleaner structure.

Every real symmetric matrix is diagonalizable, even with repeated eigenvalues. And the eigenvectors from different eigenspaces are automatically orthogonal. That is the content of the spectral theorem, which deserves its own note.

---

## Summary of the Procedure

1. Find eigenvalues: solve $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$.
2. For each $\lambda$: solve $(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$, find a basis for the eigenspace.
3. Check: total number of linearly independent eigenvectors equals $n$? If yes, proceed.
4. Form $P$ with eigenvectors as columns, $D$ with eigenvalues on diagonal (matching order).
5. Compute $P^{-1}$.
6. Verify $AP = PD$ (check a couple of columns before trusting the full product).

$$\boxed{A = PDP^{-1}, \qquad A^k = PD^kP^{-1}}$$

The columns of $P$ tell you the right basis. The diagonal of $D$ tells you how $A$ stretches along each basis direction. Together they reduce the matrix to its simplest form.
