## Why This Belongs in a Linear Algebra Course

At first it looks like analysis. Sines and cosines, integrals, convergence. But the core idea is pure linear algebra: find the best representation of a vector in terms of a basis. The only difference from everything we have done so far is that the vector space is now a space of functions, and the basis is infinite.

The philosophy is exactly the decomposition $\mathbf{u}_0 = c_1\mathbf{x}_1 + c_2\mathbf{x}_2 + \cdots$ from [[Matrix Powers]], except now the sum runs to infinity and the basis vectors are $1, \cos x, \sin x, \cos 2x, \sin 2x, \ldots$. The coefficients are found by the same projection logic that gives coordinates in any orthogonal basis.

---

## The Function Space

Let $V$ be the space of functions that are square-integrable on $[-\pi, \pi]$. The inner product on $V$ is:

$$\langle f, g \rangle = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x),g(x),dx$$

This is the continuous analogue of the dot product $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum u_i v_i$. Two functions are **orthogonal** if $\langle f, g \rangle = 0$.

The trigonometric functions are orthogonal with respect to this inner product. This is the fact that makes Fourier series work.

---

## Orthogonality of the Basis Functions

The key identities, all verified by direct integration:

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(mx)\cos(nx),dx = \begin{cases} 0 & m \neq n \ \pi & m = n \neq 0 \ 2\pi & m = n = 0 \end{cases}$$

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \sin(mx)\sin(nx),dx = \begin{cases} 0 & m \neq n \ \pi & m = n \neq 0 \end{cases}$$

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} \cos(mx)\sin(nx),dx = 0 \quad \text{for all } m, n$$

The last identity holds because $\cos(mx)\sin(nx)$ is an odd function and the interval is symmetric. The first two follow from the product-to-sum identities.

Collect these into a single statement: the functions

$$\left{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}},; \frac{\cos x}{\sqrt{\pi}},; \frac{\sin x}{\sqrt{\pi}},; \frac{\cos 2x}{\sqrt{\pi}},; \frac{\sin 2x}{\sqrt{\pi}},; \ldots\right}$$

form an **orthonormal** set in $V$. The denominators normalize each function to unit length under $\langle \cdot, \cdot \rangle$.

---

## The Fourier Series

For a function $f(x)$ on $[-\pi, \pi]$, the **Fourier series** is:

$$f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left[a_n \cos(nx) + b_n \sin(nx)\right]$$

The coefficients are found by projection -- exactly the same logic as projecting onto an orthogonal basis in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Take the inner product of both sides with the relevant basis function and use orthogonality to isolate each coefficient.

$$\boxed{a_0 = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x),dx}$$

$$\boxed{a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\cos(nx),dx, \qquad n \geq 1}$$

$$\boxed{b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\sin(nx),dx, \qquad n \geq 1}$$

The factor $a_0/2$ rather than $a_0$ is purely conventional -- it makes the formula for $a_n$ uniform for $n \geq 0$ if you use $a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\cos(nx),dx$ and note $\cos(0 \cdot x) = 1$.

> Personal note: the derivation of these formulas is not magic. Multiply both sides of the series by $\cos(mx)$ and integrate term by term. Every term on the right vanishes by orthogonality except the one where $n = m$. That surviving term gives $a_m \cdot \pi$, so $a_m = \frac{1}{\pi}\int f(x)\cos(mx),dx$. Same argument for $b_m$. This is projection. Full stop.

---

## Derivation of the Coefficient Formula

Let me do this carefully for $a_m$. Start from:

$$f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left[a_n\cos(nx) + b_n\sin(nx)\right]$$

Multiply both sides by $\cos(mx)$ and integrate over $[-\pi,\pi]$:

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\cos(mx),dx = \frac{a_0}{2}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\cos(mx),dx + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left[a_n\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\cos(nx)\cos(mx),dx + b_n\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin(nx)\cos(mx),dx\right]$$

By orthogonality:

- $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\cos(mx),dx = 0$ for $m \geq 1$, so the $a_0$ term vanishes.
- $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\sin(nx)\cos(mx),dx = 0$ for all $n, m$, so all $b_n$ terms vanish.
- $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}\cos(nx)\cos(mx),dx = 0$ for $n \neq m$, and $= \pi$ for $n = m$.

The entire right side collapses to $a_m \cdot \pi$. So:

$$a_m = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\cos(mx),dx$$

The derivation for $b_m$ is identical, multiplying by $\sin(mx)$ instead.

## Worked Example: Square Wave

Define $f$ on $[-\pi, \pi]$ by:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} -1 & -\pi \leq x < 0 \ +1 & 0 \leq x \leq \pi \end{cases}$$

and extend periodically.

**Observation:** $f$ is odd: $f(-x) = -f(x)$. An odd function times $\cos(nx)$ (even) is odd, so the integral over a symmetric interval vanishes. Therefore $a_n = 0$ for all $n$. Only sine terms survive.

**Computing $b_n$:**

$$b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x)\sin(nx),dx = \frac{1}{\pi}\left[\int_{-\pi}^{0}(-1)\sin(nx),dx + \int_{0}^{\pi}(1)\sin(nx),dx\right]$$

Since $f$ is odd and $\sin$ is odd, $f(x)\sin(nx)$ is even. So:

$$b_n = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_{0}^{\pi}\sin(nx),dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\left[-\frac{\cos(nx)}{n}\right]_0^{\pi} = \frac{2}{\pi} \cdot \frac{1-\cos(n\pi)}{n} = \frac{2(1 - (-1)^n)}{n\pi}$$

When $n$ is even: $(-1)^n = 1$, so $b_n = 0$.

When $n$ is odd: $(-1)^n = -1$, so $b_n = \dfrac{4}{n\pi}$.

The Fourier series of the square wave is:

$$f(x) = \frac{4}{\pi}\left[\sin x + \frac{\sin 3x}{3} + \frac{\sin 5x}{5} + \frac{\sin 7x}{7} + \cdots\right] = \frac{4}{\pi}\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{\sin((2k+1)x)}{2k+1}$$

At $x = \pi/2$ all terms are $\pm 1/(2k+1)$ and $f(\pi/2) = 1$, so this gives:

$$1 = \frac{4}{\pi}\left(1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5} - \frac{1}{7} + \cdots\right) \implies \frac{\pi}{4} = 1 - \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{5} - \cdots$$

The Leibniz formula for $\pi$ falls out as a special case of Fourier series at a single point.

## Worked Example: Sawtooth Wave

$$f(x) = x, \qquad x \in (-\pi, \pi)$$

Again odd, so $a_n = 0$.

$$b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} x\sin(nx),dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi} x\sin(nx),dx$$

Integrate by parts: let $u = x$, $dv = \sin(nx),dx$:

$$\int_0^{\pi} x\sin(nx),dx = \left[-\frac{x\cos(nx)}{n}\right]_0^{\pi} + \int_0^{\pi}\frac{\cos(nx)}{n},dx = -\frac{\pi\cos(n\pi)}{n} + \left[\frac{\sin(nx)}{n^2}\right]_0^{\pi}$$

The sine term vanishes (since $\sin(n\pi) = 0$). So:

$$\int_0^{\pi} x\sin(nx),dx = -\frac{\pi(-1)^n}{n} = \frac{(-1)^{n+1}\pi}{n}$$

Therefore:

$$b_n = \frac{2}{\pi} \cdot \frac{(-1)^{n+1}\pi}{n} = \frac{2(-1)^{n+1}}{n}$$

The Fourier series of $f(x) = x$ is:

$$x = 2\left[\sin x - \frac{\sin 2x}{2} + \frac{\sin 3x}{3} - \frac{\sin 4x}{4} + \cdots\right] = 2\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n}\sin(nx)$$

## Even and Odd Symmetry -- The Shortcut

Before computing any integral, check symmetry.

**If $f$ is even** ($f(-x) = f(x)$): the function has only cosine terms. All $b_n = 0$.

$$f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} a_n\cos(nx), \qquad a_n = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi}f(x)\cos(nx),dx$$

**If $f$ is odd** ($f(-x) = -f(x)$): the function has only sine terms. All $a_n = 0$.

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n\sin(nx), \qquad b_n = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^{\pi}f(x)\sin(nx),dx$$

The integration range halves and half the coefficients vanish. Always check symmetry first.

## Convergence

The Fourier series converges, but how and where depends on $f$:

**At continuity points:** the series converges to $f(x)$.

**At jump discontinuities:** the series converges to the average of the left and right limits:

$$\lim_{N\to\infty} S_N(x_0) = \frac{f(x_0^-) + f(x_0^+)}{2}$$

For the square wave at $x = 0$: $f(0^-) = -1$ and $f(0^+) = 1$, so the series converges to $0$ at $x = 0$. Check: all $\sin(n \cdot 0) = 0$, so the series is indeed $0$ there.

**Gibbs phenomenon:** near a jump discontinuity, the partial sums overshoot by approximately $9%$ of the jump height, regardless of how many terms are included. Adding more terms makes the overshoot narrower but not smaller. This is a real phenomenon, not a numerical artifact.

**Parseval's theorem:** the energy identity

$$\frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}|f(x)|^2,dx = \frac{a_0^2}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(a_n^2 + b_n^2)$$

This is the continuous analogue of $|\mathbf{v}|^2 = \sum v_i^2$ in an orthonormal basis. All the energy in $f$ is accounted for by the Fourier coefficients.

## The Complex Form

Using Euler's formula $e^{inx} = \cos(nx) + i\sin(nx)$, the Fourier series takes the cleaner form:

$$f(x) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} c_n e^{inx}$$

$$c_n = \frac{1}{2\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} f(x),e^{-inx},dx$$

The functions ${e^{inx}}_{n \in \mathbb{Z}}$ are orthogonal with respect to $\langle f, g\rangle = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f\bar{g},dx$, since:

$$\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{imx}\overline{e^{inx}},dx = \int_{-\pi}^{\pi} e^{i(m-n)x},dx = \begin{cases} 2\pi & m = n \ 0 & m \neq n \end{cases}$$

The real and complex forms carry the same information. The relation between the coefficients is:

$$c_n = \frac{a_n - ib_n}{2}, \quad c_{-n} = \frac{a_n + ib_n}{2}, \quad c_0 = \frac{a_0}{2}$$

The complex form is cleaner for computation and essential for the discrete Fourier transform and signal processing.

## The Linear Algebra Parallel

Every step here mirrors finite-dimensional linear algebra:

|Linear algebra ($\mathbb{R}^n$)|Fourier series (function space)|
|---|---|
|Vector $\mathbf{v}$|Function $f(x)$|
|Dot product $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$|Inner product $\int_{-\pi}^{\pi} fg,dx$|
|Orthonormal basis ${\mathbf{e}_i}$|Basis ${1, \cos nx, \sin nx}$|
|Coordinate $v_i = \mathbf{e}_i \cdot \mathbf{v}$|Coefficient $a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int f\cos(nx),dx$|
|$\mathbf{v} = \sum v_i \mathbf{e}_i$|$f = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum[a_n\cos nx + b_n\sin nx]$|
|$\|\mathbf{v}\|^2 = \sum v_i^2$|Parseval: $\frac{1}{\pi}\int f^2 = \frac{a_0^2}{2} + \sum(a_n^2+b_n^2)$|

The infinite sum is the only genuine novelty. The logic -- orthogonal basis, project to find coordinates, reconstruct -- is the same.

## Summary

$$\boxed{f(x) = \frac{a_0}{2} + \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left[a_n\cos(nx) + b_n\sin(nx)\right]}$$

$$a_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\cos(nx),dx, \qquad b_n = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\pi}^{\pi}f(x)\sin(nx),dx$$

Check symmetry first. Odd function: only $b_n$, integrate on $[0,\pi]$ and double. Even function: only $a_n$, integrate on $[0,\pi]$ and double. Verify the constant term $a_0/2$ is the average value of $f$ over $[-\pi,\pi]$.

The series converges to $f$ at continuity points and to the midpoint of jumps at discontinuities.
