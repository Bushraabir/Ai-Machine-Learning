## Why This Topic Exists

Last note I proved that symmetric matrices have _real_ eigenvalues. That proof implicitly acknowledged that a general real matrix might not — and that is what I want to understand now. The characteristic polynomial of an $n \times n$ real matrix has real coefficients, but its roots can be complex. When they are, what is actually happening geometrically? And what structure do those complex eigenvalues have to obey?

---

## The Basic Fact: Complex Roots Come in Conjugate Pairs

If $A$ is a real matrix and $\lambda = a + bi$ is an eigenvalue (with $b \neq 0$), then $\bar{\lambda} = a - bi$ is also an eigenvalue.

**Proof.** Suppose $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$ with $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{C}^n$, $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$. Take the complex conjugate of both sides:

$$\overline{A\mathbf{x}} = \overline{\lambda \mathbf{x}}$$

$$\bar{A}\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \bar{\lambda}\bar{\mathbf{x}}$$

But $A$ is a real matrix, so $\bar{A} = A$:

$$A\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \bar{\lambda}\bar{\mathbf{x}}$$

So $\bar{\mathbf{x}}$ is an eigenvector of $A$ with eigenvalue $\bar{\lambda}$. $\square$

Two immediate consequences:

- A real matrix of _odd_ dimension must have at least one real eigenvalue (complex roots of a real polynomial come in pairs, so an odd-degree polynomial must have at least one real root).
- The complex eigenvalues appear in conjugate pairs $a \pm bi$, and the corresponding eigenvectors also come as conjugate pairs $\mathbf{x}$ and $\bar{\mathbf{x}}$.

---

## The Canonical Example: Rotation

The $2 \times 2$ rotation matrix is the clearest place to see all of this.

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

This rotates every vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ by angle $\theta$. Over $\mathbb{R}$, no nonzero vector is sent to a scalar multiple of itself (unless $\theta = 0$ or $\theta = \pi$), so there are no real eigenvectors. The geometry is telling you something: real eigenvectors require a direction that is _preserved_ by the transformation, and pure rotation preserves no direction.

**Computing the eigenvalues:**

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta - \lambda & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta - \lambda \end{bmatrix}$$

$$= (\cos\theta - \lambda)^2 + \sin^2\theta = \lambda^2 - 2\cos\theta \cdot \lambda + 1$$

Using the quadratic formula:

$$\lambda = \frac{2\cos\theta \pm \sqrt{4\cos^2\theta - 4}}{2} = \cos\theta \pm \sqrt{\cos^2\theta - 1} = \cos\theta \pm i\sin\theta$$

So:

$$\lambda_1 = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta = e^{i\theta}, \qquad \lambda_2 = \cos\theta - i\sin\theta = e^{-i\theta}$$

Conjugate pair, as expected. Both lie on the unit circle in $\mathbb{C}$. The modulus $|\lambda| = 1$ reflects the fact that rotation preserves lengths — the eigenvalues encode this in their absolute value.

**The eigenvectors:**

For $\lambda_1 = e^{i\theta}$, solve $(A - \lambda_1 I)\mathbf{x} = 0$:

$$\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta - e^{i\theta} & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta - e^{i\theta} \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Since $\cos\theta - e^{i\theta} = -i\sin\theta$, the first row gives:

$$-i\sin\theta \cdot x_1 - \sin\theta \cdot x_2 = 0 \implies x_2 = -ix_1$$

So the eigenvector is $\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ -i \end{bmatrix}$, and by conjugation $\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ i \end{bmatrix}$ for $\lambda_2$.

These are complex vectors — there is no real direction in $\mathbb{R}^2$ that rotation fixes. The eigenvectors live in $\mathbb{C}^2$ and have no direct geometric interpretation in the original real plane.

---

## What the Complex Eigenvalue $a + bi$ Encodes

For a general $2 \times 2$ real matrix with complex eigenvalues $\lambda = a \pm bi$, write $\lambda$ in polar form:

$$\lambda = r e^{i\theta}, \qquad r = |\lambda| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}, \qquad \theta = \arctan(b/a)$$

- $r$ is the **scaling factor**: how much the transformation stretches or contracts.
- $\theta$ is the **rotation angle**: the amount of rotation mixed in.

The pure rotation matrix above had $r = 1$. A matrix that both scales and rotates would have $r \neq 1$. This is the geometric content: complex eigenvalues signal that the transformation acts like a _rotation-scaling_ in some 2D invariant subspace.

---

## The Real Canonical Form (Block Diagonalization)

Over $\mathbb{C}$, a matrix with eigenvalues $\lambda = a \pm bi$ diagonalizes to:

$$\begin{bmatrix} a + bi & 0 \ 0 & a - bi \end{bmatrix}$$

But I am working over $\mathbb{R}$ and I want a real form. The trick: instead of diagonalizing, block-diagonalize using the real and imaginary parts.

Let $\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{u} + i\mathbf{v}$ be the eigenvector for $\lambda = a + bi$ (where $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$). Then from $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$:

$$A(\mathbf{u} + i\mathbf{v}) = (a + bi)(\mathbf{u} + i\mathbf{v})$$

Expand the right side:

$$(a + bi)(\mathbf{u} + i\mathbf{v}) = a\mathbf{u} - b\mathbf{v} + i(b\mathbf{u} + a\mathbf{v})$$

Matching real and imaginary parts:

$$A\mathbf{u} = a\mathbf{u} - b\mathbf{v}$$ $$A\mathbf{v} = b\mathbf{u} + a\mathbf{v}$$

This says that $A$ acts on the real subspace $\text{span}{\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}}$ and, in the basis ${\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}}$, the matrix of this action is:

$$\begin{bmatrix} a & b \ -b & a \end{bmatrix}$$

This is the **real $2 \times 2$ block** corresponding to the complex conjugate pair $a \pm bi$. It is a rotation-scaling matrix: factor out $r = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$,

$$r\begin{bmatrix} \cos\theta & \sin\theta \ -\sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{bmatrix}$$

So in the basis of real and imaginary parts of the eigenvector, $A$ genuinely rotates and scales. The complex eigenvalue is not an artifact — it is capturing real geometric behavior that just cannot be seen from a single real eigenvector.

---

## Full Real Block Diagonal Form

For an $n \times n$ real matrix with eigenvalues:

- Real eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k$
- Complex conjugate pairs $a_j \pm b_j i$ for $j = 1, \ldots, m$ (so $k + 2m = n$)

There exists a real invertible matrix $M$ such that $A = M B M^{-1}$ where $B$ is block diagonal:

$$B = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & & & & & \ & \ddots & & & & \ & & \lambda_k & & & \ & & & \begin{bmatrix} a_1 & b_1 \ -b_1 & a_1 \end{bmatrix} & & \ & & & & \ddots & \ & & & & & \begin{bmatrix} a_m & b_m \ -b_m & a_m \end{bmatrix} \end{bmatrix}$$

Real eigenvalues give $1 \times 1$ blocks; complex conjugate pairs give $2 \times 2$ rotation-scaling blocks. No complex numbers appear anywhere in $B$ or $M$.

## A Worked Example

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is rotation by $90°$ (i.e. $\theta = \pi/2$, so $\cos\theta = 0$, $\sin\theta = 1$).

**Characteristic polynomial:**

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{bmatrix} -\lambda & -1 \ 1 & -\lambda \end{bmatrix} = \lambda^2 + 1$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda = \pm i$ (i.e. $a = 0$, $b = 1$, $r = 1$, $\theta = \pi/2$).

**Eigenvector for $\lambda = i$:**

$$(A - iI)\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} -i & -1 \ 1 & -i \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} x_1 \ x_2 \end{bmatrix} = 0$$

First row: $-ix_1 - x_2 = 0 \implies x_2 = -ix_1$. Take $x_1 = 1$:

$$\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ -i \end{bmatrix} = \underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{u}} + i\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 0 \ -1 \end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{v}}$$

**Verify the real block form.** With $a = 0$, $b = 1$:

$$A\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 1 \end{bmatrix} = a\mathbf{u} - b\mathbf{v} = 0\cdot\begin{bmatrix}1\0\end{bmatrix} - 1\cdot\begin{bmatrix}0\-1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0\1\end{bmatrix} \checkmark$$

$$A\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & -1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 0 \ -1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix} = b\mathbf{u} + a\mathbf{v} = 1\cdot\begin{bmatrix}1\0\end{bmatrix} + 0\cdot\begin{bmatrix}0\-1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1\0\end{bmatrix} \checkmark$$

The block is $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$, which is rotation by $-\pi/2$... wait, that seems off by a sign. Let me recheck.

The matrix of $A$ in the basis ${\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}}$ is found by writing $A\mathbf{u}$ and $A\mathbf{v}$ in that basis:

$A\mathbf{u} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 1 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \cdot \mathbf{u} + (-1) \cdot \mathbf{v}$, since $\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}0\-1\end{bmatrix}$.

$A\mathbf{v} = \begin{bmatrix}1\0\end{bmatrix} = 1 \cdot \mathbf{u} + 0 \cdot \mathbf{v}$.

So the block is $\begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. This matches $\begin{bmatrix} a & b \ -b & a \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$. The sign convention in the formula is consistent — the off-diagonal entries are $b$ and $-b$, which here are $1$ and $-1$. Good.

## Connection to Powers and Exponentials

If $A$ has eigenvalues $re^{\pm i\theta}$, then $A^k$ has eigenvalues $r^k e^{\pm ik\theta}$. In the real block picture, $A^k$ corresponds to a block

$$r^k \begin{bmatrix} \cos(k\theta) & \sin(k\theta) \ -\sin(k\theta) & \cos(k\theta) \end{bmatrix}$$

This is exactly what you expect: applying a rotation-scaling $k$ times scales by $r^k$ and rotates by $k\theta$.

For the matrix exponential $e^{At}$, the complex eigenvalue $a + bi$ contributes $e^{(a+bi)t} = e^{at}(\cos bt + i\sin bt)$. In real block form the corresponding block of $e^{At}$ is:

$$e^{at}\begin{bmatrix} \cos(bt) & \sin(bt) \ -\sin(bt) & \cos(bt) \end{bmatrix}$$

This is the oscillatory behavior you see in solutions to systems of ODEs with complex eigenvalues: $e^{at}$ is the envelope (growing if $a > 0$, decaying if $a < 0$, constant if $a = 0$), and $\cos bt$, $\sin bt$ are the oscillations. The real part of the eigenvalue governs stability; the imaginary part governs frequency.

---

## Why Symmetric Matrices Avoid This

Pulling the thread from the last note: symmetric matrices have $\lambda = \bar{\lambda}$ for every eigenvalue, which forces $b = 0$. The imaginary part is zero. No rotation blocks — only real $1 \times 1$ blocks. Symmetry is the condition that kills all the oscillatory behavior. A symmetric matrix only stretches and reflects, never rotates.

Antisymmetric matrices ($A = -A^T$), on the other hand, have purely imaginary eigenvalues ($a = 0$), giving pure rotation with $r = 1$ — no growth, no decay, just oscillation.

---

## Things to Come Back To

- The Jordan form over $\mathbb{C}$ when eigenvalues repeat — the block diagonal picture breaks down and you get Jordan blocks instead of diagonal entries.
- Stability of linear ODE systems: eigenvalue real parts determine whether solutions blow up, decay, or oscillate at constant amplitude.
- Normal matrices: the class of matrices (including symmetric, antisymmetric, and orthogonal) that diagonalize over $\mathbb{C}$ with an orthonormal basis. Symmetric is the real special case.

---

## Summary

A real matrix with complex eigenvalues $a \pm bi$ cannot be diagonalized over $\mathbb{R}$, but it can be block-diagonalized with $2 \times 2$ rotation-scaling blocks $\begin{bmatrix} a & b \ -b & a \end{bmatrix}$. The complex eigenvalue is not a failure of the real picture — it is the fingerprint of genuine rotational behavior in a 2D invariant subspace. The modulus $r = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ measures scaling; the argument $\theta = \arctan(b/a)$ measures rotation. Complex roots of a real characteristic polynomial always come in conjugate pairs because taking the complex conjugate of an eigenvector equation produces the conjugate eigenvector equation — and nothing about the real matrix breaks that symmetry.
