
## Why Compare These at All?

Pivots come from elimination. Eigenvalues come from the characteristic polynomial. On the surface they feel like they belong to completely different parts of the subject — one is a computational procedure, the other is a spectral property. But for symmetric matrices they are measuring the same underlying thing from two different angles, and the signs of the two sets of numbers agree in a precise sense.

This is not a coincidence. It is a theorem, and understanding it properly ties together the two biggest threads of 18.06.

---

## Quick Recall: What Each One Is

**Pivots** are the diagonal entries that appear during Gaussian elimination, after all row operations that use rows _above_ the current row. For a symmetric positive definite matrix, elimination can always be done without row exchanges, and the pivots are the numbers $d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n$ in the factorization $A = LDL^T$.

**Eigenvalues** are the roots of $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$. For a real symmetric matrix they are all real, and they are the diagonal entries of $\Lambda$ in $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$.

Two very different procedures. Same matrix. What do they have in common?

---

## The Connection: Signs Agree

**Theorem.** Let $A$ be a real symmetric matrix. Then the number of positive pivots equals the number of positive eigenvalues, and the number of negative pivots equals the number of negative eigenvalues.

In particular:

- $A$ is positive definite $\iff$ all pivots $> 0$ $\iff$ all eigenvalues $> 0$.
- $A$ is negative definite $\iff$ all pivots $< 0$ $\iff$ all eigenvalues $< 0$.
- $A$ is indefinite $\iff$ pivots (or eigenvalues) have mixed signs.

This is the **Law of Inertia** (Sylvester's law of inertia, to be precise): the number of positive, negative, and zero eigenvalues is an invariant of the matrix — it does not change under any congruence transformation $A \mapsto S^T A S$ for invertible $S$.

---

## The Product Relationship

Before proving the sign-count, notice a simpler relationship that holds for any square matrix:

**Product of pivots = determinant of $A$.**

This is immediate from $A = LDL^T$: since $\det(L) = \det(L^T) = 1$ (triangular with 1s on diagonal), $\det(A) = \det(D) = d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n$.

**Product of eigenvalues = determinant of $A$.**

This follows from the characteristic polynomial: $\det(A - \lambda I) = (\lambda_1 - \lambda)(\lambda_2 - \lambda)\cdots(\lambda_n - \lambda)$, so setting $\lambda = 0$ gives $\det(A) = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 \cdots \lambda_n$.

So both products equal $\det(A)$. But they are generally not equal entry-by-entry — individual pivots and eigenvalues are different numbers. What is preserved is only the _count of signs_.

**Trace relationship as well:**

**Sum of pivots** is generally not $\text{tr}(A)$.

**Sum of eigenvalues** = $\text{tr}(A)$.

(The trace equals the sum of diagonal entries, and also equals the sum of eigenvalues — immediate from the characteristic polynomial by comparing the coefficient of $\lambda^{n-1}$.)

So eigenvalues own the trace; both own the determinant (via product).

---

## Why the Sign Counts Agree: The Congruence Argument

This is the part worth understanding carefully.

The $LDL^T$ factorization of a symmetric matrix $A$ is a congruence: $A = L D L^T$ where $L$ is lower triangular with 1s on the diagonal. Congruence means $A = S^T D S$ for $S = L^T$ (invertible). The diagonal matrix $D$ has the pivots on its diagonal.

Sylvester's law of inertia says: **two symmetric matrices that are congruent have the same number of positive, negative, and zero diagonal entries when diagonalized**. More precisely, the number of positive eigenvalues is preserved under congruence — it is an invariant of the congruence class.

Now, $D = \text{diag}(d_1, \ldots, d_n)$ is already diagonal, so its eigenvalues _are_ its diagonal entries, which are the pivots. And $A$ is congruent to $D$. So the number of positive eigenvalues of $A$ equals the number of positive eigenvalues of $D$, which equals the number of positive pivots.

**Proof of Sylvester's law of inertia (sketch).** Suppose $A$ and $B = S^T A S$ are congruent with $S$ invertible. Suppose for contradiction that $A$ has $p$ positive eigenvalues but $B$ has fewer. Then $B$ has a subspace of dimension $> n - p$ on which $\mathbf{x}^T B \mathbf{x} \leq 0$. Pulling back through $S$ (which is a bijection on $\mathbb{R}^n$) gives a subspace of the same dimension on which $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} \leq 0$. But by the spectral theorem, $A$ with $p$ positive eigenvalues has a $p$-dimensional subspace on which $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$. A dimension count shows these two subspaces must intersect nontrivially — contradiction. $\square$

The formal version of this argument uses the min-max characterization of eigenvalues (Courant-Fischer), which I have not covered yet. But the idea is dimension counting: you cannot have a large subspace of positivity and a large subspace of non-positivity coexist in $\mathbb{R}^n$ without overlapping.

## A Concrete Example

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 2 \ 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Finding the pivots via elimination:**

First pivot: $d_1 = 2$ (the $(1,1)$ entry).

Eliminate: subtract $\frac{2}{2} = 1$ times row 1 from row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 2 \ 2 - 1\cdot 2 & 5 - 1\cdot 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 2 \ 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

Second pivot: $d_2 = 3$.

Pivots: $d_1 = 2$, $d_2 = 3$. Both positive.

**Finding the eigenvalues:**

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (2-\lambda)(5-\lambda) - 4 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 6 = (\lambda - 1)(\lambda - 6)$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = 6$. Both positive.

**Checking the relationships:**

Product: $d_1 d_2 = 6 = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 = \det(A)$. $\checkmark$

Sum: $d_1 + d_2 = 5 \neq 7 = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = \text{tr}(A)$.

Sign count: both pivots positive, both eigenvalues positive. $\checkmark$

## Another Example: Indefinite Matrix

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Pivots:**

First pivot: $d_1 = 1$.

Eliminate: subtract $2$ times row 1 from row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 0 & 1 - 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 0 & -3 \end{bmatrix}$$

Second pivot: $d_2 = -3$.

Pivots: $1$ and $-3$. One positive, one negative.

**Eigenvalues:**

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (1-\lambda)^2 - 4 = \lambda^2 - 2\lambda - 3 = (\lambda - 3)(\lambda + 1)$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 3$, $\lambda_2 = -1$. One positive, one negative.

**Sign count:** one positive pivot, one positive eigenvalue; one negative pivot, one negative eigenvalue. $\checkmark$

**Product:** $d_1 d_2 = -3 = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 = \det(A)$. $\checkmark$

The individual numbers $1, -3$ (pivots) and $3, -1$ (eigenvalues) are completely different. But the sign pattern $+, -$ is the same.

## The Subdeterminant Test: A Third Characterization

For positive definiteness, there is a third equivalent condition involving the **upper-left submatrices** (also called leading principal submatrices).

Define $A_k$ as the $k \times k$ upper-left submatrix of $A$:

$$A_1 = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} \end{bmatrix}, \quad A_2 = \begin{bmatrix} a_{11} & a_{12} \ a_{21} & a_{22} \end{bmatrix}, \quad \ldots, \quad A_n = A$$

**Theorem.** $A$ is positive definite $\iff$ $\det(A_k) > 0$ for all $k = 1, \ldots, n$.

The connection to pivots: the pivots are the ratios of consecutive subdeterminants:

$$d_k = \frac{\det(A_k)}{\det(A_{k-1})}$$

(with $\det(A_0) = 1$ by convention). So the pivots are all positive iff all the subdeterminants are positive, iff all the eigenvalues are positive. Three equivalent tests for the same thing.

For the example $A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 2 \ 2 & 5 \end{bmatrix}$: $\det(A_1) = 2 > 0$, $\det(A_2) = 10 - 4 = 6 > 0$. Positive definite. $\checkmark$

---

## The Full Picture for Symmetric Matrices

|Property|Pivots|Eigenvalues|Subdeterminants|
|---|---|---|---|
|Positive definite|all $d_k > 0$|all $\lambda_k > 0$|all $\det(A_k) > 0$|
|Positive semidefinite|all $d_k \geq 0$|all $\lambda_k \geq 0$|all $\det(A_k) \geq 0$|
|Negative definite|all $d_k < 0$|all $\lambda_k < 0$|alternating signs|
|Indefinite|mixed signs|mixed signs|—|

The alternating sign condition for negative definiteness: $\det(A_1) < 0$, $\det(A_2) > 0$, $\det(A_3) < 0$, etc. This follows because $\det(A_k) = d_1 d_2 \cdots d_k$, and if all $d_k < 0$ then $d_1 d_2 \cdots d_k$ has sign $(-1)^k$.

## Why Pivots Are Often More Useful Computationally

Eigenvalues require solving $\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$, which for large $n$ is a degree-$n$ polynomial — completely impractical. Pivots come from elimination, which costs $O(n^3)$ and is stable. So in practice, if I want to check positive definiteness of a large matrix, I run Cholesky factorization (the symmetric positive definite version of $LU$) and check whether it succeeds without encountering a zero or negative pivot. No eigenvalue computation needed.

Eigenvalues win theoretically — they give more information (actual values, not just signs) and they are basis-independent (trace, determinant). Pivots win computationally.

## Connection to $A = LDL^T$ More Explicitly

Write out the $LDL^T$ factorization for a $3\times 3$ case to make it concrete. Suppose:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} a & b & c \ b & d & e \ c & e & f \end{bmatrix}$$

After elimination:

$$L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ b/a & 1 & 0 \ c/a & \ell_{32} & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad D = \begin{bmatrix} d_1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & d_2 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & d_3 \end{bmatrix}$$

where $d_1 = a$, $d_2 = d - b^2/a$, $d_3 = f - c^2/a - (e - bc/a)^2/d_2$. Each pivot depends on all the entries above it — which is why pivots and eigenvalues are individually different numbers even though their sign patterns match.

The factorization $A = L D L^T$ says $A$ is congruent to $D$. Sylvester's law then guarantees the sign counts of $D$'s diagonal (the pivots) match the sign counts of $A$'s eigenvalues.

## Things to Come Back To

- The min-max (Courant-Fischer) theorem: it characterizes eigenvalues as solutions to variational problems over subspaces, and gives the cleanest proof of Sylvester's law.
- Cholesky factorization: for positive definite $A$, $D$ has all positive entries so we can write $D = \sqrt{D}\sqrt{D}$ and absorb the square roots into $L$, giving $A = R^T R$ for upper triangular $R$. This is Cholesky.
- Semidefinite case: when a pivot is zero, elimination stalls. The rank of $A$ equals the number of nonzero pivots, which also equals the number of nonzero eigenvalues.

---

## Summary

Pivots and eigenvalues of a symmetric matrix are not the same numbers, but they carry the same sign information. This follows from Sylvester's law of inertia: congruence ($A \mapsto S^T A S$) preserves the count of positive, negative, and zero eigenvalues, and the $LDL^T$ factorization is exactly a congruence that transforms $A$ into the diagonal matrix of its pivots. Three tests — signs of pivots, signs of eigenvalues, and signs of leading subdeterminants — are all equivalent for symmetric matrices. The practical upshot is that positive definiteness can be checked by elimination alone, without ever computing a single eigenvalue.
