## Why This Object Exists

The scalar ODE $u' = au$ has solution $u(t) = e^{at}u_0$. The matrix ODE $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ has solution $\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}_0$. The second equation is a definition: it defines $e^{At}$ to be whatever operator solves the matrix ODE. The question is whether this definition is consistent, computable, and well-behaved.

The answer is yes to all three, and the proof runs through the power series.

---

## Definition via the Power Series

$$e^{At} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \frac{(At)^k}{k!} = I + At + \frac{A^2 t^2}{2!} + \frac{A^3 t^3}{3!} + \cdots$$

This is a series of $n \times n$ matrices. It converges absolutely for every matrix $A$ and every $t \in \mathbb{R}$ (or $\mathbb{C}$). The proof uses the submultiplicativity of any matrix norm: $|A^k| \leq |A|^k$, so

$$\left|\sum_{k=0}^N \frac{(At)^k}{k!}\right| \leq \sum_{k=0}^N \frac{|A|^k |t|^k}{k!} \leq e^{|A||t|} < \infty$$

The series converges faster than the scalar exponential $e^{|A||t|}$. No issues with convergence, ever.

This definition immediately gives $e^{A \cdot 0} = I$ and $\frac{d}{dt}e^{At} = Ae^{At}$:

$$\frac{d}{dt}e^{At} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{k A^k t^{k-1}}{k!} = A\sum_{k=1}^{\infty}\frac{A^{k-1}t^{k-1}}{(k-1)!} = Ae^{At}$$

So $e^{At}$ satisfies the matrix ODE $\frac{d}{dt}e^{At} = Ae^{At}$ with initial condition $e^{A\cdot 0} = I$. This is the justification: the power series definition produces exactly the solution operator we need.

---

## The Four Fundamental Properties

Let $A$ and $B$ be $n \times n$ matrices. Then:

**1.** $e^{A \cdot 0} = I$

**2.** $\dfrac{d}{dt}e^{At} = Ae^{At} = e^{At}A$

**3.** $(e^{At})^{-1} = e^{-At}$

**4.** If $AB = BA$, then $e^{(A+B)t} = e^{At}e^{Bt}$

Property 3 follows from property 4: $e^{At}e^{-At} = e^{(A-A)t} = e^{0} = I$. So $e^{At}$ is always invertible, for every $A$ and every $t$. This is not obvious from the definition but is an immediate consequence of property 4 when commutativity holds (and $A$ commutes with $-A$).

Property 4 is the dangerous one. The scalar identity $e^{a+b} = e^ae^b$ holds for all $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$. The matrix version requires $AB = BA$. Without commutativity, $e^{A+B} \neq e^Ae^B$ in general. The correction is given by the Baker-Campbell-Hausdorff formula, which involves commutators $[A,B] = AB - BA$ and is almost never a closed form.

**Counterexample for property 4 without commutativity.** Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad B = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 0 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Then $AB = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\0&0\end{pmatrix}$ and $BA = \begin{pmatrix}0&0\0&1\end{pmatrix}$, so $AB \neq BA$. One can verify that $e^{A+B} \neq e^Ae^B$ directly. The failure of commutativity is the only obstruction.

## Computing $e^{At}$: The Four Methods

### Method 1: Diagonalization

If $A = PDP^{-1}$ with $D = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n)$, then $A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$, so

$$e^{At} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(PDP^{-1})^k t^k}{k!} = P\left(\sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{D^k t^k}{k!}\right)P^{-1} = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}$$

where

$$e^{Dt} = \begin{pmatrix} e^{\lambda_1 t} & & & \ & e^{\lambda_2 t} & & \ & & \ddots & \ & & & e^{\lambda_n t} \end{pmatrix}$$

This is the main method. It reduces the matrix exponential to scalar exponentials applied to the eigenvalues. From [[Matrix Powers]], $A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$; replace $\lambda_i^k$ with $e^{\lambda_i t}$.

**Full worked example.** Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues: $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = -1$ (upper triangular, read the diagonal).

For $\lambda_1 = 1$: $(A - I) = \begin{pmatrix}0&2\0&-2\end{pmatrix}$. Eigenvector $\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\end{pmatrix}$.

For $\lambda_2 = -1$: $(A + I) = \begin{pmatrix}2&2\0&0\end{pmatrix}$. Eigenvector $\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\-1\end{pmatrix}$.

$$P = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}, \quad P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}$$

(In this case $P^{-1} = P$, a coincidence. Verify: $PP^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1&1\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\0&-1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\0&1\end{pmatrix}$. Yes.)

$$e^{At} = P\begin{pmatrix}e^t & 0\0 & e^{-t}\end{pmatrix}P^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1&1\0&-1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}e^t & 0\0 & e^{-t}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$$

Multiply left two:

$$\begin{pmatrix}e^t & e^{-t}\0 & -e^{-t}\end{pmatrix}$$

Multiply by $P^{-1}$:

$$e^{At} = \begin{pmatrix}e^t & e^{-t}\0 & -e^{-t}\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}1&1\0&-1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}e^t & e^t - e^{-t}\0 & e^{-t}\end{pmatrix}$$

Check at $t=0$: $\begin{pmatrix}1&0\0&1\end{pmatrix} = I$. Good. Check $\frac{d}{dt}e^{At}\big|_{t=0} = \begin{pmatrix}1&2\0&-1\end{pmatrix} = A$. Good.

### Method 2: Jordan Form (Non-Diagonalizable Case)

When $A$ is defective, write $A = PJP^{-1}$ where $J$ is the Jordan normal form. Then $e^{At} = Pe^{Jt}P^{-1}$, and $e^{Jt}$ is block diagonal with one block per Jordan block.

For a Jordan block of size $m$ with eigenvalue $\lambda_0$:

$$J_m(\lambda_0) = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \lambda_0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & 0 & \lambda_0 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & & \ddots & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Write $J_m(\lambda_0) = \lambda_0 I + N$ where $N$ is the nilpotent shift matrix with $1$s on the superdiagonal and $N^m = 0$. Since $\lambda_0 I$ and $N$ commute:

$$e^{J_m(\lambda_0)t} = e^{\lambda_0 t}e^{Nt} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\left(I + Nt + \frac{N^2 t^2}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{N^{m-1}t^{m-1}}{(m-1)!}\right)$$

Explicitly:

$$e^{J_m(\lambda_0)t} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & t & \dfrac{t^2}{2!} & \cdots & \dfrac{t^{m-1}}{(m-1)!} \[6pt] 0 & 1 & t & \cdots & \dfrac{t^{m-2}}{(m-2)!} \[6pt] 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & \vdots \ \vdots & & & \ddots & t \ 0 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

The polynomial factors $1, t, t^2/2!, \ldots$ in the superdiagonals are the signature of a defective eigenvalue. This is exactly the Jordan block computation from [[Nondiagonalizable Matrices Jordan Form]], now applied to $e^{At}$ instead of $A^k$.

**The $2\times 2$ case explicitly.** $A = \begin{pmatrix}\lambda_0 & 1 \ 0 & \lambda_0\end{pmatrix}$:

$$e^{At} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\begin{pmatrix}1 & t \ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$$

This is the formula used in [[Second-Order ODEs]] to prove the $te^{\lambda_0 t}$ solution for repeated roots, and in [[Euler Spirals]] for the rotation generator.

---

### Method 3: Cayley-Hamilton

Every $n\times n$ matrix satisfies its own characteristic polynomial $p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$. By Cayley-Hamilton, $p(A) = 0$.

For a $2\times 2$ matrix with characteristic polynomial $\lambda^2 - T\lambda + D = 0$, this gives $A^2 = TA - DI$. Every power $A^k$ for $k \geq 2$ can be reduced to a linear combination of $I$ and $A$. Therefore $e^{At}$ is also a linear combination of $I$ and $A$:

$$e^{At} = \alpha(t)I + \beta(t)A$$

for scalar functions $\alpha(t)$ and $\beta(t)$ to be determined. Since $e^{\lambda t}$ must satisfy the same scalar recurrence when $\lambda$ is an eigenvalue, impose:

$$e^{\lambda_1 t} = \alpha(t) + \beta(t)\lambda_1$$ $$e^{\lambda_2 t} = \alpha(t) + \beta(t)\lambda_2$$

This is a $2\times 2$ linear system for $\alpha(t)$ and $\beta(t)$, solvable when $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$.

**Example.** $A = \begin{pmatrix}1&2\0&-1\end{pmatrix}$, eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = -1$.

$$e^t = \alpha + \beta, \qquad e^{-t} = \alpha - \beta$$

Solving: $\alpha = \frac{e^t + e^{-t}}{2} = \cosh t$, $\beta = \frac{e^t - e^{-t}}{2} = \sinh t$.

$$e^{At} = \cosh(t),I + \sinh(t),A = \begin{pmatrix}\cosh t & 0\,0 & \cosh t\end{pmatrix} + \begin{pmatrix}\sinh t & 2\sinh t \ 0 & -\sinh t\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}e^t & 2\sinh t \ 0 & e^{-t}\end{pmatrix}$$

This matches Method 1 since $e^t - e^{-t} = 2\sinh t$. Good.

For the repeated eigenvalue case ($\lambda_1 = \lambda_2 = \lambda_0$), differentiate the condition with respect to $\lambda$:

$$e^{\lambda_0 t} = \alpha + \beta\lambda_0, \qquad te^{\lambda_0 t} = \beta$$

giving $\beta = te^{\lambda_0 t}$ and $\alpha = (1 - \lambda_0 t)e^{\lambda_0 t}$.

---

### Method 4: Putzer's Algorithm

An alternative to Cayley-Hamilton that avoids solving systems. Write

$$e^{At} = r_1(t)I + r_2(t)(A - \lambda_1 I)$$

for $2\times 2$ matrices, where $r_1$ and $r_2$ satisfy

$$r_1' = \lambda_1 r_1, \quad r_1(0) = 1 \implies r_1(t) = e^{\lambda_1 t}$$ $$r_2' = \lambda_2 r_2 + r_1, \quad r_2(0) = 0$$

The second ODE is a first-order linear scalar ODE, solvable directly. This avoids matrix inversion entirely and generalizes cleanly to $n\times n$ matrices. It is particularly useful when eigenvalues are repeated.

## Geometric Interpretation

The matrix $e^{At}$ is the flow map of the vector field $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$. It takes any initial condition $\mathbf{u}_0$ and returns the position $\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}_0$ at time $t$. So:

- $e^{At}$ is a linear map from $\mathbb{R}^n$ to $\mathbb{R}^n$ for each fixed $t$.
- The family ${e^{At}}_{t \in \mathbb{R}}$ is a one-parameter group of linear maps: $e^{As}e^{At} = e^{A(s+t)}$.
- The determinant satisfies $\det(e^{At}) = e^{(\text{tr},A)t}$ (Jacobi's formula / Liouville's theorem).

The last identity is remarkable. It says the volume of any region in $\mathbb{R}^n$ expands or contracts at rate $e^{Tt}$ where $T = \text{tr}(A)$. From [[Stability of 2x2 Systems]], $T < 0$ means volume contracts, $T > 0$ means volume expands, $T = 0$ means volume is preserved (the center case).

**Proof of $\det(e^{At}) = e^{Tt}$.** For diagonalizable $A$:

$$\det(e^{At}) = \det(Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}) = \det(e^{Dt}) = \prod_{i=1}^n e^{\lambda_i t} = e^{(\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n)t} = e^{(\text{tr},A)t}$$

The general case follows by density of diagonalizable matrices.

## Special Cases Worth Knowing

### Nilpotent matrix ($A^m = 0$)

The series terminates:

$$e^{At} = I + At + \frac{A^2t^2}{2!} + \cdots + \frac{A^{m-1}t^{m-1}}{(m-1)!}$$

Polynomial in $t$, no exponentials. This is exactly the structure of a pure Jordan block with $\lambda_0 = 0$.

### Skew-symmetric matrix ($A^T = -A$)

The eigenvalues of a real skew-symmetric matrix are purely imaginary (or zero). The matrix exponential $e^{At}$ is orthogonal: $(e^{At})^T = e^{A^Tt} = e^{-At} = (e^{At})^{-1}$.

The rotation matrices $e^{\Omega t}$ from [[Euler Spirals]] are the prime example. $\Omega = \begin{pmatrix}0&-1\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ is skew-symmetric, and $e^{\Omega t} = \begin{pmatrix}\cos t & -\sin t\\sin t & \cos t\end{pmatrix}$ is a rotation matrix.

More generally, $e^{At} \in SO(n)$ whenever $A$ is skew-symmetric. The skew-symmetric matrices are the Lie algebra $\mathfrak{so}(n)$, and $e^{At}$ maps into the Lie group $SO(n)$. The matrix exponential is the exponential map of the Lie group.

### Symmetric matrix ($A^T = A$)

Eigenvalues are real (spectral theorem). $e^{At}$ is symmetric: $(e^{At})^T = e^{A^Tt} = e^{At}$. If $A$ is negative definite (all eigenvalues negative), then $e^{At} \to 0$ exponentially and $|e^{At}| \leq e^{\lambda_{\max}(A)t}$.

### Scalar multiple of identity ($A = cI$)

$$e^{cIt} = e^{ct}I$$

Obvious from the series. Every matrix exponential reduces to this in each eigenspace.

## The Cayley-Hamilton Theorem and Polynomial Reduction

For an $n\times n$ matrix with characteristic polynomial $p(\lambda) = \prod_{i=1}^n(\lambda - \lambda_i)$, Cayley-Hamilton gives $p(A) = 0$. This means $A^n$ is a linear combination of $I, A, \ldots, A^{n-1}$, and so is every higher power. Therefore $e^{At}$ is an $n\times n$ matrix whose entries are linear combinations of ${e^{\lambda_i t}, te^{\lambda_i t}, \ldots, t^{m_i-1}e^{\lambda_i t}}$ where $m_i$ is the size of the largest Jordan block for $\lambda_i$.

This is the definitive statement: the entries of $e^{At}$ are linear combinations of the functions $t^j e^{\lambda_i t}$ where $\lambda_i$ ranges over eigenvalues and $j$ ranges from $0$ to one less than the size of the corresponding Jordan block. No other functions appear. The eigenvalues of $A$ are exactly the exponential rates in $e^{At}$.

## The One Formula to Remember

$$\boxed{e^{At} = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}, \qquad e^{Dt} = \begin{pmatrix}e^{\lambda_1 t} & & \ & \ddots & \ & & e^{\lambda_n t}\end{pmatrix}}$$

When $A$ is not diagonalizable, replace $D$ with $J$ (Jordan form) and each diagonal block $e^{\lambda_i t}$ with the full Jordan block exponential.

The entries of $e^{At}$ are exactly the functions you need to solve $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$: linear combinations of $t^j e^{\lambda_i t}$, one for each Jordan block. Everything else --- stability, oscillation, growth, decay --- is a consequence of which $\lambda_i$ appear and what their real parts are.
