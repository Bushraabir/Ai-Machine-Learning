
A singular matrix is one where $\det(A) = 0$, equivalently where $\text{rank}(A) < n$ for an $n \times n$ matrix. The eigendecomposition breaks down badly here â€” $\lambda = 0$ is an eigenvalue with a non-trivial null space, and if the geometric multiplicity is less than the algebraic multiplicity, there is not even a full set of eigenvectors. Diagonalization fails.

SVD does not break down. It does not even flinch. A singular matrix has an SVD just like any other matrix â€” the only difference is that some singular values are exactly zero, and those zeros encode the singularity precisely.

This note is about what the SVD of a singular matrix looks like, what the zero singular values mean geometrically, and how SVD is the right language for understanding rank, null spaces, and near-singularity.

---

## What Singularity Means for the SVD

Let $A$ be $n \times n$ with $\text{rank}(A) = r < n$. The SVD is

$$A = U\Sigma V^T = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

where $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ and $\sigma_{r+1} = \cdots = \sigma_n = 0$.

Written out fully, $\Sigma$ is:

$$\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \sigma_2 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & \ddots & & & & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \sigma_r & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & & & & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

with $r$ nonzero diagonal entries and $n - r$ zeros. $\det(\Sigma) = 0$. Since $\det(A) = \det(U)\det(\Sigma)\det(V^T) = (\pm 1) \cdot 0 \cdot (\pm 1) = 0$, the singularity of $A$ is entirely carried by $\Sigma$.

The subspaces:

- ${v_1, \ldots, v_r}$: orthonormal basis for the **row space** $C(A^T)$
- ${v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n}$: orthonormal basis for the **null space** $N(A)$
- ${u_1, \ldots, u_r}$: orthonormal basis for the **column space** $C(A)$
- ${u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_n}$: orthonormal basis for the **left null space** $N(A^T)$

The null space has dimension $n - r > 0$ â€” this is the content of singularity. $A$ collapses the directions $v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n$ entirely to zero.

## A Worked Example: Rank-1 Matrix

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

Immediately: row 2 is twice row 1. $\text{rank}(A) = 1$. $\det(A) = 4 - 4 = 0$.

**Step 1: Compute $A^T A$.**

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 10 \ 10 & 20 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Eigenvalues of $A^T A$.**

$$\det(A^T A - \lambda I) = (5 - \lambda)(20 - \lambda) - 100 = \lambda^2 - 25\lambda = \lambda(\lambda - 25) = 0$$

$\lambda_1 = 25$, $\lambda_2 = 0$.

So $\sigma_1 = 5$, $\sigma_2 = 0$. One nonzero singular value. Rank 1 confirmed.

**Step 3: Eigenvectors of $A^T A$.**

For $\lambda_1 = 25$:

$$A^T A - 25I = \begin{bmatrix} -20 & 10 \ 10 & -5 \end{bmatrix} \rightarrow v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

For $\lambda_2 = 0$:

$$A^T A - 0 \cdot I = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 10 \ 10 & 20 \end{bmatrix} \rightarrow v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 2 \ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Check: $v_1^T v_2 = \frac{1}{5}(2 - 2) = 0$. Orthogonal. $\checkmark$

$$V = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 4: Left singular vectors.**

For $i = 1$ (only nonzero $\sigma$):

$$Av_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 4 \end{bmatrix} \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 5 \ 10 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{5}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix} = \sqrt{5}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}$$

$$u_1 = \frac{Av_1}{\sigma_1} = \frac{1}{5} \cdot \sqrt{5}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix} \cdot \sqrt{5}^{-1} \cdot \sqrt{5} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Wait â€” let me recompute cleanly.

$$Av_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1\cdot1 + 2\cdot2 \ 2\cdot1 + 4\cdot2 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 5 \ 10 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$u_1 = \frac{Av_1}{\sigma_1} = \frac{1}{5} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 5 \ 10 \end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

For $u_2$: need a unit vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ orthogonal to $u_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(1,2)^T$:

$$u_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 2 \ -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Step 5: Assemble.**

$$U = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} 5 & 0 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad V = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Notice $U = V$ here. This happens because $A$ is symmetric: $A = A^T$, so $A^T A = A^2$ and the left and right singular vectors coincide with the eigenvectors of $A$.

**Verify:** $U\Sigma V^T = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix}1&2\2&-1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}5&0\0&0\end{bmatrix}\frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}\begin{bmatrix}1&2\2&-1\end{bmatrix}^T$

$$= \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix}5&0\10&0\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1&2\2&-1\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{5}\begin{bmatrix}5&10\10&20\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1&2\2&4\end{bmatrix} = A \quad \checkmark$$

**Reading the geometry:** $v_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(1,2)^T$ spans the row space. $v_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(2,-1)^T$ spans the null space â€” check: $Av_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(2 - 2, 4 - 4)^T = 0$. $\checkmark$

The map $A$ annihilates the entire direction $(2,-1)^T$ and stretches the direction $(1,2)^T$ by factor 5, sending it to a multiple of $u_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{5}}(1,2)^T$ in the column space.

## A Rank-Deficient $3 \times 3$ Example

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row 3 = row 1 + row 2. So $\text{rank}(A) = 2$ and $\dim N(A) = 1$.

**$A^T A$:**

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}^T \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 3 \ 1 & 2 & 3 \ 3 & 3 & 6 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Null space of $A$:** Solve $Ax = 0$.

$$\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} x_1 \ x_2 \ x_3 \end{bmatrix} = 0 \implies x_1 = -x_3,\ x_2 = -x_3$$

Null space: $\text{span}\left{\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\begin{bmatrix}-1\-1\1\end{bmatrix}\right}$

This is $v_3$ â€” the right singular vector for $\sigma_3 = 0$. It is the direction $A$ collapses.

The characteristic polynomial of $A^T A$ has $\lambda = 0$ as a root (since $\text{rank}(A^T A) = \text{rank}(A) = 2$). The nonzero eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2$ satisfy:

$$\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = \text{tr}(A^T A) = 2 + 2 + 6 = 10$$ $$\lambda_1 \lambda_2 = \frac{1}{2}\left[(\text{tr}(A^T A))^2 - \text{tr}((A^T A)^2)\right] / 1$$

Actually, easier: $\text{tr}(A^T A) = |A|_F^2 = 1+0+1+0+1+1+1+1+4 = 10$ and from the characteristic polynomial constrained to the 2D nonzero eigenspace, the nonzero eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda^2 - 10\lambda + c = 0$ where $c$ can be read from the $2\times 2$ principal minors. But the exact values matter less than the structure:

$$\sigma_3 = 0, \quad \sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 > 0$$

The null space direction $v_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1,-1,1)^T$ is already determined without computing $\sigma_1, \sigma_2$ â€” it is just the null space of $A$.

## Zero Singular Values and the Null Space

This is the key structural fact.

**Claim.** $\sigma_i = 0$ if and only if $v_i \in N(A)$.

**Proof.** $\sigma_i = 0 \iff A^T A v_i = \sigma_i^2 v_i = 0 \iff |Av_i|^2 = v_i^T A^T A v_i = 0 \iff Av_i = 0 \iff v_i \in N(A)$. $\square$

So the right singular vectors for zero singular values _are_ the null space. Not just related to it â€” they are an orthonormal basis for it. The SVD automatically finds the null space.

Symmetrically, the left singular vectors $u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_n$ for the "zero rows" of $\Sigma$ form an orthonormal basis for $N(A^T)$ â€” the left null space.

The number of zero singular values equals the nullity: $\dim N(A) = n - r$.

## Numerical Rank and Near-Singularity

This is where SVD is indispensable and no other tool competes.

In exact arithmetic, the distinction between $\sigma_r > 0$ and $\sigma_{r+1} = 0$ is sharp. In floating point, it never is. Every computed singular value is perturbed by rounding errors of order $\epsilon_{\text{machine}} |A|$. The "zero" singular values become small but nonzero. How small? That depends on the matrix and the problem.

**Numerical rank** is determined by thresholding:

$$r_{\text{num}} = #{i : \sigma_i > \tau}$$

where $\tau$ is a tolerance, typically $\tau = \epsilon_{\text{machine}} \cdot \sigma_1 \cdot n$ (scaled to the size of the matrix and the machine precision).

This is the only reliable way to determine rank in floating point. The alternatives:

- **Gaussian elimination with pivoting** can diagnose rank, but it is sensitive to the pivot threshold choice and can fail for nearly singular matrices.
- **Determinant** is completely useless: $\det(A)$ can be astronomically small for a matrix that is far from singular (large $n$, large entries), or can be non-negligible for a nearly singular matrix due to cancellation.
- **Checking if $Ax = 0$ has a solution** requires knowing $A$ exactly.

SVD does not have these problems. $\sigma_i$ is a geometric quantity â€” the length of $Av_i$ for a unit vector $v_i$. It is computable to machine precision via the QR-based bidiagonalization algorithm. The gap between "large" and "small" singular values, when it exists, is unambiguous.

## The Condition Number Revisited

For a singular matrix, $\sigma_n = 0$ and the condition number

$$\kappa(A) = \frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_n} = \frac{\sigma_1}{0} = \infty$$

An infinite condition number means the linear system $Ax = b$ is either inconsistent (no solution) or has infinitely many solutions. The SVD diagnoses which:

- **Inconsistent** ($b \notin C(A)$): $b$ has a nonzero component in the left null space. Specifically, $u_i^T b \neq 0$ for some $i > r$.
- **Infinitely many solutions** ($b \in C(A)$): the system is consistent but the null space has dimension $> 0$, so you can add any multiple of $v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n$ to any particular solution.

The pseudoinverse $A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^T$ handles both cases cleanly: it gives the minimum-norm least-squares solution, projecting $b$ onto $C(A)$ and finding the solution with no component in $N(A)$.

## Near-Singular Matrices and the Gap

The most practically important case: $A$ is invertible but nearly singular. $\sigma_n$ is small but nonzero.

**Example.** The Hilbert matrix $H_n$ with $H_{ij} = 1/(i+j-1)$:

$$H_5 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1/2 & 1/3 & 1/4 & 1/5 \ 1/2 & 1/3 & 1/4 & 1/5 & 1/6 \ 1/3 & 1/4 & 1/5 & 1/6 & 1/7 \ 1/4 & 1/5 & 1/6 & 1/7 & 1/8 \ 1/5 & 1/6 & 1/7 & 1/8 & 1/9 \end{bmatrix}$$

$H_5$ is symmetric positive definite â€” all eigenvalues strictly positive, no zero singular values. But $\sigma_1 \approx 1.57$ and $\sigma_5 \approx 3.3 \times 10^{-6}$. Condition number $\approx 4.8 \times 10^5$.

For $H_{10}$, the condition number exceeds $10^{13}$ â€” beyond double precision's $\sim 10^{16}$ limit, the matrix is numerically singular.

The SVD reveals this: you see $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_k$ of reasonable size, then a cliff, then $\sigma_{k+1}, \ldots, \sigma_n$ near or below machine epsilon times $\sigma_1$. The "numerical rank" is $k$, and the corresponding right singular vectors $v_{k+1}, \ldots, v_n$ span the "numerical null space" â€” directions along which $A$ is effectively zero.

The singular matrix is the limiting case: the cliff reaches exactly zero. The near-singular matrix is approaching that limit. SVD makes the continuum visible.

## Regularization via SVD

When $A$ is (nearly) singular, the least-squares problem $\min |Ax - b|$ can have solutions that are enormous â€” the pseudoinverse amplifies the components of $b$ in directions where $\sigma_i$ is tiny, by a factor $1/\sigma_i$.

**Tikhonov regularization** replaces the pseudoinverse with a damped version. Instead of $\hat{x} = A^+ b = \sum_{i=1}^r \frac{u_i^T b}{\sigma_i} v_i$, use:

$$\hat{x}_\delta = \sum_{i=1}^r \frac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_i^2 + \delta^2} (u_i^T b) v_i$$

The factor $\frac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_i^2 + \delta^2}$ replaces $\frac{1}{\sigma_i}$. For large $\sigma_i$ (relative to $\delta$): $\frac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_i^2 + \delta^2} \approx \frac{1}{\sigma_i}$, unchanged. For small $\sigma_i$ (relative to $\delta$): $\frac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_i^2 + \delta^2} \approx \frac{\sigma_i}{\delta^2} \approx 0$, suppressed.

The regularization parameter $\delta$ smoothly interpolates between "trust small singular values" ($\delta \to 0$, pseudoinverse) and "ignore everything" ($\delta \to \infty$, $\hat{x} = 0$). Choosing $\delta$ well is the art of regularization â€” it depends on the noise level in $b$ and the desired fidelity of the solution.

This is a direct consequence of the singular structure. Without SVD, regularization is a black box. With SVD, you can see exactly which directions are being damped and by how much.

## What the Zero Singular Values "Look Like"

A mental model worth having. Take any $n \times n$ matrix of rank $r$. It acts on the unit sphere $S^{n-1}$ and maps it to an ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^n$. The semi-axes of the ellipsoid have lengths $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_n$.

For a singular matrix: $n - r$ of those semi-axes have length zero. The ellipsoid degenerates â€” it is actually an ellipsoid living in a proper subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$, an $(r)$-dimensional "pancake." The entire unit sphere gets squashed: the $n - r$ directions $v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n$ get crushed to zero, and only the $r$ directions $v_1, \ldots, v_r$ survive and get stretched.

This is the geometric content of singularity. A matrix is singular if and only if its geometric image of the unit sphere is lower-dimensional than the sphere itself. The rank is the dimension of that image. The null space is the set of directions that get crushed.

## Summary

|Property|Nonsingular $A$|Singular $A$ (rank $r < n$)|
|---|---|---|
|Singular values|$\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_n > 0$|$\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$, $\sigma_{r+1} = \cdots = \sigma_n = 0$|
|$\det(A)$|$\prod \sigma_i \neq 0$|$0$|
|$\kappa(A)$|$\sigma_1 / \sigma_n < \infty$|$\infty$|
|Null space $N(A)$|${0}$|$\text{span}{v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n}$|
|Left null space $N(A^T)$|${0}$|$\text{span}{u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_n}$|
|$Ax = b$|Unique solution|No solution or $\infty$ many|
|Pseudoinverse $A^+$|$= A^{-1}$|Min-norm least-squares solution|
|Image of unit sphere|Non-degenerate ellipsoid|Degenerate ellipsoid in $r$-dim subspace|

The SVD does not treat singular matrices as a special case. It handles them with the same formula, the same algorithm, the same geometric picture. The zero singular values are not a failure â€” they are the most informative part of the decomposition for understanding the structure of the map.
