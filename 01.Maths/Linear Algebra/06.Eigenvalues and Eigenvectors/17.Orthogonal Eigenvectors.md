## The Central Question

Why do symmetric matrices have orthogonal eigenvectors? Not just "they do" — I want to feel _why_ the symmetry condition forces eigenvectors from different eigenspaces to land perpendicular to each other. This is the question that actually matters here.

---

## Setup: What We Already Know

Recall that if $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$, then $\mathbf{x}$ is an eigenvector with eigenvalue $\lambda$. Nothing about orthogonality yet — we are just finding directions that $A$ stretches or flips without rotating.

Two eigenvectors $\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2$ corresponding to _different_ eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$ need not be orthogonal in general. For a random matrix, there is no reason to expect $\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2 = 0$.

Symmetry changes everything.

---

## The Key Theorem

**Theorem (Spectral Theorem, real case).** Let $A$ be a real symmetric matrix ($A = A^T$). Then:

1. All eigenvalues of $A$ are real.
2. Eigenvectors corresponding to _distinct_ eigenvalues are orthogonal.
3. There exists an orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^n$ consisting entirely of eigenvectors of $A$.

Point 3 is the full spectral theorem — it requires handling repeated eigenvalues too. I will prove points 1 and 2 here, and note what extra work point 3 needs.

---

## Proof that Eigenvalues are Real

This always trips me up when I first see it, because it requires going to $\mathbb{C}^n$ even though $A$ is a real matrix.

Suppose $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$ with $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{C}^n$, $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$. Take the conjugate transpose of both sides:

$$\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A^T = \bar{\lambda} \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T$$

Since $A = A^T$ (and $A$ is real, so $\bar{A} = A$):

$$\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A = \bar{\lambda} \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T$$

Now multiply both sides on the right by $\mathbf{x}$:

$$\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T A \mathbf{x} = \bar{\lambda} \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T \mathbf{x}$$

But $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda \mathbf{x}$, so the left side is also $\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T (\lambda \mathbf{x}) = \lambda \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T \mathbf{x}$.

Therefore:

$$\lambda \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T \mathbf{x} = \bar{\lambda} \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T \mathbf{x}$$

Now $\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T \mathbf{x} = |\mathbf{x}|^2 > 0$ (since $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$), so we can divide both sides by it:

$$\lambda = \bar{\lambda}$$

This means $\lambda$ is real. Done.

The same argument works for complex Hermitian matrices ($A = \bar{A}^T$), which is the general version of this. Symmetric real matrices are just Hermitian matrices whose entries happen to be real.

---

## Proof that Eigenvectors for Distinct Eigenvalues are Orthogonal

This is the one I want to really nail. Let $A\mathbf{x}_1 = \lambda_1 \mathbf{x}_1$ and $A\mathbf{x}_2 = \lambda_2 \mathbf{x}_2$ with $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$.

**Step 1.** Compute $\mathbf{x}_2^T A \mathbf{x}_1$ in two different ways using symmetry.

_Using the first eigenvector equation:_

$$\mathbf{x}_2^T A \mathbf{x}_1 = \mathbf{x}_2^T (\lambda_1 \mathbf{x}_1) = \lambda_1 (\mathbf{x}_2^T \mathbf{x}_1)$$

_Using the transpose and symmetry:_

$$\mathbf{x}_2^T A \mathbf{x}_1 = \mathbf{x}_2^T A^T \mathbf{x}_1 = (A\mathbf{x}_2)^T \mathbf{x}_1 = (\lambda_2 \mathbf{x}_2)^T \mathbf{x}_1 = \lambda_2 (\mathbf{x}_2^T \mathbf{x}_1)$$

**Step 2.** Set the two expressions equal:

$$\lambda_1 (\mathbf{x}_2^T \mathbf{x}_1) = \lambda_2 (\mathbf{x}_2^T \mathbf{x}_1)$$

$$(\lambda_1 - \lambda_2)(\mathbf{x}_2^T \mathbf{x}_1) = 0$$

**Step 3.** Since $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$, we have $\lambda_1 - \lambda_2 \neq 0$, so:

$$\mathbf{x}_2^T \mathbf{x}_1 = 0$$

The eigenvectors are orthogonal. $\square$

The elegance here is in step 1: symmetry lets us "move $A$ to the other side" — $\mathbf{x}_2^T A = (A^T \mathbf{x}_2)^T = (A\mathbf{x}_2)^T$ — which extracts the _second_ eigenvalue from what started as an expression involving the first. Symmetry is exactly the condition that lets you do this.

---

## What About Repeated Eigenvalues?

The proof above only works when $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$. If a symmetric matrix has a repeated eigenvalue, the corresponding eigenspace might be multi-dimensional — but one can still _choose_ an orthonormal basis within that eigenspace (by Gram-Schmidt). The full spectral theorem guarantees the eigenspace has the right dimension to fill out $\mathbb{R}^n$, but that requires a separate argument (usually the inductive proof via the fact that $A$ restricted to the orthogonal complement of an eigenvector is again symmetric).

For now, the case of distinct eigenvalues is the heart of it. The repeated case is a technicality.

---

## A Concrete Example

Take a $3 \times 3$ symmetric matrix and watch the orthogonality fall out explicitly.

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 2 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

The characteristic polynomial:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \det \begin{bmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 2-\lambda & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 3-\lambda \end{bmatrix}$$

Expanding along the third column:

$$= (3 - \lambda)\det\begin{bmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \ 1 & 2-\lambda \end{bmatrix} = (3 - \lambda)\left[(2-\lambda)^2 - 1\right]$$

$$= (3-\lambda)(4 - 4\lambda + \lambda^2 - 1) = (3-\lambda)(\lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 3) = (3-\lambda)(\lambda-1)(\lambda-3)$$

So the eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = 3$ (repeated).

**Eigenvector for $\lambda = 1$:**

$$A - I = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row reduce: $x_1 + x_2 = 0$, $x_3 = 0$. So $\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ -1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Eigenvectors for $\lambda = 3$:**

$$A - 3I = \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & -1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

Row reduce: $-x_1 + x_2 = 0$ (i.e. $x_1 = x_2$), $x_3$ free. Two free variables: $\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{bmatrix} 1 \ 1 \ 0 \end{bmatrix}$, $\mathbf{x}_3 = \begin{bmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 1 \end{bmatrix}$.

**Checking orthogonality:**

$$\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_2 = (1)(1) + (-1)(1) + (0)(0) = 0 \checkmark$$ $$\mathbf{x}_1 \cdot \mathbf{x}_3 = (1)(0) + (-1)(0) + (0)(1) = 0 \checkmark$$ $$\mathbf{x}_2 \cdot \mathbf{x}_3 = (1)(0) + (1)(0) + (0)(1) = 0 \checkmark$$

Three mutually orthogonal eigenvectors, as the theorem demands. The repeated eigenvalue $\lambda = 3$ happened to give an eigenspace with an orthogonal basis directly — in general I would need Gram-Schmidt inside the eigenspace.

## The Diagonalization Picture: $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$

Once I have an orthonormal set of eigenvectors $\mathbf{q}_1, \mathbf{q}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_n$, I can form the orthogonal matrix:

$$Q = \begin{bmatrix} | & | & & | \ \mathbf{q}_1 & \mathbf{q}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{q}_n \ | & | & & | \end{bmatrix}$$

Since the columns are orthonormal: $Q^T Q = I$, so $Q^{-1} = Q^T$.

The diagonalization $A = Q \Lambda Q^T$ where:

$$\Lambda = \begin{bmatrix} \lambda_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \lambda_2 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \lambda_n \end{bmatrix}$$

This is more than just diagonalization — it is a decomposition of $A$ into a sum of rank-one projections:

$$A = \lambda_1 \mathbf{q}_1 \mathbf{q}_1^T + \lambda_2 \mathbf{q}_2 \mathbf{q}_2^T + \cdots + \lambda_n \mathbf{q}_n \mathbf{q}_n^T$$

Each term $\mathbf{q}_i \mathbf{q}_i^T$ is the projection matrix onto the $i$-th eigenspace. This is the spectral decomposition, and it is the payoff for all of this orthogonality work. It says $A$ acts on any vector by projecting it onto each eigenspace and scaling by the corresponding eigenvalue.

---

## Why This Matters: Quadratic Forms

A symmetric matrix $A$ defines a quadratic form $Q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$. The spectral decomposition tells us that in the eigenvector basis, this simplifies to:

$$Q(\mathbf{x}) = \lambda_1 y_1^2 + \lambda_2 y_2^2 + \cdots + \lambda_n y_n^2$$

where $\mathbf{y} = Q^T \mathbf{x}$ are the coordinates in the eigenvector basis. The cross terms vanish precisely because the eigenvectors are orthogonal. This is the principal axis theorem. Every quadratic form is a pure sum of squares in the right coordinate system — and the right coordinate system is exactly the eigenvectors of $A$.

Positive definiteness is then just the condition that all $\lambda_i > 0$.

## Summary in One Line

Symmetry ($A = A^T$) forces eigenvectors from different eigenspaces to be orthogonal because it lets you compute $\mathbf{x}_2^T A \mathbf{x}_1$ in two ways — once extracting $\lambda_1$, once extracting $\lambda_2$ — and if $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$, the inner product must be zero.
