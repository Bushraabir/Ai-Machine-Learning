## The Setup

A second-order linear ODE with constant coefficients:

$$u'' + pu' + qu = 0$$

This is where most students first meet the characteristic equation, usually without understanding why it works. The reason it works is eigenvalues. Everything is eigenvalues.

The general theory of $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ from [[Differential Equations and e^At]] already contains the full answer. The scalar second-order equation is just a special case in disguise, once you know how to look at it.

---

## Reduction to a First-Order System

Set

$$\mathbf{u}(t) = \begin{pmatrix} u(t) \ u'(t) \end{pmatrix}$$

Then

$$\mathbf{u}'(t) = \begin{pmatrix} u'(t) \ u''(t) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} u'(t) \ -qu(t) - pu'(t) \end{pmatrix}$$

Writing this as a matrix equation:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix} u \ u' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -q & -p \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} u \ u' \end{pmatrix}$$

The matrix

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -q & -p \end{pmatrix}$$

is called the **companion matrix** of the equation $u'' + pu' + qu = 0$.

Now compute $\det(A - \lambda I)$:

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} -\lambda & 1 \ -q & -p - \lambda \end{pmatrix} = (-\lambda)(-p - \lambda) - (1)(-q) = \lambda^2 + p\lambda + q$$

The characteristic polynomial of $A$ is exactly $\lambda^2 + p\lambda + q$. This is not a coincidence. The companion matrix is engineered so that its eigenvalues are the roots of the ODE's characteristic equation. Every second-order scalar ODE secretly is a $2 \times 2$ eigenvalue problem.

---

## The General Solution (Diagonalizable Case)

Suppose the characteristic equation $\lambda^2 + p\lambda + q = 0$ has two distinct roots $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$. Then $A$ is diagonalizable, and from [[Differential Equations and e^At]] the general solution is

$$u(t) = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t}$$

This is what every ODE textbook states as a rule. The derivation via the companion matrix is the reason it is true.

The constants $c_1, c_2$ are fixed by initial conditions $u(0)$ and $u'(0)$, which amounts to solving

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ \lambda_1 & \lambda_2 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} c_1 \ c_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} u(0) \ u'(0) \end{pmatrix}$$

The coefficient matrix here is a $2 \times 2$ Vandermonde matrix. It is invertible whenever $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$.

## The Three Regimes

The discriminant of $\lambda^2 + p\lambda + q = 0$ is $\Delta = p^2 - 4q$. The sign of $\Delta$ determines the qualitative character of all solutions.

### Case 1: $\Delta > 0$ — Two Distinct Real Roots (Overdamped)

Two distinct real eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in \mathbb{R}$, $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$.

$$u(t) = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t}$$

No oscillation. Both modes are pure exponentials. The solution is a sum of two decaying (if both $\lambda_i < 0$) or growing (if either $\lambda_i > 0$) exponentials.

For the damped oscillator $u'' + pu' + qu = 0$ with large damping ($p$ large), this is the overdamped regime: the system returns to equilibrium without oscillating, but slowly.

### Case 2: $\Delta = 0$ — Repeated Real Root (Critically Damped)

A single repeated eigenvalue $\lambda_0 = -p/2$.

The companion matrix $A$ has a defective eigenvalue. It is not diagonalizable. Its Jordan form is

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda_0 & 1 \ 0 & \lambda_0 \end{pmatrix}$$

From [[Nondiagonalizable Matrices Jordan Form]], the matrix exponential of a $2 \times 2$ Jordan block is

$$e^{Jt} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & t \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is exactly why the general solution in the repeated-root case is

$$u(t) = (c_1 + c_2 t),e^{\lambda_0 t}$$

The factor of $t$ is not a trick. It is the direct consequence of the Jordan block structure. The companion matrix is defective, so one eigenvector is missing, and the generalized eigenvector contributes the polynomial correction $t$. The proof is in [[Differential Equations and e^At]] under the Jordan form computation.

### Case 3: $\Delta < 0$ — Complex Conjugate Roots (Underdamped)

The roots are $\lambda = \alpha \pm i\beta$ where

$$\alpha = -\frac{p}{2}, \qquad \beta = \frac{\sqrt{4q - p^2}}{2}$$

The complex exponentials $e^{(\alpha + i\beta)t}$ and $e^{(\alpha - i\beta)t}$ are solutions, but we want real solutions. Use Euler's formula:

$$e^{(\alpha \pm i\beta)t} = e^{\alpha t}(\cos\beta t \pm i\sin\beta t)$$

Taking real and imaginary parts, two real independent solutions are $e^{\alpha t}\cos\beta t$ and $e^{\alpha t}\sin\beta t$. The general real solution is

$$u(t) = e^{\alpha t}(c_1\cos\beta t + c_2\sin\beta t)$$

This is decaying oscillation when $\alpha < 0$, growing oscillation when $\alpha > 0$, and pure oscillation (no amplitude change) when $\alpha = 0$.

## The Damped Harmonic Oscillator

The physical model is a mass-spring-damper:

$$mu'' + bu' + ku = 0$$

Divide by $m$: $u'' + \frac{b}{m}u' + \frac{k}{m}u = 0$.

Set $p = b/m$, $q = k/m$. The natural frequency is $\omega_0 = \sqrt{k/m}$ and the damping ratio is $\zeta = \frac{b}{2\sqrt{mk}}$.

The discriminant $\Delta = p^2 - 4q = \frac{b^2}{m^2} - \frac{4k}{m} = \frac{b^2 - 4mk}{m^2}$.

The three regimes correspond exactly:

|$\zeta$|Regime|Eigenvalues|
|---|---|---|
|$\zeta > 1$|Overdamped|Two distinct negative reals|
|$\zeta = 1$|Critically damped|Repeated negative real $\lambda_0 = -\omega_0$|
|$\zeta < 1$|Underdamped|$-\zeta\omega_0 \pm i\omega_0\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}$|

The eigenvalues control everything. No eigenvalue computation, no understanding of oscillators.

## Worked Example: Underdamped

$$u'' + 2u' + 5u = 0, \qquad u(0) = 1,\quad u'(0) = 0$$

**Companion matrix:**

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -5 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Characteristic equation:**

$$\lambda^2 + 2\lambda + 5 = 0 \implies \lambda = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{4 - 20}}{2} = -1 \pm 2i$$

So $\alpha = -1$, $\beta = 2$. Both eigenvalues have $\text{Re}(\lambda) = -1 < 0$: stable.

**General solution:**

$$u(t) = e^{-t}(c_1\cos 2t + c_2\sin 2t)$$

**Initial conditions:**

$$u(0) = c_1 = 1$$

$$u'(t) = e^{-t}\big[(-c_1 + 2c_2)\cos 2t + (-c_2 - 2c_1 \cdot 0 \ldots)\big]$$

More carefully: differentiate $u(t) = e^{-t}(c_1\cos 2t + c_2\sin 2t)$:

$$u'(t) = -e^{-t}(c_1\cos 2t + c_2\sin 2t) + e^{-t}(-2c_1\sin 2t + 2c_2\cos 2t)$$

At $t = 0$:

$$u'(0) = -c_1 + 2c_2 = 0 \implies c_2 = \frac{c_1}{2} = \frac{1}{2}$$

**Solution:**

$$u(t) = e^{-t}!\left(\cos 2t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sin 2t\right)$$

The amplitude decays like $e^{-t}$ while the function oscillates at angular frequency $2$.

## Worked Example: Critically Damped

$$u'' + 4u' + 4u = 0, \qquad u(0) = 3,\quad u'(0) = -1$$

**Characteristic equation:**

$$\lambda^2 + 4\lambda + 4 = (\lambda + 2)^2 = 0 \implies \lambda_0 = -2 \text{ (repeated)}$$

**Companion matrix:**

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -4 & -4 \end{pmatrix}$$

This matrix has the single eigenvalue $\lambda_0 = -2$ with algebraic multiplicity 2, geometric multiplicity 1. It is defective. Verify:

$$A - \lambda_0 I = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ -4 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\text{rank}(A - \lambda_0 I) = 1$, so the null space is one-dimensional. One eigenvector, not two. The Jordan form is

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \ 0 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

The matrix exponential is

$$e^{At} = e^{-2t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & t \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \quad (\text{up to conjugation by } P)$$

The general solution for $u$ (the first component of $e^{At}\mathbf{u}_0$):

$$u(t) = (c_1 + c_2 t),e^{-2t}$$

**Initial conditions:**

$$u(0) = c_1 = 3$$

$$u'(t) = c_2 e^{-2t} - 2(c_1 + c_2 t)e^{-2t}$$

$$u'(0) = c_2 - 2c_1 = -1 \implies c_2 = -1 + 6 = 5$$

**Solution:**

$$u(t) = (3 + 5t),e^{-2t}$$

The solution initially increases (the $5t$ term dominates near $t = 0$) before the exponential decay takes over. At large $t$ the exponential wins: $u(t) \to 0$.

Note: $(3 + 5t)e^{-2t}$ actually reaches its maximum at $t = 1/2 - 3/5 = -1/10$... let me recompute. Setting $u'(t) = 0$: $(5 - 2(3 + 5t))e^{-2t} = 0 \implies 5 - 6 - 10t = 0 \implies t = -1/10$. So $u'(0) = -1 < 0$: the solution is already decreasing at $t = 0$ and decays monotonically to $0$.

This is critically damped: fastest possible decay to zero without oscillating.

## Worked Example: Overdamped

$$u'' + 5u' + 6u = 0, \qquad u(0) = 2,\quad u'(0) = -1$$

**Characteristic equation:**

$$\lambda^2 + 5\lambda + 6 = (\lambda + 2)(\lambda + 3) = 0 \implies \lambda_1 = -2,\quad \lambda_2 = -3$$

Both negative: stable. No oscillation.

**General solution:**

$$u(t) = c_1 e^{-2t} + c_2 e^{-3t}$$

**Initial conditions:**

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ -2 & -3 \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} c_1 \ c_2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Row 1: $c_1 + c_2 = 2$.  
Row 2: $-2c_1 - 3c_2 = -1$.

From row 2: $2c_1 + 3c_2 = 1$. Subtract $2 \times$ row 1: $c_2 = 1 - 4 = -3$. Then $c_1 = 5$.

**Solution:**

$$u(t) = 5e^{-2t} - 3e^{-3t}$$

For large $t$, the slower mode $e^{-2t}$ dominates. The solution approaches zero from above (check: $u(t) > 0$ for large $t$ since $5e^{-2t} > 3e^{-3t}$ for $t > \ln(3/5)/(-1) = \ln(5/3)$).

## Why the Repeated-Root Case Needs $te^{\lambda_0 t}$: The Full Proof

This is worth proving carefully because every ODE course states this as a fact without proof.

**Claim.** If $\lambda_0$ is a double root of $\lambda^2 + p\lambda + q = 0$, then $te^{\lambda_0 t}$ is a solution of $u'' + pu' + qu = 0$.

**Proof via the companion matrix.**

The companion matrix is

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -q & -p \end{pmatrix}$$

with $\lambda_0 = -p/2$ a double eigenvalue. Since $(A - \lambda_0 I)^2 = 0$ (the minimal polynomial is $(\lambda - \lambda_0)^2$), the matrix $A - \lambda_0 I$ is nilpotent of order 2. Write $A = \lambda_0 I + N$ where

$$N = A - \lambda_0 I = \begin{pmatrix} -\lambda_0 & 1 \ -q & -p - \lambda_0 \end{pmatrix}$$

and $N^2 = 0$.

Since $\lambda_0 I$ and $N$ commute:

$$e^{At} = e^{(\lambda_0 I + N)t} = e^{\lambda_0 t}e^{Nt} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\left(I + Nt + \frac{N^2 t^2}{2!} + \cdots\right) = e^{\lambda_0 t}(I + Nt)$$

since $N^2 = 0$. Explicitly:

$$e^{At} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 - \lambda_0 t & t \ -qt & 1 + (-p-\lambda_0)t \end{pmatrix} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 - \lambda_0 t & t \ -qt & 1 - \lambda_0 t \end{pmatrix}$$

(using $-p - \lambda_0 = -p - (-p/2) = -p/2 = -\lambda_0$, since $p = -2\lambda_0$.)

The solution with initial condition $\mathbf{u}_0 = \begin{pmatrix} u_0 \ u_0' \end{pmatrix}$ is

$$\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}_0 = e^{\lambda_0 t}\begin{pmatrix} (1 - \lambda_0 t)u_0 + t u_0' \ -qt,u_0 + (1 - \lambda_0 t)u_0' \end{pmatrix}$$

The first component (which is $u(t)$) is

$$u(t) = e^{\lambda_0 t}\big[(1 - \lambda_0 t)u_0 + t,u_0'\big] = u_0 e^{\lambda_0 t} + (u_0' - \lambda_0 u_0),t e^{\lambda_0 t}$$

Setting $c_1 = u_0$ and $c_2 = u_0' - \lambda_0 u_0$, this is exactly $(c_1 + c_2 t)e^{\lambda_0 t}$. The factor of $t$ falls out of the nilpotent structure of $N$, which itself came from the Jordan block. $\blacksquare$

This is the honest proof. The $te^{\lambda_0 t}$ formula is not an ansatz pulled from nowhere; it is a theorem about the matrix exponential of a Jordan block.

## Stability Criteria in Terms of Coefficients

For $u'' + pu' + qu = 0$, both eigenvalues have negative real part if and only if both $p > 0$ and $q > 0$.

**Proof.** The eigenvalues satisfy $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = -p$ and $\lambda_1\lambda_2 = q$ (Vieta's formulas).

- If $q \leq 0$: either the roots have opposite signs (unstable) or one root is zero (not asymptotically stable).
- If $q > 0$ and $p \leq 0$: the real parts sum to $-p \geq 0$, so at least one has non-negative real part.
- If $p > 0$ and $q > 0$: the product of eigenvalues is positive (same sign or complex conjugates) and the sum is negative, forcing both to lie in the left half-plane.

This is the Routh-Hurwitz criterion for $n = 2$.

## The Full Picture: What Eigenvalues Control

For $u'' + pu' + qu = 0$, write eigenvalues as $\lambda = \alpha \pm i\beta$:

|$\alpha$|$\beta$|Behavior|
|---|---|---|
|$\alpha < 0$|$\beta = 0$|Exponential decay, no oscillation|
|$\alpha < 0$|$\beta \neq 0$|Decaying oscillation (underdamped)|
|$\alpha = 0$|$\beta \neq 0$|Pure oscillation (no decay)|
|$\alpha > 0$|any|Growing solution (unstable)|
|$\alpha = \lambda_0$, repeated|$\beta = 0$|Critically damped; factor of $t$|

The real part controls growth/decay. The imaginary part controls oscillation frequency. The Jordan structure (defectiveness) controls polynomial corrections.

## Connection to [[Differential Equations and e^At]]

Everything here is a specialization of the general system $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ to the $2 \times 2$ companion matrix. The dictionary is:

$$u'' + pu' + qu = 0 \quad\longleftrightarrow\quad \frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix} u \ u' \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ -q & -p \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} u \ u' \end{pmatrix}$$

The eigenvalues of the companion matrix are the roots of $\lambda^2 + p\lambda + q$. The diagonalizable case gives two independent exponential solutions. The defective case gives one exponential and one $te^{\lambda_0 t}$, directly from the Jordan block. Stability is determined by the sign of the real parts, as in the general case.

Nothing is new here. The second-order scalar ODE is just the $2 \times 2$ case of the theory already built.

## Summary

$$\boxed{u'' + pu' + qu = 0 \quad\longleftrightarrow\quad \lambda^2 + p\lambda + q = 0}$$

|Case|Eigenvalues|Solution|
|---|---|---|
|Overdamped|$\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in \mathbb{R}$, distinct|$c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t} + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t}$|
|Critically damped|$\lambda_0$ repeated|$(c_1 + c_2 t)e^{\lambda_0 t}$|
|Underdamped|$\alpha \pm i\beta$|$e^{\alpha t}(c_1\cos\beta t + c_2\sin\beta t)$|

Stability: all solutions decay to zero $\iff$ $p > 0$ and $q > 0$ $\iff$ both eigenvalues have negative real part.

The $te^{\lambda_0 t}$ in the critically damped case is not a trick. It is the first-order polynomial correction forced by the Jordan block structure of the companion matrix when $A$ is defective.
