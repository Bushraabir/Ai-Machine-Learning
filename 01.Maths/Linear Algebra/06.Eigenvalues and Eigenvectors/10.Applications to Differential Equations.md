
## The Central Problem

A system of first-order linear ODEs with constant coefficients:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dt} = A\mathbf{u}, \qquad \mathbf{u}(0) = \mathbf{u}_0$$

where $A$ is an $n \times n$ constant matrix and $\mathbf{u}(t) \in \mathbb{R}^n$. This is the continuous-time analogue of the discrete recurrence $\mathbf{u}_{k+1} = A\mathbf{u}_k$ from [[Matrix Powers]]. Every structural question about the solution -- does it grow, decay, oscillate, blow up -- is answered by the eigenvalues of $A$.

The scalar case $u' = au$ has solution $u(t) = e^{at}u_0$. The matrix case has solution $\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}_0$. Eigenvalues tell us what $e^{At}$ does.

---

## Why Eigenvectors Decouple the System

Suppose $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$. Try the ansatz $\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{x}$:

$$\frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dt} = \lambda e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{x} = e^{\lambda t}(A\mathbf{x}) = A(e^{\lambda t}\mathbf{x}) = A\mathbf{u}$$

It solves the equation exactly. Each eigenvector gives a pure mode solution. The system is decoupled in the eigenvector basis -- each mode evolves independently.

If $A$ has $n$ linearly independent eigenvectors $\mathbf{x}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{x}_n$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, the general solution is:

$$\mathbf{u}(t) = c_1 e^{\lambda_1 t}\mathbf{x}_1 + c_2 e^{\lambda_2 t}\mathbf{x}_2 + \cdots + c_n e^{\lambda_n t}\mathbf{x}_n$$

Apply the initial condition $\mathbf{u}(0) = \mathbf{u}_0$:

$$\mathbf{u}_0 = c_1\mathbf{x}_1 + c_2\mathbf{x}_2 + \cdots + c_n\mathbf{x}_n$$

This is $P\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{u}_0$, so $\mathbf{c} = P^{-1}\mathbf{u}_0$. The complete solution is:

$$\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}_0 = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}\mathbf{u}_0$$

where $e^{Dt}$ is diagonal with entries $e^{\lambda_i t}$.

> Personal note: this is the continuous-time mirror of $A^k = PD^kP^{-1}$ from [[Matrix Powers]]. Replace $\lambda_i^k$ with $e^{\lambda_i t}$. The structure is identical. Strang points this out explicitly and it is worth internalizing -- discrete and continuous dynamics are the same linear algebra, different functions applied to the eigenvalues.

## The Matrix Exponential

The solution operator $e^{At}$ deserves its own definition. Define it by the power series:

$$e^{At} = I + At + \frac{(At)^2}{2!} + \frac{(At)^3}{3!} + \cdots = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(At)^k}{k!}$$

This converges for all $t$ and all matrices $A$. It satisfies:

- $e^{A \cdot 0} = I$
- $\frac{d}{dt}e^{At} = Ae^{At}$
- If $AB = BA$ then $e^{(A+B)t} = e^{At}e^{Bt}$
- $(e^{At})^{-1} = e^{-At}$

**For diagonalizable $A = PDP^{-1}$:**

$$e^{At} = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}, \qquad e^{Dt} = \begin{pmatrix} e^{\lambda_1 t} & & \ & \ddots & \ & & e^{\lambda_n t} \end{pmatrix}$$

**For a Jordan block $J_k(\lambda_0)$** (when $A$ is not diagonalizable):

$$e^{J_k(\lambda_0)t} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\begin{pmatrix} 1 & t & \frac{t^2}{2!} & \cdots & \frac{t^{k-1}}{(k-1)!} \ 0 & 1 & t & \cdots & \frac{t^{k-2}}{(k-2)!} \ \vdots & & \ddots & & \vdots \ 0 & \cdots & & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

The polynomial factors $t^j/j!$ are the signature of a defective eigenvalue. Solutions involving a Jordan block of size $k$ grow like $t^{k-1}e^{\lambda_0 t}$.

## Stability

The most important qualitative question: does $\mathbf{u}(t) \to \mathbf{0}$ as $t \to \infty$?

From the general solution $\mathbf{u}(t) = \sum c_i e^{\lambda_i t}\mathbf{x}_i$, each mode decays if and only if $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0$.

**Theorem.** The system $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ is **asymptotically stable** (all solutions decay to zero) if and only if every eigenvalue of $A$ has strictly negative real part.

|Eigenvalue condition|Behavior|
|---|---|
|All $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0$|Stable: all solutions decay to $\mathbf{0}$|
|Some $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) > 0$|Unstable: some solutions grow without bound|
|All $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) \leq 0$, some $= 0$|Neutrally stable (or unstable if defective)|
|$\lambda = \pm bi$ (purely imaginary)|Oscillates without decay|

The last row deserves attention. A purely imaginary eigenvalue $\lambda = bi$ gives a factor $e^{bit} = \cos(bt) + i\sin(bt)$ -- pure oscillation. For a defective purely imaginary eigenvalue, the Jordan block contributes a factor of $t$, and the solution grows like $t \cdot e^{bit}$. Neutral stability is fragile.

## Worked Example: $2 \times 2$ Decoupled System

$$\frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix}u\v\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-1 & 0 \ 0 & -3\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}u\v\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \begin{pmatrix}u(0)\v(0)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2\5\end{pmatrix}$$

$A$ is already diagonal. Eigenvalues $\lambda_1 = -1$, $\lambda_2 = -3$, eigenvectors $\mathbf{e}_1, \mathbf{e}_2$.

$$\begin{pmatrix}u(t)\v(t)\end{pmatrix} = 2e^{-t}\begin{pmatrix}1\0\end{pmatrix} + 5e^{-3t}\begin{pmatrix}0\1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}2e^{-t}\5e^{-3t}\end{pmatrix}$$

Both eigenvalues negative: stable. The $e^{-3t}$ mode decays faster; for large $t$, the solution approaches zero along the $u$-axis direction.

## Worked Example: $2 \times 2$ Coupled System

$$\frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix}u\v\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1 & 2 \ 0 & -1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}u\v\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \begin{pmatrix}u(0)\v(0)\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\1\end{pmatrix}$$

**Eigenvalues:** $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = -1$ (upper triangular, read diagonal).

**Eigenvectors:**

For $\lambda_1 = 1$: $(A - I) = \begin{pmatrix}0&2\0&-2\end{pmatrix}$, giving $v = 0$, $u$ free. $\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\end{pmatrix}$.

For $\lambda_2 = -1$: $(A + I) = \begin{pmatrix}2&2\0&0\end{pmatrix}$, giving $u + v = 0$. $\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\-1\end{pmatrix}$.

**Initial conditions:** $c_1\mathbf{x}_1 + c_2\mathbf{x}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}3\1\end{pmatrix}$:

$$\begin{pmatrix}1 & 1\0 & -1\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}c_1\c_2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3\1\end{pmatrix}$$

Row 2: $-c_2 = 1 \Rightarrow c_2 = -1$. Row 1: $c_1 = 3 - c_2 = 4$.

**Solution:**

$$\begin{pmatrix}u(t)\v(t)\end{pmatrix} = 4e^{t}\begin{pmatrix}1\0\end{pmatrix} - e^{-t}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}4e^t - e^{-t}\e^{-t}\end{pmatrix}$$

**Stability:** $\lambda_1 = 1 > 0$. Unstable. The $e^t$ mode grows. The $e^{-t}$ mode is irrelevant for large $t$ -- the solution is dominated entirely by the $c_1e^t\mathbf{x}_1$ term.

## Worked Example: Complex Eigenvalues -- Oscillation

$$\frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix}u\v\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & -\omega \ \omega & 0\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}u\v\end{pmatrix}$$

This is a pure rotation matrix scaled by $\omega$. Eigenvalues: $\lambda = \pm i\omega$ (purely imaginary).

The complex solution involves $e^{i\omega t} = \cos\omega t + i\sin\omega t$. Taking real and imaginary parts, the real general solution is:

$$\begin{pmatrix}u(t)\v(t)\end{pmatrix} = c_1\begin{pmatrix}\cos\omega t\\sin\omega t\end{pmatrix} + c_2\begin{pmatrix}-\sin\omega t\\cos\omega t\end{pmatrix}$$

Every solution is a circle in the $(u,v)$ plane, traversed with angular frequency $\omega$. No decay, no growth. Neutral stability.

This is the matrix form of simple harmonic motion.

## Second-Order ODEs Reduced to First-Order Systems

A single second-order ODE

$$u'' + bu' + cu = 0$$

becomes a first-order system by setting $\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix}u\u'\end{pmatrix}$:

$$\frac{d}{dt}\begin{pmatrix}u\u'\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 1\-c & -b\end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix}u\u'\end{pmatrix}$$

The companion matrix $A = \begin{pmatrix}0&1\-c&-b\end{pmatrix}$ has characteristic polynomial:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 + b\lambda + c = 0$$

which is exactly the characteristic equation you would write down directly for $u'' + bu' + cu = 0$. The eigenvalues of the companion matrix are the roots of the characteristic equation of the ODE. This is not a coincidence -- it is the same object.

**Example: damped harmonic oscillator.** $u'' + 2u' + 5u = 0$.

Characteristic equation: $\lambda^2 + 2\lambda + 5 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = -1 \pm 2i$.

Real part $-1 < 0$: stable. Imaginary part $\pm 2$: oscillates at frequency 2. The solution decays while oscillating:

$$u(t) = e^{-t}(c_1\cos 2t + c_2\sin 2t)$$

This is underdamped oscillation. The three regimes:

|Discriminant $b^2 - 4c$|Eigenvalues|Regime|
|---|---|---|
|$< 0$|$\alpha \pm i\beta$, $\alpha < 0$|Underdamped: decaying oscillation|
|$= 0$|Repeated real $\lambda = -b/2$|Critically damped: $(c_1 + c_2 t)e^{\lambda t}$|
|$> 0$|Two distinct real|Overdamped: sum of two exponentials|

The critically damped case has a repeated eigenvalue. If $b^2 = 4c$, the companion matrix has a defective eigenvalue and is not diagonalizable. The Jordan block produces the factor of $t$ in $(c_1 + c_2 t)e^{\lambda t}$ -- exactly as predicted by [[Nondiagonalizable Matrices Jordan Form]].

## Decoupling via Eigenvectors: The Change of Variables

The technique in all of the above is one idea. If $A = PDP^{-1}$, substitute $\mathbf{u} = P\mathbf{v}$:

$$P\mathbf{v}' = AP\mathbf{v} = PDP^{-1}P\mathbf{v} = PD\mathbf{v}$$

$$\mathbf{v}' = D\mathbf{v}$$

The system in $\mathbf{v}$ is diagonal -- $n$ independent scalar equations:

$$v_i' = \lambda_i v_i \implies v_i(t) = v_i(0)e^{\lambda_i t}$$

Transform back: $\mathbf{u}(t) = P\mathbf{v}(t)$. This is the eigenvector decomposition written as a change of variables. The eigenvector basis diagonalizes the system.

## Exponential of a Non-Diagonalizable Matrix

When $A = PJP^{-1}$ with $J$ the Jordan form, the matrix exponential is:

$$e^{At} = Pe^{Jt}P^{-1}$$

and $e^{Jt}$ is block diagonal with blocks $e^{J_k(\lambda_i)t}$ as given above. For a $2\times 2$ Jordan block:

$$e^{J_2(\lambda_0)t} = e^{\lambda_0 t}\begin{pmatrix}1 & t \ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$$

This gives the solution structure for a defective system. The extra factor of $t$ is the continuous-time version of the polynomial correction in $J^m$ from [[Nondiagonalizable Matrices Jordan Form]].

**Example:** $A = \begin{pmatrix}3 & 1 \ 0 & 3\end{pmatrix}$, a single Jordan block.

$$e^{At} = e^{3t}\begin{pmatrix}1 & t \ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}$$

General solution with initial condition $\mathbf{u}_0 = \begin{pmatrix}a\b\end{pmatrix}$:

$$\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{3t}\begin{pmatrix}a + bt \ b\end{pmatrix}$$

The $v$-component decays like a pure exponential; the $u$-component grows like $te^{3t}$. The eigenvalue is $3 > 0$, so the system is unstable. But even if $\lambda_0 < 0$, the $te^{\lambda_0 t}$ term would still be present -- it decays to zero, but slower than $e^{\lambda_0 t}$ alone for large $t$.

## Fourier Series and Differential Equations

The connection between [[Fourier Series]] and differential equations is that sines and cosines are the eigenfunctions of $d^2/dx^2$.

On $[0, \pi]$ with boundary conditions $u(0) = u(\pi) = 0$:

$$-\frac{d^2u}{dx^2} = \lambda u$$

The solutions are $u_n(x) = \sin(nx)$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_n = n^2$. These are the eigenvectors of the differential operator $-d^2/dx^2$, which is a Hermitian operator on function space.

The Fourier sine series is the eigenfunction expansion of a function in this operator's eigenbasis. Solving the heat equation $u_t = u_{xx}$ amounts to writing $u(x,0)$ in the Fourier basis and letting each mode decay:

$$u(x,t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n e^{-n^2 t}\sin(nx)$$

The eigenvalue $\lambda_n = n^2$ appears in the exponent as $e^{-n^2 t}$. Higher modes decay faster. This is the continuous-spectrum analogue of $\mathbf{u}(t) = \sum c_i e^{\lambda_i t}\mathbf{x}_i$. Same structure, infinite-dimensional.

## Summary of the Eigenvalue-ODE Dictionary

|Property|$\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$|
|---|---|
|Solution|$\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}_0 = \sum c_i e^{\lambda_i t}\mathbf{x}_i$|
|Stability|All $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) < 0$|
|Oscillation|$\text{Re}(\lambda_i) = 0$, $\text{Im}(\lambda_i) \neq 0$|
|Growth|Some $\text{Re}(\lambda_i) > 0$|
|Dominant behavior|Largest $\text{Re}(\lambda_i)$ controls long-time behavior|
|Defective eigenvalue|Extra polynomial factor $t^j$ in solution|
|Repeated real root|Critically damped; Jordan block|
|Second-order ODE|Reduce to first-order system; companion matrix|

$$\boxed{\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{At}\mathbf{u}_0, \qquad e^{At} = Pe^{Dt}P^{-1}, \qquad [e^{Dt}]_{ii} = e^{\lambda_i t}}$$

Everything reduces to: find the eigenvalues, find the eigenvectors, write the initial condition in the eigenvector basis, let each mode evolve independently.
