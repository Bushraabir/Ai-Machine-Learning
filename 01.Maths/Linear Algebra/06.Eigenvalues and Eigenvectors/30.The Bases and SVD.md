
This note is about what the SVD actually _is_ at the level of bases — not the formula $A = U\Sigma V^T$, which is a computational object, but the statement that every linear map $A: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ has a pair of orthonormal bases, one for the input space and one for the output space, in which $A$ acts by pure scaling.

That sentence is the theorem. Everything else is unpacking it.

---

## The Central Question

Eigendecomposition asks: is there a single basis ${v_1, \ldots, v_n}$ for $\mathbb{R}^n$ such that $A$ maps each $v_i$ to a scalar multiple of itself? If yes, $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$.

This requires $A$ to be square, and it fails when $A$ is defective. Even when it succeeds, the basis $S$ is generally not orthonormal — the eigenvectors of a non-symmetric matrix are not perpendicular.

SVD asks a different and more natural question: is there an orthonormal basis ${v_1, \ldots, v_n}$ for $\mathbb{R}^n$ (the input space) and an orthonormal basis ${u_1, \ldots, u_m}$ for $\mathbb{R}^m$ (the output space) such that

$$Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$$

for every $i$? The answer is always yes, for every matrix $A$, rectangular or square, any rank.

This is the content of SVD. Not a factorization formula — a statement about the existence of good bases.

---

## Why Two Bases, Not One

For a square matrix $A: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$, input and output live in the same space. So it is meaningful to ask for a single basis that works for both — this is eigendecomposition.

For a rectangular matrix $A: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ with $m \neq n$, input and output live in different spaces. There is no such thing as a single basis. You must choose a basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$ and a separate basis for $\mathbb{R}^m$. The question becomes: can these two bases be chosen so that $A$ acts diagonally between them?

SVD says yes, and more: they can be chosen to be orthonormal.

Even for square $A$, this is a stronger and cleaner statement than eigendecomposition. Eigendecomposition demands that $A$ maps $v_i$ to a multiple of $v_i$ — the same vector. SVD only demands that $A$ maps $v_i$ to a multiple of $u_i$, a potentially different vector. The relaxation is what makes it universal.

---

## The Diagram

Write the action of $A$ as a commutative diagram:

$$\mathbb{R}^n \xrightarrow{A} \mathbb{R}^m$$

In the $V$-basis for $\mathbb{R}^n$ and the $U$-basis for $\mathbb{R}^m$, the map $A$ becomes $\Sigma$:

$$\begin{array}{ccc} \mathbb{R}^n & \xrightarrow{A} & \mathbb{R}^m \ !!!!!!!!!!!!!{}_{V}\uparrow\phantom{V} & & \phantom{U}\uparrow{}_{U}!!!!!!!!!!!! \ \mathbb{R}^n & \xrightarrow{\Sigma} & \mathbb{R}^m \end{array}$$

More precisely: in coordinates relative to the standard basis, the map is $A$. Change to the $V$-basis in the domain and the $U$-basis in the codomain, and the map becomes $\Sigma$ — diagonal, pure scaling.

The formula $A = U\Sigma V^T$ says exactly this: $V^T$ is the change-of-basis matrix from standard to $V$-coordinates in $\mathbb{R}^n$, $\Sigma$ is the map in those coordinates, and $U$ is the change-of-basis matrix from $U$-coordinates back to standard in $\mathbb{R}^m$.

$$A = \underbrace{U}_{\text{change basis back}} \underbrace{\Sigma}_{\text{scale}} \underbrace{V^T}_{\text{change basis}}$$

## The Four Subspaces, Revisited

The bases $U$ and $V$ do not treat all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ and $\mathbb{R}^m$ symmetrically. Let $r = \text{rank}(A)$.

Partition the columns of $V$ into two groups:

$$V = \begin{bmatrix} \underbrace{v_1 \cdots v_r}_{\text{row space of }A} & \underbrace{v_{r+1} \cdots v_n}_{\text{null space of }A} \end{bmatrix}$$

Partition the columns of $U$ into two groups:

$$U = \begin{bmatrix} \underbrace{u_1 \cdots u_r}_{\text{column space of }A} & \underbrace{u_{r+1} \cdots u_m}_{\text{left null space of }A} \end{bmatrix}$$

The action of $A$:

- $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$ for $i = 1, \ldots, r$ — the row space maps isomorphically onto the column space, with scaling by $\sigma_i$.
- $Av_i = 0$ for $i = r+1, \ldots, n$ — the null space maps to zero.
- $u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_m$ span the left null space: $A^T u_i = 0$ for $i > r$.

The four fundamental subspaces are not just described by the SVD — they are _given_ by it, with explicit orthonormal bases. The abstract orthogonality relations (row space $\perp$ null space, column space $\perp$ left null space) become visible: they hold because $V$ and $U$ are orthogonal matrices, so their columns are automatically perpendicular.

Strang's "big picture" is:

$$\underbrace{C(A^T)}_{\text{row space}} \xrightarrow{A,\ \text{invertible}} \underbrace{C(A)}_{\text{column space}}$$ $$\underbrace{N(A)}_{\text{null space}} \xrightarrow{A} \underbrace{{0}}$$

SVD gives this picture with explicit orthonormal bases: ${v_1,\ldots,v_r}$ for the row space, ${v_{r+1},\ldots,v_n}$ for the null space, ${u_1,\ldots,u_r}$ for the column space, ${u_{r+1},\ldots,u_m}$ for the left null space. And the map from row space to column space is not just "invertible" — it is diagonal with factors $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$.

---

## Choosing Bases: A Sequence of Decisions

It is illuminating to think about how much freedom you have at each step in constructing the SVD bases, and where the constraints come from.

**Step 1.** Diagonalize $A^T A$ (symmetric, positive semidefinite). By the spectral theorem, this can always be done with an orthonormal eigenvector basis. The eigenvectors are the right singular vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$, the columns of $V$.

The eigenvalues $\sigma_i^2 \geq 0$ are uniquely determined (if all distinct). The eigenvectors $v_i$ are determined up to sign (if eigenvalues are distinct) or up to an orthogonal transformation within each eigenspace (if there are repeated eigenvalues).

**Step 2.** For $i \leq r$, define $u_i = Av_i / \sigma_i$. These are forced — no choice. Given the choice of $v_i$, the $u_i$ are determined (up to the sign of $v_i$, which propagates to $u_i$).

**Step 3.** Extend ${u_1, \ldots, u_r}$ to a full orthonormal basis of $\mathbb{R}^m$. The vectors $u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_m$ spanning the left null space can be chosen freely (any orthonormal basis for the left null space works). They do not affect $A$ at all, since $\Sigma$ has zeros in those rows.

**Conclusion.** The singular values $\sigma_1 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r$ are uniquely determined by $A$ — they are invariants of the linear map, independent of any basis choice. The singular vectors have freedom only in degenerate cases (repeated singular values) or in the null space parts. In the generic case (all singular values distinct), $U$ and $V$ are determined up to signing of columns.

## What Changes Between Eigendecomposition and SVD

Let $A$ be a symmetric positive definite $n \times n$ matrix. Then:

- Spectral theorem: $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$, with $Q$ orthogonal and $\Lambda$ diagonal with positive entries.
- SVD: $A = U\Sigma V^T$.

For a symmetric positive definite matrix, these coincide: $U = V = Q$ and $\Sigma = \Lambda$. The input and output bases are the same because $A$ maps its own eigenvectors to multiples of themselves — input and output are in the same space and the eigenvectors are orthogonal.

Now break symmetry. Take a non-symmetric square matrix $A$ with real eigenvalues. Eigendecomposition gives $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$, but $S$ is generally not orthogonal — the eigenvectors are not perpendicular. SVD gives $A = U\Sigma V^T$ with $U \neq V$ (the input and output bases differ) and both orthogonal.

The eigendecomposition is asking: in what basis does $A$ look diagonal? SVD is asking: in what _pair_ of bases does $A$ look diagonal? The extra freedom of choosing two separate bases (rather than one) is exactly what makes SVD universally possible and orthonormal.

## The Basis Interpretation of Low-Rank Approximation

The outer product form $A = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$ now has a clear basis interpretation.

Each pair $(u_i, v_i)$ is a "mode": $v_i$ is a direction in input space, $u_i$ is the corresponding direction in output space, and $\sigma_i$ is how strongly $A$ couples them. The rank-$k$ approximation

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

keeps only the $k$ most strongly coupled input-output direction pairs. It discards all modes with coupling strength $\sigma_{k+1}, \ldots, \sigma_r$.

The Eckart-Young theorem says this is the best you can do with rank $k$: no other choice of $k$ input-output pairs (not necessarily orthonormal, not necessarily from the SVD) can give a smaller Frobenius or spectral norm error. The SVD modes are sorted in descending order of importance, and truncation from the bottom is optimal.

This is why the SVD basis is special. Any orthonormal basis ${w_1, \ldots, w_n}$ for $\mathbb{R}^n$ and ${z_1, \ldots, z_m}$ for $\mathbb{R}^m$ would let you write

$$A = \sum_{i,j} c_{ij} z_i w_j^T$$

for some coefficients $c_{ij}$ — but this expansion is not diagonal in general ($c_{ij} \neq 0$ for $i \neq j$). Only the SVD bases produce a diagonal expansion: $c_{ij} = \sigma_i \delta_{ij}$. The input directions $v_i$ and output directions $u_i$ are matched one-to-one by $A$ with no cross-coupling.

## Change of Basis: Putting It All Together

Let me write this out pedantically once, because I want to be completely clear about what $V^T$ and $U$ are doing.

Let $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$ be a vector. Write $x$ in the $V$-basis:

$$x = \sum_{i=1}^n \hat{x}_i v_i, \qquad \hat{x}_i = v_i^T x$$

In coordinates: $\hat{x} = V^T x$. Now apply $A$:

$$Ax = A\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \hat{x}_i v_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^r \hat{x}_i \sigma_i u_i$$

(Terms $i > r$ vanish since $Av_i = 0$.) Write this in the $U$-basis: the $i$-th coordinate in the $U$-basis of $Ax$ is $u_i^T(Ax) = \hat{x}_i \sigma_i$. So in coordinates:

$$\widehat{Ax} = \Sigma \hat{x}$$

where $\widehat{Ax} = U^T(Ax)$ is the $U$-coordinate representation of $Ax$ and $\hat{x} = V^T x$ is the $V$-coordinate representation of $x$.

The diagram in coordinates:

$$\hat{x} = V^T x \xrightarrow{\Sigma} \widehat{Ax} = \Sigma \hat{x} \xrightarrow{U} Ax = U\widehat{Ax}$$

Composing: $Ax = U\Sigma V^T x = Ax$. Consistent.

The change of basis from standard to $V$-coordinates is $V^T$ (because $V$ is orthogonal: $(V^T)^{-1} = V$). In the $V$-basis, $A$ acts as $\Sigma$. Then $U$ converts back from $U$-coordinates to standard coordinates.

## Why Orthonormality of the Bases Matters

The bases $U$ and $V$ are not just any two bases that diagonalize $A$. They are orthonormal. This has consequences everywhere:

**Inverse is transpose.** $U^{-1} = U^T$ and $V^{-1} = V^T$. The change-of-basis computation costs a matrix-vector multiply, not a solve.

**Norms are preserved.** $|Vx| = |x|$ for any $x$. Rotating a vector does not change its length. So all the stretching done by $A$ is encoded entirely in $\Sigma$, not distributed across $U$ and $V$.

**Numerical stability.** Orthogonal matrices have condition number 1 — they are perfectly conditioned. Computing $U^T b$ or $V^T x$ in floating point introduces no amplification of errors. This is why SVD-based algorithms (least squares via SVD, pseudoinverse computation, PCA) are numerically stable even when $A$ is ill-conditioned.

**Geometric cleanliness.** The singular values $\sigma_i = |Av_i|$ are the lengths of the images of the right singular vectors. This is a geometric quantity — the amount of stretching in direction $v_i$. It is well-defined and does not depend on how we scale $v_i$ (since $v_i$ is a unit vector).

If $U$ and $V$ were not orthonormal, the singular values would depend on the normalization of the basis vectors and would lose their geometric meaning.

## The Closest Orthogonal Matrix Problem

A beautiful application of the SVD basis interpretation: given a square matrix $A$, find the orthogonal matrix $Q$ closest to $A$ in Frobenius norm.

**Solution.** Let $A = U\Sigma V^T$ be the SVD. The closest orthogonal matrix is

$$Q = UV^T$$

**Proof sketch.** We want to minimize $|A - Q|_F$ over orthogonal $Q$. Write

$$|A - Q|_F^2 = |U\Sigma V^T - Q|_F^2 = |\Sigma - U^T Q V|_F^2$$

(using the fact that Frobenius norm is invariant under orthogonal multiplication). Since $U^T Q V$ is orthogonal (product of orthogonal matrices), the problem reduces to finding the orthogonal matrix $P = U^T Q V$ closest to $\Sigma$ in Frobenius norm. Since $\Sigma$ is diagonal with non-negative entries, the closest orthogonal matrix to $\Sigma$ is $I$ (set each diagonal of $P$ to $+1$). So $P = I$, giving $Q = UV^T$. $\square$

This is the **orthogonal Procrustes problem** and it arises in computer vision (aligning point clouds), robotics (finding the best rotation), and structural biology (aligning protein structures).

## Summary: The Three Levels of Understanding SVD

**Level 1 — Formula.** $A = U\Sigma V^T$ with $U, V$ orthogonal and $\Sigma$ diagonal with non-negative entries. This is what you write on an exam.

**Level 2 — Factorization.** $A^T A = V\Sigma^2 V^T$, so $V$ diagonalizes $A^T A$, and $u_i = Av_i/\sigma_i$. This is how you compute it.

**Level 3 — Bases.** There exist orthonormal bases ${v_i}$ for $\mathbb{R}^n$ and ${u_i}$ for $\mathbb{R}^m$ such that $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$. In these coordinates, $A$ acts as pure scaling. This is what it means.

Level 3 is the right way to think about it. The SVD is the statement that every linear map, no matter how complicated, becomes a diagonal map in the right pair of orthonormal coordinates. The singular values are the diagonal entries — the intrinsic scaling factors of the map, independent of any coordinate choice.
