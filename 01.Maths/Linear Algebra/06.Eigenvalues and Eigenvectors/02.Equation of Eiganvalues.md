
## Where It Comes From

The eigenvalue condition is $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$ for some nonzero $\mathbf{x}$. Rearrange:

$$(A - \lambda I)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$$

This is a homogeneous linear system. A homogeneous system has a nonzero solution if and only if the coefficient matrix is singular. Singular means determinant zero. So:

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = 0$$

This is the **characteristic equation**. The eigenvalues of $A$ are precisely the roots of this equation.

> Personal note: the logic here is airtight and worth internalizing. We are not defining eigenvalues via determinants -- we are saying that the geometric condition (nonzero vector in the null space) forces a purely algebraic condition (determinant vanishes). The determinant is just a tool to detect singularity.

---

## The Characteristic Polynomial

Expanding $\det(A - \lambda I)$ yields a polynomial in $\lambda$ of degree $n$, called the **characteristic polynomial** of $A$, often written $p(\lambda)$.

$$p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$$

By the fundamental theorem of algebra, $p(\lambda)$ has exactly $n$ roots in $\mathbb{C}$, counted with multiplicity. Those roots are the $n$ eigenvalues.

---

## The $2 \times 2$ Formula

For

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{pmatrix}$$

we compute:

$$A - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} a - \lambda & b \ c & d - \lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (a - \lambda)(d - \lambda) - bc$$

$$= \lambda^2 - (a + d)\lambda + (ad - bc)$$

$$= \lambda^2 - \text{tr}(A),\lambda + \det(A)$$

So the characteristic equation for any $2 \times 2$ matrix is:

$$\boxed{\lambda^2 - \text{tr}(A),\lambda + \det(A) = 0}$$

and by the quadratic formula:

$$\lambda = \frac{\text{tr}(A) \pm \sqrt{,\text{tr}(A)^2 - 4\det(A),}}{2}$$

This is as closed-form as it gets. For $3 \times 3$ and beyond, there is no general formula worth using in practice -- expand the determinant directly.

---

## Worked Example: $2 \times 2$

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\text{tr}(A) = 4 + 3 = 7$, $\quad \det(A) = 12 - 2 = 10$

Characteristic equation:

$$\lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 10 = 0$$

$$(\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2) = 0$$

$$\lambda_1 = 5, \quad \lambda_2 = 2$$

Sanity check: $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = 7 = \text{tr}(A)$. $\quad \lambda_1 \lambda_2 = 10 = \det(A)$. Both hold.

## Worked Example: $3 \times 3$

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \ 0 & 3 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is upper triangular, so the eigenvalues are the diagonal entries directly:

$$\lambda_1 = 1, \quad \lambda_2 = 3, \quad \lambda_3 = 2$$

The characteristic polynomial is $(1 - \lambda)(3 - \lambda)(2 - \lambda)$ -- you can verify by expanding $\det(A - \lambda I)$, but for triangular matrices the diagonal product is all you need.

For a non-triangular $3 \times 3$, say:

$$B = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$B - \lambda I = \begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 2-\lambda & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

Expand along the first row:

$$\det(B - \lambda I) = (2-\lambda)\det\begin{pmatrix} 2-\lambda & 1 \ 1 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix} - 1 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 2-\lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

$$= (2-\lambda)\bigl[(2-\lambda)^2 - 1\bigr] - \bigl[(2-\lambda) - 0\bigr]$$

$$= (2-\lambda)\bigl[(2-\lambda)^2 - 1\bigr] - (2-\lambda)$$

$$= (2-\lambda)\bigl[(2-\lambda)^2 - 1 - 1\bigr]$$

$$= (2-\lambda)\bigl[(2-\lambda)^2 - 2\bigr]$$

Setting this to zero: $\lambda = 2$, or $(2-\lambda)^2 = 2$, giving $\lambda = 2 \pm \sqrt{2}$.

So $\lambda_1 = 2 - \sqrt{2}$, $\lambda_2 = 2$, $\lambda_3 = 2 + \sqrt{2}$.

Check: $\text{tr}(B) = 6 = (2-\sqrt{2}) + 2 + (2+\sqrt{2}) = 6$. Good.

## The General Structure of $p(\lambda)$

For an $n \times n$ matrix, the characteristic polynomial always takes the form:

$$p(\lambda) = (-1)^n \lambda^n + (-1)^{n-1}\text{tr}(A),\lambda^{n-1} + \cdots + \det(A)$$

The two coefficients we can always read off immediately are:

- **Leading term:** $(-1)^n \lambda^n$ (from the diagonal of $A - \lambda I$)
- **Constant term:** $p(0) = \det(A - 0 \cdot I) = \det(A)$
- **Second coefficient:** involves $\text{tr}(A)$

These give the two universal relations that must hold for any matrix:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i = \text{tr}(A) \qquad \prod_{i=1}^n \lambda_i = \det(A)$$

Use these as a check after every computation. If they fail, there is an arithmetic error somewhere.

---

## What the Equation Tells You Immediately

Before solving anything, the characteristic equation tells you:

|Fact|What it means|
|---|---|
|$p(0) = \det(A) = 0$|$\lambda = 0$ is an eigenvalue; $A$ is singular|
|$p(0) \neq 0$|$A$ is invertible|
|All roots real|Happens automatically for real symmetric matrices|
|Complex conjugate roots|Occur in pairs for real matrices with complex eigenvalues|
|Repeated root|Algebraic multiplicity $> 1$; matrix may or may not be defective|

## A Note on Computation

In practice, for $n \geq 4$, computing $\det(A - \lambda I)$ by hand becomes unpleasant. Numerical methods (QR algorithm, etc.) are used instead. In a course setting, all hand-computed examples will be structured so the polynomial factors cleanly -- look for integer roots first via the rational root theorem, or exploit special structure (symmetry, triangular form, block structure).

The characteristic equation is not just a computational device. It is the bridge between the geometry (eigenvectors, invariant subspaces) and the algebra (roots of polynomials, factorization). The Cayley-Hamilton theorem -- which says $p(A) = 0$, i.e., every matrix satisfies its own characteristic equation -- is a consequence of this same structure, and it is one of the more striking facts in linear algebra.

---

## Summary

$$\boxed{p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I) = 0}$$

Form $(A - \lambda I)$. Take its determinant. Set it to zero. Solve for $\lambda$. For $2 \times 2$ matrices use the trace-determinant formula. For triangular matrices read off the diagonal. For everything else, expand and factor carefully.

Always verify with: $\sum \lambda_i = \text{tr}(A)$ and $\prod \lambda_i = \det(A)$.
