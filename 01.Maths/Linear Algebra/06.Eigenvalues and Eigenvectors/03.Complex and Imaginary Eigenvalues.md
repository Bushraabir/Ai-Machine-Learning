## When Does This Happen?

The characteristic polynomial $p(\lambda) = \det(A - \lambda I)$ has real coefficients whenever $A$ is a real matrix. But a polynomial with real coefficients can absolutely have complex roots. The quadratic $\lambda^2 + 1 = 0$ has no real roots. Its roots are $\lambda = \pm i$.

So a perfectly ordinary real matrix can have complex eigenvalues. This is not pathological. It happens whenever the matrix encodes a rotation or a rotation-with-scaling, and it carries geometric meaning that is worth understanding precisely.

---

## Complex Roots Come in Conjugate Pairs

**Theorem.** If $A$ is a real matrix and $\lambda = a + bi$ (with $b \neq 0$) is an eigenvalue, then $\bar{\lambda} = a - bi$ is also an eigenvalue, and its eigenvector is the complex conjugate of the one for $\lambda$.

_Proof._ Suppose $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$. Take the complex conjugate of both sides:

$$\overline{A\mathbf{x}} = \overline{\lambda\mathbf{x}}$$

Since $A$ has real entries, $\overline{A\mathbf{x}} = A\bar{\mathbf{x}}$. So:

$$A\bar{\mathbf{x}} = \bar{\lambda}\bar{\mathbf{x}}$$

Thus $\bar{\lambda}$ is an eigenvalue with eigenvector $\bar{\mathbf{x}}$.

This means complex eigenvalues of real matrices always arrive in conjugate pairs $a \pm bi$. You will never see a lone complex eigenvalue on a real matrix. For a $3 \times 3$ real matrix, you either get three real eigenvalues, or one real and two complex conjugates.

---

## The Prototype: A Pure Rotation Matrix

The clearest example. Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

This rotates every vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ by angle $\theta$. No real vector stays on its own line under a proper rotation (unless $\theta = 0$ or $\pi$), so we expect no real eigenvectors.

### Characteristic Equation

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \det\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta - \lambda & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta - \lambda \end{pmatrix}$$

$$= (\cos\theta - \lambda)^2 + \sin^2\theta$$

$$= \lambda^2 - 2\cos\theta,\lambda + \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta$$

$$= \lambda^2 - 2\cos\theta,\lambda + 1 = 0$$

Quadratic formula:

$$\lambda = \frac{2\cos\theta \pm \sqrt{4\cos^2\theta - 4}}{2} = \cos\theta \pm \sqrt{\cos^2\theta - 1} = \cos\theta \pm i\sin\theta$$

So the eigenvalues are:

$$\lambda_1 = \cos\theta + i\sin\theta = e^{i\theta}, \qquad \lambda_2 = \cos\theta - i\sin\theta = e^{-i\theta}$$

These lie on the unit circle in $\mathbb{C}$. Their modulus is $|\lambda| = 1$. This makes complete sense: rotation does not change lengths, so the eigenvalues must have modulus 1.

### The Geometric Content

The modulus $|\lambda| = \sqrt{a^2 + b^2}$ tells you whether the transformation is expanding, contracting, or preserving lengths. The argument $\arg(\lambda) = \arctan(b/a)$ encodes the rotation angle. For pure rotation, $|\lambda| = 1$ and the argument is exactly $\pm\theta$.

## Worked Example: A Concrete $2 \times 2$

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

### Characteristic Equation

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (1-\lambda)(3-\lambda) + 2 = \lambda^2 - 4\lambda + 5 = 0$$

$$\lambda = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{16 - 20}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm \sqrt{-4}}{2} = \frac{4 \pm 2i}{2} = 2 \pm i$$

So $\lambda_1 = 2 + i$ and $\lambda_2 = 2 - i$.

Check: $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = 4 = \text{tr}(A)$. $\lambda_1\lambda_2 = (2+i)(2-i) = 4 + 1 = 5 = \det(A)$. Both hold.

### Eigenvector for $\lambda_1 = 2 + i$

$$(A - (2+i)I) = \begin{pmatrix} 1-(2+i) & -2 \ 1 & 3-(2+i) \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} -1-i & -2 \ 1 & 1-i \end{pmatrix}$$

Take the first row: $(-1-i)x_1 - 2x_2 = 0$. Set $x_1 = 2$:

$$x_2 = \frac{(-1-i)(2)}{-2} = 1 + i$$

Eigenvector:

$$\mathbf{x}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 1+i \end{pmatrix}$$

You can verify the second row is automatically satisfied -- it always is once the determinant is zero.

The eigenvector for $\lambda_2 = 2 - i$ is $\bar{\mathbf{x}}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 1-i \end{pmatrix}$ by the conjugate theorem. No need to recompute.

## What $\lambda = a + bi$ Means Geometrically

Write $\lambda = re^{i\theta}$ where $r = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and $\theta = \arctan(b/a)$.

When $A$ acts repeatedly (think $A^k\mathbf{x}$, or solutions to $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$), the complex eigenvalue governs the behavior:

- $r > 1$: the solution spirals outward (unstable)
- $r < 1$: the solution spirals inward (stable, decays to zero)
- $r = 1$: the solution orbits (neutral, pure rotation)

The real part $a$ of $\lambda = a + bi$ is what governs stability for differential equations. If $a < 0$, the mode $e^{\lambda t}$ decays. If $a > 0$, it grows. The imaginary part $b$ provides the oscillation frequency.

For the example above, $\lambda = 2 \pm i$ has real part $2 > 0$, so the system $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ spirals outward and diverges.

---

## The Real Canonical Form

Working over $\mathbb{R}$, we cannot diagonalize a matrix with complex eigenvalues using real eigenvectors -- there are none. But we can block-diagonalize it. For each conjugate pair $\lambda = a \pm bi$, the corresponding $2 \times 2$ block in the real canonical form is:

$$\begin{pmatrix} a & -b \ b & a \end{pmatrix} = r\begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & \cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

where $r = \sqrt{a^2+b^2}$ and $\theta = \arctan(b/a)$.

This is a rotation by $\theta$ followed by a scaling by $r$. The real structure of the transformation is rotation-plus-dilation in a 2-dimensional invariant subspace.

For the example $A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & -2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$ with $\lambda = 2 \pm i$: here $a = 2$, $b = 1$, so $r = \sqrt{5}$ and $\theta = \arctan(1/2)$.

## Special Case: Purely Imaginary Eigenvalues

When $a = 0$, the eigenvalues are $\lambda = \pm bi$, purely imaginary. This means:

- $\text{tr}(A) = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = bi + (-bi) = 0$, so the trace is zero
- $\det(A) = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 = (bi)(-bi) = b^2 > 0$

The associated differential equation $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ has purely oscillatory solutions -- no growth or decay. This is the case for undamped harmonic oscillators and conservative physical systems.

A real symmetric matrix never has purely imaginary eigenvalues (its eigenvalues are always real). Purely imaginary eigenvalues appear in skew-symmetric matrices ($A^T = -A$).

**Skew-symmetric example:**

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -3 \ 3 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$\det(A - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 + 9 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = \pm 3i$.

Pure imaginary. $\text{tr}(A) = 0$. This matrix rotates vectors in the plane (scaled by 3).

## Summary of What to Watch For

|Eigenvalue form|$\text{tr}(A)$|$\det(A)$|Geometric behavior|
|---|---|---|---|
|$\lambda = a \pm bi,; a > 0$|$2a > 0$|$a^2 + b^2 > 0$|Outward spiral|
|$\lambda = a \pm bi,; a < 0$|$2a < 0$|$a^2 + b^2 > 0$|Inward spiral (stable)|
|$\lambda = \pm bi$|$0$|$b^2 > 0$|Pure rotation/oscillation|
|$\lambda$ both real, same sign|same sign as $\lambda$|positive|Node (no oscillation)|
|$\lambda$ both real, opposite sign|could be anything|negative|Saddle point|

The sign of $\det(A)$ is the quickest tell: if $\det(A) < 0$, the eigenvalues are real and of opposite sign. If $\det(A) > 0$ and $\text{tr}(A)^2 < 4\det(A)$, the eigenvalues are complex.

---

## Core Equations

$$\boxed{\lambda = \frac{\text{tr}(A) \pm \sqrt{,\text{tr}(A)^2 - 4\det(A),}}{2}}$$

Complex eigenvalues occur when the discriminant is negative:

$$\text{tr}(A)^2 - 4\det(A) < 0$$

In that case $\lambda = a \pm bi$ where $a = \dfrac{\text{tr}(A)}{2}$ and $b = \dfrac{\sqrt{4\det(A) - \text{tr}(A)^2}}{2}$.
