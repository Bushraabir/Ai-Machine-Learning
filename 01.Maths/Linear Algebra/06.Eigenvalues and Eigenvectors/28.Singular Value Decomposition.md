
The singular value decomposition is the decomposition. Not the most elegant, not the most specialized â€” the most universally useful factorization in all of applied linear algebra. Every matrix has one, rectangular or square, singular or invertible, real or complex. It reveals the complete geometric action of a linear map, and it underlies an enormous range of applications: least squares, low-rank approximation, PCA, image compression, pseudoinverse, numerical rank detection.

If eigendecomposition answers "what does $A$ do to its own eigenvectors," SVD answers "what does $A$ do to any vector, in a geometrically clean way." The two questions are different, and SVD is the deeper answer.

---

## The Statement

**Theorem** (SVD). Let $A$ be an $m \times n$ real matrix. Then there exist:

- $U$: an $m \times m$ orthogonal matrix
- $\Sigma$: an $m \times n$ matrix with non-negative entries $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$ on the diagonal and zeros elsewhere, where $r = \text{rank}(A)$
- $V$: an $n \times n$ orthogonal matrix

such that

$$A = U \Sigma V^T$$

The diagonal entries $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$ are the **singular values** of $A$. The columns of $U$ are the **left singular vectors**, the columns of $V$ are the **right singular vectors**.

Written out in full, $\Sigma$ looks like this for $m > n$ with rank $r < n$:

$$\Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sigma_1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ 0 & \sigma_2 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & \ddots & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & \sigma_r \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \ \vdots & & & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$r$ nonzero diagonal entries, everything else zero.

---

## The Geometric Picture

This is the insight that makes SVD worth understanding deeply, not just as a formula.

Every linear map $A: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^m$ acts on the unit sphere $S^{n-1} \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ and maps it to an ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^m$. The SVD makes this precise:

$$A = U \Sigma V^T$$

Read right to left:

1. $V^T$ rotates (and possibly reflects) the unit sphere in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Since $V$ is orthogonal, $V^T$ is a rigid motion â€” it maps the sphere to itself, just reorienting it. Specifically, $V^T$ aligns the right singular vectors $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ with the coordinate axes.
    
2. $\Sigma$ stretches along the coordinate axes by factors $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$ (and collapses the last $n - r$ directions to zero). The unit sphere becomes an ellipsoid with semi-axes of length $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$.
    
3. $U$ rotates the resulting ellipsoid in $\mathbb{R}^m$, aligning its axes with the left singular vectors $u_1, \ldots, u_m$.
    

So: **rotation, then stretching, then rotation**. The singular values are the lengths of the semi-axes of the image ellipsoid. This is the complete description of what $A$ does.

The eigendecomposition $A = S \Lambda S^{-1}$ uses the same basis twice (eigenvectors for both input and output) and requires $A$ to be square and diagonalizable. The SVD uses two separate orthonormal bases â€” one for the input space $\mathbb{R}^n$, one for the output space $\mathbb{R}^m$ â€” and works for every matrix without exception.

---

## Existence: The Proof

**Proof.** We construct $U$, $\Sigma$, $V$ explicitly.

Consider the symmetric positive semidefinite matrix $A^T A$ (size $n \times n$). By the spectral theorem for symmetric matrices, it has a full orthonormal eigenvector basis $v_1, \ldots, v_n$ with eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \cdots \geq \lambda_n \geq 0$.

Define $\sigma_i = \sqrt{\lambda_i}$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$. These are non-negative. Let $r$ be the number of nonzero $\sigma_i$.

For $i = 1, \ldots, r$, define

$$u_i = \frac{Av_i}{\sigma_i}$$

**Claim:** ${u_1, \ldots, u_r}$ is an orthonormal set in $\mathbb{R}^m$.

**Proof of claim:** $$u_i^T u_j = \frac{(Av_i)^T(Av_j)}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = \frac{v_i^T A^T A v_j}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = \frac{v_i^T (\lambda_j v_j)}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} = \frac{\lambda_j}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} v_i^T v_j = \frac{\sigma_j^2}{\sigma_i \sigma_j} \delta_{ij} = \sigma_j / \sigma_i \cdot \delta_{ij}$$

For $i = j$: $u_i^T u_i = \sigma_i / \sigma_i = 1$. For $i \neq j$: $u_i^T u_j = 0$. Orthonormal. $\checkmark$

Extend ${u_1, \ldots, u_r}$ to a full orthonormal basis ${u_1, \ldots, u_m}$ of $\mathbb{R}^m$ (by Gram-Schmidt, say). Let $U = [u_1 \cdots u_m]$ and $V = [v_1 \cdots v_n]$.

Now check $AV = U\Sigma$:

- For $i \leq r$: $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$ by definition.
- For $i > r$: $\sigma_i = 0$, so $A^T A v_i = 0$, which gives $|Av_i|^2 = v_i^T A^T A v_i = 0$, so $Av_i = 0$.

In both cases, $Av_i = \sigma_i u_i$ (with $\sigma_i = 0$ for $i > r$). Since $V$ is orthogonal, $A = AV V^T = U\Sigma V^T$. $\square$

---

## Connection to Eigenvalues

The singular values of $A$ are the square roots of the eigenvalues of $A^T A$ (equivalently, of $AA^T$, which has the same nonzero eigenvalues).

$$A^T A = (U\Sigma V^T)^T (U\Sigma V^T) = V\Sigma^T U^T U \Sigma V^T = V(\Sigma^T \Sigma)V^T$$

So $V$ diagonalizes $A^T A$ with eigenvalues $\sigma_1^2, \ldots, \sigma_r^2, 0, \ldots, 0$.

$$AA^T = U\Sigma V^T V \Sigma^T U^T = U(\Sigma \Sigma^T) U^T$$

So $U$ diagonalizes $AA^T$ with the same nonzero eigenvalues.

This is the computational entry point: to find the SVD of $A$, diagonalize $A^T A$.

**Warning.** Forming $A^T A$ explicitly and diagonalizing it is numerically unstable for ill-conditioned matrices â€” squaring the matrix squares the condition number. Production SVD algorithms (e.g., in LAPACK) work with $A$ directly via bidiagonalization and do not form $A^T A$.

---

## The Four Fundamental Subspaces via SVD

SVD makes the four fundamental subspaces completely explicit.

Let $A = U\Sigma V^T$ with rank $r$.

- **Column space** $C(A) = \text{span}{u_1, \ldots, u_r}$: the first $r$ columns of $U$.
- **Left null space** $N(A^T) = \text{span}{u_{r+1}, \ldots, u_m}$: the last $m - r$ columns of $U$.
- **Row space** $C(A^T) = \text{span}{v_1, \ldots, v_r}$: the first $r$ columns of $V$.
- **Null space** $N(A) = \text{span}{v_{r+1}, \ldots, v_n}$: the last $n - r$ columns of $V$.

And crucially: $A$ maps $v_i \mapsto \sigma_i u_i$ for $i \leq r$, and $v_i \mapsto 0$ for $i > r$. The SVD is the four fundamental subspaces theorem given explicit, orthonormal bases. Strang's "big picture" of linear algebra â€” the two orthogonal complements, the map between them â€” is exactly what $\Sigma$ encodes.

---

## Reduced (Thin) SVD

For an $m \times n$ matrix with $m \geq n$, the full SVD has $U$ of size $m \times m$, which is large. The **reduced SVD** (also called economy or thin SVD) keeps only the first $r$ columns:

$$A = U_r \Sigma_r V_r^T$$

where:

- $U_r$ is $m \times r$ (first $r$ columns of $U$, orthonormal but not square)
- $\Sigma_r$ is $r \times r$ diagonal with $\sigma_1, \ldots, \sigma_r$
- $V_r$ is $n \times r$ (first $r$ columns of $V$)

This contains all the information. The remaining columns of $U$ span the left null space and multiply zero columns of $\Sigma$ â€” they contribute nothing to $A$.

---

## Outer Product Form and Low-Rank Approximation

The most powerful way to write the SVD:

$$A = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

Each term $\sigma_i u_i v_i^T$ is a **rank-one matrix** â€” an outer product. The SVD expresses $A$ as a sum of $r$ rank-one matrices, weighted by the singular values.

This is the foundation of **low-rank approximation**.

**Eckart-Young Theorem.** The best rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ in both the spectral norm and the Frobenius norm is

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

obtained by keeping only the first $k$ terms. The approximation error is:

$$|A - A_k|_2 = \sigma_{k+1}$$ $$|A - A_k|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_{k+1}^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2}$$

No rank-$k$ matrix is closer to $A$ (in either norm) than $A_k$. This is a non-trivial theorem â€” the proof uses the minimax characterization of singular values.

**Example: image compression.** An $m \times n$ grayscale image is a matrix of pixel values. Its SVD gives a hierarchy of rank-one images, each capturing a coarser or finer feature. Keeping the top $k$ terms requires storing $k(m + n + 1)$ numbers instead of $mn$. For a $1000 \times 1000$ image with rank $k = 50$, you store $100{,}050$ numbers instead of $1{,}000{,}000$ â€” a $10\times$ compression â€” with the truncation error controlled by $\sigma_{51}$.

## A Worked Example

Let

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

This is $3 \times 2$, rank 2. Compute $A^T A$:

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 9 & 0 \ 0 & 4 \end{bmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues of $A^T A$: $\lambda_1 = 9$, $\lambda_2 = 4$. Eigenvectors: $v_1 = e_1$, $v_2 = e_2$. So

$$\sigma_1 = 3, \quad \sigma_2 = 2, \qquad V = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I_2$$

Compute $u_1 = Av_1 / \sigma_1 = (3, 0, 0)^T / 3 = (1, 0, 0)^T$ and $u_2 = Av_2 / \sigma_2 = (0, 2, 0)^T / 2 = (0, 1, 0)^T$.

Extend to a basis of $\mathbb{R}^3$: $u_3 = (0, 0, 1)^T$.

$$U = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{bmatrix} = I_3, \qquad \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} 3 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

So $A = I_3 \cdot \Sigma \cdot I_2^T = \Sigma$. Trivially correct. A slightly more interesting example:

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}$$

$$A^T A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \ 1 & 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Eigenvalues of $\begin{bmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$: characteristic polynomial $(2-\lambda)^2 - 1 = 0 \Rightarrow \lambda = 3$ or $\lambda = 1$.

$$\sigma_1 = \sqrt{3}, \quad \sigma_2 = 1$$

Eigenvectors:

- $\lambda = 3$: $(A^T A - 3I)v = 0 \Rightarrow \begin{bmatrix} -1 & 1 \ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}v = 0 \Rightarrow v_1 = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\1\end{bmatrix}$
    
- $\lambda = 1$: $(A^T A - I)v = 0 \Rightarrow \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 1 \end{bmatrix}v = 0 \Rightarrow v_2 = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\-1\end{bmatrix}$
    

$$V = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Now compute $u_1 = Av_1 / \sigma_1$:

$$Av_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1\1\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}2\1\1\end{bmatrix}$$

$$u_1 = \frac{Av_1}{\sigma_1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \begin{bmatrix}2\1\1\end{bmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{bmatrix}2\1\1\end{bmatrix}$$

$$Av_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}0\-1\1\end{bmatrix}, \quad u_2 = \frac{Av_2}{\sigma_2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}0\-1\1\end{bmatrix}$$

Find $u_3$ orthogonal to $u_1, u_2$ (e.g., via cross product or Gram-Schmidt):

$$u_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\begin{bmatrix}-1\1\1\end{bmatrix}$$

(Verify: $u_3^T u_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{18}}(-2 + 1 + 1) = 0$, $u_3^T u_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(0 - 1 + 1) = 0$. Good.)

$$U = \begin{bmatrix} \dfrac{2}{\sqrt{6}} & 0 & \dfrac{-1}{\sqrt{3}} \[8pt] \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & \dfrac{-1}{\sqrt{2}} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \[8pt] \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{6}} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}} & \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}} \end{bmatrix}, \qquad \Sigma = \begin{bmatrix} \sqrt{3} & 0 \ 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}, \qquad V = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & -1 \end{bmatrix}$$

## The Pseudoinverse

For an invertible square matrix, $A^{-1} = V\Sigma^{-1}U^T$. The SVD makes this obvious: $(U\Sigma V^T)^{-1} = V\Sigma^{-1}U^T$.

For a rectangular or rank-deficient matrix, this formula still makes sense if we replace $\Sigma^{-1}$ with $\Sigma^+$: invert the nonzero singular values and leave zeros as zeros.

$$\Sigma^+ = \begin{bmatrix} 1/\sigma_1 & & & \ & \ddots & & \ & & 1/\sigma_r & \ & & & 0 \end{bmatrix} \quad \text{(transposed shape: } n \times m \text{)}$$

The **Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse** is

$$A^+ = V\Sigma^+ U^T$$

Properties:

- $AA^+ A = A$
- $A^+ A A^+ = A^+$
- $(AA^+)^T = AA^+$
- $(A^+ A)^T = A^+ A$

**Least squares.** The least squares solution to $Ax = b$ (minimizing $|Ax - b|_2$) with minimum norm is $\hat{x} = A^+ b$. This is the solution that lies entirely in the row space of $A$.

Explicitly:

$$\hat{x} = A^+ b = V\Sigma^+ U^T b = \sum_{i=1}^r \frac{u_i^T b}{\sigma_i} v_i$$

Each term projects $b$ onto $u_i$, scales by $1/\sigma_i$, and contributes $v_i$ to the solution. Small singular values amplify noise in $b$ â€” this is the source of ill-conditioning in least squares.

---

## Condition Number

The **condition number** of $A$ (for linear systems or least squares) is

$$\kappa(A) = \frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_r}$$

the ratio of largest to smallest singular value. It measures how much a relative perturbation in $b$ can be amplified in the solution $x$. A large condition number means the problem is ill-conditioned: small errors in data can produce large errors in the answer.

For a square invertible matrix: $\kappa(A) = |A|_2 |A^{-1}|_2 = \sigma_1 / \sigma_n$.

The matrix is effectively rank-deficient (numerically singular) if $\sigma_r / \sigma_1$ is smaller than machine epsilon $\approx 10^{-16}$. This is how production software determines numerical rank â€” not by checking whether entries are exactly zero, but by thresholding singular values.

---

## Spectral Norm and Frobenius Norm

SVD gives clean expressions for two important matrix norms:

$$|A|_2 = \sigma_1 \qquad \text{(spectral norm = largest singular value)}$$

$$|A|_F = \sqrt{\sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2 + \cdots + \sigma_r^2} \qquad \text{(Frobenius norm)}$$

The spectral norm is the maximum stretching factor: $|A|_2 = \max_{|x|=1} |Ax|$, achieved when $x = v_1$.

The Frobenius norm is the Euclidean norm of all matrix entries: $|A|_F^2 = \sum_{i,j} a_{ij}^2 = \text{tr}(A^T A)$.

---

## Principal Component Analysis

PCA is applied SVD. Given a data matrix $X$ of size $m \times n$ ($m$ observations, $n$ features), center the data so columns have mean zero. Then compute the SVD:

$$X = U\Sigma V^T$$

The columns of $V$ (right singular vectors) are the **principal components** â€” the orthogonal directions of maximum variance in the data. The $i$-th principal component $v_i$ captures variance proportional to $\sigma_i^2$. The low-rank approximation $X_k$ is the best rank-$k$ summary of the data in the least-squares sense.

Equivalently, PCA diagonalizes the sample covariance matrix $X^T X / (m-1)$. The eigenvectors of $X^T X$ are the columns of $V$. SVD gives you PCA without ever forming the covariance matrix â€” numerically preferable for high-dimensional data.

---

## Comparison: SVD vs Eigendecomposition

|Property|Eigendecomposition $A = S\Lambda S^{-1}$|SVD $A = U\Sigma V^T$|
|---|---|---|
|Exists for|Square, diagonalizable matrices|Every matrix|
|Shape|$n \times n$ only|$m \times n$, any shape|
|Basis matrices|$S$, $S^{-1}$ (not necessarily orthogonal)|$U$, $V$ (orthogonal)|
|Diagonal entries|Eigenvalues (complex in general)|Singular values (real, non-negative, always)|
|Same basis input/output|Yes|No (different bases for domain and codomain)|
|Geometric meaning|Scaling along eigenvectors|Rotation, stretch, rotation|
|Numerical stability|Poor if $S$ is ill-conditioned|Excellent ($U$, $V$ orthogonal, condition number 1)|

For a symmetric positive definite matrix $A$, the two coincide: $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ where $Q$ is orthogonal (spectral theorem), and this is simultaneously the eigendecomposition and the SVD with $\sigma_i = \lambda_i$.

## A Personal Remark

The first time you see SVD, it looks like a more complicated eigendecomposition for rectangular matrices. That undersells it completely.

The eigendecomposition is about invariant directions â€” vectors the matrix does not rotate, only scales. It is a special property of specific vectors. SVD is about the geometry of the map itself, for every input, without exception. The singular values are not a property of special vectors; they are a property of the transformation.

The Eckart-Young theorem is the moment where SVD reveals its depth. The fact that truncating the SVD gives the best low-rank approximation â€” not just a good one, the best possible one â€” in a strong norm sense, is genuinely non-obvious. It says that the information in a matrix is stratified: $\sigma_1$ captures the most, $\sigma_2$ the next most, and so on, in a precise quantitative sense. That is why SVD is at the heart of data science, signal processing, and numerical linear algebra simultaneously.

Strang calls it the most important factorization in applied linear algebra. He is right.
