
This note is the concrete payoff of [[Singular Value Decomposition]]. We established the Eckart-Young theorem abstractly — the rank-$k$ truncation $A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$ is the best rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ in both the spectral and Frobenius norms. Here we make that statement visible and quantitative in the context of images.

A grayscale image is a matrix. Compressing the image is approximating that matrix with a low-rank one. Everything else is bookkeeping.

---

## The Setup

A grayscale image of resolution $m \times n$ is exactly an $m \times n$ matrix $A$ where entry $A_{ij} \in [0, 255]$ (or $[0,1]$ after normalization) is the pixel intensity at row $i$, column $j$.

Storing $A$ naively requires $mn$ numbers.

The SVD gives

$$A = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

where $r = \text{rank}(A)$, $u_i \in \mathbb{R}^m$, $v_i \in \mathbb{R}^n$, and $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2 \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_r > 0$.

The rank-$k$ approximation is

$$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$$

Storing $A_k$ requires: $k$ values of $\sigma_i$, $k$ vectors $u_i$ of length $m$, $k$ vectors $v_i$ of length $n$. Total:

$$k(1 + m + n) \approx k(m + n) \text{ numbers}$$

**Compression ratio:**

$$\frac{\text{storage of } A_k}{\text{storage of } A} = \frac{k(m + n)}{mn}$$

For this to be less than 1 (i.e., to actually compress), we need

$$k < \frac{mn}{m+n}$$

For a square $n \times n$ image, this means $k < n/2$. For a $1000 \times 1000$ image:

- $k = 10$: store $20{,}000$ numbers instead of $1{,}000{,}000$. Compression ratio $0.02$.
- $k = 50$: store $100{,}000$ numbers. Ratio $0.10$.
- $k = 100$: store $200{,}000$ numbers. Ratio $0.20$.

The question is always: how large does $k$ need to be before $A_k$ is visually indistinguishable from $A$?

---

## What Makes an Image Compressible

Not all matrices are equally compressible. The SVD always exists, but the compression is only good if the singular values decay fast — if most of the "energy" of the image is concentrated in the first few singular values.

Define the **energy** of the full image and the rank-$k$ approximation:

$$|A|_F^2 = \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2, \qquad |A_k|_F^2 = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2$$

The **fraction of energy captured** by the rank-$k$ approximation:

$$\frac{|A_k|_F^2}{|A|_F^2} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2}$$

The **approximation error** in Frobenius norm:

$$|A - A_k|_F = \sqrt{\sum_{i=k+1}^r \sigma_i^2}$$

Natural images (photographs, portraits, landscapes) tend to have rapidly decaying singular values — the image has spatial coherence, so nearby pixels are correlated, and most variation lives in low-frequency structure. Text, random noise, and fine-grained textures compress poorly.

Structured images — smooth gradients, faces, skies — compress extremely well. A photograph of a blue sky is nearly rank-1: almost every row is the same vector.

---

## The Rank-One Term

It is worth understanding what a single term $\sigma_i u_i v_i^T$ looks like visually.

$u_i v_i^T$ is a rank-one matrix. Every row is a scalar multiple of $v_i^T$, and the scalar for row $j$ is $(u_i)_j$. So the image encoded by $u_i v_i^T$ has a single "pattern" $v_i$ repeated across rows, modulated by the weights in $u_i$.

- $v_i$ captures a horizontal pattern (a spatial frequency or shape across columns).
- $u_i$ captures how that pattern varies across rows.
- $\sigma_i$ scales how strongly that pattern contributes.

The first term $\sigma_1 u_1 v_1^T$ captures the dominant pattern: typically the overall brightness distribution of the image. Subsequent terms add finer and finer corrections.

This is not coincidence — it is the content of the Eckart-Young theorem. The greedy decomposition into rank-one terms, ordered by $\sigma_i$, is provably optimal. You cannot do better by choosing a different set of rank-one matrices.

## Color Images

A color image is three matrices stacked: one each for the red, green, and blue channels. Compress each channel separately:

$$R = U_R \Sigma_R V_R^T, \quad G = U_G \Sigma_G V_G^T, \quad B = U_B \Sigma_B V_B^T$$

Storage for rank-$k$ approximation of a color image:

$$3k(m + n) \text{ numbers vs. } 3mn$$

Compression ratio is the same as the grayscale case, just with a factor of 3 throughout. Alternatively, convert to a color space like YCbCr (luminance + two chrominance channels) and compress the chrominance channels more aggressively, since the human eye is more sensitive to luminance variation — this is what JPEG does in spirit.

---

## SVD vs. JPEG: What's Actually Used

It is worth being honest about where SVD image compression sits relative to production formats.

**JPEG** does not use SVD. It uses the **Discrete Cosine Transform (DCT)**: divide the image into $8 \times 8$ blocks, apply the DCT to each block (a fixed orthogonal transform, not data-dependent), then quantize and entropy-code the coefficients. The DCT basis functions are fixed sinusoids, not the data-adapted singular vectors.

**Why JPEG uses DCT instead of SVD:**

- DCT basis is fixed and universal — encoder and decoder both know it, no need to transmit $U$ and $V$.
- SVD is data-adaptive — you must transmit the singular vectors along with the singular values, which costs storage. For small $k$, the overhead of transmitting $U$ and $V$ may dominate.
- DCT on $8 \times 8$ blocks is $O(n^2 \log n)$ via the Fast Fourier Transform; SVD on the full image is $O(mn \cdot \min(m,n))$, which is expensive.

**Where SVD compression wins:**

- When the image has strong global structure (the singular vectors capture global patterns, not just $8 \times 8$ local ones).
- As a teaching and analysis tool — SVD compression is a clean, mathematically transparent demonstration of what low-rank approximation means.
- In applications like video compression, where the matrix is a difference frame (highly low-rank), or in hyperspectral imaging.

**JPEG 2000** uses wavelet transforms instead of DCT — closer in spirit to SVD (multiresolution, globally supported basis functions) but still with a fixed basis.

The honest summary: SVD gives the optimal low-rank approximation in the Frobenius sense, but "optimal" in that algebraic sense is not the same as "best for human visual perception." Perceptual image quality is not measured by Frobenius norm. JPEG's quantization tables are tuned to human visual sensitivity, not matrix norms.

## The Numerical Experiment You Should Run

If you have access to Python:

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from PIL import Image

# Load image and convert to grayscale float matrix
img = np.array(Image.open("photo.jpg").convert("L"), dtype=float)
m, n = img.shape

# Compute SVD
U, s, Vt = np.linalg.svd(img, full_matrices=False)

# Plot singular value decay
plt.semilogy(s)
plt.xlabel("index $i$")
plt.ylabel("$\\sigma_i$")
plt.title("Singular value decay")
plt.show()

# Rank-k approximations
for k in [5, 20, 50, 100]:
    Ak = U[:, :k] @ np.diag(s[:k]) @ Vt[:k, :]
    energy = np.sum(s[:k]**2) / np.sum(s**2)
    ratio = k * (m + n) / (m * n)
    print(f"k={k}: energy={energy:.4f}, compression ratio={ratio:.4f}")
    plt.imshow(Ak, cmap="gray", vmin=0, vmax=255)
    plt.title(f"Rank-{k} approximation")
    plt.axis("off")
    plt.show()
```

What to look for:

- The singular value plot on a log scale. For a natural photograph, you typically see a rapid initial decay followed by a long, slowly decaying tail. The "knee" of the curve is roughly where truncation becomes visually acceptable.
- At $k = 5$, the image should be recognizable but blurry. At $k = 50$, often near-indistinguishable from the original at normal viewing distance.
- Compare a photograph (smooth, compressible) against a random noise image (flat singular value spectrum, incompressible). The noise image requires $k \approx r$ to look right.

The singular value spectrum is a fingerprint of the image's complexity. A nearly flat spectrum means the image has no low-rank structure. A steeply decaying spectrum means the image is well-approximated by a few rank-one matrices.

## Quantifying the Error Precisely

Let $E_k = |A - A_k|_F / |A|_F$ be the relative approximation error. Then:

$$E_k = \sqrt{\frac{\sum_{i=k+1}^r \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2}} = \sqrt{1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2}{\sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2}}$$

This is computable from the singular values alone, without looking at the image. Given a target error tolerance $\varepsilon$, the minimum $k$ needed is the smallest integer such that

$$\sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i^2 \geq (1 - \varepsilon^2) \sum_{i=1}^r \sigma_i^2$$

This is the principled way to choose $k$: decide on an acceptable error level, then read off $k$ from the singular value spectrum. It is completely determined by the data.

## Connection to Randomized SVD

For very large images or matrices, computing the full SVD is expensive: $O(mn \cdot \min(m,n))$. If you only need the top-$k$ singular vectors/values, you do not need the full decomposition.

**Randomized SVD** (Halko, Martinsson, Tropp 2011) computes an approximate rank-$k$ SVD in $O(mn \log k)$ time:

1. Draw a random $n \times (k + p)$ Gaussian matrix $\Omega$ (with $p$ a small oversampling parameter, e.g. $p = 10$).
2. Form $Y = A\Omega$ (size $m \times (k+p)$): $k+p$ random linear combinations of columns of $A$.
3. Compute $Q$: orthonormal basis for the columns of $Y$ (via QR).
4. Form $B = Q^T A$ (size $(k+p) \times n$).
5. Compute the SVD of the small matrix $B = \hat{U}\hat{\Sigma}\hat{V}^T$.
6. Set $U = Q\hat{U}$.

The output $U\hat{\Sigma}\hat{V}^T$ is a near-optimal rank-$k$ approximation to $A$ with high probability. The key insight: $Y = A\Omega$ captures the dominant column space of $A$ because the random matrix is unlikely to miss directions with large singular values. The subsequent steps are just cleaning up the approximation.

For a $10^4 \times 10^4$ image, full SVD costs $\sim 10^{12}$ operations. Randomized SVD for top-50 components costs $\sim 10^7$. This is why randomized linear algebra has become central to large-scale data science.

## Summary

|Quantity|Formula|
|---|---|
|Full storage|$mn$|
|Rank-$k$ storage|$k(m + n)$ (plus $k$ singular values)|
|Compression ratio|$k(m+n) / mn$|
|Approximation error (Frobenius)|$\sqrt{\sum_{i > k} \sigma_i^2}$|
|Energy captured|$\sum_{i \leq k} \sigma_i^2 ,/, \sum_i \sigma_i^2$|
|Optimal rank-$k$ approx|$A_k = \sum_{i=1}^k \sigma_i u_i v_i^T$ (Eckart-Young)|

The deeper point: image compression is not special. The same mathematics applies to any matrix you want to approximate with a low-rank one — recommender systems (user-item rating matrices), genomics (gene expression matrices), natural language (word co-occurrence matrices). SVD finds the low-dimensional structure hiding in high-dimensional data. Images are just the most visually intuitive instance of this.
