## The Statement Worth Knowing Cold

There are several versions of the spectral theorem. I want to state them in increasing generality, because the progression reveals what the theorem is actually about.

**Version 1 (Real symmetric).** If $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n\times n}$ and $A^T = A$, then there exists an orthogonal matrix $Q$ and a real diagonal matrix $\Lambda$ such that

$$A = Q\Lambda Q^T$$

**Version 2 (Complex Hermitian).** If $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n\times n}$ and $A^* = A$ (where $A^* = \bar{A}^T$), then there exists a unitary matrix $U$ ($U^*U = I$) and a real diagonal matrix $\Lambda$ such that

$$A = U\Lambda U^*$$

**Version 3 (Normal matrices).** $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n\times n}$ is unitarily diagonalizable if and only if $A^_A = AA^_$ (i.e., $A$ is normal). In this case $A = U\Lambda U^*$ with $U$ unitary and $\Lambda$ diagonal (possibly complex).

The real symmetric case is the most important for applications. The normal matrix case is the most general. The Hermitian case sits between them.

The theorem is saying something deep: symmetry (of the right kind) forces perfect diagonalizability with orthogonal eigenvectors. No Jordan blocks, no complex eigenvalues for the real symmetric case, no missing eigenvectors anywhere. The algebraic structure of $A^T = A$ completely controls the spectral structure.

---

## What the Theorem Is Really Saying

Every $n\times n$ matrix acts on $\mathbb{R}^n$ as a linear map. For a general matrix, this action can twist, shear, and stretch space in complicated ways --- the eigenvectors need not be orthogonal, and repeated eigenvalues may not have enough eigenvectors.

For a symmetric matrix, none of that happens. The action is pure stretching: there exist $n$ mutually perpendicular directions (the eigenvectors $\mathbf{q}_1,\ldots,\mathbf{q}_n$) such that $A$ simply scales each direction by a real factor $\lambda_i$. In coordinates aligned with the eigenvectors, $A$ looks like

$$A\mathbf{x} = \lambda_1(\mathbf{q}_1^T\mathbf{x})\mathbf{q}_1 + \lambda_2(\mathbf{q}_2^T\mathbf{x})\mathbf{q}_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n(\mathbf{q}_n^T\mathbf{x})\mathbf{q}_n$$

This is the spectral decomposition $A = \sum_i\lambda_i\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$ in a more geometric form. The coefficient $\mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{x}$ is the component of $\mathbf{x}$ in the direction $\mathbf{q}_i$, and $A$ scales that component by $\lambda_i$.

A symmetric matrix does not rotate. It only stretches and reflects. This geometric fact is equivalent to $A^T = A$.

## The Proof, Done Properly

The proof I want here is the one by induction that also explains why Jordan blocks cannot appear. [[Symmetric Matrices]] has the three-part proof; I want to go deeper on the key step here and think about what the proof is actually using.

**What the proof needs from symmetry.** The key identity is: for any $\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^n$,

$$\mathbf{y}^T(A\mathbf{x}) = (A\mathbf{y})^T\mathbf{x}$$

This is just $\mathbf{y}^TA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}^TA^T\mathbf{x}$ rearranged using $A = A^T$. In words: $A$ is self-adjoint with respect to the standard inner product. The inner product sees no difference between $A$ acting on the left or the right.

This one identity implies everything:

**Real eigenvalues.** Suppose $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$ over $\mathbb{C}$. Apply self-adjointness to $\mathbf{y} = \bar{\mathbf{x}}$:

$$\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T(A\mathbf{x}) = (A\bar{\mathbf{x}})^T\mathbf{x} = \overline{(A\mathbf{x})}^T\mathbf{x} = \overline{\lambda\mathbf{x}}^T\mathbf{x} = \bar{\lambda}|\mathbf{x}|^2$$

But also $\bar{\mathbf{x}}^T(A\mathbf{x}) = \bar{\mathbf{x}}^T(\lambda\mathbf{x}) = \lambda|\mathbf{x}|^2$. So $\lambda = \bar{\lambda}$, meaning $\lambda \in \mathbb{R}$.

**Orthogonality.** Suppose $A\mathbf{x} = \lambda\mathbf{x}$ and $A\mathbf{y} = \mu\mathbf{y}$ with $\lambda\neq\mu$. Then:

$$\lambda(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y}) = (A\mathbf{x})^T\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}^T(A\mathbf{y}) = \mu(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y})$$

So $(\lambda-\mu)(\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y}) = 0$, giving $\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y} = 0$.

**No Jordan blocks.** This is the step that is usually skipped. Suppose $\lambda_0$ is an eigenvalue and $(A - \lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{x}$ where $\mathbf{x}$ is the eigenvector ($A\mathbf{x} = \lambda_0\mathbf{x}$). This would mean $\mathbf{v}$ is a generalized eigenvector of order 2, i.e., a Jordan block of size $\geq 2$ exists.

Compute $\mathbf{x}^T(A-\lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v}$ in two ways:

Way 1: $\mathbf{x}^T(A-\lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x} = |\mathbf{x}|^2 > 0$.

Way 2: By self-adjointness of $A - \lambda_0 I$ (which is also symmetric):

$$\mathbf{x}^T(A-\lambda_0 I)\mathbf{v} = ((A-\lambda_0 I)\mathbf{x})^T\mathbf{v} = (A\mathbf{x} - \lambda_0\mathbf{x})^T\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}^T\mathbf{v} = 0$$

Contradiction: $|\mathbf{x}|^2 > 0$ but also $= 0$. So no generalized eigenvectors of order 2 can exist. By induction, no Jordan block of size $\geq 2$ exists for any eigenvalue. $\square$

This is the clean proof that Jordan blocks are impossible for symmetric matrices. The self-adjoint property forces $(A-\lambda_0 I)$ to be orthogonal to its own image on the eigenspace, which is impossible if the image is nonzero. Defectiveness is self-contradictory for symmetric matrices.

## The Three Spectral Theorems Side by Side

||Real symmetric|Complex Hermitian|Normal|
|---|---|---|---|
|Condition|$A = A^T$|$A = A^*$|$AA^* = A^*A$|
|Diagonalizing matrix|Orthogonal $Q$|Unitary $U$|Unitary $U$|
|Eigenvalues|Real|Real|Complex|
|Key property|Self-adjoint over $\mathbb{R}$|Self-adjoint over $\mathbb{C}$|Commutes with adjoint|

**Normal matrices** include: Hermitian ($A^* = A$), skew-Hermitian ($A^* = -A$), and unitary ($A^*A = I$) matrices, plus products and sums thereof. All are unitarily diagonalizable.

The rotation matrix $\Omega = \begin{pmatrix}0&-1\1&0\end{pmatrix}$ from [[The Matrix Exponential]] and [[Euler Spirals]] is skew-symmetric ($\Omega^T = -\Omega$), hence normal, hence unitarily diagonalizable with eigenvalues $\pm i$. It is not orthogonally diagonalizable over $\mathbb{R}$ since its eigenvalues are not real --- you need the complex eigenvectors $\begin{pmatrix}1\\pm i\end{pmatrix}$.

## The Spectral Decomposition as a Projection Sum

The decomposition $A = Q\Lambda Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$ deserves its own analysis because each term $P_i = \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$ is a projection.

**Properties of $P_i = \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T$:**

- $P_i^2 = \mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T = \mathbf{q}_i(1)\mathbf{q}_i^T = P_i$: idempotent (a projection).
- $P_i^T = P_i$: symmetric (an orthogonal projection).
- $P_i\mathbf{q}_i = \mathbf{q}_i$: fixes $\mathbf{q}_i$.
- $P_i\mathbf{q}_j = \mathbf{0}$ for $j\neq i$: kills all other eigenvectors.
- $\sum_{i=1}^n P_i = QQ^T = I$: resolution of the identity.
- $P_iP_j = \mathbf{0}$ for $i\neq j$: projections onto orthogonal subspaces are disjoint.

The spectral theorem says $A$ decomposes into $n$ orthogonal rank-1 projections, each scaled by the corresponding eigenvalue:

$$A = \lambda_1 P_1 + \lambda_2 P_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n P_n$$

This is the most explicit form of the theorem. It says $A$ acts on any vector $\mathbf{x}$ by:

1. Project $\mathbf{x}$ onto each eigendirection.
2. Scale each projection by the eigenvalue.
3. Sum.

When some eigenvalues are repeated, the corresponding projections can be grouped into a higher-rank orthogonal projection onto the full eigenspace.

## Applying Functions via the Spectral Decomposition

For any function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, define

$$f(A) = \sum_{i=1}^n f(\lambda_i)P_i = Qf(\Lambda)Q^T$$

where $f(\Lambda) = \text{diag}(f(\lambda_1),\ldots,f(\lambda_n))$.

This is well-defined whenever $f$ is defined on the spectrum of $A$. The examples:

$$A^k = \sum \lambda_i^k P_i \qquad \text{(connects to [[Matrix Powers]])}$$

$$e^{At} = \sum e^{\lambda_i t} P_i \qquad \text{(connects to [[The Matrix Exponential]])}$$

$$A^{-1} = \sum \frac{1}{\lambda_i} P_i \qquad (\text{requires all } \lambda_i \neq 0)$$

$$\log A = \sum \log(\lambda_i) P_i \qquad (\text{requires all } \lambda_i > 0)$$

$$A^{1/2} = \sum \sqrt{\lambda_i}, P_i \qquad (\text{requires all } \lambda_i \geq 0)$$

$$\sin(At) = \sum \sin(\lambda_i t) P_i$$

$$\cos(At) = \sum \cos(\lambda_i t) P_i$$

The last two are the solution operators for the wave equation $\mathbf{u}'' = A\mathbf{u}$ (with $A$ negative definite so that $-A$ is positive definite and $\sqrt{-A}$ makes sense):

$$\mathbf{u}(t) = \cos(\sqrt{-A},t),\mathbf{u}_0 + \sin(\sqrt{-A},t)(\sqrt{-A})^{-1}\mathbf{u}'_0$$

Every matrix function of a symmetric matrix is defined by applying the scalar function to the eigenvalues. The eigenvectors do not change, only the scaling factors. This is the power of the spectral decomposition.

**Consistency check.** Since $f(A) = Qf(\Lambda)Q^T$, we have $f(A) = A$ when $f(\lambda) = \lambda$ (trivial), $f(A) = I$ when $f(\lambda) = 1$ (gives $QIQ^T = I$), and $f(A)g(A) = (fg)(A)$ (composition of functions). The algebra of functions of $A$ is commutative: $f(A)g(A) = g(A)f(A)$. This is because all such matrices are simultaneously diagonalized by $Q$.

## The Spectral Theorem and Quadratic Forms

From [[Symmetric Matrices]], the quadratic form $Q(\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}$ becomes $\sum_i\lambda_i c_i^2$ in the eigenvector basis where $c_i = \mathbf{q}_i^T\mathbf{x}$.

The spectral theorem is the algebraic core of the **principal axis theorem** for quadratic forms: every real quadratic form can be brought to the sum-of-squares form $\sum\lambda_i y_i^2$ by an orthogonal change of variables $\mathbf{y} = Q^T\mathbf{x}$.

The sign pattern of the $\lambda_i$ determines the geometry:

**All $\lambda_i > 0$ (positive definite).** Level set ${\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = 1}$ is an ellipsoid with semi-axes $1/\sqrt{\lambda_i}$ along $\mathbf{q}_i$. The largest eigenvalue corresponds to the shortest axis (most strongly constrained direction), the smallest eigenvalue to the longest axis (least constrained direction).

**All $\lambda_i < 0$ (negative definite).** Level set ${\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = -1}$ is an ellipsoid. No real solutions to $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} = 1$.

**Mixed signs (indefinite).** Level set is a hyperboloid. There exist directions where $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} > 0$ and directions where $\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x} < 0$. The origin is a saddle point of the quadratic form.

**Some $\lambda_i = 0$ (positive semidefinite or indefinite).** The quadratic form is zero in those directions; the level set is unbounded along the null space of $A$.

This geometry is the foundation of the second-derivative test in multivariable calculus. If $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ has a critical point at $\mathbf{x}^_$, the Hessian $H = D^2f(\mathbf{x}^_)$ is symmetric, and:

- $H \succ 0$: local minimum (level sets are ellipsoids)
- $H \prec 0$: local maximum
- $H$ indefinite: saddle point

The spectral theorem for the Hessian is the second-derivative test.

## The Condition Number

For a positive definite symmetric matrix, the **condition number** is

$$\kappa(A) = \frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\min}}$$

This measures how close $A$ is to singular and how much the solution $A^{-1}\mathbf{b}$ is amplified by perturbations in $\mathbf{b}$.

Specifically, if $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ and $A(\mathbf{x}+\delta\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{b}+\delta\mathbf{b}$, then

$$\frac{|\delta\mathbf{x}|}{|\mathbf{x}|} \leq \kappa(A)\frac{|\delta\mathbf{b}|}{|\mathbf{b}|}$$

A large condition number means small perturbations in $\mathbf{b}$ cause large changes in $\mathbf{x}$: the system is ill-conditioned. From the spectral decomposition:

$$|A| = \lambda_{\max}, \qquad |A^{-1}| = \frac{1}{\lambda_{\min}}, \qquad \kappa(A) = |A|\cdot|A^{-1}| = \frac{\lambda_{\max}}{\lambda_{\min}}$$

The spectral theorem gives exact formulas for the norms. For a general matrix, $|A| = \sigma_{\max}$ (largest singular value), but for symmetric positive definite matrices, singular values equal eigenvalues, so $\kappa = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$.

The Rayleigh quotient bounds from [[Symmetric Matrices]] make this concrete: $A\mathbf{x}$ can be at most $\lambda_{\max}|\mathbf{x}|$ and at least $\lambda_{\min}|\mathbf{x}|$ in norm. The ratio $\lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ is exactly how much the geometry of $A$ distorts lengths.

## The Courant-Fischer Min-Max Theorem

The eigenvalues of a symmetric matrix have a variational characterization that goes beyond the Rayleigh quotient for extreme eigenvalues.

**Theorem (Courant-Fischer).** Order the eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \leq \lambda_2 \leq \cdots \leq \lambda_n$. Then:

$$\lambda_k = \min_{\dim(V)=k};\max_{\mathbf{x}\in V,,\mathbf{x}\neq 0} \frac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}} = \max_{\dim(V)=n-k+1};\min_{\mathbf{x}\in V,,\mathbf{x}\neq 0}\frac{\mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{x}}{\mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{x}}$$

where the outer min (or max) is over all subspaces $V$ of the given dimension.

Special cases: $\lambda_1 = \min_{\mathbf{x}\neq 0} R(\mathbf{x})$ and $\lambda_n = \max_{\mathbf{x}\neq 0} R(\mathbf{x})$, the Rayleigh quotient bounds from [[Symmetric Matrices]].

The full theorem is the foundation for:

- **Interlacing theorems**: if $B$ is a principal submatrix of $A$, the eigenvalues of $B$ interlace those of $A$.
- **Perturbation theory**: $|\lambda_k(A+E) - \lambda_k(A)| \leq |E|$ (Weyl's inequality). Eigenvalues of symmetric matrices are Lipschitz continuous in the matrix.
- **Numerical methods**: the Lanczos algorithm, conjugate gradient, and other Krylov methods converge at rates determined by the eigenvalue distribution and the condition number.

## Why Symmetry Is the Right Condition

It is worth asking: what is special about $A = A^T$ that makes all of this work?

The answer is that $A = A^T$ is exactly the condition for $A$ to be self-adjoint with respect to the standard inner product. Self-adjointness means

$$\langle A\mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}\rangle = \langle\mathbf{x}, A\mathbf{y}\rangle \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y}$$

where $\langle\mathbf{x},\mathbf{y}\rangle = \mathbf{x}^T\mathbf{y}$. This is $\mathbf{y}^TA\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{y}$, i.e., $(A\mathbf{x})^T\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}^T(A\mathbf{y})$, which is $\mathbf{x}^TA^T\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{x}^TA\mathbf{y}$, i.e., $A = A^T$.

Self-adjointness is the algebraic condition that makes eigenvectors orthogonal (as proved above). The standard proof of the spectral theorem in functional analysis --- for compact self-adjoint operators on infinite-dimensional Hilbert spaces --- is the same inductive structure, just with compactness replacing finite-dimensionality to guarantee that eigenvalues exist.

The infinite-dimensional version: the Laplacian $-d^2/dx^2$ on $[0,\pi]$ with Dirichlet boundary conditions is self-adjoint with respect to the $L^2$ inner product. Its eigenfunctions are $\sin(nx)$ with eigenvalues $n^2$. The spectral theorem says these eigenfunctions form an orthonormal basis of $L^2([0,\pi])$ --- this is exactly the Fourier sine series. The spectral theorem for symmetric matrices and the completeness of the Fourier basis are the same theorem in finite and infinite dimensions.

## Summary

$$\boxed{A = A^T \implies A = Q\Lambda Q^T = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i,\mathbf{q}_i\mathbf{q}_i^T}$$

with all $\lambda_i \in \mathbb{R}$, all $\mathbf{q}_i$ orthonormal. No exceptions, no Jordan blocks, no complex eigenvalues.

The spectral theorem is not just a computational tool. It is the statement that symmetric matrices are, in a precise sense, the nicest matrices: they are exactly the linear maps that are pure stretching in $n$ orthogonal directions, with no rotation, no shearing, no defectiveness.

Everything in the theory of symmetric matrices --- quadratic forms, positive definiteness, the condition number, the Rayleigh quotient, Courant-Fischer, matrix functions, the connection to Fourier series --- follows from this one decomposition and the self-adjointness that produces it.
