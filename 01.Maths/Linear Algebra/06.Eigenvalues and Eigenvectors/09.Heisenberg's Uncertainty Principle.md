## What It Actually Says

Most presentations of the uncertainty principle are physical. Position and momentum cannot both be known precisely. Measuring one disturbs the other. This is true, but it obscures what is mathematically happening.

The uncertainty principle is a theorem in functional analysis. It is a statement about Fourier transforms. It says that a function and its Fourier transform cannot both be simultaneously concentrated. If one is sharply peaked, the other must be spread out. The physics is a consequence of the mathematics, not the other way around.

This note derives the uncertainty principle from the linear algebra of operators on function spaces, the same framework as [[Fourier Series]].

---

## The Setup: Quantum Mechanics in One Line

In quantum mechanics, the state of a particle is described by a **wave function** $\psi(x) \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$, normalized so that:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |\psi(x)|^2,dx = 1$$

The physical interpretation: $|\psi(x)|^2$ is a probability density. The probability of finding the particle in $[a,b]$ is $\int_a^b |\psi(x)|^2,dx$.

**Position** and **momentum** are not numbers attached to the particle. They are **operators** acting on $\psi$:

$$\hat{x} = x \cdot \quad \text{(multiply by } x\text{)}, \qquad \hat{p} = -i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}$$

where $\hbar = h/2\pi$ is the reduced Planck constant.

The **expected value** (mean) of position is:

$$\langle x \rangle = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x,|\psi(x)|^2,dx = \langle \psi, \hat{x}\psi \rangle$$

The **variance** in position is:

$$(\Delta x)^2 = \langle (x - \langle x\rangle)^2 \rangle = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (x - \langle x\rangle)^2,|\psi(x)|^2,dx$$

And $\Delta x$ is the standard deviation -- the spread of the position measurement.

Similarly for momentum, using the Fourier transform $\hat{\psi}(k)$ of $\psi$:

$$(\Delta p)^2 = \langle (\hat{p} - \langle \hat{p}\rangle)^2 \rangle$$

The uncertainty principle states:

$$\boxed{\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}}$$

## The Mathematical Core: Non-Commuting Operators

The reason position and momentum cannot be simultaneously sharp is that their operators **do not commute**.

Define the **commutator** of two operators $A$ and $B$:

$$[A, B] = AB - BA$$

If $[A,B] = 0$, the operators commute -- they share eigenvectors and can be simultaneously diagonalized. Measuring one does not disturb the other.

If $[A,B] \neq 0$, they cannot be simultaneously diagonalized. There is no basis of common eigenvectors. This is the algebraic source of uncertainty.

**Theorem.** $[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar$.

_Proof._ Apply $[\hat{x}, \hat{p}]$ to a test function $\psi(x)$:

$$[\hat{x}, \hat{p}]\psi = \hat{x}(\hat{p}\psi) - \hat{p}(\hat{x}\psi)$$

$$= x\left(-i\hbar\frac{d\psi}{dx}\right) - \left(-i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}(x\psi)\right)$$

$$= -i\hbar x\frac{d\psi}{dx} + i\hbar\left(\psi + x\frac{d\psi}{dx}\right)$$

$$= -i\hbar x\frac{d\psi}{dx} + i\hbar\psi + i\hbar x\frac{d\psi}{dx}$$

$$= i\hbar\psi$$

So $[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar \cdot I$. The commutator is a nonzero scalar times the identity. These operators are as far from commuting as possible -- no function of $\hat{x}$ or $\hat{p}$ can make them commute.

This single computation is the algebraic heart of the uncertainty principle.

## The Robertson Uncertainty Relation

The uncertainty principle for position and momentum is a special case of a general theorem about any two operators.

**Theorem (Robertson, 1929).** Let $A$ and $B$ be Hermitian operators on a Hilbert space and $\psi$ a normalized state. Then:

$$\Delta A \cdot \Delta B \geq \frac{1}{2}\left|\langle [A,B] \rangle\right|$$

where $\Delta A = \sqrt{\langle A^2\rangle - \langle A\rangle^2}$ is the standard deviation of $A$ in state $\psi$.

Applying this to $A = \hat{x}$, $B = \hat{p}$:

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle i\hbar\rangle| = \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

This is Heisenberg's uncertainty principle. It is a corollary of the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality applied to operators. Let me prove it.

---

## Proof via Cauchy-Schwarz

This is the rigorous derivation. It is worth doing in full.

Without loss of generality, shift so that $\langle A \rangle = 0$ and $\langle B \rangle = 0$ (replace $A$ by $A - \langle A\rangle I$ and similarly for $B$; the commutator is unchanged). Then:

$$(\Delta A)^2 = \langle A^2 \rangle = \langle \psi, A^2\psi\rangle = \langle A\psi, A\psi\rangle = |A\psi|^2$$

$$(\Delta B)^2 = |B\psi|^2$$

By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality on the inner product space:

$$|A\psi|^2 \cdot |B\psi|^2 \geq |\langle A\psi, B\psi\rangle|^2$$

So:

$$(\Delta A)^2(\Delta B)^2 \geq |\langle A\psi, B\psi\rangle|^2$$

Now write $\langle A\psi, B\psi\rangle = \langle\psi, AB\psi\rangle$ (since $A$ is Hermitian, $\langle A\psi, \cdot\rangle = \langle\psi, A\cdot\rangle$). Decompose into real and imaginary parts:

$$\langle\psi, AB\psi\rangle = \frac{1}{2}\langle\psi,(AB+BA)\psi\rangle + \frac{1}{2}\langle\psi,(AB-BA)\psi\rangle$$

The first term is real (since $AB + BA$ is Hermitian). The second term is $\frac{1}{2}\langle\psi,[A,B]\psi\rangle$, which is purely imaginary when $[A,B]$ is anti-Hermitian (as it is for $[A,B] = i\hbar I$).

So $|\langle A\psi, B\psi\rangle|^2 \geq \left|\frac{1}{2}\langle\psi,[A,B]\psi\rangle\right|^2 = \frac{1}{4}|\langle[A,B]\rangle|^2$.

Therefore:

$$(\Delta A)^2(\Delta B)^2 \geq \frac{1}{4}|\langle[A,B]\rangle|^2$$

$$\Delta A \cdot \Delta B \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle[A,B]\rangle|$$

For $[A,B] = [\hat{x},\hat{p}] = i\hbar$:

$$\Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{1}{2}|i\hbar| = \frac{\hbar}{2}$$

The proof is complete. The uncertainty principle is Cauchy-Schwarz plus the canonical commutation relation.

> Personal note: this derivation makes clear that the uncertainty principle has nothing to do with the clumsiness of measurement instruments or the physical disturbance caused by photons bouncing off electrons. It is a geometric fact about the inner product structure of $L^2(\mathbb{R})$. Cauchy-Schwarz is as clean as it gets -- the inequality $|\langle u, v\rangle| \leq |u||v|$ is what is doing the work.

## The Fourier Side: Time-Frequency Uncertainty

The same principle appears in signal processing with no physics at all.

A signal $f(t)$ and its Fourier transform $\hat{f}(\omega)$ cannot both be concentrated. Define the time spread and frequency spread:

$$(\Delta t)^2 = \frac{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} t^2|f(t)|^2,dt}{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|f(t)|^2,dt}, \qquad (\Delta\omega)^2 = \frac{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\omega^2|\hat{f}(\omega)|^2,d\omega}{\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}|\hat{f}(\omega)|^2,d\omega}$$

**Theorem (Fourier uncertainty).** For any $f \in L^2(\mathbb{R})$:

$$\Delta t \cdot \Delta\omega \geq \frac{1}{2}$$

This is the quantum uncertainty principle with $\hbar = 1$. The momentum operator $\hat{p} = -i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}$ corresponds to $-i\frac{d}{dt}$ in signal processing, whose Fourier transform is multiplication by $\omega$. The same algebra, the same commutation relation, the same Cauchy-Schwarz.

The uncertainty principle in signal processing says: you cannot design a filter that is simultaneously sharp in time and sharp in frequency. Short pulses have broad spectra. Pure tones are infinitely extended in time.

## The State That Achieves Equality

Cauchy-Schwarz is an equality when the two vectors are proportional: $A\psi = \lambda B\psi$ for some scalar $\lambda$.

For the position-momentum uncertainty principle, equality holds when:

$$\hat{x}\psi = -i\alpha\hat{p}\psi \quad \text{for some real } \alpha > 0$$

Substituting $\hat{p} = -i\hbar\frac{d}{dx}$:

$$x\psi = -i\alpha\left(-i\hbar\frac{d\psi}{dx}\right) = -\alpha\hbar\frac{d\psi}{dx}$$

This is a separable ODE:

$$\frac{d\psi}{\psi} = -\frac{x}{\alpha\hbar},dx \implies \psi(x) = Ce^{-x^2/(2\alpha\hbar)}$$

The **Gaussian** is the unique state that saturates the uncertainty bound. It achieves $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p = \hbar/2$.

The Fourier transform of a Gaussian is a Gaussian. A narrow Gaussian in position has a broad Gaussian in momentum, and vice versa. The product of their widths is always exactly $\hbar/2$. Gaussians are the optimal trade-off -- they minimize the product of uncertainties simultaneously.

This is why Gaussian wave packets appear everywhere in quantum mechanics: they are the states of minimum uncertainty.

## Eigenvectors of $\hat{x}$ and $\hat{p}$

In finite dimensions, commuting matrices share eigenvectors. What about here?

**Eigenvectors of $\hat{x}$** would satisfy $x\psi(x) = \lambda\psi(x)$, i.e., $\psi(x) = 0$ for all $x \neq \lambda$. The only such object is the Dirac delta $\delta(x - \lambda)$ -- not a function in $L^2$, but a distribution. The particle is perfectly localized at $x = \lambda$, so $\Delta x = 0$. But the Fourier transform of $\delta(x - \lambda)$ is $e^{-i\lambda\omega}$, which has constant modulus everywhere -- $\Delta p = \infty$.

**Eigenvectors of $\hat{p}$** satisfy $-i\hbar\frac{d\psi}{dx} = p\psi$, giving $\psi(x) = e^{ipx/\hbar}$ -- a pure plane wave. Not in $L^2(\mathbb{R})$ either (not normalizable), but has $\Delta p = 0$ and $\Delta x = \infty$.

In both extreme cases the product $\Delta x \cdot \Delta p$ is formally $0 \cdot \infty$ -- saturated but not realized by any physical state. Physical states live strictly between these extremes.

## The Linear Algebra Summary

Everything in this note is an instance of one algebraic principle:

**Two operators that do not commute cannot be simultaneously diagonalized. If $[A,B] = iC$ where $C$ is Hermitian and nonzero, then $\Delta A \cdot \Delta B \geq \frac{1}{2}|\langle C\rangle|$ in any state.**

The uncertainty principle is not mysterious. It is the statement that $\hat{x}$ and $\hat{p}$ are like two matrices that share no eigenvectors -- they represent incompatible measurements. And the quantitative lower bound on their product of uncertainties comes directly from Cauchy-Schwarz, the same inequality that bounds dot products in $\mathbb{R}^n$.

The infinite-dimensional setting (functions instead of vectors) and the continuous spectrum (distributions instead of eigenvectors) add technical complications. But the linear algebraic skeleton -- inner products, operators, commutativity, Cauchy-Schwarz -- is the same as in everything we have done before.

## Summary

|Object|Mathematical meaning|
|---|---|
|Wave function $\psi(x)$|Unit vector in $L^2(\mathbb{R})$|
|Observable|Hermitian operator on $L^2(\mathbb{R})$|
|Measurement outcome|Eigenvalue of the operator|
|Expected value $\langle A\rangle$|$\langle\psi, A\psi\rangle$|
|Standard deviation $\Delta A$|$\|A\psi - \langle A\rangle\psi\|$|
|Uncertainty principle|Cauchy-Schwarz applied to $\|(A - \langle A\rangle)\psi\|$ and $\|(B - \langle B\rangle)\psi\|$|
|Minimum uncertainty state|Gaussian: eigenvector of $A + i\alpha B$|

$$\boxed{[\hat{x}, \hat{p}] = i\hbar \implies \Delta x \cdot \Delta p \geq \frac{\hbar}{2}}$$

The commutation relation is the input. Cauchy-Schwarz is the engine. The uncertainty bound is the output.
