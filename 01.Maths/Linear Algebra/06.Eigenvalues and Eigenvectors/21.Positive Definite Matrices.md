## The Central Definition

A real symmetric matrix $A$ is **positive definite** if for every nonzero vector $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$$

The expression $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ is called a **quadratic form**. Positive definiteness says the quadratic form is strictly positive everywhere except at the origin.

This is a condition on a matrix that looks like an inner product condition. In fact, it _is_ saying that $\langle \mathbf{x}, \mathbf{y} \rangle_A := \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{y}$ defines a valid inner product on $\mathbb{R}^n$ — positive, symmetric, and bilinear. Positive definite matrices are exactly the matrices of valid inner products.

That is the geometric heart of the definition.

---

## Five Equivalent Tests

For a real symmetric matrix $A$, the following are all equivalent:

1. $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$ (definition)
2. All eigenvalues $\lambda_i > 0$
3. All pivots $d_i > 0$
4. All leading subdeterminants $\det(A_k) > 0$ for $k = 1, \ldots, n$
5. $A = R^T R$ for some matrix $R$ with independent columns (Cholesky-type factorization)

Tests 2, 3, 4 were connected in the previous note via Sylvester's law of inertia. Here I want to prove the equivalences involving the quadratic form (test 1) and the factorization (test 5), and really understand what each test is seeing.

---

## Test 1 $\iff$ Test 2: Quadratic Form and Eigenvalues

Since $A$ is symmetric, $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ with $Q$ orthogonal. Substitute into the quadratic form. Let $\mathbf{y} = Q^T \mathbf{x}$ (a change of variables — since $Q$ is orthogonal, as $\mathbf{x}$ ranges over all nonzero vectors, so does $\mathbf{y}$):

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T Q \Lambda Q^T \mathbf{x} = (Q^T\mathbf{x})^T \Lambda (Q^T\mathbf{x}) = \mathbf{y}^T \Lambda \mathbf{y} = \lambda_1 y_1^2 + \lambda_2 y_2^2 + \cdots + \lambda_n y_n^2$$

This is a sum of squares, weighted by eigenvalues. It is positive for all nonzero $\mathbf{y}$ if and only if every $\lambda_i > 0$.

This computation also reveals the geometry: in the eigenvector coordinate system, the quadratic form is a pure weighted sum of squares. The "shape" of the level set $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 1$ is an ellipsoid, with axes in the eigenvector directions and axis lengths $1/\sqrt{\lambda_i}$. Large eigenvalue $\to$ short axis (tight curvature). Small eigenvalue $\to$ long axis (shallow curvature).

---

## Test 1 $\iff$ Test 5: Quadratic Form and Factorization

**($5 \Rightarrow 1$).** If $A = R^T R$ with $R$ having independent columns, then:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T R^T R \mathbf{x} = (R\mathbf{x})^T(R\mathbf{x}) = |R\mathbf{x}|^2 \geq 0$$

This is zero only when $R\mathbf{x} = 0$. Since $R$ has independent columns, $R\mathbf{x} = 0 \implies \mathbf{x} = 0$. So the quadratic form is strictly positive for $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$.

**($1 \Rightarrow 5$).** If $A$ is positive definite, all pivots are positive (test 3, from the previous note). Write $A = LDL^T$ where $L$ is unit lower triangular and $D = \text{diag}(d_1, \ldots, d_n)$ with all $d_i > 0$. Write $D = D^{1/2} D^{1/2}$ where $D^{1/2} = \text{diag}(\sqrt{d_1}, \ldots, \sqrt{d_n})$. Then:

$$A = L D^{1/2} D^{1/2} L^T = (L D^{1/2})(L D^{1/2})^T$$

Set $R^T = L D^{1/2}$ (lower triangular with $\sqrt{d_i}$ on the diagonal, hence invertible). Then $A = R^T R$. $\square$

This is the **Cholesky factorization**: $A = R^T R$ where $R$ is upper triangular with positive diagonal entries. It is the symmetric positive definite analogue of $LU$ decomposition, and it is the most efficient way to solve $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b}$ when $A$ is known to be positive definite.

## What the Five Tests Are Actually Seeing

This is the thing I want to make explicit.

**Test 1 (quadratic form)** is the analytic/geometric condition. It asks: does $A$ define a valid inner product? Geometrically: are all level sets $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = c$ closed ellipsoids (not hyperbolas, not unbounded)?

**Test 2 (eigenvalues)** is the spectral condition. It asks: does $A$ only stretch, never flip? In every direction, does $A$ push outward?

**Test 3 (pivots)** is the computational condition. It asks: does Gaussian elimination on $A$ succeed without encountering zero or negative pivots? Equivalently, is the $LDL^T$ factorization well-defined with positive $D$?

**Test 4 (subdeterminants)** is the determinantal condition. It asks: are all upper-left corners of $A$ themselves positive definite? This is a nested condition — the whole matrix is positive definite iff every upper-left submatrix is.

**Test 5 (factorization)** is the algebraic condition. It asks: is $A$ a Gram matrix? That is, can $A$ be written as $R^T R$ for some $R$ with independent columns? This is the factorization that makes the positivity obvious.

All five are the same condition. Which one is most useful depends on context.

## A Worked Example: Verifying All Five Tests

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 \ 2 & 3 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Test 1 (quadratic form):**

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 4x_1^2 + 4x_1 x_2 + 3x_2^2$$

Complete the square: $4x_1^2 + 4x_1 x_2 + x_2^2 + 2x_2^2 = (2x_1 + x_2)^2 + 2x_2^2 \geq 0$, with equality only at $\mathbf{x} = 0$. Positive definite. $\checkmark$

**Test 2 (eigenvalues):**

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = (4-\lambda)(3-\lambda) - 4 = \lambda^2 - 7\lambda + 8$$

$$\lambda = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{49 - 32}}{2} = \frac{7 \pm \sqrt{17}}{2}$$

Both roots: $\frac{7 + \sqrt{17}}{2} \approx 5.56$ and $\frac{7 - \sqrt{17}}{2} \approx 1.44$. Both positive. $\checkmark$

**Test 3 (pivots):**

First pivot: $d_1 = 4$. Eliminate: subtract $\frac{2}{4} = \frac{1}{2}$ times row 1 from row 2:

$$\begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 \ 0 & 3 - 1 \end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 2 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Second pivot: $d_2 = 2$. Both positive. $\checkmark$

**Test 4 (subdeterminants):**

$\det(A_1) = 4 > 0$. $\det(A_2) = \det(A) = 12 - 4 = 8 > 0$. $\checkmark$

Note: $d_1 d_2 = 8 = \det(A)$. $\checkmark$

**Test 5 (Cholesky factorization):**

From $A = LDL^T$:

$$L = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 1/2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}, \quad D = \begin{bmatrix} 4 & 0 \ 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

Then $R^T = L D^{1/2} = \begin{bmatrix}1&0\1/2&1\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}2&0\0&\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}2&0\1&\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix}$

So $R = \begin{bmatrix}2&1\0&\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix}$ and:

$$R^T R = \begin{bmatrix}2&0\1&\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}2&1\0&\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4&2\2&1+2\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}4&2\2&3\end{bmatrix} = A \checkmark$$

## Failing the Test: Indefinite Matrix

$$B = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 2 & 1 \end{bmatrix}$$

Test 1: $\mathbf{x}^T B \mathbf{x} = x_1^2 + 4x_1 x_2 + x_2^2$. Take $\mathbf{x} = \begin{bmatrix}1\-1\end{bmatrix}$: gives $1 - 4 + 1 = -2 < 0$. Not positive definite.

Test 2: eigenvalues $3$ and $-1$. One negative. Fail.

Test 3: first pivot $1 > 0$, second pivot $1 - 4 = -3 < 0$. Fail.

Test 4: $\det(A_1) = 1 > 0$, $\det(A_2) = 1 - 4 = -3 < 0$. Fail.

The geometry: $\mathbf{x}^T B \mathbf{x} = 1$ is a hyperbola, not an ellipse. The quadratic form takes both positive and negative values.

## Positive Semidefinite

A symmetric matrix $A$ is **positive semidefinite** if $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{x}$.

The analogous tests: all eigenvalues $\geq 0$, all pivots $\geq 0$ (with possible zeros), all leading subdeterminants $\geq 0$, and $A = R^T R$ for some $R$ (possibly with dependent columns).

The key difference from positive definite: the quadratic form can be zero for $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$. The null space of $A$ is nontrivial. The level set $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 0$ is not just the origin — it is the entire null space.

The most important example of a positive semidefinite matrix: any Gram matrix $A^T A$ for a matrix $A$.

$$\mathbf{x}^T (A^T A) \mathbf{x} = (A\mathbf{x})^T(A\mathbf{x}) = |A\mathbf{x}|^2 \geq 0$$

This is zero when $A\mathbf{x} = 0$, i.e., when $\mathbf{x} \in \ker(A)$. So $A^T A$ is positive definite iff $A$ has independent columns (trivial null space).

## The Geometry of the Quadratic Form

In the eigenvector basis, $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \lambda_1 y_1^2 + \cdots + \lambda_n y_n^2$. The level set $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 1$ is:

$$\frac{y_1^2}{(1/\sqrt{\lambda_1})^2} + \cdots + \frac{y_n^2}{(1/\sqrt{\lambda_n})^2} = 1$$

This is an ellipsoid with semi-axes $1/\sqrt{\lambda_1}, \ldots, 1/\sqrt{\lambda_n}$ aligned with the eigenvectors.

Large $\lambda_i$: the matrix is "stiff" in direction $\mathbf{q}_i$ — the quadratic form rises sharply, the ellipsoid is narrow.

Small $\lambda_i$: the matrix is "soft" in direction $\mathbf{q}_i$ — the quadratic form rises slowly, the ellipsoid is elongated.

This is exactly what the condition number $\kappa(A) = \lambda_{\max}/\lambda_{\min}$ measures: how "elliptic" (far from spherical) the level sets are. A well-conditioned positive definite matrix has nearly spherical level sets; an ill-conditioned one has a very elongated ellipsoid — which is why iterative solvers struggle with it.

## Energy Interpretation

In mechanics and physics, $\frac{1}{2}\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}$ appears as potential or kinetic energy (for a quadratic system). Positive definiteness says the energy is always non-negative and zero only at equilibrium $\mathbf{x} = 0$. The eigenvalues are the normal mode frequencies squared. Positive definiteness of the stiffness matrix guarantees stable oscillations; an indefinite stiffness matrix means the equilibrium is a saddle point — physically unstable.

## The Principal Axes Theorem

A clean statement of what the diagonalization $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$ means for the quadratic form:

**Theorem.** For every real symmetric $A$, there exists an orthonormal basis ${\mathbf{q}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{q}_n}$ (the eigenvectors) such that in the coordinates $\mathbf{y} = Q^T \mathbf{x}$:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \lambda_1 y_1^2 + \lambda_2 y_2^2 + \cdots + \lambda_n y_n^2$$

No cross terms. Every quadratic form can be reduced to a sum of squares in the right coordinate system. The eigenvectors give the principal axes, and the eigenvalues give the coefficients.

This is why symmetric matrices are the natural object in optimization: at a critical point, the second-order behavior is described by the Hessian matrix (which is symmetric), and the quadratic form $\mathbf{x}^T H \mathbf{x}$ tells you whether you are at a minimum (positive definite $H$), maximum (negative definite), or saddle point (indefinite).

## The Test that Fails First

When checking positive definiteness by hand, the fastest test is usually:

1. Check $a_{11} > 0$ (first pivot, first subdeterminant).
2. Check $\det(A) > 0$ (product of pivots, last subdeterminant).
3. For $3 \times 3$: also check $\det(A_2) > 0$.

For a $2 \times 2$ symmetric matrix $A = \begin{bmatrix}a&b\b&d\end{bmatrix}$: positive definite iff $a > 0$ and $ad - b^2 > 0$.

This is the condition that comes up constantly in multivariable calculus when classifying critical points: positive definite Hessian means local minimum, negative definite means local maximum.

## Summary

A real symmetric matrix is positive definite when $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} > 0$ for all $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$. This one condition has five equivalent formulations: positive quadratic form, positive eigenvalues, positive pivots, positive leading subdeterminants, and Cholesky factorization $A = R^T R$. In the eigenvector coordinate system the quadratic form becomes a pure weighted sum of squares $\sum \lambda_i y_i^2$, and the level sets are ellipsoids with axes aligned to the eigenvectors. The Cholesky factorization makes positivity obvious by writing $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = |R\mathbf{x}|^2$. The whole theory is really about recognizing when a symmetric matrix defines a valid inner product — and all five tests are just five different ways to ask the same question.
