## The Question

Given the system

$$\frac{d\mathbf{u}}{dt} = A\mathbf{u}, \qquad A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{u}(0) = \mathbf{u}_0$$

does every solution decay to zero? Does some solution blow up? Does the system oscillate forever without growth or decay?

From [[Differential Equations and e^At]], the answer is determined entirely by the eigenvalues of $A$. But for a $2\times 2$ matrix, the eigenvalues are determined by just two numbers: the trace and the determinant. Everything reduces to two scalar quantities computed in seconds from $A$.

This note is about reading stability off the matrix without computing eigenvalues explicitly.

---

## Trace, Determinant, and the Characteristic Equation

The characteristic polynomial of $A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \ c & d \end{pmatrix}$ is

$$\det(A - \lambda I) = \lambda^2 - (a+d)\lambda + (ad - bc) = \lambda^2 - T\lambda + D$$

where

$$T = \text{tr}(A) = a + d, \qquad D = \det(A) = ad - bc$$

The eigenvalues are

$$\lambda = \frac{T \pm \sqrt{T^2 - 4D}}{2}$$

By Vieta's formulas:

$$\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = T, \qquad \lambda_1 \lambda_2 = D$$

The trace is the sum of eigenvalues. The determinant is the product. These two numbers encode the eigenvalues up to a sign ambiguity resolved by the discriminant $\Delta = T^2 - 4D$.

---

## The Stability Conditions

From [[Differential Equations and e^At]], the system is asymptotically stable if and only if both eigenvalues have strictly negative real part.

**Theorem.** The system $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ is asymptotically stable if and only if

$$T < 0 \quad \text{and} \quad D > 0$$

**Proof.**

$(\Rightarrow)$ Suppose both $\text{Re}(\lambda_1) < 0$ and $\text{Re}(\lambda_2) < 0$.

Then $T = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2$ satisfies $T = \text{Re}(\lambda_1) + \text{Re}(\lambda_2) < 0$ (eigenvalues of a real matrix come in conjugate pairs, so imaginary parts cancel in the sum).

For the determinant: if eigenvalues are real, $D = \lambda_1\lambda_2 > 0$ since both are negative. If eigenvalues are complex conjugates $\alpha \pm i\beta$, then $D = (\alpha + i\beta)(\alpha - i\beta) = \alpha^2 + \beta^2 > 0$.

$(\Leftarrow)$ Suppose $T < 0$ and $D > 0$.

Case 1: $\Delta = T^2 - 4D \geq 0$ (real eigenvalues). Both eigenvalues are real. Their product $D > 0$ means they have the same sign. Their sum $T < 0$ means both are negative.

Case 2: $\Delta < 0$ (complex eigenvalues $\alpha \pm i\beta$). Then $T = 2\alpha < 0$ gives $\alpha < 0$, so both eigenvalues have negative real part. $\blacksquare$

**Corollary.** For a $2\times 2$ matrix, no eigenvalue computation is needed. Just check: is the trace negative and the determinant positive?

---

## The Full Classification

Let me organize all qualitative behaviors in the $(T, D)$ plane. This is the **trace-determinant plane**, and it is the complete stability diagram for $2\times 2$ systems.

The parabola $\Delta = T^2 - 4D = 0$, i.e. $D = T^2/4$, separates real from complex eigenvalues.

### Above the parabola: $D > T^2/4$ (complex eigenvalues $\alpha \pm i\beta$, $\beta \neq 0$)

Here $\Delta < 0$, so $\lambda = \alpha \pm i\beta$ with $\alpha = T/2$ and $\beta = \sqrt{D - T^2/4}$.

|Region|$T$|$D$|Behavior|Name|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|$T < 0$, $D > T^2/4$|negative|positive|Decaying oscillation|Stable spiral|
|$T = 0$, $D > 0$|zero|positive|Pure oscillation|Center|
|$T > 0$, $D > T^2/4$|positive|positive|Growing oscillation|Unstable spiral|

### On the parabola: $D = T^2/4$ (repeated real eigenvalue $\lambda_0 = T/2$)

|$T$|Behavior|Name|
|---|---|---|
|$T < 0$|Decaying; solution $(c_1 + c_2 t)e^{\lambda_0 t}$|Stable degenerate node|
|$T > 0$|Growing; same form|Unstable degenerate node|

The companion matrix is defective here. From [[Second-Order ODEs]], the $te^{\lambda_0 t}$ factor comes from the Jordan block.

### Below the parabola: $D < T^2/4$ (two distinct real eigenvalues)

Here $\lambda_1, \lambda_2 \in \mathbb{R}$, $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$.

|Region|$T$|$D$|Eigenvalues|Behavior|Name|
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
|$D > 0$, $T < 0$|negative|positive|Both negative|Exponential decay|Stable node|
|$D > 0$, $T > 0$|positive|positive|Both positive|Exponential growth|Unstable node|
|$D < 0$|any|negative|One positive, one negative|Saddle; one grows, one decays|Saddle|

### On the boundary axes

|Condition|Behavior|
|---|---|
|$D = 0$, $T \neq 0$|One eigenvalue is zero; line of equilibria|
|$D = 0$, $T = 0$|Both eigenvalues zero; $A$ nilpotent or zero|
|$T = 0$, $D > 0$|Purely imaginary eigenvalues; center (neutrally stable)|
|$T = 0$, $D < 0$|One positive, one negative real; saddle|

## The Trace-Determinant Plane

Draw the $(T, D)$ plane. The axes and the parabola $D = T^2/4$ divide it into regions, each with its own phase portrait type.

```
D
|         Stable spiral | Unstable spiral
|              *        |        *
|         .   . D=T²/4  .   .
|       .                       .
|     .    Stable node | Unstable node
|   .                           .
|----+---------------------------+---- T
|        Saddle  |  Saddle
|                |
```

The stable region is the open quadrant $T < 0$, $D > 0$, which includes both stable spirals (above the parabola) and stable nodes (below the parabola). The boundary $T = 0$, $D > 0$ is the center: neutrally stable, not asymptotically stable.

**The saddle ($D < 0$) is always unstable**, regardless of $T$. This is because the eigenvalues have opposite signs whenever $D < \lambda_1\lambda_2 < 0$... wait: $D = \lambda_1\lambda_2 < 0$ means one positive and one negative eigenvalue. The positive one always wins.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Stable spiral

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & -2 \ 2 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

$T = -2$, $D = (-1)(-1) - (-2)(2) = 1 + 4 = 5$.

Check: $T < 0$ and $D > 0$. Stable.

Discriminant: $\Delta = T^2 - 4D = 4 - 20 = -16 < 0$. Complex eigenvalues.

$$\lambda = \frac{-2 \pm \sqrt{-16}}{2} = -1 \pm 2i$$

Real part $-1 < 0$: stable. Imaginary part $\pm 2$: oscillates at frequency $2$. Every solution spirals inward toward the origin.

### Example 2: Saddle

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \ 0 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

$T = -1$, $D = (1)(-2) = -2$.

$D < 0$: saddle. Eigenvalues are $1$ and $-2$ (diagonal matrix, read off directly). The solution along the $x$-axis grows; the solution along the $y$-axis decays. The $x$-axis is the unstable manifold; the $y$-axis is the stable manifold.

Note: $T < 0$ but $D < 0$. The negative trace is irrelevant. $D < 0$ is sufficient for a saddle.

### Example 3: Center

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -3 \ 3 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

$T = 0$, $D = 0 - (-3)(3) = 9 > 0$.

Purely imaginary eigenvalues: $\lambda = \pm 3i$. Every solution is a closed ellipse (circle, after rescaling). No decay, no growth. This is the continuous-time analogue of the rotation matrix from [[Matrix Powers]].

**Critical observation.** The center is neutrally stable: solutions do not decay to zero, but they also do not blow up. It sits exactly on the boundary $T = 0$ of the stable region. This boundary is not robust: perturb $A$ to make $T$ slightly negative and you get a stable spiral; make $T$ slightly positive and you get an unstable spiral. The center is structurally unstable as a phase portrait type.

### Example 4: Stable node vs. stable spiral

$$A_1 = \begin{pmatrix} -3 & 1 \ 1 & -3 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad A_2 = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & -4 \ 1 & -1 \end{pmatrix}$$

For $A_1$: $T = -6$, $D = 9 - 1 = 8$. $\Delta = 36 - 32 = 4 > 0$. Two distinct real eigenvalues: $\lambda = \frac{-6 \pm 2}{2} = -2$ or $-4$. Both negative: stable node.

For $A_2$: $T = -2$, $D = 1 + 4 = 5$. $\Delta = 4 - 20 = -16 < 0$. Complex eigenvalues: $\lambda = -1 \pm 2i$. Stable spiral.

Both are stable. The difference: in the stable node, solutions approach the origin along straight-line directions (the eigenvectors). In the stable spiral, solutions wind around the origin as they decay.

### Example 5: Degenerate (repeated eigenvalue)

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} -2 & 1 \ 0 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$$

$T = -4$, $D = 4$. $\Delta = 16 - 16 = 0$. Repeated eigenvalue $\lambda_0 = -2$.

$A$ is already in Jordan form. The general solution is (from [[Second-Order ODEs]] and [[Differential Equations and e^At]]):

$$\mathbf{u}(t) = e^{-2t}\begin{pmatrix}1 & t \ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix}\mathbf{u}_0 = e^{-2t}\begin{pmatrix} u_0 + v_0 t \ v_0 \end{pmatrix}$$

The $v$-component decays like $e^{-2t}$. The $u$-component initially grows (the $v_0 t e^{-2t}$ term), then decays. The exponential wins. Stable.

## The Routh-Hurwitz Conditions for $n = 2$

For the general $2\times 2$ case, the conditions $T < 0$ and $D > 0$ are the Routh-Hurwitz stability criteria. They are necessary and sufficient.

The proof above shows exactly why: for real eigenvalues, $T < 0$ and $D > 0$ force both eigenvalues negative. For complex eigenvalues, $T = 2\alpha < 0$ forces negative real part, and $D = \alpha^2 + \beta^2 > 0$ is automatic.

For comparison, from [[Second-Order ODEs]], the scalar ODE $u'' + pu' + qu = 0$ has companion matrix with $T = -p$ and $D = q$. The Routh-Hurwitz conditions $T < 0$ and $D > 0$ become $p > 0$ and $q > 0$, which is exactly what was proved there. The $2\times 2$ stability theorem contains the scalar second-order ODE stability theorem as a special case.

## What the Phase Portrait Looks Like in Each Case

The phase portrait of $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ is the family of solution curves in the $(u_1, u_2)$ plane.

**Stable node.** Two distinct negative eigenvalues $\lambda_1 < \lambda_2 < 0$. Every solution curve approaches the origin tangent to the slow eigenvector (the one for $\lambda_2$, the eigenvalue closer to zero). The fast direction $\mathbf{x}_1$ is visible only near the origin.

**Unstable node.** Same picture, arrows reversed, flowing away from the origin.

**Stable spiral.** Complex eigenvalues $\alpha \pm i\beta$, $\alpha < 0$. Solution curves spiral inward. The direction of rotation is determined by the sign of the off-diagonal entries.

**Center.** Closed ellipses around the origin. The axes and shape of the ellipse depend on the eigenvectors.

**Saddle.** Two real eigenvalues of opposite sign. Four separatrices: the stable manifold (solutions that flow into the origin, aligned with the negative eigenvalue's eigenvector) and the unstable manifold (solutions that flow away, aligned with the positive eigenvalue's eigenvector). All other trajectories are hyperbolic curves that approach the unstable manifold for large $t$.

**Degenerate node.** On the parabola $D = T^2/4$. Solution curves approach the origin tangent to the single eigenvector. The $te^{\lambda_0 t}$ term causes trajectories to bend toward the eigenvector direction before decaying.

---

## A Warning About the Center

The center ($T = 0$, $D > 0$) is the most deceptively fragile case. In exact arithmetic, pure imaginary eigenvalues give closed orbits forever. In practice:

Any small perturbation $A \to A + \epsilon B$ generically changes $T$ to $T + \epsilon, \text{tr}(B) \neq 0$, turning the center into a spiral (stable or unstable depending on the sign of $\text{tr}(B)$). The center is a codimension-1 phenomenon sitting on the boundary between two qualitatively different behaviors.

This matters physically. A conservative mechanical system (no friction) has a Hamiltonian structure that forces $T = 0$ exactly. Add any small damping (friction) and $T < 0$; remove energy and the center becomes a stable spiral. This is why real physical oscillators always decay: any perturbation that breaks the exact Hamiltonian structure destroys the center.

## The Nonlinear Case: Linearization

For a nonlinear system $\mathbf{u}' = \mathbf{f}(\mathbf{u})$ near an equilibrium $\mathbf{u}^*$, the Jacobian

$$A = D\mathbf{f}(\mathbf{u}^_) = \begin{pmatrix} \partial f_1/\partial u_1 & \partial f_1/\partial u_2 \ \partial f_2/\partial u_1 & \partial f_2/\partial u_2 \end{pmatrix}\bigg|_{\mathbf{u}^_}$$

determines local stability via the Hartman-Grobman theorem: if $A$ has no eigenvalue with zero real part (i.e., not a center or degenerate case), the nonlinear system near $\mathbf{u}^*$ is topologically equivalent to the linear system $\mathbf{v}' = A\mathbf{v}$.

The conditions are again $T < 0$ and $D > 0$. Every question about local stability of a $2\times 2$ nonlinear system reduces to this.

The center is the exceptional case where linearization is insufficient: the nonlinear terms determine whether the equilibrium is actually a center, a stable spiral, or an unstable spiral.

## Summary

For $\mathbf{u}' = A\mathbf{u}$ with $A$ a $2\times 2$ real matrix, let $T = \text{tr}(A)$ and $D = \det(A)$.

$$\boxed{\text{Asymptotically stable} \iff T < 0 \text{ and } D > 0}$$

|$D$|$T$|$\Delta = T^2 - 4D$|Type|
|---|---|---|---|
|$D > 0$|$T < 0$|$\Delta < 0$|Stable spiral|
|$D > 0$|$T < 0$|$\Delta = 0$|Stable degenerate node|
|$D > 0$|$T < 0$|$\Delta > 0$|Stable node|
|$D > 0$|$T = 0$|$\Delta < 0$|Center (neutrally stable)|
|$D > 0$|$T > 0$|$\Delta < 0$|Unstable spiral|
|$D > 0$|$T > 0$|$\Delta = 0$|Unstable degenerate node|
|$D > 0$|$T > 0$|$\Delta > 0$|Unstable node|
|$D < 0$|any|$\Delta > 0$|Saddle (always unstable)|

Everything depends on two numbers. This is the power of the $2\times 2$ case: the full qualitative theory of planar linear systems fits in a single diagram.
