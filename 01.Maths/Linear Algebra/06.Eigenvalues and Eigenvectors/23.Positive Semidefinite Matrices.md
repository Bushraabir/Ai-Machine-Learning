## The Definition

A real symmetric matrix $A$ is **positive semidefinite (PSD)** if for every $\mathbf{x} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} \geq 0$$

The difference from positive definite (PD) is the $\geq$ instead of $>$. The quadratic form is allowed to be zero for nonzero $\mathbf{x}$.

That single change — allowing equality — has deep consequences. PSD matrices can be singular. Their null space is nontrivial. The level set ${\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 0}$ is no longer just the origin — it is the entire null space of $A$. The geometry collapses in certain directions.

I want to understand PSD not just as "PD with some eigenvalues equal to zero" but as its own object with its own character.

---

## The Canonical Example: $A^T A$

The single most important class of PSD matrices is Gram matrices.

For any matrix $A$ (not necessarily square, not necessarily symmetric), the matrix $A^T A$ is always symmetric and positive semidefinite.

**Proof.**

Symmetry: $(A^T A)^T = A^T (A^T)^T = A^T A$. $\checkmark$

Semidefiniteness: for any $\mathbf{x}$, $$\mathbf{x}^T (A^T A) \mathbf{x} = (A\mathbf{x})^T (A\mathbf{x}) = |A\mathbf{x}|^2 \geq 0$$

This is zero when $A\mathbf{x} = 0$, i.e., when $\mathbf{x} \in \ker(A)$.

So:

- $A^T A$ is **positive definite** $\iff$ $\ker(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$ $\iff$ $A$ has linearly independent columns.
- $A^T A$ is **positive semidefinite but not definite** $\iff$ $A$ has a nontrivial null space $\iff$ $A$ has dependent columns.

Every PSD matrix is a Gram matrix of some other matrix. This is not obvious — it follows from the Cholesky-type factorization I will prove below.

---

## Five Tests, Adjusted for Semidefiniteness

For a real symmetric matrix $A$, the following are equivalent:

1. $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{x}$ (definition)
2. All eigenvalues $\lambda_i \geq 0$
3. All pivots $d_i \geq 0$ (with possible zeros, requiring row exchanges or stopping)
4. All leading subdeterminants $\det(A_k) \geq 0$
5. $A = R^T R$ for some matrix $R$ (columns may be dependent)

The proofs of $1 \iff 2$ and $1 \iff 5$ are almost identical to the PD case, with $>$ replaced by $\geq$ throughout.

**Test 3 is the delicate one.** When a pivot is zero, Gaussian elimination stalls — there is nothing to eliminate with. The matrix is singular and the $LDL^T$ factorization in the full sense does not exist. But the sign of the pivots that do exist is still meaningful, and the rank of $A$ equals the number of nonzero pivots.

**Test 4 is also more subtle.** For PD, all $\det(A_k) > 0$. For PSD, some $\det(A_k)$ may be zero. But the first time a leading subdeterminant is zero signals a zero pivot at that stage, which signals a zero eigenvalue arriving at that level of the induction.

---

## Proof: Test 1 $\iff$ Test 2

Since $A$ is symmetric, $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$. Let $\mathbf{y} = Q^T \mathbf{x}$:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{y}^T \Lambda \mathbf{y} = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i y_i^2$$

This is $\geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{y}$ iff every $\lambda_i \geq 0$. $\square$

The zero directions: if $\lambda_k = 0$ for some $k$, then taking $\mathbf{y} = \mathbf{e}_k$ (the $k$-th standard basis vector) gives $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 0$, with $\mathbf{x} = Q\mathbf{e}_k = \mathbf{q}_k \neq \mathbf{0}$.

---

## Proof: Test 1 $\iff$ Test 5

**($5 \Rightarrow 1$).** If $A = R^T R$:

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T R^T R \mathbf{x} = |R\mathbf{x}|^2 \geq 0$$

Zero when $R\mathbf{x} = 0$ — possible even for $\mathbf{x} \neq 0$ if $R$ has dependent columns.

**($1 \Rightarrow 5$).** If $A$ is PSD, all eigenvalues $\lambda_i \geq 0$, so $\sqrt{\lambda_i}$ is real. Define $\Lambda^{1/2} = \text{diag}(\sqrt{\lambda_1}, \ldots, \sqrt{\lambda_n})$. Then:

$$A = Q\Lambda Q^T = Q \Lambda^{1/2} \Lambda^{1/2} Q^T = (Q\Lambda^{1/2})(Q\Lambda^{1/2})^T = R^T R$$

where $R^T = Q\Lambda^{1/2}$, i.e., $R = \Lambda^{1/2} Q^T$.

Note: if some $\lambda_i = 0$, the corresponding column of $R^T = Q\Lambda^{1/2}$ is zero, so $R$ has dependent columns. Consistent with the observation above. $\square$

The matrix $A^{1/2} := Q\Lambda^{1/2}Q^T$ is the **square root** of $A$ — a PSD matrix satisfying $(A^{1/2})^2 = A$. This only makes sense because eigenvalues are non-negative. For an indefinite matrix the square root does not exist (over $\mathbb{R}$).

---

## Rank and the Null Space

For a PSD matrix $A$:

$$\ker(A) = \text{eigenspace for } \lambda = 0$$

**Proof.** If $A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{0}$ then certainly $A\mathbf{x} = 0 \cdot \mathbf{x}$, so $\mathbf{x}$ is an eigenvector for $\lambda = 0$.

Conversely, if $\lambda_k = 0$ and $A\mathbf{q}_k = 0$, then $\mathbf{q}_k \in \ker(A)$. $\square$

This means:

$$\text{rank}(A) = n - \dim(\ker A) = \text{number of nonzero eigenvalues} = \text{number of nonzero pivots}$$

For a PD matrix, $\ker(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$ so $A$ is invertible. For a PSD matrix that is not PD, $A$ is singular. The null space is the "flat" direction of the quadratic form — the directions along which $A$ neither stretches nor compresses, because it collapses them to zero.

---

## The Geometry: Collapsed Ellipsoid

For PD, the level set ${\mathbf{x} : \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 1}$ is a bounded ellipsoid with all finite, nonzero semi-axes $1/\sqrt{\lambda_i}$.

For PSD with some $\lambda_k = 0$: the "semi-axis" in the $\mathbf{q}_k$ direction is $1/\sqrt{0} = \infty$. The ellipsoid degenerates — it becomes an infinite cylinder (or slab, or all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ if all eigenvalues are zero). The level set $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 1$ is unbounded.

Equivalently, $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = 0$ is satisfied by the entire subspace $\ker(A) = \text{span}{\mathbf{q}_k : \lambda_k = 0}$, not just the origin. The quadratic form is flat along the null space.

This is the geometric distinction I should keep in mind: PD $=$ compact ellipsoid, PSD $=$ degenerate collapsed ellipsoid extending to infinity in the null directions.

## A Worked Example

$$A = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 2 \end{bmatrix}$$

**Test 2 (eigenvalues):**

$\text{tr}(A) = 4$, $\det(A) = 0$ (rows 1 and 2 are identical, so $A$ is singular).

Since $\det(A) = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 \lambda_3 = 0$, at least one eigenvalue is zero.

The matrix has rank 2 (two independent rows: row 1 and row 3). So the null space is 1-dimensional, meaning exactly one zero eigenvalue.

For the third row acting independently: by inspection, $A\begin{bmatrix}0\0\1\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}0\0\2\end{bmatrix} = 2\begin{bmatrix}0\0\1\end{bmatrix}$, so $\lambda = 2$ with eigenvector $\mathbf{q}_3 = \begin{bmatrix}0\0\1\end{bmatrix}$.

The null space: $A\mathbf{x} = 0$ gives $x_1 + x_2 = 0$ and $2x_3 = 0$, so $\mathbf{x} = t\begin{bmatrix}1\-1\0\end{bmatrix}$. Zero eigenvalue with eigenvector $\mathbf{q}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\-1\0\end{bmatrix}$.

Remaining eigenvalue: $\text{tr}(A) = \lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \lambda_3 = 0 + \lambda_2 + 2 = 4$, so $\lambda_2 = 2$ with eigenvector orthogonal to both $\mathbf{q}_1$ and $\mathbf{q}_3$: $\mathbf{q}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{bmatrix}1\1\0\end{bmatrix}$.

Eigenvalues: $0, 2, 2$. All $\geq 0$. PSD (not PD). $\checkmark$

**Test 1 (quadratic form):**

$$\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = x_1^2 + 2x_1 x_2 + x_2^2 + 2x_3^2 = (x_1 + x_2)^2 + 2x_3^2 \geq 0$$

Zero when $x_1 + x_2 = 0$ and $x_3 = 0$, i.e., along $\begin{bmatrix}1\-1\0\end{bmatrix}$ — exactly the null space. $\checkmark$

**Test 5 (factorization):**

$A = R^T R$ with $R = \Lambda^{1/2} Q^T$. Since $\lambda_1 = 0$, the first row of $R$ is the zero vector. But we can also read it from the quadratic form directly: $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} = (x_1 + x_2)^2 + (\sqrt{2}, x_3)^2 = |R\mathbf{x}|^2$ with

$$R = \begin{bmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & \sqrt{2} \end{bmatrix}$$

A $2 \times 3$ matrix — fewer rows than columns, hence dependent columns. $A^T A$ of this $R$ is not $A$, but $R^T R$ is:

$$R^T R = \begin{bmatrix}1&0\1&0\0&\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix}\begin{bmatrix}1&1&0\0&0&\sqrt{2}\end{bmatrix} = \begin{bmatrix}1&1&0\1&1&0\0&0&2\end{bmatrix} = A \checkmark$$

## PSD is Closed Under Addition and Positive Scaling

If $A$ and $B$ are PSD, then:

- $A + B$ is PSD: $\mathbf{x}^T(A+B)\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} + \mathbf{x}^T B \mathbf{x} \geq 0$.
- $cA$ is PSD for any $c \geq 0$: $\mathbf{x}^T(cA)\mathbf{x} = c(\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x}) \geq 0$.

This makes the set of $n \times n$ PSD matrices a **convex cone** in the space of symmetric matrices. Any non-negative linear combination of PSD matrices is PSD.

This is heavily used in optimization — semidefinite programming (SDP) optimizes a linear objective over this cone, and it is one of the richest and most useful classes of convex optimization problems.

---

## PSD vs PD: The Boundary

PD matrices form an open set in the space of symmetric matrices. You can perturb a PD matrix slightly in any direction and it remains PD (all eigenvalues stay positive under small perturbation).

PSD matrices that are not PD sit on the boundary of this open set. A PSD matrix with a zero eigenvalue is "one perturbation away" from becoming indefinite — perturb it in the wrong direction and a zero eigenvalue becomes negative.

Concretely: $A_\epsilon = A + \epsilon I$ is PD for any $\epsilon > 0$ if $A$ is PSD (since eigenvalues of $A_\epsilon$ are $\lambda_i + \epsilon > 0$). This is called **Tikhonov regularization** — adding $\epsilon I$ to a singular or near-singular PSD matrix to make it invertible. The price is a small perturbation of the solution; the gain is numerical stability.

## PSD in Statistics: Covariance Matrices

Every covariance matrix is PSD. If $X$ is a random vector with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$, the covariance matrix is:

$$\Sigma = \mathbb{E}[(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T]$$

This is PSD because it is an expectation of rank-one PSD matrices $\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T$ (each outer product $(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^T$ satisfies $\mathbf{x}^T \mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T \mathbf{x} = (\mathbf{v}^T \mathbf{x})^2 \geq 0$). Expectations preserve the inequality.

$\Sigma$ is PD iff no component of $\mathbf{X}$ is an exact linear combination of the others — i.e., no perfect collinearity. If some variables are perfectly linearly dependent, $\Sigma$ is PSD but singular.

In principal component analysis (PCA), the covariance matrix $\Sigma$ is diagonalized as $\Sigma = Q\Lambda Q^T$, the eigenvectors give the principal axes of variation, and the eigenvalues give the variance in each direction. Zero eigenvalues correspond to directions of zero variance — flat, degenerate directions in the data.

## The Loewner Order

PSD matrices carry a natural partial order. For two symmetric matrices $A$ and $B$:

$$A \succeq B \quad \iff \quad A - B \text{ is PSD}$$

This is the **Loewner order** (or semidefinite order). It satisfies:

- $A \succeq A$ (reflexive)
- $A \succeq B$ and $B \succeq A$ $\implies$ $A = B$ (antisymmetric)
- $A \succeq B$ and $B \succeq C$ $\implies$ $A \succeq C$ (transitive)

But it is only a partial order — most pairs of symmetric matrices are incomparable.

In this order, $A \succeq 0$ means $A$ is PSD, and $A \succ 0$ means $A$ is PD. The PSD cone is the set of matrices $\succeq 0$ in the Loewner order.

This notation appears constantly in optimization, control theory, and statistics, so it is worth knowing.

## Things to Come Back To

- Semidefinite programming (SDP): optimization over the PSD cone. The feasible set is convex (the PSD cone is convex), and the objective is linear in the matrix variable. Far more expressive than linear programming.
- The matrix square root $A^{1/2}$ and its uniqueness for PSD matrices: among all PSD matrices $B$ with $B^2 = A$, there is exactly one. This is not true for indefinite matrices.
- Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse: for a PSD matrix $A$ with spectral decomposition $A = Q\Lambda Q^T$, the pseudoinverse is $A^+ = Q\Lambda^+ Q^T$ where $\Lambda^+$ replaces each nonzero $\lambda_i$ with $1/\lambda_i$ and leaves zeros as zeros. This gives the minimum-norm least-squares solution.
- Kernel methods in machine learning: kernel matrices are PSD by construction, and the theory of reproducing kernel Hilbert spaces is built entirely on PSD operators.

## Summary

A real symmetric matrix is positive semidefinite when $\mathbf{x}^T A \mathbf{x} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{x}$, with the equality allowed for nonzero $\mathbf{x}$ — specifically for $\mathbf{x} \in \ker(A)$. The five tests from positive definiteness carry over with $>$ replaced by $\geq$, except that test 3 (pivots) becomes delicate when a pivot is zero and elimination stalls. The Gram matrix $A^T A$ is the canonical example and the factorization $A = R^T R$ characterizes PSD completely. The geometry is a degenerate ellipsoid: finite axes in the positive eigenvalue directions, infinite extent in the null space directions. PSD matrices form a convex cone, closed under addition and positive scaling, and the Loewner order $A \succeq B$ generalizes the notion of "larger" to symmetric matrices. The PSD condition is everywhere in applications — covariance matrices, kernel matrices, Hessians at saddle points, and the constraints of semidefinite programs.
