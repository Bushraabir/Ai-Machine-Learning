## What the cross product actually is

Most people first see the cross product as a formula to memorize. That is the wrong way to hold it. The cross product is the unique vector perpendicular to two given vectors whose length encodes the area of the parallelogram they span, and whose direction follows the right-hand rule. The formula is a consequence of that characterization, not the definition.

The cross product only exists in $\mathbb{R}^3$ (and, for different reasons, $\mathbb{R}^7$). It is not a general operation — its existence is a special accident of three dimensions, which becomes clear once you understand it properly via the determinant.

---

## Definition via the determinant

Let $a = (a_1, a_2, a_3)$ and $b = (b_1, b_2, b_3)$ be vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$. Their cross product is defined as the formal expansion:

$$a \times b = \det \begin{pmatrix} e_1 & e_2 & e_3 \ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expand along row 1 by cofactors:

$$a \times b = e_1 \det\begin{pmatrix} a_2 & a_3 \ b_2 & b_3 \end{pmatrix} - e_2 \det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_3 \ b_1 & b_3 \end{pmatrix} + e_3 \det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 \ b_1 & b_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

Written out:

$$a \times b = \begin{pmatrix} a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2 \ a_3 b_1 - a_1 b_3 \ a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1 \end{pmatrix}$$

The entries are the three $2 \times 2$ minors of the $2 \times 3$ matrix $\begin{pmatrix} a \ b \end{pmatrix}$, with alternating signs. The determinant notation is formal — $e_1, e_2, e_3$ are not numbers — but it is the right mnemonic because it encodes the cofactor structure exactly.

---

## The two fundamental properties

Everything about the cross product follows from two facts.

**Property 1 — Perpendicularity.** $a \times b$ is perpendicular to both $a$ and $b$.

_Proof._ The cleaner argument: $(a \times b) \cdot a$ is the scalar triple product $a \cdot (a \times b)$, which equals:

$$\det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \ a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \end{pmatrix} = 0$$

because two rows are identical. The same argument gives $(a \times b) \cdot b = 0$ by putting $b$ in row 1. The repeated-row property of the determinant is doing all the work here.

**Property 2 — Magnitude equals area.**

$$|a \times b| = |a||b|\sin\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $a$ and $b$.

_Proof._ Compute $|a \times b|^2$ directly and expand:

$$|a \times b|^2 = (a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2)^2 + (a_3 b_1 - a_1 b_3)^2 + (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)^2$$

After expanding and regrouping:

$$= (a_1^2 + a_2^2 + a_3^2)(b_1^2 + b_2^2 + b_3^2) - (a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2 + a_3 b_3)^2$$

$$= |a|^2 |b|^2 - (a \cdot b)^2 = |a|^2|b|^2(1 - \cos^2\theta) = |a|^2|b|^2\sin^2\theta$$

The identity $|a \times b|^2 = |a|^2|b|^2 - (a\cdot b)^2$ is the **Lagrange identity**. It is worth knowing on its own — it encodes the relationship between the dot product and the cross product in a single line, and it appeared already in [[Area of a Parallelogram]] when we verified that $|a||b|\sin\theta = |a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1|$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

---

## The right-hand rule

The two properties above determine $a \times b$ up to a sign — there are two unit vectors perpendicular to the plane of $a$ and $b$. The sign convention is fixed by:

$$e_1 \times e_2 = e_3$$

That is, the cross product of the first standard basis vector with the second gives the third, consistent with a right-handed coordinate system. This forces the direction: point the fingers of the right hand from $a$ toward $b$ through the smaller angle, and the thumb points in the direction of $a \times b$.

The cyclic consequences:

$$e_1 \times e_2 = e_3, \qquad e_2 \times e_3 = e_1, \qquad e_3 \times e_1 = e_2$$

$$e_2 \times e_1 = -e_3, \qquad e_3 \times e_2 = -e_1, \qquad e_1 \times e_3 = -e_2$$

Cyclic permutations of $(1,2,3)$ give $+e_k$; anticyclic give $-e_k$. This is the same cyclic structure as the sign of a permutation.

---

## Algebraic properties

**Anticommutativity:** $$b \times a = -(a \times b)$$

_Proof._ Swapping rows 2 and 3 in the formal determinant changes the sign.

**Not associative.** In general, $a \times (b \times c) \neq (a \times b) \times c$. There is no group structure here.

**Bilinearity.** Linear in each argument: $$a \times (b + c) = a \times b + a \times c, \qquad (a + b) \times c = a \times c + b \times c$$ $$(ca) \times b = c(a \times b) = a \times (cb)$$

These follow from linearity of the determinant in each row.

**Self-cross-product:** $$a \times a = 0$$

Two identical rows, determinant is zero. Geometrically: a degenerate parallelogram of zero area.

## The scalar triple product

The **scalar triple product** of $a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}^3$ is:

$$a \cdot (b \times c) = \det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

_Proof._ Compute $b \times c$ explicitly, then dot with $a$:

$$a \cdot (b \times c) = a_1(b_2 c_3 - b_3 c_2) - a_2(b_1 c_3 - b_3 c_1) + a_3(b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1)$$

This is the cofactor expansion of the determinant along row 1. Exact match.

**Geometric meaning.** $|a \cdot (b \times c)|$ is the volume of the parallelepiped spanned by $a$, $b$, $c$. The magnitude of $b \times c$ is the area of the base (the $b$-$c$ parallelogram), and $|a \cdot (b\times c)| / |b \times c|$ is the perpendicular height of $a$ above that base. Volume = base area $\times$ height.

**Cyclic symmetry.** Row swaps change the sign of the determinant, so:

$$a \cdot (b \times c) = b \cdot (c \times a) = c \cdot (a \times b)$$ $$a \cdot (b \times c) = -a \cdot (c \times b)$$

The dot and cross can also be exchanged: $a \cdot (b \times c) = (a \times b) \cdot c$. This is the **mixed product identity**.

## Worked example

$$a = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \ 5 \ 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$a \times b = \det\begin{pmatrix} e_1 & e_2 & e_3 \ 1 & 2 & 3 \ 4 & 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$= e_1\det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 3 \ 5 & 6 \end{pmatrix} - e_2 \det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \ 4 & 6 \end{pmatrix} + e_3\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 4 & 5 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$= e_1(12 - 15) - e_2(6 - 12) + e_3(5 - 8) = \begin{pmatrix} -3 \ 6 \ -3 \end{pmatrix}$$

Verify perpendicularity:

$$(-3)(1) + (6)(2) + (-3)(3) = -3 + 12 - 9 = 0 \checkmark$$ $$(-3)(4) + (6)(5) + (-3)(6) = -12 + 30 - 18 = 0 \checkmark$$

Area of the parallelogram:

$$|a \times b| = \sqrt{9 + 36 + 9} = \sqrt{54} = 3\sqrt{6}$$

Note that $b - a = (3,3,3) = 3(1,1,1)$, so $b$ and $a$ are close to parallel (they nearly lie on the same line through the origin). The small area $3\sqrt{6} \approx 7.35$ for vectors of length $\sqrt{14} \approx 3.74$ and $\sqrt{77} \approx 8.77$ reflects this near-parallelism.

## The BAC-CAB rule

The **vector triple product**:

$$a \times (b \times c) = b(a \cdot c) - c(a \cdot b)$$

The name comes from reading the right side as $\mathbf{B}(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{C}) - \mathbf{C}(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B})$.

_Proof._ Direct component expansion — tedious but straightforward. Worth doing once to believe it.

The geometric content: $b \times c$ is perpendicular to the $b$-$c$ plane, so $a \times (b \times c)$ is perpendicular to $b \times c$, which forces it back into the $b$-$c$ plane. The formula says it is a specific linear combination of $b$ and $c$, with coefficients given by dot products with $a$.

---

## Why the cross product only exists in $\mathbb{R}^3$

In $\mathbb{R}^2$: there is no third dimension for the perpendicular vector to live in. The closest analogue is the scalar $a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1$ — the $2\times 2$ determinant, a signed area, not a vector.

In $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n \geq 4$: given two vectors $a, b$, the subspace perpendicular to both has dimension $n - 2$. For $n = 4$, that is a $2$-dimensional plane — there is no canonical single vector to pick from it. You would need $n-1$ input vectors to produce one output vector via the determinant.

In $\mathbb{R}^3$ specifically: the perpendicular complement of two linearly independent vectors is exactly $1$-dimensional. That is why the cross product is a map $\mathbb{R}^3 \times \mathbb{R}^3 \to \mathbb{R}^3$.

The correct generalization is the **exterior product** $a \wedge b \in \bigwedge^2 \mathbb{R}^n$. This space has dimension $\binom{n}{2}$. In $\mathbb{R}^3$, $\binom{3}{2} = 3$, so $\bigwedge^2\mathbb{R}^3 \cong \mathbb{R}^3$ as vector spaces, and the cross product is the identification between them via the metric (the Hodge star $\star$). In $\mathbb{R}^4$, $\binom{4}{2} = 6 \neq 4$, and the identification breaks down completely. Exterior algebra is the right language for all of this — the cross product is an artifact of the coincidence $\binom{3}{2} = 3$.

## Things to think about later

- [ ] Prove BAC-CAB by components. Then find a cleaner proof using the scalar triple product identity.
- [ ] The cross product is related to a skew-symmetric matrix: $a \times b = [a]_\times b$ where $[a]_\times$ is the $3\times 3$ skew-symmetric matrix with entries from $a$. Write this out and verify. This is important in robotics and rigid body mechanics.
- [ ] The cross product as a Lie bracket: $\mathbb{R}^3$ with the cross product is a Lie algebra. Which one? (It is $\mathfrak{so}(3)$, the Lie algebra of the rotation group.)
- [ ] Read about the Hodge star operator and how $a \times b = \star(a \wedge b)$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$. This is the clean story.
- [ ] In physics, torque $\tau = r \times F$ and angular momentum $L = r \times p$ are **pseudovectors** — they transform differently from ordinary vectors under reflections (they pick up an extra sign). This is because the cross product involves a determinant, which changes sign under orientation-reversing transformations. The determinant encodes orientation, so the cross product does too.

## Summary

$a \times b$ is defined by the formal cofactor expansion of a $3\times 3$ determinant with $e_1, e_2, e_3$ in row 1 and $a, b$ in rows 2 and 3. Perpendicularity follows from the repeated-row property of the determinant: $(a \times b) \cdot a = \det[\text{matrix with two equal rows}] = 0$. The magnitude $|a \times b| = |a||b|\sin\theta$ follows from the Lagrange identity. The scalar triple product $a \cdot (b \times c) = \det[a; b; c]$ gives the volume of the parallelepiped. The cross product exists only in $\mathbb{R}^3$ because only there is $\binom{n}{2} = n$, which is the coincidence that makes $\bigwedge^2\mathbb{R}^3 \cong \mathbb{R}^3$.
