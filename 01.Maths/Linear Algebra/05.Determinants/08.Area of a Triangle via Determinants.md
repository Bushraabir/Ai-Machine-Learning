## Why this belongs in a linear algebra course

At first glance, "area of a triangle" sounds like it belongs in a calculus or geometry course. But it is one of the most direct illustrations of what a determinant _actually measures_ geometrically. The $2 \times 2$ determinant is a signed area. Everything else — the triangle formula, the parallelogram interpretation, the higher-dimensional volume formulas — flows from taking that seriously.

This note is about making that connection rigorous, not just quoting the formula.

---

## The parallelogram first

Before triangles, fix the foundational fact.

Let $u, v \in \mathbb{R}^2$. The **signed area** of the parallelogram spanned by $u$ and $v$ is:

$$\text{signed area} = \det \begin{pmatrix} u_1 & v_1 \ u_2 & v_2 \end{pmatrix} = u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1$$

The actual (unsigned) area is the absolute value:

$$\text{area of parallelogram} = |u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1|$$

**Why is this true?** The three properties of the determinant force it:

1. The area of the unit square (columns $e_1, e_2$) is 1. Normalization gives $\det I = 1$. Check.
2. Scaling one side by $c$ scales the area by $c$. Multilinearity in each column. Check.
3. Shearing a parallelogram (adding a multiple of one side to the other) does not change its area. The alternating property ($\det$ changes sign when columns swap, so adding a multiple of one column to another leaves $\det$ unchanged) captures this. Check.

These three properties characterize area, and they are exactly the three properties that characterize the determinant. So the determinant _is_ the signed area — not analogous to it, not proportional to it. Equal to it.

The sign encodes orientation: positive if $v$ is counterclockwise from $u$, negative if clockwise.

---

## From parallelogram to triangle — the origin case

A triangle with one vertex at the origin and the other two at $P = (p_1, p_2)$ and $Q = (q_1, q_2)$ is exactly _half_ the parallelogram spanned by $P$ and $Q$:

$$\text{area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2} \left| \det \begin{pmatrix} p_1 & q_1 \ p_2 & q_2 \end{pmatrix} \right| = \frac{1}{2} |p_1 q_2 - p_2 q_1|$$

The factor of $\frac{1}{2}$ is because the diagonal of the parallelogram cuts it into two congruent triangles.

## The general case — three arbitrary vertices

Now let the triangle have vertices at $A = (a_1, a_2)$, $B = (b_1, b_2)$, $C = (c_1, c_2)$, none necessarily at the origin.

Translate so that $A$ is at the origin: replace $B$ with $B - A$ and $C$ with $C - A$. Translation does not change area.

$$\text{area} = \frac{1}{2} \left| \det \begin{pmatrix} b_1 - a_1 & c_1 - a_1 \ b_2 - a_2 & c_2 - a_2 \end{pmatrix} \right|$$

Expand the determinant:

$$= \frac{1}{2} |(b_1 - a_1)(c_2 - a_2) - (c_1 - a_1)(b_2 - a_2)|$$

This is correct but a little opaque. There is a cleaner way to write it using a $3 \times 3$ determinant.

---

## The $3 \times 3$ formula — the elegant version

$$\text{area of triangle with vertices } A, B, C = \frac{1}{2} \left| \det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & 1 \ b_1 & b_2 & 1 \ c_1 & c_2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \right|$$

**Proof.** Expand along column 3 (the column of 1s) using cofactors. The cofactors of the third column are:

$$C_{13} = +\det\begin{pmatrix} b_1 & b_2 \ c_1 & c_2 \end{pmatrix}, \quad C_{23} = -\det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 \ c_1 & c_2 \end{pmatrix}, \quad C_{33} = +\det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 \ b_1 & b_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

So:

$$\det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & 1 \ b_1 & b_2 & 1 \ c_1 & c_2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = (b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1) - (a_1 c_2 - a_2 c_1) + (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)$$

Now check this equals the expanded $2 \times 2$ translation formula. Expand $(b_1 - a_1)(c_2 - a_2) - (c_1 - a_1)(b_2 - a_2)$:

$$= b_1 c_2 - b_1 a_2 - a_1 c_2 + a_1 a_2 - c_1 b_2 + c_1 a_2 + a_1 b_2 - a_1 a_2$$

$$= (b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1) - (a_1 c_2 - a_2 c_1) + (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)$$

Identical. So the $3 \times 3$ formula is precisely the translated $2 \times 2$ formula, just written more symmetrically. The third column of 1s is encoding the translation back to the origin without needing to do it explicitly.

**Alternative proof via row operations.** Subtract row 1 from rows 2 and 3:

$$\det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & 1 \ b_1 & b_2 & 1 \ c_1 & c_2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = \det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & 1 \ b_1 - a_1 & b_2 - a_2 & 0 \ c_1 - a_1 & c_2 - a_2 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expand along column 3: only the $(1,3)$ entry is nonzero, contributing $1 \cdot C_{13}$:

$$= (+1) \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} b_1 - a_1 & b_2 - a_2 \ c_1 - a_1 & c_2 - a_2 \end{pmatrix}$$

which is exactly the translated $2 \times 2$ determinant. Clean.

## Worked example

Find the area of the triangle with vertices $A = (1, 0)$, $B = (4, 3)$, $C = (2, 5)$.

$$\det \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \ 4 & 3 & 1 \ 2 & 5 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Subtract row 1 from rows 2 and 3:

$$= \det \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 1 \ 3 & 3 & 0 \ 1 & 5 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expand along column 3 (only the $(1,3)$ entry survives):

$$= 1 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 3 \ 1 & 5 \end{pmatrix} = (15 - 3) = 12$$

$$\text{area} = \frac{1}{2} |12| = 6$$

## The signed area and orientation

Without the absolute value:

$$\text{signed area} = \frac{1}{2} \det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & 1 \ b_1 & b_2 & 1 \ c_1 & c_2 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

- **Positive** if $A \to B \to C$ is traversed counterclockwise.
- **Negative** if $A \to B \to C$ is traversed clockwise.
- **Zero** if $A$, $B$, $C$ are collinear.

The collinearity condition is worth isolating:

$$A, B, C \text{ are collinear} \iff \det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & 1 \ b_1 & b_2 & 1 \ c_1 & c_2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = 0$$

This makes sense — three points are collinear iff the matrix whose rows are their homogeneous coordinates is singular. The determinant detects exactly this.

## Extension to $\mathbb{R}^3$ — triangle in space

If the three vertices live in $\mathbb{R}^3$, say $A, B, C \in \mathbb{R}^3$, the formula changes because we can no longer use the $2 \times 2$ determinant directly. Instead, use the cross product.

Form the edge vectors:

$$u = B - A, \qquad v = C - A$$

Then:

$$\text{area of triangle} = \frac{1}{2} |u \times v|$$

where $u \times v$ is the cross product. The cross product itself is computed as:

$$u \times v = \det \begin{pmatrix} e_1 & e_2 & e_3 \ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

(expanding along row 1, treating $e_1, e_2, e_3$ as formal unit vectors). The magnitude $|u \times v|$ is the area of the parallelogram spanned by $u$ and $v$ in $\mathbb{R}^3$, and half of that is the triangle area.

This is the natural generalization: in $\mathbb{R}^2$, the $2 \times 2$ determinant gives signed area directly; in $\mathbb{R}^3$, we need the cross product (itself defined via a $3 \times 3$ determinant) and then take the norm.

---

## Why the column of 1s? — the projective geometry view

The $3 \times 3$ formula with the column of 1s has a deeper explanation. In **homogeneous coordinates**, the point $(x, y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$ is represented as $(x, y, 1) \in \mathbb{R}^3$ (on the plane $z = 1$ in projective space). The $3 \times 3$ determinant of three such points is an affine invariant — it is preserved under translations and linear maps that fix the $z = 1$ plane. This is exactly why the formula works for _any_ triangle placement without having to translate to the origin by hand.

I do not need the full projective geometry machinery here, but it is worth knowing that the "1" column is not an algebraic trick — it is encoding the affine structure of the plane.

## Things to think about later

- [ ] Prove directly from the three determinant properties (multilinearity, alternating, normalization) that the $2 \times 2$ determinant equals the signed area of the spanned parallelogram. I claimed this but only argued it heuristically above.
- [ ] The collinearity condition $\det = 0$ can be used to find the equation of a line through two points. Work this out: if $A = (a_1, a_2)$ and $B = (b_1, b_2)$, then the line through them is ${(x,y) : \det \begin{pmatrix} x & y & 1 \ a_1 & a_2 & 1 \ b_1 & b_2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} = 0}$. Verify this gives the standard line equation.
- [ ] What is the $n$-dimensional analogue? The volume of an $n$-simplex (generalization of a triangle/tetrahedron) with vertices $v_0, v_1, \ldots, v_n \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is $\frac{1}{n!} |\det(v_1 - v_0 \mid v_2 - v_0 \mid \cdots \mid v_n - v_0)|$. The $\frac{1}{2}$ for triangles is $\frac{1}{2!}$; for tetrahedra it becomes $\frac{1}{3!} = \frac{1}{6}$.

## Summary

$$\text{area of triangle with vertices } A, B, C \in \mathbb{R}^2 = \frac{1}{2} \left| \det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & 1 \ b_1 & b_2 & 1 \ c_1 & c_2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \right|$$

The $3 \times 3$ determinant equals twice the area because: subtract row 1 from rows 2 and 3, expand along the third column, and recover the $2 \times 2$ determinant of the translated edge vectors — which is the parallelogram area. The $\frac{1}{2}$ converts parallelogram to triangle. The column of 1s encodes translation invariance. The sign of the determinant encodes orientation; zero determinant means collinearity.
