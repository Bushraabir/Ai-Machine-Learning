## Two triple products — do not confuse them

Given three vectors $a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}^3$, there are two natural products involving all three:

The **scalar triple product**: $a \cdot (b \times c)$ — a scalar.

The **vector triple product**: $a \times (b \times c)$ — a vector.

This note is about the scalar triple product. The vector triple product has its own identity (the BAC-CAB rule: $a \times (b \times c) = b(a \cdot c) - c(a \cdot b)$) and lives in a different part of the story. Keep them separate.

---

## The scalar triple product equals a determinant

$$a \cdot (b \times c) = \det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Proof.** From the cross product note:

$$b \times c = \begin{pmatrix} b_2 c_3 - b_3 c_2 \ b_3 c_1 - b_1 c_3 \ b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Take the dot product with $a$:

$$a \cdot (b \times c) = a_1(b_2 c_3 - b_3 c_2) + a_2(b_3 c_1 - b_1 c_3) + a_3(b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1)$$

This is exactly the cofactor expansion of $\det[a; b; c]$ along row 1 — each term $a_i$ multiplied by its cofactor $C_{1i}$, which is $(-1)^{1+i}$ times the corresponding $2 \times 2$ minor from rows 2 and 3. Check term by term:

- $a_1 \cdot (+1) \cdot (b_2 c_3 - b_3 c_2)$: cofactor $C_{11}$, sign $(-1)^{1+1} = +1$. Matches.
- $a_2 \cdot (-1) \cdot (b_1 c_3 - b_3 c_1)$: cofactor $C_{12}$, sign $(-1)^{1+2} = -1$. And $-(b_3 c_1 - b_1 c_3) = -(b_3 c_1 - b_1 c_3)$. Matches since $b_3 c_1 - b_1 c_3 = -(b_1 c_3 - b_3 c_1)$.
- $a_3 \cdot (+1) \cdot (b_1 c_2 - b_2 c_1)$: cofactor $C_{13}$, sign $+1$. Matches.

So $a \cdot (b \times c) = \det[a; b; c]$.

## Volume of the parallelepiped

The three vectors $a, b, c$ span a **parallelepiped** — the solid with sides along $a$, $b$, $c$ and all translates thereof. Its eight vertices are ${0, a, b, c, a+b, a+c, b+c, a+b+c}$.

**Theorem.**

$$\text{Volume of parallelepiped} = |a \cdot (b \times c)| = \left|\det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{pmatrix}\right|$$

**Proof.** Use the base-times-height decomposition.

Take the parallelogram spanned by $b$ and $c$ as the base. Its area is $|b \times c|$ (from the cross product note).

The height is the perpendicular distance from $a$ to the plane spanned by $b$ and $c$. The unit normal to that plane is $\hat{n} = (b \times c)/|b \times c|$. The height is:

$$h = |a \cdot \hat{n}| = \left|a \cdot \frac{b \times c}{|b \times c|}\right| = \frac{|a \cdot (b \times c)|}{|b \times c|}$$

Therefore:

$$\text{Volume} = \text{base} \times \text{height} = |b \times c| \cdot \frac{|a \cdot (b \times c)|}{|b \times c|} = |a \cdot (b \times c)|$$

And since $a \cdot (b \times c) = \det[a; b; c]$, the volume is $|\det[a; b; c]|$.

The signed version $a \cdot (b \times c) = \det[a; b; c]$ carries orientation: positive if $(a, b, c)$ is a right-handed triple, negative if left-handed.

## Worked example

$$a = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad b = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 2 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad c = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 0 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} = 1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 = 6$$

This is a rectangular box with side lengths 1, 2, 3 — volume $= 6$. Correct.

---

## A less trivial example

$$a = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 0 \end{pmatrix}, \quad b = \begin{pmatrix} 0 \ 1 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad c = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 0 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 3 \ 2 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expand along row 1:

$$= 1 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix} - 2 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 3 \ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix} + 0$$

$$= 1(1 - 0) - 2(0 - 6) = 1 + 12 = 13$$

Volume $= 13$.

## Cyclic symmetry

The scalar triple product is _cyclic_: you can rotate the three vectors through the product without changing the value:

$$a \cdot (b \times c) = b \cdot (c \times a) = c \cdot (a \times b)$$

**Why.** Each of these equals $\det[a; b; c]$ — just with rows permuted by a cyclic permutation $(123) \to (231) \to (312)$. A cyclic permutation of three elements is an _even_ permutation (it is the composition of two transpositions), so the determinant is unchanged.

Swapping any two vectors changes the sign:

$$a \cdot (b \times c) = -b \cdot (a \times c) = -a \cdot (c \times b)$$

These are odd permutations, so the determinant changes sign.

Also note: dot and cross can be swapped without changing the value:

$$a \cdot (b \times c) = (a \times b) \cdot c$$

**Proof.** Both equal $\det[a; b; c]$. The left side expands along row 1 (the $a$-row); the right side expands along row 3 (the $c$-row). Both give the same determinant.

## When the volume is zero — linear dependence

$$\det[a; b; c] = 0 \iff \text{volume} = 0 \iff a, b, c \text{ are linearly dependent}$$

If the three vectors lie in a plane (or a line, or one is zero), the parallelepiped collapses — it has no thickness, hence zero volume. This is the same as saying the matrix with rows $a, b, c$ is singular.

This is another instance of the central equivalence: zero determinant, singular matrix, linearly dependent rows/columns, and degenerate geometry are all the same condition expressed in different languages.

## The scalar triple product and the adjugate

Recall from the cofactors note: $A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} \operatorname{adj}(A)$.

The columns of $\operatorname{adj}(A)$ are built from cross products of pairs of rows of $A$. Specifically, the $j$-th column of $\operatorname{adj}(A)$ is proportional to the cross product of the two rows of $A$ that are _not_ row $j$. (This is exactly what the $2\times 2$ minors in the cofactor matrix compute.)

So the adjugate inverse formula, Cramer's rule, the cross product, and the scalar triple product are all manifestations of the same underlying determinant structure. None of them are separate topics.

## Linear transformations scale volume by $|\det A|$

In the area note, we established that a linear map $T(x) = Ax$ scales areas in $\mathbb{R}^2$ by $|\det A|$. The same holds in $\mathbb{R}^3$ for volumes.

**Theorem.** If $S \subset \mathbb{R}^3$ has volume $V$, then $T(S) = {Ax : x \in S}$ has volume $|\det A| \cdot V$.

**Proof for parallelepipeds.** The parallelepiped spanned by $a, b, c$ has volume $|\det[a; b; c]|$. Under $T$, it maps to the parallelepiped spanned by $Aa, Ab, Ac$, which has volume:

$$|\det[Aa; Ab; Ac]| = |\det(A[a; b; c])| = |\det A| \cdot |\det[a; b; c]|$$

using $\det(AB) = \det A \det B$.

This is why $|\det A|$ is the volume scaling factor of the linear map $A$. Special cases:

- $|\det A| = 1$: volume-preserving (rotations, reflections, shears with unit determinant).
- $|\det A| = 0$: the map collapses $\mathbb{R}^3$ into a plane or lower — all volumes become zero.
- $|\det A| = k$: all volumes scale by $k$.

## The Jacobian in 3D integration

The 3D change of variables formula: if $x = g(u)$ is a differentiable bijection from region $D$ to $g(D)$,

$$\iiint_{g(D)} f(x) , dV = \iiint_D f(g(u)) \left|\det \frac{\partial g}{\partial u}\right| , du$$

The factor $|\det \partial g / \partial u|$ is the Jacobian. It is the volume scaling factor of the linearization $dg_u$ at each point $u$ — exactly the volume theorem above, applied infinitesimally.

This is the bridge between 18.06 and multivariable calculus. When you change variables in a triple integral, the $|J|$ factor is just the absolute value of the $3 \times 3$ determinant of partial derivatives. Its geometric meaning is: how much does the map locally stretch or compress volumes?

## Things to think about later

- [ ] Prove the cyclic symmetry $a \cdot (b \times c) = b \cdot (c \times a)$ directly (without appealing to the determinant) by showing both sides compute the same signed volume.
- [ ] The vector triple product: $(a \times b) \times c = (a \cdot c)b - (b \cdot c)a$. Prove by direct expansion. The geometric content: $(a \times b) \times c$ lies in the plane spanned by $a$ and $b$ (since it is a linear combination of $a$ and $b$). Why is this geometrically obvious?
- [ ] Generalize: the volume of an $n$-dimensional parallelepiped spanned by $n$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is $|\det[v_1; v_2; \ldots; v_n]|$. The same proof by uniqueness of the determinant works verbatim. The Jacobian in $n$ dimensions is an $n \times n$ determinant.

## Summary

$$\text{Volume} = |a \cdot (b \times c)| = \left|\det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & a_2 & a_3 \ b_1 & b_2 & b_3 \ c_1 & c_2 & c_3 \end{pmatrix}\right|$$

The scalar triple product equals the $3 \times 3$ determinant with $a, b, c$ as rows. Proof: the dot product with $b \times c$ is the cofactor expansion along row 1. The base-times-height argument gives volume $= |a \cdot (b \times c)|$. The triple product is cyclic (even permutations preserve sign), antisymmetric (odd permutations flip sign), and zero exactly when the three vectors are linearly dependent. Linear maps scale volume by $|\det A|$ — this is the geometric content of the $3 \times 3$ determinant and the source of the Jacobian in triple integrals.
