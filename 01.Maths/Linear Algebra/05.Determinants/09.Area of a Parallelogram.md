
## Why this belongs in a linear algebra course

At first this feels like it should be in a geometry or calculus course. But the connection runs deep: the determinant was _defined_ algebraically (three properties, or the permutation formula), and yet it turns out to compute area. That is not a coincidence — it is the theorem. Understanding why $|\det A|$ equals area is understanding what the determinant _is_ geometrically.

This note builds the result from scratch, checks it against what we know, and then pushes into the $3 \times 3$ case and linear transformations.

---

## Setup — the parallelogram spanned by two vectors

Let $a = \begin{pmatrix} a_1 \ a_2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $b = \begin{pmatrix} b_1 \ b_2 \end{pmatrix}$ be two vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$.

The **parallelogram** spanned by $a$ and $b$ is the set

$${s , a + t , b : 0 \leq s, t \leq 1}$$

Its vertices are $0$, $a$, $b$, and $a + b$.

$$\text{Area} = |a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1| = \left|\det \begin{pmatrix} a_1 & b_1 \ a_2 & b_2 \end{pmatrix}\right|$$

The matrix here has $a$ and $b$ as its _columns_. The claim is that the absolute value of its determinant equals the area. Let me prove this from the properties of area, not from a formula.

---

## Proof from first principles

Treat area as a function $f(a, b)$ — the area of the parallelogram with sides $a$ and $b$. What properties must $f$ satisfy?

**Property 1 — Multilinearity.** If you scale one side by $c$, the area scales by $|c|$: $$f(ca, b) = |c| , f(a, b)$$

More carefully: the _signed_ area $D(a, b) = a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1$ is linear in each argument separately (this follows from how the formula depends on the entries). Unsigned area is $|D(a,b)|$.

**Property 2 — Antisymmetry.** Swapping the two sides changes the sign of $D$: $$D(b, a) = b_1 a_2 - b_2 a_1 = -D(a, b)$$

The unsigned area doesn't change (the parallelogram is the same), but the _orientation_ flips.

**Property 3 — Normalization.** The unit square has area 1: $$D(e_1, e_2) = \det I = 1$$

Now: $D(a, b)$ is a function of two vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ that is linear in each argument, antisymmetric, and equals 1 on the standard basis. These are exactly the three defining properties of the $2 \times 2$ determinant with columns $a, b$. By uniqueness of the determinant (Strang Ch. 5), $D(a,b) = \det[a \mid b]$.

Therefore: **the signed area of the parallelogram spanned by $a$ and $b$ equals $\det[a \mid b]$, and the unsigned area equals $|\det[a \mid b]|$.**

---

## Verification — geometric formula agrees

The classical formula for the area of a parallelogram is base times height.

Take $a$ as the base. Its length is $|a| = \sqrt{a_1^2 + a_2^2}$.

The height is the component of $b$ perpendicular to $a$:

$$h = |b| \sin\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $a$ and $b$. So:

$$\text{Area} = |a| \cdot |b| \sin\theta$$

Now verify this equals $|a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1|$. We know:

$$|a|^2 |b|^2 = (a_1^2 + a_2^2)(b_1^2 + b_2^2)$$

$$|a|^2 |b|^2 \sin^2\theta = |a|^2|b|^2(1 - \cos^2\theta) = |a|^2|b|^2 - (a \cdot b)^2$$

Expand:

$$(a_1^2 + a_2^2)(b_1^2 + b_2^2) - (a_1 b_1 + a_2 b_2)^2$$

$$= a_1^2 b_2^2 - 2a_1 b_1 a_2 b_2 + a_2^2 b_1^2 = (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)^2$$

So $|a||b|\sin\theta = |a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1|$. The two formulas agree exactly. Good — the determinant formula is not new geometry, it is the same geometry written algebraically.

## Worked example — $2 \times 2$

$$a = \begin{pmatrix} 3 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix} = (3)(4) - (1)(1) = 11$$

Area $= 11$.

Check geometrically: $|a| = \sqrt{10}$, $|b| = \sqrt{17}$, $a \cdot b = 7$.

$$\text{Area} = \sqrt{|a|^2|b|^2 - (a\cdot b)^2} = \sqrt{10 \cdot 17 - 49} = \sqrt{170 - 49} = \sqrt{121} = 11$$

Consistent.

## The signed area and orientation

The _signed_ area $\det[a \mid b]$ carries orientation information.

- $\det[a \mid b] > 0$: $b$ is counterclockwise from $a$. The ordered pair $(a, b)$ is a positively oriented basis.
- $\det[a \mid b] < 0$: $b$ is clockwise from $a$. The pair is negatively oriented.
- $\det[a \mid b] = 0$: $a$ and $b$ are parallel (collinear). The parallelogram is degenerate — it collapses to a line segment and has zero area.

The sign is a global property of orientation: the right-hand rule in $\mathbb{R}^2$. When we later talk about change of variables in integration, it is the signed determinant (the Jacobian) that appears in the formula, not the unsigned area. The sign encodes whether the transformation preserves or reverses orientation.

## Extension to $\mathbb{R}^3$ — parallelogram in 3-space

Now $a, b \in \mathbb{R}^3$. The parallelogram spanned by them lives in a plane in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

The area formula comes from the cross product:

$$\text{Area} = |a \times b|$$

where

$$a \times b = \begin{pmatrix} a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2 \ a_3 b_1 - a_1 b_3 \ a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Notice: each component of $a \times b$ is a $2 \times 2$ determinant — a minor of the matrix $[a \mid b]$.

This is not a coincidence. The cross product is the mechanism by which the $3 \times 3$ determinant encodes area in a subspace. Specifically:

$$|a \times b|^2 = (a_2 b_3 - a_3 b_2)^2 + (a_3 b_1 - a_1 b_3)^2 + (a_1 b_2 - a_2 b_1)^2$$

This equals $|a|^2|b|^2 - (a \cdot b)^2 = |a|^2|b|^2\sin^2\theta$ by the same Lagrange identity as before. So the formula $|a \times b| = |a||b|\sin\theta$ holds, consistent with geometry.

## Extension to $\mathbb{R}^3$ — parallelepiped and volume

Now take _three_ vectors $a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}^3$. They span a **parallelepiped** — the 3-dimensional analogue of the parallelogram.

$$\text{Volume} = \left|\det\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & b_1 & c_1 \ a_2 & b_2 & c_2 \ a_3 & b_3 & c_3 \end{pmatrix}\right|$$

Proof structure: exactly the same as the $2 \times 2$ case. A function of three vectors that is multilinear in each argument, alternating (changes sign under column swap), and equals 1 on ${e_1, e_2, e_3}$ must be the $3 \times 3$ determinant. The volume satisfies all three properties. Uniqueness does the rest.

Alternatively, compute directly: the volume equals base area times height.

$$\text{Volume} = |a \times b| \cdot h$$

where $h$ is the component of $c$ along the direction perpendicular to the $a$-$b$ plane, i.e., along $\hat{n} = (a \times b)/|a \times b|$:

$$h = c \cdot \frac{a \times b}{|a \times b|}$$

So:

$$\text{Volume} = |a \times b| \cdot \frac{|c \cdot (a \times b)|}{|a \times b|} = |c \cdot (a \times b)|$$

The scalar triple product $c \cdot (a \times b)$ is exactly $\det[a \mid b \mid c]$ (expand and check). This gives the volume formula directly.

## How linear transformations scale area

This is the theorem that makes determinants central to multivariable calculus.

Let $T : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}^2$ be the linear map $T(x) = Ax$. Let $S$ be any region in $\mathbb{R}^2$ with area $|S|$.

**Theorem.** The area of $T(S)$ is $|\det A| \cdot |S|$.

**Proof sketch.** It suffices to check for parallelograms — any region can be approximated by small parallelograms (this is the content of the change of variables theorem in integration). A parallelogram spanned by $u$ and $v$ has area $|\det[u \mid v]|$. Its image under $T$ is spanned by $Au$ and $Av$, which has area:

$$|\det[Au \mid Av]| = |\det(A[u \mid v])| = |\det A| \cdot |\det[u \mid v]|$$

using the multiplicativity of the determinant, $\det(AB) = \det A \det B$.

So $|\det A|$ is the _area scaling factor_ of the linear map $A$. This is the geometric content of the determinant.

Special cases:

- $|\det A| = 1$: the map preserves area (e.g., rotations, reflections).
- $|\det A| = 0$: the map collapses the plane into a line or a point — all areas become 0.
- $|\det A| = 2$: the map doubles all areas.

In $\mathbb{R}^3$, the same statement holds with "area" replaced by "volume."

## The change of variables formula — why this matters beyond 18.06

In multivariable calculus, if you change variables $x = g(u)$ in an integral over a region $D$:

$$\iint_{g(D)} f(x) , dA = \iint_D f(g(u)) \left|\det \frac{\partial g}{\partial u}\right| , du$$

The factor $|\det \partial g / \partial u|$ is the Jacobian determinant. It measures how much the nonlinear map $g$ locally scales area near each point $u$. This is exactly the linear-transformation area-scaling theorem applied infinitesimally.

So Cramer's rule, the adjugate, and the change-of-variables formula in calculus all trace back to the same object: the determinant as a signed volume. Linear algebra and multivariable calculus are not separate subjects here — they are the same idea at different scales.

## A concrete failure case — zero area

$$a = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 1 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 4 \ 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 4 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = 4 - 4 = 0$$

$b = 2a$, so the vectors are parallel. The "parallelogram" degenerates to a line segment. Area is 0. The matrix $[a \mid b]$ is singular — its columns are linearly dependent. The determinant being zero simultaneously means: singular matrix, linearly dependent columns, degenerate parallelogram, zero area. These are all the same fact.

## Summary

The area of the parallelogram spanned by columns $a$ and $b$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is $|\det[a \mid b]|$. The proof is that signed area satisfies the three defining properties of the determinant, and the determinant is unique. In $\mathbb{R}^3$, two vectors give area via the cross product (whose components are $2\times 2$ minors), and three vectors give volume via $|\det[a \mid b \mid c]|$. The key theorem: a linear map $A$ scales all areas by $|\det A|$, which is why the Jacobian appears in the change of variables formula.
