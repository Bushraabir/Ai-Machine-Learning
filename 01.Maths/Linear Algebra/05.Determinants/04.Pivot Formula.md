## The Central Idea

We now have two formulas for the determinant — the permutation formula and the cofactor expansion. Both are correct, both are theoretically fundamental, and both are computationally expensive for large $n$. The permutation formula has $n!$ terms. The cofactor expansion is recursive and still requires exponential work without care.

The pivot formula is the third formula, and it is the one you actually use to compute determinants efficiently. It says:

$$\det(A) = (-1)^{\text{number of row swaps}} \times d_1 \times d_2 \times \cdots \times d_n$$

where $d_1, d_2, \ldots, d_n$ are the **pivots** produced by Gaussian elimination — with row exchanges permitted (partial pivoting), but no scaling of rows. The sign factor accounts for the row exchanges.

This is $O(n^3)$ computation, the same cost as solving $Ax = b$. In practice, computing $\det(A)$ via elimination and reading off the pivots is the only sensible approach for $n \geq 4$.

---

## Background: What Elimination Does to the Determinant

Recall the three elementary row operations and their effect on $\det$:

**Row swap:** Exchange rows $i$ and $j$. $$\det \to -\det$$ Sign flips. (Property 2.)

**Row scaling:** Multiply row $i$ by a scalar $c$. $$\det \to c \cdot \det$$ (Property 3, linearity in one row.)

**Row addition:** Add $c$ times row $j$ to row $i$. $$\det \to \det$$ Unchanged. (Property 5, derived from linearity and equal-row zero.)

Gaussian elimination uses only row additions (subtracting multiples of pivot rows from rows below) and, when necessary, row swaps to bring a nonzero entry into the pivot position. It never scales rows. Therefore:

- Each row addition: determinant unchanged.
- Each row swap: determinant changes sign.

After elimination, $A$ has been reduced to an upper triangular matrix $U$ (or $LU$ more precisely). The determinant of an upper triangular matrix is the product of its diagonal entries — its pivots.

Therefore:

$$\det(A) = (-1)^s \cdot d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n$$

where $s$ is the number of row swaps performed and $d_1, \ldots, d_n$ are the pivots of $U$.

---

## The $LU$ and $PA = LU$ Factorizations

Without row swaps, Gaussian elimination produces $A = LU$ where:

- $L$ is unit lower triangular (ones on the diagonal, multipliers $\ell_{ij}$ below).
- $U$ is upper triangular with the pivots $d_1, \ldots, d_n$ on the diagonal.

$$\det(A) = \det(L) \cdot \det(U) = 1 \cdot d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n$$

since $\det(L) = 1$ (product of diagonal entries of $L$, all of which are 1).

With row swaps, elimination produces $PA = LU$ where $P$ is a permutation matrix recording the row exchanges. Then:

$$\det(P) \cdot \det(A) = \det(L) \cdot \det(U) = d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n$$

$$\det(A) = \frac{d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n}{\det(P)} = \det(P)^{-1} \cdot d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n$$

Since $P$ is a permutation matrix, $\det(P) = \pm 1$, so $\det(P)^{-1} = \det(P)$. Writing $s$ for the number of row swaps in $P$:

$$\boxed{\det(A) = (-1)^s \cdot d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n}$$

## A Simple $2 \times 2$ Derivation

Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}a & b \ c & d\end{pmatrix}$$

Assume $a \neq 0$. Eliminate:

$$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - \frac{c}{a} R_1: \qquad \begin{pmatrix}a & b \ 0 & d - \frac{bc}{a}\end{pmatrix}$$

No row swaps, $s = 0$. Pivots: $d_1 = a$, $d_2 = d - bc/a$.

$$\det(A) = a \cdot \left(d - \frac{bc}{a}\right) = ad - bc$$

This agrees exactly with the $2 \times 2$ formula. The pivot formula and the permutation formula give the same answer — as they must.

## A $3 \times 3$ Example Without Row Swaps

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 & 1 \ 4 & 3 & 3 \ 8 & 7 & 9\end{pmatrix}$$

**Eliminate column 1.**

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$ (multiplier $\ell_{21} = 2$):

$$\begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 8 & 7 & 9\end{pmatrix}$$

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 4R_1$ (multiplier $\ell_{31} = 4$):

$$\begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 3 & 5\end{pmatrix}$$

**Eliminate column 2.**

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 3R_2$ (multiplier $\ell_{32} = 3$):

$$U = \begin{pmatrix}2 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2\end{pmatrix}$$

No row swaps, $s = 0$. Pivots: $d_1 = 2$, $d_2 = 1$, $d_3 = 2$.

$$\det(A) = (-1)^0 \cdot 2 \cdot 1 \cdot 2 = 4$$

**Verify by cofactor expansion along row 1:**

$$\det(A) = 2\det\begin{pmatrix}3&3\7&9\end{pmatrix} - 1\det\begin{pmatrix}4&3\8&9\end{pmatrix} + 1\det\begin{pmatrix}4&3\8&7\end{pmatrix}$$ $$= 2(27-21) - 1(36-24) + 1(28-24) = 12 - 12 + 4 = 4 \checkmark$$

## A $3 \times 3$ Example With a Row Swap

$$A = \begin{pmatrix}0 & 2 & 1 \ 3 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 2 & 4\end{pmatrix}$$

The $(1,1)$ entry is zero — we cannot use it as a pivot. Swap rows 1 and 2:

$$\xrightarrow{\text{swap } R_1 \leftrightarrow R_2} \begin{pmatrix}3 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 1 \ 1 & 2 & 4\end{pmatrix}$$

One row swap so far: $s = 1$.

**Eliminate column 1.**

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - \frac{1}{3}R_1$:

$$\begin{pmatrix}3 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 5/3 & 4\end{pmatrix}$$

**Eliminate column 2.**

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - \frac{5/3}{2}R_2 = R_3 - \frac{5}{6}R_2$:

$$U = \begin{pmatrix}3 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 4 - 5/6\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}3 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 19/6\end{pmatrix}$$

One row swap, $s = 1$. Pivots: $d_1 = 3$, $d_2 = 2$, $d_3 = 19/6$.

$$\det(A) = (-1)^1 \cdot 3 \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{19}{6} = -1 \cdot 19 = -19$$

**Verify by cofactor expansion along row 1 of the original $A$:**

$$\det(A) = 0 \cdot C_{11} + 2 \cdot C_{12} + 1 \cdot C_{13}$$

$$C_{12} = -\det\begin{pmatrix}3&0\1&4\end{pmatrix} = -(12-0) = -12$$

$$C_{13} = +\det\begin{pmatrix}3&1\1&2\end{pmatrix} = 6-1 = 5$$

$$\det(A) = 0 + 2(-12) + 1(5) = -24 + 5 = -19 \checkmark$$

## A $4 \times 4$ Example

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \ 2 & 5 & 1 & 3 \ 0 & 1 & 3 & 2 \ 1 & 3 & 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Eliminate column 1** (pivot $d_1 = 1$):

$R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_4 \leftarrow R_4 - R_1$ ($R_3$ unchanged since $a_{31}=0$):

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 3 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Eliminate column 2** (pivot $d_2 = 1$):

$R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - R_2$, $R_4 \leftarrow R_4 - R_2$:

$$\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Eliminate column 3** (pivot $d_3 = 2$):

$R_4 \leftarrow R_4 - \frac{1}{2}R_3$:

$$U = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 3/2 \end{pmatrix}$$

No row swaps, $s = 0$. Pivots: $d_1 = 1$, $d_2 = 1$, $d_3 = 2$, $d_4 = 3/2$.

$$\det(A) = (-1)^0 \cdot 1 \cdot 1 \cdot 2 \cdot \frac{3}{2} = 3$$

This computation took one pass of elimination — far more efficient than the 24-term permutation formula or the four $3\times 3$ cofactor expansions required by cofactor expansion along a row.

## What the Pivots Tell You Beyond $\det(A)$

The pivot formula is useful not just for computing $\det(A)$ but for reading off structural information:

**Singularity.** $\det(A) = 0 \iff$ at least one pivot is zero. A zero pivot means elimination has produced a zero row, i.e., $A$ is singular. The pivot formula makes this completely transparent.

**Number of positive and negative pivots.** For a symmetric matrix $A$, the number of positive pivots equals the number of positive eigenvalues (Sylvester's law of inertia). So the signs of the pivots carry spectral information.

**Positive definiteness.** A symmetric matrix $A$ is positive definite if and only if all pivots are positive. This is the pivot test for positive definiteness, and it is equivalent to $\det(A_k) > 0$ for all leading submatrices $A_k$ — which is the next formula.

## The Pivot Formula and Leading Submatrices

Let $A_k$ denote the $k \times k$ **leading submatrix** of $A$ — the top-left $k \times k$ block:

$$A_k = \begin{pmatrix}a_{11} & \cdots & a_{1k} \ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ a_{k1} & \cdots & a_{kk}\end{pmatrix}$$

**Theorem.** When all leading submatrices are invertible (equivalently, when elimination requires no row swaps for any of them):

$$d_k = \frac{\det(A_k)}{\det(A_{k-1})}$$

with the convention $\det(A_0) = 1$.

_Derivation._ Consider $A_k$ and apply the pivot formula:

$$\det(A_k) = d_1 d_2 \cdots d_k$$

and

$$\det(A_{k-1}) = d_1 d_2 \cdots d_{k-1}$$

Dividing:

$$\frac{\det(A_k)}{\det(A_{k-1})} = d_k \qquad \square$$

This gives a formula for each pivot in terms of determinants of leading submatrices. It is rarely used to compute pivots (elimination is faster), but it is theoretically important — it shows that the pivots are ratios of consecutive leading determinants.

**Example.** For $A = \begin{pmatrix}2&1&1\4&3&3\8&7&9\end{pmatrix}$:

$$A_1 = \begin{pmatrix}2\end{pmatrix} \implies \det(A_1) = 2$$ $$A_2 = \begin{pmatrix}2&1\4&3\end{pmatrix} \implies \det(A_2) = 6-4 = 2$$ $$A_3 = A \implies \det(A_3) = 4 \text{ (computed earlier)}$$

Pivots:

$$d_1 = \frac{\det(A_1)}{\det(A_0)} = \frac{2}{1} = 2 \checkmark$$

$$d_2 = \frac{\det(A_2)}{\det(A_1)} = \frac{2}{2} = 1 \checkmark$$

$$d_3 = \frac{\det(A_3)}{\det(A_2)} = \frac{4}{2} = 2 \checkmark$$

These match the pivots found earlier by elimination.

## Why This Formula Is Better Than Cofactor Expansion

|Method|Operations for $n \times n$|When to use|
|---|---|---|
|Permutation formula|$O(n!)$|Never, except for theory|
|Cofactor expansion|$O(n!)$ naive, $O(n \cdot (n-1)!)$ recursive|$n \leq 3$ by hand|
|Pivot formula (elimination)|$O(n^3)$|Always for $n \geq 4$|

The gap between $O(n^3)$ and $O(n!)$ is enormous. For $n = 20$: $n^3 = 8000$ operations, $n! \approx 2.4 \times 10^{18}$ operations. There is no competition.

---

## The Connection to All Three Determinant Formulas

The three formulas are not independent — they are three windows onto the same object.

The **permutation formula** is the definition from first principles. It is what $\det$ literally is when you unpack Properties 1–3.

The **cofactor expansion** reorganizes the permutation formula by grouping the $n!$ terms according to which column is chosen from one row. It is recursive and gives a way to compute by hand for small matrices.

The **pivot formula** reorganizes the computation entirely, replacing the $n!$ term sum with $n$ multiplications after $O(n^3)$ row operations. It works because:

1. Row additions do not change $\det$ (Property 5).
2. Row swaps negate $\det$ (Property 2).
3. The determinant of the resulting triangular matrix is the product of its diagonal entries (Property 7).

The pivot formula is therefore a consequence of all three fundamental properties, assembled in the most computationally efficient order.

## Things to Remember (for Myself)

- $\det(A) = (-1)^s \cdot d_1 d_2 \cdots d_n$ where $s$ is the number of row swaps during elimination and $d_i$ are the pivots.
- Row additions do not change $\det$. Only row swaps change it (by $-1$ each).
- Never scale rows during elimination when computing $\det$ — scaling by $c$ multiplies $\det$ by $c$, which corrupts the result.
- $d_k = \det(A_k)/\det(A_{k-1})$: pivots are ratios of consecutive leading determinants. Useful theoretically, but compute pivots by elimination in practice.
- Zero pivot $\Leftrightarrow$ singular matrix $\Leftrightarrow \det(A) = 0$.
- For a symmetric matrix, signs of pivots = signs of eigenvalues (Sylvester's law of inertia).
- Positive definite $\Leftrightarrow$ all pivots positive $\Leftrightarrow$ all leading determinants positive.
- The pivot formula is $O(n^3)$. Use it for any $n \geq 4$.
