
## What this note is doing

Cramer's rule gives an explicit closed-form expression for each component $x_j$ of the solution to $Ax = b$. We derived it in passing at the end of the cofactors note — here I want to actually prove it carefully, understand the geometry, and be honest about when it matters and when it doesn't.

The punchline before the proof: $x_j = \det(B_j) / \det(A)$, where $B_j$ is $A$ with its $j$-th column replaced by $b$. The mystery is _why_ replacing a column by $b$ and taking the determinant extracts the $j$-th component of $x$. That's what I want to make transparent.

---

## Setup

Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ invertible matrix, $b \in \mathbb{R}^n$. We want to solve

$$Ax = b$$

Write $A$ in terms of its columns:

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \ a_1 & a_2 & \cdots & a_n \ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix}$$

Then $Ax = b$ says:

$$x_1 a_1 + x_2 a_2 + \cdots + x_n a_n = b$$

The solution exists and is unique since $A$ is invertible.

---

## The key construction

Define $B_j$ as the matrix obtained from $A$ by replacing column $j$ with $b$:

$$B_j = \begin{pmatrix} | & & | & | & | & & | \ a_1 & \cdots & a_{j-1} & b & a_{j+1} & \cdots & a_n \ | & & | & | & | & & | \end{pmatrix}$$

Now substitute $b = Ax = x_1 a_1 + \cdots + x_n a_n$ into column $j$:

$$B_j = \begin{pmatrix} | & & | & | & | & & | \ a_1 & \cdots & a_{j-1} & x_1 a_1 + \cdots + x_n a_n & a_{j+1} & \cdots & a_n \ | & & | & | & | & & | \end{pmatrix}$$

Use _multilinearity_ of the determinant in column $j$ to split this sum:

$$\det B_j = \det!\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & \cdots & a_{j-1} & x_1 a_1 & a_{j+1} & \cdots & a_n \end{pmatrix} + \cdots + \det!\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & \cdots & a_{j-1} & x_n a_n & a_{j+1} & \cdots & a_n \end{pmatrix}$$

Pull each scalar $x_k$ out:

$$\det B_j = \sum_{k=1}^{n} x_k \cdot \det!\begin{pmatrix} a_1 & \cdots & a_{j-1} & a_k & a_{j+1} & \cdots & a_n \end{pmatrix}$$

Now the crucial observation: in the $k$-th term, we have the matrix $A$ with column $j$ replaced by $a_k$.

- If $k \neq j$: the matrix has two identical columns ($a_k$ appears in its original position _and_ in position $j$). The determinant of a matrix with two equal columns is $0$.
- If $k = j$: the column $a_k = a_j$ goes back into position $j$, so the matrix is exactly $A$ again.

Therefore every term in the sum vanishes except $k = j$:

$$\det B_j = x_j \cdot \det A$$

Since $\det A \neq 0$:

$$\boxed{x_j = \frac{\det B_j}{\det A}}$$

This is Cramer's rule. The proof is just multilinearity and the vanishing of repeated-column determinants — both are consequences of the three fundamental properties. Nothing mysterious.

## Full explicit example — $2 \times 2$

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 1 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 7 \ 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det A = (3)(2) - (1)(1) = 5$$

**For $x_1$:** Replace column 1 of $A$ with $b$:

$$B_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 7 & 1 \ 4 & 2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det B_1 = (7)(2) - (1)(4) = 10$$

$$x_1 = \frac{10}{5} = 2$$

**For $x_2$:** Replace column 2 of $A$ with $b$:

$$B_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 3 & 7 \ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \det B_2 = (3)(4) - (7)(1) = 5$$

$$x_2 = \frac{5}{5} = 1$$

Verify: $3(2) + 1(1) = 7$ and $1(2) + 2(1) = 4$. Correct.

## Full explicit example — $3 \times 3$

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 3 & 1 \ 0 & 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad b = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

First compute $\det A$ by cofactor expansion along row 1:

$$\det A = 2 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix} - 1 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + 0 \cdot (\cdots)$$

$$= 2(12 - 1) - 1(4 - 0) = 2(11) - 4 = 18$$

**For $x_1$:** Replace column 1 with $b$:

$$B_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 & 0 \ 2 & 3 & 1 \ 3 & 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

Expand along row 1:

$$\det B_1 = 1 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 1 \ 1 & 4 \end{pmatrix} - 1 \cdot \det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + 0 = (12-1) - (8-3) = 11 - 5 = 6$$

$$x_1 = \frac{6}{18} = \frac{1}{3}$$

**For $x_2$:** Replace column 2 with $b$:

$$B_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \ 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det B_2 = 2\det\begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \ 3 & 4 \end{pmatrix} - 1\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 4 \end{pmatrix} + 0 = 2(8-3) - (4-0) = 10 - 4 = 6$$

$$x_2 = \frac{6}{18} = \frac{1}{3}$$

**For $x_3$:** Replace column 3 with $b$:

$$B_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 \ 1 & 3 & 2 \ 0 & 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det B_3 = 2\det\begin{pmatrix} 3 & 2 \ 1 & 3 \end{pmatrix} - 1\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 \ 0 & 3 \end{pmatrix} + 1\det\begin{pmatrix} 1 & 3 \ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

$$= 2(9-2) - (3-0) + (1-0) = 14 - 3 + 1 = 12$$

$$x_3 = \frac{12}{18} = \frac{2}{3}$$

## The geometric interpretation — area and volume ratios

This is the part most textbooks skip over, and I think it is the most illuminating framing.

In $\mathbb{R}^2$, $|\det A|$ is the area of the parallelogram spanned by the columns of $A$. Similarly, $|\det B_j|$ is the area of the parallelogram formed by replacing column $j$ with $b$.

So Cramer's rule says:

$$x_j = \frac{\text{signed area of parallelogram with } b \text{ in position } j}{\text{signed area of parallelogram spanned by columns of } A}$$

In $\mathbb{R}^3$, replace "area" with "volume of parallelepiped."

More precisely: the solution $x$ is the unique vector of _coordinates_ of $b$ in the basis formed by the columns of $A$. The $j$-th coordinate is the ratio of the "volume" with $b$ substituted into the $j$-th slot to the total volume of the basis parallelepiped. This is a genuinely geometric statement — it says that determinants measure "how much of the basis is accounted for by $b$ in each direction."

For the $2 \times 2$ case, draw it: $a_1, a_2$ span a parallelogram. Replacing $a_1$ with $b$ and taking the ratio of areas gives $x_1$ — how far along $a_1$ you need to go to reach $b$ (while $a_2$ keeps its position). That is the right geometric picture.

## Connection to the adjugate — recovering the formula from last time

From the cofactors note, we had:

$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det A} \operatorname{adj}(A)$$

So $x = A^{-1} b = \frac{1}{\det A} \operatorname{adj}(A) b$. The $j$-th component is:

$$x_j = \frac{1}{\det A} \sum_{i=1}^{n} C_{ij} , b_i$$

But $\sum_{i=1}^{n} b_i , C_{ij}$ is exactly the cofactor expansion of $\det B_j$ _along column $j$_ — because $B_j$ agrees with $A$ in all columns except column $j$, which is $b$, and expanding along that column gives $\sum_i b_i C_{ij}$ (the cofactors $C_{ij}$ are computed from $A$, which is the same as from $B_j$ since the other columns are unchanged).

So Cramer's rule and the adjugate formula are _the same statement_, just written differently. Cramer's rule is the adjugate formula evaluated component by component.

## Honest assessment — when does this actually matter?

Cramer's rule is computationally useless beyond $n = 3$. Computing $n+1$ determinants of $n \times n$ matrices costs $O(n \cdot n!) $ operations with the cofactor formula, versus $O(n^3)$ for Gaussian elimination. For $n = 10$: cofactor is on the order of $10 \times 10! \approx 36$ million operations; elimination is $\sim 1000$. There is no contest.

Where Cramer's rule is genuinely valuable:

**Theoretical / symbolic work.** If $A$ has entries that are polynomials or rational functions in some parameter $t$, then $x_j = \det(B_j(t)) / \det(A(t))$ is an explicit rational function of $t$. This shows the solution varies continuously (even analytically) with the parameters, as long as $\det A(t) \neq 0$. Elimination in this setting would require knowing the pivot structure for each $t$, which is much harder to state cleanly.

**Sensitivity / perturbation analysis.** Differentiating Cramer's rule with respect to parameters gives formulas for how the solution changes — useful in stability analysis.

**Small systems with symbolic entries.** A $2 \times 2$ or $3 \times 3$ system where the entries are symbols: Cramer's rule gives a clean closed form immediately.

Strang is explicit in 18.06: Cramer's rule is "not the way to solve $Ax = b$," but it is "the way to _understand_ the solution."

## Summary

$$x_j = \frac{\det B_j}{\det A}$$

where $B_j$ is $A$ with column $j$ replaced by $b$. The proof: multilinearity of $\det$ in column $j$ expands $\det B_j$ into a sum over $k$; the repeated-column property kills every term except $k = j$, leaving $x_j \det A$. Cramer's rule is Gaussian elimination's ugly computational cousin and the adjugate formula's clean theoretical face.
