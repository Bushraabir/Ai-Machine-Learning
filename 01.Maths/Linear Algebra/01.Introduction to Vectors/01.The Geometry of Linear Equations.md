The whole course is really one question asked a thousand different ways:
$$\boxed{Ax = b}$$Before we touch elimination, determinants, eigenvalues — any of it — Strang forces you to _see_ what this equation means geometrically. Not just symbolically. That's the point of lecture 1. He wants you to build three separate mental pictures and hold all three simultaneously. I think that's the most honest thing a professor can do on day one.

# A Concrete 2×2 System

Let's anchor everything on a running example. Take:
Solve the system:

$$
2x - y = 0
$$
$$
-x + 2y = 3
$$
In matrix form:
$$
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 
2 & -1 \\ 
-1 & 2 
\end{bmatrix}}_{A} 
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 
x \\ 
y 
\end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{x}} = 
\underbrace{\begin{bmatrix} 
0 \\ 
3 
\end{bmatrix}}_{\mathbf{b}}
$$Everything below is just three different ways of staring at this same object:
## Picture 1 — The Row Picture
**One equation at a time. Each row draws a geometric object.**

| Dimension | Each equation is...      | Solution is...                  |
| --------- | ------------------------ | ------------------------------- |
| 2D        | a line in the $xy$-plane | intersection point of two lines |
| 3D        | a plane in $xyz$-space   | point where three planes meet   |
| $n$D      | a hyperplane             | intersection of $n$ hyperplanes |
For our example:
- Row 1: $2x - y = 0$ → a line through the origin
- Row 2: $-x + 2y = 3$ → another line
- Solution: the point $(1, 2)$ where they cross

> The row picture is intuitive for 2D. It breaks down completely by 4D. 
## Picture 2 — The Column Picture 

**This is the one that scales.**

Rewrite the system by pulling out columns of $A$:
$$
x
\begin{bmatrix}
2 \\
-1
\end{bmatrix}
+
y
\begin{bmatrix}
-1 \\
2
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
3
\end{bmatrix}
$$

The question is no longer "where do two lines cross?" The question is:

> _What combination of the column vectors $\mathbf{c}_1$ and $\mathbf{c}_2$ produces $b$?_

### The Derivation (what's actually happening)

Define column vectors:

$$
\mathbf{c}_1 =
\begin{bmatrix}
2 \\
-1
\end{bmatrix},
\quad
\mathbf{c}_2 =
\begin{bmatrix}
-1 \\
2
\end{bmatrix}
$$

We want scalars $x, y$ such that
$$
x\mathbf{c}_1 + y\mathbf{c}_2 = \mathbf{b}
$$
Check with solution $(x,y) = (1,2)$:
$$
1 \cdot
\begin{bmatrix}
2 \\
-1
\end{bmatrix}
+
2 \cdot
\begin{bmatrix}
-1 \\
2
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
2 \\
-1
\end{bmatrix}
+
\begin{bmatrix}
-2 \\
4
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
2 - 2 \\
-1 + 4
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
3
\end{bmatrix}
=
\mathbf{b}
\quad ✓
$$

Geometrically: place $\mathbf{c}_1$ tip-to-tail with $2\mathbf{c}_2$. The resulting vector lands exactly on $\mathbf{b}$.  
Therefore $\mathbf{b}$ is a **linear combination** of the columns $\mathbf{c}_1$ and $\mathbf{c}_2$..

### Why This Picture is Superior

As $n$ grows, you can't visualize hyperplanes intersecting. But you _can_ always think about column vectors and whether their combinations span the space. The column picture generalizes perfectly — it's what all the later theory (column space, rank, null space) is built on.

## Picture 3 — Matrix × Vector, Two Ways

### Way 1 — By Columns (Strang's preferred)

$$
Ax = x_1 \mathbf{c}_1 + x_2 \mathbf{c}_2 + \cdots + x_n \mathbf{c}_n
$$

$Ax$ is a **linear combination of the columns of $A$**.  
The entries of $x$ are the weights.

$$
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 \\
-1 & 2
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
2
\end{bmatrix}
=
1
\begin{bmatrix}
2 \\
-1
\end{bmatrix}
+
2
\begin{bmatrix}
-1 \\
2
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
3
\end{bmatrix}
$$


### Way 2 — By Rows (dot products)

Each entry of $Ax$ is the **dot product of a row of $A$ with $x$**:
$$
(Ax)_i = \mathbf{r}_i \cdot x = \sum_j A_{ij} x_j
$$$$
\begin{bmatrix}
2 & -1 \\
-1 & 2
\end{bmatrix}
\begin{bmatrix}
1 \\
2
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
(2)(1) + (-1)(2) \\
(-1)(1) + (2)(2)
\end{bmatrix}
=
\begin{bmatrix}
0 \\
3
\end{bmatrix}
$$
Both give the same answer — they must — but they *mean* different things.
- The **column way** explains the **structure** of the transformation.
- The **row way** explains the **computation**.

> **Internalize this:**  
> The row method computes.  
> The column method *reveals*.

## The Big Picture — When Does a Solution Exist?

Here's the deeper question Strang slips in at the end: 
>[!quote] **can we solve $Ax = b$ for _every_ $b$?**

This is really asking: _do the column vectors of $A$ span the whole space?_

### Non-Singular (Invertible) Case — "Good"

The columns are **linearly independent**. Their linear combinations fill all of $\mathbb{R}^n$. Every $b$ is reachable. Exactly one solution $x$ exists for every $b$. see more [[07.Linear Independence]]

### Singular Case — "Bad"

The columns are **linearly dependent** — they all lie in some lower-dimensional subspace (a plane, a line, etc.). Most $b$ vectors live _outside_ that subspace, so no solution exists.

### Derivation of Why Singularity Kills Us (3×3 case)

Suppose columns $\mathbf{c}_1, \mathbf{c}_2, \mathbf{c}_3 \in \mathbb{R}^3$ all lie in the same plane $\Pi$ through the origin.

Then every linear combination $x_1\mathbf{c}_1 + x_2\mathbf{c}_2 + x_3\mathbf{c}_3$ also lies in $\Pi$ (planes are closed under linear combinations — check: $\Pi$ is a subspace).

So $\text{Col}(A) = \Pi \subsetneq \mathbb{R}^3$.

For any $b \notin \Pi$: no solution. The equation $Ax = b$ is **inconsistent**.

This is the first hint of what we'll later call **rank** — how much of $\mathbb{R}^n$ the columns actually cover. see more[[28.Singular Value Decomposition]]

## Core Definitions to Lock In

| Term                             | Definition                                                                    |
| -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Linear combination**           | $\sum_i c_i \mathbf{v}_i$ for scalars $c_i$ and vectors $\mathbf{v}_i$        |
| **Column space** $\text{Col}(A)$ | Set of all vectors $Ax$ as $x$ ranges over $\mathbb{R}^n$                     |
| **Non-singular matrix**          | $A$ s.t. $\text{Col}(A) = \mathbb{R}^n$ (columns are independent, span fully) |
| **Singular matrix**              | $A$ s.t. $\text{Col}(A) \subsetneq \mathbb{R}^n$ (columns are dependent)      |
