_This is the note I wish I had written before I learned LU decomposition. The standard presentation just states $A = LU$ and shows you the algorithm. But if you actually track what happens when you multiply the elimination matrices together, the $L$ matrix falls out completely naturally — and the fact that it is unit lower triangular with the multipliers sitting exactly where you'd hope becomes obvious rather than magical._

---

## Setup

We are doing Gaussian elimination on an $n \times n$ matrix $A$ (assume no row swaps needed for now — I will handle pivoting separately). At each step we eliminate below the current pivot using a row replacement operation.

Each such operation is left-multiplication by an **elimination matrix** (a special elementary matrix of Type 3 from the Gauss-Jordan note). Let me be precise about what these look like.

---

## The Elimination Matrix $E_{ij}$

The matrix that subtracts $\ell_{ij}$ times row $j$ from row $i$ (with $i > j$, so we are eliminating below the pivot in column $j$) is:

$$E_{ij} = I - \ell_{ij} \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T$$

where $\mathbf{e}_i$ is the $i$-th standard basis vector. Explicitly, for $n = 4$, the matrix that eliminates entry $(3,1)$ using multiplier $\ell_{31}$:

$$E_{31} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \ -\ell_{31} & 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

It is the identity with $-\ell_{31}$ in position $(3,1)$.

**The multiplier** $\ell_{ij}$ is defined as:

$$\ell_{ij} = \frac{a_{ij}^{(j)}}{a_{jj}^{(j)}}$$

where the superscript $(j)$ means "at the stage when we are working on column $j$". It is the ratio of the entry to be eliminated to the current pivot.

**Inverse of $E_{ij}$:** Simply flip the sign of the off-diagonal entry:

$$E_{ij}^{-1} = I + \ell_{ij} \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T$$

In the $4 \times 4$ example:

$$E_{31}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 0 & 0 \ \ell_{31} & 0 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

This is the matrix that _restores_ the eliminated entry. Note it is still unit lower triangular.

---

## The Full Sequence of Elimination Steps

For a $4 \times 4$ matrix, the forward elimination phase produces a sequence of elimination matrices. Working column by column:

**Column 1:** eliminate entries $(2,1)$, $(3,1)$, $(4,1)$:

$$E_{21},\quad E_{31},\quad E_{41}$$

**Column 2:** eliminate entries $(3,2)$, $(4,2)$:

$$E_{32},\quad E_{42}$$

**Column 3:** eliminate entry $(4,3)$:

$$E_{43}$$

After applying all of these (in order, left to right):

$$E_{43} , E_{42} , E_{32} , E_{41} , E_{31} , E_{21} , A = U$$

where $U$ is upper triangular. So:

$$A = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{41}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1} E_{42}^{-1} E_{43}^{-1} , U$$

We define $L = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{41}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1} E_{42}^{-1} E_{43}^{-1}$. Then $A = LU$.

The question is: what does this product of inverse elimination matrices actually look like?

---

## The Key Miracle: Multipliers Drop In Without Interaction

This is the structural fact that makes LU beautiful.

**Claim.** When the elimination matrices act on columns in order (column 1 first, then column 2, etc.) and there are no row swaps, the product of inverse elimination matrices is:

$$L = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{41}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1} E_{42}^{-1} E_{43}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \ \ell_{21} & 1 & 0 & 0 \ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 & 0 \ \ell_{41} & \ell_{42} & \ell_{43} & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

The multipliers just sit in their natural positions. There is no cross-contamination between them.

**Why?** The key is a product rule for elimination matrices in the same column.

---

## Lemma: Elimination Matrices in the Same Column Compose Cleanly

**Lemma.** For $i > j$ and $k > j$ (two elimination matrices in column $j$):

$$E_{ij}^{-1} E_{kj}^{-1} = (I + \ell_{ij}\mathbf{e}_i\mathbf{e}_j^T)(I + \ell_{kj}\mathbf{e}_k\mathbf{e}_j^T)$$

$$= I + \ell_{ij}\mathbf{e}_i\mathbf{e}_j^T + \ell_{kj}\mathbf{e}_k\mathbf{e}_j^T + \ell_{ij}\ell_{kj} \underbrace{\mathbf{e}_i\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k}_{= \mathbf{e}_i \cdot 0 = 0 \text{ since } j \neq k} \mathbf{e}_j^T$$

$$= I + \ell_{ij}\mathbf{e}_i\mathbf{e}_j^T + \ell_{kj}\mathbf{e}_k\mathbf{e}_j^T$$

The cross term vanishes because $\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k = 0$ (since $j \neq k$ when $k > j$). So the two rank-1 corrections simply add, placing $\ell_{ij}$ in position $(i,j)$ and $\ell_{kj}$ in position $(k,j)$ with no interaction.

This extends: any product of inverse elimination matrices from the **same column** $j$ gives a matrix that is the identity with all the corresponding multipliers sitting in column $j$ below the diagonal. Clean.

---

## Lemma: Elimination Matrices Across Different Columns Also Compose Cleanly

Now consider two inverse elimination matrices from different columns: $E_{ij}^{-1}$ (column $j$) and $E_{kl}^{-1}$ (column $l$, with $l > j$, so column $l$ comes later in elimination).

$$(I + \ell_{ij}\mathbf{e}_i\mathbf{e}_j^T)(I + \ell_{kl}\mathbf{e}_k\mathbf{e}_l^T) = I + \ell_{ij}\mathbf{e}_i\mathbf{e}_j^T + \ell_{kl}\mathbf{e}_k\mathbf{e}_l^T + \ell_{ij}\ell_{kl}\mathbf{e}_i\underbrace{(\mathbf{e}_j^T\mathbf{e}_k)}_{=0 \text{ if } j \neq k}\mathbf{e}_l^T$$

The cross term vanishes because $j < l$ and during column $l$ elimination we work on rows $k > l > j$, so $k \neq j$, giving $\mathbf{e}_j^T \mathbf{e}_k = 0$.

Again the cross term is zero and the multipliers drop independently into their positions.

---

## The Full Product: Proof That $L$ Has the Multipliers

Putting the two lemmas together and applying them inductively across all columns:

$$L = \prod_{\substack{j < i}} E_{ij}^{-1} = I + \sum_{\substack{j < i}} \ell_{ij} \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T$$

The sum $\sum_{j < i} \ell_{ij} \mathbf{e}_i \mathbf{e}_j^T$ is a matrix whose $(i,j)$ entry (for $i > j$) is $\ell_{ij}$ and all other entries are zero. Adding the identity gives the unit diagonal. So:

$$L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & & & \ \ell_{21} & 1 & & \ \ell_{31} & \ell_{32} & 1 & \ \ell_{41} & \ell_{42} & \ell_{43} & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

exactly as claimed. $\blacksquare$

**The cross terms all vanish** because every pair of elimination matrices $(i,j)$ and $(k,l)$ with $j < l$ satisfies $k > l > j$, so $k \neq j$.

This would **fail** with row swaps: a permutation matrix can move multipliers around and mix them, destroying the clean structure. That is why $PA = LU$ (with a permutation matrix $P$) is the correct statement when pivoting is needed.

---

## Explicit $4 \times 4$ Worked Example

Let

$$A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 \ 4 & 3 & 3 & 1 \ 8 & 7 & 9 & 5 \ 6 & 7 & 9 & 8 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 1: Eliminate column 1 (pivot = 2).**

Multipliers: $$\ell_{21} = \frac{4}{2} = 2, \quad \ell_{31} = \frac{8}{2} = 4, \quad \ell_{41} = \frac{6}{2} = 3$$

$$E_{41}E_{31}E_{21}A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 3 & 5 & 5 \ 0 & 4 & 6 & 8 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 2: Eliminate column 2 (pivot = 1).**

Multipliers: $$\ell_{32} = \frac{3}{1} = 3, \quad \ell_{42} = \frac{4}{1} = 4$$

$$E_{42}E_{32}(\cdots)A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 4 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 3: Eliminate column 3 (pivot = 2).**

Multiplier: $$\ell_{43} = \frac{2}{2} = 1$$

$$E_{43}(\cdots)A = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 1 & 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & 2 & 2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 & 2 \end{pmatrix} = U$$

**Reading off $L$:** The multipliers drop directly into $L$:

$$L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & 0 \ 2 & 1 & 0 & 0 \ 4 & 3 & 1 & 0 \ 3 & 4 & 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Verification:** $LU$ should give $A$. Check the $(3,1)$ entry: $L$ row 3 times $U$ col 1 = $4 \cdot 2 + 3 \cdot 0 + 1 \cdot 0 + 0 \cdot 0 = 8$. $\checkmark$

---

## What Goes Wrong With Row Swaps

Suppose at column 2, we needed to swap rows 2 and 3 before eliminating. The permutation matrix $P_{23}$ acts by:

$$P_{23} E_{21} A$$

Now $E_{31}$ and $E_{21}$ have already been applied. The multiplier $\ell_{31}$ was computed relative to the original row order. After $P_{23}$ swaps the rows, $\ell_{31}$ is no longer in position $(3,1)$ — it has moved to $(2,1)$. The subsequent elimination for column 2 will contaminate this entry.

The fix is to permute $A$ **first** (choosing row interchanges in advance via partial pivoting), then factor $PA = LU$, where $P$ is the total permutation matrix recording all swaps. Then $L$ again has the multipliers sitting cleanly in their positions, but relative to the permuted matrix $PA$.

In practice, LAPACK and all serious numerical libraries do this. The LU they return is really $PA = LU$ with $P$ stored implicitly as a permutation vector.

---

## Why the Order of Multiplication Matters

A common confusion: why do we write $E_{43} \cdots E_{21} A = U$ (the $E$'s applied left to right in increasing order) but then form $L$ as the product of inverses in the same increasing order? Let me make this explicit.

The operations are applied as: $$\underbrace{E_{43}}_{6} \underbrace{E_{42}}_{5} \underbrace{E_{32}}_{4} \underbrace{E_{41}}_{3} \underbrace{E_{31}}_{2} \underbrace{E_{21}}_{1} A = U$$

So $E_{21}$ is applied first (rightmost), then $E_{31}$, and so on. To recover $A$:

$$A = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{41}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1} E_{42}^{-1} E_{43}^{-1} U = LU$$

The inverse product reverses the order. The reason $L$ is still clean is that the cross-term argument above holds regardless of which direction we multiply — all the cross terms are zero.

---

## The LU Decomposition: Summary

$$A = LU$$

where:

- $L$ is **unit lower triangular**: ones on the diagonal, the elimination multipliers $\ell_{ij}$ in position $(i,j)$ for $i > j$, zeros above the diagonal.
- $U$ is **upper triangular**: the pivots on the diagonal (which are nonzero iff $A$ is invertible), computed quantities above, zeros below.

**Existence:** $A = LU$ (without row swaps) exists iff all leading principal submatrices of $A$ are invertible (equivalently, all pivots are nonzero during elimination).

**Uniqueness:** Given $A = LU$ with $L$ unit lower triangular and $U$ upper triangular, the factorization is unique (if it exists).

**Existence with row swaps:** $PA = LU$ always exists for any invertible $A$, where $P$ is a permutation matrix.

---

## Using $LU$ to Solve $Ax = b$

Once $A = LU$ is computed (cost $O(n^3)$), solving $Ax = b$ becomes:

$$LUx = b \implies \begin{cases} Ly = b & \text{(forward substitution, } O(n^2)\text{)} \ Ux = y & \text{(backward substitution, } O(n^2)\text{)} \end{cases}$$

For each new right-hand side $b$, the $O(n^3)$ factorization is reused and only $O(n^2)$ work is needed. This is why $LU$ is the standard method for repeated solves, rather than computing $A^{-1}$ explicitly.

---

## Things to Not Confuse

- The multipliers are computed from the **current** state of the matrix at each step, not from the original $A$. After row operations, the entries change. $\ell_{32} = a_{32}^{(2)} / a_{22}^{(2)}$ uses the entry in position $(3,2)$ **after** column 1 elimination.
- $L$ stores the multipliers exactly as computed. They do not change during subsequent elimination steps (this is the content of the miracle above).
- The product $E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} \cdots$ is **not** the same as $(E_{43} \cdots E_{21})^{-1}$. The former is an ordered product of individual inverses in the same order; the latter reverses the order. They happen to give the same $L$ only because of the vanishing cross terms.
