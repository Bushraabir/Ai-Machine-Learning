_A unit vector is just a vector of length one. That sounds almost too simple to deserve its own note. But unit vectors are everywhere â€” they are the clean carriers of direction, stripped of any magnitude. The moment you normalise a vector you are separating its two pieces of information: where it points and how long it is. That separation turns out to be the right thing to do in an embarrassing number of situations._

---

## Definition

A vector $\mathbf{u} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is a **unit vector** if:

$$|\mathbf{u}| = 1$$

equivalently, $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u} = 1$.

Every nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$ can be **normalised** to a unit vector:

$$\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}$$

$\hat{\mathbf{v}}$ points in the same direction as $\mathbf{v}$ and has length exactly 1. The hat notation $\hat{(\cdot)}$ signals "unit vector in this direction" and I will use it consistently.

**Verification:**

$$|\hat{\mathbf{v}}| = \left|\frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}\right| = \frac{|\mathbf{v}|}{|\mathbf{v}|} = 1 \qquad \checkmark$$

using the homogeneity property $|c\mathbf{v}| = |c||\mathbf{v}|$ with $c = 1/|\mathbf{v}|$.

## The Standard Basis Vectors

The most important unit vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ are the standard basis vectors:

$$\mathbf{e}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\0\\vdots\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{e}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}0\1\0\\vdots\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \ldots, \quad \mathbf{e}_n = \begin{pmatrix}0\0\0\\vdots\1\end{pmatrix}$$

Each $\mathbf{e}_i$ has $|\mathbf{e}_i| = 1$ and $\mathbf{e}_i\cdot\mathbf{e}_j = \delta_{ij}$ (Kronecker delta: 1 if $i=j$, 0 if $i\neq j$). They are an **orthonormal** set â€” mutually orthogonal unit vectors.

In $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$, these are also written $\hat{\mathbf{i}}, \hat{\mathbf{j}}, \hat{\mathbf{k}}$ in physics. I prefer the $\mathbf{e}_i$ notation since it generalises cleanly to $\mathbb{R}^n$.

---

## Unit Vectors and the Dot Product

When $\mathbf{u}$ is a unit vector, the dot product formula simplifies dramatically. For any $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta = |\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$$

So $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$ is exactly the **signed length of the projection of $\mathbf{v}$ onto $\mathbf{u}$** â€” the scalar projection, with no division needed.

More explicitly, the projection of $\mathbf{v}$ onto the direction $\hat{\mathbf{u}}$:

$$\text{proj}_{\hat{\mathbf{u}}},\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}}{\hat{\mathbf{u}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}},\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}}{1},\hat{\mathbf{u}} = (\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}),\hat{\mathbf{u}}$$

The denominator $|\hat{\mathbf{u}}|^2 = 1$ disappears. Unit vectors make projection formulas clean.

**Reading off coordinates.** If ${\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1, \ldots, \hat{\mathbf{u}}_n}$ is an orthonormal basis (mutually orthogonal unit vectors), then any $\mathbf{v}$ has the expansion:

$$\mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n (\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i),\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i$$

The $i$-th coordinate of $\mathbf{v}$ in this basis is simply the dot product $\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i$. No matrix inversion, no solving a linear system â€” just dot products. This is the defining advantage of orthonormal bases.

_Proof._ Write $\mathbf{v} = \sum_j c_j \hat{\mathbf{u}}_j$. Take the dot product of both sides with $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i$:

$$\mathbf{v}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i = \sum_j c_j (\hat{\mathbf{u}}_j\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i) = \sum_j c_j \delta_{ji} = c_i \qquad \blacksquare$$

## Unit Vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$: Parameterisation

Every unit vector in $\mathbb{R}^2$ can be written as:

$$\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\theta) = \begin{pmatrix}\cos\theta \ \sin\theta\end{pmatrix}$$

for some angle $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$. The set of all unit vectors in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is the unit circle $S^1$.

This parameterisation is worth internalising: $\theta$ is measured counterclockwise from the positive $x$-axis. Two unit vectors $\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\alpha)$ and $\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\beta)$ make an angle $\beta - \alpha$ between them, and their dot product is:

$$\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\alpha)\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\beta) = \cos\alpha\cos\beta + \sin\alpha\sin\beta = \cos(\beta - \alpha)$$

This is the cosine addition formula, now understood as a dot product of unit vectors.

---

## Unit Vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$: Spherical Coordinates

Every unit vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$ lives on the unit sphere $S^2$. In spherical coordinates:

$$\hat{\mathbf{u}}(\phi, \theta) = \begin{pmatrix}\sin\phi\cos\theta \ \sin\phi\sin\theta \ \cos\phi\end{pmatrix}$$

where $\phi \in [0,\pi]$ is the polar angle from the positive $z$-axis and $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$ is the azimuthal angle in the $x$-$y$ plane.

Check: $\sin^2\phi\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\phi\sin^2\theta + \cos^2\phi = \sin^2\phi(\cos^2\theta+\sin^2\theta) + \cos^2\phi = \sin^2\phi + \cos^2\phi = 1$. $\checkmark$

## Orthonormal Sets and Matrices

A set of vectors ${\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1, \ldots, \hat{\mathbf{u}}_k}$ is **orthonormal** if:

$$\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i \cdot \hat{\mathbf{u}}_j = \delta_{ij} = \begin{cases}1 & i = j \ 0 & i \neq j\end{cases}$$

Every orthonormal set is automatically linearly independent.

_Proof._ Suppose $\sum_i c_i \hat{\mathbf{u}}_i = \mathbf{0}$. Take the dot product of both sides with $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_j$:

$$0 = \mathbf{0}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_j = \sum_i c_i(\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_j) = \sum_i c_i \delta_{ij} = c_j$$

So $c_j = 0$ for every $j$. $\blacksquare$

When $k = n$, an orthonormal set in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is an **orthonormal basis**.

### Orthogonal Matrices

If we pack an orthonormal basis ${\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1, \ldots, \hat{\mathbf{u}}_n}$ into the columns of a matrix $Q$:

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \ \hat{\mathbf{u}}_1 & \hat{\mathbf{u}}_2 & \cdots & \hat{\mathbf{u}}_n \ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix}$$

then the orthonormality conditions $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_j = \delta_{ij}$ are precisely the statement:

$$Q^T Q = I$$

_Check._ The $(i,j)$ entry of $Q^TQ$ is $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i^T\hat{\mathbf{u}}_j = \hat{\mathbf{u}}_i\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_j = \delta_{ij}$. $\checkmark$

Such a matrix is called **orthogonal** (a slight misnomer â€” the columns are orthonormal, not merely orthogonal). From $Q^TQ = I$ we immediately get $Q^{-1} = Q^T$: the inverse costs nothing to compute, it is just the transpose.

If the rows of $Q$ are also orthonormal (which follows from $Q^TQ = I$ when $Q$ is square), then also $QQ^T = I$.

**Orthogonal matrices preserve lengths and dot products:**

$$|Q\mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{v}| \quad \text{for all } \mathbf{v}$$

_Proof._ $|Q\mathbf{v}|^2 = (Q\mathbf{v})^T(Q\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{v}^TQ^TQ\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}^TI\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{v}|^2$. $\blacksquare$

And:

$$(Q\mathbf{u})\cdot(Q\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{u}^TQ^TQ\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$$

So an orthogonal matrix is a **rigid motion** (a rotation, or a rotation composed with a reflection). It moves vectors around without stretching or squishing anything.

$\det(Q) = \pm 1$: $+1$ for rotations, $-1$ for rotations with a reflection.

## The Gram-Schmidt Process

Given any linearly independent set ${\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k}$, Gram-Schmidt produces an orthonormal set ${\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1, \ldots, \hat{\mathbf{u}}_k}$ spanning the same subspace. This is how orthonormal bases are constructed in practice.

**Step 1.** Take the first vector and normalise:

$$\mathbf{w}_1 = \mathbf{v}_1, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{u}}_1 = \frac{\mathbf{w}_1}{|\mathbf{w}_1|}$$

**Step $j$ (for $j = 2, 3, \ldots, k$).** Subtract from $\mathbf{v}_j$ its projections onto all previously found orthonormal vectors, then normalise:

$$\mathbf{w}_j = \mathbf{v}_j - \sum_{i=1}^{j-1}(\mathbf{v}_j\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i),\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{u}}_j = \frac{\mathbf{w}_j}{|\mathbf{w}_j|}$$

The key step is the subtraction: we remove every component of $\mathbf{v}_j$ that lies in the span of $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1, \ldots, \hat{\mathbf{u}}_{j-1}$. What remains, $\mathbf{w}_j$, is orthogonal to all of them. Normalising then makes it a unit vector.

$\mathbf{w}_j \neq \mathbf{0}$ is guaranteed by linear independence: if $\mathbf{w}_j = \mathbf{0}$, then $\mathbf{v}_j$ would be in $\text{span}{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_{j-1}}$, contradicting independence.

**This is also QR decomposition.** Writing out what Gram-Schmidt does in matrix language: $A = QR$ where $Q$ has orthonormal columns and $R$ is upper triangular. $R_{ij} = \mathbf{v}_j\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_i$ (the projection coefficients) for $i \leq j$.

## Worked Example: Gram-Schmidt in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Take:

$$\mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\1\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_3 = \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 1.** $\mathbf{w}_1 = \mathbf{v}_1 = (1,1,0)^T$. $|\mathbf{w}_1| = \sqrt{2}$.

$$\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 2.** Project $\mathbf{v}_2$ onto $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1$:

$$\mathbf{v}_2\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(1\cdot1 + 0\cdot1 + 1\cdot0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$

Subtract:

$$\mathbf{w}_2 = \mathbf{v}_2 - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\1\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1/2\1/2\0\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}1/2\-1/2\1\end{pmatrix}$$

$|\mathbf{w}_2| = \sqrt{1/4 + 1/4 + 1} = \sqrt{3/2}$.

$$\hat{\mathbf{u}}_2 = \sqrt{\frac{2}{3}}\begin{pmatrix}1/2\-1/2\1\end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 3.** Project $\mathbf{v}_3$ onto $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1$ and $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_2$:

$$\mathbf{v}_3\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(0+1+0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}$$

$$\mathbf{v}_3\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(0-1+2) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}$$

Subtract:

$$\mathbf{w}_3 = \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\begin{pmatrix}1\1\0\end{pmatrix} - \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\begin{pmatrix}1\-1\2\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}0\1\1\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1/2\1/2\0\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}1/6\-1/6\1/3\end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix}-2/3\2/3\2/3\end{pmatrix}$$

$|\mathbf{w}_3| = \frac{2}{3}\sqrt{3}$.

$$\hat{\mathbf{u}}_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}\begin{pmatrix}-1\1\1\end{pmatrix}$$

**Verify:** $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_1\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1+1+0) = 0$. $\hat{\mathbf{u}}_2\cdot\hat{\mathbf{u}}_3 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}\cdot\frac{1}{\sqrt{3}}(-1-1+2) = 0$. $\checkmark$

## Unit Vectors and the Normal Distribution

_This connection surprised me when I first saw it._

Let $\mathbf{Z} = (Z_1, \ldots, Z_n)^T$ with $Z_i \stackrel{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The joint density is:

$$f(\mathbf{z}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{n/2}} e^{-|\mathbf{z}|^2/2}$$

This density depends on $\mathbf{z}$ **only through $|\mathbf{z}|$** â€” it is spherically symmetric. Every direction is equally likely. This means:

**If $Q$ is any orthogonal matrix, $Q\mathbf{Z} \stackrel{d}{=} \mathbf{Z}$.**

_Proof._ $Q\mathbf{Z}$ is multivariate normal with mean $\mathbf{0}$ and covariance $QIQ^T = QQ^T = I$. So $Q\mathbf{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, I) = \mathbf{Z}$ in distribution. $\blacksquare$

**Decomposition into radius and direction.** Write $\mathbf{Z} = R\hat{\mathbf{U}}$ where:

$$R = |\mathbf{Z}|, \qquad \hat{\mathbf{U}} = \frac{\mathbf{Z}}{|\mathbf{Z}|}$$

$R$ is the radial distance and $\hat{\mathbf{U}}$ is the unit vector giving the direction. The spherical symmetry says $\hat{\mathbf{U}}$ is **uniformly distributed on the unit sphere $S^{n-1}$**, and $R$ and $\hat{\mathbf{U}}$ are **independent**. The radius $R$ has distribution related to $\chi_n$ (chi distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom): $R^2 = |\mathbf{Z}|^2 \sim \chi^2_n$.

This decomposition â€” separating length from direction â€” is the geometric heart of chi-squared distributions, $t$-distributions, and spherical symmetry arguments throughout probability.

## Things to Not Confuse

- **Orthogonal vs. orthonormal.** Orthogonal: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$. Orthonormal: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$ AND $|\mathbf{u}| = |\mathbf{v}| = 1$. An orthogonal set is not necessarily orthonormal. An orthonormal set is always orthogonal.
- **Orthogonal matrix.** Despite the name, an orthogonal matrix has **orthonormal** columns. The naming is historical and unfortunate.
- The formula $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}\mathbf{v}$ simplifies to $(\mathbf{u}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{v}})\hat{\mathbf{v}}$ when $\mathbf{v}$ is a unit vector. Always check whether the denominator is 1 before writing it out.
- $\hat{\mathbf{v}}$ and $-\hat{\mathbf{v}}$ are both unit vectors in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$ and $-\mathbf{v}$ respectively. "The unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$" means $+\hat{\mathbf{v}}$, not $\pm\hat{\mathbf{v}}$.
