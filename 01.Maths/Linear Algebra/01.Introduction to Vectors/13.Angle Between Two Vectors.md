The angle between two vectors is one of those things that feels obvious in $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$ — you can see it — but becomes a definition in $\mathbb{R}^n$. The dot product is what makes it work. Once I understood that the cosine formula is not a theorem in high dimensions but a definition, and that Cauchy-Schwarz is what guarantees the definition is consistent, the whole story clicked._

---

## The Definition

For nonzero vectors $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the **angle** $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ between them is defined by:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|}$$

equivalently:

$$\theta = \arccos\left(\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|}\right)$$

**Why this is well-defined.** The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality guarantees:

$$|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$$

so $-1 \leq \dfrac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|} \leq 1$, which is exactly the range of $\cos$. Without Cauchy-Schwarz this formula could produce a value outside $[-1,1]$ and $\arccos$ would be undefined. Cauchy-Schwarz is not a consequence of this definition — it is the logical prerequisite.

**In $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$** this definition agrees with the geometric angle you see. The formula is a theorem there, derived from the law of cosines (see the Length and Dot Product note). In $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n \geq 4$, we have no independent visual notion of angle, so this formula is taken as the definition.

**Convention:** $\theta \in [0,\pi]$. We always take the angle in the range $[0°, 180°]$, never reflex angles. This is because $\cos$ is injective on $[0,\pi]$, so the angle is uniquely determined.

---

## Computing the Angle

From the definition, the computation is always the same three steps:

1. Compute $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i$
2. Compute $|\mathbf{u}| = \sqrt{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u}}$ and $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}}$
3. Divide: $\cos\theta = \dfrac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|}$, then take $\arccos$

If only $\cos\theta$ is needed (not $\theta$ itself), stop at step 3.

### Worked Example in $\mathbb{R}^3$

$$\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix}1\2\2\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}2\-1\2\end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 1.** $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = (1)(2) + (2)(-1) + (2)(2) = 2 - 2 + 4 = 4$

**Step 2.** $|\mathbf{u}| = \sqrt{1 + 4 + 4} = \sqrt{9} = 3$, $\quad |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{4 + 1 + 4} = \sqrt{9} = 3$

**Step 3.** $\cos\theta = \dfrac{4}{3 \cdot 3} = \dfrac{4}{9}$, $\quad \theta = \arccos!\left(\dfrac{4}{9}\right) \approx 63.6°$

### Worked Example in $\mathbb{R}^4$

$$\mathbf{u} = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\-1\1\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix}2\1\1\0\end{pmatrix}$$

**Step 1.** $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 2 + 0 - 1 + 0 = 1$

**Step 2.** $|\mathbf{u}| = \sqrt{1+0+1+1} = \sqrt{3}$, $\quad |\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{4+1+1+0} = \sqrt{6}$

**Step 3.** $\cos\theta = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}\cdot\sqrt{6}} = \dfrac{1}{\sqrt{18}} = \dfrac{1}{3\sqrt{2}}$, $\quad \theta = \arccos!\left(\dfrac{1}{3\sqrt{2}}\right) \approx 76.4°$

The procedure is identical regardless of dimension.

## Special Cases

|$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$|$\theta$|Relationship|
|---|---|---|
|$= \|\mathbf{u}\|\mathbf{v}\|$|$= 0°$|Same direction, $\mathbf{v} = c\mathbf{u}$, $c > 0$|
|$> 0$|$\in (0°, 90°)$|Acute|
|$= 0$|$= 90°$|Orthogonal|
|$< 0$|$\in (90°, 180°)$|Obtuse|
|$= -\|\mathbf{u}\|\mathbf{v}\|$|$= 180°$|Opposite directions, $\mathbf{v} = c\mathbf{u}$, $c < 0$|

The sign of the dot product alone tells you which side of $90°$ you are on. That is often all you need.

## The Role of Direction: Normalised Form

Since $\cos\theta$ depends only on the directions of $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$, not their magnitudes, we can always reduce to unit vectors:

$$\cos\theta = \hat{\mathbf{u}}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{v}}$$

where $\hat{\mathbf{u}} = \mathbf{u}/|\mathbf{u}|$ and $\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \mathbf{v}/|\mathbf{v}|$. The angle is a property of directions, not lengths.

This means: scaling $\mathbf{u}$ or $\mathbf{v}$ by any positive scalar does not change $\theta$. Scaling by a negative scalar replaces $\theta$ with $\pi - \theta$.

---

## Angle and Cauchy-Schwarz: Two Sides of the Same Coin

Cauchy-Schwarz says $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$. Dividing both sides by $|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$:

$$\left|\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|}\right| \leq 1$$

This is exactly $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$. Without Cauchy-Schwarz the angle is not well-defined. In $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$ where angle is defined geometrically, $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$ proves Cauchy-Schwarz. The two are logically equivalent, with causality depending on which direction you come from.

**Equality.** $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ iff $|\cos\theta| = 1$ iff $\theta \in {0°, 180°}$ iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are parallel. All the same statement.

---

## Angle in Terms of Projection

The scalar projection of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ is:

$$\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|} = |\mathbf{u}|\cos\theta$$

This is the signed length of the shadow of $\mathbf{u}$ in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$. Positive when $\theta < 90°$, zero when $\theta = 90°$, negative when $\theta > 90°$.

The full vector projection:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}\mathbf{v} = \frac{|\mathbf{u}|\cos\theta}{|\mathbf{v}|}\mathbf{v}$$

And the Pythagorean decomposition confirms the geometry:

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2 = \underbrace{|\mathbf{u}|^2\cos^2\theta}_{|\text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}|^2} + \underbrace{|\mathbf{u}|^2\sin^2\theta}_{|\mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}|^2}$$

which is just $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$.

## Connection to Correlation: Angle in $L^2$

Let $X$ and $Y$ be square-integrable random variables. Define the centred versions $\tilde{X} = X - E[X]$ and $\tilde{Y} = Y - E[Y]$, and regard them as vectors in $L^2(\Omega, P)$ with inner product $\langle \tilde{X}, \tilde{Y}\rangle = E[\tilde{X}\tilde{Y}] = \text{Cov}(X,Y)$ and norm $|\tilde{X}| = \text{SD}(X)$.

The angle $\theta$ between $\tilde{X}$ and $\tilde{Y}$ in this space:

$$\cos\theta = \frac{\langle\tilde{X},\tilde{Y}\rangle}{|\tilde{X}||\tilde{Y}|} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{SD}(X),\text{SD}(Y)} = \rho(X,Y)$$

The Pearson correlation coefficient is the cosine of the angle between the centred random variables. Every geometric fact about angles now has a statistical translation:

- $|\rho| \leq 1$: Cauchy-Schwarz in $L^2$.
- $\rho = 1$: $\theta = 0°$, $\tilde{Y} = c\tilde{X}$ for $c > 0$, perfect positive linear relationship.
- $\rho = -1$: $\theta = 180°$, $\tilde{Y} = c\tilde{X}$ for $c < 0$, perfect negative linear relationship.
- $\rho = 0$: $\theta = 90°$, uncorrelated = orthogonal in $L^2$.

_This is the same inner product space that Hilbert used to unify analysis and geometry. The fact that it applies here — to random variables — is not a metaphor. It is a theorem._

## The Angle Between a Vector and a Subspace

The angle between a vector $\mathbf{v}$ and a subspace $\mathcal{W}$ is defined as:

$$\theta(\mathbf{v}, \mathcal{W}) = \min_{\mathbf{w} \in \mathcal{W},\ \mathbf{w} \neq \mathbf{0}} \theta(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})$$

the smallest angle between $\mathbf{v}$ and any vector in $\mathcal{W}$. The minimising vector $\mathbf{w}^*$ is the projection $\text{proj}_\mathcal{W}\mathbf{v}$, and the minimum angle satisfies:

$$\cos\theta(\mathbf{v}, \mathcal{W}) = \frac{|\text{proj}_\mathcal{W}\mathbf{v}|}{|\mathbf{v}|}$$

The complementary angle (between $\mathbf{v}$ and the orthogonal complement $\mathcal{W}^\perp$) is $90° - \theta$.

---

## Things to Not Confuse

- $\theta \in [0°, 180°]$ always. There is no notion of a signed or oriented angle between two vectors without additional structure.
- $\theta(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = \theta(\mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u})$. The angle is symmetric — the formula is symmetric in $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.
- $\theta = 0$ requires $\mathbf{v} = c\mathbf{u}$ with $c > 0$. The zero vector has no angle with anything — $|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}| = 0$ makes the formula undefined.
- The angle between $\mathbf{u}$ and $-\mathbf{v}$ is $\pi - \theta$ where $\theta$ is the angle between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$. Flipping one vector reflects the angle around $90°$.
- Obtuse angle ($\theta > 90°$) does not mean the vectors are "going away from each other" in any absolute sense — it just means the dot product is negative, i.e., $\mathbf{u}$ has a negative component in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$.
