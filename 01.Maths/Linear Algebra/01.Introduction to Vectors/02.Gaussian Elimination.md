## Why Does This Matter?

Solving $Ax = b$ directly is hard. Gaussian elimination is the _systematic_ way to make it easy. It converts a messy, coupled system into a triangular one where the solution unravels from the bottom up.

$$ Ax = b \xrightarrow{\text{forward elimination}} Ux = c \xrightarrow{\text{back substitution}} x $$

**Why upper triangular?** Because each equation then has one more unknown than the one below it. You solve the last equation first (1 unknown), plug in, solve the second-to-last (2 unknowns), and so on. Clean, recursive, $O(n^2)$.

```
Upper triangular structure:
┌               ┐
│ x   x   x   x │   ← 4 unknowns
│ 0   x   x   x │   ← 3 unknowns
│ 0   0   x   x │   ← 2 unknowns
│ 0   0   0   x │   ← 1 unknown  ← solve this first
└               ┘
```

## Setting Up: The Augmented Matrix

Given the system:

$$ \begin{aligned} 2x + y - z &= 8 \\ -3x - y + 2z &= -11 \\ -2x + y + 2z &= -3 \end{aligned} $$

Write it as an **augmented matrix** $[A \mid b]$:
$$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \\ -3 & -1 & 2 & -11 \\ -2 & 1 & 2 & -3 \end{array} \right] $$

> **Rule:** Every row operation applied to the left side must also be applied to the right side. The vertical bar just keeps coefficients and constants visually separated.

## The Three Legal Row Operations

These are the only moves allowed. They preserve the solution set.

| Operation                            | Notation                     | What it does                     |
| ------------------------------------ | ---------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Swap two rows                        | $R_i \leftrightarrow R_j$    | Reorder equations                |
| Scale a row                          | $R_i \leftarrow c \cdot R_i$ | Multiply equation by nonzero $c$ |
| Add a multiple of one row to another | $R_i \leftarrow R_i + k R_j$ | **This is the main one**         |
The third operation is the workhorse. It lets you zero out entries below the pivot without changing the solution.

## The Algorithm: 

```
1. Write augmented matrix [A | b]
2. Pick a pivot (first nonzero in current column)
3. Eliminate all entries BELOW the pivot (use row op 3)
4. Move to next column/row and repeat
5. Result: upper triangular U
6. Back-substitute to get x
```

**Step 1: Augmented matrix:**
	$$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} 2 & 1 & -1 & 8 \\ -3 & -1 & 2 & -11 \\ -2 & 1 & 2 & -3 \end{array} \right] $$
**Step 2: First pivot: $a_{11} = 2$**
	Compute multipliers: $$ \ell_{21} = \frac{-3}{2} = -\frac{3}{2}, \qquad \ell_{31} = \frac{-2}{2} = -1 $$

> **Multiplier formula:** $\ell_{ij} = \dfrac{a_{ij}}{a_{jj}}$ (entry to eliminate ÷ pivot)

Apply: $$ R_2 \leftarrow R_2 + \tfrac{3}{2} R_1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad \left(-3 + 3,; -1 + \tfrac{3}{2},; 2 - \tfrac{3}{2},; -11 + 12\right) = \left(0,; \tfrac{1}{2},; \tfrac{1}{2},; 1\right) $$$$ R_3 \leftarrow R_3 + R_1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad (-2+2,; 1+1,; 2-1,; -3+8) = (0,; 2,; 1,; 5) $$
Result after step 2:
$$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} \boxed{2} & 1 & -1 & 8 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} & 1 \\ 0 & 2 & 1 & 5 \end{array} \right] $$

**Step 3: Second pivot: $a_{22}^{(2)} = \frac{1}{2}$**
Multiplier: $$ \ell_{32} = \frac{2}{1/2} = 4 $$
Apply: $$ R_3 \leftarrow R_3 - 4R_2 \quad \Rightarrow \quad (0,; 2 - 2,; 1 - 2,; 5 - 4) = (0,; 0,; -1,; 1) $$

Result after Step 3:
$$ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc|c} \boxed{2} & 1 & -1 & 8 \\ 0 & \boxed{\frac{1}{2}} & \frac{1}{2} & 1 \\ 0 & 0 & \boxed{-1} & 1 \end{array} \right] $$

Three pivots: $2,; \frac{1}{2},; -1$ — all nonzero → elimination succeeded.

**Step 4: Back Substitution**
Start from the bottom row and work upward:
$$ \text{Row 3:}\quad -z = 1 ;\Rightarrow; \boxed{z = -1} $$
$$ \text{Row 2:}\quad \tfrac{1}{2}y + \tfrac{1}{2}(-1) = 1 ;\Rightarrow; \tfrac{1}{2}y = \tfrac{3}{2} ;\Rightarrow; \boxed{y = 3} $$
$$ \text{Row 1:}\quad 2x + 3 - (-1) = 8 ;\Rightarrow; 2x = 4 ;\Rightarrow; \boxed{x = 2} $$
**Solution:** $x = 2; y = 3; z = -1$

**Verify:**

| Equation                  | Check |
| ------------------------- | ----- |
| $2(2) + 3 - (-1) = 8$     | ✓     |
| $-3(2) - 3 + 2(-1) = -11$ | ✓     |
| $-2(2) + 3 + 2(-1) = -3$  | ✓     |

## Failure Modes

Elimination produces $n$ pivots if it works. If a pivot is zero, two cases arise.

### Case 1: Temporary Failure (Fixable)
The pivot position is 0, but a nonzero entry exists _below_ it in the same column.
```
Before swap:          After swap R₁ ↔ R₂:
┌ 0   2   1 ┐         ┌ 3   5  -1 ┐
│ 3   5  -1 │   →     │ 0   2   1 │
└ 1   4   2 ┘         └ 1   4   2 ┘
     ↑                      ↑
  zero pivot!            now OK
```
**Fix:** Swap the zero-pivot row with any row below that has a nonzero entry in that column. This is encoded by a **permutation matrix** $P$.

### Case 2: Complete Failure (Singular Matrix)
The pivot position is 0 and _all_ entries below it are also 0. No swap can fix this.
**Example:**
$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 1 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \xrightarrow{R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - R_1} \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$
Second pivot is 0 with nothing below it → **matrix is singular**.
Two sub-cases for $[A \mid b]$:
```
No Solution (inconsistent):         Infinitely Many Solutions:
┌ 1   1 | 2 ┐                       ┌ 1   1 | 2 ┐
└ 0   0 | 5 ┘                       └ 0   0 | 0 ┘
        ↑                                   ↑
   0 = 5 → impossible              0 = 0 → one free variable
```
**Geometric meaning of singularity:**
- The rows of $A$ are linearly dependent (one row is a combination of others)
- The null space $\ker(A)$ is nontrivial (nonzero vectors map to zero)
- $$
\ker\!\left(
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 1 \\
1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\right)
=
\operatorname{span}\!\left\{
\begin{pmatrix}
1 \\
-1
\end{pmatrix}
\right\}
$$

## Elimination as Matrix Multiplication

>[!point] Every row operation is secretly a matrix multiplication.

### Elementary Matrices
The operation $R_i \leftarrow R_i - \ell R_j$ corresponds to multiplying by: $E_{ij} = I - \ell, e_i e_j^T$
where $e_i$ is the $i$-th standard basis vector.
**Left multiply = [[01.Row Operations|row operation.]]** 
**Right multiply = [[02.Column Operations|column operation.]]**
Example with $A = \begin{pmatrix}1&2\\3&4\end{pmatrix}$, $E = \begin{pmatrix}1&0\\2&1\end{pmatrix}$:
$$ EA = \begin{pmatrix}1&2\\5&8\end{pmatrix} \quad \text{(row operation)} \qquad\qquad AE = \begin{pmatrix}5&2\\11&4\end{pmatrix} \quad \text{(column operation)} $$

### The Elimination Chain

For the worked example, the elimination multipliers were $\ell_{21} = -\frac{3}{2}, \qquad\ell_{31} = -1, \qquad\ell_{32} = 4$
The corresponding **elementary elimination matrices** are
$$
E_{21} =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
\frac{3}{2} & 1 & 0 \\
0 & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\qquad
E_{31} =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 0 \\
1 & 0 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
\qquad
E_{32} =
\begin{pmatrix}
1 & 0 & 0 \\
0 & 1 & 0 \\
0 & -4 & 1
\end{pmatrix}
$$
$$ E_{32}, E_{31}, E_{21}, A = U $$

## The LU Factorization

If no row swaps are needed, we can write:

$$ \boxed{A = LU} $$

**How?** Invert the elimination chain:

$$ E_{32} E_{31} E_{21} A = U ;\Rightarrow; A = E_{21}^{-1} E_{31}^{-1} E_{32}^{-1} U = LU $$

**Inverting an elementary matrix is trivial** — just flip the sign of $\ell$: $$ E_{ij} = I - \ell, e_i e_j^T ;\Rightarrow; E_{ij}^{-1} = I + \ell, e_i e_j^T $$

Example: $$ E_{32} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&-4&1\end{pmatrix} ;\Rightarrow; E_{32}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}1&0&0\\0&1&0\\0&4&1\end{pmatrix} $$

### The Magic of L

The multipliers $\ell_{ij}$ pack neatly into the lower triangle of $L$:

$$ L = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \\ \frac{3}{2} & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & -4 & 1 \end{pmatrix} \qquad U = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 & -1 \\ 0 & \frac{1}{2} & \frac{1}{2} \\ 0 & 0 & -1 \end{pmatrix} $$

**Verify:** $LU = A$ ✓

> **Key property of $L$:** It is _unit lower triangular_ — 1s on the diagonal, multipliers below. The multipliers go in exactly the position where they were used during elimination. see more: [[10. A = LU]]

### With Row Swaps: $PA = LU$

If row swaps are needed, the factorization becomes:
$$ PA = LU $$
where $P$ is a **permutation matrix** — a reordered identity. Properties:
$$ P^{-1} = P^T, \qquad P^2 = I \text{ (for simple swaps)}, \qquad \det(P) = \pm 1 $$
Example — swapping rows 1 and 2 of a $3\times3$:
$$ P_{12} = \begin{pmatrix}0&1&0\\1&0&0\\0&0&1\end{pmatrix} $$

see more[[16.PA = LU]]


## Why LU? (Practical Value)

Once you have $A = LU$, solving $Ax = b$ for **many different $b$** is cheap:

$$ Ax = b ;\Rightarrow; LUx = b ;\Rightarrow; \underbrace{Ly = b}_{\text{forward sub}} ;\text{then}; \underbrace{Ux = y}_{\text{back sub}} $$

Both forward and back substitution are $O(n^2)$. Elimination itself is $O(n^3)$, but you only do it **once** for $A$ — then reuse $L$ and $U$ for any new $b$.

---

## Complexity Summary

| Phase                           | Cost     |
| ------------------------------- | -------- |
| Forward elimination (build $U$) | $O(n^3)$ |
| Forward substitution ($Ly = b$) | $O(n^2)$ |
| Back substitution ($Ux = y$)    | $O(n^2)$ |

---

## The One-Sentence Summary

> Gaussian elimination turns a complicated system into an easy one — **same solution, simpler form** — by applying invertible row operations that are secretly matrix multiplications, producing the factorization $A = LU$.

## Connection to the Bigger Picture

| Concept                 | What it means for Gaussian Elimination                                             |
| ----------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Rank**                | Number of nonzero pivots                                                           |
| **Invertibility**       | $A$ invertible $\iff$ all pivots nonzero                                           |
| **Null space**          | Nonzero if any pivot is missing                                                    |
| **Determinant**         | Product of the pivots (up to sign from swaps)                                      |
| **LU factorization**    | The algebraic encoding of elimination                                              |
| **Numerical stability** | Use **partial pivoting** ($PA = LU$) in practice to avoid division by tiny numbers |
