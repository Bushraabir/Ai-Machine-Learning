_The dot product is the first time a purely algebraic operation carries genuine geometric content. Before this, vectors are just lists of numbers. After this, they have angles between them, they have lengths, they have a sense of how much they point in the same direction. Everything metric in linear algebra — projections, orthogonality, least squares, the geometry of the normal distribution — flows from this one operation. I want to understand it completely._

---

## Length

The **length** (Euclidean norm) of a vector $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + \cdots + v_n^2} = \sqrt{\mathbf{v}^T \mathbf{v}}$$

For $n = 2$ this is Pythagoras. For $n = 3$ it is Pythagoras applied twice. For general $n$ it is the definition — we are extending the Euclidean notion of distance by declaring that the same formula holds in all dimensions.

**Properties of the norm.** For all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$:

1. $|\mathbf{v}| \geq 0$, with $|\mathbf{v}| = 0$ iff $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ — _positive definiteness_
2. $|c\mathbf{v}| = |c| |\mathbf{v}|$ — _homogeneity_
3. $|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$ — _triangle inequality_

Properties 1 and 2 are immediate from the definition. Property 3 requires Cauchy-Schwarz and is proved below.

**Unit vectors.** A vector with $|\mathbf{v}| = 1$ is a unit vector. Every nonzero vector can be normalized:

$$\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}$$

$\hat{\mathbf{v}}$ points in the same direction as $\mathbf{v}$ and has length exactly 1.

**Distance between two vectors:**

$$\text{dist}(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}) = |\mathbf{u} - \mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{\sum_{i=1}^n (u_i - v_i)^2}$$

This makes $\mathbb{R}^n$ a metric space.

## The Dot Product

**Definition.** For $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n u_i v_i$$

The dot product takes two vectors and returns a scalar.

Observe immediately:

$$\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n v_i^2 = |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

So the norm is recovered from the dot product: $|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}}$.

**Algebraic properties.** For all $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and $c \in \mathbb{R}$:

1. $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{u}$ — _symmetry_
2. $(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w}\cdot\mathbf{v}$ — _additivity_
3. $(c\mathbf{u}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = c(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})$ — _homogeneity_
4. $\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v} \geq 0$, with equality iff $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ — _positive definiteness_

Properties 1–4 say the dot product is an **inner product** on $\mathbb{R}^n$. This structure is what separates an inner product space from a mere vector space.

Properties 2 and 3 together say the dot product is **bilinear**: linear in each argument separately. This is the computation rule that makes everything tractable.

## The Geometric Formula

**Theorem.** For any $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ with $\theta$ the angle between them:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$$

_Proof._ Consider the triangle with vertices at $\mathbf{0}$, $\mathbf{u}$, and $\mathbf{v}$. The side opposite $\theta$ is $\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}$. The law of cosines gives:

$$|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$$

Expand the left side using the algebraic properties:

$$|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}|^2 = (\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u})\cdot(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v} - 2\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u} = |\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + |\mathbf{u}|^2$$

Equating the two expressions:

$$|\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + |\mathbf{u}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$$

Cancel $|\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2$ from both sides and divide by $-2$:

$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta \qquad \blacksquare$$

**This formula is how angle is defined in $\mathbb{R}^n$ for $n > 3$.** In $\mathbb{R}^2$ and $\mathbb{R}^3$ we already have geometric angles and can verify this formula. For $n \geq 4$, we have no independent geometric notion of angle — we use this formula as the definition:

$$\cos\theta := \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|}$$

and Cauchy-Schwarz (proved below) guarantees $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$ so this is always a valid angle.

## Orthogonality

$\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are **orthogonal** (written $\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$) if:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$$

From the geometric formula: $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0 \iff \cos\theta = 0 \iff \theta = 90°$.

**Convention:** $\mathbf{0}$ is orthogonal to every vector (since $\mathbf{0}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$ for all $\mathbf{v}$). This is purely algebraic — the zero vector has no direction, so "perpendicular" has no geometric meaning for it, but the algebraic definition still works.

**The Pythagorean Theorem** in $\mathbb{R}^n$: if $\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$ then:

$$|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

_Proof._

$$|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}|^2 = (\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v})\cdot(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}) = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2\underbrace{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}_{=0} + |\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2 \qquad \blacksquare$$

This extends: if $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$ are mutually orthogonal then:

$$\left|\sum_{i=1}^k \mathbf{v}_i\right|^2 = \sum_{i=1}^k |\mathbf{v}_i|^2$$

## The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

**Theorem.**

$$|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$$

with equality iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly dependent (one is a scalar multiple of the other, or one is zero).

_Proof._ If $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$, both sides are zero and the inequality holds trivially. Assume $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$.

Consider $f(t) = |\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}|^2$ for $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Since it is a squared norm, $f(t) \geq 0$ for all $t$. Expand:

$$f(t) = (\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v})\cdot(\mathbf{u}-t\mathbf{v}) = |\mathbf{u}|^2 - 2t(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + t^2|\mathbf{v}|^2$$

This is a quadratic in $t$ with positive leading coefficient $|\mathbf{v}|^2 > 0$, and it is nonneg­ative for all $t$. A nonneg­ative quadratic $at^2 + bt + c \geq 0$ (with $a > 0$) has discriminant $\leq 0$:

$$b^2 - 4ac \leq 0$$

Here $a = |\mathbf{v}|^2$, $b = -2(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})$, $c = |\mathbf{u}|^2$. So:

$$4(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 - 4|\mathbf{v}|^2|\mathbf{u}|^2 \leq 0$$

$$(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2$$

$$|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}| \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Equality** holds iff $f(t) = 0$ for some $t$, i.e., $\mathbf{u} = t\mathbf{v}$ for some $t$ — exactly when $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly dependent.

_Note: this proof works in any inner product space — Hilbert spaces, function spaces, $L^2$, everything. The key was only that $f(t) \geq 0$ for all $t$, which follows from positive definiteness of the inner product._

## The Triangle Inequality

**Theorem.**

$$|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$$

_Proof._ Square both sides (both sides are nonneg­ative, so squaring preserves the inequality):

$$|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}|^2 \leq (|\mathbf{u}|+|\mathbf{v}|)^2$$

Left side:

$$|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

Right side:

$$(|\mathbf{u}|+|\mathbf{v}|)^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}| + |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

So the inequality reduces to:

$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$$

which follows from Cauchy-Schwarz (since $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} \leq |\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$). $\blacksquare$

## The Parallelogram Law

$$|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}|^2 + |\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}|^2 = 2|\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2|\mathbf{v}|^2$$

_Proof._ Direct expansion:

$$|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

$$|\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 - 2\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

Add: the $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$ terms cancel. $\blacksquare$

**Geometric meaning:** the sum of the squares of the diagonals of a parallelogram equals the sum of the squares of all four sides. This characterizes inner product spaces: a norm comes from an inner product iff it satisfies the parallelogram law. If a norm fails the parallelogram law (like the $\ell^1$ norm), there is no inner product producing it.

**Polarization identity.** Rearranging, the dot product can be recovered from the norm alone:

$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \frac{1}{4}\left(|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}|^2 - |\mathbf{u}-\mathbf{v}|^2\right)$$

This means inner product and norm carry identical information. You can work entirely with one and recover the other.

## Projection

The **projection of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$**:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2},\mathbf{v}$$

The scalar coefficient $\dfrac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}$ is the coordinate of $\mathbf{u}$ in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$. Multiplying by $\mathbf{v}$ gives the vector in that direction.

**Decomposition.** Every $\mathbf{u}$ splits uniquely into:

$$\mathbf{u} = \underbrace{\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2},\mathbf{v}}_{\parallel \text{ to } \mathbf{v}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{u} - \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2},\mathbf{v}}_{\perp \text{ to } \mathbf{v}}$$

**Verification of orthogonality.** Let $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u}$. Then:

$$\mathbf{e}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \left(\mathbf{u} - \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}\mathbf{v}\right)\cdot\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} - \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}) = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0 \qquad \checkmark$$

**Minimum distance interpretation.** The projection $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u}$ is the point on the line ${t\mathbf{v} : t \in \mathbb{R}}$ closest to $\mathbf{u}$.

_Proof._ We want to minimise $|\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}|^2$ over $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Expand:

$$|\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 - 2t(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + t^2|\mathbf{v}|^2$$

This is the same quadratic as in the Cauchy-Schwarz proof. Minimise over $t$ by taking the derivative and setting to zero:

$$\frac{d}{dt}|\mathbf{u}-t\mathbf{v}|^2 = -2(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + 2t|\mathbf{v}|^2 = 0 \implies t^* = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}$$

So the closest point is $t^*\mathbf{v} = \text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u}$. $\blacksquare$

_This is the one-dimensional version of least squares. The higher-dimensional version — projecting onto a subspace — is exactly the same idea, just with a subspace replacing the line._

## Correlation as a Cosine

In statistics, the Pearson correlation coefficient between two random variables $X$ and $Y$ is:

$$\rho(X,Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sqrt{\text{Var}(X)\text{Var}(Y)}}$$

Now think of centred random variables $X - E[X]$ and $Y - E[Y]$ as vectors in the space of random variables with the inner product $\langle X, Y\rangle = E[XY]$. Then:

$$\langle X - \mu_X,, Y - \mu_Y\rangle = E[(X-\mu_X)(Y-\mu_Y)] = \text{Cov}(X,Y)$$

$$|X - \mu_X| = \sqrt{E[(X-\mu_X)^2]} = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)} = \text{SD}(X)$$

So:

$$\rho(X,Y) = \frac{\langle X-\mu_X,, Y-\mu_Y\rangle}{|X-\mu_X||Y-\mu_Y|} = \cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between the centred random variables viewed as vectors.

**Cauchy-Schwarz says $|\rho| \leq 1$**, which is the statement that correlation is always between $-1$ and $1$. This is not a separate fact — it is literally Cauchy-Schwarz applied to the inner product space of random variables.

**$\rho = 1$** iff $Y - \mu_Y = c(X-\mu_X)$ for some $c > 0$: perfect positive linear relationship, angle $= 0$.

**$\rho = -1$** iff $Y - \mu_Y = c(X-\mu_X)$ for some $c < 0$: perfect negative linear relationship, angle $= 180°$.

**$\rho = 0$** iff the centred variables are orthogonal in this function space. Uncorrelated is the function-space version of perpendicular.

_This is one of the most satisfying things I have learned: correlation is a cosine, Cauchy-Schwarz bounds it, and the geometry of the dot product completely explains why $|\rho| \leq 1$._

## The Dot Product in Matrix Language

Everything above can be written cleanly using matrix multiplication.

For column vectors $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} \quad (1\times n)(n\times 1) = 1\times 1 \text{ scalar}$$

The outer product (rank-1 matrix):

$$\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T \quad (n\times 1)(1\times n) = n\times n \text{ matrix}$$

The projection formula in matrix form:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T}{\mathbf{v}^T\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u} = P\mathbf{u}$$

where $P = \dfrac{\mathbf{v}\mathbf{v}^T}{\mathbf{v}^T\mathbf{v}}$ is the **projection matrix** onto the line spanned by $\mathbf{v}$.

**Properties of $P$:**

- $P^2 = P$ — _idempotent_ (projecting twice gives the same result as projecting once)
- $P^T = P$ — _symmetric_
- $\text{rank}(P) = 1$

These two properties ($P^2 = P$ and $P^T = P$) characterise orthogonal projection matrices in general, not just rank-1 ones.

|Length|$\|\mathbf{v}\| = \sqrt{\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}}$|Distance from origin|

|Angle|$\cos\theta = \frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{u}\|\mathbf{v}\|}$|Defined by dot product in $\mathbb{R}^n$|

|Projection|$\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{\|\mathbf{v}\|^2}\mathbf{v}$|Closest point on line, minimum distance|
|Correlation|$\rho = \cos\theta$|Angle between centred random variables|

## Things to Not Confuse

- $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$ is a **scalar**. $\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T$ is an $n\times n$ **matrix**. The order of transpose matters completely.
- The projection of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ is **not** symmetric in $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$. $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u} \neq \text{proj}_{\mathbf{u}}\mathbf{v}$ in general.
- Cauchy-Schwarz gives $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$, not $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$. The absolute value matters when $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}$ is negative.
- Orthogonal vectors are linearly independent (unless one is zero). Independent vectors need not be orthogonal. These are different conditions.
