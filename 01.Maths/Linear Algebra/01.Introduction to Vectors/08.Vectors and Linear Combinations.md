## Vectors:
A vector $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ is an ordered $n$-tuple of real numbers:
$$\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \\ v_2 \\ \vdots \\ v_n \end{pmatrix}$$
I will always write vectors as column vectors. A row vector is $\mathbf{v}^T$ There are two ways to think about $\mathbf{v}$:
1) **As a point.** $\mathbf{v}$ is a location in $\mathbb{R}^n$, the point with coordinates $(v_1, \ldots, v_n)$.
2) **As an arrow.** $\mathbf{v}$ is a displacement — a direction and a magnitude — rooted at the origin and ending at $(v_1, \ldots, v_n)$.
Both pictures are useful. The arrow picture is better for understanding linear combinations geometrically. The point picture is better for probability (a random vector is a point in sample space). I will move between them freely.

## Vector Operations
1) **Addition.** For $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$: $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} u_1 + v_1 \ u_2 + v_2 \ \vdots \ u_n + v_n \end{pmatrix}$
Geometrically (arrow picture): place the tail of $\mathbf{v}$ at the tip of $\mathbf{u}$. The sum $\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}$ is the arrow from the origin to the new tip. This is the parallelogram law.
![[Pasted image 20260409145351.png]]
2) **Scalar multiplication.** For $c \in \mathbb{R}$: $c\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} cv_1 \ cv_2 \ \vdots \ cv_n \end{pmatrix}$
Geometrically: stretch $\mathbf{v}$ by factor $|c|$, reversing direction if $c < 0$. The set ${c\mathbf{v} : c \in \mathbb{R}}$ is the line through the origin in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$ (and its negative).
![[Pasted image 20260409145614.png]]

These two operations are all that $\mathbb{R}^n$ needs to be a vector space. Everything else — subspaces, bases, linear maps — is built on top of them.

## Linear Combinations:
Given vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k \in \mathbb{R}^n$ and scalars $c_1, c_2, \ldots, c_k \in \mathbb{R}$, the expression $c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k \mathbf{v}_k$ is called a **linear combination** of $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$ with coefficients $c_1, \ldots, c_k$.
This is the central operation of all of linear algebra. Everything — matrix-vector products, solutions to $Ax = b$, projections, the entire subject — is a statement about which linear combinations are possible.
#### The Column Picture of $Ax$
If $A$ is $m \times n$ with columns $\mathbf{a}^{(1)}, \ldots, \mathbf{a}^{(n)}$, then for $x = (x_1, \ldots, x_n)^T$: $Ax = x_1 \mathbf{a}^{(1)} + x_2 \mathbf{a}^{(2)} + \cdots + x_n \mathbf{a}^{(n)}$
$Ax$ is a linear combination of the columns of $A$ with coefficients given by $x$. This is the column picture of matrix-vector multiplication, and it reframes every question about $Ax = b$:

_Does there exist a linear combination of the columns of $A$ that equals $\mathbf{b}$?_

If yes, the coefficients are a solution $x$. If the solution is unique, the representation is unique.

## The Span:
## Span of a Set of Vectors

The **span** of a set of vectors $\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\}$ is the set of all their linear combinations: $\text{span}\{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k\} = \left\{ \sum_{i=1}^{k} c_i \mathbf{v}_i \;:\; c_1, \ldots, c_k \in \mathbb{R} \right\}$
The span is always a **subspace** of $\mathbb{R}^n$. It satisfies three closure properties:
- Contains $\mathbf{0}$ (set all $c_i = 0$)
- Closed under **addition**
- Closed under **scalar multiplication**

![[Pasted image 20260409151205.png]]

#### Geometric Example:
- **$n = 2$, one nonzero vector $\mathbf{v}$:** $\text{span}{\mathbf{v}} = {c\mathbf{v} : c \in \mathbb{R}}$ This is a line through the origin in direction $\mathbf{v}$.
- **$n = 2$, two vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2$ not parallel:** $\text{span}{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2} = \mathbb{R}^2$ Any point in $\mathbb{R}^2$ can be reached. The two vectors form a (possibly skewed) coordinate system.
- **$n = 3$, two vectors not parallel:** $\text{span}{\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2} = \text{a plane through the origin}$
- **$n = 3$, three vectors:** Either $\text{span} = \mathbb{R}^3$ (if none lies in the plane spanned by the other two), or $\text{span}$ is a plane or line (if they are redundant).
The question of when adding a new vector actually enlarges the span is exactly the question of linear independence.

## Linear Independence:
Vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$ are **linearly independent** if the only solution to $c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k \mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0}$ is $c_1 = c_2 = \cdots = c_k = 0$. They are **linearly dependent** if there exists a nontrivial solution (at least one $c_i \neq 0$).

**Geometrically:** if the vectors are dependent, at least one of them is redundant — it already lives in the span of the others. Dependence is the statement that the set of vectors has "more members than directions."

**Restatement in terms of the matrix:** $\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k$ are linearly independent iff the matrix $V = [\mathbf{v}_1 \mid \cdots \mid \mathbf{v}_k]$ has $\text{null}(V) = {\mathbf{0}}$, i.e., $Vc = \mathbf{0}$ implies $c = \mathbf{0}$.

### Important Points:
1) Any set containing the zero vector is linearly dependent. (Take $c_i = 1$ for the zero vector's index, all others zero.)
2) A single nonzero vector is always linearly independent.
3) Two vectors are linearly dependent iff one is a scalar multiple of the other — i.e., they point in the same or opposite direction.
4) More than $n$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ are always linearly dependent. You cannot have more independent directions than the dimension of the space.
5) Exactly $n$ linearly independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ span all of $\mathbb{R}^n$ — they form a basis.

## Basis and Dimension:
A **basis** for a subspace $V \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$ is a set of vectors that is:
1. linearly independent, and
2. spans $V$.
A basis is a minimal spanning set, or equivalently, a maximal independent set.

**Theorem (Basis Theorem).** Any two bases for the same subspace have the same number of vectors.This common number is the **dimension** of the subspace, $\dim(V)$.

_Proof sketch._ Suppose ${\mathbf{u}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{u}_p}$ and ${\mathbf{w}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{w}_q}$ are both bases for $V$. Since ${\mathbf{u}_i}$ spans $V$, each $\mathbf{w}_j$ is a linear combination of the $\mathbf{u}_i$'s. By a replacement argument (Steinitz exchange lemma), this forces $q \leq p$. By symmetry, $p \leq q$. So $p = q$. $\blacksquare$

**The standard basis** for $\mathbb{R}^n$: $\mathbf{e}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\\0\\\vdots\\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{e}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}0\\1\\\vdots\\0\end{pmatrix}, \quad \ldots, \quad \mathbf{e}_n = \begin{pmatrix}0\\0\\\vdots\\1\end{pmatrix}$ Every $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$ has a unique representation $\mathbf{v} = \sum_{i=1}^n v_i \mathbf{e}_i$ — the coordinates $v_i$ are exactly the coefficients in the standard basis expansion.

**Uniqueness of representation.** In any basis ${\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k}$ for $V$, every vector $\mathbf{w} \in V$ has a **unique** representation as a linear combination of the basis vectors.

_Proof._ Suppose $\mathbf{w} = \sum c_i \mathbf{v}_i = \sum d_i \mathbf{v}_i$. Subtracting: $\sum (c_i - d_i)\mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{0}$. By independence, $c_i = d_i$ for all $i$. $\blacksquare$ This uniqueness is what makes coordinates meaningful.

- ## The Four Fundamental Subspaces
For $A$ an $m \times n$ matrix, the four subspaces associated to $A$ are:
1) **Column space** $\text{col}(A) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$: the span of the columns of $A$. This is the set of all $\mathbf{b}$ for which $Ax = \mathbf{b}$ has a solution. Dimension = $\text{rank}(A)$.
2) **Null space** $\text{null}(A) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$: the set of all $x$ with $Ax = \mathbf{0}$. Dimension = $n - \text{rank}(A)$ (by rank-nullity).
3) **Row space** $\text{row}(A) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$: the span of the rows of $A$, equivalently $\text{col}(A^T)$. Dimension = $\text{rank}(A)$.
4) **Left null space** $\text{null}(A^T) \subseteq \mathbb{R}^m$: the set of all $y$ with $A^T y = \mathbf{0}$. Dimension = $m - \text{rank}(A)$.

**The Rank-Nullity Theorem:**
$\dim(\text{col}(A)) + \dim(\text{null}(A)) = n$
$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n$

_Proof._ Let $r = \text{rank}(A)$. Row reduce $A$ to RREF. There are $r$ pivot columns and $n - r$ free columns. The $n - r$ free variables parametrize $\text{null}(A)$, giving a basis of $n - r$ vectors. So $\text{nullity}(A) = n - r$. $\blacksquare$

**The orthogonality relations** (Fundamental Theorem of Linear Algebra):
$\text{col}(A) \perp \text{null}(A^T), \qquad \text{row}(A) \perp \text{null}(A)$
So $\mathbb{R}^m = \text{col}(A) \oplus \text{null}(A^T)$ and $\mathbb{R}^n = \text{row}(A) \oplus \text{null}(A)$. Every vector in $\mathbb{R}^n$ splits uniquely into a row-space component and a null-space component.

## Linear Combinations and Probability

_This is the connection I keep thinking about when I study Stat 110._

If $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ are random variables and $\mathbf{a} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n)^T$ is a fixed vector, then:$Y = \mathbf{a}^T \mathbf{X} = a_1 X_1 + a_2 X_2 + \cdots + a_n X_n$ is a linear combination of the $X_i$'s, and it is the most natural object in multivariate statistics.
1) **[[02.Linearity of Expectation|Expectation]] is linear:** $E[Y] = \mathbf{a}^T E[\mathbf{X}] = \mathbf{a}^T \boldsymbol{\mu}$
2) **[[11.Variance|Variance]] of a linear combination:** $\text{Var}(Y) = \mathbf{a}^T \Sigma \mathbf{a}$ where $\Sigma = \text{Cov}(\mathbf{X})$ is the $n \times n$ covariance matrix. This is a quadratic form in $\mathbf{a}$, and it is always $\geq 0$ — which is exactly the statement that $\Sigma$ is positive semidefinite.
3) **Multivariate linear combination:** if $A$ is $m \times n$ and $\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^n$, then $\mathbf{Y} = A\mathbf{X} \in \mathbb{R}^m$ is a vector of linear combinations: $E[\mathbf{Y}] = A\boldsymbol{\mu}, \qquad \text{Cov}(\mathbf{Y}) = A \Sigma A^T$ This is the formula from the $ABA^T$ [[05.Inverse of ABA^T]]note. Every column of $A\mathbf{X}$ is a linear combination of the $X_i$'s, and the covariance structure transforms accordingly.

**Why the span matters in probability:** if $\mathbf{X}$ has a singular covariance matrix $\Sigma$ (i.e., $\text{rank}(\Sigma) < n$), then the distribution of $\mathbf{X}$ is supported on a proper subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$ — the column space of $\Sigma$. The random vector $\mathbf{X}$ lives in a lower-dimensional affine subspace with probability 1. This is a degenerate Gaussian, and it cannot have a density with respect to Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$.

## Things to Not Confuse

- **Span vs. subspace.** Every span is a subspace, but not every subspace is described as the span of a finite set. (In finite dimensions, every subspace is the span of finitely many vectors — just take a basis. But the description isn't unique.)
- **Linearly independent vs. orthogonal.** Orthogonal vectors are always linearly independent, but independent vectors need not be orthogonal. Independence is purely about linear relations; orthogonality is a metric condition.
- **Basis vs. spanning set.** A spanning set may be redundant. A basis is a spanning set with no redundancy. A linearly independent set may not span the whole space. A basis does both.
- **$\text{rank}(A) = \dim(\text{col}(A)) = \dim(\text{row}(A))$.** Column rank equals row rank. This is a theorem, not a definition, and it is genuinely surprising the first time.
