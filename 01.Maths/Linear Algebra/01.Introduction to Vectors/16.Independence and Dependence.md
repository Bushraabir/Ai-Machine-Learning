Vectors $\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_k \in \mathbb{R}^n$ are **linearly independent** if the only solution to $c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + c_2 \mathbf{v}_2 + \cdots + c_k \mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0}$ is $c_1 = c_2 = \cdots = c_k = 0$.

They are **linearly dependent** if there exists a solution with at least one $c_i \neq 0$ — meaning one of the vectors can be written as a linear combination of the others. Dependence means redundancy. One vector in the collection is "already in the span" of the rest.

I want to be careful here: independence is a property of a _collection_ of vectors, not of a single vector in isolation. Asking "is $\mathbf{v}$ independent?" makes no sense. Asking "is ${\mathbf{v}_1, \mathbf{v}_2}$ independent?" makes perfect sense.

## Trnslating to Matrices

Pack the vectors as columns of a matrix $A$:
$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} | & | & & | \\ \mathbf{v}_1 & \mathbf{v}_2 & \cdots & \mathbf{v}_k \\ | & | & & | \end{pmatrix} $$

Then the equation $c_1 \mathbf{v}_1 + \cdots + c_k \mathbf{v}_k = \mathbf{0}$ is exactly $A\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$.

So:

- The columns are **independent** $\iff$ $\mathcal{N}(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$ $\iff$ $A\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$ has only the trivial solution.
- The columns are **dependent** $\iff$ $\mathcal{N}(A) \neq {\mathbf{0}}$ $\iff$ there exists a nonzero $\mathbf{c}$ with $A\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$.

This is the central restatement I keep coming back to. **Independence of columns = triviality of the nullspace.**

---

## The Rank Criterion

After row reduction, $A$ has $r$ pivots. The rank of $A$ is $r$.

$$ \text{columns of } A \text{ are independent} \iff r = k \text{ (number of columns)} $$

Every column must contain a pivot. No free variables, no nonzero solutions in the null space.

If $r < k$, there are $k - r$ free variables, which means $k - r$ independent directions in the nullspace — that many "dependence relations" among the columns.

---

## A Full Example with Row Reduction

Take three vectors in $\mathbb{R}^4$:

$$ \mathbf{v}_1 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 2 \ 0 \ 3 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_2 = \begin{pmatrix} 2 \ 4 \ 0 \ 6 \end{pmatrix}, \quad \mathbf{v}_3 = \begin{pmatrix} 1 \ 0 \ 1 \ 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Form the matrix $A$ with these as columns and row reduce:

$$ A = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 2 & 4 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \ 3 & 6 & 1 \end{pmatrix} $$

Row operations: $R_2 \leftarrow R_2 - 2R_1$, $R_4 \leftarrow R_4 - 3R_1$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & -2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Then $R_3 \leftarrow R_3 + \frac{1}{2}R_2$, $R_4 \leftarrow R_4 - R_2$:

$$ \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 2 & 1 \ 0 & 0 & -2 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} $$

Pivots are in columns 1 and 3. Column 2 is a free column — it has no pivot. So $r = 2 < 3 = k$, and the columns are **dependent**.

The dependence relation: column 2 is exactly $2 \times$ column 1. I can read this off directly from the matrix: $\mathbf{v}_2 = 2\mathbf{v}_1$. One vector is a scalar multiple of another, so any three vectors containing both must be dependent.

The nullspace vector: setting the free variable $c_2 = 1$, back-substitution gives $c_1 = -2, c_3 = 0$. Check: $-2\mathbf{v}_1 + 1\cdot\mathbf{v}_2 + 0\cdot\mathbf{v}_3 = \mathbf{0}$. Correct.

---

## Geometric Intuition

Two vectors are dependent iff one is a scalar multiple of the other — they point along the same line.

Three vectors are dependent iff all three lie in the same 2-dimensional plane through the origin.

In general: $k$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ are dependent iff they all lie in some subspace of dimension strictly less than $k$.

Independence means: each new vector genuinely escapes the span of the previous ones.

$$ \mathbf{v}_j \notin \text{span}{\mathbf{v}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{v}_{j-1}} \quad \text{for each } j $$

That is precisely what "adds a new dimension" means.

---

## The Counting Bound — More Vectors Than Dimensions

**Theorem:** Any collection of more than $n$ vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is necessarily linearly dependent.

**Proof:** If $A$ is $n \times k$ with $k > n$, then $r \leq n < k$, so there are free variables and the nullspace is nontrivial.

This is a dimension-counting argument. $\mathbb{R}^n$ can "support" at most $n$ independent directions. Asking for more is asking for the impossible.

The contrapositive: if I have $n$ independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^n$, I have a **basis** — I cannot add any more without introducing dependence.

---

## Zero Vector Destroys Independence

If any $\mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{0}$, the collection is immediately dependent: take $c_i = 1$ and all other $c_j = 0$. Then $c_i \mathbf{v}_i = \mathbf{0}$ nontrivially.

The zero vector can never be part of a linearly independent set. It contributes nothing to the span and everything to the dependence.

---

## Dependence and the Nullspace — Reading Off Relations

Once I have the row echelon form, I can read off every dependence relation.

The key fact from elimination: **the pivot columns of $A$ are linearly independent; the free columns are linear combinations of the pivot columns to their left.**

If column $j$ is a free column, I can find the specific combination by solving $A\mathbf{c} = \mathbf{0}$ with the free variable for column $j$ set to 1 and all other free variables set to 0. The resulting $\mathbf{c}$ gives the explicit dependence relation.

This is not just an existence statement — row reduction gives me the _exact_ dependence relation, constructively.

---

## Independence and the Four Subspaces

The connection to the four fundamental subspaces is tight:

|Condition|Subspace consequence|
|---|---|
|Columns independent ($r = k$)|$\mathcal{N}(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$, nullity $= 0$|
|Columns dependent ($r < k$)|$\dim \mathcal{N}(A) = k - r > 0$|
|Rows independent ($r = m$)|$\mathcal{N}(A^T) = {\mathbf{0}}$, left nullspace trivial|
|Full column rank $r = k \leq m$|$A^T A$ is invertible|
|Full row rank $r = m \leq k$|$A A^T$ is invertible|

The rank-nullity theorem ties everything together:

$$ r + (k - r) = k \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad \text{rank} + \text{nullity} = \text{number of columns} $$

---

## When $A$ is Square: Independence $\iff$ Invertibility

If $A$ is $n \times n$, then:

$$ \text{columns independent} \iff r = n \iff \det(A) \neq 0 \iff A \text{ is invertible} \iff A\mathbf{x} = \mathbf{b} \text{ has a unique solution for every } \mathbf{b} $$

All of these are equivalent. This is the "big theorem" of square matrices. It's worth knowing that all these conditions fail simultaneously — if one fails, they all fail.

Dependence of columns means: some nonzero vector $\mathbf{c}$ maps to $\mathbf{0}$ under $A$, so $A$ squashes something, so it cannot be invertible.

---

## Testing Independence in Practice

**Method 1 (Row reduction):** Form $A$ with vectors as columns. Row reduce. Count pivots. If pivots $=$ columns, independent.

**Method 2 (Determinant, square case):** If $k = n$, compute $\det(A)$. Nonzero iff independent.

**Method 3 (Rank):** Compute $\text{rank}(A)$. If $\text{rank}(A) = k$, independent.

**Method 4 (Inspection — use before computing):**

- If any vector is zero: dependent immediately.
- If any vector is a scalar multiple of another: dependent immediately.
- If $k > n$: dependent immediately, no computation needed.

---

## What Independence Is Really About

The deeper point — one I keep coming back to — is that linear independence is about **non-redundancy of information**. Each independent vector contributes something the others cannot: it points in a genuinely new direction, it expands the span, it increases the rank by 1.

A basis is a maximally efficient description of a subspace: independent (no redundancy), spanning (no gaps). Remove a vector and you lose coverage. Add a vector and you introduce redundancy.

The whole theory of dimension, rank, and bases rests on this. If I understand dependence, I understand why $\mathbb{R}^n$ has dimension $n$ and not something else — because any $n+1$ vectors must be dependent, and $n$ independent vectors always exist.
