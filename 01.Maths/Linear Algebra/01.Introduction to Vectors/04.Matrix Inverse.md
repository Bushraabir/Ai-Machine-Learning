Let $A$ be an $n \times n$ matrix. A matrix $B$ is called the **inverse** of $A$ if:
$$AB = BA = I_n$$
When such $B$ exists, $A$ is called **invertible** (or nonsingular), and we write $B = A^{-1}$.
**The inverse is unique.** Suppose $B$ and $C$ both satisfy $AB = I$ and $CA = I$. Then: $C = CI = C(AB) = (CA)B = IB = B$
So there is at most one inverse. We can speak of _the_ inverse.
**One-sided inverses suffice for square matrices.** If $A$ is $n \times n$ and $AB = I$ for some $B$, then automatically $BA = I$ as well. This is not obvious and requires rank arguments:

_Proof._ $AB = I$ implies $\text{rank}(A) \geq \text{rank}(AB) = \text{rank}(I) = n$, so $\text{rank}(A) = n$, so $A$ has trivial null space, so $Ax = 0 \implies x = 0$. Now consider $BA$: for any $x$, $A(BAx) = (AB)Ax = Ax$, so $A(BAx - x) = 0$, so $BAx - x = 0$, so $BAx = x$. Thus $BA = I$. $\blacksquare$

_This is why the one-sided inverse condition is enough for square matrices, and why left-inverse and right-inverse automatically coincide._

## The Invertible Matrix Theorem

For a square $n \times n$ matrix $A$, the following are all equivalent. If any one holds, all hold; if any one fails, all fail.

1. $A$ is invertible.
2. $Ax = \mathbf{0}$ has only the trivial solution $x = \mathbf{0}$.
3. The RREF of $A$ is $I_n$.
4. $A$ is a product of elementary matrices.
5. $Ax = \mathbf{b}$ has a unique solution for every $\mathbf{b} \in \mathbb{R}^n$.
6. $\text{rank}(A) = n$.
7. $\text{null}(A) = {\mathbf{0}}$ (trivial null space).
8. The columns of $A$ are linearly independent.
9. The columns of $A$ span $\mathbb{R}^n$.
10. The rows of $A$ are linearly independent.
11. $\det(A) \neq 0$.
12. $0$ is not an eigenvalue of $A$.
13. $A^T$ is invertible.

I will not prove all of these here, but the logical skeleton is: (1) $\Leftrightarrow$ (3) via Gauss-Jordan; (3) $\Leftrightarrow$ (2) $\Leftrightarrow$ (7) $\Leftrightarrow$ (8) via rank-nullity; (8) $\Leftrightarrow$ (9) since both say $\text{rank}(A) = n$; (11) $\Leftrightarrow$ (1) via the determinant formula; (12) $\Leftrightarrow$ (11) since $\det(A) = \prod_i \lambda_i$. The whole theorem is one big equivalence class.

## Algebraic Properties

**(i) Reverse order law:**

$$(AB)^{-1} = B^{-1} A^{-1}$$

_Proof._ $(AB)(B^{-1}A^{-1}) = A(BB^{-1})A^{-1} = AIA^{-1} = I$. $\blacksquare$

This extends: $(A_1 A_2 \cdots A_k)^{-1} = A_k^{-1} \cdots A_2^{-1} A_1^{-1}$.

**(ii) Inverse of transpose:**

$$(A^T)^{-1} = (A^{-1})^T$$

_Proof._ $(A^T)(A^{-1})^T = (A^{-1} A)^T = I^T = I$. $\blacksquare$

This is why the notation $A^{-T}$ is unambiguous: it does not matter whether you transpose first or invert first.

**(iii) Inverse of inverse:**

$$(A^{-1})^{-1} = A$$

Obvious from the definition: $A \cdot A^{-1} = I$ says $A$ is the inverse of $A^{-1}$.

**(iv) Inverse of scalar multiple:**

$$(\alpha A)^{-1} = \frac{1}{\alpha} A^{-1} \quad (\alpha \neq 0)$$

**(v) Powers:**

$$A^{-k} := (A^{-1})^k = (A^k)^{-1}$$

## Formulas for Small Cases

### $1 \times 1$:
Trivially: if $A = (a)$, then $A^{-1} = (1/a)$ provided $a \neq 0$.

### $2 \times 2$:
$$A = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}, \qquad A^{-1} = \frac{1}{ad - bc} \begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix}$$
provided $\det(A) = ad - bc \neq 0$.
The pattern: swap the diagonal entries, negate the off-diagonal entries, divide by the determinant. Worth memorizing.

_Proof._ Direct multiplication:
$$\frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \end{pmatrix}\begin{pmatrix} d & -b \\ -c & a \end{pmatrix} = \frac{1}{ad-bc}\begin{pmatrix} ad-bc & 0 \\ 0 & ad-bc \end{pmatrix} = I. \quad \checkmark$$

### $n \times n$: The Adjugate Formula:
For general $n$:
$$A^{-1} = \frac{1}{\det(A)} \text{adj}(A)$$
where $\text{adj}(A)$ is the **adjugate** (classical adjoint): the transpose of the cofactor matrix.

The $(i,j)$ entry of $\text{adj}(A)$ is the $(j,i)$ cofactor of $A$: $[\text{adj}(A)]_{ij} = C_{ji} = (-1)^{j+i} M_{ji}$ where $M_{ji}$ is the $(j,i)$ minor (determinant of $A$ with row $j$ and column $i$ deleted).This formula is theoretically important (it shows $A^{-1}$ depends rationally on the entries of $A$) but computationally terrible for $n \geq 4$. Never use it numerically. Use [[06.Gauss–Jordan Elimination|Gauss-Jordan.]]

## Computing $A^{-1}$: Gauss-Jordan

The practical method. Augment $A$ with $I$ and row reduce until the left block is $I$:
$$[A \mid I] \xrightarrow{\text{row ops}} [I \mid A^{-1}]$$

The elementary matrices $E_1, E_2, \ldots, E_p$ encoding the row operations satisfy $E_p \cdots E_1 A = I$, so $E_p \cdots E_1 = A^{-1}$. Applying the same sequence to $I$ on the right gives $A^{-1}$.

If at any point during row reduction the left block develops a zero row, $A$ is singular. Stop.

**Cost:** $O(n^3)$ operations. This is the same order as matrix multiplication, and it is optimal up to constants.

## The Determinant Criterion

$A$ is invertible if and only if $\det(A) \neq 0$.
This follows from two facts:
- $\det(AB) = \det(A)\det(B)$
- $\det(I) = 1$

If $AB = I$ then $\det(A)\det(B) = 1$, so $\det(A) \neq 0$. Conversely, the adjugate formula gives an explicit inverse when $\det(A) \neq 0$.

## The Rank-Nullity View

For $A : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ (viewing $A$ as a linear map):

$$\text{rank}(A) + \text{nullity}(A) = n$$

$A$ is invertible if $\text{nullity}(A) = 0$ if $\text{rank}(A) = n$.

This is the clean way to think about it geometrically. Invertibility means $A$ neither collapses any direction (injective) nor misses any direction (surjective). For linear maps on finite-dimensional spaces, these two conditions are equivalent.

## Inverses of Special Matrices

**Diagonal matrix:**
$$D = \begin{pmatrix} d_1 & & \ & \ddots & \ & & d_n \end{pmatrix}, \qquad D^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/d_1 & & \ & \ddots & \ & & 1/d_n \end{pmatrix}$$

provided all $d_i \neq 0$.
**Orthogonal matrix** ($Q^T Q = I$, i.e., columns are orthonormal):
$$Q^{-1} = Q^T$$
This is the nicest possible inverse - computing it costs nothing, it's just a transpose.

**Lower triangular matrix:**

If $L$ is lower triangular with nonzero diagonal entries $l_{11}, \ldots, l_{nn}$, then $L^{-1}$ is also lower triangular, and can be found by forward substitution. The diagonal entries of $L^{-1}$ are $1/l_{ii}$.

**Upper triangular matrix:** Same story, backward substitution, $U^{-1}$ is upper triangular.

**Block diagonal matrix:**
$$\begin{pmatrix} A_1 & & \\ & \ddots & \\ & & A_k \end{pmatrix}^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix} A_1^{-1} & & \\ & \ddots & \\ & & A_k^{-1} \end{pmatrix}$$

provided each block $A_i$ is individually invertible.

**Block matrix ([[08.Schur Complement]]):**
$$\begin{pmatrix} A & B \\ C & D \end{pmatrix}^{-1}$$
requires the Schur complement $S = D - CA^{-1}B$ (assuming $A$ invertible):
$$= \begin{pmatrix} A^{-1} + A^{-1}B S^{-1} C A^{-1} & -A^{-1}B S^{-1} \\ -S^{-1}CA^{-1} & S^{-1} \end{pmatrix}$$

This shows up everywhere in multivariate Gaussian conditioning and GLS. The Schur complement $S = D - CA^{-1}B$ is the key object.

## The Sherman-Morrison Formula

A rank-1 update to an invertible matrix:
$$(A + \mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T)^{-1} = A^{-1} - \frac{A^{-1}\mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T A^{-1}}{1 + \mathbf{v}^T A^{-1} \mathbf{u}}$$

provided $1 + \mathbf{v}^T A^{-1} \mathbf{u} \neq 0$.

_Proof._ Multiply $(A + \mathbf{u}\mathbf{v}^T)$ by the right-hand side and verify you get $I$. The key step uses the scalar $1 + \mathbf{v}^T A^{-1} \mathbf{u}$ to collapse the rank-1 correction. $\blacksquare$

**Why this matters:** if you already have $A^{-1}$ and you perturb $A$ by a rank-1 matrix, you get the new inverse in $O(n^2)$ time instead of $O(n^3)$. This is the computational foundation of sequential updates in statistics (online learning, recursive least squares, the Kalman filter).

The generalization to rank-$k$ updates is the Woodbury identity:

$$(A + UCV)^{-1} = A^{-1} - A^{-1}U(C^{-1} + VA^{-1}U)^{-1}VA^{-1}$$

## Invertibility and the Determinant: A Deeper Note

The determinant is a polynomial in the entries of $A$. The set of singular matrices is ${A : \det(A) = 0}$, a closed set of measure zero in $\mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$. In other words, a random matrix (say with i.i.d. Gaussian entries) is invertible with probability 1. Singularity is the exceptional case, not the rule.

This has a practical implication: if you see a nearly-singular matrix in computation (determinant very small but not exactly zero), the issue is usually numerical conditioning, not theoretical non-invertibility. The **condition number** $\kappa(A) = |A| |A^{-1}|$ measures how close $A$ is to being singular from a numerical standpoint.

## When There Is No Inverse: The Pseudoinverse

For a non-square or singular matrix, the ordinary inverse does not exist. The **Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse** $A^+$ generalizes the inverse:

- If $A$ has full column rank: $A^+ = (A^T A)^{-1} A^T$ (the left inverse, used in least squares).
- If $A$ has full row rank: $A^+ = A^T (A A^T)^{-1}$ (the right inverse).
- In general: $A^+ = V \Sigma^+ U^T$ where $A = U\Sigma V^T$ is the SVD and $\Sigma^+$ replaces each nonzero singular value $\sigma_i$ by $1/\sigma_i$.

The pseudoinverse gives the minimum-norm least-squares solution to $Ax = b$: $x = A^+ b$.

---

## What Fails Without Invertibility

If $A$ is singular:

- $Ax = b$ either has no solution or infinitely many.
- $Ax = 0$ has a nontrivial solution - $A$ collapses some direction entirely.
- The transformation $x \mapsto Ax$ is not bijective: information is lost.
- In probability: if $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \Sigma)$ and $\Sigma$ is singular, the distribution is degenerate - it lives on a lower-dimensional affine subspace and has no density with respect to Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}^n$.

## A Proof I Want to Keep

**Claim:** If $A$ and $B$ are $n \times n$ and $AB = I$, then $A$ is invertible and $B = A^{-1}$.

_Proof._ $AB = I$ implies $\text{rank}(AB) = n$. But $\text{rank}(AB) \leq \text{rank}(A) \leq n$, so $\text{rank}(A) = n$. Thus $A$ is invertible; let $C = A^{-1}$. Then $B = IB = (CA)B = C(AB) = CI = C = A^{-1}$. $\blacksquare$

The same argument with left and right swapped shows: $BA = I \implies B = A^{-1}$ as well.
