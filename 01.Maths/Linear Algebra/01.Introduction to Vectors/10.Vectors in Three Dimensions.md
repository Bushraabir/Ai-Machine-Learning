Two dimensions is where you build intuition. Three dimensions is where you test it. The jump from $\mathbb{R}^2$ to $\mathbb{R}^3$ introduces something genuinely new — the cross product, which has no analogue in any other dimension except $\mathbb{R}^7$, and a richer geometry of planes, lines, and their intersections. I want to nail this down carefully before moving to $\mathbb{R}^n$, because the geometric pictures formed here are the ones I will invoke forever after._

---

## Coordinates and Notation

A vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$:

$$\mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} v_1 \ v_2 \ v_3 \end{pmatrix} = v_1 \mathbf{e}_1 + v_2 \mathbf{e}_2 + v_3 \mathbf{e}_3$$

where the standard basis vectors are:

$$\mathbf{e}_1 = \begin{pmatrix}1\0\0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{e}_2 = \begin{pmatrix}0\1\0\end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{e}_3 = \begin{pmatrix}0\0\1\end{pmatrix}$$

also written $\hat{\mathbf{i}}, \hat{\mathbf{j}}, \hat{\mathbf{k}}$ in physics notation. I will use the column vector form throughout.

The **length** (Euclidean norm) of $\mathbf{v}$:

$$|\mathbf{v}| = \sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2 + v_3^2}$$

This is Pythagoras applied twice: first in the $x$-$y$ plane to get $\sqrt{v_1^2 + v_2^2}$, then in the vertical plane to get $\sqrt{(\sqrt{v_1^2+v_2^2})^2 + v_3^2}$.

A **unit vector** has $|\mathbf{v}| = 1$. The unit vector in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$:

$$\hat{\mathbf{v}} = \frac{\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}$$

## The Dot Product

**Definition.** For $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}^T \mathbf{v} = u_1 v_1 + u_2 v_2 + u_3 v_3$$

This is a scalar.

**Geometric interpretation.** The dot product encodes the angle $\theta$ between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}| |\mathbf{v}| \cos\theta$$

_Proof._ Place $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ tail-to-tail. The triangle with sides $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{v}$, and $\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}$ has:

$$|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta \quad \text{(law of cosines)}$$

But also:

$$|\mathbf{v} - \mathbf{u}|^2 = (\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u})^T(\mathbf{v}-\mathbf{u}) = |\mathbf{v}|^2 - 2\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} + |\mathbf{u}|^2$$

Equating: $\mathbf{u}^T\mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$. $\blacksquare$

**Consequences:**

- $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0 \iff \theta = 90° \iff \mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{v}$.
- $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} > 0 \iff \theta < 90°$ (vectors on the same side).
- $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} < 0 \iff \theta > 90°$ (vectors on opposite sides).
- $\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{v}|^2$ always.

**Algebraic properties:**

- Symmetry: $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{u}$
- Linearity in each argument: $(c\mathbf{u}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = c(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})$ and $(\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{w}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w} \cdot \mathbf{v}$
- Positive definiteness: $\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v} \geq 0$, with equality iff $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$

These three properties make the dot product an **inner product** on $\mathbb{R}^3$.

### The Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

$$|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| |\mathbf{v}|$$

with equality iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are parallel (one is a scalar multiple of the other).

_Proof._ From the geometric formula $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$ and $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$. For a purely algebraic proof not relying on the angle interpretation: consider $f(t) = |\mathbf{u} + t\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2t(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + t^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 \geq 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. This quadratic in $t$ is nonneg­ative everywhere, so its discriminant is $\leq 0$: $4(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 - 4|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 \leq 0$. $\blacksquare$

## Projection

The **projection of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$** is the component of $\mathbf{u}$ in the direction of $\mathbf{v}$:

$$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}} \mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v}} \mathbf{v} = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2} \mathbf{v}$$

The scalar $\dfrac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|}$ is the **scalar projection** (signed length of the shadow of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$).

The **rejection** (component of $\mathbf{u}$ perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}$):

$$\mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}} \mathbf{u}$$

These two pieces are orthogonal and sum to $\mathbf{u}$. This orthogonal decomposition is the seed of all of least squares theory.

## The Cross Product

This is the operation that is genuinely new in $\mathbb{R}^3$.

**Definition.** For $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^3$:

$$\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \begin{pmatrix} u_2 v_3 - u_3 v_2 \ u_3 v_1 - u_1 v_3 \ u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1 \end{pmatrix}$$

The mnemonic: expand the determinant

$$\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \det \begin{pmatrix} \mathbf{e}_1 & \mathbf{e}_2 & \mathbf{e}_3 \ u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

along the first row. Keep this formula — it is both the definition and the easiest way to compute.

**Geometric interpretation.**

1. $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$ is **orthogonal to both $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$**:

$$(\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}) \cdot \mathbf{u} = 0, \qquad (\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$$

_Proof of first._ Direct computation: $(u_2v_3 - u_3v_2)u_1 + (u_3v_1 - u_1v_3)u_2 + (u_1v_2 - u_2v_1)u_3 = u_1u_2v_3 - u_1u_3v_2 + u_2u_3v_1 - u_1u_2v_3 + u_1u_3v_2 - u_2u_3v_1 = 0$. $\blacksquare$

2. The **magnitude**:

$$|\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{u}| |\mathbf{v}| \sin\theta$$

where $\theta \in [0, \pi]$ is the angle between $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$.

This equals the area of the parallelogram spanned by $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$. (Base $|\mathbf{u}|$, height $|\mathbf{v}|\sin\theta$.)

3. The **direction**: $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$ points in the direction given by the right-hand rule. Curl the fingers of the right hand from $\mathbf{u}$ toward $\mathbf{v}$; the thumb points in the direction of $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$.

**Algebraic properties:**

- Anti-symmetry: $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = -(\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{u})$. Order matters.
- Linearity in each argument: $(c\mathbf{u}) \times \mathbf{v} = c(\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v})$ and $(\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{w})\times\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} + \mathbf{w}\times\mathbf{v}$.
- $\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ for any $\mathbf{v}$.
- $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} \iff \mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are parallel (or one is zero).
- **Not associative:** $(\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}) \times \mathbf{w} \neq \mathbf{u} \times (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w})$ in general.

**Standard basis cross products:**

$$\mathbf{e}_1 \times \mathbf{e}_2 = \mathbf{e}_3, \qquad \mathbf{e}_2 \times \mathbf{e}_3 = \mathbf{e}_1, \qquad \mathbf{e}_3 \times \mathbf{e}_1 = \mathbf{e}_2$$

and reversing each gives a minus sign. Think of the cycle $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 1$: going around the cycle forward gives $+$, backward gives $-$.

## The Triple Products

### Scalar Triple Product

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}) = \det \begin{pmatrix} u_1 & u_2 & u_3 \ v_1 & v_2 & v_3 \ w_1 & w_2 & w_3 \end{pmatrix}$$

**Geometric meaning:** the signed volume of the parallelepiped spanned by $\mathbf{u}$, $\mathbf{v}$, $\mathbf{w}$. The sign is positive if $(\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w})$ form a right-handed triple, negative if left-handed.

**Key property:** cyclic permutations preserve the value:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}) = \mathbf{v} \cdot (\mathbf{w} \times \mathbf{u}) = \mathbf{w} \cdot (\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v})$$

and swapping any two adjacent vectors flips the sign (since the determinant changes sign under row swap).

**Consequence:** $\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}) = 0$ iff the three vectors are coplanar (the parallelepiped has zero volume, i.e., $\mathbf{u}$ lives in the plane spanned by $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$).

### Vector Triple Product

$$\mathbf{u} \times (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}) = (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{w})\mathbf{v} - (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})\mathbf{w}$$

This is the **BAC-CAB rule**: $\mathbf{A} \times (\mathbf{B} \times \mathbf{C}) = \mathbf{B}(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{C}) - \mathbf{C}(\mathbf{A}\cdot\mathbf{B})$.

Note that $\mathbf{u} \times (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w})$ lies in the plane spanned by $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$ — it is a linear combination of $\mathbf{v}$ and $\mathbf{w}$, as the formula makes explicit.

## Lines in $\mathbb{R}^3$

A line is determined by a point $\mathbf{p}$ on it and a direction vector $\mathbf{d}$:

$$\ell = {\mathbf{p} + t\mathbf{d} : t \in \mathbb{R}}$$

This is a **parametric** description: varying $t$ over $\mathbb{R}$ traces out the entire line.

Two lines in $\mathbb{R}^3$ can be:

- **Intersecting:** they meet at exactly one point.
- **Parallel:** same direction, no intersection.
- **Skew:** different directions, no intersection, not in the same plane. (This cannot happen in $\mathbb{R}^2$.)

The distance between two skew lines with directions $\mathbf{d}_1$, $\mathbf{d}_2$ passing through points $\mathbf{p}_1$, $\mathbf{p}_2$:

$$\text{dist} = \frac{|(\mathbf{p}_2 - \mathbf{p}_1) \cdot (\mathbf{d}_1 \times \mathbf{d}_2)|}{|\mathbf{d}_1 \times \mathbf{d}_2|}$$

The numerator is the scalar triple product — the component of $\mathbf{p}_2 - \mathbf{p}_1$ along the common perpendicular direction $\mathbf{d}_1 \times \mathbf{d}_2$.

---

## Planes in $\mathbb{R}^3$

A plane is determined by a point $\mathbf{p}_0$ on it and a **normal vector** $\mathbf{n}$ perpendicular to it:

$$\mathbf{n} \cdot (\mathbf{x} - \mathbf{p}_0) = 0$$

Expanding: $n_1 x_1 + n_2 x_2 + n_3 x_3 = n_1 p_{01} + n_2 p_{02} + n_3 p_{03} = d$, giving the standard form:

$$n_1 x_1 + n_2 x_2 + n_3 x_3 = d$$

The normal vector $\mathbf{n} = (n_1, n_2, n_3)^T$ is read off directly from the coefficients.

**Finding the normal from two vectors in the plane:** if $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ lie in the plane, then $\mathbf{n} = \mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$ is normal to the plane. This is the main computational use of the cross product.

**Distance from a point to a plane.** Distance from $\mathbf{q}$ to the plane $\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{x} = d$:

$$\text{dist} = \frac{|\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{q} - d|}{|\mathbf{n}|}$$

_Derivation._ The signed distance is the scalar projection of $\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}_0$ onto $\hat{\mathbf{n}}$:

$$\frac{(\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{p}_0) \cdot \mathbf{n}}{|\mathbf{n}|} = \frac{\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{q} - \mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{p}_0}{|\mathbf{n}|} = \frac{\mathbf{n}\cdot\mathbf{q} - d}{|\mathbf{n}|}$$

Taking the absolute value gives the unsigned distance. $\blacksquare$

## Linear Independence in $\mathbb{R}^3$

Three vectors $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{w} \in \mathbb{R}^3$ are linearly independent iff:

$$\mathbf{u} \cdot (\mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}) \neq 0$$

i.e., the scalar triple product is nonzero, i.e., the parallelepiped they span has nonzero volume.

Equivalently, they are independent iff the $3 \times 3$ matrix with these vectors as rows (or columns) has nonzero determinant.

The span of three independent vectors in $\mathbb{R}^3$ is all of $\mathbb{R}^3$ — they form a basis.

The span of two independent vectors is a plane through the origin. The span of one nonzero vector is a line through the origin.

---

## The Connection to Determinants

The determinant of a $3 \times 3$ matrix $A = [\mathbf{a}^{(1)} \mid \mathbf{a}^{(2)} \mid \mathbf{a}^{(3)}]$ is:

$$\det(A) = \mathbf{a}^{(1)} \cdot (\mathbf{a}^{(2)} \times \mathbf{a}^{(3)})$$

This is the signed volume of the parallelepiped spanned by the three columns. Three facts follow immediately:

1. $\det(A) = 0$ iff the columns are linearly dependent (zero volume).
2. $|\det(A)|$ is the volume scaling factor: if you apply $A$ to the unit cube, you get a parallelepiped of volume $|\det(A)|$.
3. $\det(A) > 0$ iff $A$ preserves orientation (right-handed to right-handed); $\det(A) < 0$ iff $A$ reverses orientation.

## Summary of the Two Products

|Property|Dot product $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}$|Cross product $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}$|
|---|---|---|
|Output type|Scalar|Vector in $\mathbb{R}^3$|
|Symmetry|$\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{u}$|$\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v} = -\mathbf{v}\times\mathbf{u}$|
|Zero condition|$\theta = 90°$ (perpendicular)|$\theta = 0°$ or $180°$ (parallel)|
|Magnitude|$\|\mathbf{u}\|\mathbf{v}\|\cos\theta$|$\|\mathbf{u}\|\mathbf{v}\|\sin\theta$|
|Geometric meaning|Projection / angle|Area of parallelogram / normal direction|
|Generalizes to $\mathbb{R}^n$?|Yes (inner product)|No (only $\mathbb{R}^3$ and $\mathbb{R}^7$)|

## Things to Not Confuse

- $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$ means $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are **parallel**. $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$ means they are **perpendicular**. These are opposite extremes.
- The cross product is **not associative**. Do not write $\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v} \times \mathbf{w}$ without parentheses — it is ambiguous.
- The normal to a plane $n_1x_1 + n_2x_2 + n_3x_3 = d$ is $(n_1, n_2, n_3)^T$. The plane itself is not through the origin unless $d = 0$.
- A line in $\mathbb{R}^3$ cannot be described by a single linear equation. A single linear equation $\mathbf{n} \cdot \mathbf{x} = d$ describes a **plane**. A line requires two such equations (intersection of two planes) or a parametric form.
