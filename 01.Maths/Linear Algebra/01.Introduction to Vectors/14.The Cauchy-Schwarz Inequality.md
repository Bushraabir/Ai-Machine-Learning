>[!theorem]
For any $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:
$$\boxed{|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}|,|\mathbf{v}|}$$
>
with equality if and only if $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are **linearly dependent** — that is, one is a scalar multiple of the other, or one is zero.
>
Squaring both sides (valid since both sides are nonnegative):
>
$$(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2,|\mathbf{v}|^2 = (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{u})(\mathbf{v} \cdot \mathbf{v})$$
>
The squared form is cleaner to work with algebraically and is often what appears in proofs.

## What This Says Geometrically

Recall that the dot product satisfies $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|\cos\theta$, where $\theta$ is the angle between the two vectors.
Cauchy–Schwarz is nothing more than the statement: $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$

Every form of this inequality in every setting is a version of this statement. The angle between two vectors is real, so its cosine cannot exceed 1 in absolute value. The rest is generalizations to infinite dimensions and abstract spaces.

## Proof 1:
This is the proof that generalizes furthest. It works in any inner product space, not just $\mathbb{R}^n$. It requires only that the inner product is **positive definite**.

**Setup.** If $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$, both sides are zero — trivial. Assume $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$.

Define $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ by: $f(t) = |\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}|^2$ -> This is the **squared distance** from $\mathbf{u}$ to the vector $t\mathbf{v}$ on the line through $\mathbf{v}$.

**Key observation.** Since we are squaring a length: $f(t) \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } t \in \mathbb{R}$
**Expand.** Using bi-linearity of the inner product: $f(t) = (\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}) \cdot (\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}) = |\mathbf{u}|^2 - 2t(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}) + t^2|\mathbf{v}|^2$
Rewriting in standard form $at^2 + bt + c$:$f(t) = \underbrace{|\mathbf{v}|^2}_{a}, t^2 - \underbrace{2(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})}_{-b}, t + \underbrace{|\mathbf{u}|^2}_{c}$
**Apply the discriminant condition.** This is a quadratic in $t$ with positive leading coefficient $a = |\mathbf{v}|^2 > 0$. A nonnegative quadratic with positive leading coefficient must have non-positive discriminant: $b^2 - 4ac \leq 0$
![[Pasted image 20260409202413.png]]

**Substitute.** With $a = |\mathbf{v}|^2$, $b = -2(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})$, $c = |\mathbf{u}|^2$:
$(-2(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}))^2 - 4,|\mathbf{v}|^2,|\mathbf{u}|^2 \leq 0$
$4(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 - 4|\mathbf{v}|^2|\mathbf{u}|^2 \leq 0$
$(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2,|\mathbf{v}|^2$

Taking square roots of both sides (both sides nonnegative):

$$|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}|,|\mathbf{v}| \qquad \blacksquare$$

## The Equality Conditio: Proof 1 in Detail

This deserves its own careful treatment.

- Equality $|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ occurs when $(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2$, which means the discriminant is **exactly zero**.
Discriminant $= 0$ means the parabola $f(t)$ touches the $t$-axis at exactly one point $t^*$. That means:
$$f(t^*) = 0$$$$ \implies |\mathbf{u} - t^*\mathbf{v}|^2 = 0$$
A squared norm is zero only for the zero vector:

$$\mathbf{u} - t^*\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0} $$$$\implies \mathbf{u} = t^*\mathbf{v}$$
So $\mathbf{u}$ is a scalar multiple of $\mathbf{v}$. They are **linearly dependent**.

**What is $t^*$?** Minimise $f(t)$ by differentiating:
$$f'(t) = 2|\mathbf{v}|^2 t - 2(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}) = 0 $$$$
\implies t^* = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}$$
This is exactly the **scalar projection coefficient**. The projection of $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$ is:
$\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}},\mathbf{u} = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2},\mathbf{v} = t^*\mathbf{v}$
So $t^*$ is asking: how much of $\mathbf{v}$ do I need to best approximate $\mathbf{u}$? If the minimum distance is zero, $\mathbf{u}$ lies exactly on the line spanned by $\mathbf{v}$.

## Proof 2 — Lagrange's Identity (Algebraic, for $\mathbb{R}^n$)

This proof gives more than just the inequality — it gives an **identity** that the inequality follows from.

**Lagrange's Identity:**

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 - (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 = \sum_{1 \leq i < j \leq n} (u_i v_j - u_j v_i)^2$$

**Proof of the identity.** Expand the left side:

$$\left(\sum_i u_i^2\right)!\left(\sum_j v_j^2\right) - \left(\sum_i u_i v_i\right)^2 = \sum_{i,j} u_i^2 v_j^2 - \sum_{i,j} u_i v_i u_j v_j$$
Separate the diagonal ($i = j$) terms, which cancel:
$$= \sum_{i \neq j} u_i^2 v_j^2 - \sum_{i \neq j} u_i u_j v_i v_j$$
Use the symmetry $i \leftrightarrow j$ to write this as:
$$= \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i \neq j}\left(u_i^2 v_j^2 + u_j^2 v_i^2 - 2u_i u_j v_i v_j\right) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i \neq j}(u_i v_j - u_j v_i)^2 = \sum_{i < j}(u_i v_j - u_j v_i)^2 \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Cauchy–Schwarz follows immediately.** The right side is a sum of squares, hence $\geq 0$:
$$|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 - (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 \geq 0 \implies (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2$$

**Equality condition.** Each term $(u_i v_j - u_j v_i)^2 = 0$, meaning $u_i v_j = u_j v_i$ for all $i,j$. This is precisely the condition for $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ to be proportional.

**Special case in $\mathbb{R}^2$.** The sum has exactly one term ($i=1, j=2$):

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 = (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 + (u_1 v_2 - u_2 v_1)^2$$

The second term is $|\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}|^2$ (the square of the cross product magnitude). So this identity says:

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2\cos^2\theta + |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2\sin^2\theta$$

which is just $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$. Cauchy–Schwarz in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is Pythagoras in disguise.

## Proof 3 — The Projection Argument (Most Geometric)

This is the proof that makes everything transparent.

**Decompose $\mathbf{u}$.** Project $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$:

$$\mathbf{u} = \underbrace{\frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}\mathbf{v}}_{\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{e}}_{\perp, \mathbf{v}}$$

where the error vector $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u}$ satisfies $\mathbf{e} \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$ (it is perpendicular to $\mathbf{v}$).

**Apply Pythagoras.** Since $\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u} \perp \mathbf{e}$:

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2 = |\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{e}|^2$$

Since $|\mathbf{e}|^2 \geq 0$:

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2 \geq |\text{proj}_{\mathbf{v}}\mathbf{u}|^2 = \frac{(\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}$$

Multiply both sides by $|\mathbf{v}|^2$:

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 \geq (\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v})^2 \qquad \blacksquare$$
![[Pasted image 20260409203816.png]]
**Why this proof is the right one to understand.** Cauchy–Schwarz says: the shadow of $\mathbf{u}$ on $\mathbf{v}$ is at most as long as $\mathbf{u}$. The shadow is one component of $\mathbf{u}$. A component cannot exceed the whole. This is visually obvious. The proof just makes it rigorous.

## Comparing the Three Proofs

All three proofs are the same idea in different clothing:

|Proof|Key quantity|Why it's $\geq 0$|
|---|---|---|
|Discriminant|$\|\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}\|^2$ as a quadratic|Squared norm|
|Lagrange|$\sum_{i<j}(u_iv_j - u_jv_i)^2$|Sum of squares|
|Projection|$\|\mathbf{e}\|^2 = \|\mathbf{u}\|^2 - \|\text{proj}\|^2$|Squared norm|

In every case, the inequality comes from the nonnegativity of a squared quantity. That is the entire soul of Cauchy–Schwarz: **something nonnegative forces the inequality**.

---

## The Classical Form — Finite Sums

Before inner products existed as a concept, Cauchy (1821) proved the inequality for sums. For real numbers $a_1, \ldots, a_n$ and $b_1, \ldots, b_n$:

$$\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i\right)^2 \leq \left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i^2\right)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n b_i^2\right)$$

This is the vector form with $\mathbf{u} = (a_1, \ldots, a_n)^T$ and $\mathbf{v} = (b_1, \ldots, b_n)^T$. The sum $\sum a_i b_i$ is the dot product.

---

## The Integral Form — $L^2$ Functions

Schwarz (1885) extended the inequality to integrals. For square-integrable functions $f, g : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$:

$$\left(\int_a^b f(x)g(x),dx\right)^2 \leq \left(\int_a^b f(x)^2,dx\right)\left(\int_a^b g(x)^2,dx\right)$$

This is Cauchy–Schwarz in $L^2([a,b])$ with inner product $\langle f, g \rangle = \int_a^b f(x)g(x),dx$.

**The proof is identical.** Define $f(t) = \int_a^b (u(x) - t,v(x))^2,dx \geq 0$ for all $t$. Expand, get a nonnegative quadratic, discriminant $\leq 0$. Word-for-word the discriminant argument.

---

## The Probability Form — Random Variables

For square-integrable random variables $X$ and $Y$:

$$|E[XY]| \leq \sqrt{E[X^2]},\sqrt{E[Y^2]}$$

or in the centred form:

$$|\text{Cov}(X,Y)| \leq \text{SD}(X)\cdot\text{SD}(Y)$$

**Proof.** Define $g(t) = E[(X - tY)^2] \geq 0$ (expectation of a nonnegative quantity). Expand: $g(t) = E[X^2] - 2t,E[XY] + t^2 E[Y^2]$. Nonnegative quadratic, discriminant $\leq 0$:

$$4(E[XY])^2 - 4,E[X^2],E[Y^2] \leq 0 \implies |E[XY]| \leq \sqrt{E[X^2],E[Y^2]} \qquad \blacksquare$$

**The correlation bound.** Applying to $X - E[X]$ and $Y - E[Y]$:

$$|\rho(X,Y)| = \frac{|\text{Cov}(X,Y)|}{\text{SD}(X),\text{SD}(Y)} \leq 1$$

This is why correlation is bounded between $-1$ and $1$. Cauchy–Schwarz is the reason.

**Equality condition.** $X = cY$ almost surely for some constant $c$. Perfect linear dependence.

---

## Cauchy–Schwarz Implies the Triangle Inequality

This is the logical chain that matters.

**Claim.** $|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$.

**Proof.** Expand $|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}|^2$:

$$|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2,\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} + |\mathbf{v}|^2$$

Apply $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} \leq |\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}|$ and then Cauchy–Schwarz:

$$\leq |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| + |\mathbf{v}|^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}| + |\mathbf{v}|^2 = (|\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|)^2$$

Take square roots: $|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$. $\blacksquare$

**The logical order is:**

$$\text{Positive definiteness} \implies \text{Cauchy–Schwarz} \implies \text{Triangle inequality} \implies \text{Norm axioms satisfied}$$

We never need to verify the triangle inequality separately. Cauchy–Schwarz gives it for free.

---

## The Weighted Version

For positive weights $w_1, \ldots, w_n > 0$:

$$\left(\sum_{i=1}^n w_i a_i b_i\right)^2 \leq \left(\sum_{i=1}^n w_i a_i^2\right)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n w_i b_i^2\right)$$

This is Cauchy–Schwarz in the weighted inner product $\langle \mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b} \rangle_w = \sum_i w_i a_i b_i$. The proof is unchanged. In probability, if $X$ and $Y$ are discrete with $P(X = x_i) = w_i$, this is exactly the random-variable form.

## Applications

**Bounding a dot product without the angle.** If $|\mathbf{u}| \leq M$ and $|\mathbf{v}| \leq N$, then $|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq MN$, without needing to know $\theta$.

**AM-GM inequality as a special case.** Take $\mathbf{u} = (\sqrt{a}, \sqrt{b})^T$ and $\mathbf{v} = (\sqrt{b}, \sqrt{a})^T$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$:

$$(\sqrt{a}\sqrt{b} + \sqrt{b}\sqrt{a})^2 \leq (a+b)(b+a) \implies 4ab \leq (a+b)^2 \implies \sqrt{ab} \leq \frac{a+b}{2}$$

**Jensen's inequality for $x^2$.** Cauchy–Schwarz with $Y = 1$:

$$|E[X \cdot 1]|^2 \leq E[X^2],E[1^2] \implies E[X]^2 \leq E[X^2]$$

This is Jensen applied to the convex function $x^2$.

**Hölder's inequality** is the $L^p$-$L^q$ generalisation. Cauchy–Schwarz is the $p = q = 2$ case:

$$|E[XY]| \leq |X|_p,|Y|_q \qquad \text{where } \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1$$

## The Equality Case — Summary

This is worth stating carefully in every form.

|Setting|Equality condition|
|---|---|
|Vectors $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$|$\mathbf{u} = c\mathbf{v}$ for some scalar $c$|
|Sequences $(a_i), (b_i)$|$a_i = c,b_i$ for all $i$|
|Functions $f, g \in L^2$|$f = c,g$ almost everywhere|
|Random variables $X, Y$|$X = cY$ almost surely|
|Correlation|$|

Equality does **not** require $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{v}$. It requires proportionality. The scalar $c$ can be negative — antiparallel vectors also achieve equality.

---

## What Breaks the Inequality

Cauchy–Schwarz relies on **positive definiteness** of the inner product: $\langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{w} \rangle \geq 0$ with equality only at $\mathbf{0}$.

In **Minkowski space** (the inner product of special relativity, signature $(-,+,+,+)$), positive definiteness fails. For timelike vectors, the analogue of Cauchy–Schwarz is **reversed**. This is not a flaw — it is the geometry of spacetime.

The takeaway: Cauchy–Schwarz is not a universal truth. It is a consequence of positive definiteness. Remove that assumption and the inequality can fail.

---

## Common Errors

**Missing the absolute value.** The inequality is $|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ — the absolute value on the left is necessary. Without it, $\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v} \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ is also true but strictly weaker (it only bounds the dot product from above, not from below).

**Confusing equality with $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{v}$.** Equality requires $\mathbf{u} = c\mathbf{v}$, not $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{v}$. Vectors of different lengths pointing in the same direction achieve equality.

**Almost sure vs everywhere.** In the random variable form, equality is $X = cY$ almost surely — a set of measure zero where this fails is permitted. For pathological probability spaces this distinction matters.

**Direction of the inequality.** Cauchy–Schwarz gives an **upper bound** on $|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}|$. It gives no lower bound. The dot product can be anywhere in $[-|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|,, |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|]$.

## Things to Think About

- The discriminant proof used only positive definiteness, bilinearity, and symmetry. Which of these can be weakened? What happens in a **semi-inner product space** where $\langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{w} \rangle = 0$ does not force $\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{0}$?
- The projection proof assumed that the projection formula $t^* = \frac{\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}$ is valid. What is the underlying assumption? Can you derive $t^*$ directly from the condition $(\mathbf{u} - t^*\mathbf{v}) \cdot \mathbf{v} = 0$?
- Lagrange's identity shows that in $\mathbb{R}^2$, Cauchy–Schwarz is $\cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta = 1$. This means Cauchy–Schwarz in $\mathbb{R}^2$ is an identity, not just an inequality. Why does this not contradict the strict inequality that holds when the vectors are not parallel?
- The probability form requires $E[X^2] < \infty$ and $E[Y^2] < \infty$. What goes wrong if $X$ has infinite variance? Is the inequality still true?
