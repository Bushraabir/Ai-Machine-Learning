_This is one of those inequalities that shows up so often, in so many different forms, that you begin to suspect it is not really one theorem but a family of related ideas held together by a common structure. That suspicion is correct. The algebraic proof in $\mathbb{R}^n$ works because of positive definiteness of the inner product. The same proof, word for word, works in $L^2$. And in $L^2$, Cauchy-Schwarz is what bounds correlation, bounds covariance, and underlies every projection argument in probability. I want to understand the inequality deeply enough that I can rediscover it in any setting I encounter it._

---

## Statement

For any $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$:

$$|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$$

with equality iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly dependent (one is a scalar multiple of the other, or one is zero).

Squaring both sides (both sides are nonneg­ative):

$$(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 = (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u})(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v})$$

This squared form is often cleaner to work with algebraically.

---

## Proof 1: The Discriminant Argument

This is the proof I consider canonical. It requires only that the inner product is positive definite, so it works in any inner product space.

If $\mathbf{v} = \mathbf{0}$, both sides are zero and the inequality is trivial. Assume $\mathbf{v} \neq \mathbf{0}$.

Define $f : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ by:

$$f(t) = |\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}|^2 = (\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v})\cdot(\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v})$$

By positive definiteness, $f(t) \geq 0$ for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$. Expanding by bilinearity:

$$f(t) = \mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{u} - 2t(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}) + t^2(\mathbf{v}\cdot\mathbf{v}) = |\mathbf{v}|^2 t^2 - 2(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})t + |\mathbf{u}|^2$$

This is a quadratic in $t$ with positive leading coefficient $|\mathbf{v}|^2 > 0$. A quadratic $at^2 + bt + c$ with $a > 0$ that is nonneg­ative for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$ must have discriminant $\leq 0$:

$$b^2 - 4ac \leq 0$$

Substituting $a = |\mathbf{v}|^2$, $b = -2(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})$, $c = |\mathbf{u}|^2$:

$$4(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 - 4|\mathbf{v}|^2|\mathbf{u}|^2 \leq 0$$

$$(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2$$

Taking square roots (both sides nonneg­ative): $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$. $\blacksquare$

**Equality condition.** The discriminant equals zero iff $f(t) = 0$ for exactly one $t$, i.e., $|\mathbf{u} - t^_\mathbf{v}|^2 = 0$ for some $t^_$, i.e., $\mathbf{u} = t^*\mathbf{v}$. So equality holds iff $\mathbf{u}$ is a scalar multiple of $\mathbf{v}$, i.e., they are linearly dependent. $\blacksquare$

_Note: the optimal $t^_$ is the minimiser of $f$, which by calculus is $t^* = \dfrac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}$. This is exactly the scalar projection coefficient. The proof and the projection formula are the same computation.*

---

## Proof 2: Direct Algebraic (Lagrange's Identity)

For $\mathbb{R}^n$ specifically, there is an identity that proves Cauchy-Schwarz and gives more information.

**Lagrange's Identity:**

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 - (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 = \sum_{1 \leq i < j \leq n} (u_i v_j - u_j v_i)^2$$

_Proof._ Expand both sides. The left side:

$$\left(\sum_i u_i^2\right)\left(\sum_j v_j^2\right) - \left(\sum_i u_i v_i\right)^2$$

$$= \sum_{i,j} u_i^2 v_j^2 - \sum_{i,j} u_i v_i u_j v_j$$

$$= \sum_{i \neq j} u_i^2 v_j^2 - \sum_{i \neq j} u_i u_j v_i v_j$$

$$= \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i \neq j}\left(u_i^2 v_j^2 + u_j^2 v_i^2 - 2u_i u_j v_i v_j\right) = \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i \neq j}(u_i v_j - u_j v_i)^2 = \sum_{i < j}(u_i v_j - u_j v_i)^2 \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Cauchy-Schwarz follows immediately:** the right side is a sum of squares, hence $\geq 0$, so $|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 \geq (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2$.

**Equality condition:** all terms $(u_i v_j - u_j v_i)^2 = 0$, i.e., $u_i v_j = u_j v_i$ for all $i,j$, i.e., $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are proportional.

**In $\mathbb{R}^2$:** the sum has one term, $i=1, j=2$:

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 - (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 = (u_1v_2 - u_2v_1)^2$$

The right side is $|\mathbf{u} \times \mathbf{v}|^2$ (the squared magnitude of the cross product, extended to the $z$-component). In $\mathbb{R}^2$, Cauchy-Schwarz becomes $|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 = (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 + |\mathbf{u}\times\mathbf{v}|^2$, which is just $1 = \cos^2\theta + \sin^2\theta$.

---

## Proof 3: Projection Argument

The cleanest geometric proof. Project $\mathbf{u}$ onto $\mathbf{v}$:

$$\mathbf{u} = \underbrace{\frac{\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}\mathbf{v}}_{\text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}} + \underbrace{\mathbf{e}}_{\perp, \mathbf{v}}$$

where $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{u} - \text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}$ satisfies $\mathbf{e}\cdot\mathbf{v} = 0$. By Pythagoras:

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2 = |\text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}|^2 + |\mathbf{e}|^2 \geq |\text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}|^2 = \frac{(\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2}{|\mathbf{v}|^2}$$

Multiplying both sides by $|\mathbf{v}|^2$:

$$|\mathbf{u}|^2|\mathbf{v}|^2 \geq (\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v})^2 \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Equality** iff $|\mathbf{e}|^2 = 0$ iff $\mathbf{e} = \mathbf{0}$ iff $\mathbf{u} = \text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}$ iff $\mathbf{u}$ is a multiple of $\mathbf{v}$.

_This proof is the one that makes the geometry transparent: Cauchy-Schwarz says the shadow of $\mathbf{u}$ on $\mathbf{v}$ is at most as long as $\mathbf{u}$ itself. Of course it is — the shadow is one component of $\mathbf{u}$, and a component cannot exceed the whole._

---

## Three Proofs, One Idea

All three proofs are the same argument in different clothing:

|Proof|Key step|Geometric reading|
|---|---|---|
|Discriminant|$\|\mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}\|^2 \geq 0$, discriminant $\leq 0$|A squared norm is nonneg­ative|
|Lagrange|$\sum_{i<j}(u_iv_j - u_jv_i)^2 \geq 0$|Sum of squares is nonneg­ative|
|Projection|$\|\mathbf{u}\|^2 \geq \|\text{proj}_\mathbf{v}\mathbf{u}\|^2$|Component $\leq$ whole|

In every case, the inequality comes from nonnegativity of a squared quantity. That is the soul of Cauchy-Schwarz.

## The Inequality for Sums: Classical Form

Before inner products, Cauchy proved the inequality for finite sums and Schwarz extended it to integrals. The finite-sum form: for real numbers $a_1, \ldots, a_n$ and $b_1, \ldots, b_n$:

$$\left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i b_i\right)^2 \leq \left(\sum_{i=1}^n a_i^2\right)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n b_i^2\right)$$

This is exactly the vector form with $\mathbf{u} = (a_1,\ldots,a_n)^T$ and $\mathbf{v} = (b_1,\ldots,b_n)^T$.

---

## The Inequality in $L^2$: The Integral Form

For square-integrable functions $f, g : [a,b] \to \mathbb{R}$:

$$\left(\int_a^b f(x)g(x),dx\right)^2 \leq \left(\int_a^b f(x)^2,dx\right)\left(\int_a^b g(x)^2,dx\right)$$

This is Cauchy-Schwarz in $L^2([a,b])$ with inner product $\langle f, g\rangle = \int_a^b f(x)g(x),dx$. The proof is word-for-word the discriminant argument: $\int_a^b (f - tg)^2,dx \geq 0$ for all $t$, expand, set discriminant $\leq 0$.

---

## The Inequality for Random Variables

For square-integrable random variables $X$ and $Y$ on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$:

$$|E[XY]| \leq \sqrt{E[X^2]}\sqrt{E[Y^2]}$$

or in the centred form (using $\text{Cov}$ and $\text{SD}$):

$$|\text{Cov}(X,Y)| \leq \text{SD}(X),\text{SD}(Y)$$

**Proof.** Same discriminant argument. Consider $g(t) = E[(X - tY)^2] = E[X^2] - 2tE[XY] + t^2E[Y^2] \geq 0$ for all $t$ (expectation of a nonneg­ative random variable). This is a nonneg­ative quadratic in $t$, so:

$$4(E[XY])^2 - 4E[X^2]E[Y^2] \leq 0 \implies |E[XY]| \leq \sqrt{E[X^2]E[Y^2]} \qquad \blacksquare$$

**The correlation bound $|\rho(X,Y)| \leq 1$** is this applied to $X - E[X]$ and $Y - E[Y]$.

Equality iff $X - E[X] = c(Y - E[Y])$ a.s. for some constant $c$, i.e., $X$ and $Y$ are in a perfect (almost sure) linear relationship.

---

## Cauchy-Schwarz and the Triangle Inequality

Cauchy-Schwarz implies the triangle inequality for the norm. For any $\mathbf{u}, \mathbf{v}$:

$$|\mathbf{u} + \mathbf{v}|^2 = |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} + |\mathbf{v}|^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| + |\mathbf{v}|^2 \leq |\mathbf{u}|^2 + 2|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}| + |\mathbf{v}|^2 = (|\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|)^2$$

Taking square roots: $|\mathbf{u}+\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}| + |\mathbf{v}|$.

So the logical order is: positive definiteness of the inner product $\Rightarrow$ Cauchy-Schwarz $\Rightarrow$ triangle inequality $\Rightarrow$ norm axioms are satisfied.

## A Weighted Version

For positive weights $w_1, \ldots, w_n > 0$:

$$\left(\sum_{i=1}^n w_i a_i b_i\right)^2 \leq \left(\sum_{i=1}^n w_i a_i^2\right)\left(\sum_{i=1}^n w_i b_i^2\right)$$

This is Cauchy-Schwarz in the weighted inner product $\langle\mathbf{a}, \mathbf{b}\rangle_w = \sum_i w_i a_i b_i$. The proof is unchanged. In probability: if $X$ and $Y$ are discrete with $P(X = x_i) = w_i$, this is the random variable form $|E[XY]| \leq \sqrt{E[X^2]}\sqrt{E[Y^2]}$.

## Applications

**Bounding a dot product without computing the angle.** If I know $|\mathbf{u}| \leq M$ and $|\mathbf{v}| \leq N$, then $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq MN$ without knowing the angle.

**Correlation is bounded.** $|\rho(X,Y)| \leq 1$ follows directly.

**Bounding expectations.** $|E[XY]| \leq \sqrt{E[X^2]}\sqrt{E[Y^2]}$, used constantly in probability to bound moments.

**The AM-GM inequality** is a special case of Cauchy-Schwarz: take $\mathbf{u} = (\sqrt{a}, \sqrt{b})^T$ and $\mathbf{v} = (\sqrt{b}, \sqrt{a})^T$ in $\mathbb{R}^2$:

$$(\sqrt{a}\sqrt{b} + \sqrt{b}\sqrt{a})^2 \leq (a+b)(b+a) \implies 4ab \leq (a+b)^2 \implies \sqrt{ab} \leq \frac{a+b}{2}$$

**Jensen's inequality** for the square function: $E[X]^2 \leq E[X^2]$. This is Cauchy-Schwarz with $Y = 1$: $|E[X \cdot 1]|^2 \leq E[X^2] \cdot E[1^2] = E[X^2]$.

**Hölder's inequality** is the $L^p$-$L^q$ generalisation of Cauchy-Schwarz ($p=q=2$ gives Cauchy-Schwarz):

$$|E[XY]| \leq |X|_p |Y|_q, \qquad \frac{1}{p} + \frac{1}{q} = 1$$

## The Equality Case: When Does It Bind?

Equality $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| = |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ iff $\mathbf{u}$ and $\mathbf{v}$ are linearly dependent.

In the sum form: $\left(\sum a_ib_i\right)^2 = \left(\sum a_i^2\right)\left(\sum b_i^2\right)$ iff $\exists, c$ such that $a_i = cb_i$ for all $i$.

In the random variable form: $|E[XY]|^2 = E[X^2]E[Y^2]$ iff $X = cY$ almost surely for some constant $c$.

The equality case is often as important as the inequality itself: it tells you the extremal configurations, the cases where the bound is tight. In statistics, $|\rho| = 1$ is the equality case, characterising perfect linear dependence.

---

## What the Inequality Really Says

The Cauchy-Schwarz inequality is the statement that the cosine of an angle is at most 1 in absolute value. Everything else is notational variation.

The deep reason it holds is that the inner product is positive definite. Positive definiteness means $\langle \mathbf{w}, \mathbf{w}\rangle \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{w}$, with equality only at $\mathbf{0}$. Apply this to $\mathbf{w} = \mathbf{u} - t\mathbf{v}$ for the optimal $t$, and Cauchy-Schwarz falls out.

Remove positive definiteness and the inequality can fail. In Minkowski space (the inner product of special relativity, which has signature $(-,+,+,+)$), the analogue of Cauchy-Schwarz is reversed for timelike vectors.

---

## Things to Not Confuse

- The inequality is $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$, with the absolute value on the left. Without it, $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ is also true but weaker (and also follows from Cauchy-Schwarz since $\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v} \leq |\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}|$).
- Equality does not require $\mathbf{u} = \mathbf{v}$. It requires $\mathbf{u} = c\mathbf{v}$ for some scalar $c$, positive or negative.
- In the random variable form, equality is almost sure: $X = cY$ almost everywhere, not everywhere. The negligible set matters for pathological cases.
- The inequality direction: Cauchy-Schwarz gives an upper bound on $|\mathbf{u}\cdot\mathbf{v}|$. It does not give a lower bound. Dot products can be arbitrarily close to $-|\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$.
