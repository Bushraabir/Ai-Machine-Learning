## The Big Picture

The whole game here is this: given $Ax = b$, I want to find $x$. Elimination is the _systematic_ way to do it — turn $A$ into something I can actually work with: an **upper triangular matrix $U$**.

Why upper triangular? Because then back substitution becomes trivial. The last equation has one unknown, second-to-last has two, and so on. Clean and recursive.

The process: $A \xrightarrow{\text{elimination}} U$, and simultaneously $b \xrightarrow{\text{same ops}} c$, giving me $Ux = c$.

---

## The Mechanics

### Pivots and Multipliers

The **pivot** is the leading nonzero entry I'm currently working with. Starting at $a_{11}$:

$$\ell_{i1} = \frac{a_{i1}}{a_{11}}, \quad i = 2, 3, \ldots, n$$

Then I subtract $\ell_{i1}$ times row 1 from row $i$. This zeros out the entire first column below the pivot.

Move to the next pivot $a_{22}$ (now modified), repeat. After $n-1$ stages, I have $U$.

> **Personal note:** The multipliers $\ell_{ij}$ are not throwaway numbers — they're going to come back when I study LU decomposition. They literally _are_ $L$. Don't forget this.

### The Augmented Matrix

To handle $b$ simultaneously, work with $[A \mid b]$. Every row operation applied to $A$ gets applied to $b$ too.

After elimination: $[A \mid b] \longrightarrow [U \mid c]$

Then solve $Ux = c$ by back substitution.

### Back Substitution

From $Ux = c$, starting at the bottom:

$$x_n = \frac{c_n}{u_{nn}}$$

$$x_{n-1} = \frac{c_{n-1} - u_{n-1,n} \cdot x_n}{u_{n-1,n-1}}$$

And so on upward. Clean, explicit, $O(n^2)$.

## Failure Modes

Elimination succeeds iff I get $n$ nonzero pivots. Two failure cases:

| Situation                               | What Happens          | Remedy                         |
| --------------------------------------- | --------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| Zero in pivot position, nonzero below   | **Temporary failure** | Row exchange (permutation)     |
| Zero in pivot position, all zeros below | **Complete failure**  | Matrix is singular — no remedy |

The second case is not just computational — it's telling me something deep: $A$ is not invertible. The columns are linearly dependent. There's a nontrivial null space. These are all the same fact.

## Elimination as Matrix Multiplication

This is where it gets beautiful. Each elimination step is _itself_ a matrix.

### Elementary Matrices $E_{ij}$

The operation "subtract $\ell \cdot \text{row } j$ from row $i$" corresponds to left-multiplication by:

$$E_{ij} = I - \ell \cdot e_i e_j^T$$

where $e_i, e_j$ are standard basis vectors. Explicitly, $E_{ij}$ looks like the identity matrix with $-\ell$ in position $(i,j)$.

**Example:** Subtract 3 times row 1 from row 2:

$$E_{21} = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 \ -3 & 1 & 0 \ 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

Left-multiply: $E_{21} A$ does exactly that row operation.

> **Key insight I need to remember:** Left-multiplication = row operations. Right-multiplication = column operations. This distinction matters everywhere.

### The Full Elimination Chain

$$E_{32} E_{31} E_{21} A = U$$

By the **associative law** (which holds for matrix multiplication, though commutativity doesn't):

$$(E_{32} E_{31} E_{21}) A = U$$

So if I define $E = E_{32} E_{31} E_{21}$, then $EA = U$. This is one step away from LU decomposition.

### Permutation Matrices $P$

Row exchanges are handled by **permutation matrices** — obtained by permuting rows of $I$.

Key properties:

- $P^T = P^{-1}$ (permutation matrices are orthogonal)
- $P \cdot A$ permutes the rows of $A$

If row exchanges are needed, the full elimination chain becomes $PA = LU$. That's the _general_ factorization.

---

## Inverses of Elementary Matrices

This is elegant: $E_{ij}$ subtracts $\ell$ times row $j$ from row $i$. To undo that:

$$E_{ij}^{-1} = I + \ell \cdot e_i e_j^T$$

i.e., _add_ $\ell$ times row $j$ back to row $i$. The inverse just flips the sign of the off-diagonal entry.

This means elimination is fully reversible — of course it is, since I haven't changed the solution set.

The deeper point: since $EA = U$, we get $A = E^{-1} U = LU$ where $L = E^{-1}$ is **lower triangular**. The multipliers $\ell_{ij}$ slot directly into the lower triangle of $L$. This is the **LU decomposition**, and it's just elimination with bookkeeping.

---

## The Proof I Want to Remember

**Claim:** If no row exchanges are needed, then $A = LU$ where $L$ is unit lower triangular with entries $\ell_{ij}$ (the elimination multipliers) and $U$ is upper triangular.

**Sketch:** Elimination produces $E_{n-1} \cdots E_1 A = U$ where each $E_k$ is an elementary matrix. Therefore $A = E_1^{-1} \cdots E_{n-1}^{-1} U$. Define $L = E_1^{-1} \cdots E_{n-1}^{-1}$. Since inverses of lower triangular elementary matrices are lower triangular, and products of lower triangular matrices are lower triangular, $L$ is lower triangular. The $(i,j)$ entry of $L$ for $i > j$ is exactly $\ell_{ij}$ — the multiplier used to eliminate position $(i,j)$. The diagonal entries of $L$ are all 1 (unit lower triangular). $\blacksquare$

> The miracle: the multipliers don't interact. Even though I'm composing many elementary matrices, the off-diagonal entries of $L$ are just the raw multipliers, not some complicated product. This is why LU is so computationally clean.
