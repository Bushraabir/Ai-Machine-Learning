
> _This is the computation I come back to whenever I need a reminder that indicator r.v.'s + covariance is the right tool. The variance formula has that haunting factor $(N-n)/(N-1)$ — the finite population correction — and deriving it cleanly via covariance of indicators is the only proof worth knowing._

---

## The Setup

You have an urn with $N$ balls: $K$ are white (successes), $N-K$ are black (failures). Draw $n$ balls **without replacement**. Let: $$X = \text{number of white balls drawn}$$

We want $\text{Var}(X)$.

**Indicator decomposition** — the move that makes everything tractable: $$X = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n, \qquad X_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{ball } i \text{ is white}]$$

where "ball $i$" means the $i$-th ball drawn.

---

## Mean (Warm-Up)

By symmetry, every draw is equally likely to be any of the $N$ balls, so: $$\mathbb{E}[X_i] = \mathbb{P}(\text{draw } i \text{ is white}) = \frac{K}{N}$$

By linearity: $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] = n \cdot \frac{K}{N} = \frac{nK}{N}$$

This is the expected number of white balls. Intuition: if you draw $n$ from $N$ balls of which $K$ are white, you'd expect fraction $K/N$ to be white. Perfect.

---

## Variance: The Covariance Decomposition

Since $X = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$, the variance formula for a sum gives:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) + 2\sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$$

There are $n$ diagonal terms and $\binom{n}{2}$ off-diagonal covariance terms. By symmetry, all diagonal terms are equal and all off-diagonal terms are equal. So:

$$\text{Var}(X) = n,\text{Var}(X_1) + n(n-1),\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2)$$

(The factor $2\binom{n}{2} = n(n-1)$ accounts for all ordered pairs $i \neq j$, each contributing $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$ once.)

Now compute each piece.

---

## Computing $\text{Var}(X_1)$

$X_1 \sim \text{Bernoulli}(K/N)$, so: $$\text{Var}(X_1) = \frac{K}{N}\left(1 - \frac{K}{N}\right) = \frac{K(N-K)}{N^2}$$

---

## Computing $\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2)$

Use the computational formula: $$\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2) = \mathbb{E}[X_1 X_2] - \mathbb{E}[X_1]\mathbb{E}[X_2]$$

**$\mathbb{E}[X_1]\mathbb{E}[X_2]$:** Both equal $K/N$, so this is $K^2/N^2$.

**$\mathbb{E}[X_1 X_2]$:** Since $X_1, X_2$ are indicators, $X_1 X_2 = 1$ iff both draws are white: $$\mathbb{E}[X_1 X_2] = \mathbb{P}(X_1 = 1 \text{ and } X_2 = 1)$$

$$= \mathbb{P}(\text{draw 1 is white}) \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{draw 2 is white} \mid \text{draw 1 is white})$$

$$= \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{K-1}{N-1}$$

This is where **without replacement** bites: after drawing one white ball, there are $K-1$ white balls left among $N-1$ remaining.

Therefore: $$\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2) = \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{K-1}{N-1} - \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{K}{N}$$

$$= \frac{K}{N}\left(\frac{K-1}{N-1} - \frac{K}{N}\right)$$

$$= \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{N(K-1) - K(N-1)}{N(N-1)}$$

Expand the numerator: $$N(K-1) - K(N-1) = NK - N - KN + K = K - N = -(N-K)$$

So: $$\text{Cov}(X_1, X_2) = \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{-(N-K)}{N(N-1)} = -\frac{K(N-K)}{N^2(N-1)}$$

> _The covariance is negative. This makes sense — drawing without replacement means seeing a white ball makes seeing another white ball slightly less likely. The balls are competing for the white "slots."_

---

## Assembling the Variance

$$\text{Var}(X) = n \cdot \frac{K(N-K)}{N^2} + n(n-1) \cdot \left(-\frac{K(N-K)}{N^2(N-1)}\right)$$

Factor out $\frac{nK(N-K)}{N^2}$:

$$= \frac{nK(N-K)}{N^2}\left(1 - \frac{n-1}{N-1}\right)$$

Simplify the bracket: $$1 - \frac{n-1}{N-1} = \frac{N-1 - (n-1)}{N-1} = \frac{N-n}{N-1}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = n \cdot \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{N-K}{N} \cdot \frac{N-n}{N-1}}$$

---

## The Finite Population Correction Factor

Write $p = K/N$ (the proportion of white balls). Then:

$$\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p) \cdot \underbrace{\frac{N-n}{N-1}}_{\text{FPC}}$$

Compare to the **Binomial$(n,p)$** variance: $\text{Var}(X_{\text{bin}}) = np(1-p)$.

The hypergeometric variance is the binomial variance **times** the finite population correction (FPC): $$\text{FPC} = \frac{N-n}{N-1} \in [0, 1]$$

The FPC is always $\leq 1$, so the hypergeometric variance is **always less than or equal to** the binomial variance. This is the mathematical expression of a fundamental truth:

> _Drawing without replacement reduces variance. Once you've seen some of the population, there's less uncertainty about the rest. Information accumulates faster without replacement._

**Boundary cases:**

- $n = 1$: FPC $= 1$. A single draw is identical to Bernoulli sampling — with or without replacement makes no difference.
- $n = N$: FPC $= 0$. You've drawn everyone. $X = K$ with certainty. No randomness, zero variance. Correct.
- $N \to \infty$ with $p = K/N$ fixed: FPC $\to 1$. Sampling without replacement from a huge population is essentially the same as with replacement. Hypergeometric $\to$ Binomial.

---

## Why Indicators + Covariance Is the Right Framework

You could also compute $\text{Var}(X)$ directly from the PMF: $$\mathbb{P}(X = k) = \frac{\binom{K}{k}\binom{N-K}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}}$$

and grind through $\mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ using combinatorial identities. It works, but it's brutal and opaque.

The indicator approach:

1. Reduces everything to $\text{Var}(X_i)$ and $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$ — two small, interpretable calculations.
2. Makes the negative covariance visible — you _see_ why without-replacement decreases variance.
3. Generalises immediately. The same framework handles the variance of the number of matches in a random permutation, birthday coincidences, or any "sampling without replacement" problem.

**The moral:** whenever you have $X = \sum X_i$ with indicator $X_i$'s, go straight to the covariance decomposition. The PMF is for computing probabilities. Indicators are for computing expectations and variances.

---

## Summary of the Key Numbers

|Quantity|Value|
|---|---|
|$\mathbb{E}[X_i]$|$K/N$|
|$\text{Var}(X_i)$|$K(N-K)/N^2$|
|$\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$, $i \neq j$|$-K(N-K)/[N^2(N-1)]$|
|$\mathbb{E}[X]$|$nK/N$|
|$\text{Var}(X)$|$np(1-p)(N-n)/(N-1)$|

Note that $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = -\text{Var}(X_i)/(N-1)$. This ratio is exact and beautiful — the covariance between any two indicators is exactly $-1/(N-1)$ times the variance of each indicator. It falls out of the algebra but also has a direct combinatorial explanation.

---

## The $-1/(N-1)$ Ratio, Explained

$$\frac{\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)}{\text{Var}(X_i)} = \frac{-K(N-K)/[N^2(N-1)]}{K(N-K)/N^2} = -\frac{1}{N-1}$$

Why $-1/(N-1)$? Because the $N$ indicators $Y_1, \ldots, Y_N$ for the _entire_ population (whether each ball is white) sum to exactly $K$, a constant. So $\text{Var}(\sum_{i=1}^N Y_i) = 0$, which forces:

$$N,\text{Var}(Y_1) + N(N-1),\text{Cov}(Y_1, Y_2) = 0$$

$$\implies \text{Cov}(Y_1, Y_2) = -\frac{\text{Var}(Y_1)}{N-1}$$

The constraint that the population total is fixed forces all pairwise covariances to be exactly $-\text{Var}/(N-1)$. This is the deep reason the FPC takes the form it does.
