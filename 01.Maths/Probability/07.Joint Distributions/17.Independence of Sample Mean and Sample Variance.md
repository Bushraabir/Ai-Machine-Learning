
> **Core Theorem:** If $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then the sample mean $\bar{X}_n$ and the sample variance $S^2_n$ are **independent**.

This is a **striking and non-obvious fact** — $\bar{X}$ and $S^2$ are both computed from the _same_ data, yet they carry zero information about each other. This independence is what underpins all of classical inference: t-tests, F-tests, confidence intervals.

**Uniqueness Warning:** This independence is _special to the normal distribution_. For non-normal populations, $\bar{X}$ and $S^2$ are generally _not_ independent.

---

## Definitions

### Sample Mean

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$

### Sample Variance (unbiased)

$$S^2_n = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$$

**Why $n-1$?**  
Using $n-1$ (Bessel's correction) makes $S^2$ an **unbiased estimator** of $\sigma^2$: $$\mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2$$

_Intuition:_ We lose one degree of freedom because $S^2$ is computed relative to $\bar{X}$, which itself was estimated from the data.

---

## 🔑 The Theorem (Fisher, 1925)

**Theorem (Independence of $\bar{X}$ and $S^2$):**  
Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. Then:

1. $\bar{X}_n \sim \mathcal{N}!\left(\mu,, \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$
2. $\dfrac{(n-1)S^2_n}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$
3. $\bar{X}_n \perp!!!\perp S^2_n$

All three conclusions hold **jointly**.

---

## 🔨 Proof Strategy (Three Approaches)

### Approach 1: Orthogonal Decomposition (Geometric / Most Elegant)

**Key Idea:** Decompose the data vector into two orthogonal components — one capturing the mean, one capturing the deviations.

Define the data vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)^T \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

**Step 1 — Decompose the sum of squares:** $$\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X})^2 + n(\bar{X} - \mu)^2$$

This is a **Pythagorean decomposition** in $\mathbb{R}^n$:

- The vector $(\bar{X}, \ldots, \bar{X})$ lies in the **1-dimensional subspace** $V_1 = \text{span}{\mathbf{1}}$
- The residual vector $(X_1 - \bar{X}, \ldots, X_n - \bar{X})$ lies in $V_1^\perp$ (the **$(n-1)$-dimensional** orthogonal complement)

**Step 2 — Use rotational invariance of the normal:**  
Since $X_i \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, the standardized vector $\mathbf{Z} = (X_i - \mu)/\sigma$ is spherically symmetric. Any orthogonal rotation of $\mathbf{Z}$ preserves the joint distribution.

**Step 3 — Independence follows from orthogonality:**  
$\bar{X}$ is the projection of $\mathbf{X}$ onto $V_1$. $S^2$ depends only on the projection onto $V_1^\perp$. Since these subspaces are **orthogonal** and the distribution is **rotationally symmetric** (i.e., normal), the two projections are **independent**.

$$\boxed{\bar{X} \perp!!!\perp (X_1 - \bar{X}, \ldots, X_n - \bar{X})}$$

Since $S^2$ is a function of only the residuals, independence follows.

---

### Approach 2: MGF / Direct Calculation

**Step 1 — Rewrite** $X_i = \bar{X} + (X_i - \bar{X})$.

**Step 2 — Show the cross term vanishes:** $$\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}) = 0 \quad \text{(always, by definition of } \bar{X}\text{)}$$

**Step 3 — Joint MGF factorizes:**  
For any linear combination $a\bar{X} + b S^2$, if the joint MGF factors as $M_{\bar{X}}(a) \cdot M_{S^2}(b)$, then $\bar{X} \perp!!!\perp S^2$.

This works because for normal RVs, **uncorrelated $\Rightarrow$ independent** (a property _unique_ to the multivariate normal).

**Key calculation:** $$\text{Cov}(\bar{X},, X_j - \bar{X}) = \text{Cov}!\left(\bar{X}, X_j\right) - \text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} - \frac{\sigma^2}{n} = 0$$

Since all $(X_j - \bar{X})$ are uncorrelated with $\bar{X}$, and all are jointly normal, they are independent of $\bar{X}$.

---

### Approach 3: Cochran's Theorem (Most General)

**Cochran's Theorem:**  
If $\mathbf{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, I_n)$ and $|\mathbf{Z}|^2 = Q_1 + Q_2 + \cdots + Q_k$ where each $Q_i$ is a quadratic form with rank $r_i$, and $\sum r_i = n$, then:

- Each $Q_i \sim \chi^2_{r_i}$
- $Q_1, Q_2, \ldots, Q_k$ are mutually independent

**Application:**  
$$\sum_{i=1}^n Z_i^2 = n\bar{Z}^2 + \sum_{i=1}^n(Z_i - \bar{Z})^2$$

where $Z_i = (X_i - \mu)/\sigma$. The first term has rank 1, the second has rank $n-1$, and $1 + (n-1) = n$. By Cochran:

- $n\bar{Z}^2 \sim \chi^2_1$ (equivalently, $\sqrt{n}\bar{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$)
- $\sum(Z_i - \bar{Z})^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$, which is $\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}$
- They are **independent** ✓

---

## 📊 The Chi-Squared Connection

The statement $\dfrac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$ deserves its own unpacking.

**Definition:** $\chi^2_k = \sum_{i=1}^k Z_i^2$ where $Z_i \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

**Why $n-1$ degrees of freedom?**  
The $n$ residuals $(X_i - \bar{X})$ satisfy the **one linear constraint** $\sum(X_i - \bar{X}) = 0$, so they have only $n-1$ free dimensions. Equivalently, estimating $\mu$ by $\bar{X}$ "uses up" 1 degree of freedom.

| Source           | Distribution                   | df    |
| ---------------- | ------------------------------ | ----- |
| $\bar{X}$ (mean) | $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$ | 1     |
| $S^2$ (variance) | $\chi^2_{n-1}$ (scaled)        | $n-1$ |
| Total            | $\chi^2_n$ (scaled)            | $n$   |

---

## 🎯 Why This Matters: The t-Distribution

The independence theorem is the **exact reason** the Student's t-statistic works.

$$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S / \sqrt{n}}$$

**Step 1:** The numerator $Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$.

**Step 2:** $V = \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$.

**Step 3:** $Z \perp!!!\perp V$ (by the independence theorem).

**Definition of t-distribution:** $T = \dfrac{Z}{\sqrt{V/(n-1)}}$ where $Z \perp!!!\perp V$, $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, $V \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$.

Notice $\sigma^2$ cancels: $$T = \frac{(\bar{X} - \mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n})}{\sqrt{S^2/\sigma^2}} = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}$$

**Without independence, this derivation collapses.** The t-statistic would not have a known distribution.

## ⚡ Key Formulas Summary

| Quantity              | Formula                                  | Distribution                       |
| --------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| Sample mean           | $\bar{X} = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i$          | $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$     |
| Sample variance       | $S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum(X_i-\bar{X})^2$ | $\frac{\sigma^2}{n-1}\chi^2_{n-1}$ |
| Standardized variance | $\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2}$              | $\chi^2_{n-1}$                     |
| t-statistic           | $\frac{\bar{X}-\mu}{S/\sqrt{n}}$         | $t_{n-1}$                          |
| Independence          | $\bar{X} \perp!!!\perp S^2$              | (Normal data only)                 |

## 🔗 Why Normality Is Essential

For non-normal distributions, $\bar{X}$ and $S^2$ are **correlated in general**. Concretely:

**Counterexample (Exponential):**  
Let $X_i \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Exp}(\lambda)$. Then:

- $\bar{X} = 1/\lambda$ estimates the mean
- $S^2$ estimates the variance $1/\lambda^2$

Both depend on $\lambda$, so knowing $\bar{X}$ gives information about $S^2$. They are **not independent**.

**The mechanism that breaks for non-normals:**  
Orthogonality of projections (geometric proof) is always valid, but orthogonal normal vectors being independent is special to the multivariate normal (rotational symmetry). For non-normal distributions, $\text{Cov} = 0$ does **not** imply independence.

---


> **Why is this theorem surprising?**  
> $\bar{X}$ and $S^2$ are both computed from the same $n$ data points. You might expect that a large $\bar{X}$ would somehow be associated with a particular behavior in $S^2$. But for normals, the location of the data cloud ($\bar{X}$) and the spread of the data cloud ($S^2$) are **completely decoupled**.

> **Geometric intuition:**  
> Think of the data as a point in $\mathbb{R}^n$. $\bar{X}$ is its projection onto the "all-equal" axis (the 1-dimensional diagonal). $S^2$ is determined by how far it is from that axis. These are **orthogonal** pieces of information.
