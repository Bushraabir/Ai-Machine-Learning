

> _The Cauchy is probability's most wanted counterexample. It looks like a Normal — symmetric, unimodal, bell-shaped — but it has no mean, no variance, no MGF. It is the distribution that breaks every theorem you thought was unconditional. Keep it close._

## Definition

A random variable $X$ has the **standard Cauchy distribution** if its PDF is

$$\boxed{f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}.}$$

The corresponding CDF is

$$F_X(x) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\arctan(x).$$

The **general Cauchy** with location $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$ and scale $\gamma > 0$ has PDF

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi\gamma\left[1 + \left(\frac{x-\mu}{\gamma}\right)^2\right]}.$$

We write $X \sim \text{Cauchy}(\mu, \gamma)$. The standard Cauchy is $\text{Cauchy}(0,1)$.

---

## Verifying It's a Valid PDF

**Nonnegativity:** $1 + x^2 > 0$ for all $x$, so $f_X(x) > 0$. ✓

**Normalisation:** We need $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}, dx = 1$.

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}, dx = \frac{1}{\pi}\left[\arctan(x)\right]_{-\infty}^{\infty} = \frac{1}{\pi}\left(\frac{\pi}{2} - \left(-\frac{\pi}{2}\right)\right) = \frac{1}{\pi} \cdot \pi = 1. \quad \checkmark$$

## The Canonical Construction: Ratio of Two Normals

This is the result Stat 110 leads with, and it's the right way to first encounter the Cauchy.

**Theorem.** If $X, Y \stackrel{iid}{\sim} N(0,1)$, then $T = X/Y \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$.

**Proof.** We derive the PDF of $T = X/Y$ by conditioning on $Y$.

For $Y = y \neq 0$, the conditional distribution of $T$ given $Y = y$ is $T \mid Y = y \sim N(0, 1/y^2) \cdot \text{sign}(y)$... actually, let me be careful and go through the CDF directly.

Fix $t \in \mathbb{R}$. By LOTP:

$$f_T(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{T \mid Y}(t \mid y), f_Y(y), dy.$$

Given $Y = y$, we have $T = X/y$, so $T \mid Y = y$ has the distribution of $X/y$ where $X \sim N(0,1)$. The PDF of $X/y$ at $t$ is $|y| \cdot \phi(ty)$ where $\phi$ is the standard normal PDF:

$$f_{T \mid Y}(t \mid y) = |y|,\phi(ty) = |y| \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-t^2 y^2/2}.$$

Therefore:

$$f_T(t) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |y| \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-t^2 y^2/2} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-y^2/2}, dy = \frac{1}{2\pi} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |y|, e^{-y^2(1+t^2)/2}, dy.$$

Since the integrand is even in $y$:

$$= \frac{1}{2\pi} \cdot 2\int_0^{\infty} y, e^{-y^2(1+t^2)/2}, dy.$$

Substitute $u = y^2(1+t^2)/2$, so $du = y(1+t^2), dy$:

$$= \frac{1}{\pi} \int_0^{\infty} \frac{1}{1+t^2} e^{-u}, du = \frac{1}{\pi(1+t^2)} \cdot \left[-e^{-u}\right]_0^{\infty} = \frac{1}{\pi(1+t^2)}.$$

This is exactly the Cauchy PDF. $\square$

**Geometric intuition.** Throw a dart uniformly at the unit circle — equivalently, choose $\theta \sim \text{Uniform}(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$ and draw the line through the origin at angle $\theta$. Where it hits the line $y = 1$ has $x$-coordinate $\tan(\theta) \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$.

This is a second construction: $X \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$ iff $X \stackrel{d}{=} \tan(\theta)$ where $\theta \sim \text{Uniform}(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$.

_Verification:_ $F_X(x) = P(\tan\theta \leq x) = P(\theta \leq \arctan x) = \frac{\arctan x - (-\pi/2)}{\pi} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\arctan x}{\pi}$. Differentiate: $f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}$. ✓

## The Mean Does Not Exist

This is the theorem. Not "the mean is infinite" — the mean **does not exist**.

**Claim.** $E[X]$ is undefined for $X \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$.

**Proof.** For $E[X]$ to exist in the Lebesgue sense, we need $E[|X|] < \infty$. Compute:

$$E[|X|] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} |x| \cdot \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}, dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^{\infty} \frac{x}{1+x^2}, dx = \frac{2}{\pi}\left[\frac{1}{2}\ln(1+x^2)\right]_0^{\infty} = +\infty.$$

Since $E[|X|] = \infty$, the expectation $E[X]$ is not defined — neither $+\infty$ nor $-\infty$ nor any real number. $\square$

**The naive objection:** "But surely by symmetry $E[X] = 0$?" This is the subtle point. Symmetry alone is not sufficient to conclude $E[X] = 0$. The correct statement is: if $E[|X|] < \infty$ and $f_X$ is symmetric about 0, then $E[X] = 0$. Without integrability, you cannot invoke symmetry to assign a value.

The rigorous statement: the improper Riemann integral $\lim_{R\to\infty}\int_{-R}^R x \cdot f_X(x), dx = 0$ by symmetry. But this is the **principal value** of the integral, not the Lebesgue integral. The Lebesgue integral requires the positive and negative parts to be separately finite, which fails here. The Lebesgue integral of $X$ does not exist; the Cauchy principal value is 0.

_This distinction — Lebesgue integral vs Cauchy principal value — is one of the places where the formalism is doing real work._

---

## No Variance, No MGF

Since the mean doesn't exist, variance ($E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$) is not even a meaningful quantity. But we can verify $E[X^2] = \infty$ directly:

$$E[X^2] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{x^2}{\pi(1+x^2)}, dx = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{x^2}{1+x^2}, dx = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\left(1 - \frac{1}{1+x^2}\right)dx = \infty.$$

The MGF is $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$. For $t \neq 0$:

$$E[e^{tX}] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{e^{tx}}{\pi(1+x^2)}, dx.$$

For $t > 0$, as $x \to +\infty$: $e^{tx}/(\pi(1+x^2)) \to \infty$ faster than any polynomial, and the integral diverges. So $M_X(t) = \infty$ for all $t \neq 0$. **The MGF does not exist anywhere except $t = 0$.**

This is catastrophic for the standard toolkit — all MGF-based proofs of the CLT, moment calculations, etc., fail completely for the Cauchy.

---

## The Characteristic Function Exists

The one analytic tool that works is the **characteristic function**:

$$\varphi_X(t) = E[e^{itX}] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{e^{itx}}{\pi(1+x^2)}, dx = e^{-|t|}.$$

Since $|e^{itx}| = 1$, the integrand is bounded in absolute value by $f_X(x)$, which is integrable. So Fubini applies and $\varphi_X(t)$ is well-defined for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

The evaluation $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{e^{itx}}{\pi(1+x^2)}, dx = e^{-|t|}$ follows from contour integration (residue theorem — close in the upper or lower half-plane depending on the sign of $t$). The result $e^{-|t|}$ is the characteristic function of the standard Cauchy.

**This is the right object.** The characteristic function always exists for any distribution. The Cauchy's characteristic function has the form $e^{-|t|}$, which is not differentiable at $t = 0$ — consistent with the non-existence of moments ($\varphi_X'(0) = iE[X]$, which doesn't exist).

---

## The CLT Fails — Badly

If $X_1, X_2, \ldots \stackrel{iid}{\sim} \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$, then the sample mean

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{X_1 + \cdots + X_n}{n} \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$$

for every $n$. The distribution of $\bar{X}_n$ does not converge to a normal — it stays Cauchy forever, with exactly the same spread as a single observation. Averaging does nothing.

**Proof.** By the ratio-of-normals construction: if $X_i = U_i / V_i$ with $U_i, V_i \stackrel{iid}{\sim} N(0,1)$, then

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{\sum U_i}{\sum V_i} \cdot \frac{1}{n} \cdot n = \frac{U_1 + \cdots + U_n}{V_1 + \cdots + V_n}.$$

But $U_1 + \cdots + U_n \sim N(0,n)$ and $V_1 + \cdots + V_n \sim N(0,n)$, so their ratio is $N(0,n)/N(0,n) \stackrel{d}{=} N(0,1)/N(0,1) \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$. $\square$

Alternatively via characteristic functions: $\varphi_{\bar{X}_n}(t) = \left(\varphi_X(t/n)\right)^n = \left(e^{-|t|/n}\right)^n = e^{-|t|}$. So $\bar{X}_n$ has the same characteristic function as $X_1$, hence the same distribution.

**Why does the CLT fail?** Because the CLT requires finite variance ($E[X^2] < \infty$). The Cauchy has no finite moments at all. The CLT is not wrong — its hypotheses are just not met.

---

## The Cauchy Is a Stable Distribution

The calculation $\bar{X}_n \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$ is a special case of **stability**: a distribution is _stable_ if a sum of independent copies, when scaled, has the same distribution.

More precisely, $X$ is stable if for $X_1, X_2 \stackrel{iid}{\sim} X$, there exist $a, b > 0$ such that $X_1 + X_2 \stackrel{d}{=} aX + b$.

The Cauchy is a stable distribution with index $\alpha = 1$. The Normal is stable with index $\alpha = 2$ (the only stable distribution with finite variance). The Lévy distribution is stable with index $\alpha = 1/2$.

The generalised CLT says: for i.i.d. variables in the domain of attraction of a stable distribution with index $\alpha$, appropriately scaled sums converge to that stable distribution. The classical CLT is the $\alpha = 2$ case.

---

## Median and Mode Exist

Even though moments don't exist, the median and mode are perfectly well-defined.

**Median:** By symmetry of $f_X$ around 0, $P(X \leq 0) = 1/2$, so the median is 0.

**Mode:** $f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}$ is maximised at $x = 0$, so the mode is 0.

**Interquartile range:** $F_X^{-1}(3/4) - F_X^{-1}(1/4) = \tan(\pi/4) - \tan(-\pi/4) = 1 - (-1) = 2$.

The IQR is a sensible measure of spread for the Cauchy; variance is not. This is a general lesson: for heavy-tailed distributions, quantile-based summaries (median, IQR) are more appropriate than moment-based ones.

## Connection to the $t$-Distribution

The **Student $t$-distribution** with $\nu$ degrees of freedom has PDF

$$f(x) = \frac{\Gamma!\left(\frac{\nu+1}{2}\right)}{\sqrt{\nu\pi},\Gamma!\left(\frac{\nu}{2}\right)}\left(1 + \frac{x^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2}.$$

At $\nu = 1$: the $t_1$ distribution is exactly the standard Cauchy. The Cauchy is the $t$-distribution with one degree of freedom.

As $\nu \to \infty$: the $t_\nu$ distribution converges to $N(0,1)$. The Cauchy ($\nu = 1$) sits at the heavy-tailed extreme.

For $\nu \geq 2$: $E[X] = 0$. For $\nu \geq 3$: $\text{Var}(X) = \nu/(\nu-2)$. At $\nu = 1$ (Cauchy): neither exists.
