

> _The continuous forms aren't new theorems — they're the same theorems wearing different clothes. Once you see that the conditional PDF is just a ratio of joint to marginal, Bayes and LOTP fall out immediately. The hard part was always the discrete case; this is just calculus on top of it._

## Setup: The Conditional PDF

Before writing down Bayes or LOTP, I need the conditional PDF. The definition is:

$$f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_Y(y)}, \quad \text{provided } f_Y(y) > 0.$$

This is defined pointwise in $y$ and is a valid PDF in $x$ for each fixed $y$:

- Nonnegativity: clear.
- Integrates to 1: $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_Y(y)}, dx = \frac{1}{f_Y(y)} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx = \frac{f_Y(y)}{f_Y(y)} = 1$.

Think of $f_{X|Y}(x|y)$ as: given that $Y$ has landed at the value $y$, what does the density of $X$ look like? We're slicing the joint surface at height $y$ and renormalising.

---

## The Continuous LOTP

The Law of Total Probability says: to get the marginal of $X$, average the conditional density of $X$ given $Y$ over all values of $Y$, weighted by $f_Y$.

$$\boxed{f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y), f_Y(y), dy}$$

**Proof.** Just substitute the definition $f_{X|Y}(x|y) = f_{X,Y}(x,y)/f_Y(y)$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y), f_Y(y), dy = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{f_{X,Y}(x,y)}{f_Y(y)} \cdot f_Y(y), dy = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y), dy = f_X(x). \quad \square$$

The last step is exactly the definition of the marginal PDF. So LOTP is just marginalisation written in conditional language.

**Compare with discrete LOTP:**

$$P(X = x) = \sum_y P(X = x \mid Y = y), P(Y = y).$$

Structurally identical — sum replaced by integral, PMF replaced by PDF.

---

## The Continuous Bayes' Rule

Starting from the symmetry of the joint PDF:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y), f_Y(y) = f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x), f_X(x).$$

Solving for $f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y)$:

$$\boxed{f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x), f_X(x)}{f_Y(y)}}$$

Substituting LOTP for the denominator:

$$f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) = \frac{f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x), f_X(x)}{\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x'), f_X(x'), dx'}$$

This is the form that actually gets used in Bayesian inference — the denominator is the normalising constant, often written just as $C$ or "proportional to" signs swallow it entirely.

**The Bayesian reading:**

|Object|Role|
|---|---|
|$f_X(x)$|Prior: what I believe about $X$ before seeing data|
|$f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x)$|Likelihood: probability of observing $Y = y$ if $X = x$|
|$f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y)$|Posterior: updated belief about $X$ after observing $Y = y$|
|$f_Y(y)$|Marginal likelihood: normalising constant, often ignored|

The formula says: **posterior $\propto$ likelihood $\times$ prior**.

## Mixed Forms

This is where it gets interesting — and where the continuous Bayes' rule reveals its full generality. $X$ and $Y$ don't have to be the same type.

### Case 1: Continuous $X$, Discrete $Y$

$X$ is continuous (e.g. an unknown parameter), $Y$ is discrete (e.g. a count or a coin flip outcome).

LOTP:

$$P(Y = k) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P(Y = k \mid X = x), f_X(x), dx.$$

Bayes:

$$f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid Y = k) = \frac{P(Y = k \mid X = x), f_X(x)}{P(Y = k)}.$$

This is the fundamental setup for **Bayesian parameter estimation**: $X = \theta$ is an unknown continuous parameter, $Y$ is discrete data. The prior $f_\theta$ is a PDF; the likelihood $P(Y = k \mid \theta)$ is a PMF evaluated at the data.

### Case 2: Discrete $X$, Continuous $Y$

$X$ is discrete (e.g. a hypothesis label), $Y$ is continuous (e.g. a measurement).

LOTP:

$$f_Y(y) = \sum_x f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x), P(X = x).$$

Bayes:

$$P(X = x \mid Y = y) = \frac{f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x), P(X = x)}{f_Y(y)}.$$

This is the setup for **continuous-data classification** or **hypothesis testing with continuous observations**: which discrete hypothesis $X$ is most probable given continuous evidence $Y$?

## Worked Example 1: Beta-Binomial (The Canonical Bayesian Example)

This is the example Blitzstein builds the whole Bayesian chapter around, and rightly so.

**Setup.** A coin has unknown bias $p$. I place a prior $p \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)$ — a continuous distribution on $(0,1)$ encoding my prior belief. I flip the coin $n$ times and observe $k$ heads. What is the posterior distribution of $p$?

Here $X = p$ (continuous), $Y = k$ (discrete).

**Prior:** $f_p(p) = \frac{1}{B(a,b)} p^{a-1}(1-p)^{b-1}$, $p \in (0,1)$.

**Likelihood:** $P(Y = k \mid p) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$.

**Posterior via Bayes:**

$$f_{p \mid Y}(p \mid k) = \frac{P(Y = k \mid p), f_p(p)}{P(Y = k)} \propto p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \cdot p^{a-1}(1-p)^{b-1} = p^{a+k-1}(1-p)^{b+n-k-1}.$$

I recognise this as the kernel of a $\text{Beta}(a+k,, b+n-k)$ distribution. So:

$$p \mid Y = k ;\sim; \text{Beta}(a+k,, b+n-k).$$

**What just happened:** I never computed the normalising constant $P(Y = k)$ explicitly. The proportionality argument — recognise the kernel, read off the distribution — is the standard technique in Bayesian inference. The Beta prior is **conjugate** to the Binomial likelihood precisely because this works out.

**Intuition:** The prior $\text{Beta}(a,b)$ acts like we already observed $a$ heads and $b$ tails in pseudo-data. After seeing $k$ real heads and $n-k$ real tails, we update to $\text{Beta}(a+k, b+n-k)$. Bayesian updating is just adding counts.

## Worked Example 2: Normal-Normal (The Other Canonical Example)

**Setup.** An unknown signal $\theta \sim N(\mu_0, \tau^2)$ (prior). I observe a noisy measurement $X \mid \theta \sim N(\theta, \sigma^2)$. What is the posterior $\theta \mid X = x$?

**Posterior via Bayes** (dropping normalising constants):

$$f_{\theta \mid X}(\theta \mid x) \propto f_{X \mid \theta}(x \mid \theta), f_\theta(\theta)$$

$$\propto \exp!\left(-\frac{(x-\theta)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \cdot \exp!\left(-\frac{(\theta - \mu_0)^2}{2\tau^2}\right).$$

Combine the exponents — both are quadratic in $\theta$ — and complete the square:

$$-\frac{(x-\theta)^2}{2\sigma^2} - \frac{(\theta-\mu_0)^2}{2\tau^2} = -\frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{1}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}\right)!\left(\theta - \mu_1\right)^2 + \text{const in } \theta,$$

where

$$\mu_1 = \frac{\frac{x}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\mu_0}{\tau^2}}{\frac{1}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}}, \qquad \frac{1}{\tau_1^2} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau^2}.$$

The posterior is $\theta \mid X = x \sim N(\mu_1, \tau_1^2)$. The Normal is conjugate to itself.

**What to notice:**

- The posterior mean $\mu_1$ is a **precision-weighted average** of the prior mean $\mu_0$ and the observation $x$. Precision = $1/\text{variance}$.
- As $\sigma^2 \to 0$ (very clean measurement), $\mu_1 \to x$: the data overwhelms the prior.
- As $\tau^2 \to 0$ (very strong prior), $\mu_1 \to \mu_0$: the prior overwhelms the data.
- The posterior precision $1/\tau_1^2$ = prior precision + data precision. Precisions add. This is the right way to think about combining information.

## Worked Example 3: LOTP for a Marginal

**Setup.** $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and $Y \mid X = x \sim \text{Uniform}(0, x)$. Find the marginal PDF of $Y$.

$$f_Y(y) = \int_0^1 f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x), f_X(x), dx.$$

For fixed $y$, the condition $Y \mid X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,x)$ requires $x > y$ (otherwise $y$ is outside the support of the conditional). So:

$$f_Y(y) = \int_y^1 \frac{1}{x} \cdot 1, dx = \left[\ln x\right]_y^1 = -\ln y, \quad 0 < y < 1.$$

**Check:** $\int_0^1 (-\ln y), dy = \left[-y\ln y + y\right]_0^1 = 1$. ✓ (Using $\lim_{y\to 0^+} y\ln y = 0$.)

The marginal of $Y$ is $f_Y(y) = -\ln y$ — not a named distribution you'd recognise immediately, but it's a valid PDF and the derivation via continuous LOTP is clean.

## The Proportionality Shortcut

In practice, the denominator $f_Y(y)$ in Bayes' rule is a constant with respect to $x$ (it's an integral over $x'$, so $x$ has been integrated out). So:

$$f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) \propto f_{Y \mid X}(y \mid x), f_X(x)$$

where "$\propto$" means "proportional to, as a function of $x$." The normalising constant is determined by the requirement that the posterior integrate to 1.

**Workflow:**

1. Write $f_{Y|X}(y|x) \cdot f_X(x)$ and simplify, treating $y$ as fixed.
2. Recognise the kernel — the functional form in $x$.
3. Read off the distribution; the normalising constant comes for free.

If you can't recognise the kernel, you compute the integral explicitly. But recognising it is almost always faster and is the expected method in Stat 110.

## Summary: All Four Forms at Once

Let $X$ be the unknown, $Y$ be the observed. Suppressing arguments for clarity:

||$Y$ discrete|$Y$ continuous|
|---|---|---|
|**$X$ discrete**|$P(X \mid Y) = \frac{P(Y \mid X),P(X)}{P(Y)}$|$P(X \mid Y) = \frac{f_{Y\mid X},P(X)}{f_Y}$|
|**$X$ continuous**|$f_{X\mid Y} = \frac{P(Y \mid X),f_X}{P(Y)}$|$f_{X\mid Y} = \frac{f_{Y\mid X},f_X}{f_Y}$|

Every cell has the same structure: likelihood times prior, divided by normalising constant. The types of the objects differ; the formula does not.
