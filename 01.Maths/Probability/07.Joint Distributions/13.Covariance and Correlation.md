## Covariance: The Definition

For random variables $X$ and $Y$: $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X])(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y])]$$

This is the **expected product of deviations**. When $X$ is above its mean at the same time $Y$ is above its mean (or both below), the product is positive — they're moving together. When they move oppositely, the product is negative.

**The computational formula** — the one I actually use: $$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$$

_Proof:_ Expand the definition directly: $$\mathbb{E}[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)] = \mathbb{E}[XY - \mu_Y X - \mu_X Y + \mu_X\mu_Y]$$ By linearity: $$= \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mu_Y\mathbb{E}[X] - \mu_X\mathbb{E}[Y] + \mu_X\mu_Y = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mu_X\mu_Y$$

> _Memorise this: $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$. It's the analogue of $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$, which is just $\text{Cov}(X,X)$._

---

## Variance as a Special Case

$$\text{Cov}(X, X) = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mu_X)^2] = \text{Var}(X)$$

So variance is covariance with itself. Every variance identity is a covariance identity in disguise.

---

## Properties of Covariance

All of these follow from linearity of expectation. No independence needed unless stated.

**Symmetry:** $$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \text{Cov}(Y, X)$$

**Bilinearity** — this is the big one: $$\text{Cov}(aX + bY, Z) = a,\text{Cov}(X,Z) + b,\text{Cov}(Y,Z)$$

And in both arguments: $$\text{Cov}!\left(\sum_i a_i X_i,, \sum_j b_j Y_j\right) = \sum_i \sum_j a_i b_j, \text{Cov}(X_i, Y_j)$$

**Shift invariance:** Adding a constant does nothing: $$\text{Cov}(X + c, Y) = \text{Cov}(X, Y)$$

because $\mathbb{E}[X+c] = \mathbb{E}[X] + c$, so the constant cancels in the deviation.

**Independence implies zero covariance:** $$X \perp Y \implies \text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$$

_Proof:_ If $X \perp Y$, then $\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$, so $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y] = 0$.

> _The converse is FALSE. Zero covariance does not imply independence. Classic counterexample: $X \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, $Y = X^2$. Then $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[X^3] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[X^2] = 0 - 0 = 0$, but $Y$ is completely determined by $X$. Covariance only detects **linear** dependence._

---

## Variance of a Sum

The payoff of bilinearity. For any $X, Y$: $$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2,\text{Cov}(X, Y)$$

_Proof:_ $$\text{Var}(X+Y) = \text{Cov}(X+Y, X+Y) = \text{Cov}(X,X) + 2\text{Cov}(X,Y) + \text{Cov}(Y,Y)$$

by bilinearity. Done.

For $n$ variables, in full generality: $$\text{Var}!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) + 2\sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$$

If the $X_i$ are **pairwise uncorrelated** (not necessarily independent — just $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = 0$ for $i \neq j$), then: $$\text{Var}!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i)$$

This is the version that makes the CLT and sampling theory work.

## The Covariance Matrix

For a random vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)^\top$, the **covariance matrix** is: $$\Sigma = \text{Cov}(\mathbf{X}) = \mathbb{E}[(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})(\mathbf{X} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top]$$

so $\Sigma_{ij} = \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$ and $\Sigma_{ii} = \text{Var}(X_i)$.

Key properties:

- $\Sigma$ is **symmetric**: $\Sigma = \Sigma^\top$.
- $\Sigma$ is **positive semi-definite**: $\mathbf{v}^\top \Sigma \mathbf{v} \geq 0$ for all $\mathbf{v} \in \mathbb{R}^n$.

_Why PSD?_ Because $\mathbf{v}^\top \Sigma \mathbf{v} = \text{Var}(\mathbf{v}^\top \mathbf{X}) \geq 0$ — variance is always non-negative.

This matrix is central to multivariate normals, PCA, and any multivariate inference.

## Correlation: Making It Dimensionless

Covariance has units of $(\text{units of } X) \times (\text{units of } Y)$. That makes it incomparable across problems. Fix it by normalising:

$$\text{Corr}(X,Y) = \rho_{XY} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\sqrt{\text{Var}(X),\text{Var}(Y)}} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{SD}(X),\text{SD}(Y)}$$

This is the **Pearson correlation coefficient**.

---

## The Crucial Bound: $|\rho| \leq 1$

**Theorem.** For any $X, Y$ with finite variances: $$-1 \leq \text{Corr}(X,Y) \leq 1$$

_Proof via the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality._ The probabilistic form of Cauchy-Schwarz states: $$(\mathbb{E}[UV])^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[U^2],\mathbb{E}[V^2]$$

for any random variables $U, V$. Apply with $U = X - \mu_X$ and $V = Y - \mu_Y$: $$\text{Cov}(X,Y)^2 = (\mathbb{E}[UV])^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[U^2],\mathbb{E}[V^2] = \text{Var}(X),\text{Var}(Y)$$

Divide both sides by $\text{Var}(X),\text{Var}(Y)$: $$\rho^2 \leq 1 \implies |\rho| \leq 1 \qquad \square$$

**When is $|\rho| = 1$?** Cauchy-Schwarz is tight iff $U = cV$ for some constant $c$, i.e. $X - \mu_X = c(Y - \mu_Y)$, i.e. $X$ and $Y$ are in an exact **affine relationship** $X = aY + b$. Correlation $\pm 1$ means perfectly linearly dependent.

_Alternative proof via variance:_ Consider $Z = \frac{X}{\sigma_X} \pm \frac{Y}{\sigma_Y}$. Then $\text{Var}(Z) \geq 0$, expanding gives $2 \pm 2\rho \geq 0$, so $\rho \leq 1$ and $\rho \geq -1$. Clean and self-contained.

---

## Interpretation

|$\rho$|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$+1$|Perfect positive linear relationship|
|$(0, 1)$|Positive association (above average together)|
|$0$|No **linear** relationship (may still be dependent)|
|$(-1, 0)$|Negative association|
|$-1$|Perfect negative linear relationship|

**What $\rho$ does not capture:**

- Non-linear dependence ($Y = X^2$ can have $\rho = 0$)
- The slope of the relationship (that's regression — $\rho$ is scale-free)
- Causation (obviously)

---

## Alternative Proof of the Bound: Completing the Square

This one is slick and elementary — no Cauchy-Schwarz needed.

For any $t \in \mathbb{R}$, define $Z = tX + Y$. Since variance is non-negative: $$0 \leq \text{Var}(Z) = t^2\text{Var}(X) + 2t,\text{Cov}(X,Y) + \text{Var}(Y)$$

This is a quadratic in $t$ that is always $\geq 0$, so its discriminant must be $\leq 0$: $$\Delta = 4,\text{Cov}(X,Y)^2 - 4,\text{Var}(X),\text{Var}(Y) \leq 0$$ $$\implies \text{Cov}(X,Y)^2 \leq \text{Var}(X),\text{Var}(Y) \implies |\rho| \leq 1$$

> _I love this proof. It's pure algebra once you remember that variance $\geq 0$. No linear algebra needed, no inner product spaces. Just: a quadratic in $t$ that's always non-negative has non-positive discriminant._

---

## Connection to Regression

The slope in simple linear regression of $Y$ on $X$ is: $$\hat{\beta} = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{Var}(X)}$$

And correlation satisfies: $$\rho = \hat{\beta} \cdot \frac{\text{SD}(X)}{\text{SD}(Y)}$$

So $\rho$ is the regression slope after standardising both variables to have unit variance. This is why $\rho$ is scale-free and $\hat{\beta}$ is not.

---

## Key Identities to Have Memorised

$$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$$ $$\text{Var}(X \pm Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) \pm 2,\text{Cov}(X,Y)$$ $$\text{Cov}(aX, bY) = ab,\text{Cov}(X,Y)$$ $$\rho(X,Y) = \rho!\left(\frac{X - \mu_X}{\sigma_X},, \frac{Y - \mu_Y}{\sigma_Y}\right)$$

The last one says: correlation is just the covariance of the standardised variables.
