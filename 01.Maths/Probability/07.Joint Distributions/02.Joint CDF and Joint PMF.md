> _The joint PMF is conceptually simple — it's just a table of probabilities. The joint CDF is the object that unifies discrete, continuous, and mixed distributions under one roof. Understanding both, and the exact relationship between them, is what separates someone who "knows probability" from someone who actually understands its foundations._

---

## Joint PMF (Discrete Case)

### Definition

Let $X$ and $Y$ be discrete RVs on the same probability space. Their **joint PMF** is the function:

$$p_{X,Y}(x, y) = P(X = x,, Y = y)$$

defined for all $(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$. It is zero outside the (countable) support.

**Validity conditions:** $$p_{X,Y}(x,y) \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } (x,y)$$ $$\sum_{x} \sum_{y} p_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1$$

where the sums are over the support.

### Computing Probabilities from the Joint PMF

For any event $A \subseteq \mathbb{R}^2$:

$$P((X,Y) \in A) = \sum_{(x,y) \in A} p_{X,Y}(x,y)$$

This is the fundamental usage. Every probability question about $(X,Y)$ reduces to summing the joint PMF over the relevant region.

### The Joint PMF Table

For finite-support discrete RVs, the joint PMF is most naturally represented as a table. If $X \in {x_1, \ldots, x_m}$ and $Y \in {y_1, \ldots, y_n}$:

$$\begin{array}{c|cccc|c} & y_1 & y_2 & \cdots & y_n & p_X \ \hline x_1 & p_{11} & p_{12} & \cdots & p_{1n} & \sum_j p_{1j} \ x_2 & p_{21} & p_{22} & \cdots & p_{2n} & \sum_j p_{2j} \ \vdots & \vdots & & & \vdots & \vdots \ x_m & p_{m1} & p_{m2} & \cdots & p_{mn} & \sum_j p_{mj} \ \hline p_Y & \sum_i p_{i1} & \sum_i p_{i2} & \cdots & \sum_i p_{in} & 1 \end{array}$$

Row sums give $p_X$; column sums give $p_Y$. The grand total is 1. This is why it's called a **marginalisation** — you sum across the margins of the table.

### Worked Example: Two Dice

Roll two fair dice. Let $X$ = value of die 1, $Y$ = value of die 2. Then:

$$p_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{36}, \quad x,y \in {1,2,3,4,5,6}$$

This is a uniform joint PMF on a $6 \times 6$ grid. Every cell has mass $1/36$.

What is $P(X + Y = 7)$? Sum over all cells where $x + y = 7$: $$P(X+Y=7) = \sum_{\substack{x,y \ x+y=7}} \frac{1}{36} = \frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6}$$

(The six pairs: $(1,6),(2,5),(3,4),(4,3),(5,2),(6,1)$.)

What is $P(X > Y)$? Sum over the lower triangle: $$P(X > Y) = \sum_{x=1}^6 \sum_{y=1}^{x-1} \frac{1}{36} = \frac{1}{36}\sum_{x=1}^6 (x-1) = \frac{0+1+2+3+4+5}{36} = \frac{15}{36} = \frac{5}{12}$$

### Worked Example: Dependent Discrete Joint

Let $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ and $X \mid N = n \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$. Find the joint PMF of $(N, X)$.

$$p_{N,X}(n, x) = P(N=n), P(X=x \mid N=n) = e^{-\lambda}\frac{\lambda^n}{n!} \cdot \binom{n}{x}p^x(1-p)^{n-x}$$

for $n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$ and $x = 0, 1, \ldots, n$. (Zero otherwise.)

This joint is the starting point for deriving that $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda p)$ — you marginalise over $n$:

$$p_X(x) = \sum_{n=x}^\infty e^{-\lambda}\frac{\lambda^n}{n!}\binom{n}{x}p^x(1-p)^{n-x}$$

which, after algebra (factor out $e^{-\lambda p}(\lambda p)^x/x!$ and recognise the remaining sum as $e^{\lambda(1-p)}$), gives $p_X(x) = e^{-\lambda p}(\lambda p)^x / x!$. This is Poisson thinning done at the PMF level rather than via PGFs.

## Joint CDF

### Definition

The **joint CDF** of $(X,Y)$ — whether discrete, continuous, or mixed — is:

$$F_{X,Y}(x,y) = P(X \leq x,, Y \leq y)$$

for all $(x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$.

The joint CDF is the **universal** representation: it exists for every joint distribution without exception, regardless of whether the joint PMF or joint PDF exists.

### Properties of the Joint CDF

**P1. Monotonicity.** $F_{X,Y}(x,y)$ is non-decreasing in each argument separately: $$x_1 \leq x_2 \implies F_{X,Y}(x_1, y) \leq F_{X,Y}(x_2, y)$$ and similarly in $y$.

**P2. Right-continuity.** $F_{X,Y}$ is right-continuous in each argument: $$\lim_{x' \downarrow x} F_{X,Y}(x', y) = F_{X,Y}(x,y), \quad \lim_{y' \downarrow y} F_{X,Y}(x, y') = F_{X,Y}(x,y)$$

**P3. Boundary limits.** $$\lim_{x \to -\infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y) = 0, \qquad \lim_{y \to -\infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y) = 0$$ $$\lim_{x \to \infty,, y \to \infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1$$

**P4. Marginal recovery.** Sending one argument to $+\infty$: $$\lim_{y \to \infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y) = F_X(x), \qquad \lim_{x \to \infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y) = F_Y(y)$$

**P5. Rectangle probability formula (crucial).** For $x_1 < x_2$, $y_1 < y_2$:

$$P(x_1 < X \leq x_2,, y_1 < Y \leq y_2) = F(x_2, y_2) - F(x_1, y_2) - F(x_2, y_1) + F(x_1, y_1)$$

> **This is the inclusion-exclusion for rectangles.** Draw the four corners: you want the probability in the rectangle. You add the top-right CDF value, subtract the two "side" values (which over-subtract the bottom-left), and add back the bottom-left. Get this formula tattooed. It's used constantly, and it's easy to get the signs wrong under pressure.

**Proof.** Write ${x_1 < X \leq x_2, y_1 < Y \leq y_2}$ as a set difference:

$$= {X \leq x_2, Y \leq y_2} \setminus \left[{X \leq x_1, Y \leq y_2} \cup {X \leq x_2, Y \leq y_1}\right]$$

By inclusion-exclusion: $$P = F(x_2,y_2) - P(X \leq x_1, Y \leq y_2) - P(X \leq x_2, Y \leq y_1) + P(X \leq x_1, Y \leq y_1)$$ $$= F(x_2,y_2) - F(x_1,y_2) - F(x_2,y_1) + F(x_1,y_1) \qquad \square$$

**P6. 2-increasing (supermodularity condition).** The above formula must always be $\geq 0$: $$F(x_2,y_2) - F(x_1,y_2) - F(x_2,y_1) + F(x_1,y_1) \geq 0$$

for all $x_1 \leq x_2$, $y_1 \leq y_2$. This is necessary and sufficient for $F$ to be a valid joint CDF. A function satisfying this is called **2-increasing** (or supermodular in this context).

This is the property that separates a valid joint CDF from just any function satisfying P1–P4. Marginal CDFs can be chosen freely; their "coupling" must satisfy the rectangle inequality.

### Recovering the Joint PMF from the CDF (Discrete Case)

For a discrete $(X,Y)$, the joint PMF is recovered by:

$$p_{X,Y}(x,y) = P(X = x, Y = y) = F(x,y) - F(x^-,y) - F(x,y^-) + F(x^-,y^-)$$

where $F(x^-, y) = \lim_{x' \uparrow x} F(x',y)$ is the left limit. In the discrete case with integer-valued support this simplifies to:

$$p_{X,Y}(x,y) = F(x,y) - F(x-1,y) - F(x,y-1) + F(x-1,y-1)$$

This is the 2D analogue of $P(X = k) = F(k) - F(k-1)$ for the univariate discrete case.

**Proof.** By the rectangle formula with $x_1 = x-1$, $x_2 = x$, $y_1 = y-1$, $y_2 = y$: $$P(X = x, Y = y) = F(x,y) - F(x-1,y) - F(x,y-1) + F(x-1,y-1) \qquad \square$$

### Recovering the Joint PDF from the CDF (Continuous Case)

If $F_{X,Y}$ is sufficiently smooth (mixed partial derivative exists and is continuous):

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{\partial^2 F_{X,Y}}{\partial x, \partial y}(x,y)$$

**Proof.** By the fundamental theorem of calculus applied twice: $$F_{X,Y}(x,y) = \int_{-\infty}^x \int_{-\infty}^y f_{X,Y}(s,t), dt, ds$$

Differentiating in $x$ then $y$ (justified by continuity of $f$) gives $f_{X,Y}(x,y)$. $\square$

> _The formula $f = \partial^2 F / \partial x \partial y$ is the 2D version of $f = F'$. In one dimension you take one derivative to get the density. In two dimensions you take the mixed partial. The rectangle formula for probabilities is the 2D version of $P(a < X \leq b) = F(b) - F(a)$._

## Relationship Between Joint PMF and Joint CDF

For a discrete $(X,Y)$ with support ${(x_i, y_j)}$:

$$F_{X,Y}(x,y) = \sum_{x_i \leq x} \sum_{y_j \leq y} p_{X,Y}(x_i, y_j)$$

It's a cumulative sum over all mass points in the lower-left quadrant $(-\infty, x] \times (-\infty, y]$.

The joint CDF of a discrete distribution is a **step function** in each variable — flat everywhere except at the support points, where it jumps by the mass $p_{X,Y}(x_i, y_j)$.

**Schematic for $(X,Y) \in {0,1}^2$ with joint PMF:**

||$Y=0$|$Y=1$|
|---|---|---|
|$X=0$|$a$|$b$|
|$X=1$|$c$|$d$|

Then: $$F(0,0) = a, \quad F(0,1) = a+b, \quad F(1,0) = a+c, \quad F(1,1) = a+b+c+d = 1$$

And recovering the PMF: $$p(1,1) = F(1,1) - F(0,1) - F(1,0) + F(0,0) = 1 - (a+b) - (a+c) + a = d \checkmark$$

## Independence in Terms of the Joint CDF

$X$ and $Y$ are independent if and only if:

$$F_{X,Y}(x,y) = F_X(x), F_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$$

This is the CDF version of the factorisation condition. It holds for all distributions — discrete, continuous, mixed — without requiring the existence of a density or PMF.

For the PMF version: if $F$ factors, then so does the PMF: $$p_{X,Y}(x,y) = \Delta_{xx}\Delta_{yy} F(x,y) = \Delta_{xx} F_X(x) \cdot \Delta_{yy} F_Y(y) = p_X(x), p_Y(y)$$

where $\Delta_{xx} f(x) = f(x) - f(x^-)$ denotes the jump at $x$.

---

## Marginal CDFs from the Joint CDF

$$F_X(x) = \lim_{y \to \infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y), \qquad F_Y(y) = \lim_{x \to \infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y)$$

These limits always exist (since $F$ is bounded and monotone) and give valid univariate CDFs. This is property P4 above — sending the other variable to $+\infty$ collapses the constraint on it.

## Sklar's Theorem (Copulas) — A Glimpse

The factorisation issue — that marginals don't determine the joint — has a complete theoretical resolution:

**Theorem (Sklar, 1959).** For any joint CDF $F_{X,Y}$ with marginals $F_X$ and $F_Y$, there exists a function $C: [0,1]^2 \to [0,1]$ (called a **copula**) such that:

$$F_{X,Y}(x,y) = C(F_X(x), F_Y(y))$$

If $F_X$ and $F_Y$ are continuous, $C$ is unique. Conversely, for any copula $C$ and any marginal CDFs $F_X$, $F_Y$, the above formula defines a valid joint CDF.

The copula $C$ encodes all the dependence structure of $(X,Y)$, completely separated from the marginals. Independence corresponds to $C(u,v) = uv$ (the product copula). The upper Fréchet–Hoeffding bound $C(u,v) = \min(u,v)$ corresponds to $X = T(Y)$ a.s. for some increasing $T$ (comonotonicity).

> _I'm recording this because it's the right way to think about why marginals don't determine the joint. The joint CDF = copula + marginals. Specifying both marginals only pins down the margins of the table; the copula is the free parameter that controls the interior._

## Pitfalls

**1. The rectangle formula has four terms, not two.**  
$P(x_1 < X \leq x_2, y_1 < Y \leq y_2) \neq F(x_2,y_2) - F(x_1,y_1)$. That's wrong — it misses the two off-diagonal terms. Always write all four.

**2. Strict vs non-strict inequalities matter in the discrete case.**  
$P(X < x) \neq P(X \leq x)$ when $X$ has a point mass at $x$. Specifically $P(X \leq x) - P(X < x) = P(X = x)$. In the continuous case they're equal, which is why this pitfall only bites in the discrete/mixed setting.

**3. The joint CDF is right-continuous, not left-continuous.**  
$F_{X,Y}(x,y) = P(X \leq x, Y \leq y)$ uses $\leq$, not $<$. So jumps happen at the support points, and $F$ is right-continuous with left limits. This is a convention, but it's universal.

**4. Not every function satisfying P1–P4 is a valid joint CDF.**  
You also need the 2-increasing condition (P6). Example: $F(x,y) = F_X(x)$ for all $y \geq 0$ and $0$ otherwise is monotone in each argument and has the right limits — but the rectangle probability $F(x_2,y_2) - F(x_1,y_2) - F(x_2,y_1) + F(x_1,y_1)$ can be negative, so it's invalid.

## Summary

||Joint PMF|Joint CDF|
|---|---|---|
|**Exists for**|Discrete $(X,Y)$ only|All $(X,Y)$|
|**Definition**|$p(x,y) = P(X=x, Y=y)$|$F(x,y) = P(X \leq x, Y \leq y)$|
|**Recover probabilities**|Sum over region|Rectangle formula|
|**Recover marginals**|Sum over other variable|Send other variable to $+\infty$|
|**Independence**|$p_{X,Y} = p_X p_Y$|$F_{X,Y} = F_X F_Y$|
|**From the other**|$p = \Delta_x \Delta_y F$ (difference operator)|$F(x,y) = \sum_{x_i \leq x}\sum_{y_j \leq y} p(x_i,y_j)$|
