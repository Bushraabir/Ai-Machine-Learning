
> _Marginalizing a joint PDF is the continuous act of forgetting. You integrate out the variable you don't care about, and what's left is the unconditional law of the one you do. The logic is identical to the discrete case — sums just became integrals._


Let $(X, Y)$ have joint PDF $f_{X,Y}$. The **marginal PDF of $X$** is

$$\boxed{f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y), dy,}$$

and symmetrically the **marginal PDF of $Y$** is

$$f_Y(y) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx.$$

This is the continuous analogue of summing over rows (or columns) in the discrete joint PMF table. The name _marginal_ is a literal relic — you'd write the row totals in the margin of a hand-computed table.

---

## Proof That It's a Valid PDF

**Claim:** $f_X$ defined above is a valid PDF.

_Nonnegativity:_ For each fixed $x$, $f_{X,Y}(x,y) \geq 0$ for all $y$, so the integral is $\geq 0$. ✓

_Normalisation:_

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x), dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y), dy, dx = 1.$$

The interchange of the two integrals is justified by **Tonelli's theorem**: since $f_{X,Y} \geq 0$, we can swap the order of integration freely, and the double integral equals 1 by normalisation of the joint PDF. ✓

_(Tonelli applies here — not Fubini — because Tonelli only requires nonnegativity, whereas Fubini requires absolute integrability. For PDFs nonnegativity is always given, so Tonelli is the right citation. In practice everyone just swaps and doesn't mention it, but I want to know why I'm allowed to.)_

---

## The LOTP Connection

The marginal PDF is the continuous version of the Law of Total Probability. Using the definition of the conditional PDF:

$$f_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X,Y}(x,y), dy = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y), f_Y(y), dy.$$

This is LOTP in density form: to get the unconditional density of $X$ at $x$, average the conditional density $f_{X|Y}(x|y)$ over all possible values of $Y$, weighted by $f_Y$.

**Mnemonic:** _marginalization = LOTP on the joint, regardless of discrete or continuous._

## Setting Up the Limits: The Only Hard Part

Computing a marginal PDF is conceptually trivial — the entire difficulty is setting the limits of integration correctly when the support is not a rectangle.

**The procedure, always:**

1. Draw the support of $(X,Y)$ in the $xy$-plane.
2. To compute $f_X(x)$: fix $x$ at an arbitrary value in its marginal range. Draw the vertical slice $x = \text{const}$ through the support. The endpoints of that slice are the limits of the inner integral over $y$.
3. To compute $f_Y(y)$: fix $y$ and draw the horizontal slice. The endpoints give the limits of the inner integral over $x$.

**The limits depend on which variable is the outer one** — this is the single step where errors happen.

---

## Worked Example 1: Rectangular Support (Easy)

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = e^{-x-y}, \quad x > 0,; y > 0.$$

Support is the first quadrant — a rectangle (in the sense that the support of $Y$ does not depend on $x$, and vice versa).

$$f_X(x) = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-x-y}, dy = e^{-x} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-y}, dy = e^{-x} \cdot 1 = e^{-x}, \quad x > 0.$$

So $X \sim \text{Expo}(1)$. By symmetry $Y \sim \text{Expo}(1)$.

No geometric thinking needed here — rectangular support means the limits are constants.

---

## Worked Example 2: Triangular Support (The Real Test)

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = 2, \quad 0 < x < y < 1.$$

The support is the triangle ${(x,y) : 0 < x < y < 1}$ — above the diagonal $y = x$ in the unit square.

**Marginal of $X$:** Fix $x \in (0,1)$. The vertical slice $x = \text{const}$ hits the support for $y \in (x, 1)$ — $y$ must be above $x$ (from the constraint $x < y$) and below 1.

$$f_X(x) = \int_x^1 2, dy = 2(1-x), \quad 0 < x < 1.$$

**Marginal of $Y$:** Fix $y \in (0,1)$. The horizontal slice $y = \text{const}$ hits the support for $x \in (0, y)$ — $x$ must be positive and below $y$.

$$f_Y(y) = \int_0^y 2, dx = 2y, \quad 0 < y < 1.$$

**Verification:**

$$\int_0^1 2(1-x), dx = 2!\left[x - \frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^1 = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 1. \checkmark$$

$$\int_0^1 2y, dy = \left[y^2\right]_0^1 = 1. \checkmark$$

**Named distributions:** $f_X(x) = 2(1-x)$ is $\text{Beta}(1,2)$. $f_Y(y) = 2y$ is $\text{Beta}(2,1)$. Both are Beta distributions, which makes sense — the uniform distribution on the ordered simplex ${0 < x < y < 1}$ has Beta marginals.

---

## Worked Example 3: Support Boundary Depending on $x$

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = 3x, \quad 0 < y < x < 1.$$

_(Same support as before but with $x$ and $y$ swapped — now $y < x$, so the support is the triangle below the diagonal.)_

**Verify normalisation first:** $\int_0^1 \int_0^x 3x, dy, dx = \int_0^1 3x^2, dx = 1$. ✓

**Marginal of $X$:** Fix $x \in (0,1)$; $y$ ranges from $0$ to $x$.

$$f_X(x) = \int_0^x 3x, dy = 3x^2, \quad 0 < x < 1. \qquad [\text{Beta}(3,1)]$$

**Marginal of $Y$:** Fix $y \in (0,1)$; $x$ ranges from $y$ to $1$ (we need $x > y$).

$$f_Y(y) = \int_y^1 3x, dx = 3 \cdot \frac{1 - y^2}{2} = \frac{3}{2}(1-y^2), \quad 0 < y < 1.$$

**The trap:** when computing $f_Y$, the lower limit of the $x$-integral is $y$, not $0$. The natural instinct is to write $\int_0^1$, but that would include the region above the diagonal where the density is zero. Drawing the support and slicing horizontally makes the correct limits obvious.

---

## Worked Example 4: A Genuinely Interesting One

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{\pi}, \quad x^2 + y^2 < 1.$$

$(X,Y)$ is uniform on the unit disk. The normalising constant $1/\pi$ is correct since the disk has area $\pi$.

**Marginal of $X$:** Fix $x \in (-1,1)$. The vertical slice at $x$ intersects the disk for $y \in (-\sqrt{1-x^2},, \sqrt{1-x^2})$.

$$f_X(x) = \int_{-\sqrt{1-x^2}}^{\sqrt{1-x^2}} \frac{1}{\pi}, dy = \frac{2\sqrt{1-x^2}}{\pi}, \quad -1 < x < 1.$$

This is the **arcsine-adjacent** distribution — or more precisely, $\frac{2}{\pi}\sqrt{1-x^2}$, which is the PDF of $\cos(\Theta)$ where $\Theta \sim \text{Uniform}(0,\pi)$. It's also related to the semicircle law in random matrix theory.

By symmetry $f_Y(y) = \frac{2\sqrt{1-y^2}}{\pi}$.

**Are $X$ and $Y$ independent?** No: the support is a disk, not a rectangle. Knowing $|X|$ is close to 1 forces $Y \approx 0$. The marginals being identical doesn't make them independent.

## What Marginals Don't Carry: Dependence

This deserves repeating in the continuous setting.

The marginal PDFs $f_X$ and $f_Y$ together do not determine the joint $f_{X,Y}$. Two pairs of random variables can have identical marginals but completely different dependence structures.

**Example.** Let $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1$ on the unit square (independent uniforms). Now let $U = V = W$ where $W \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, so $f_{U,V}$ is concentrated on the diagonal and doesn't even have a density. Both have $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$ marginals. The joints are as different as they can be.

This is precisely the problem that **copulas** solve: they separate the marginal structure from the dependence structure. Sklar's theorem says every joint distribution can be written as a copula applied to its marginals. Worth a dedicated note eventually.

## The Marginal CDF from the Joint CDF

If I have the joint CDF $F_{X,Y}(x,y) = P(X \leq x, Y \leq y)$, the marginal CDFs are recovered by taking limits:

$$F_X(x) = P(X \leq x) = P(X \leq x, Y < \infty) = \lim_{y \to \infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y).$$

$$F_Y(y) = \lim_{x \to \infty} F_{X,Y}(x,y).$$

Then differentiating gives the marginal PDFs in the usual way. I almost never use this route — differentiating $F_{X,Y}$ and then taking limits is more painful than just integrating $f_{X,Y}$ directly — but it's good to know the connection.

## Extension to $n$ Variables

For a random vector $(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ with joint density $f_{X_1,\ldots,X_n}$, the marginal density of $(X_1, \ldots, X_k)$ for $k < n$ is

$$f_{X_1,\ldots,X_k}(x_1,\ldots,x_k) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \cdots \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_{X_1,\ldots,X_n}(x_1,\ldots,x_n), dx_{k+1} \cdots dx_n.$$

**Example — Multivariate Normal.** If $(X_1,\ldots,X_n) \sim N(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$, then any marginal $(X_1,\ldots,X_k)$ is also multivariate normal with the corresponding subvector of $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and the top-left $k \times k$ submatrix of $\Sigma$. This is one of the defining nice properties of the multivariate normal — marginals stay in the same family.
