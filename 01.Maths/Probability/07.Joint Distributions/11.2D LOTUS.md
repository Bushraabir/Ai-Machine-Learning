

> _[[07.LOTUS]]says you don't need to find the distribution of $g(X,Y)$ to compute its expectation. You just integrate $g$ against the joint density you already have. This sounds obvious — it is not. It is a theorem, and a useful one._

## Motivation

Suppose $(X, Y)$ has joint PDF $f_{X,Y}$ and I want to compute $E[g(X,Y)]$ for some function $g : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$.

The naive approach: find the distribution of $Z = g(X,Y)$ — its PDF or CDF — then compute $E[Z] = \int z, f_Z(z), dz$.

The LOTUS approach: skip finding $f_Z$ entirely and compute directly.

Finding the distribution of $Z = g(X,Y)$ is often painful. It requires a change of variables, setting up inequalities, differentiating CDFs. LOTUS bypasses all of this.

---

## The Theorem

**Theorem (2D LOTUS).** Let $(X,Y)$ be a continuous random vector with joint PDF $f_{X,Y}$, and let $g : \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ be a measurable function. Then:

$$\boxed{E[g(X,Y)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x,y), f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy,}$$

provided the integral converges absolutely (i.e., $E[|g(X,Y)|] < \infty$).

For the discrete case, with joint PMF $p_{X,Y}$:

$$E[g(X,Y)] = \sum_x \sum_y g(x,y), p_{X,Y}(x,y).$$

**What the theorem says:** the expectation of a function of $(X,Y)$ is computed by integrating that function against the joint density. You do not need to know the distribution of $g(X,Y)$.

---

## Why It's a Theorem, Not a Definition

$E[Z]$ is defined as $\int z, f_Z(z), dz$ for a random variable $Z$ with PDF $f_Z$. The statement that this equals $\int!\int g(x,y) f_{X,Y}(x,y),dx,dy$ when $Z = g(X,Y)$ is a genuine result — it requires proof.

**Proof sketch (continuous case).** By definition, $E[g(X,Y)] = \int z, f_{g(X,Y)}(z), dz$. The PDF $f_{g(X,Y)}$ is obtained by pushing forward the joint measure $(X,Y)$ through $g$. The change-of-variables formula for measures gives exactly the right-hand side. More concretely, for $g \geq 0$:

$$E[g(X,Y)] = \int_0^{\infty} P(g(X,Y) > t), dt = \int_0^{\infty} \iint_{{g(x,y)>t}} f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy, dt.$$

Swapping the order of integration (Tonelli — all terms are nonneg):

$$= \iint \left(\int_0^{g(x,y)} dt\right) f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy = \iint g(x,y), f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy. \quad \square$$

For general $g$: decompose $g = g^+ - g^-$ and apply the nonneg result to each part, assuming absolute integrability.

## Special Cases Worth Naming

**Linearity.** $E[aX + bY + c] = a,E[X] + b,E[Y] + c$.

Proof via 2D LOTUS with $g(x,y) = ax+by+c$ and $f_{X,Y}$ arbitrary:

$$E[aX+bY+c] = \iint (ax+by+c), f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy$$ $$= a\iint x, f_{X,Y}, dx, dy + b\iint y, f_{X,Y}, dx, dy + c.$$

The first double integral equals $\int x, f_X(x), dx = E[X]$ (marginalise out $y$). Similarly for $E[Y]$. Linearity of expectation follows — and note it holds regardless of whether $X$ and $Y$ are independent.

**Product of independent r.v.'s.** If $X \perp Y$, then $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_X(x) f_Y(y)$, so:

$$E[g(X)h(Y)] = \iint g(x)h(y), f_X(x), f_Y(y), dx, dy = \int g(x) f_X(x), dx \cdot \int h(y) f_Y(y), dy = E[g(X)], E[h(Y)].$$

This is the factorisation identity for independent r.v.'s, proved directly from 2D LOTUS + independence.

**Covariance.** $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = E[(X - E[X])(Y - E[Y])]$. By 2D LOTUS:

$$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \iint (x-\mu_X)(y-\mu_Y), f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy.$$

Expanding and using linearity: $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = E[XY] - E[X],E[Y]$, where $E[XY] = \iint xy, f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy$ by 2D LOTUS with $g(x,y) = xy$.

## Worked Example 1: $E[XY]$ for a Triangular Joint

$(X,Y)$ uniform on the triangle ${0 < x < y < 1}$, so $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = 2$.

$$E[XY] = \int_0^1\int_0^y xy \cdot 2, dx, dy = \int_0^1 2y \cdot \frac{y^2}{2}, dy = \int_0^1 y^3, dy = \frac{1}{4}.$$

Compare: $E[X] = \int_0^1 2x(1-x), dx = 2\left[\frac{x^2}{2} - \frac{x^3}{3}\right]_0^1 = \frac{1}{3}$.

$E[Y] = \int_0^1 2y \cdot y, dy = \int_0^1 2y^2, dy = \frac{2}{3}$.

So $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{3}\cdot\frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{4} - \frac{2}{9} = \frac{9-8}{36} = \frac{1}{36} > 0$.

Positive covariance makes sense: large $X$ forces large $Y$ (since $Y > X$).

---

## Worked Example 2: $E[X^2 + Y^2]$ for Uniform on the Disk

$(X,Y)$ uniform on the unit disk, $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1/\pi$ for $x^2 + y^2 < 1$.

$$E[X^2 + Y^2] = \iint_{x^2+y^2 < 1} \frac{x^2+y^2}{\pi}, dx, dy.$$

Switch to polar coordinates: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, Jacobian $= r$.

$$= \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^1 r^2 \cdot r, dr, d\theta = \frac{1}{\pi} \cdot 2\pi \cdot \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{2}.$$

By symmetry $E[X^2] = E[Y^2]$, so $E[X^2] = \frac{1}{4}$.

Also $E[X] = 0$ by symmetry, so $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \frac{1}{4}$.

---

## Worked Example 3: $E[\max(X,Y)]$

$(X,Y)$ i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$, so $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1$ on $[0,1]^2$. Let $M = \max(X,Y)$.

**Method 1 (LOTUS directly):**

$$E[\max(X,Y)] = \int_0^1\int_0^1 \max(x,y), dx, dy.$$

Split the square into two triangles:

$$= \int_0^1\int_0^y y, dx, dy + \int_0^1\int_0^x x, dy, dx = \int_0^1 y^2, dy + \int_0^1 x^2, dx = \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{2}{3}.$$

**Method 2 (via CDF of $M$):** $F_M(m) = P(X \leq m, Y \leq m) = m^2$, so $f_M(m) = 2m$ and $E[M] = \int_0^1 m \cdot 2m, dm = 2/3$. ✓

LOTUS gets there without ever computing $f_M$. Both methods agree — the point of LOTUS is that Method 1 is often simpler to set up.

---

## Worked Example 4: $E[e^{X+Y}]$ for Independent Exponentials

$X, Y \stackrel{iid}{\sim} \text{Expo}(1)$, $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = e^{-x}e^{-y}$, $x,y > 0$.

$$E[e^{X+Y}] = \int_0^{\infty}\int_0^{\infty} e^{x+y} \cdot e^{-x-y}, dx, dy = \int_0^{\infty}\int_0^{\infty} 1, dx, dy = \infty.$$

$e^{X+Y}$ has infinite expectation — consistent with the MGF of $\text{Expo}(1)$ being $M_X(t) = 1/(1-t)$, which only exists for $t < 1$, so $t = 1$ gives $\infty$.

But: $E[e^{tX+sY}] = E[e^{tX}]E[e^{sY}] = \frac{1}{1-t}\cdot\frac{1}{1-s}$ for $t, s < 1$.

This is the joint MGF of $(X,Y)$, computed via 2D LOTUS + independence.

## The Non-Rectangular Support Issue (Again)

When the support of $(X,Y)$ is not a rectangle, 2D LOTUS requires careful limit-setting — exactly the same issue as in marginalisation.

**Rule:** Always draw the support. For the inner integral, fix the outer variable and determine the range of the inner variable by intersecting with the support.

**Example.** $(X,Y)$ uniform on ${0 < x < y < 1}$, $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = 2$. Compute $E[Y - X]$.

$$E[Y-X] = \int_0^1\int_0^y (y-x)\cdot 2, dx, dy = \int_0^1 2\left[xy - \frac{x^2}{2}\right]_0^y dy = \int_0^1 2\left(y^2 - \frac{y^2}{2}\right) dy = \int_0^1 y^2, dy = \frac{1}{3}.$$

Sanity check: $E[Y] = 2/3$, $E[X] = 1/3$, so $E[Y-X] = 1/3$. ✓ (Linearity of expectation gives the same answer with less work — useful check.)

---

## When 2D LOTUS Fails: Integrability

$E[g(X,Y)]$ is only well-defined when $E[|g(X,Y)|] < \infty$. If this fails, the expectation doesn't exist.

**Example.** $(X,Y)$ i.i.d. $N(0,1)$, $g(x,y) = x/y$. Then $X/Y \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$ (as we showed in [[09.Cauchy Distribution]]), which has no mean. Indeed:

$$E!\left[\frac{X}{Y}\right] = \iint \frac{x}{y} \cdot \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}, dx, dy$$

does not converge absolutely — the integral of the absolute value is $\infty$.

The practical check: before applying 2D LOTUS, verify (or at least expect) that $\iint |g(x,y)| f_{X,Y}(x,y),dx,dy < \infty$. For $g \geq 0$ this is automatic (Tonelli) — the expectation is either finite or $+\infty$. For sign-changing $g$, check absolute integrability first.

---

## The Discrete-Continuous Mixed Case

2D LOTUS extends immediately to hybrid distributions. If $X$ is continuous with PDF $f_X$ and $Y$ is discrete with PMF $p_Y$, and they are independent:

$$E[g(X,Y)] = \sum_y \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x,y), f_X(x), p_Y(y), dx = \sum_y p_Y(y) \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x,y), f_X(x), dx.$$

This is LOTUS for the mixed joint — sum over the discrete variable, integrate over the continuous one. Fubini/Tonelli justifies the interchange.

**Example.** $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, $X \mid N = n \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$. Find $E[X]$.

$$E[X] = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} E[X \mid N=n], P(N=n) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} np \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^n}{n!} = p\lambda \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^{n-1}}{(n-1)!} = p\lambda.$$

_(This is LOTP for expectations, which is itself a consequence of 2D LOTUS + the tower property.)_

---

## Summary

2D LOTUS is the workhorse for computing expectations involving two random variables. The key points:

- **You do not need the distribution of $g(X,Y)$.** Integrate $g$ against the joint density directly.
- **The hard part is the limits**, not the theorem. Draw the support. Fix the outer variable, determine the inner limits.
- **Integrability matters.** For $g \geq 0$, Tonelli guarantees the integral is well-defined (possibly $+\infty$). For sign-changing $g$, check $E[|g|] < \infty$ before claiming $E[g]$ exists.
- **Linearity of expectation and the independence factorisation are both special cases**, derivable immediately from 2D LOTUS.
