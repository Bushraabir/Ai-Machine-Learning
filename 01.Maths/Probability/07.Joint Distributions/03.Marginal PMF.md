

> _The whole point of the marginal distribution is to forget. You have a joint law on $(X, Y)$ and you want to think about just one of them — so you sum the other out of existence._

---


## The Setup

Say we have two discrete r.v.'s $X$ and $Y$ defined on the same probability space. Their **joint PMF** is

$$p_{X,Y}(x,y) = P(X = x,, Y = y).$$

Everything about the pair lives here. But sometimes I only care about $X$ alone — not because $Y$ is unimportant, but because I want to study $X$ unconditionally. To recover the _law of $X$ alone_, I need to **marginalize out** $Y$.

---

## Definition

$$\boxed{p_X(x) = P(X = x) = \sum_{y} p_{X,Y}(x,y)}$$

where the sum ranges over all values $y$ in the support of $Y$.

Symmetrically,

$$p_Y(y) = \sum_{x} p_{X,Y}(x,y).$$

These are called the **marginal PMFs** of $X$ and $Y$ respectively.

---

## Proof That It's Actually a PMF

This always trips me up on first glance — let me make sure it checks out.

**Claim:** $p_X$ defined above is a valid PMF.

_Nonnegativity:_ For any fixed $x$, $p_{X,Y}(x,y) \geq 0$ for all $y$, so a sum of them is $\geq 0$. ✓

_Sums to 1:_

$$\sum_x p_X(x) = \sum_x \sum_y p_{X,Y}(x,y) = \sum_{x,y} p_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1.$$

The interchange of the double sum is valid since all terms are nonneg (Tonelli, or just finite support in the discrete case). ✓

**That's it.** The marginal is a genuine PMF.

---

## The "Summing Over Rows/Columns" Picture

If I write the joint PMF as a table with $x$ indexing rows and $y$ indexing columns, then:

- $p_X(x)$ = the **row sum** for row $x$
- $p_Y(y)$ = the **column sum** for column $y$

This is literally why they're called _marginal_ — historically, you'd write the row totals in the margin of the table. The name is a relic of hand computation and I find it kind of charming.

---

## Worked Example: Dice Sum

Let $X = $ first die, $Y = $ second die, both fair, independent. The joint PMF is

$$p_{X,Y}(i,j) = \frac{1}{36}, \quad i,j \in {1,\ldots,6}.$$

The marginal:

$$p_X(i) = \sum_{j=1}^{6} \frac{1}{36} = \frac{6}{36} = \frac{1}{6}.$$

Unsurprising — marginalization recovers the Uniform${1,\ldots,6}$ distribution, as it should.

Now let $S = X + Y$ and $D = X - Y$. These are _not_ independent — knowing $S$ tells me something about $D$. The joint PMF of $(S,D)$ is more interesting. Its marginal on $S$ gives the distribution of the sum of two dice:

$$p_S(k) = \frac{6 - |k - 7|}{36}, \quad k \in {2,\ldots,12}.$$

_(Derivation: count the number of $(i,j)$ pairs with $i + j = k$. For $k \leq 7$ there are $k-1$ such pairs; for $k > 7$ there are $13 - k$.)_

---

## The Law of Total Probability Connection

Notice:

$$p_X(x) = \sum_y p_{X,Y}(x,y) = \sum_y P(X = x \mid Y = y), P(Y = y) = \sum_y p_{X|Y}(x|y), p_Y(y).$$

This is just LOTP in disguise, conditioning on the partition ${Y = y}$.

**Mnemonic I keep coming back to:** _marginalization = LOTP on the joint._

---

## What Marginals Don't Tell You

**The marginals do not determine the joint.** This is one of the most important things to internalize early.

**Example.** Let $(X,Y)$ be uniform on ${(0,0),(0,1),(1,0),(1,1)}$. Then $X \sim \text{Bern}(1/2)$ and $Y \sim \text{Bern}(1/2)$.

But now let $(X', Y')$ be such that $X' \sim \text{Bern}(1/2)$ and $Y' = X'$ always. Then $Y' \sim \text{Bern}(1/2)$ too. But the joints are completely different: the first puts mass $1/4$ on each of the four corners; the second puts mass $1/2$ on $(0,0)$ and $1/2$ on $(1,1)$.

**Same marginals. Totally different dependence structure.**

This is why joint distributions exist in the first place — marginals lose dependence information. Copulas (later) are precisely the machinery for isolating what the joint tells you _beyond_ the marginals.

---

## Independence Criterion

$(X, Y)$ are independent iff

$$p_{X,Y}(x,y) = p_X(x), p_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } (x,y).$$

In words: the joint _factors_ into the product of the marginals. This is the definition.

A useful sanity check: if I compute the marginals and their product doesn't give me back the joint everywhere, I know they're dependent. If it does, they're independent.

## Extension to More Variables

For a random vector $(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ with joint PMF $p_{X_1,\ldots,X_n}$, the marginal of $(X_1, \ldots, X_k)$ for $k < n$ is

$$p_{X_1,\ldots,X_k}(x_1,\ldots,x_k) = \sum_{x_{k+1}} \cdots \sum_{x_n} p_{X_1,\ldots,X_n}(x_1,\ldots,x_n).$$

**Example — Multinomial.** If $(X_1,\ldots,X_k) \sim \text{Mult}_k(n, \mathbf{p})$, then the marginal of $X_i$ alone is $\text{Bin}(n, p_i)$. The proof is just marginalization: summing out the other components collapses the multinomial to a binomial. I spent longer convincing myself of this than I should have — eventually I just wrote out the generating function and it fell out immediately.

## Common Mistake to Avoid

When computing $p_X(x)$, the sum is over _all possible values of $Y$_, not just the values that appear "next to" $x$ in some table. If the support of $Y$ depends on $x$ (a **non-rectangular support**), be careful:

**Example.** $(X, Y)$ uniform on ${(x,y) : 1 \leq x \leq y \leq 3,, x,y \in \mathbb{Z}}$, which has 6 points: $(1,1),(1,2),(1,3),(2,2),(2,3),(3,3)$.

Then $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = 1/6$ on those six points and $0$ elsewhere. To get $p_X(1)$:

$$p_X(1) = p_{X,Y}(1,1) + p_{X,Y}(1,2) + p_{X,Y}(1,3) = \frac{3}{6} = \frac{1}{2}.$$

The support of $Y$ given $X = 1$ is ${1,2,3}$, but given $X = 3$ it's just ${3}$. This kind of asymmetry makes things like $E[Y|X]$ interesting — worth revisiting in [[Conditional Expectation]].
