
> _Reality does not respect the discrete/continuous divide. Insurance payouts, option prices, rainfall totals — these are neither purely discrete nor purely continuous. The hybrid is not a pathology. It is the generic case, and discrete and continuous are the special ones._

## The Problem with the Discrete/Continuous Dichotomy

Every introductory probability course splits random variables into discrete (PMF) and continuous (PDF). But what about:

- **Insurance:** a payout that is exactly $0$ with probability $1/2$ (no accident), and otherwise exponentially distributed.
- **Censored data:** a survival time that is observed exactly if death occurred, but recorded as $T > C$ (a point mass at $C$) if the subject was censored.
- **Option pricing:** a call option worth exactly $0$ (with positive probability) if it expires out of the money, and continuously distributed otherwise.

These are **hybrid** (or **mixed**) random variables — they have both a discrete component (point masses) and a continuous component (a density). Neither a PMF alone nor a PDF alone describes them.

The CDF is the object that handles all cases uniformly.

---

## The CDF Is Always Well-Defined

For any random variable $X$ — discrete, continuous, or hybrid — the CDF

$$F_X(x) = P(X \leq x)$$

is well-defined, right-continuous, nondecreasing, with $F_X(-\infty) = 0$ and $F_X(\infty) = 1$.

A hybrid $X$ has a CDF that is:

- **Flat** on intervals where there is no mass (neither discrete nor continuous),
- **Jumps** at points with positive probability (the discrete atoms),
- **Rises smoothly** on intervals where $X$ has a density.

The Lebesgue decomposition theorem says every CDF decomposes uniquely into a discrete part, an absolutely continuous part, and a singular continuous part. For all distributions we encounter in probability courses, the singular part is zero — so every mixed distribution is a convex combination of a purely discrete and purely continuous distribution.

---

## The Decomposition

Write the CDF of a hybrid $X$ as:

$$F_X(x) = p \cdot F_{\text{disc}}(x) + (1-p) \cdot F_{\text{cont}}(x), \quad p \in [0,1],$$

where:

- $F_{\text{disc}}$ is the CDF of a purely discrete distribution (a step function),
- $F_{\text{cont}}$ is the CDF of a purely continuous distribution (a continuous function),
- $p$ is the total probability mass sitting on discrete atoms.

Equivalently, there exist:

- A set of atoms ${x_1, x_2, \ldots}$ with point masses $p_i = P(X = x_i) > 0$ summing to $p$,
- A density $f_{\text{cont}}(x)$ for the continuous part, integrating to $1-p$.

The full "density" of $X$ — in the sense of a measure — is:

$$\mu_X = p_{\text{disc}} + (1-p), f_{\text{cont}}(x), dx,$$

where $p_{\text{disc}} = \sum_i p_i \delta_{x_i}$ is a sum of Dirac delta masses.

_I won't use measure theory throughout — the CDF and the case-by-case approach is cleaner for computation._

---

## Computing Probabilities: Always Go Through the CDF

For a hybrid $X$, probabilities of intervals are:

$$P(a < X \leq b) = F_X(b) - F_X(a).$$

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = F_X(b) - F_X(a^-) = F_X(b) - \lim_{x \nearrow a} F_X(x).$$

The distinction between $F_X(a)$ and $F_X(a^-)$ matters when $a$ is an atom:

$$P(X = a) = F_X(a) - F_X(a^-).$$

For continuous points, $F_X(a) = F_X(a^-)$ and $P(X = a) = 0$.

## The Canonical Example: Zero-Inflated Exponential

$$X = \begin{cases} 0 & \text{with probability } p \ Y & \text{with probability } 1-p \end{cases}$$

where $Y \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$, independent of the Bernoulli trial.

**CDF:**

$$F_X(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < 0 \ p + (1-p)(1 - e^{-\lambda x}) & x \geq 0. \end{cases}$$

At $x = 0$: $F_X(0) = p$. So there's a jump of size $p$ at 0, corresponding to $P(X = 0) = p$. For $x > 0$, $F_X$ rises smoothly.

**Expected value:**

$$E[X] = p \cdot 0 + (1-p) \cdot E[Y] = \frac{1-p}{\lambda}.$$

This is LOTP for expectations: $E[X] = E[X \mid \text{atom}]\cdot p + E[X \mid \text{continuous}]\cdot (1-p)$.

**General formula for $E[X]$:** For any hybrid $X = $ discrete atoms + continuous density:

$$E[X] = \sum_i x_i , p_i + \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x, f_{\text{cont}}(x), dx.$$

This is just linearity of expectation applied to the decomposition.

## Worked Example 1: Insurance Payout

A policy pays $0$ if there is no claim (probability $3/4$), and pays an amount $\text{Expo}(1/1000)$ (mean payout $1000) if there is a claim (probability $1/4$).

Let $X$ be the payout.

**CDF:** For $x < 0$: $F_X(x) = 0$. For $x \geq 0$:

$$F_X(x) = \frac{3}{4} + \frac{1}{4}(1 - e^{-x/1000}) = 1 - \frac{1}{4}e^{-x/1000}.$$

Jump of $3/4$ at $x = 0$. Smooth rise for $x > 0$.

**Mean payout:**

$$E[X] = \frac{3}{4} \cdot 0 + \frac{1}{4} \cdot 1000 = 250.$$

**$P(X > 500)$:**

$$P(X > 500) = 1 - F_X(500) = \frac{1}{4}e^{-1/2} \approx \frac{1}{4}(0.6065) \approx 0.1516.$$

## Worked Example 2: The Censored Random Variable

In survival analysis, a subject either dies (event time $T \sim f_T$) or is censored at time $C$ (still alive at end of study). The **observed time** is:

$$X = \min(T, C), \quad \Delta = \mathbf{1}[T \leq C].$$

If $T \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$ and $C$ is a fixed constant:

$$P(X = C) = P(T > C) = e^{-\lambda C}.$$

$$P(X \leq x) = P(T \leq x),\mathbf{1}[x < C] + 1 \cdot \mathbf{1}[x \geq C] = (1 - e^{-\lambda x}),\mathbf{1}[x < C] + \mathbf{1}[x \geq C].$$

So $X$ has:

- A continuous density $\lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ on $[0, C)$,
- A point mass $e^{-\lambda C}$ at $x = C$.

This is a hybrid distribution — it is impossible to describe $X$ with either a PMF or a PDF alone.

## Worked Example 3: Mixed Bayes (The Key Probability Example)

This is the Stat 110 use case. A continuous prior, discrete likelihood — or vice versa.

**Setup.** A coin has bias $p \sim \text{Beta}(1,1) = \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ (continuous prior). I flip it once and observe $X \in {0,1}$ (discrete).

The joint distribution of $(p, X)$ is hybrid: $p$ is continuous, $X$ is discrete. The marginal of $X$ is discrete; the marginal of $p$ is continuous; the joint has no single representation as either a PMF or a PDF.

The joint "density" is:

$$f_{p, X}(p, x) = P(X = x \mid p), f_p(p) = p^x(1-p)^{1-x} \cdot 1, \quad p \in (0,1),; x \in {0,1}.$$

**Marginal of $X$:**

$$P(X = 1) = \int_0^1 p \cdot 1, dp = \frac{1}{2}.$$

**Posterior of $p$ given $X = 1$:**

$$f_{p \mid X}(p \mid 1) = \frac{P(X=1 \mid p), f_p(p)}{P(X=1)} = \frac{p \cdot 1}{1/2} = 2p, \quad p \in (0,1).$$

So $p \mid X = 1 \sim \text{Beta}(2,1)$. ✓

This is **mixed Bayes**: the prior is a continuous PDF, the likelihood is a discrete PMF, the posterior is a continuous PDF. The formula structure is identical to pure continuous Bayes — you just mix the object types appropriately.

## Expectation via LOTP: The General Tool

For a hybrid $X$ and any event $A$, LOTP gives:

$$E[X] = E[X \mid A], P(A) + E[X \mid A^c], P(A^c).$$

This always works regardless of whether $X$ is discrete, continuous, or hybrid. The conditioning splits $X$ into pieces that may each be easier to handle.

**Example.** $X$ is zero-inflated Exponential as above. Let $A = {X = 0}$.

$$E[X] = E[X \mid X = 0], P(X=0) + E[X \mid X > 0], P(X > 0) = 0 \cdot p + \frac{1}{\lambda}\cdot(1-p) = \frac{1-p}{\lambda}.$$

Here $E[X \mid X > 0] = E[\text{Expo}(\lambda)] = 1/\lambda$ because given the continuous part is realised, $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$.

## The CDF Characterises Everything

For a hybrid $X$, the CDF $F_X$ completely characterises the distribution. From $F_X$:

- **Identify atoms:** points where $F_X$ jumps. The jump size at $x_0$ is $P(X = x_0) = F_X(x_0) - F_X(x_0^-)$.
- **Identify continuous density:** wherever $F_X$ is differentiable, $f_{\text{cont}}(x) = F_X'(x)$.
- **Recover all probabilities:** $P(X \in B) = \int_B dF_X$ (Lebesgue-Stieltjes integral — the unified notation that handles both sums over atoms and integrals over densities).

The Lebesgue-Stieltjes integral $\int g(x), dF_X(x)$ is the right way to write $E[g(X)]$ for any distribution:

- Discrete: $\sum_i g(x_i), p_i$.
- Continuous: $\int g(x), f_X(x), dx$.
- Hybrid: both terms summed.

This is the formalism that unifies everything. I don't need it for computation — LOTP does the work — but it's the reason there's no separate theory for each case.

---

## Common Mistake: Forgetting Point Masses in the CDF

For a hybrid $X$ with an atom at $x_0$:

$$P(X < x_0) = F_X(x_0^-) \neq F_X(x_0) = P(X \leq x_0).$$

The difference is $P(X = x_0) > 0$.

When computing $P(a \leq X \leq b)$, always be explicit about whether endpoints are included:

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = F_X(b) - F_X(a^-).$$

If $a$ is an atom and you write $F_X(b) - F_X(a)$ instead, you lose the mass at $a$. This is a silent error — the formula looks right and the answer is just slightly off.

## Why This Matters Beyond Probability Theory

The hybrid framework is not an abstract curiosity. It appears everywhere:

- **Econometrics:** Tobit models — a continuous outcome censored at zero (wages, consumption). The observed variable is hybrid.
- **Biostatistics:** Survival data with administrative censoring — the exact structure of Worked Example 2.
- **Finance:** Digital options, barrier options — positive probability of ending at exactly zero or exactly at the barrier.
- **Mixture models:** Latent variable models where a hidden discrete variable selects which continuous component generates the data. The marginal over the hidden variable is hybrid.
- **Renewal theory:** The age of a renewal process at a fixed time has a point mass at zero (if a renewal just occurred) plus a continuous component.

In all these cases, the right tool is the CDF, LOTP conditioning on the discrete/continuous split, and the Lebesgue-Stieltjes expectation formula.
