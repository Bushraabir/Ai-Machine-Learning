

> _This is the distribution. Everything in classical statistics — regression, PCA, hypothesis testing, Kalman filtering, Gaussian processes — lives here. The multivariate normal is not just "the normal but in higher dimensions." It has geometric structure that the univariate case hides from you. The elliptical level sets, the covariance matrix as a shape operator, the way conditioning collapses a joint into a lower-dimensional normal — all of this is worth understanding at a bone-deep level._

---

## Definition: Three Equivalent Ways

There are three definitions, each illuminating a different aspect. Know all three.

### Definition 1 — via the PDF (when $\Sigma$ is invertible)

A random vector $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_k)^\top$ has the **Multivariate Normal** distribution $\mathcal{N}_k(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$ if its joint PDF is:

$$f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{k/2}|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

where $\boldsymbol{\mu} \in \mathbb{R}^k$ is the mean vector and $\Sigma$ is a $k \times k$ positive definite covariance matrix.

**The exponent** $(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})$ is the **Mahalanobis distance squared** from $\mathbf{x}$ to $\boldsymbol{\mu}$. It is a quadratic form in $\mathbf{x}$, so the level sets $f(\mathbf{x}) = c$ are **ellipsoids** centred at $\boldsymbol{\mu}$, with axes determined by the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$ and half-lengths proportional to the square roots of the eigenvalues.

### Definition 2 — via Linear Transformations (the constructive definition)

$\mathbf{X} \sim \mathcal{N}_k(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$ if and only if: $$\mathbf{X} = A\mathbf{Z} + \boldsymbol{\mu}$$ where $\mathbf{Z} = (Z_1, \ldots, Z_m)^\top$ with $Z_i \overset{i.i.d.}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, and $A$ is a $k \times m$ matrix satisfying $AA^\top = \Sigma$.

Such an $A$ always exists (e.g. the Cholesky factor of $\Sigma$, or $A = U\Lambda^{1/2}$ from the spectral decomposition $\Sigma = U\Lambda U^\top$). This definition works even when $\Sigma$ is singular — the distribution is still well-defined, it just doesn't have a density with respect to Lebesgue measure on $\mathbb{R}^k$.

> _This is the definition that makes simulating multivariate normals trivial: generate i.i.d. standard normals, multiply by the Cholesky factor of $\Sigma$, shift by $\boldsymbol{\mu}$. Done._

### Definition 3 — via Univariate Marginals (the characterisation)

$\mathbf{X}$ is multivariate normal if and only if every linear combination $\mathbf{a}^\top \mathbf{X} = a_1 X_1 + \cdots + a_k X_k$ is (univariate) normal, for every $\mathbf{a} \in \mathbb{R}^k$.

This is the most useful characterisation for proofs. To show something is MVN, show all its linear projections are normal. To disprove MVN, find one linear combination that isn't.

**Warning.** The converse of the marginal statement is FALSE: each $X_i$ being marginally normal does NOT imply $\mathbf{X}$ is jointly multivariate normal. There exist bivariate distributions with both marginals $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ that are not bivariate normal. Always need to check the _joint_ structure.

## Mean and Covariance

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{X}] = \boldsymbol{\mu}, \qquad \text{Cov}(\mathbf{X}) = \Sigma$$

_Proof via Definition 2._ $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{X}] = A\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{Z}] + \boldsymbol{\mu} = \boldsymbol{\mu}$. For the covariance: $$\text{Cov}(\mathbf{X}) = \text{Cov}(A\mathbf{Z} + \boldsymbol{\mu}) = A,\text{Cov}(\mathbf{Z}),A^\top = A I_m A^\top = AA^\top = \Sigma$$

where we used that $Z_i$ are i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$, so $\text{Cov}(\mathbf{Z}) = I_m$.

## Linear Transformations Close Under MVN

**Theorem.** If $\mathbf{X} \sim \mathcal{N}_k(\boldsymbol{\mu}, \Sigma)$ and $B$ is an $m \times k$ matrix, $\mathbf{c} \in \mathbb{R}^m$, then: $$B\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{c} \sim \mathcal{N}_m(B\boldsymbol{\mu} + \mathbf{c},; B\Sigma B^\top)$$

_Proof._ Write $\mathbf{X} = A\mathbf{Z} + \boldsymbol{\mu}$. Then $B\mathbf{X} + \mathbf{c} = BA\mathbf{Z} + (B\boldsymbol{\mu} + \mathbf{c})$, which has the form of Definition 2 with matrix $BA$ and mean $B\boldsymbol{\mu} + \mathbf{c}$. Covariance: $(BA)(BA)^\top = BAA^\top B^\top = B\Sigma B^\top$. $\square$

This is the property that makes the multivariate normal so powerful: any affine image of an MVN is MVN. Marginals, sums, projections — all normal.

**Marginals:** Taking $B = e_i^\top$ (the $i$-th standard basis row vector) gives $X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_i, \Sigma_{ii})$.

**Sums:** $\sum_i a_i X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{a}^\top\boldsymbol{\mu},; \mathbf{a}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{a})$. (This is Definition 3, now as a theorem.)

## The Key Theorem: Zero Covariance $\Leftrightarrow$ Independence

For jointly normal random variables, uncorrelatedness is equivalent to independence: $$\mathbf{X}_1 \perp \mathbf{X}_2 \iff \text{Cov}(\mathbf{X}_1, \mathbf{X}_2) = 0$$

_Proof ($\Rightarrow$):_ Independence always implies zero covariance.

_Proof ($\Leftarrow$):_ Partition $\mathbf{X} = (\mathbf{X}_1^\top, \mathbf{X}_2^\top)^\top$ with $\text{Cov}(\mathbf{X}_1, \mathbf{X}_2) = 0$. The joint PDF factors:

$$f(\mathbf{x}_1, \mathbf{x}_2) \propto \exp!\left(-\tfrac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right)$$

When $\text{Cov}(\mathbf{X}_1,\mathbf{X}_2) = 0$, the covariance matrix is block-diagonal: $$\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix}\Sigma_{11} & 0 \ 0 & \Sigma_{22}\end{pmatrix}, \quad \Sigma^{-1} = \begin{pmatrix}\Sigma_{11}^{-1} & 0 \ 0 & \Sigma_{22}^{-1}\end{pmatrix}$$

The exponent splits: $$(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu}) = (\mathbf{x}_1-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1)^\top\Sigma_{11}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_1-\boldsymbol{\mu}_1) + (\mathbf{x}_2-\boldsymbol{\mu}_2)^\top\Sigma_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_2-\boldsymbol{\mu}_2)$$

So $f(\mathbf{x}_1,\mathbf{x}_2) = f_1(\mathbf{x}_1)\cdot f_2(\mathbf{x}_2)$. Factoring of the joint density is exactly independence. $\square$

> _This is the one property of the multivariate normal that has no analogue in general. For arbitrary distributions, you can have zero covariance with strong dependence. In the Gaussian world, covariance structure IS the dependence structure — completely._

## Conditional Distribution

This is where MVN gets truly beautiful. Partition: $$\mathbf{X} = \begin{pmatrix}\mathbf{X}_1 \ \mathbf{X}_2\end{pmatrix} \sim \mathcal{N}!\left(\begin{pmatrix}\boldsymbol{\mu}_1 \ \boldsymbol{\mu}_2\end{pmatrix},; \begin{pmatrix}\Sigma_{11} & \Sigma_{12} \ \Sigma_{21} & \Sigma_{22}\end{pmatrix}\right)$$

where $\mathbf{X}_1 \in \mathbb{R}^p$, $\mathbf{X}_2 \in \mathbb{R}^q$.

**Theorem (Conditional distribution).** Assuming $\Sigma_{22}$ is invertible: $$\mathbf{X}_1 \mid \mathbf{X}_2 = \mathbf{x}_2 ;\sim; \mathcal{N}!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1|2},; \Sigma_{1|2}\right)$$

where: $$\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1|2} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 + \Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2)$$ $$\Sigma_{1|2} = \Sigma_{11} - \Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{21}$$

_Proof sketch._ Write $\mathbf{X}_1 = \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 + \Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{X}_2 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2) + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ where $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} = \mathbf{X}_1 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 - \Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{X}_2 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2)$. One can verify $\text{Cov}(\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}, \mathbf{X}_2) = 0$ (direct calculation), so by the key theorem, $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon} \perp \mathbf{X}_2$. Given $\mathbf{X}_2 = \mathbf{x}_2$, the expression for $\mathbf{X}_1$ reduces to a fixed affine shift of $\boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$, which is normal with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1|2}$ and covariance $\Sigma_{1|2}$. $\square$

### Reading the Formulas

**Conditional mean:** $\boldsymbol{\mu}_{1|2} = \boldsymbol{\mu}_1 + \Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}(\mathbf{x}_2 - \boldsymbol{\mu}_2)$

This is a linear function of $\mathbf{x}_2$. The matrix $\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}$ is exactly the matrix of regression coefficients when regressing $\mathbf{X}_1$ on $\mathbf{X}_2$. Conditioning in a Gaussian is _the same thing_ as linear regression. This is not a coincidence — it is the theorem.

**Conditional covariance:** $\Sigma_{1|2} = \Sigma_{11} - \Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{21}$

This is the **Schur complement** of $\Sigma_{22}$ in $\Sigma$. Two remarkable facts:

1. $\Sigma_{1|2}$ does not depend on the observed value $\mathbf{x}_2$. Conditioning always reduces variance by the same amount, regardless of what we observe. This is a purely Gaussian phenomenon.
2. $\Sigma_{1|2} \leq \Sigma_{11}$ (in the PSD sense) — conditioning can only reduce uncertainty. The reduction $\Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{21}$ is always PSD.

## The Geometry: Ellipsoids and Eigenstructure

The PDF's level sets are the ellipsoids: $${(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}) = c^2}$$

Spectral decompose $\Sigma = U\Lambda U^\top$ where $U$ is orthogonal (eigenvectors) and $\Lambda = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_k)$ (eigenvalues, all positive since $\Sigma \succ 0$).

In the coordinate system aligned with $U$, the ellipsoid becomes a sphere rescaled by $\sqrt{\lambda_i}$ in each direction. The eigenvectors of $\Sigma$ are the **principal axes** of the ellipsoid; the eigenvalues determine the **half-lengths** $c\sqrt{\lambda_i}$.

- Large $\lambda_i$ = high variance in that direction = elongated ellipsoid axis.
- Small $\lambda_i$ = low variance in that direction = compressed ellipsoid axis.
- Equal $\lambda_i$ = $\Sigma = \sigma^2 I$ = spherical level sets = i.i.d. normal coordinates.

**The Mahalanobis distance** $d_M(\mathbf{x}) = \sqrt{(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})}$ is Euclidean distance after the change of variables $\tilde{\mathbf{x}} = \Lambda^{-1/2}U^\top(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})$ — it whitens the distribution. In whitened coordinates, the MVN is exactly i.i.d. $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and all level sets are spheres.

## The Moment Generating Function

$$M_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{t}) = \mathbb{E}[e^{\mathbf{t}^\top \mathbf{X}}] = \exp!\left(\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{t} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{t}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{t}\right)$$

_Proof._ Reduce to the univariate case: $\mathbf{t}^\top\mathbf{X} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{t}^\top\boldsymbol{\mu},, \mathbf{t}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{t})$ by Definition 3, so $\mathbb{E}[e^{\mathbf{t}^\top\mathbf{X}}]$ is the MGF of a univariate normal evaluated at 1: $$= \exp!\left(\mathbf{t}^\top\boldsymbol{\mu} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{t}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{t}\right) \qquad \square$$

The MGF uniquely characterises the distribution and is the fastest way to identify sums and linear transforms as normal.

## Bivariate Normal: The Special Case to Visualise

For $k=2$, $\boldsymbol{\mu} = (\mu_1, \mu_2)^\top$, $\Sigma = \begin{pmatrix}\sigma_1^2 & \rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 \ \rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 & \sigma_2^2\end{pmatrix}$:

$$f(x_1,x_2) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_1\sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho^2}}\exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)}\left[\frac{(x_1-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} - 2\rho\frac{(x_1-\mu_1)(x_2-\mu_2)}{\sigma_1\sigma_2} + \frac{(x_2-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2}\right]\right)$$

The conditional distribution $X_1 \mid X_2 = x_2$: $$X_1 \mid X_2 = x_2 ;\sim; \mathcal{N}!\left(\mu_1 + \rho\frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_2}(x_2-\mu_2),;; \sigma_1^2(1-\rho^2)\right)$$

Reading this:

- Conditional mean shifts by $\rho\frac{\sigma_1}{\sigma_2}$ per unit deviation of $x_2$ from $\mu_2$. This is the regression coefficient.
- Conditional variance is $\sigma_1^2(1-\rho^2)$ — reduced from $\sigma_1^2$ by factor $(1-\rho^2)$. At $\rho = \pm 1$: zero conditional variance (exact linear relationship). At $\rho = 0$: no reduction ($X_1 \perp X_2$).

## Summary of Key Properties

|Property|Statement|
|---|---|
|Closure under linear maps|$B\mathbf{X}+\mathbf{c} \sim \mathcal{N}(B\boldsymbol{\mu}+\mathbf{c}, B\Sigma B^\top)$|
|Marginals|$X_i \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_i, \Sigma_{ii})$|
|Uncorrelated $\Leftrightarrow$ independent|Only for jointly normal r.v.'s|
|Conditional distribution|Normal, mean linear in conditioning value|
|Conditional variance|Constant (doesn't depend on observed value)|
|Level sets|Ellipsoids aligned with eigenvectors of $\Sigma$|
|MGF|$\exp(\boldsymbol{\mu}^\top\mathbf{t} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{t}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{t})$|

_What I find most striking: the conditional covariance $\Sigma_{1|2} = \Sigma_{11} - \Sigma_{12}\Sigma_{22}^{-1}\Sigma_{21}$ is the same for every value of $\mathbf{x}_2$. In a non-Gaussian distribution, variance could depend on what you condition on. In a Gaussian, it never does. The uncertainty remaining after observation is fixed by the covariance structure alone — observation tells you where you are, but the shape of your residual uncertainty is predetermined._
