

> _The standard proof of linearity goes: "use the definition, rearrange the sum." Fine. But it gives no intuition for why independence never enters. The 2D LOTUS proof does. Once you see this, linearity stops feeling like an algebraic accident and starts feeling geometrically inevitable._

---

## What We're Proving

**Theorem (Linearity of Expectation).** For any two random variables $X$ and $Y$ defined on the same probability space (no independence required): $$\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y]$$

---

## The Setup: Joint Distribution

$X$ and $Y$ have a joint distribution. Whether they're discrete, continuous, or mixed — there exists a joint PMF/PDF $p(x, y)$ (or $f(x,y)$) over the sample space $\mathbb{R}^2$.

The marginals recover from it: $$p_X(x) = \sum_y p(x,y), \qquad p_Y(y) = \sum_x p(x,y)$$

This is the key structural fact. The marginal is obtained by **summing out** (integrating out) the other variable. No independence. No factoring. Just the definition of a joint distribution.

---

## The 2D LOTUS Proof

**LOTUS (Law Of The Unconscious Statistician)** in 2D says: if $g: \mathbb{R}^2 \to \mathbb{R}$ is a function of two random variables, then: $$\mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)] = \sum_x \sum_y g(x,y), p(x,y)$$

We want $\mathbb{E}[X + Y]$, so take $g(x,y) = x + y$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \sum_x \sum_y (x + y), p(x,y)$$

Split the sum (linearity of _finite_ summation — this is where we cash in):

$$= \sum_x \sum_y x, p(x,y) ;+; \sum_x \sum_y y, p(x,y)$$

**First term:** Factor out $x$ from the inner sum over $y$:

$$\sum_x \sum_y x, p(x,y) = \sum_x x \underbrace{\left(\sum_y p(x,y)\right)}_{= , p_X(x)} = \sum_x x, p_X(x) = \mathbb{E}[X]$$

**Second term:** Factor out $y$ from the inner sum over $x$:

$$\sum_x \sum_y y, p(x,y) = \sum_y y \underbrace{\left(\sum_x p(x,y)\right)}_{= , p_Y(y)} = \sum_y y, p_Y(y) = \mathbb{E}[Y]$$

Therefore: $$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y]}$$

$\square$

---

## Why Independence Never Appears

Look carefully at where the proof uses the joint distribution $p(x,y)$.

It uses it exactly once: to recover the marginals by summing out. The step $\sum_y p(x,y) = p_X(x)$ is just the definition of the marginal — it holds for **any** joint distribution, dependent or not.

Independence would give us $p(x,y) = p_X(x),p_Y(y)$. But we never needed to factor the joint. The proof works in the 2D grid of $(x,y)$ pairs and collapses each dimension separately. The dependence structure of $X$ and $Y$ determines _which_ cells $(x,y)$ have probability mass and _how much_ — but once you sum out a row or a column, the other variable's marginal is what's left. Every time. Regardless of correlation.

> _The intuition: $\mathbb{E}[X]$ only cares about the marginal of $X$. $\mathbb{E}[Y]$ only cares about the marginal of $Y$. And $\mathbb{E}[X+Y]$ is just asking both questions simultaneously. The joint structure determines how $X$ and $Y$ co-vary, but marginals don't care about that — they've already forgotten it._

## The Continuous Version

Identical argument with integrals. By 2D LOTUS:

$$\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty}\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} (x + y), f(x,y), dx, dy$$

Split the integrand:

$$= \int!!\int x, f(x,y), dx, dy ;+; \int!!\int y, f(x,y), dx, dy$$

**First integral:** Integrate out $y$ first:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x,y),dy\right) dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x, f_X(x), dx = \mathbb{E}[X]$$

**Second integral:** Integrate out $x$ first:

$$\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} y \left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x,y), dx\right) dy = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} y, f_Y(y), dy = \mathbb{E}[Y]$$

Done. Same proof, same punchline. Fubini's theorem justifies swapping the order of integration (provided the integral converges absolutely, which $\mathbb{E}[|X|] < \infty$ and $\mathbb{E}[|Y|] < \infty$ guarantee).

## The Geometric Picture

Think of the joint PMF $p(x,y)$ as a **mass distribution over a 2D grid**. Each lattice point $(x,y)$ carries weight $p(x,y)$.

- $\mathbb{E}[X]$ is the **$x$-coordinate of the centre of mass** of this distribution.
- $\mathbb{E}[Y]$ is the **$y$-coordinate of the centre of mass**.
- $\mathbb{E}[X+Y]$ is the **centre of mass projected onto the line $x = y$**, i.e. the expected value of the random variable that measures "diagonal distance."

The centre of mass of a 2D distribution decomposes into its coordinates. Of course it does — that's what centres of mass do. Independence has nothing to do with it. Whether the mass is smeared along the diagonal (positive correlation) or anti-diagonal (negative correlation), the $x$-coordinate of the centre is still $\mathbb{E}[X]$ and the $y$-coordinate is still $\mathbb{E}[Y]$.

> _This is the deepest version of the intuition. Linearity of expectation = linearity of centres of mass. And centres of mass are always linear in coordinates._

## Corollaries That Fall Out Immediately

Once you have $\mathbb{E}[X+Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y]$, everything else is algebra:

**Scaling:** $\mathbb{E}[cX] = c,\mathbb{E}[X]$ (trivial from the definition — multiply $g(x) = cx$ through the PMF/PDF).

**General linearity:** For any $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$: $$\mathbb{E}[aX + bY] = a,\mathbb{E}[X] + b,\mathbb{E}[Y]$$

**Finite sums:** By induction on $n$: $$\mathbb{E}!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i]$$

No independence anywhere. This is why we can compute $\mathbb{E}[\text{number of fixed points of a random permutation}]$ or $\mathbb{E}[\text{number of matches in birthday problem}]$ so effortlessly — we write the count as a sum of indicator r.v.'s and linearity does the rest.

---

## The Indicator Trick (LOTUS + Linearity in Action)

If $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ are events (possibly dependent!), and $X = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ counts how many occur, then:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{A_i}] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{P}(A_i)$$

This works because $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_A] = \mathbb{P}(A)$ (trivial from LOTUS) and linearity requires nothing from the $A_i$'s.

_Example — matches in a hat-check problem (derangements):_ $n$ people, hats returned randomly. $X_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{person } i \text{ gets own hat}]$. The $X_i$ are terribly dependent. But: $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{P}(X_i = 1) = \sum_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{n} = 1$$

Always 1, regardless of $n$. Slick. And impossible to see without linearity.

---

## Technical Note: When Does This Fail?

The proof requires that the expectations exist (i.e. $\mathbb{E}[|X|] < \infty$ and $\mathbb{E}[|Y|] < \infty$). If either expectation is infinite, the rearrangement of sums/integrals may not be valid (you'd be doing $\infty - \infty$). So technically:

$$\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y] \quad \text{provided } \mathbb{E}[|X|] + \mathbb{E}[|Y|] < \infty$$

In practice this is always satisfied in the problems we work with, but worth knowing.
