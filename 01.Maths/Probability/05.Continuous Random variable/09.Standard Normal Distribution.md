
> _Everything about the Normal family reduces to the standard Normal. Not approximately — exactly. Once I understand $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ completely, I understand $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ for free via a single linear transformation. The standard Normal is not a special case; it is the master copy._


## Definition

$Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ has PDF:

$$\varphi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}, \quad z \in \mathbb{R}$$

and CDF:

$$\Phi(z) = \int_{-\infty}^z \varphi(t), dt = \int_{-\infty}^z \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-t^2/2}, dt$$

$\varphi$ is the PDF. $\Phi$ is the CDF. These two letters are reserved for this distribution throughout all of statistics and I should never use them for anything else in a probability context.

Both have no closed form for arbitrary $z$ — $\Phi$ is a special function, evaluated numerically. But its properties are entirely derivable, and those properties are what I actually use.

---

## Properties of $\varphi$

**Symmetry.** $\varphi(-z) = \varphi(z)$ for all $z$. The PDF is an even function. This follows directly from the exponent: $(-z)^2 = z^2$.

**Mode.** $\varphi$ is maximized at $z = 0$, where $\varphi(0) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \approx 0.3989$.

**Inflection points.** Compute $\varphi''(z)$:

$$\varphi'(z) = -z \cdot \varphi(z)$$

$$\varphi''(z) = -\varphi(z) - z \cdot \varphi'(z) = -\varphi(z) + z^2 \varphi(z) = (z^2 - 1)\varphi(z)$$

$\varphi''(z) = 0$ when $z^2 = 1$, i.e., $z = \pm 1$. The PDF transitions from concave to convex (and vice versa) at $z = \pm 1$. The inflection points are exactly one standard deviation from the mean.

**Decay.** For large $|z|$:

$$\varphi(z) \sim \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}$$

The decay is super-exponential — faster than $e^{-c|z|}$ for any $c > 0$. This is why the Normal has light tails: moments of all orders are finite, and the MGF exists everywhere on $\mathbb{R}$.

---

## Properties of $\Phi$

**Symmetry.** For all $z$:

$$\Phi(-z) = 1 - \Phi(z)$$

**Proof.** By the symmetry of $\varphi$:

$$\Phi(-z) = \int_{-\infty}^{-z} \varphi(t), dt$$

Substitute $t = -s$, $dt = -ds$. When $t = -\infty$, $s = \infty$; when $t = -z$, $s = z$:

$$= \int_{\infty}^{z} \varphi(-s)(-ds) = \int_z^{\infty} \varphi(s), ds = 1 - \Phi(z) \quad \square$$

This identity is used constantly. Whenever I need $\Phi$ of a negative argument, I reflect:

$$\Phi(-z) = 1 - \Phi(z)$$

**The CDF at zero.** $\Phi(0) = 1/2$. By symmetry, half the probability lies below 0 and half above.

**Derivative.** $\Phi'(z) = \varphi(z)$. The PDF is the derivative of the CDF — this is the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus, nothing more.

The values $z = 1.645$ and $z = 1.96$ come up in every confidence interval and hypothesis test. The first is the 95th percentile; the second puts 97.5% of the mass below it, leaving 2.5% in each tail — giving a 95% two-sided interval.

**The 68-95-99.7 rule** follows directly:

$$P(|Z| \leq 1) = \Phi(1) - \Phi(-1) = 2\Phi(1) - 1 \approx 0.6827$$ $$P(|Z| \leq 2) = 2\Phi(2) - 1 \approx 0.9545$$ $$P(|Z| \leq 3) = 2\Phi(3) - 1 \approx 0.9973$$

The general formula for symmetric intervals: $P(|Z| \leq z) = 2\Phi(z) - 1$.

## Standardization — How Everything Reduces to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$

If $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, define:

$$Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}$$

Then $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

**Proof.** The CDF of $Z$:

$$P(Z \leq z) = P!\left(\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma} \leq z\right) = P(X \leq \mu + \sigma z) = F_X(\mu + \sigma z)$$

where $F_X$ is the CDF of $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. By definition of the Normal CDF:

$$F_X(\mu + \sigma z) = \Phi!\left(\frac{(\mu + \sigma z) - \mu}{\sigma}\right) = \Phi(z) \quad \square$$

So any probability involving a general Normal reduces to $\Phi$:

$$P(X \leq x) = \Phi!\left(\frac{x - \mu}{\sigma}\right)$$

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \Phi!\left(\frac{b-\mu}{\sigma}\right) - \Phi!\left(\frac{a-\mu}{\sigma}\right)$$

This is the computation I do every time I work with a Normal distribution.

## Moments of $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$

The mean and variance were derived in [[08.The Normal Normalizing Constant]]]:

$$E[Z] = 0, \quad \text{Var}(Z) = 1$$

All odd moments are zero by symmetry: $E[Z^{2k+1}] = 0$ for all $k \geq 0$.

Even moments follow the pattern derived from the MGF:

$$E[Z^{2k}] = (2k-1)!! = 1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2k-1)$$

**Derivation.** From the MGF $M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2}$, expand:

$$e^{t^2/2} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(t^2/2)^k}{k!} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^{2k}}{2^k k!}$$

The coefficient of $t^{2k}$ is $\frac{1}{2^k k!}$. Since $M_Z(t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{E[Z^n]}{n!} t^n$, the coefficient of $t^{2k}$ is $\frac{E[Z^{2k}]}{(2k)!}$. Equating:

$$\frac{E[Z^{2k}]}{(2k)!} = \frac{1}{2^k k!} \implies E[Z^{2k}] = \frac{(2k)!}{2^k k!}$$

Now verify this equals $(2k-1)!!$:

$$\frac{(2k)!}{2^k k!} = \frac{1 \cdot 2 \cdot 3 \cdot 4 \cdots (2k-1) \cdot 2k}{2^k \cdot k!} = \frac{(1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2k-1)) \cdot (2 \cdot 4 \cdots 2k)}{2^k \cdot k!} = \frac{(2k-1)!! \cdot 2^k k!}{2^k k!} = (2k-1)!!$$

So:

$$E[Z^2] = 1, \quad E[Z^4] = 3, \quad E[Z^6] = 15, \quad E[Z^8] = 105$$

The kurtosis $E[Z^4] = 3$ is the baseline for excess kurtosis: any distribution with $E[Z^4] > 3$ has heavier tails than the Normal (leptokurtic), and any with $E[Z^4] < 3$ has lighter tails (platykurtic).

## The MGF

$$M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

The MGF exists for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$ — a consequence of the super-exponential decay of $\varphi$. This is not true for heavy-tailed distributions like the $t$ or Cauchy, whose MGFs do not exist.

**Derivation** was done in [[06.Normal Distribution]] by completing the square. I record it here for completeness:

$$M_Z(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tz} e^{-z^2/2}, dz = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty \exp!\left(-\frac{z^2 - 2tz}{2}\right) dz$$

Complete the square: $z^2 - 2tz = (z-t)^2 - t^2$:

$$= \frac{e^{t^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(z-t)^2/2}, dz = e^{t^2/2} \cdot 1$$

The integral is 1 because $e^{-(z-t)^2/2} / \sqrt{2\pi}$ is the PDF of $\mathcal{N}(t, 1)$, integrating to 1.

## The Quantile Function $\Phi^{-1}$

The quantile function (inverse CDF) $\Phi^{-1}(p)$ gives the value $z$ such that $\Phi(z) = p$. It has no closed form.

**Key quantiles:**

|$p$|$\Phi^{-1}(p)$|Common name|
|---|---|---|
|$0.500$|$0$|Median|
|$0.841$|$1$|One $\sigma$ above mean|
|$0.950$|$1.645$|95th percentile|
|$0.975$|$1.96$|Used in 95% CI|
|$0.990$|$2.326$|99th percentile|
|$0.995$|$2.576$|Used in 99% CI|

**Symmetry of the quantile function.** Since $\Phi(-z) = 1 - \Phi(z)$:

$$\Phi^{-1}(1-p) = -\Phi^{-1}(p)$$

So I only need to know quantiles above the median — those below follow by negation.

## The Tail Probability and the Mill's Ratio

For large $z > 0$, the tail probability $P(Z > z) = 1 - \Phi(z)$ has the asymptotic bound:

$$\frac{\varphi(z)}{z}\left(1 - \frac{1}{z^2}\right) \leq 1 - \Phi(z) \leq \frac{\varphi(z)}{z}$$

**Proof of the upper bound.** For $t > 0$, note $1 < t/z$ when $t > z$. So:

$$1 - \Phi(z) = \int_z^\infty \varphi(t), dt < \int_z^\infty \frac{t}{z} \varphi(t), dt = \frac{1}{z} \int_z^\infty t \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-t^2/2}, dt$$

The last integral is $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \Big[-e^{-t^2/2}\Big]_z^\infty = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2} = \varphi(z)$. So:

$$1 - \Phi(z) < \frac{\varphi(z)}{z} \quad \square$$

The practical implication: tail probabilities decay like $\frac{e^{-z^2/2}}{z\sqrt{2\pi}}$, which is extremely fast. At $z = 4$: $P(Z > 4) \approx 3.2 \times 10^{-5}$. At $z = 5$: $P(Z > 5) \approx 2.9 \times 10^{-7}$.

This exponentially fast decay is what makes the Normal a "thin-tailed" distribution and distinguishes it sharply from power-law tails like the Cauchy or Pareto.

## $Z^2 \sim \chi^2(1)$

If $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, then $Z^2 \sim \chi^2(1)$.

**Proof.** Let $Y = Z^2$. For $y > 0$:

$$P(Y \leq y) = P(Z^2 \leq y) = P(-\sqrt{y} \leq Z \leq \sqrt{y}) = \Phi(\sqrt{y}) - \Phi(-\sqrt{y}) = 2\Phi(\sqrt{y}) - 1$$

Differentiate to get the PDF of $Y$:

$$f_Y(y) = 2 \cdot \varphi(\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} = \frac{\varphi(\sqrt{y})}{\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \frac{e^{-y/2}}{\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} y^{-1/2} e^{-y/2}$$

Recognize this as $\text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2)$, using $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$:

$$f_Y(y) = \frac{(1/2)^{1/2}}{\Gamma(1/2)} y^{1/2 - 1} e^{-y/2} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} y^{-1/2} e^{-y/2} \quad \checkmark$$

This is the $\chi^2(1)$ distribution by definition. It is also $\text{Gamma}(1/2,, 1/2)$, confirming the Gamma–chi-squared connection in [[02.Formula Reference — All Distributions]]].

## The Half-Normal Distribution

The absolute value $|Z|$ for $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ has the half-Normal distribution.

For $y > 0$:

$$P(|Z| \leq y) = P(-y \leq Z \leq y) = 2\Phi(y) - 1$$

PDF of $|Z|$:

$$f_{|Z|}(y) = 2\varphi(y) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} e^{-y^2/2}, \quad y > 0$$

The factor of 2 comes from folding the symmetric distribution onto the positive half-line.

**Mean of $|Z|$:**

$$E[|Z|] = \int_0^\infty y \cdot 2\varphi(y), dy = 2 \int_0^\infty \frac{y}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-y^2/2}, dy = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \Big[-e^{-y^2/2}\Big]_0^\infty = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}$$

This value $\sqrt{2/\pi} \approx 0.798$ comes up in the derivation of the mean of the Rayleigh distribution and in certain shrinkage estimators.

## The Standard Normal as a Building Block

The standard Normal generates several other distributions through simple operations:

| Operation                                   | Result                               |
| ------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------ |
| $Z^2$                                       | $\chi^2(1) = \text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2)$ |
| $Z_1^2 + \cdots + Z_k^2$, i.i.d.            | $\chi^2(k) = \text{Gamma}(k/2, 1/2)$ |
| $Z / \sqrt{V/k}$, $V \sim \chi^2(k)$ indep. | $t(k)$ (Student's $t$)               |
| $Z_1 / Z_2$, i.i.d.                         | $\text{Cauchy}(0,1)$                 |
| $e^{\mu + \sigma Z}$                        | $\text{LogNormal}(\mu, \sigma^2)$    |
| $\mu + \sigma Z$                            | $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$         |

The last row is the standardization map in reverse — from $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ to any Normal. The others show the standard Normal sitting at the center of the named distribution families.

## The Gaussian Integral in Different Forms

Several equivalent forms come up in computations and it helps to have them all in one place:

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}, dz = \sqrt{2\pi}$$

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2}, dz = \sqrt{\pi}$$

$$\int_0^\infty e^{-z^2}, dz = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2}$$

$$\int_0^\infty e^{-az^2}, dz = \frac{1}{2}\sqrt{\frac{\pi}{a}}, \quad a > 0$$

The second follows from the first by substituting $z \mapsto z/\sqrt{2}$. The third is half the second by symmetry. The fourth generalizes the third. These show up when computing moments of the Normal, working with the Gamma function at half-integer arguments, and evaluating Gaussian integrals in other contexts.

The connection to the Gamma function: setting $t = az^2$, $dt = 2az, dz$ in the fourth form gives:

$$\int_0^\infty e^{-az^2}, dz = \frac{1}{2a^{1/2}} \int_0^\infty t^{-1/2} e^{-t}, dt = \frac{\Gamma(1/2)}{2\sqrt{a}} = \frac{\sqrt{\pi}}{2\sqrt{a}}$$

confirming $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$, which is the same $\sqrt{\pi}$ that generates the $\sqrt{2\pi}$ normalizing constant.
