
> _The CLT is the reason probability is useful. It says that the universe, on aggregate, is Normal — not because individual components are Normal, but because addition, done enough times, forces Normality regardless of what you started with. That is a miracle that deserves a proof._

## The Statement — Precisely

**Theorem (Central Limit Theorem).** Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. random variables with mean $\mu = E[X_i]$ and variance $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}(X_i)$, where $\sigma^2 \in (0, \infty)$. Define the standardized sum:

$$Z_n = \frac{S_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma}$$

where $S_n = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ and $\bar{X}_n = S_n / n$. Then:

$$Z_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

That is, for every $x \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\lim_{n \to \infty} P(Z_n \leq x) = \Phi(x)$$

Three conditions that must all hold: the $X_i$ are identically distributed, they are independent, and the variance is finite and positive. Each condition is doing real work.

---

## What the CLT Is and Is Not Saying

The CLT is a statement about **convergence in distribution** of a sequence of standardized partial sums. It is not saying:

- That $S_n$ is approximately Normal. It is not — $S_n$ has mean $n\mu$ and variance $n\sigma^2$, both growing without bound.
- That $\bar{X}_n$ is approximately Normal. It is not — $\bar{X}_n$ has mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2/n$, collapsing to a point.
- That the distribution of $X_i$ is approximately Normal.

It is saying that after the specific standardization $Z_n = (\bar{X}_n - \mu) / (\sigma/\sqrt{n})$, the distribution of the result converges to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

Practically, this means: for large $n$,

$$\bar{X}_n \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}!\left(\mu, \frac{\sigma^2}{n}\right)$$

and

$$S_n \overset{\text{approx}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(n\mu, n\sigma^2)$$

These are approximations whose quality depends on $n$ and on how far the $X_i$ are from Normal. The CLT says they become exact in the limit.

---

## Proof via MGFs

This is the proof I want to own completely. It requires that the MGF of $X_i$ exists in a neighborhood of zero — a condition slightly stronger than finite variance, but satisfied by all the named distributions I work with.

**Setup.** Without loss of generality, standardize each $X_i$ first. Let:

$$Y_i = \frac{X_i - \mu}{\sigma}$$

Then the $Y_i$ are i.i.d. with $E[Y_i] = 0$, $E[Y_i^2] = 1$. And:

$$Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$$

**Step 1: MGF of $Z_n$.**

By independence:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = E!\left[e^{t Z_n}\right] = E!\left[\exp!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}} \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i\right)\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n E!\left[e^{(t/\sqrt{n}) Y_i}\right] = \left[M_Y!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n$$

The factorization uses independence of the $Y_i$: $M_{Y_1 + \cdots + Y_n}(t) = \prod_i M_{Y_i}(t)$.

**Step 2: Taylor expand $M_Y$ around 0.**

Since $M_Y$ exists in a neighborhood of 0, it is infinitely differentiable there. Expand:

$$M_Y(s) = M_Y(0) + M_Y'(0) s + \frac{M_Y''(0)}{2} s^2 + O(s^3)$$

Now: $M_Y(0) = 1$, $M_Y'(0) = E[Y] = 0$, $M_Y''(0) = E[Y^2] = 1$. So:

$$M_Y(s) = 1 + \frac{s^2}{2} + O(s^3)$$

**Step 3: Substitute $s = t/\sqrt{n}$.**

$$M_Y!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})$$

**Step 4: Raise to the $n$-th power and take $n \to \infty$.**

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right)^n$$

Use the fundamental limit $\left(1 + \frac{x}{n} + o(1/n)\right)^n \to e^x$:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) \to e^{t^2/2}$$

**Step 5: Identify the limit.**

$e^{t^2/2}$ is the MGF of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. By the **continuity theorem for MGFs** — if $M_{Z_n}(t) \to M(t)$ for all $t$ in a neighborhood of 0, and $M$ is the MGF of some distribution, then $Z_n \xrightarrow{d}$ that distribution — we conclude $Z_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. $\square$

---

## The Continuity Theorem — Why the Proof Is Complete

The continuity theorem for MGFs is doing real work in the last step. It says MGF convergence implies distributional convergence. I am using it as a black box here, but I should know what it asserts:

> If $M_{X_n}(t) \to M_X(t)$ for all $t$ in a neighborhood of zero, then $X_n \xrightarrow{d} X$.

The converse also holds. This theorem is the MGF analogue of the continuity theorem for characteristic functions (Fourier transforms of distributions), which is the version used in the fully general proof of the CLT that does not require the MGF to exist. The characteristic function proof covers, for instance, $X_i \sim \text{Cauchy}$ — but wait, the CLT fails there. Let me be precise about why.

---

## Where the CLT Fails — The Conditions Are Not Decorative

### Finite variance is necessary

The CLT requires $\sigma^2 < \infty$. For $X_i \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$, the variance is infinite. The standardized sum $Z_n$ does not converge to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. In fact, $\bar{X}_n \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$ for every $n$ — the distribution does not shrink at all. Averaging Cauchy random variables accomplishes nothing because heavy-tailed outliers dominate the sum regardless of sample size.

More precisely: $\text{Cauchy}$ is a **stable distribution** with index $\alpha = 1$. Stable distributions with $\alpha < 2$ have infinite variance, and their normalized sums converge to a non-Gaussian stable distribution rather than the Normal. The Normal is the $\alpha = 2$ stable distribution.

### Independence is necessary

If the $X_i$ are identically distributed but dependent, the CLT can fail. The simplest example: let $X_i = X$ for all $i$, where $X$ is a fixed random variable with mean 0 and variance 1. Then $S_n = nX$ and $Z_n = \sqrt{n} X$. The distribution of $Z_n$ does not converge — it just scales $X$ by $\sqrt{n}$, blowing up.

Less pathologically, for stationary time series with strong positive serial correlation, the variance of $S_n$ grows faster than $n$, and the CLT fails in the standard form. Modified versions (the CLT for mixing processes) exist under weaker dependence conditions, but they require the dependence to decay fast enough.

### Identical distribution — the Lindeberg condition

The i.i.d. assumption can be relaxed. The **Lindeberg CLT** covers independent but not identically distributed random variables, replacing the i.i.d. assumption with a condition that no single summand dominates the sum. Precisely, the Lindeberg condition is:

For every $\varepsilon > 0$:

$$\frac{1}{s_n^2} \sum_{i=1}^n E!\left[(X_i - \mu_i)^2 \cdot \mathbf{1}_{|X_i - \mu_i| > \varepsilon s_n}\right] \to 0$$

where $s_n^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n \sigma_i^2$. This says: the contribution to the total variance from any individual term with large deviation goes to zero. Under this condition, the CLT holds without identical distribution.

The i.i.d. CLT I proved above is a special case of the Lindeberg CLT, where all $\mu_i = \mu$, $\sigma_i = \sigma$, and the Lindeberg condition reduces to the finiteness of the second moment.

---

## Rates of Convergence — The Berry-Esseen Theorem

The CLT tells me the limit but not the rate. The **Berry-Esseen theorem** gives a quantitative bound:

$$\sup_x \left|P(Z_n \leq x) - \Phi(x)\right| \leq \frac{C \cdot E[|Y|^3]}{\sqrt{n}}$$

where $C$ is a universal constant (best known value $C \approx 0.4748$) and $Y = (X - \mu)/\sigma$.

**What this means:** The error in the Normal approximation is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ uniformly in $x$, controlled by the third absolute moment of the standardized variable. Distributions with heavier tails (larger third moment) converge more slowly. Symmetric distributions (odd third moment = 0) often converge faster in practice.

**Practical implication:** For the Bernoulli$(p)$ case, $E[|Y|^3] = (p^2(1-p) + (1-p)^2 p) / (p(1-p))^{3/2}$, which blows up as $p \to 0$ or $p \to 1$. This explains why the Normal approximation to the Binomial is poor when $p$ is close to 0 or 1 — and why the Poisson approximation is preferred in that regime.

## The CLT in Action — Applications

### Binomial approximation

$\text{Bin}(n,p) \approx \mathcal{N}(np, np(1-p))$ for large $n$. More precisely:

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) \approx \Phi!\left(\frac{b + \frac{1}{2} - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right) - \Phi!\left(\frac{a - \frac{1}{2} - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)$$

The $\pm 1/2$ terms are the **continuity correction**, which accounts for approximating a discrete distribution by a continuous one. Without it, $P(X = k) \approx 0$ (since a single point has zero Normal probability). With it, $P(X = k) \approx P(k - 1/2 \leq Y \leq k + 1/2)$ for $Y \sim \mathcal{N}(np, np(1-p))$.

### Poisson approximation

$\text{Pois}(n) \approx \mathcal{N}(n, n)$ for large $n$, by the same argument: $\text{Pois}(n)$ is the sum of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Pois}(1)$ variables.

### Sample mean and confidence intervals

The core statistical application: if $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d. with unknown mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, then for large $n$:

$$P!\left(\left|\bar{X}_n - \mu\right| \leq z_{\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}\right) \approx 1 - \alpha$$

where $z_{\alpha/2} = \Phi^{-1}(1 - \alpha/2)$. For $\alpha = 0.05$: $z_{0.025} = 1.96$. This is the 95% confidence interval for $\mu$:

$$\bar{X}_n \pm 1.96 \cdot \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$$

The entire frequentist theory of estimation and hypothesis testing for means rests on the CLT. Without it, this interval would have no justification for non-Normal data.

## The CLT vs. the Law of Large Numbers

These two theorems are often conflated. They are different statements about different things.

**Law of Large Numbers (LLN):** $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{P} \mu$. The sample mean converges in probability to the true mean. This is about where $\bar{X}_n$ ends up — it concentrates at $\mu$.

**CLT:** $\frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. After zooming in by a factor of $\sqrt{n}$, the fluctuations of $\bar{X}_n$ around $\mu$ are Normally distributed.

The LLN describes the first-order behavior: $\bar{X}_n \approx \mu$. The CLT describes the second-order behavior: the fluctuations around $\mu$ have size $\sigma/\sqrt{n}$ and a Normal shape.

To remember the distinction: the LLN says the error goes to zero. The CLT says, right before it reaches zero, what shape it had.

## The Delta Method — CLT for Nonlinear Functions

If $g$ is differentiable at $\mu$ with $g'(\mu) \neq 0$, and $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, then:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\ [g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2)$$

**Proof sketch.** Taylor expand $g$ around $\mu$:

$$g(\bar{X}_n) \approx g(\mu) + g'(\mu)(\bar{X}_n - \mu)$$

Multiply both sides by $\sqrt{n}$:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)) \approx g'(\mu) \cdot \sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\ [g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2)$$

The delta method is the CLT for smooth transformations of sample means. It is used constantly in statistics — for confidence intervals of proportions, odds ratios, log-transformed means — anywhere you want to apply the CLT to a quantity that is a function of $\bar{X}_n$ rather than $\bar{X}_n$ itself.

## A Personal Summary of Why This Theorem Is Deep

The CLT is deep for three reasons that I try to hold simultaneously.

**First**, it is universal. The distribution of $X_i$ is irrelevant — Bernoulli, Poisson, Exponential, uniform, anything — as long as it has finite variance. The limiting distribution is always the same. The Normal is not a special case; it is a fixed point. All finite-variance distributions, under summation and rescaling, flow toward the Normal.

**Second**, it explains a pattern in nature. Heights, measurement errors, test scores, and countless other natural quantities are approximately Normal not because the underlying mechanism is Gaussian, but because these quantities are the aggregate of many small independent contributions. The CLT is the mathematical explanation for why bell curves appear everywhere.

**Third**, the proof reveals something structural. The key step is the Taylor expansion $M_Y(s) = 1 + s^2/2 + O(s^3)$. The constant and linear terms are fixed for all standardized variables: $M_Y(0) = 1$ always, $M_Y'(0) = E[Y] = 0$ by construction. The first term that distinguishes distributions is the quadratic term, $E[Y^2]/2 = 1/2$, which is also fixed by the standardization. Everything that distinguishes the original distribution is in the $O(s^3)$ remainder, which gets killed by raising to the $n$-th power as $n \to \infty$. The Normal emerges because standardization pins down the first two terms of the MGF expansion, and those two terms are enough to determine the limit.
