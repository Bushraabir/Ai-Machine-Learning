
> _The folded normal is what happens when you care about magnitude but not sign. It sounds like a minor technicality — until you realize it's secretly the half-normal, the $\chi$ distribution in disguise, and the reason $|Z|$ behaves so cleanly. Don't sleep on it._

---

## Definition

Let $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. The **folded normal** is the distribution of:

$$Y = |X|$$

We are literally "folding" the real line at zero — the negative half folds onto the positive half. The name is geometric and exact.

> _Think of the PDF of $X$ as a piece of paper. Folding it at zero means the left half flips onto the right half and the two layers add. That's it. That's the whole construction._

---

## PDF Derivation

This is a **transformation of a random variable** — one of the core techniques from Stat 110. Let's do it properly.

**Step 1: CDF approach.**

For $y \geq 0$:

$$F_Y(y) = P(Y \leq y) = P(|X| \leq y) = P(-y \leq X \leq y)$$

$$= \Phi!\left(\frac{y - \mu}{\sigma}\right) - \Phi!\left(\frac{-y - \mu}{\sigma}\right)$$

**Step 2: Differentiate to get the PDF.**

$$f_Y(y) = \frac{d}{dy} F_Y(y) = \frac{1}{\sigma}\phi!\left(\frac{y-\mu}{\sigma}\right) + \frac{1}{\sigma}\phi!\left(\frac{y+\mu}{\sigma}\right), \qquad y \geq 0$$

where $\phi$ is the standard normal PDF. Expanding:

$$\boxed{f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}}\left[\exp!\left(-\frac{(y-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) + \exp!\left(-\frac{(y+\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)\right], \quad y \geq 0}$$

and $f_Y(y) = 0$ for $y < 0$.

> _Two bumps, potentially. One centred at $+\mu$, one at $-\mu$ that got folded over to $+\mu$ as well — except they don't generally land on top of each other unless $\mu = 0$. This is why the shape depends heavily on $\mu/\sigma$._

---

## The Special Case: $\mu = 0$ (Half-Normal)

When $\mu = 0$, the two exponential terms are equal and the PDF collapses to:

$$f_Y(y) = \frac{2}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp!\left(-\frac{y^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma} \exp!\left(-\frac{y^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad y \geq 0$$

This is the **half-normal distribution**. It is the folded normal's cleanest avatar.

**Why the factor of 2?** Because all the probability mass that used to live on $(-\infty, 0)$ has been folded onto $(0, \infty)$, doubling the density there. Normalization is preserved:

$$\int_0^{\infty} \frac{2}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-y^2/2\sigma^2},dy = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{2} = 1 \checkmark$$

## Moments

### General $\mu$, $\sigma$

Let $\lambda = \mu/\sigma$ (the signal-to-noise ratio, morally). Then:

**Mean:**

$$E[Y] = \sigma\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}},\exp!\left(-\frac{\mu^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) + \mu\left[1 - 2\Phi!\left(-\frac{\mu}{\sigma}\right)\right]$$

$$= \sigma\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}},e^{-\lambda^2/2} + \mu\left(2\Phi(\lambda) - 1\right)$$

**Second moment** (this one's clean):

$$E[Y^2] = E[X^2] = \mu^2 + \sigma^2$$

Because $Y^2 = X^2$, so $E[Y^2] = E[X^2] = \text{Var}(X) + (E[X])^2 = \sigma^2 + \mu^2$. Elegant.

**Variance:**

$$\text{Var}(Y) = \mu^2 + \sigma^2 - (E[Y])^2$$

### Special case $\mu = 0$:

$$E[Y] = \sigma\sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}, \qquad E[Y^2] = \sigma^2, \qquad \text{Var}(Y) = \sigma^2!\left(1 - \frac{2}{\pi}\right)$$

> _The mean of the half-normal is $\sigma\sqrt{2/\pi}$. That factor $\sqrt{2/\pi}$ shows up constantly in truncated/folded normal calculations. It comes from $E[Z \mid Z > 0] = \sqrt{2/\pi}$ for $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, which is itself a nice exercise via the Gaussian integral._

**Derivation of $E[|Z|]$ for $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$:**

$$E[|Z|] = 2\int_0^{\infty} z \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2},dz$$

Let $u = z^2/2$, $du = z,dz$:

$$= \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_0^{\infty} e^{-u},du = \frac{2}{\sqrt{2\pi}} = \sqrt{\frac{2}{\pi}}$$

## Connection to the $\chi$ Distribution

Let $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Then $|Z|$ is the half-normal, which is also the **$\chi$ distribution with 1 degree of freedom** ($\chi_1$).

More generally: if $Z_1, \ldots, Z_k \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, then:

$$\sqrt{Z_1^2 + \cdots + Z_k^2} \sim \chi_k$$

The folded normal ($k=1$) is the simplest case. The $\chi_2$ distribution is the Rayleigh distribution. These are all "radial" distributions — they describe the distance from the origin in $k$-dimensional standard normal space.

$$|Z| \sim \chi_1 \qquad \longrightarrow \qquad Z^2 \sim \chi^2_1$$

So the folded normal sits right at the base of the $\chi$ and $\chi^2$ family trees. When you square it, you get the $\chi^2_1$ distribution. When you generalize to $k$ dimensions, you get $\chi_k$.

---

## The Folding as a Change of Variables — Formally

The function $g(x) = |x|$ is **not monotone** on all of $\mathbb{R}$, so the standard one-to-one change-of-variables formula doesn't directly apply. You split the domain:

$$g^{-1}({y}) = {y, -y} \quad \text{for } y > 0$$

The correct formula for the density of $Y = g(X)$ is:

$$f_Y(y) = \sum_{x : g(x) = y} \frac{f_X(x)}{|g'(x)|}$$

Here $g'(x) = \text{sgn}(x)$, so $|g'(x)| = 1$ everywhere except $x = 0$. The two preimages are $x = y$ and $x = -y$:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(y) \cdot 1 + f_X(-y) \cdot 1 = f_X(y) + f_X(-y)$$

which exactly recovers the PDF we derived via the CDF method. Good — the two approaches agree, as they must.

> _I always find it satisfying when the CDF method and the change-of-variables formula give the same answer. It's not obvious they should — one is analytic, one is geometric — but of course they do. The distribution of $Y$ is unique._

---

## Shape Behaviour as $\mu/\sigma$ Varies

The ratio $\lambda = |\mu|/\sigma$ controls everything:

|$\lambda$|Shape of $f_Y$|
|---|---|
|$\lambda \approx 0$|Half-normal: unimodal, mode at 0, right-skewed|
|$\lambda \approx 1$|Slightly bimodal tendency; skewness decreasing|
|$\lambda \gg 1$|Approximately $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ truncated to $(0,\infty)$; nearly symmetric|

When $\mu$ is very large relative to $\sigma$, almost all the mass of $X$ is already positive, so $|X| \approx X$ and the folded normal looks like the original normal. The folding is asymptotically irrelevant.

When $\mu = 0$, the distribution is maximally "folded" — equal mass on each side of zero, all of it collapsed to the right.

---

## Why This Comes Up

**1. Absolute errors.** In estimation, you often care about $|X - \mu|$, not the signed error. Folded normal = distribution of absolute deviation when the error is Gaussian.

**2. Signal processing / physics.** When you measure an amplitude and lose phase information, you're folding. The Rician distribution (for instance) is a generalization.

**3. The $\chi$ family.** As noted above — understanding the folded normal is the first step toward understanding $\chi_k$, which underlies the $t$-distribution, $F$-distribution, and hence all of classical inference.

**4. Simulation.** If you generate $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and want a positive-valued standard-normal-shaped variable, $|Z|$ is your cleanest option.

## Truncated vs. Folded — Don't Confuse These

This tripped me up early. They are **not the same**:

| | Folded Normal $Y = |X|$ | Truncated Normal $Y = X \mid X > 0$ | |---|---|---| | **Construction** | Reflect negative values | Throw away negative values | | **Total probability** | 1 (all mass kept) | 1 (renormalized) | | **PDF at $y > 0$** | $f_X(y) + f_X(-y)$ | $\frac{f_X(y)}{\Phi(\mu/\sigma)}$ (normalizing constant) | | **Mass below 0** | 0 (by construction) | 0 (conditioned away) | | **$E[Y]$** | $E[|X|]$ | $E[X \mid X > 0]$ |

The truncated normal _conditions_ on being positive and adjusts the density upward to compensate. The folded normal _adds_ the density from $-y$ to the density at $+y$. Different objects, different applications.

## Personal Notes

- The second moment formula $E[Y^2] = \mu^2 + \sigma^2$ is the one to tattoo on your brain. It falls out immediately because $Y^2 = X^2$, and it means you can always get $\text{Var}(Y)$ once you have $E[Y]$.
- The derivation via CDF is the _right_ way to think about non-monotone transformations in general. Get comfortable with it — it reappears constantly (e.g., deriving the $\chi^2$ density, the $F$ density, etc.).
- The $\mu = 0$ case is so clean that it's worth solving everything for the half-normal first and then thinking about what breaks for $\mu \neq 0$.
- Blitzstein emphasizes the **LOTUS** and the **CDF method** as the two workhorses for distributions of transformations. This note is a good case study for the CDF method with a non-injective function.
