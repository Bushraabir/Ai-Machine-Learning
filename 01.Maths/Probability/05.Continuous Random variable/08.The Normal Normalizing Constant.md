# Why $c = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}$

> _The Gaussian integral cannot be solved by any standard technique. It requires leaving the real line entirely, going to $\mathbb{R}^2$, exploiting rotational symmetry, and coming back down. The fact that $\pi$ appears in a probability distribution is a direct consequence of that geometry._


## The Setup

The standard Normal PDF is claimed to be:

$$f(z) = c \cdot e^{-z^2/2}, \quad z \in \mathbb{R}$$

for some constant $c > 0$. For $f$ to be a valid PDF, it must integrate to 1:

$$c \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}, dz = 1 \implies c = \frac{1}{\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}, dz}$$

So everything reduces to computing $I = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}, dz$.

---

## Why Direct Antidifferentiation Fails

The function $e^{-z^2/2}$ has no elementary antiderivative. This is not a gap in technique — it is a theorem (Liouville's theorem on elementary integrals) that no composition of polynomials, exponentials, logarithms, and trigonometric functions equals $\int e^{-z^2/2}, dz$. The antiderivative exists as a function (by the Fundamental Theorem of Calculus) but cannot be written in closed form.

This means every standard single-variable technique — substitution, integration by parts, partial fractions — will fail. The definite integral over all of $\mathbb{R}$ must be computed by a different route entirely.

---

## The Double Integral Trick

The key insight: if I cannot compute $I$ directly, compute $I^2$ instead and take a square root.

$$I^2 = \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2}, dx\right)!\left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-y^2/2}, dy\right)$$

I have written the same integral twice, using different dummy variables $x$ and $y$. Since these are independent integrals, I can combine them into a double integral over $\mathbb{R}^2$:

$$I^2 = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2} \cdot e^{-y^2/2}, dx, dy = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x^2 + y^2)/2}, dx, dy$$

The exponents add because the integrand factors. Now I have converted a one-dimensional problem I cannot solve into a two-dimensional problem I can — because $x^2 + y^2$ is the squared distance from the origin in $\mathbb{R}^2$, which is naturally expressed in polar coordinates.

---

## Polar Coordinate Transformation

Substitute $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, where $r \in [0, \infty)$ and $\theta \in [0, 2\pi)$.

The key facts:

- $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$ (Pythagorean theorem)
- The area element transforms as $dx, dy = r, dr, d\theta$ (the factor $r$ is the Jacobian of the polar coordinate transformation)

The Jacobian $r$ is not optional — it accounts for the fact that at radius $r$, a small angular slice $d\theta$ sweeps out an arc of length $r, d\theta$, so the area element grows with $r$.

The integral becomes:

$$I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\infty e^{-r^2/2} \cdot r, dr, d\theta$$

The limits change: $r$ runs from $0$ to $\infty$ (all distances from origin), $\theta$ runs from $0$ to $2\pi$ (full rotation). Together these cover all of $\mathbb{R}^2$ exactly once.

---

## Evaluating the Transformed Integral

The double integral separates into a product because the integrand factors as a function of $r$ alone times a function of $\theta$ alone:

$$I^2 = \left(\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta\right)!\left(\int_0^\infty r e^{-r^2/2}, dr\right)$$

**Outer integral:**

$$\int_0^{2\pi} d\theta = 2\pi$$

This is where $\pi$ enters. The full rotation around the origin contributes exactly $2\pi$.

**Inner integral:**

$$\int_0^\infty r e^{-r^2/2}, dr$$

Substitute $u = r^2/2$, so $du = r, dr$. When $r = 0$, $u = 0$; when $r \to \infty$, $u \to \infty$:

$$\int_0^\infty e^{-u}, du = \Big[-e^{-u}\Big]_0^\infty = 0 - (-1) = 1$$

The factor $r$ in the integrand is precisely the $du$ from the substitution. Without it — if the integrand were just $e^{-r^2/2}$ — this substitution would not work. The Jacobian $r$ is what makes the inner integral tractable.

---

## Final Result

$$I^2 = 2\pi \cdot 1 = 2\pi$$

$$I = \sqrt{2\pi}$$

Therefore:

$$c = \frac{1}{I} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}$$

And the standard Normal PDF is confirmed:

$$f(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}$$

---

## Why $\pi$ Appears Here

This is worth pausing on. $\pi$ appears in the Normal distribution not because of anything circular in the probability setup, but because:

1. The trick requires going to $\mathbb{R}^2$.
2. In $\mathbb{R}^2$, the natural coordinate system for $e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}$ is polar.
3. The angular integration over a full rotation produces $2\pi$.

The circle is hiding inside the Gaussian. The rotational symmetry of $e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}$ — it depends only on the distance $r = \sqrt{x^2+y^2}$ from the origin, not on the angle — is the property that makes the polar transformation so powerful. This same rotational symmetry is one of the characterizations of the Normal distribution: it is the only spherically symmetric distribution with independent components.

---

## Derivation of the Mean — $E[Z] = 0$

With the PDF established, compute the mean:

$$E[Z] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty z \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}, dz$$

**By symmetry.** The integrand is $z \cdot f(z)$. Since $f(z) = f(-z)$ (even function) and $z$ is odd, the product $z \cdot f(z)$ is an odd function. The integral of any odd function over a symmetric interval is zero:

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty (\text{odd function}), dz = 0$$

provided the integral converges absolutely, which it does here since $z \cdot e^{-z^2/2}$ decays faster than any polynomial.

Therefore $E[Z] = 0$.

---

## Derivation of the Variance — $\text{Var}(Z) = 1$

Since $E[Z] = 0$:

$$\text{Var}(Z) = E[Z^2] - (E[Z])^2 = E[Z^2]$$

By LOTUS:

$$E[Z^2] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty z^2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}, dz = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty z^2 e^{-z^2/2}, dz$$

**Integration by parts.** Write $z^2 e^{-z^2/2} = z \cdot \left(z e^{-z^2/2}\right)$ and set:

$$u = z \implies du = dz$$ $$dv = z e^{-z^2/2}, dz \implies v = -e^{-z^2/2}$$

where $v$ is found by the substitution $w = z^2/2$, $dw = z, dz$, giving $\int z e^{-z^2/2}, dz = -e^{-z^2/2}$.

Apply integration by parts $\int u, dv = uv - \int v, du$:

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty z^2 e^{-z^2/2}, dz = \Big[-z e^{-z^2/2}\Big]_{-\infty}^\infty + \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}, dz$$

**Boundary term.** Evaluate $\lim_{z \to \pm\infty} z e^{-z^2/2}$. The exponential decay dominates the linear growth:

$$\lim_{z \to \infty} z e^{-z^2/2} = 0, \quad \lim_{z \to -\infty} z e^{-z^2/2} = 0$$

So the boundary term $\Big[-z e^{-z^2/2}\Big]_{-\infty}^\infty = 0 - 0 = 0$.

**Remaining integral.** What is left is:

$$\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}, dz = I = \sqrt{2\pi}$$

exactly the Gaussian integral already computed.

Therefore:

$$E[Z^2] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \sqrt{2\pi} = 1$$

$$\text{Var}(Z) = 1 \quad \square$$

---

## The Structural Elegance

The variance derivation has a loop in it that is worth appreciating:

$$\int z^2 e^{-z^2/2}, dz \xrightarrow{\text{IBP}} \int e^{-z^2/2}, dz$$

Integration by parts converts the $z^2$ moment integral back into the normalizing integral. The variance equals 1 because the normalizing integral equals $\sqrt{2\pi}$, and the $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}$ in front of the PDF cancels it exactly. The normalizing constant and the variance are not independent — the same integral $I$ determines both.

This self-referential structure is not a coincidence. For any distribution in the exponential family, the moments are derivatives of the log-normalizing constant. For the Normal, this becomes the observation that differentiating $\log Z(\theta)$ with respect to the natural parameter recovers the moments — and the variance being 1 for the standard Normal is the simplest instance of this.
