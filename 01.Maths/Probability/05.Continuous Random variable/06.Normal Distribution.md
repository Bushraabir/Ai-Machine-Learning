

> _Every distribution, pushed far enough, wants to be Normal. That is not a metaphor — it is a theorem. The Central Limit Theorem is the reason the Normal is everywhere, and until you understand that theorem deeply, the Normal feels like a lucky coincidence rather than an inevitability._


---

## Definition

$X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ has PDF:

$$f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma \sqrt{2\pi}} \exp!\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \quad x \in \mathbb{R}$$

Two parameters: $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$ is the mean (location), $\sigma^2 > 0$ is the variance (spread). The distribution is symmetric about $\mu$, bell-shaped, and supported on all of $\mathbb{R}$.

The standard Normal is the special case $\mu = 0$, $\sigma^2 = 1$:

$$Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1), \quad \varphi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}$$

Everything about the general Normal reduces to the standard one via standardization: if $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

## The Normalization Constant — Why $\sqrt{2\pi}$

The fact that $\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2}, dx = \sqrt{2\pi}$ is not obvious. It is one of those results that every analyst knows and everyone should derive at least once. The trick is to square the integral and convert to polar coordinates.

Let $I = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-x^2/2}, dx$. Then:

$$I^2 = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(x^2 + y^2)/2}, dx, dy$$

Convert to polar: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $dx,dy = r,dr,d\theta$, $x^2 + y^2 = r^2$:

$$I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi} \int_0^\infty e^{-r^2/2} \cdot r, dr, d\theta = 2\pi \int_0^\infty r e^{-r^2/2}, dr$$

Let $u = r^2/2$, $du = r,dr$:

$$I^2 = 2\pi \int_0^\infty e^{-u}, du = 2\pi \cdot 1 = 2\pi$$

Therefore $I = \sqrt{2\pi}$. $\square$

This is clean. The Gaussian integral does not yield to direct computation — it requires lifting to two dimensions, exploiting rotational symmetry, and collapsing back down. The appearance of $\pi$ is a consequence of that symmetry, not a numerical accident.

## Mean, Variance, and MGF

### Mean

By symmetry of $\varphi$ about 0, $E[Z] = 0$ for the standard Normal. For the general case, if $X = \mu + \sigma Z$:

$$E[X] = \mu + \sigma E[Z] = \mu$$

I can also compute it directly. Since $\varphi$ is symmetric, the integrand $z \cdot \varphi(z)$ is odd, and the integral over $\mathbb{R}$ is zero.

### Variance

$$\text{Var}(Z) = E[Z^2] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty z^2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}, dz$$

Integrate by parts: let $u = z$, $dv = z e^{-z^2/2}, dz$, so $v = -e^{-z^2/2}$:

$$E[Z^2] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\left[-z e^{-z^2/2}\Big|_{-\infty}^\infty + \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2/2}, dz\right] = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\left[0 + \sqrt{2\pi}\right] = 1$$

So $\text{Var}(Z) = 1$, and $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2 \text{Var}(Z) = \sigma^2$.

### MGF

$$M_Z(t) = E[e^{tZ}] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tz} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}, dz = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tz - z^2/2}, dz$$

Complete the square in the exponent:

$$tz - \frac{z^2}{2} = -\frac{1}{2}(z^2 - 2tz) = -\frac{1}{2}(z-t)^2 + \frac{t^2}{2}$$

So:

$$M_Z(t) = \frac{e^{t^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-(z-t)^2/2}, dz = e^{t^2/2} \cdot 1 = e^{t^2/2}$$

The integral equals 1 because $e^{-(z-t)^2/2}$ is (up to the $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}$ normalizer) a $\mathcal{N}(t,1)$ PDF integrating to 1, and we already pulled that normalizer out.

For the general $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$:

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{t(\mu + \sigma Z)}] = e^{\mu t} M_Z(\sigma t) = e^{\mu t} \cdot e^{\sigma^2 t^2 / 2} = \exp!\left(\mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right)$$

This is the MGF I need to know cold. The completing-the-square trick is the move that makes it tractable — I use it in so many other Normal calculations that it should become reflexive.

## Moments via the MGF

From $M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2}$, expand as a power series:

$$e^{t^2/2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(t^2/2)^n}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{t^{2n}}{2^n \cdot n!}$$

The coefficient of $t^k / k!$ in the MGF is $E[Z^k]$. From the expansion, odd powers of $t$ have zero coefficient, confirming all odd moments are zero (by symmetry). For even moments, the coefficient of $t^{2n}$ is $\frac{1}{2^n \cdot n!}$, so:

$$E[Z^{2n}] = \frac{(2n)!}{2^n \cdot n!} = (2n-1)!! = 1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2n-1)$$

where $(2n-1)!!$ is the double factorial. So:

$$E[Z^2] = 1, \quad E[Z^4] = 3, \quad E[Z^6] = 15, \quad E[Z^8] = 105, \ldots$$

The fourth standardized moment (kurtosis) of the Normal is $E[Z^4] = 3$. This is why excess kurtosis is defined as $E[Z^4] - 3$ — it measures departure from Normality.

## Linear Transformations

If $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and $Y = aX + b$ with $a \neq 0$, then:

$$Y \sim \mathcal{N}(a\mu + b,\ a^2 \sigma^2)$$

**Proof via MGF:**

$$M_Y(t) = E[e^{t(aX+b)}] = e^{bt} M_X(at) = e^{bt} \exp!\left(\mu(at) + \frac{\sigma^2(at)^2}{2}\right) = \exp!\left((a\mu+b)t + \frac{a^2\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right)$$

This is the MGF of $\mathcal{N}(a\mu + b, a^2\sigma^2)$. $\square$

The Normal family is closed under linear transformations. This is not true of most distributions. It is the reason the Normal is analytically tractable.

## Sums of Independent Normals

If $X_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$ and $X_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$ independently, then:

$$X_1 + X_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_1 + \mu_2,\ \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2)$$

**Proof via MGF:**

$$M_{X_1+X_2}(t) = M_{X_1}(t) \cdot M_{X_2}(t) = \exp!\left(\mu_1 t + \frac{\sigma_1^2 t^2}{2}\right)\exp!\left(\mu_2 t + \frac{\sigma_2^2 t^2}{2}\right) = \exp!\left((\mu_1+\mu_2)t + \frac{(\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)t^2}{2}\right)$$

Done. This generalizes: the sum of any finite number of independent Normals is Normal. Variances add (assuming independence). Means always add.

> The means adding is just linearity of expectation — no independence needed. The variances adding requires independence. I should never confuse these two facts.

## The CDF and $\Phi$

There is no closed-form expression for the CDF of the standard Normal:

$$\Phi(z) = \int_{-\infty}^z \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-t^2/2}, dt$$

This integral cannot be expressed in terms of elementary functions. It is a special function, tabulated and computed numerically.

**Symmetry:** $\Phi(-z) = 1 - \Phi(z)$. This follows from the symmetry of $\varphi$.

**Useful values to know:**

|$z$|$\Phi(z)$|Interpretation|
|---|---|---|
|$0$|$0.5$|Median equals mean|
|$1$|$\approx 0.8413$|One standard deviation above mean|
|$1.645$|$\approx 0.95$|95th percentile|
|$1.96$|$\approx 0.975$|Used in 95% confidence intervals|
|$2$|$\approx 0.9772$|Two standard deviations|
|$2.576$|$\approx 0.995$|99th percentile|
|$3$|$\approx 0.9987$|Three standard deviations|

**The 68-95-99.7 rule:** $$P(|X - \mu| \leq \sigma) \approx 0.6827$$ $$P(|X - \mu| \leq 2\sigma) \approx 0.9545$$ $$P(|X - \mu| \leq 3\sigma) \approx 0.9973$$

These come from $2\Phi(k) - 1$ for $k = 1, 2, 3$. Memorize them — they are used constantly.

## The Log-Normal Distribution

If $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $Y = e^X$ is **log-Normal** — its logarithm is Normal.

$$E[Y] = E[e^X] = M_X(1) = e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$$

$$\text{Var}(Y) = E[Y^2] - (E[Y])^2 = e^{2\mu + 2\sigma^2} - e^{2\mu + \sigma^2} = e^{2\mu + \sigma^2}(e^{\sigma^2} - 1)$$

The mean of the log-Normal is $e^{\mu + \sigma^2/2}$, not $e^\mu$. The $\sigma^2/2$ correction comes from the MGF evaluated at $t=1$, and it matters. Stock prices, income distributions, and biological measurements are often modeled as log-Normal precisely because their logarithms are approximately Normal.

## The Normal as a Limit — The Central Limit Theorem

This is the theorem that makes the Normal unavoidable.

**Theorem (CLT).** Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2 < \infty$. Define the standardized sample mean:

$$Z_n = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} = \frac{\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu)}{\sigma}$$

Then $Z_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ as $n \to \infty$.

**Proof via MGFs** (under the additional assumption that the MGF of $X_i$ exists in a neighborhood of 0):

Let $Y_i = (X_i - \mu)/\sigma$, so $E[Y_i] = 0$, $\text{Var}(Y_i) = 1$. Then $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$.

The MGF of $Z_n$:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left[M_Y!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n$$

Now expand $M_Y(s)$ around $s = 0$:

$$M_Y(s) = 1 + E[Y]s + \frac{E[Y^2]}{2}s^2 + O(s^3) = 1 + 0 + \frac{s^2}{2} + O(s^3)$$

Setting $s = t/\sqrt{n}$:

$$M_Y!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})$$

Therefore:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right)^n \to e^{t^2/2} \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

The limit $\left(1 + \frac{x}{n}\right)^n \to e^x$ is the standard exponential limit. The result $e^{t^2/2}$ is exactly $M_Z(t)$ for $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. By the continuity theorem for MGFs, $Z_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. $\square$

**What the CLT is actually saying:**

The CLT is a statement about the _shape_ of the distribution of $\bar{X}_n$, after standardization. It says that regardless of the original distribution of the $X_i$ (as long as finite variance exists), the aggregate behavior of the sum normalizes to Gaussian. The Gaussian is the fixed point of this normalization procedure.

The finite-variance condition is essential. The CLT fails for Cauchy-distributed $X_i$ because $\text{Var}(X_i) = \infty$. The sample mean of Cauchy r.v.s has the same Cauchy distribution regardless of $n$ — there is no normalization and no convergence to Gaussian.

## The Normal and the Poisson — A Connection Worth Knowing

For large $\lambda$, $\text{Pois}(\lambda) \approx \mathcal{N}(\lambda, \lambda)$. This follows from the CLT: $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$ is the sum of $\lambda$ i.i.d. $\text{Pois}(1)$ random variables (each with mean 1 and variance 1), so by the CLT:

$$\frac{X - \lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

Similarly, $\text{Bin}(n, p) \approx \mathcal{N}(np, np(1-p))$ for large $n$. Same argument: the Binomial is a sum of $n$ i.i.d. Bernoulli$(p)$ r.v.s.

These approximations are the pre-computer method for computing Binomial and Poisson probabilities. They are less important now computationally, but they reveal the structural unity underlying all three distributions.

## Gaussian Tail Bounds — Why Tails Matter

The Normal has light tails — exponentially decaying, in fact. For large $z > 0$:

$$P(Z > z) = 1 - \Phi(z) \approx \frac{\varphi(z)}{z} = \frac{1}{z\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}$$

This is the **Mills ratio** approximation. More precisely:

$$\frac{\varphi(z)}{z}\left(1 - \frac{1}{z^2}\right) \leq P(Z > z) \leq \frac{\varphi(z)}{z}$$

The upper bound is easy to prove: integrate by parts on $\int_z^\infty \varphi(t),dt$, bounding $1/t \leq 1/z$ for $t \geq z$. This bound is useful for showing that Normal tail probabilities decay extremely fast — much faster than, say, the Cauchy or $t$-distribution tails.

## Characterizations of the Normal

The Normal is not just a convenient distribution — it is characterized by several deep properties.

**Characterization 1 (Rotational invariance).** If $X$ and $Y$ are i.i.d. with the property that $(X,Y)$ is rotationally symmetric (i.e., the distribution of $(X,Y)$ depends only on $X^2 + Y^2$), then $X$ and $Y$ are Normal. This is essentially how Maxwell derived the velocity distribution of gas particles.

**Characterization 2 (Maximum entropy).** Among all distributions on $\mathbb{R}$ with fixed mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, the Normal maximizes differential entropy $h(X) = -\int f \log f$. The Normal is the most spread-out, least-informative distribution consistent with knowing only the mean and variance.

**Characterization 3 (Stability).** The Normal is a **stable distribution**: the sum of independent Normals is Normal (after rescaling). More precisely, if $X_1, X_2 \overset{iid}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, then $\frac{X_1 + X_2}{\sqrt{2}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The shape is preserved under convolution and rescaling.

These three characterizations — rotational symmetry, maximum entropy, stability — are independent reasons the Normal appears in physics, information theory, and probability. It is not an arbitrary choice.
