 


---

> _The exponential distribution is the continuous version of the geometric. Once you see that, everything else — memorylessness, the Poisson connection, the minimum of exponentials — falls into place. It's one of the most structurally rich distributions in all of probability, and it's built from a single parameter._

---

## Definition

A random variable $X$ has the **exponential distribution** with rate parameter $\lambda > 0$, written $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$, if its PDF is:

$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x > 0$$

and $f(x) = 0$ for $x \leq 0$.

The CDF is:

$$F(x) = P(X \leq x) = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \geq 0$$

**Survival function** (the complement, used constantly):

$$P(X > x) = e^{-\lambda x}$$

> _The survival function is the cleaner object here. Almost everything about the exponential is easier to see through $P(X > x) = e^{-\lambda x}$ than through $F(x)$. Train yourself to think in terms of survival probabilities._

---

## Normalization Check

$$\int_0^{\infty} \lambda e^{-\lambda x},dx = \lambda \cdot \frac{1}{\lambda} = 1 \checkmark$$

## Parameters — Rate vs. Scale

There are two conventions. Know both:

|Parametrization|Parameter|PDF|Mean|
|---|---|---|---|
|**Rate** (Stat 110 / this note)|$\lambda > 0$|$\lambda e^{-\lambda x}$|$1/\lambda$|
|**Scale**|$\beta = 1/\lambda > 0$|$\frac{1}{\beta}e^{-x/\beta}$|$\beta$|

Blitzstein uses rate. Much of engineering uses scale. When you read a paper, check which one they mean — getting this backwards doubles or halves everything.

## Moments

### Mean

$$E[X] = \int_0^{\infty} x \lambda e^{-\lambda x},dx$$

Integrate by parts: $u = x$, $dv = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}dx$:

$$= \left[-x e^{-\lambda x}\right]_0^{\infty} + \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\lambda x},dx = 0 + \frac{1}{\lambda} = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

Or more elegantly — use the **survival function formula** for non-negative random variables:

$$E[X] = \int_0^{\infty} P(X > x),dx = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\lambda x},dx = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

> _The survival function formula $E[X] = \int_0^\infty P(X > t),dt$ is one of the most useful tools in Stat 110. Blitzstein loves it. Internalize it — it makes computing means of non-negative RVs trivial whenever the survival function is clean, and the exponential is the canonical example._

### Variance

First compute $E[X^2]$ via integration by parts (or recognize the pattern):

$$E[X^n] = \frac{n!}{\lambda^n}$$

This follows from the fact that $\int_0^\infty x^n \lambda e^{-\lambda x},dx = \frac{n!}{\lambda^n}$ — recognizable as the Gamma integral. So:

$$E[X^2] = \frac{2}{\lambda^2}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} - \frac{1}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}$$

$$\text{SD}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

The mean equals the standard deviation. This is a hallmark of the exponential — high variance relative to the mean. It's the distribution of "mostly short, occasionally very long" waiting times.

### MGF

$$M(t) = E[e^{tX}] = \int_0^{\infty} e^{tx} \lambda e^{-\lambda x},dx = \lambda \int_0^{\infty} e^{-(\lambda - t)x},dx = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}, \quad t < \lambda$$

The MGF exists only for $t < \lambda$ — it blows up at $t = \lambda$ because the integral diverges. This is expected: the exponential has a heavy right tail (relative to Gaussian), and the MGF can't be defined everywhere.

You can recover moments: $M'(0) = \frac{1}{\lambda}$, $M''(0) = \frac{2}{\lambda^2}$, confirming the above.

## The Memoryless Property

This is the soul of the exponential distribution.

**Theorem.** $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$ if and only if $X$ is a non-negative continuous random variable satisfying:

$$P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t) \quad \text{for all } s, t \geq 0$$

**Proof ($\Rightarrow$):** Direct computation.

$$P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = \frac{P(X > s + t)}{P(X > s)} = \frac{e^{-\lambda(s+t)}}{e^{-\lambda s}} = e^{-\lambda t} = P(X > t) \quad \blacksquare$$

**Proof ($\Leftarrow$, uniqueness):** Suppose $G(t) = P(X > t)$ satisfies $G(s+t) = G(s)G(t)$ for all $s,t \geq 0$ (this is what memorylessness says multiplicatively). With $G$ right-continuous and $G(0) = 1$, the only solution is $G(t) = e^{-\lambda t}$ for some $\lambda > 0$. This is a standard result about Cauchy's functional equation on $[0,\infty)$. $\blacksquare$

**What it means:** If you've been waiting $s$ units of time and the event hasn't happened, the remaining waiting time has the _same_ distribution as if you just started. The past is irrelevant. The process has no memory of how long you've waited.

> _This is the continuous analogue of the geometric distribution's memorylessness: $P(X > m+n \mid X > m) = P(X > n)$ for $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$. The exponential is the geometric's continuous limit — and memorylessness is the property that pins down both uniquely._

**Philosophical note.** Memorylessness is extremely special. It characterizes exactly two distributions: Geometric (discrete) and Exponential (continuous). Every other distribution on ${0,1,2,\ldots}$ or $[0,\infty)$ has memory. If you're modelling a lightbulb that gets _more_ likely to fail as it ages, the exponential is the wrong model.

## Connection to the Geometric: Taking the Limit

Let $X_n \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ where $p = \lambda/n$ (probability of success per trial). The number of failures before success, scaled by $1/n$, converges in distribution to $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$ as $n \to \infty$.

Concretely: $X_n/n \xrightarrow{d} \text{Expo}(\lambda)$.

**Heuristic derivation.** The survival function of $\text{Geom}(\lambda/n)$ at $k$ steps:

$$P(X_n > k) = (1 - \lambda/n)^k$$

Set $k = \lfloor nt \rfloor$ for continuous time $t$:

$$P(X_n/n > t) = \left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n}\right)^{nt} \to e^{-\lambda t}$$

which is exactly the exponential survival function. The limit is clean because $(1 - \lambda/n)^n \to e^{-\lambda}$.

> _This is the prototype for the general "rare events in many trials" paradigm. Each trial has a tiny probability of success; the waiting time in continuous time is exponential. This is the foundation of the Poisson process._

## Connection to the Poisson Process

The exponential distribution and the Poisson distribution are two faces of the same coin.

**Setup.** A **Poisson process** with rate $\lambda$ is a model for random arrivals in continuous time where:

1. The number of arrivals in any interval of length $t$ is $\text{Pois}(\lambda t)$.
2. Non-overlapping intervals are independent.

**Key fact.** The waiting time between consecutive arrivals in a Poisson process is $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$.

**Proof sketch.** Let $T_1$ = time of first arrival. Then:

$$P(T_1 > t) = P(\text{no arrivals in } [0,t]) = e^{-\lambda t}$$

which is the exponential survival function. The inter-arrival times $T_1, T_2, T_3, \ldots$ are i.i.d. $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$. Memorylessness ensures the process resets after each arrival.

So: **Poisson counts, Exponential waiting times — same process, two views**.

|Question|Distribution|
|---|---|
|How many arrivals in $[0,t]$?|$\text{Pois}(\lambda t)$|
|How long until the next arrival?|$\text{Expo}(\lambda)$|
|How long until the $k$-th arrival?|$\text{Gamma}(k, \lambda)$|

## Minimum of Independent Exponentials

This is one of the most important and beautiful facts about the exponential.

**Theorem.** Let $X_1 \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda_1), \ldots, X_n \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda_n)$, all independent. Then:

$$M = \min(X_1, \ldots, X_n) \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n)$$

**Proof.** Use the survival function and independence:

$$P(M > t) = P(X_1 > t, \ldots, X_n > t) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(X_i > t) = \prod_{i=1}^n e^{-\lambda_i t} = e^{-(\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n)t}$$

This is the survival function of $\text{Expo}(\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n)$. $\blacksquare$

**Furthermore:** given that the minimum is $X_j$ (i.e., $X_j$ "wins"), we have:

$$P(X_j = M) = P(\min = X_j) = \frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n}$$

**Proof.** By LOTP and the continuous analogue:

$$P(X_j < X_k) = \int_0^{\infty} P(X_k > x) \lambda_j e^{-\lambda_j x},dx = \int_0^{\infty} e^{-\lambda_k x} \lambda_j e^{-\lambda_j x},dx = \frac{\lambda_j}{\lambda_j + \lambda_k}$$

Generalizing: $P(X_j = \min) = \frac{\lambda_j}{\sum_i \lambda_i}$.

> _This is the race interpretation. You have $n$ independent exponential clocks ticking simultaneously. The first one to go off determines the winner. The winning probability is proportional to the rate — the faster clock wins more often. And the time until someone wins is itself exponential with the total rate. I find this almost unreasonably elegant._

**Application.** In a system with $n$ components failing independently at rates $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$, the system fails at rate $\sum \lambda_i$ (if it fails when any component fails — series system). This is the engineering reliability interpretation.

## Scaling Property

If $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$ and $c > 0$, then:

$$cX \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda/c)$$

**Proof.**

$$P(cX > t) = P!\left(X > \frac{t}{c}\right) = e^{-\lambda t/c}$$

which is the survival function of $\text{Expo}(\lambda/c)$. $\blacksquare$

In particular, $\lambda X \sim \text{Expo}(1)$. So every exponential is just a scaled $\text{Expo}(1)$. This is the "standard exponential" — analogous to the standard normal $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

## Connection to the Gamma Distribution

The **Gamma distribution** $\text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$ generalizes the exponential. Specifically:

$$\text{Expo}(\lambda) = \text{Gamma}(1, \lambda)$$

Moreover, if $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Expo}(\lambda)$, then:

$$X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$$

This is the distribution of the waiting time until the $n$-th Poisson arrival.

**Proof via MGFs.** The MGF of a sum of independent RVs is the product of MGFs:

$$M_{X_1 + \cdots + X_n}(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right)^n$$

which is precisely the MGF of $\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$. $\blacksquare$

> _So exponential is to Gamma as Bernoulli is to Binomial. One trial vs. $n$ trials. One arrival vs. $n$ arrivals. The pattern is exact._

---

## Lack of Memory Makes It Markovian

The memoryless property is precisely what makes Poisson processes and continuous-time Markov chains tractable. In a CTMC, the time spent in each state is exponentially distributed — and memorylessness ensures the chain's future depends only on the current state, not the history. Without the exponential distribution, the theory of CTMCs would collapse.

This is deeper than it sounds: the exponential is special not just as a distribution, but as the _only_ distribution that makes the Markov property work in continuous time.

## Summary of Key Facts

|Property|Value|
|---|---|
|PDF|$\lambda e^{-\lambda x}$, $x > 0$|
|CDF|$1 - e^{-\lambda x}$|
|Survival|$e^{-\lambda x}$|
|Mean|$1/\lambda$|
|Variance|$1/\lambda^2$|
|MGF|$\lambda/(\lambda - t)$, $t < \lambda$|
|Memoryless|Yes — uniquely so among continuous distributions|
|Min of $n$ Expo($\lambda_i$)|$\text{Expo}(\sum \lambda_i)$|
|Sum of $n$ i.i.d. Expo($\lambda$)|$\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$|
|Scaling: $cX$|$\text{Expo}(\lambda/c)$|

## Personal Notes

- Always use the survival function $P(X > x) = e^{-\lambda x}$ as your primary object. The CDF is just $1$ minus that and is uglier to work with algebraically.
- The minimum-of-exponentials result is one of the first things I reach for in applied problems. Competing risks, first-passage times, reliability — they all reduce to this.
- The $E[X] = \int_0^\infty P(X > t),dt$ formula + exponential survival function = instant mean computation. Drill this until it's a reflex.
- Memorylessness is an _axiom_ in disguise. If your physical system has memory — aging components, queues with priority — the exponential is the wrong model and you need Weibull, Erlang, or something else. Always ask yourself: is memorylessness justified here?
- The MGF blowing up at $t = \lambda$ is a meaningful fact, not a technicality. It signals that the exponential has a genuinely heavy right tail — not heavy like Pareto, but heavy relative to the Gaussian. All the moments exist, but the tail is slow enough that the Laplace transform can't be extended to all of $\mathbb{R}$.
