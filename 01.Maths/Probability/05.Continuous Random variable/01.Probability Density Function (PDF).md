# PDF — Probability Density Functions

> _A continuous random variable can't be pinned to a single point. The density function is how we describe the "shape" of its randomness._

---

## The Core Problem

Discrete probability is clean: $P(X = k)$ makes immediate sense. But once we move to continuous random variables — think a waiting time, a measurement, a temperature — we hit a wall.

**The fundamental issue:** For any continuous $X$, $P(X = x) = 0$ for every single $x$.

This is not a technicality to wave away. It's the geometric fact that a single point has measure zero on the real line. Probability lives in _intervals_, not points. So we need a new object entirely.

---

## Definition

Let $X$ be a continuous random variable. A function $f: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ is the **probability density function (PDF)** of $X$ if:

1. $f(x) \geq 0$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$
2. $\displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f(x), dx = 1$
3. For any $a \leq b$:

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = \int_a^b f(x), dx$$

That's it. The PDF is just a non-negative function whose integral over all of $\mathbb{R}$ is 1, and which gives probabilities via integration.

> **Key insight:** $f(x)$ is _not_ a probability. $f(x)$ can exceed 1. It's a _density_ — probability per unit length. Probability only emerges when you integrate over a set.

---

## The CDF Connection — and How to Move Between Them

This is the most mechanically important relationship in all of continuous probability. You need to be completely fluent in both directions.

### Definition of the CDF

$$F(x) = P(X \leq x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t), dt$$

The CDF accumulates area under the density curve from $-\infty$ up to $x$. It is always:

- Non-decreasing
- Right-continuous
- $\lim_{x \to -\infty} F(x) = 0$ and $\lim_{x \to \infty} F(x) = 1$

### Direction 1: PDF → CDF (integrate)

Given $f$, integrate from $-\infty$ to $x$:

$$F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f(t), dt$$

**Worked example — Exponential($\lambda$):**

$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \geq 0$$

$$F(x) = \int_0^x \lambda e^{-\lambda t}, dt = \left[-e^{-\lambda t}\right]_0^x = 1 - e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \geq 0$$

Check: $F(0) = 0$ ✓, $F(\infty) = 1$ ✓, $F$ non-decreasing ✓.

**The piecewise trap.** If $f$ has a restricted support (i.e., $f = 0$ outside some interval), you must handle $F$ piecewise. For $X \sim \text{Unif}(a,b)$:

$$F(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < a \ \dfrac{x - a}{b - a} & a \leq x \leq b \ 1 & x > b \end{cases}$$

_Don't forget the outer cases._ This is where exam errors creep in.

---

### Direction 2: CDF → PDF (differentiate)

By the **Fundamental Theorem of Calculus**, wherever $F$ is differentiable:

$$f(x) = F'(x)$$

This is the cleaner direction mechanically — just differentiate. But you need to check:

1. $F'(x) \geq 0$ everywhere (it should be, if $F$ is non-decreasing — a nice sanity check)
2. $\int F'(x), dx = 1$ (guaranteed if $F$ goes from 0 to 1)

**Worked example.** Suppose you're handed:

$$F(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < 0 \ 1 - e^{-x^2} & x \geq 0 \end{cases}$$

Differentiate on $x > 0$:

$$f(x) = F'(x) = 2x e^{-x^2}, \quad x > 0$$

And $f(x) = 0$ for $x < 0$. Check: $f(x) \geq 0$ ✓. Verify normalization:

$$\int_0^\infty 2x e^{-x^2}, dx = \left[-e^{-x^2}\right]_0^\infty = 0 - (-1) = 1 \checkmark$$

**What if $F$ isn't differentiable everywhere?** At isolated jump discontinuities of $f$ (like the boundary of a Uniform's support), $F$ has a kink — it's continuous but not differentiable there. The PDF is undefined at those exact points, but since they're measure-zero sets, it doesn't matter for any integral computation.

---

### The Two-Direction Summary

| You have | You want             | Operation                          |
| -------- | -------------------- | ---------------------------------- |
| $f(x)$   | $F(x)$               | $F(x) = \int_{-\infty}^x f(t), dt$ |
| $F(x)$   | $f(x)$               | $f(x) = F'(x)$                     |
| $F(x)$   | $P(a \leq X \leq b)$ | $F(b) - F(a)$                      |
| $f(x)$   | $P(a \leq X \leq b)$ | $\int_a^b f(x), dx$                |

The last two are equivalent — use whichever is computationally easier in the moment.

---

### A Common Exam Move: Finding $P(a \leq X \leq b)$ via the CDF

Once you have $F$, you never need to integrate again:

$$P(a \leq X \leq b) = F(b) - F(a)$$

_This is just the definition of the CDF applied twice, using additivity of integrals._ It's often faster than re-integrating $f$ from scratch.

**Example.** $X \sim \text{Expo}(2)$. Find $P(1 \leq X \leq 3)$.

$$F(x) = 1 - e^{-2x}$$

$$P(1 \leq X \leq 3) = F(3) - F(1) = (1 - e^{-6}) - (1 - e^{-2}) = e^{-2} - e^{-6}$$

Done in two lines, no integration required.

## Why $f(x)$ Can Be Greater Than 1

This trips people up. Consider $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 0.1)$. Then:

$$f(x) = \begin{cases} 10 & 0 \leq x \leq 0.1 \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Here $f(x) = 10 \gg 1$. But check: $\int_0^{0.1} 10, dx = 1$. ✓

The density being large just means probability is _concentrated_ in that region. The value $f(x)$ is better read as: _"in a tiny window $[x, x+dx]$, the probability is approximately $f(x), dx$."_

## Expectation and Variance

For $X$ with density $f$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x \cdot f(x), dx$$

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x) \cdot f(x), dx \quad \text{(LOTUS)}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$$

LOTUS (Law of the Unconscious Statistician) is particularly powerful — you don't need to find the distribution of $g(X)$ first.

---

## The Two Workhorses: Uniform and Exponential

### Uniform Distribution: $X \sim \text{Unif}(a, b)$

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{b-a}, \quad a \leq x \leq b$$

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{a+b}{2}, \qquad \text{Var}(X) = \frac{(b-a)^2}{12}$$

The uniform is the "maximum ignorance" distribution on a bounded interval. Everything is equally likely per unit length.

### Exponential Distribution: $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$

$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x \geq 0$$

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda}, \qquad \text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2}$$

The exponential is the continuous analogue of the Geometric. It models waiting times, lifetimes, inter-arrival times. Its defining property is **memorylessness**:

$$P(X > s + t \mid X > s) = P(X > t)$$

_Proof._ Direct calculation using $P(X > t) = e^{-\lambda t}$.

---

## Normal Distribution: $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

The Gaussian is everywhere because of the **Central Limit Theorem** — sums of i.i.d. random variables with finite variance converge in distribution to a normal. The PDF itself doesn't have a closed-form antiderivative (hence the $\Phi$ function and z-tables), but its moment-generating function is beautifully tractable:

$$M_X(t) = e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2 / 2}$$

Standardization: if $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, then $Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \sim N(0,1)$.

---

## A Proof Worth Sitting With

**Claim:** The Exponential is the unique memoryless continuous distribution on $[0, \infty)$.

**Proof sketch.** Let $X$ have CDF $F$ with $F(0) = 0$ and the memoryless property: $P(X > s+t) = P(X>s) \cdot P(X>t)$ for all $s,t \geq 0$.

Let $G(t) = P(X > t) = 1 - F(t)$. The condition becomes: $$G(s+t) = G(s) \cdot G(t)$$

This is Cauchy's functional equation. The only right-continuous solutions with $G(0)=1$ and $G$ decreasing to $0$ are $G(t) = e^{-\lambda t}$ for some $\lambda > 0$. ✓

This is the _right_ way to understand the exponential — not as an arbitrary formula, but as the inevitable consequence of memorylessness. The Poisson process makes this even clearer.

---

## Pitfalls and Things to Remember

- **$f(x) \geq 0$ everywhere**, not just on the "support." On regions outside the support, $f = 0$.
- **Check normalization.** Every time you write down a PDF, verify $\int f = 1$.
- **Endpoints don't matter.** $P(a \leq X \leq b) = P(a < X < b)$ for continuous RVs. This is often useful.
- **Density vs. Mass.** If $X$ is continuous, never write $P(X = x_0)$ in your calculations as though it contributes anything.
