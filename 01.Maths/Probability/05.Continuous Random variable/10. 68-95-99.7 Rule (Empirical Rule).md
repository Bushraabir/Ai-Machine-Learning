## The Statement

Let $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$. Then:

$$P(\mu - \sigma \leq X \leq \mu + \sigma) \approx 0.6827$$ $$P(\mu - 2\sigma \leq X \leq \mu + 2\sigma) \approx 0.9545$$ $$P(\mu - 3\sigma \leq X \leq \mu + 3\sigma) \approx 0.9973$$

Or in the standardized form â€” let $Z = \frac{X - \mu}{\sigma} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$:

$$P(|Z| \leq 1) \approx 0.6827 \qquad P(|Z| \leq 2) \approx 0.9545 \qquad P(|Z| \leq 3) \approx 0.9973$$

These aren't definitions. They are _computed facts_ about the normal distribution that follow from the shape of the Gaussian PDF.

---

## Where Does This Actually Come From?

The normal PDF is:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi},\sigma} \exp!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

The rule is just evaluating:

$$P(\mu - k\sigma \leq X \leq \mu + k\sigma) = \int_{\mu - k\sigma}^{\mu + k\sigma} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi},\sigma} e^{-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}}, dx$$

Standardise with $z = \frac{x-\mu}{\sigma}$, $dz = \frac{dx}{\sigma}$:

$$= \int_{-k}^{k} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}, dz = 2,\Phi(k) - 1$$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. Now plug in:

| $k$ | $\Phi(k)$ | $2\Phi(k)-1$ |
| --- | --------- | ------------ |
| 1   | 0.8413    | **0.6827**   |
| 2   | 0.9772    | **0.9545**   |
| 3   | 0.9987    | **0.9973**   |

> _This integral has no closed form â€” $\Phi$ is not elementary. That used to bother me. Now I think it's beautiful: the most important distribution in all of probability has a CDF you can't write in closed form. You have to respect it._

---

## The Proof That $\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2/2},dx = \sqrt{2\pi}$

This is the normalization constant and it's worth knowing cold. The trick is squaring the integral.

**Claim:** $I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2/2},dx = \sqrt{2\pi}$

**Proof.**

$$I^2 = \left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-x^2/2},dx\right)!!\left(\int_{-\infty}^{\infty} e^{-y^2/2},dy\right) = \iint_{\mathbb{R}^2} e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2},dx,dy$$

Convert to polar coordinates: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$, $dx,dy = r,dr,d\theta$:

$$I^2 = \int_0^{2\pi}\int_0^{\infty} e^{-r^2/2} \cdot r ,dr, d\theta = 2\pi \int_0^{\infty} r,e^{-r^2/2},dr$$

Substitute $u = r^2/2$, $du = r,dr$:

$$= 2\pi \int_0^{\infty} e^{-u},du = 2\pi \cdot 1 = 2\pi$$

So $I = \sqrt{2\pi}$. $\blacksquare$

> _Every time I see this proof I'm briefly annoyed that the solution is "square it and use polar coordinates" â€” and then immediately grateful. It's one of the great tricks in analysis._

## Symmetry Makes It Cheap

Because the standard normal PDF $\phi(z) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-z^2/2}$ is **even** â€” i.e., $\phi(-z) = \phi(z)$ â€” we get:

$$P(|Z| \leq k) = 2,\Phi(k) - 1$$

This symmetry isn't accidental. It reflects the fact that the normal is determined entirely by $\mu$ and $\sigma^2$, and the density depends only on $(x-\mu)^2$.

Concretely, this means:

$$P(Z > 1) = P(Z < -1) = \frac{1 - 0.6827}{2} \approx 0.1587$$

The **tail probability** at $1\sigma$ is about $15.87$ on each side. At $3\sigma$, each tail is only $0.135$ â€” this is the origin of the "$3\sigma$ event" language in physics and finance.

## Why This Rule Matters Beyond Memorization

### 1. It Quantifies Rarity

A "$5\sigma$ event" (famously invoked in the Higgs boson discovery) corresponds to: $$P(|Z| > 5) \approx 5.7 \times 10^{-7}$$ That's about 1 in 1.7 million. The rule gives you the intuition for what sigma-multiples mean in terms of probability mass.

### 2. It's the Practical Output of the CLT

The [[07.The Central Limit Theorem]] says $\bar{X}_n \approx \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$ for large $n$. The 68-95-99.7 rule immediately tells you:

- 95% confidence intervals are roughly $\bar{X} \pm 2\cdot\frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n}}$
- This is where the "2 standard errors" rule of thumb in statistics comes from.

### 3. Chebyshev vs. Empirical Rule

[[Chebyshev's Inequality]] gives a **distribution-free** bound: $$P(|X - \mu| \geq k\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{k^2}$$

At $k=2$: Chebyshev says at most $25$ of mass is outside $2\sigma$. The empirical rule says **only $4.55%** for the normal. The normal is _much_ better concentrated than Chebyshev guarantees â€” Chebyshev is tight for pathological distributions, not the bell curve.

> _I used to confuse these two. They answer the same question but from totally different angles. Chebyshev: any distribution, weak bound. Empirical rule: normal only, sharp fact._

---

## The Tail Behavior â€” Exponential Decay

For $z$ large, the standard normal tail decays like:

$$P(Z > z) \approx \frac{\phi(z)}{z} = \frac{1}{z\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}$$

This is a **sub-Gaussian** tail â€” faster than any polynomial, but not as fast as a compactly supported distribution. This decay rate is what makes the normal so well-behaved in probability theory and why tools like MGFs and Hoeffding's inequality work cleanly for it.

## Personal Notes

- The numbers $68, 95, 99.7$ are worth having in working memory â€” you'll use them constantly in applied settings, hypothesis testing, and when reading papers.
- Don't just memorize the percentages. Know that they come from $2\Phi(k)-1$ and know why the factor of 2 is there (symmetry of $\phi$).
- The hardest part conceptually: the rule is only exactly true for the **normal**. For any other distribution, you need Chebyshev or distribution-specific bounds. The empirical rule is a normal-specific fact, not a universal law.
- Joe Blitzstein in Stat 110 emphasizes the **story** behind distributions â€” the empirical rule is part of the normal's "story": it's the canonical unimodal symmetric distribution, and these percentages are the fingerprint of that shape.
