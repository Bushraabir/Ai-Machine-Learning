

> _Independence is one of those words that feels intuitive until you try to prove something with it. Then you realize the intuition was doing a lot of hidden work, and you need the definition to take over._

---

## Two Notions, One Word

The word "independent" appears in two distinct but related contexts in probability:

1. **Independence of events:** $A$ and $B$ are independent if knowing $A$ occurred gives no information about whether $B$ occurred.
2. **Independence of random variables:** $X$ and $Y$ are independent if knowing the value of $X$ gives no information about the distribution of $Y$.

These are not the same definition. The second generalizes the first in a precise way. I want to understand both from the ground up, understand the relationship between them, and understand the common errors that come from conflating them.

---

## Part I — Independence of Events

### The Definition

Events $A$ and $B$ are **independent** if:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) \cdot P(B)$$

That is the definition. Not $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$, at least not as the primary definition. The conditional form is equivalent when $P(B) > 0$, but the product form is primary because it does not require conditioning to be defined.

**Why the product form is primary:** Conditional probability $P(A \mid B) = P(A \cap B) / P(B)$ is undefined when $P(B) = 0$. But two events can be independent even when one of them has probability zero — the product form handles this without issue. Defining independence via the product form avoids the degenerate case cleanly.

**The conditional restatement:** When $P(B) > 0$:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B) \iff P(A \mid B) = P(A)$$

This is the intuitive version: conditioning on $B$ does not change the probability of $A$. By symmetry, it also equals $P(B \mid A) = P(B)$ when $P(A) > 0$. Independence is symmetric.

### Mutual Independence of Multiple Events

For three events $A$, $B$, $C$, **mutual independence** requires all of the following:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$$ $$P(A \cap C) = P(A)P(C)$$ $$P(B \cap C) = P(B)P(C)$$ $$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A)P(B)P(C)$$

All four conditions. The first three say the events are **pairwise independent**. The fourth is an additional condition. Pairwise independence does not imply mutual independence.

**The canonical counterexample — Pairwise but not mutually independent:**

Toss two fair coins. Let:

- $A$ = first coin is Heads
- $B$ = second coin is Heads
- $C$ = the two coins match (both H or both T)

Then $P(A) = P(B) = P(C) = 1/2$.

Check pairwise independence: $$P(A \cap B) = P(\text{HH}) = 1/4 = P(A)P(B) \quad \checkmark$$ $$P(A \cap C) = P(\text{HH}) = 1/4 = P(A)P(C) \quad \checkmark$$ $$P(B \cap C) = P(\text{HH}) = 1/4 = P(B)P(C) \quad \checkmark$$

Check mutual independence: $$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(\text{HH}) = 1/4 \neq \frac{1}{8} = P(A)P(B)P(C) \quad \times$$

So $A$, $B$, $C$ are pairwise independent but not mutually independent. Knowing $A$ and $B$ both occurred tells you $C$ occurred with certainty — so $A$ and $B$ together are not independent of $C$.

> This example should be internalized. It shows that independence is not just a pairwise property. For $n$ events to be mutually independent, all $2^n - n - 1$ subset conditions must hold. This is a genuinely stronger requirement.

For $n$ events $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ to be mutually independent:

$$P!\left(\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i\right) = \prod_{i \in S} P(A_i) \quad \text{for every subset } S \subseteq {1, \ldots, n}$$

### Independence vs. Disjointness — The Most Common Confusion

If $A$ and $B$ are disjoint ($A \cap B = \emptyset$) and both have positive probability, then:

$$P(A \cap B) = 0 \neq P(A)P(B) > 0$$

So disjoint events with positive probability are **never independent**. In fact, they are maximally _dependent_: knowing $A$ occurred tells you with certainty that $B$ did not.

Independence says the events have no information about each other. Disjointness says one rules out the other completely. These are opposite extremes on the spectrum of dependence.

> I remind myself of this whenever I am tempted to say "these events don't overlap so they should be independent." That is exactly backwards.

## Part II — Independence of Random Variables

### The Definition

Random variables $X$ and $Y$ are **independent** if for all sets $A$ and $B$:

$$P(X \in A, Y \in B) = P(X \in A) \cdot P(Y \in B)$$

Equivalently — and this is the version I use most in practice — $X$ and $Y$ are independent if and only if their **joint CDF factors**:

$$F_{X,Y}(x, y) = F_X(x) \cdot F_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

For discrete random variables, this becomes: the joint PMF factors into the marginals:

$$P(X = x, Y = y) = P(X = x) \cdot P(Y = y) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

For continuous random variables: the joint PDF factors:

$$f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

The factorization condition is both necessary and sufficient.

### The Relationship to Event Independence

Let $A \in \sigma(X)$ be any event defined in terms of $X$ (i.e., any event of the form ${X \in B}$), and similarly $C \in \sigma(Y)$. Then:

$$X \perp Y \implies A \text{ and } C \text{ are independent events}$$

Random variable independence implies event independence for all events generated by those random variables. This is why random variable independence is strictly stronger than any particular pair of events being independent.

Conversely, if every event generated by $X$ is independent of every event generated by $Y$, then $X$ and $Y$ are independent as random variables. The random variable definition is exactly the requirement that the event independence holds simultaneously and universally.

### Checking Independence via Joint Density Factorization

In practice, for continuous random variables, the most direct method is:

1. Write down $f_{X,Y}(x,y)$.
2. Check whether it factors as $g(x) \cdot h(y)$ for some functions $g$ and $h$.
3. If it does, then $X \perp Y$ (and $g$ and $h$ are proportional to the marginals).

**Crucial subtlety — the support must factor too.** If the support of $(X,Y)$ is a region that cannot be written as a Cartesian product $S_X \times S_Y$, then $X$ and $Y$ cannot be independent, regardless of whether the density formula factors.

**Counterexample:** Suppose $(X,Y)$ is uniform on the triangle ${(x,y) : x > 0, y > 0, x + y < 1}$. The joint density is $f(x,y) = 2$ on this region. The density formula is a constant, which trivially factors. But $X$ and $Y$ are not independent — knowing $X = 0.9$ forces $Y < 0.1$. The support is not a rectangle, so independence fails.

The correct factorization condition is:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = g(x) h(y) \quad \text{for all } (x,y) \in \mathbb{R}^2$$

not just on the support. When the support is a non-rectangular region, $f_{X,Y}(x,y) = 0$ for points outside it but $f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y) > 0$ for those same points, so the product form fails globally.

## Part III — What Independence Buys You

Independence is not just a philosophical statement. It is a computational engine. Here are the key consequences, each with its justification.

### 1. Expectation of a Product

$$X \perp Y \implies E[XY] = E[X] \cdot E[Y]$$

**Proof (continuous):**

$$E[XY] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty xy \cdot f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty xy \cdot f_X(x) f_Y(y), dx, dy$$

$$= \left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty x f_X(x), dx\right)!\left(\int_{-\infty}^\infty y f_Y(y), dy\right) = E[X] \cdot E[Y]$$

The factorization of the joint density is what allows the double integral to split into a product of single integrals.

**The converse is false.** $E[XY] = E[X]E[Y]$ — called **uncorrelatedness** — does not imply independence. This is one of the most important non-implications in probability.

**Counterexample:** Let $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $X = Z$, $Y = Z^2$. Then:

$$E[XY] = E[Z^3] = 0 = E[Z] \cdot E[Z^2] = 0 \cdot 1$$

So $E[XY] = E[X]E[Y]$, but $X$ and $Y$ are clearly dependent — knowing $X = z$ tells you $Y = z^2$ with certainty.

### 2. Variance of a Sum

$$X \perp Y \implies \text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$$

**Proof:**

$$\text{Var}(X+Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\text{Cov}(X,Y)$$

Independence implies $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y] = 0$. So the cross-term vanishes.

**Note:** $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$ (uncorrelated) does not imply independence, as shown above. But independence does imply zero covariance.

### 3. MGF of a Sum

$$X \perp Y \implies M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t)$$

**Proof:**

$$M_{X+Y}(t) = E[e^{t(X+Y)}] = E[e^{tX} e^{tY}] = E[e^{tX}] \cdot E[e^{tY}] = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t)$$

The second equality uses independence via the product expectation rule. This is how I prove that sums of independent Poissons are Poisson, sums of independent Normals are Normal, sums of independent Gammas are Gamma.

### 4. LOTUS for Independent Variables

$$X \perp Y \implies E[g(X) h(Y)] = E[g(X)] \cdot E[h(Y)]$$

for any functions $g$ and $h$. This follows from the same splitting argument as the expectation of a product, applied to $g(X)$ and $h(Y)$ which are themselves independent (functions of independent r.v.s are independent).

## Part IV — Functions of Independent Random Variables

**Theorem.** If $X \perp Y$ and $g, h$ are measurable functions, then $g(X) \perp h(Y)$.

**Proof sketch.** Any event defined in terms of $g(X)$ is of the form ${g(X) \in A} = {X \in g^{-1}(A)}$, which is an event in $\sigma(X)$. Similarly for $h(Y)$. Since $X \perp Y$, all events in $\sigma(X)$ are independent of all events in $\sigma(Y)$. $\square$

This is used constantly without comment. When I write the MGF factorization $E[e^{tX} e^{tY}] = E[e^{tX}]E[e^{tY}]$, I am applying this theorem to $g(x) = e^{tx}$ and $h(y) = e^{ty}$.

## Part V — Conditional Independence

Events $A$ and $B$ are **conditionally independent given $C$** if:

$$P(A \cap B \mid C) = P(A \mid C) \cdot P(B \mid C)$$

Random variables $X$ and $Y$ are **conditionally independent given $Z$** if, for all $x, y, z$:

$$f_{X,Y \mid Z}(x, y \mid z) = f_{X \mid Z}(x \mid z) \cdot f_{Y \mid Z}(y \mid z)$$

**Conditional independence does not imply marginal independence, and vice versa.**

**Example (conditional independence, not marginal):** $X$ and $Y$ are the results of two different tests for the same disease. Marginally, they are correlated — both depend on whether the person has the disease. But conditionally on the disease status $Z$, the tests are independent (each test is a noisy independent measurement given the true state).

**Example (marginal independence, not conditional):** Two fair coins $X$ and $Y$ are independent. But condition on $C = {X + Y = 1}$ (exactly one Head). Then given $C$, knowing $X$ tells you $Y$ exactly. Marginally independent, conditionally dependent.

## Part VI — The Hierarchy of Dependence Concepts

It is worth placing all the concepts in order:

$$\text{Mutual independence} \implies \text{Pairwise independence}$$ $$\text{Independence of r.v.s} \implies \text{Zero covariance (uncorrelated)}$$ $$\text{Independence of r.v.s} \implies \text{Every pair of generated events is independent}$$

None of these arrows reverse in general. The failure modes are:

|Claim|True?|Counterexample|
|---|---|---|
|Pairwise ind. $\Rightarrow$ mutual ind.|No|Coins $A, B, C$ example above|
|Uncorrelated $\Rightarrow$ independent|No|$X = Z$, $Y = Z^2$, $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$|
|Disjoint $\Rightarrow$ independent|No|Disjoint events are maximally dependent|
|Cond. ind. $\Rightarrow$ marginal ind.|No|Disease-test example|
|Marginal ind. $\Rightarrow$ cond. ind.|No|Two coins conditioned on sum|

For **jointly Normal** random variables, uncorrelated does imply independent. This is a special property of the Normal, not a general fact.

**Proof:** If $(X,Y)$ is jointly Normal and $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$, the joint density is:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_X\sigma_Y}\exp!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu_X)^2}{2\sigma_X^2} - \frac{(y-\mu_Y)^2}{2\sigma_Y^2}\right) = f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$$

The joint density factors because the covariance term in the bivariate Normal exponent vanishes when $\rho = 0$. This factorization is exactly the independence condition.
