
> _The Poisson process is the first serious stochastic process you meet, and it rewards the time you put into it. Everything connects: the Exponential inter-arrival times, the Poisson counts, the Gamma waiting times, the splitting and merging — they're not separate facts. They're the same object from different angles. Get the definition right first, then everything else is theorem._

---

## Definition (Axiomatic)

A **Poisson process** with rate $\lambda > 0$ is a collection of random variables ${N(t) : t \geq 0}$ — where $N(t)$ counts the number of arrivals in $[0, t]$ — satisfying:

1. **$N(0) = 0$** — the process starts empty.
    
2. **Independent increments** — for any $0 \leq t_1 < t_2 \leq t_3 < t_4$, the increments $N(t_2) - N(t_1)$ and $N(t_4) - N(t_3)$ are independent. Non-overlapping intervals don't talk to each other.
    
3. **Stationary increments** — $N(t+s) - N(s) \overset{d}{=} N(t)$ for all $s, t \geq 0$. The distribution of the count in an interval depends only on its _length_, not its position.
    
4. **Poisson counts** — $N(t) \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda t)$ for all $t \geq 0$.
    

Some texts replace axiom 4 with the infinitesimal characterisation:

$$P(N(h) = 1) = \lambda h + o(h), \qquad P(N(h) \geq 2) = o(h)$$

as $h \to 0$. This says: in a tiny interval, at most one arrival happens, and it happens with probability roughly $\lambda h$. Axiom 4 is then a _theorem_ derived from axioms 1–3 plus the infinitesimal condition. For us, taking axiom 4 as given is cleaner.

> _The two definitions are equivalent — the infinitesimal one is more physically intuitive ("rare events in tiny intervals"), the axiomatic one with Poisson counts is mathematically cleaner. Blitzstein uses the second. Know both exist._

## The Poisson Distribution Refresher

Since $N(t) \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda t)$:

$$P(N(t) = k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^k}{k!}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

$$E[N(t)] = \lambda t, \qquad \text{Var}(N(t)) = \lambda t$$

The rate $\lambda$ has units of **arrivals per unit time**. The expected count in $[0,t]$ is $\lambda t$ — linear in $t$, as it should be.

---

## Inter-Arrival Times Are Exponential

Let $T_1$ be the time of the first arrival, $T_2$ the time between the first and second arrival, and so on. Formally:

$$T_1 = \inf{t > 0 : N(t) = 1}$$ $$T_n = \text{time between }(n-1)\text{-th and }n\text{-th arrival}$$

**Theorem.** The inter-arrival times $T_1, T_2, T_3, \ldots$ are i.i.d. $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$.

**Proof.**

For $T_1$: using the survival function,

$$P(T_1 > t) = P(N(t) = 0) = e^{-\lambda t}$$

So $T_1 \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$.

For $T_2$: given $T_1 = s$, the time until the next arrival after $s$ has the same distribution as $T_1$, by **stationary and independent increments** — the process after time $s$ is a fresh Poisson process with the same rate. So $T_2 \mid T_1 = s \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$ for any $s$, meaning $T_2 \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$ and $T_2 \perp T_1$.

By induction, all $T_n$ are i.i.d. $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$. $\blacksquare$

> _This is the Poisson process's memorylessness in disguise. The process doesn't remember how long it's been since the last arrival. It resets. This is exactly the Exponential's memoryless property at the process level._

---

## Arrival Times Are Gamma

Let $S_n = T_1 + T_2 + \cdots + T_n$ be the time of the $n$-th arrival (the **$n$-th order statistic** of arrivals).

**Theorem.** $S_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$.

**Proof.** $S_n$ is a sum of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$ random variables. By the MGF:

$$M_{S_n}(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right)^n$$

which is the MGF of $\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$. $\blacksquare$

The PDF of $S_n$ is:

$$f_{S_n}(t) = \frac{\lambda^n t^{n-1} e^{-\lambda t}}{(n-1)!}, \quad t > 0$$

**Sanity check.** $P(S_n \leq t) = P(N(t) \geq n)$: the $n$-th arrival happens by time $t$ if and only if there are at least $n$ arrivals in $[0,t]$. This gives a beautiful identity between the Gamma CDF and the Poisson tail:

$$\sum_{k=n}^{\infty} \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^k}{k!} = \int_0^t \frac{\lambda^n s^{n-1} e^{-\lambda s}}{(n-1)!},ds$$

> _This identity is non-obvious analytically but completely obvious probabilistically. Two different ways to ask "has the $n$-th arrival happened yet?" This is exactly the kind of thing Blitzstein means when he says to think about what events mean, not just what the formulas say._

## The Three Views of the Same Process

|Question|Answer|Distribution|
|---|---|---|
|How many arrivals in $[0,t]$?|$N(t)$|$\text{Pois}(\lambda t)$|
|How long between arrivals $n-1$ and $n$?|$T_n$|$\text{Expo}(\lambda)$|
|When does the $n$-th arrival happen?|$S_n$|$\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$|

These are not three different processes. They are one process, described three ways. Any question about the Poisson process can be answered from any of these three angles — pick the cleanest one for the problem at hand.

## Merging Poisson Processes

**Theorem.** If $N_1(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_1)$ and $N_2(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_2)$ are independent Poisson processes, then their superposition $N(t) = N_1(t) + N_2(t)$ is a Poisson process with rate $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2$:

$$N(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$$

**Proof.** For any interval of length $t$, $N_1(t) \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda_1 t)$ and $N_2(t) \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda_2 t)$ independently. The sum of independent Poisson random variables is Poisson:

$$N(t) = N_1(t) + N_2(t) \sim \text{Pois}((\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)t)$$

Independent and stationary increments are preserved by superposition. $\blacksquare$

> _Merging is the Poisson process analogue of the minimum-of-exponentials result. Both say: combine independent Poisson/exponential objects and you stay in the same family, with rates adding. The two facts are related — the first arrival in the merged process is $\min(T_1^{(1)}, T_1^{(2)}) \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$, which is exactly the minimum-of-exponentials theorem._

## Splitting (Thinning) a Poisson Process

**Theorem.** Take a $\text{PP}(\lambda)$ and independently mark each arrival as type 1 with probability $p$ and type 2 with probability $1-p$. Let $N_1(t)$ and $N_2(t)$ count type-1 and type-2 arrivals respectively. Then:

$$N_1(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda p), \quad N_2(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda(1-p)), \quad N_1 \perp N_2$$

**Proof.** Fix $t$. We need to show $N_1(t) \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda p t)$ and $N_1(t) \perp N_2(t)$.

Condition on $N(t) = n$: each of the $n$ arrivals is independently type 1 with probability $p$, so $N_1(t) \mid N(t) = n \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$.

By LOTP (law of total probability), using the Poisson PMF for $N(t)$:

$$P(N_1(t) = k) = \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^n}{n!}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda p t)^k}{k!} \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \frac{(\lambda(1-p)t)^{n-k}}{(n-k)!} = \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda p t)^k}{k!} \cdot e^{\lambda(1-p)t}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-\lambda p t}(\lambda p t)^k}{k!}$$

So $N_1(t) \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda p t)$. A symmetric argument gives $N_2(t) \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda(1-p)t)$.

For independence: the joint PMF factors as the product of the marginals (verify by a similar calculation, or note that the conditional distribution $N_1(t) \mid N(t) = n$ is Binomial, and the joint PMF of $(N_1(t), N_2(t))$ factors). $\blacksquare$

> _The independence of $N_1$ and $N_2$ is the surprising part. You'd think that knowing how many type-1 arrivals there are tells you something about type-2 arrivals — after all, they share the same parent process. But no. The Poisson structure absorbs the dependence. This is one of the Poisson process's truly remarkable properties._

## Conditioning on the Number of Arrivals: Uniform Order Statistics

**Theorem.** Given $N(t) = n$, the $n$ arrival times $S_1, S_2, \ldots, S_n$ are distributed as the **order statistics** of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0, t)$ random variables.

That is, conditional on $n$ arrivals in $[0,t]$, the arrival times are uniformly and independently scattered over $[0,t]$ — we just sort them.

**Proof sketch.** The joint density of the ordered arrival times $s_1 < s_2 < \cdots < s_n$ given $N(t) = n$ is:

$$f(s_1, \ldots, s_n \mid N(t) = n) = \frac{n!}{t^n}, \quad 0 < s_1 < \cdots < s_n < t$$

which is exactly the joint density of the order statistics of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0,t)$ random variables ($n!/t^n$ on the ordered simplex). $\blacksquare$

> _This is the theorem that makes the Poisson process feel inevitable. If you're going to place $n$ random points in $[0,t]$ with no memory and no preferred location, what else could they be but uniform? The Poisson process is the unique process consistent with this "complete randomness" — arrivals are as unsophisticated as possible._

**Application.** This theorem turns many Poisson process calculations into uniform distribution calculations, which are often much simpler. For example: given 10 arrivals in $[0,1]$, what is the expected time of the 3rd arrival? It's $E[U_{(3)}]$ for $U_{(3)}$ the 3rd order statistic of 10 i.i.d. Uniform$(0,1)$ variables, which is $3/11$.

## The Poisson Process as a Limit of Bernoulli Processes

Consider $n$ independent Bernoulli trials, each in a tiny interval of length $h = t/n$, each with success probability $p = \lambda h = \lambda t / n$. The total number of successes is:

$$\text{Bin}!\left(n, \frac{\lambda t}{n}\right) \xrightarrow{d} \text{Pois}(\lambda t) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

This is the Poisson limit of the Binomial, applied continuously. The Poisson process is the continuous-time limit of a Bernoulli process where:

- Time is divided into $n$ tiny slots of length $1/n$
- Each slot has an arrival with probability $\lambda/n$
- Slots are independent

As $n \to \infty$, discrete slots become continuous time, and Binomial counts become Poisson counts.

> _This is the "rare events" paradigm at the process level. The Poisson process is what you get when you take a Bernoulli process, let the time slots shrink to zero, and keep the rate fixed. The Binomial$\to$Poisson limit is a corollary._

## Why the Poisson Process Is Everywhere

The Poisson process is the canonical model for **"arrivals occurring randomly in time"** because it is the unique process satisfying:

- Independent increments (the past doesn't affect the future)
- Stationary increments (no preferred time)
- Orderliness (at most one arrival at any instant)

These are minimal, natural conditions for "completely random" arrivals. Any process satisfying these axioms must be Poisson. This is a uniqueness theorem — the Poisson process is not just _a_ model for random arrivals, it is _the_ model.

**Examples:**

- Radioactive decay events in a detector
- Customer arrivals at a queue
- Mutations along a DNA strand
- Requests to a web server
- Photon arrivals at a telescope sensor

In all of these: events arrive independently, at a roughly constant rate, with at most one per instant. Poisson process.

## The Non-Homogeneous Poisson Process

A mild but important generalisation: replace the constant rate $\lambda$ with a rate function $\lambda(t) \geq 0$ that varies over time.

Then $N(t) \sim \text{Pois}(\Lambda(t))$ where $\Lambda(t) = \int_0^t \lambda(s),ds$ is the **cumulative intensity**.

The axioms of independent and stationary increments still hold (for increments of $\Lambda$, not $t$), but the Exponential inter-arrival time result fails — inter-arrival times are no longer i.i.d. Expo.

> _The non-homogeneous version is what you use when the rate changes over time: more customers at lunchtime, more server requests during business hours. The key object becomes $\Lambda(t)$, not $\lambda$._

## Personal Notes

- The three-views table is the most useful organising principle. Any Poisson process problem is asking you about counts (Poisson), inter-arrival times (Exponential), or arrival times (Gamma). Identify which view is cleanest before computing.
- The splitting independence is always surprising to people. Burn this into memory: thinning a Poisson process gives two _independent_ Poisson processes. This is used constantly in queueing theory, genetics, and network modelling.
- The uniform order statistics theorem is the theorem I return to most often in applications. It reduces "where are the arrivals?" to a uniform distribution problem, which is almost always easier.
- The merging + splitting theorems together say: the Poisson family is closed under both operations, with rates combining additively. This closure is what makes the Poisson process so analytically tractable.
- The non-homogeneous process is more realistic in almost every application. The key insight: it's just a time-changed homogeneous process. If you set $\tau = \Lambda(t)$, the process in $\tau$-time is homogeneous Poisson with rate 1.
