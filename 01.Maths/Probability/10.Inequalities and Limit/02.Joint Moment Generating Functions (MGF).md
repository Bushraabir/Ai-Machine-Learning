

> _The MGF of a single random variable already feels like a miracle — you pack all the moments into one function and differentiation hands them back to you. The joint MGF is that same miracle, but now it encodes the entire dependence structure between variables. The thing that floored me: independence is equivalent to the joint MGF factoring. Not "related to" — equivalent. The joint MGF is a complete fingerprint of the joint distribution (when it exists)._

## Prerequisites

- Single-variable MGF: $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$, and that it determines the distribution (when finite in a neighborhood of 0)
- Joint distributions, joint PDF/PMF
- Independence: $X \perp Y \iff$ joint factors as product of marginals
- Linearity of expectation, and $\mathbb{E}[g(X)h(Y)] = \mathbb{E}[g(X)]\mathbb{E}[h(Y)]$ when $X \perp Y$

## 1. Definition

### 1.1 The joint MGF of a random vector

Let $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_k)$ be a random vector. The **joint MGF** is the function $M_\mathbf{X} : \mathbb{R}^k \to [0,\infty]$ defined by:

$$ M_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{t}) = M_{X_1,\ldots,X_k}(t_1,\ldots,t_k) := \mathbb{E}!\left[e^{t_1 X_1 + t_2 X_2 + \cdots + t_k X_k}\right] = \mathbb{E}!\left[e^{\mathbf{t} \cdot \mathbf{X}}\right] $$

for $\mathbf{t} = (t_1, \ldots, t_k) \in \mathbb{R}^k$, provided this expectation is finite.

> _We say the joint MGF exists if $M_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{t}) < \infty$ for all $\mathbf{t}$ in some open neighborhood of $\mathbf{0} \in \mathbb{R}^k$. Same condition as the univariate case, just in higher dimension._

---

### 1.2 The two-variable case written out

For $(X, Y)$, the joint MGF is:

$$ M_{X,Y}(s, t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{sX + tY}] $$

If $(X,Y)$ has joint PDF $f_{X,Y}$:

$$ M_{X,Y}(s,t) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{sx + ty} f_{X,Y}(x,y),dx,dy $$

This is the **bilateral Laplace transform** of the joint density. The MGF literally is a transform — no accident it determines the distribution.

---

## 2. Recovering Marginal MGFs

This is the first thing to verify when you see the definition. Setting $t = 0$:

$$ M_{X,Y}(s, 0) = \mathbb{E}[e^{sX + 0 \cdot Y}] = \mathbb{E}[e^{sX}] = M_X(s) $$

More generally, to get the marginal MGF of $X_i$, set all $t_j = 0$ for $j \neq i$.

> _The marginals are embedded in the joint MGF by zeroing out the other variables. This is completely analogous to getting a marginal density by integrating out the other variable — zeroing a parameter in the MGF corresponds to integrating out in density space._

---

## 3. The Fundamental Theorem: Independence ↔ Factorization

### 3.1 Statement

$$ X_1, \ldots, X_k \text{ are independent} \iff M_{X_1,\ldots,X_k}(t_1,\ldots,t_k) = M_{X_1}(t_1)\cdots M_{X_k}(t_k) $$

for all $(t_1,\ldots,t_k)$ in a neighborhood of $\mathbf{0}$.

### 3.2 Proof ($\Rightarrow$)

If $X_1,\ldots,X_k$ are independent:

$$ M_{X_1,\ldots,X_k}(t_1,\ldots,t_k) = \mathbb{E}!\left[\prod_{i=1}^k e^{t_i X_i}\right] = \prod_{i=1}^k \mathbb{E}[e^{t_i X_i}] = \prod_{i=1}^k M_{X_i}(t_i) $$

The second equality uses: if $X_1,\ldots,X_k$ are independent, then $g_1(X_1),\ldots,g_k(X_k)$ are independent for any measurable $g_i$, and hence the expectation of their product equals the product of their expectations. $\square$

### 3.3 Proof ($\Leftarrow$)

If the joint MGF factors, then since the MGF determines the distribution (when it exists in a neighborhood of 0), the joint distribution must be the product of the marginals. But joint distribution = product of marginals is exactly independence. $\square$

> _The $(\Leftarrow)$ direction is where you need to actually believe that the MGF determines the distribution — that's a non-trivial theorem (it uses the fact that moment sequences, under growth conditions, uniquely determine a distribution; or equivalently, the Laplace/Fourier transform is injective on distributions). Take it as given for now, but remember it's doing real work here._

## 4. Extracting Moments

### 4.1 Mixed moments via differentiation

The whole point of the MGF is that moments come from derivatives. For the joint:

$$ \frac{\partial^{m+n}}{\partial s^m ,\partial t^n} M_{X,Y}(s,t)\Bigg|_{s=0,,t=0} = \mathbb{E}[X^m Y^n] $$

**Why?** Expand the exponential:

$$ M_{X,Y}(s,t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{sX+tY}] = \mathbb{E}!\left[\sum_{m=0}^\infty\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{s^m t^n}{m!,n!} X^m Y^n\right] = \sum_{m=0}^\infty\sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{\mathbb{E}[X^m Y^n]}{m!,n!},s^m t^n $$

So the joint MGF is the double power series with coefficients $\mathbb{E}[X^m Y^n]/(m!,n!)$. Differentiating $m$ times in $s$ and $n$ times in $t$ and setting $s=t=0$ picks out the $(m,n)$ coefficient times $m!,n!$, giving $\mathbb{E}[X^m Y^n]$.

> _This is the same song as the univariate case. The MGF is a generating function for all moments — including mixed ones like $\mathbb{E}[XY]$, $\mathbb{E}[X^2Y]$, etc. Those mixed moments encode correlation and dependence._

### 4.2 Getting $\text{Cov}(X,Y)$ from the joint MGF

$$ \text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y] $$

$$ = \frac{\partial^2 M_{X,Y}}{\partial s,\partial t}\Bigg|_{(0,0)} - \frac{\partial M_{X,Y}}{\partial s}\Bigg|_{(0,0)} \cdot \frac{\partial M_{X,Y}}{\partial t}\Bigg|_{(0,0)} $$

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, the joint MGF factors, so the mixed partial $\partial^2 M/\partial s,\partial t = M_X'(s)M_Y'(t)$, and at $(0,0)$ this equals $\mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$. So $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$, recovering the fact that independence implies uncorrelatedness.

## 5. MGF of a Linear Combination

### 5.1 General formula

Let $Z = a_1 X_1 + \cdots + a_k X_k$. Then:

$$ M_Z(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tZ}] = \mathbb{E}[e^{t(a_1X_1+\cdots+a_kX_k)}] = \mathbb{E}[e^{(ta_1)X_1+\cdots+(ta_k)X_k}] = M_\mathbf{X}(ta_1,\ldots,ta_k) $$

> _The MGF of any linear combination lives inside the joint MGF — just evaluate it at the right point. This is extremely useful: you never need to find the distribution of $Z$ explicitly to get its MGF._

### 5.2 Sum of independent r.v.'s

If $X_1,\ldots,X_k$ are independent, then for $Z = X_1 + \cdots + X_k$:

$$ M_Z(t) = M_\mathbf{X}(t,t,\ldots,t) = \prod_{i=1}^k M_{X_i}(t) $$

This is the standard fact: MGF of a sum of independents = product of MGFs.

## 6. Worked Example: Bivariate Normal

### 6.1 Setup

Let $(X,Y) \sim \text{Bivariate Normal}$ with means $\mu_X, \mu_Y$, variances $\sigma_X^2, \sigma_Y^2$, and correlation $\rho$. The joint PDF is:

$$ f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_X\sigma_Y\sqrt{1-\rho^2}}\exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)}\left[\frac{(x-\mu_X)^2}{\sigma_X^2} - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_X)(y-\mu_Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y} + \frac{(y-\mu_Y)^2}{\sigma_Y^2}\right]\right) $$

### 6.2 The joint MGF

$$ M_{X,Y}(s,t) = \exp!\left(\mu_X s + \mu_Y t + \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_X^2 s^2 + 2\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y st + \sigma_Y^2 t^2)\right) $$

> _Derivation: complete the square inside the integral, recognize a Gaussian. I won't reproduce the full algebra here, but the key step is that $sX + tY$ is itself normal with mean $s\mu_X + t\mu_Y$ and variance $\sigma_X^2 s^2 + 2\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y st + \sigma_Y^2 t^2$, and $\mathbb{E}[e^W] = e^{\mu_W + \sigma_W^2/2}$ for $W \sim N(\mu_W,\sigma_W^2)$._

### 6.3 Independence in the bivariate normal

For the bivariate normal, the joint MGF factors as $M_X(s)M_Y(t)$ if and only if the cross term $\rho\sigma_X\sigma_Y st$ vanishes, i.e., $\rho = 0$.

So for the **bivariate normal**: $X \perp Y \iff \text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0 \iff \rho = 0$.

> _This is special to the normal family. In general, uncorrelated does NOT imply independent. The bivariate normal is one of the rare cases where they're equivalent, and the joint MGF makes this completely transparent._

### 6.4 Verifying marginals

Setting $t=0$:

$$ M_{X,Y}(s,0) = \exp!\left(\mu_X s + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_X^2 s^2\right) = M_X(s) $$

which is the MGF of $N(\mu_X, \sigma_X^2)$. ✓

## 7. Worked Example: $(X,Y)$ with $X,Y$ iid $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$

$M_X(t) = M_Y(t) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}$ for $t < \lambda$.

Since $X \perp Y$:

$$ M_{X,Y}(s,t) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda-s}\cdot\frac{\lambda}{\lambda-t} $$

**MGF of $X+Y$:** evaluate at $(t,t)$:

$$ M_{X+Y}(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda-t}\right)^2 $$

This is the MGF of $\text{Gamma}(2,\lambda)$. So $X+Y \sim \text{Gamma}(2,\lambda)$. ✓ (Consistent with the reproductive property of the Gamma.)

**Mixed moment:** $\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y] = (1/\lambda)^2 = 1/\lambda^2$ by independence. Can verify by differentiating the joint MGF in $s$ and $t$ and evaluating at $(0,0)$.

## 8. Why the Joint MGF Determines the Joint Distribution

This is the theorem I always take on faith but should understand at least heuristically.

**Theorem.** If $M_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{t})$ is finite for all $\mathbf{t}$ in an open neighborhood of $\mathbf{0}$, then the joint distribution of $\mathbf{X}$ is uniquely determined by $M_\mathbf{X}$.

**Heuristic:** The joint MGF is the bilateral Laplace transform of the joint law. The bilateral Laplace transform is injective on a suitable space of measures (this follows from the Fourier inversion theorem applied to the characteristic function, obtained by $\mathbf{t} \mapsto i\mathbf{t}$). The condition "finite in a neighborhood of $\mathbf{0}$" ensures the MGF is analytic there, so the power series (i.e., all mixed moments) determines it.

> _In practice: if two joint distributions have the same joint MGF wherever it's defined, they're the same distribution. This is how we identify distributions — compute the MGF, match it to something known._

## 11. Open Questions / Things to Sit With

- **Characteristic function analog:** The joint **characteristic function** $\varphi_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{t}) = \mathbb{E}[e^{i\mathbf{t}\cdot\mathbf{X}}]$ always exists (no finiteness condition needed) and also determines the joint distribution. Is everything here translatable to that setting? Yes — and that's the "right" object for heavy-tailed distributions.
- **Cumulant generating function:** $\log M_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{t})$ gives the **joint cumulant generating function**. The mixed cumulants $\kappa_{mn} = \partial_s^m\partial_t^n \log M_{X,Y}\big|_{(0,0)}$ measure "pure" dependence — $\kappa_{11} = \text{Cov}(X,Y)$ and higher mixed cumulants vanish iff joint normality (under appropriate conditions). Worth understanding.
- **Multivariate normal in full generality:** The joint MGF is $M_\mathbf{X}(\mathbf{t}) = \exp(\boldsymbol\mu^\top \mathbf{t} + \frac{1}{2}\mathbf{t}^\top\Sigma\mathbf{t})$. The covariance matrix $\Sigma$ appears explicitly. This is the cleanest characterization of the multivariate normal.
