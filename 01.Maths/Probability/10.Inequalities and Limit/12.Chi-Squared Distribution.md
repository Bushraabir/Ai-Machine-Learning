> _"The chi-squared isn't some arbitrary distribution someone cooked up for hypothesis testing. It falls out of the normal completely naturally — it's what happens when you square standard normals and add them. Everything else follows from that one fact."_

## The Big Picture

I used to think of $\chi^2$ as a statistical tool — something you invoke for goodness-of-fit tests. That framing puts the cart before the horse. The chi-squared distribution is a **derived distribution**: it's the answer to the question _"what is the distribution of a sum of squared standard normals?"_ That question arises constantly — estimating variance, testing independence, understanding quadratic forms in linear models. The distribution is just answering something natural.

Four facts to internalize before anything else:

1. $\chi^2_1 = Z^2$ where $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ — the entire family starts here.
2. $\chi^2_n$ is the sum of $n$ independent $\chi^2_1$'s — the parameter literally counts squared normals.
3. $\chi^2_n = \mathrm{Gamma}(n/2,, 1/2)$ — all Gamma machinery applies directly.
4. By CLT, $\chi^2_n$ looks increasingly normal as $n \to \infty$ — it's a living demonstration of CLT.

## Definition

### The Right Way to Think About It

Let $Z_1, \ldots, Z_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Define:

$$X = \sum_{i=1}^n Z_i^2$$

Then $X \sim \chi^2_n$, the **chi-squared distribution with $n$ degrees of freedom**.

The parameter $n$ is called degrees of freedom because here it literally is one: the number of unconstrained squared standard normals being summed. Later in regression and contingency tables the terminology gets murkier, but the root meaning is always "rank of the quadratic form."

### The Analytical Handle

$$\chi^2_n ;=; \mathrm{Gamma}!\left(\frac{n}{2},; \frac{1}{2}\right)$$

using the shape-rate parameterization: $X \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ has PDF $\propto x^{\alpha-1}e^{-\beta x}$ on $(0,\infty)$.

---

## The PDF

For $x > 0$:

$$f_n(x) = \frac{1}{2^{n/2},\Gamma(n/2)}; x^{n/2 - 1}; e^{-x/2}$$

This is Gamma($n/2$, $1/2$) written out explicitly.

Special cases worth memorizing:

- **$n=1$:** $f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi x}},e^{-x/2}$ — there's a singularity at $0$, because squaring a $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ concentrates mass near zero.
- **$n=2$:** $f(x) = \frac{1}{2}e^{-x/2}$ — this is $\mathrm{Expo}(1/2)$. The chi-squared with 2 degrees of freedom is exponential. Clean and worth knowing cold.

---

## Deriving $\chi^2_1$: The Base Case

**Claim:** If $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$, then $Z^2 \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(1/2,, 1/2)$.

**Proof via CDF:**

For $x > 0$: $$F_{Z^2}(x) = P(Z^2 \leq x) = P(-\sqrt{x} \leq Z \leq \sqrt{x}) = 2\Phi(\sqrt{x}) - 1$$

Differentiate with respect to $x$: $$f_{Z^2}(x) = 2\phi(\sqrt{x}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}} = \frac{\phi(\sqrt{x})}{\sqrt{x}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi x}},e^{-x/2}$$

Rewrite to match the Gamma form: $$f_{Z^2}(x) = \frac{1}{2^{1/2},\Gamma(1/2)},x^{1/2-1},e^{-x/2}$$

using $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$.

This is exactly $\mathrm{Gamma}(1/2,, 1/2)$. $\blacksquare$

> _The appearance of $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$ is the same $\sqrt{\pi}$ that normalizes the Gaussian integral $\int e^{-x^2},dx = \sqrt{\pi}$. The normalization constants are the same constant in different clothes. I find this genuinely satisfying — squaring a normal and integrating brings you right back to the same transcendental constant._

---

## Building $\chi^2_n$: The Additive Property

**Claim:** If $X_1 \sim \chi^2_{n_1}$ and $X_2 \sim \chi^2_{n_2}$ are independent, then $X_1 + X_2 \sim \chi^2_{n_1 + n_2}$.

**Proof via MGFs:**

The MGF of $\chi^2_n$ (derived below) is: $$M_{\chi^2_n}(t) = (1 - 2t)^{-n/2}, \qquad t < \tfrac{1}{2}$$

By independence: $$M_{X_1+X_2}(t) = (1-2t)^{-n_1/2} \cdot (1-2t)^{-n_2/2} = (1-2t)^{-(n_1+n_2)/2}$$

This is the MGF of $\chi^2_{n_1+n_2}$, and MGFs uniquely determine distributions. $\blacksquare$

The $\chi^2_n$ being a sum of $n$ independent $\chi^2_1$'s is just Gamma additivity: $$\mathrm{Gamma}!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right) * \cdots * \mathrm{Gamma}!\left(\tfrac{1}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right) = \mathrm{Gamma}!\left(\tfrac{n}{2}, \tfrac{1}{2}\right)$$

---

## The MGF

$$M_X(t) = (1 - 2t)^{-n/2}, \qquad t < \tfrac{1}{2}$$

**Proof (start from $\chi^2_1$):**

$$M_{Z^2}(t) = E[e^{tZ^2}] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tz^2},\frac{e^{-z^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}},dz = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-z^2(1-2t)/2},dz$$

This is a Gaussian integral with variance $1/(1-2t)$, valid for $t < 1/2$. It evaluates to:

$$M_{Z^2}(t) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-2t}} = (1-2t)^{-1/2}$$

By independence and i.i.d.: $$M_{\chi^2_n}(t) = \left[(1-2t)^{-1/2}\right]^n = (1-2t)^{-n/2} \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _The key step is recognizing that $e^{tz^2} \cdot e^{-z^2/2} = e^{-z^2(1-2t)/2}$ is just another Gaussian, with variance $1/(1-2t)$. The MGF is doing a change of variance. This trick — completing the Gaussian integral by reading off the normalizing constant — comes up constantly._

---

## Moments

From the Gamma moments $E[\mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha,\beta)] = \alpha/\beta$ and $\mathrm{Var} = \alpha/\beta^2$:

$$E[\chi^2_n] = \frac{n/2}{1/2} = n \qquad \mathrm{Var}(\chi^2_n) = \frac{n/2}{(1/2)^2} = 2n$$

Or directly: since $E[Z_i^2] = 1$ and $\mathrm{Var}(Z_i^2) = E[Z^4] - (E[Z^2])^2 = 3 - 1 = 2$, linearity gives the same answer. (The fourth moment $E[Z^4] = 3$ comes from the general formula $E[Z^{2k}] = (2k-1)!! = 1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2k-1)$.)

The ratio $\mathrm{Var}/E = 2$ is constant in $n$ — a useful sanity check.

---

## Asymptotics: CLT for $\chi^2_n$

Since $\chi^2_n = \sum_{i=1}^n Z_i^2$ is a sum of i.i.d. r.v.'s with mean 1 and variance 2, the CLT gives immediately:

$$\frac{\chi^2_n - n}{\sqrt{2n}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

The chi-squared distribution is itself a demonstration of what CLT does. For large $n$, using a normal table for chi-squared critical values is justified.

A sharper approximation (Wilson-Hilferty, 1931): $(\chi^2_n/n)^{1/3}$ is approximately $\mathcal{N}(1 - 2/(9n),; 2/(9n))$. The cube-root transformation stabilizes the skewness. Worth knowing the approximation exists even if I won't derive it now.

## Key Relationships

### Sample Variance

If $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and $S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar{X})^2$, then:

$$\frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$$

and moreover $\bar{X} \perp S^2$ (independent!). This is **Cochran's theorem** — one of the foundational results of classical statistics. The degrees of freedom drop from $n$ to $n-1$ because one degree of freedom is consumed estimating $\mu$ with $\bar{X}$.

The proof runs through the spectral decomposition of the quadratic form $\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2/\sigma^2 = \mathbf{Z}^\top(I - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top)\mathbf{Z}$ where $\mathbf{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}_n(\mathbf{0}, I)$. The matrix $I - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$ is idempotent with rank $n-1$. Deserves its own note.

### $t$-Distribution

If $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $V \sim \chi^2_n$ are independent:

$$T = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{V/n}} \sim t_n$$

This is why a $t$-test has $n-1$ degrees of freedom: the $Z$ in the numerator is the standardized $\bar{X}$, and the $V \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$ in the denominator is (proportional to) $S^2$, the estimated variance. Dividing by the estimated rather than known $\sigma$ inflates the tails — $t_n \to \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ as $n \to \infty$ by LLN (the denominator $\to 1$).

### $F$-Distribution

If $U \sim \chi^2_m$ and $V \sim \chi^2_n$ are independent:

$$F = \frac{U/m}{V/n} \sim F_{m,n}$$

A ratio of scaled chi-squareds. Appears in ANOVA (ratio of between-group to within-group variance) and in comparing two sample variances.

### Quadratic Forms in Normal Vectors (the general picture)

If $\mathbf{Z} \sim \mathcal{N}_n(\mathbf{0}, I)$ and $A$ is an $n \times n$ idempotent matrix with $\mathrm{rank}(A) = r$, then:

$$\mathbf{Z}^\top A,\mathbf{Z} \sim \chi^2_r$$

Every chi-squared that appears in statistics is secretly a quadratic form in a normal vector, and its degrees of freedom is the rank of the projection matrix. The sample variance case, the goodness-of-fit statistic, the residual sum of squares in regression — all of them.

## Why Chi-Squared Appears in Tests

### Goodness-of-Fit

Observed counts $O_i$ vs. expected counts $E_i = np_i$ across $k$ categories:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i=1}^k \frac{(O_i - E_i)^2}{E_i} \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{k-1}$$

**Mechanically why:** Each $(O_i - E_i)/\sqrt{E_i}$ is approximately $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ by CLT (it's a standardized count). Squaring and summing gives approximately $\chi^2_k$. The constraint $\sum O_i = n$ reduces rank by 1, giving $\chi^2_{k-1}$. The precise derivation goes through the multivariate CLT applied to the multinomial.

### Independence in Contingency Tables

$r \times c$ table, observed $O_{ij}$, expected under independence $E_{ij} = R_i C_j / n$:

$$\chi^2 = \sum_{i,j}\frac{(O_{ij} - E_{ij})^2}{E_{ij}} \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_{(r-1)(c-1)}$$

Degrees of freedom: $(r-1)(c-1)$ — the number of cells that are "free" after fixing all marginals.

### Likelihood Ratio Tests (Wilks' Theorem)

For a null hypothesis that reduces a $p$-parameter model by $d$ constraints:

$$-2\log\Lambda \xrightarrow{d} \chi^2_d$$

The $\chi^2_d$ arises because the log-likelihood is locally quadratic near the MLE (Taylor expansion), so the likelihood ratio statistic is asymptotically a quadratic form in $d$ approximately normal quantities. This is the most general justification — essentially every chi-squared test in statistics is a special case of Wilks' theorem.

> _Wilks' theorem is the reason chi-squared tests are so ubiquitous. It says: any likelihood ratio test, for any model, asymptotically distributes as chi-squared with degrees of freedom equal to the number of constraints tested. That's a breathtaking level of generality._
