

> _The Central Limit Theorem says sums of i.i.d. finite-variance random variables converge to normal. But three specific families — Binomial, Poisson, Gamma — converge to normal in their own natural parameter limits, and each convergence has its own flavor, its own rate, and its own practical lesson. Understanding why each one converges, not just that it does, is the goal of this note._

## 0. The Unifying Idea

All three families are **sums of i.i.d. random variables** in disguise:

$$\text{Bin}(n,p) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Bern}(p) \qquad \text{Pois}(\lambda) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Pois}(\lambda/n) \qquad \text{Gamma}(n,\lambda) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Expo}(\lambda)$$

The CLT applies to each. The only question is: which parameter grows, how fast, and what does the error look like?

|Family|Growing Parameter|Mean|Variance|Rate of Convergence|
|---|---|---|---|---|
|$\text{Bin}(n,p)$|$n \to \infty$, $p$ fixed|$np$|$np(1-p)$|$O(1/\sqrt{n})$|
|$\text{Pois}(\lambda)$|$\lambda \to \infty$|$\lambda$|$\lambda$|$O(1/\sqrt{\lambda})$|
|$\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$|$n \to \infty$, $\lambda$ fixed|$n/\lambda$|$n/\lambda^2$|$O(1/\sqrt{n})$|

## 1. Binomial $\to$ Normal

### 1.1 Statement

Let $S_n \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$ with $p \in (0,1)$ fixed, $\mu = np$, $\sigma^2 = np(1-p)$. Then:

$$\frac{S_n - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

That is, for fixed $a < b$:

$$\mathbb{P}!\left(a \leq \frac{S_n - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \leq b\right) \to \Phi(b) - \Phi(a)$$

where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF.

### 1.2 Why: The CLT Applied Directly

$S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ where $X_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bern}(p)$. Each $X_i$ has mean $p$ and variance $p(1-p)$. The CLT applies immediately. This is the de Moivre–Laplace theorem, historically the first CLT (proved for $p = 1/2$ by de Moivre in 1733, generalized by Laplace).

### 1.3 MGF Proof Sketch

The MGF of $\text{Bern}(p)$ is $M(s) = 1 - p + pe^s$. The standardized sum has MGF:

$$M_{Z_n}(s) = \left[M!\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)\right]^n \cdot e^{-nps/\sqrt{np(1-p)}}$$

Taylor-expanding $\log M(s/\sqrt{n})$ around $s = 0$:

$$\log M_{Z_n}(s) \to \frac{s^2}{2}$$

which is the log-MGF of $N(0,1)$. $\square$

### 1.4 Continuity Correction

Since $S_n$ is integer-valued and $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ is continuous, replace:

$$\mathbb{P}(S_n = k) \approx \mathbb{P}!\left(k - \tfrac{1}{2} \leq N \leq k + \tfrac{1}{2}\right)$$

More generally:

$$\mathbb{P}(S_n \leq k) \approx \Phi!\left(\frac{k + \frac{1}{2} - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)$$

The $\frac{1}{2}$ correction is the **continuity correction**. It materially improves accuracy, especially for small $n$ or extreme $p$.

> [!tip] When to Use Continuity Correction Always apply it when approximating point probabilities $\mathbb{P}(S_n = k)$ or when $n$ is moderate. For large $n$ the correction becomes negligible relative to $\sqrt{n}$, but it costs nothing to include it.

### 1.5 Berry-Esseen Bound

The error in the normal approximation is controlled quantitatively. For $X_i$ i.i.d. with mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$, and $\rho = \mathbb{E}[|X_i - \mu|^3] < \infty$:

$$\sup_x \left|\mathbb{P}!\left(\frac{S_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \leq x\right) - \Phi(x)\right| \leq \frac{C\rho}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}}$$

where $C \leq 0.4748$ (best known constant). For Binomial:

$$\text{Error} \leq \frac{C}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}$$

The approximation is good when $np(1-p)$ is large — i.e., when neither $p$ nor $1-p$ is too small and $n$ is large.

### 1.6 When Not to Use the Normal Approximation

- $p$ close to 0 or 1 with moderate $n$: use [[Poisson Approximation]] instead ($\lambda = np$)
- $p$ close to 1 with moderate $n$: flip and use Poisson on failures
- Rule of thumb: normal approximation is good when $np \geq 5$ and $n(1-p) \geq 5$

### 1.7 Numerical Example

$S_{100} \sim \text{Bin}(100, 0.4)$. Compute $\mathbb{P}(S_{100} \leq 35)$.

$\mu = 40$, $\sigma = \sqrt{24} \approx 4.899$.

Without continuity correction: $\Phi!\left(\frac{35 - 40}{4.899}\right) = \Phi(-1.021) \approx 0.1537$.

With continuity correction: $\Phi!\left(\frac{35.5 - 40}{4.899}\right) = \Phi(-0.918) \approx 0.1793$.

Exact (by direct computation): $\approx 0.1795$. The correction is nearly exact.

## 2. Poisson $\to$ Normal

### 2.1 Statement

Let $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$. Then as $\lambda \to \infty$:

$$\frac{X - \lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

### 2.2 Why: Poisson as a Sum

The key representation: if $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Pois}(\lambda/n)$, then $X_1 + \cdots + X_n \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$. As $\lambda \to \infty$, take $n = \lambda$ and each summand is $\text{Pois}(1)$ with mean 1 and variance 1. The CLT gives:

$$\frac{\text{Pois}(\lambda) - \lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^\lambda \text{Pois}(1) - \lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

### 2.3 MGF Proof

The MGF of $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$ is $M(s) = e^{\lambda(e^s - 1)}$. The log-MGF of the standardized variable $Z = (X - \lambda)/\sqrt{\lambda}$ is:

$$\log M_Z(s) = \lambda!\left(e^{s/\sqrt{\lambda}} - 1\right) - s\sqrt{\lambda}$$

Taylor-expand $e^{s/\sqrt{\lambda}} = 1 + s/\sqrt{\lambda} + s^2/(2\lambda) + s^3/(6\lambda^{3/2}) + \cdots$:

$$\log M_Z(s) = \lambda!\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt{\lambda}} + \frac{s^2}{2\lambda} + O(\lambda^{-3/2})\right) - s\sqrt{\lambda} = \frac{s^2}{2} + O(\lambda^{-1/2}) \to \frac{s^2}{2}$$

The log-MGF converges to $s^2/2$, the log-MGF of $N(0,1)$. $\square$

### 2.4 The Poisson–Binomial–Normal Triangle

$$\text{Bin}(n,p) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty,, np \to \lambda} \text{Pois}(\lambda) \xrightarrow{\lambda \to \infty} N(\lambda, \lambda)$$

Going directly: $\text{Bin}(n,p) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty,, p\text{ fixed}} N(np, np(1-p))$.

The Poisson is an intermediate stop between Binomial and Normal in parameter space. When $p$ is small and $\lambda = np$ is moderate, Poisson is better. When $\lambda$ is large, Normal is good. The triangle tells you which approximation to reach for.

### 2.5 Continuity Correction

Same logic as for the Binomial — $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$ is integer-valued:

$$\mathbb{P}(X \leq k) \approx \Phi!\left(\frac{k + \frac{1}{2} - \lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}}\right)$$

### 2.6 Rule of Thumb

The normal approximation to the Poisson is good when $\lambda \geq 20$ or so. For $\lambda < 20$, work with the Poisson directly.

### 2.7 Numerical Example

$X \sim \text{Pois}(25)$. Compute $\mathbb{P}(X \leq 20)$.

$\mu = \sigma^2 = 25$, $\sigma = 5$.

Without correction: $\Phi!\left(\frac{20 - 25}{5}\right) = \Phi(-1) \approx 0.1587$.

With correction: $\Phi!\left(\frac{20.5 - 25}{5}\right) = \Phi(-0.9) \approx 0.1841$.

Exact: $\approx 0.1827$. Again the correction wins substantially.

## 3. Gamma $\to$ Normal

### 3.1 Statement

Let $X \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$ (shape $n$, rate $\lambda$), so $\mathbb{E}[X] = n/\lambda$ and $\text{Var}(X) = n/\lambda^2$. Then as $n \to \infty$:

$$\frac{X - n/\lambda}{\sqrt{n}/\lambda} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

### 3.2 Why: Gamma as a Sum of Exponentials

The fundamental representation: if $T_1, \ldots, T_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Expo}(\lambda)$, then:

$$T_1 + \cdots + T_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$$

Each $T_i$ has mean $1/\lambda$ and variance $1/\lambda^2$. The CLT gives:

$$\frac{\sum_{i=1}^n T_i - n/\lambda}{\sqrt{n}/\lambda} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1) \qquad \square$$

No MGF argument needed — this is just the CLT applied to Exponential summands.

### 3.3 MGF Verification

The MGF of $\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$ is $M(s) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - s}\right)^n$ for $s < \lambda$. The log-MGF of the standardized variable $Z = (X - n/\lambda)/(\sqrt{n}/\lambda)$:

$$\log M_Z(s) = n\log!\left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - s\lambda/\sqrt{n}}\right) - \frac{s\sqrt{n}}{\lambda} \cdot \frac{n}{\lambda}$$

Let $u = s/\sqrt{n}$. Taylor-expand $-\log(1 - u) = u + u^2/2 + u^3/3 + \cdots$:

$$\log M_Z(s) = n\left(\frac{s}{\sqrt{n}} + \frac{s^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right) - s\sqrt{n} = \frac{s^2}{2} + O(n^{-1/2}) \to \frac{s^2}{2} \qquad \square$$

### 3.4 The Chi-Squared Special Case

$\chi^2_n = \text{Gamma}(n/2, 1/2)$ (shape $n/2$, rate $1/2$). Mean $= n$, variance $= 2n$. By the same result:

$$\frac{\chi^2_n - n}{\sqrt{2n}} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

Equivalently (Fisher's approximation, sharper):

$$\sqrt{2\chi^2_n} - \sqrt{2n-1} \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$$

The Fisher form converges faster because it removes skewness to first order.

### 3.5 Connection: Poisson Arrival Times

If arrivals are Poisson($\lambda$) and $T_n = $ time of the $n$-th arrival, then $T_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$. The normal approximation to $T_n$ says: for large $n$, the $n$-th arrival time is approximately $N(n/\lambda, n/\lambda^2)$. This is the CLT for renewal processes.

### 3.6 Numerical Example

$X \sim \text{Gamma}(30, 2)$. Compute $\mathbb{P}(X \leq 18)$.

$\mu = 30/2 = 15$, $\sigma^2 = 30/4 = 7.5$, $\sigma \approx 2.739$.

$\mathbb{P}(X \leq 18) \approx \Phi!\left(\frac{18 - 15}{2.739}\right) = \Phi(1.095) \approx 0.8633$.

> [!note] No continuity correction for Gamma — it is already a continuous distribution. The normal approximation applies directly without adjustment.

## 4. Rates of Convergence and the Berry-Esseen Perspective

For all three families, the Berry-Esseen theorem quantifies the error:

$$\sup_x \left|\mathbb{P}(Z_n \leq x) - \Phi(x)\right| \leq \frac{C \cdot \mathbb{E}[|X_i - \mu|^3]}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}}$$

The third absolute moment controls the rate. For each family:

|Family|Third Central Moment $\rho$|Error Bound|
|---|---|---|
|$\text{Bern}(p)$|$(1-p)^3 p + p^3(1-p)$|$\frac{C(1 - 2p(1-p))}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}$|
|$\text{Pois}(\lambda/n)$ (each summand)|$\lambda/n$|$C/\sqrt{\lambda}$|
|$\text{Expo}(\lambda)$|$2/\lambda^3$|$C \cdot 2/\sqrt{n}$|

The approximation degrades when:

- $p \to 0$ or $p \to 1$ in the Binomial (skewness increases)
- $\lambda$ is small for the Poisson (heavy right tail)
- $n$ is small for the Gamma (Exponential is very skewed: skewness $= 2$)

---

## 5. Skewness: Why the Convergence Has Direction

All three families are **right-skewed** for finite parameters. The skewness of each:

$$\text{Skew}(\text{Bin}(n,p)) = \frac{1 - 2p}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

$$\text{Skew}(\text{Pois}(\lambda)) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \to 0 \quad \text{as } \lambda \to \infty$$

$$\text{Skew}(\text{Gamma}(n,\lambda)) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{n}} \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Convergence to normal is equivalent to skewness going to zero at rate $1/\sqrt{n}$ or $1/\sqrt{\lambda}$. The normal approximation is most accurate in the center of the distribution and least accurate in the tails, where skewness persists longest.

> [!note] For fixed $n$ or $\lambda$, the right tail of each distribution is heavier than the normal approximation predicts, and the left tail is lighter. This is why the Chernoff bound (which uses the exact MGF) beats the normal approximation for tail probabilities — the normal misses the asymmetry.

## 6. The Square Root Trick (Variance-Stabilizing Transformations)

Sometimes instead of standardizing $X$ directly, we transform it to make the variance constant (independent of the mean), then apply the normal approximation to the transformed variable.

### 6.1 Poisson: Square Root Transform

If $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$, then $\sqrt{X}$ has approximately constant variance:

$$\sqrt{X} \approx N!\left(\sqrt{\lambda} - \frac{1}{8\sqrt{\lambda}},; \frac{1}{4}\right)$$

So $2\sqrt{X} \approx N(2\sqrt{\lambda}, 1)$ and the variance is approximately $1/4$ regardless of $\lambda$. This is the **Anscombe transform**. Used in statistics to stabilize Poisson data before applying normal-based tests.

### 6.2 Binomial: Arcsin Transform

If $\hat{p} = S_n/n \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)/n$, the variance $p(1-p)/n$ depends on $p$. The variance-stabilizing transform is:

$$\arcsin!\left(\sqrt{\hat{p}}\right) \approx N!\left(\arcsin!\left(\sqrt{p}\right),; \frac{1}{4n}\right)$$

Variance $1/(4n)$ is now independent of $p$.

### 6.3 Gamma: Log Transform

If $X \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$, then $\log X$ has approximately constant variance:

$$\log X \approx N!\left(\psi(n) - \log\lambda,; \psi'(n)\right) \approx N!\left(\log(n/\lambda),; \frac{1}{n}\right)$$

where $\psi$ is the digamma function and $\psi'(n) \approx 1/n$ for large $n$.

## 7. Summary: Which Approximation When?

### Binomial $\text{Bin}(n,p)$:

|Regime|Best Approximation|
|---|---|
|$n$ large, $p$ fixed, $np(1-p) \geq 10$|Normal with continuity correction|
|$n$ large, $p$ small, $np = \lambda$ moderate|Poisson($\lambda$)|
|$n$ large, $p$ small, $np$ large|Normal|
|$n$ small, any $p$|Exact|

### Poisson($\lambda$):

|Regime|Best Approximation|
|---|---|
|$\lambda \geq 20$|Normal with continuity correction|
|$\lambda < 20$|Exact Poisson|

### Gamma$(n, \lambda)$:

|Regime|Best Approximation|
|---|---|
|$n \geq 30$|Normal (no continuity correction needed)|
|$n < 30$|Exact Gamma / use software|

## 9. Open Questions / Things to Sit With

- [ ] **Edgeworth expansions:** The normal approximation is the zeroth-order term. The Edgeworth expansion adds corrections: $f_{Z_n}(x) \approx \phi(x)\left[1 + \frac{\kappa_3}{6\sqrt{n}}H_3(x) + \cdots\right]$ where $H_3$ is the third Hermite polynomial and $\kappa_3$ is the third cumulant. This explains why skewed distributions have predictable errors in the normal approximation.
- [ ] **Local CLT:** The CLT gives CDF convergence. The local CLT says the PMF/PDF itself converges: $\sqrt{n},p_{S_n}(np + x\sqrt{n}) \to \phi(x)$ uniformly. This is needed for things like local limit theorems for random walks.
- [ ] **Poisson CLT vs Binomial CLT:** The Poisson is the limit of Bin$(n, \lambda/n)$ as $n \to \infty$. The normal is the limit of Bin$(n,p)$ as $n \to \infty$ with $p$ fixed. These are different limits (rare events vs common events). The transition between them is at $p \sim 1/\sqrt{n}$.
- [ ] **Multivariate normal convergence:** The Multinomial$(n, p_1, \ldots, p_k)$ converges to a multivariate normal. The covariance matrix has $\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = -np_ip_j$ for $i \neq j$. The singular covariance (since $\sum X_i = n$) means the limit lives on a hyperplane.
- [ ] **Rate of convergence for the Gamma:** The Gamma has skewness $2/\sqrt{n}$, larger than the Binomial ($\sim 1/\sqrt{n}$) for comparable parameters. Does this mean the Gamma converges slower? Quantify using Berry-Esseen.
