## 1. Convexity — What I Actually Need

### 1.1 Definition

$g : I \to \mathbb{R}$ (on an interval $I$) is **convex** if for all $x, y \in I$ and $\lambda \in [0,1]$:

$$ g(\lambda x + (1-\lambda)y) \leq \lambda g(x) + (1-\lambda)g(y) $$

Geometrically: the chord between any two points on the graph lies _above_ the graph. The function "curves upward."

$g$ is **concave** if $-g$ is convex, i.e., the inequality flips.

### 1.2 The second derivative test

If $g$ is twice differentiable:

$$ g \text{ convex} \iff g''(x) \geq 0 \text{ for all } x \in I $$ $$ g \text{ concave} \iff g''(x) \leq 0 \text{ for all } x \in I $$

> _Examples I should know cold:_
> 
> - _Convex: $x^2$, $e^x$, $x^p$ for $p \geq 1$ or $p \leq 0$ (on $x>0$), $-\log x$, $|x|$_
> - _Concave: $\log x$, $\sqrt{x}$, $x^p$ for $0 < p < 1$ (on $x>0$)_

### 1.3 The supporting line (tangent line) property

This is the key geometric fact I need for the proof.

$g$ is convex $\iff$ for every $c \in I$, there exists a constant $a$ (the slope of a supporting line at $c$) such that:

$$ g(x) \geq g(c) + a(x - c) \quad \text{for all } x \in I $$

The line $\ell(x) = g(c) + a(x-c)$ passes through $(c, g(c))$ and lies _below_ $g$ everywhere. If $g$ is differentiable at $c$, we take $a = g'(c)$ and this is just the tangent line. If $g$ is not differentiable (e.g., $|x|$ at 0), any subgradient works.

> _This is the one-line proof of Jensen. The supporting line linearizes $g$ from below, and expectation is linear. That's it._

## 2. Jensen's Inequality

### 2.1 Statement

Let $X$ be a random variable and $g$ a convex function. Then:

$$ \boxed{g(\mathbb{E}[X]) \leq \mathbb{E}[g(X)]} $$

provided both sides exist (i.e., $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ is well-defined, possibly $+\infty$).

If $g$ is **concave**, the inequality reverses:

$$ g(\mathbb{E}[X]) \geq \mathbb{E}[g(X)] $$

### 2.2 Intuition before proof

Expectation is a weighted average — a "center of mass." Jensen says: for a convex function, applying the function after averaging gives a smaller value than averaging after applying the function. Convexity means the function "curves up," so individual values $g(X(\omega))$ are pulled upward relative to $g$ evaluated at the average.

> _The slogan: averaging then applying ≤ applying then averaging, for convex $g$._
> 
> _Equivalently: a convex function of a mean ≤ mean of the convex function. I mix this up sometimes. Burn in the direction: $g(\mathbb{E}[X]) \leq \mathbb{E}[g(X)]$._

### 2.3 Proof (supporting line)

Let $\mu = \mathbb{E}[X]$. Since $g$ is convex, there exists $a \in \mathbb{R}$ such that:

$$ g(x) \geq g(\mu) + a(x - \mu) \quad \text{for all } x $$

This holds pointwise for every value $x$ that $X$ takes. So, substituting $x = X$:

$$ g(X) \geq g(\mu) + a(X - \mu) \quad \text{almost surely} $$

Take expectations of both sides (expectation preserves $\geq$ a.s.):

$$ \mathbb{E}[g(X)] \geq \mathbb{E}[g(\mu) + a(X-\mu)] = g(\mu) + a(\mathbb{E}[X] - \mu) = g(\mu) + a \cdot 0 = g(\mu) $$

$$ \therefore \quad \mathbb{E}[g(X)] \geq g(\mathbb{E}[X]) \qquad \square $$

> _The proof is three lines. The magic is that the supporting line linearizes $g$ from below, and linearity of expectation kills the $(X - \mu)$ term. The whole proof is: find a linear lower bound on $g$, take $\mathbb{E}$, watch the linear part vanish._

### 2.4 Equality condition

Equality $g(\mathbb{E}[X]) = \mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ holds iff $g(X) = g(\mu) + a(X-\mu)$ a.s., i.e., $X$ lies a.s. on the supporting line at $\mu$. This happens iff:

- $X$ is constant a.s. (trivial case), OR
- $g$ is linear on the support of $X$

> _Equality for $g(x) = x^2$: requires $X$ constant a.s., i.e., $\text{Var}(X) = 0$. Consistent with $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \iff \text{Var}(X) = 0$._

## 3. The Most Important Special Cases

### 3.1 $g(x) = x^2$ (convex)

$$ (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2] $$

Equivalently: $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \geq 0$.

Jensen is the proof that variance is non-negative. I always knew variance was non-negative from the definition $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2] \geq 0$, but Jensen gives it differently — via convexity of $x^2$.

### 3.2 $g(x) = e^x$ (convex)

$$ e^{\mathbb{E}[X]} \leq \mathbb{E}[e^X] $$

This is fundamental in the MGF world. If $X$ has MGF $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$, then Jensen gives $M_X(t) \geq e^{t\mathbb{E}[X]}$ for all $t$. The MGF always dominates the "naïve exponential."

### 3.3 $g(x) = \log x$ (concave, $x > 0$)

$$ \log(\mathbb{E}[X]) \geq \mathbb{E}[\log X] $$

Equivalently: the arithmetic mean is at least the geometric mean.

**AM-GM as Jensen:** Let $X$ be uniform on ${x_1,\ldots,x_n}$ with each $x_i > 0$. Then:

$$ \log!\left(\frac{x_1+\cdots+x_n}{n}\right) \geq \frac{\log x_1 + \cdots + \log x_n}{n} = \log(x_1\cdots x_n)^{1/n} $$

Exponentiating: $\frac{x_1+\cdots+x_n}{n} \geq (x_1\cdots x_n)^{1/n}$. That's AM-GM. $\square$

> _Jensen unifies AM-GM, the variance inequality, and the MGF lower bound under one theorem. That's why it's worth understanding deeply rather than just citing._

### 3.4 $g(x) = -\log x$ (convex) — the information inequality

For any two probability distributions $p, q$ (on a finite set for simplicity), the KL divergence satisfies:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p ,|, q) = \sum_x p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} \geq 0 $$

**Proof via Jensen.** Let $X$ be a r.v. with distribution $q$, and consider $Z = p(X)/q(X)$ (the likelihood ratio). Apply Jensen with $g = -\log$ (convex):

$$ -\log\mathbb{E}_q[Z] \leq \mathbb{E}_q[-\log Z] $$

$$ \mathbb{E}_q[Z] = \sum_x q(x)\cdot\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \sum_x p(x) = 1 $$

So $-\log(1) \leq \mathbb{E}_q[-\log(p(X)/q(X))]$, i.e., $0 \leq \mathbb{E}_q[\log(q(X)/p(X))] = D_{\text{KL}}(q|p)$.

> _Information theory runs on Jensen. The non-negativity of KL divergence — the most important inequality in information theory — is just Jensen applied to $-\log$. I find this genuinely beautiful._

## 4. The Discrete Proof (Finite Case)

Before the full proof by supporting lines, here's the finite/discrete version — cleaner for building intuition, and useful if you need to prove Jensen by induction.

**Claim.** For $\lambda_1,\ldots,\lambda_n \geq 0$ with $\sum \lambda_i = 1$ and $g$ convex:

$$ g!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i x_i\right) \leq \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i g(x_i) $$

**Proof by induction.** Base case $n=2$: this is the definition of convexity.

Inductive step: assume true for $n-1$. Write $\lambda = \lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_{n-1}$ (so $\lambda_n = 1-\lambda$, $\lambda > 0$ WLOG). Then:

$$ g!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i x_i\right) = g!\left(\lambda \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda}x_i + (1-\lambda)x_n\right) $$

Apply the $n=2$ case with "points" $y = \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda}x_i$ and $x_n$:

$$ \leq \lambda, g!\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda}x_i\right) + (1-\lambda),g(x_n) $$

Apply the inductive hypothesis to the first term:

$$ \leq \lambda \sum_{i=1}^{n-1}\frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda}g(x_i) + (1-\lambda)g(x_n) = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i g(x_i) \qquad \square $$

The probabilistic statement follows by taking $\lambda_i = \mathbb{P}(X = x_i)$.

## 5. Conditional Jensen

An important extension: Jensen holds conditionally.

If $g$ is convex, then:

$$ g(\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}]) \leq \mathbb{E}[g(X) \mid \mathcal{G}] \quad \text{a.s.} $$

for any sub-$\sigma$-algebra $\mathcal{G}$.

**Proof.** Same supporting line argument: $g(X) \geq g(\mathbb{E}[X\mid\mathcal{G}]) + a(X - \mathbb{E}[X\mid\mathcal{G}])$ pointwise. Take conditional expectation given $\mathcal{G}$; the term $\mathbb{E}[X\mid\mathcal{G}]$ is $\mathcal{G}$-measurable so it comes out, and $\mathbb{E}[X - \mathbb{E}[X\mid\mathcal{G}]\mid\mathcal{G}] = 0$ by the projection property of conditional expectation. $\square$

> _Conditional Jensen appears in martingale theory — it's why $g(M_n)$ is a submartingale when $M_n$ is a martingale and $g$ is convex. Keep this in the back of my mind for when I get to martingales._

## 8. Common Mistakes

**Mixing up the direction.** For convex $g$: $g(\mathbb{E}[X]) \leq \mathbb{E}[g(X)]$. The expectation "goes inside" on the bigger side. Mnemonic: convex curves up, so $g$ applied to individual values is larger on average than $g$ of the center.

**Forgetting the existence condition.** Jensen requires $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ to be well-defined. If $g(X)$ is not integrable, Jensen doesn't apply. This matters for heavy-tailed $X$.

**Applying Jensen to functions of multiple variables.** Jensen is for a single convex function of a single r.v. For $g(X,Y)$, convexity in each argument separately is not enough — you need joint convexity. This is subtler.

## 9. Open Questions / Things to Sit With

- **Reverse Jensen (the variational form):** $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sup_{\ell \text{ linear}, \ell \leq g} \mathbb{E}[\ell(X)]$. The expectation of a convex function is the supremum of expectations of linear functions that lie below it. This is the Fenchel-Legendre duality perspective. Worth understanding once I see more convex analysis.
- **Operator Jensen:** For matrices, there's a Jensen inequality for operator convex functions: $g(A) \leq g(B)$ if $A \leq B$ (in the PSD order) and $g$ is operator monotone. This shows up in quantum information. Far future, but good to know it exists.
- **When is Jensen tight?** The gap $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] - g(\mathbb{E}[X])$ is related to $\text{Var}(X) \cdot g''(\mu)/2$ by Taylor expansion. Quantifying this gap is the subject of refinements of Jensen.
