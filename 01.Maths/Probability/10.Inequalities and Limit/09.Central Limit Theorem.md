> _"The LLN tells you where the sample mean goes. The CLT tells you how it gets there — and the answer is shockingly universal."_

---

## The Big Picture

The LLN said $\bar{X}_n \to \mu$. Fine. But $\bar{X}_n - \mu \to 0$ is a degenerate statement — it converges to a point. If I zoom in at exactly the right rate, something non-trivial is sitting there. That's the CLT.

The miracle is this: **no matter what distribution you start with** (subject to finite variance), if you standardize the sample mean, you always get the same limiting object — the standard normal. The Gaussian isn't just one distribution among many. It's an _attractor_ in the space of distributions under convolution and scaling.

This is genuinely shocking. Sum a thousand Bernoullis, sum a thousand Exponentials, sum a thousand Uniforms — zoom in at scale $\sqrt{n}$, and you see the same bell curve every time. The proof reveals why.

---

## Setup

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be **i.i.d.** with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2 \in (0, \infty)$.

Define the **standardized sample mean**:

$$Z_n = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{\displaystyle\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \mu)}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}$$

The CLT says $Z_n$ converges _in distribution_ to $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

---

## Statement

$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

Equivalently, for all $x \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$P(Z_n \leq x) \to \Phi(x) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Or in the most practically useful form:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n X_i ;\dot{\sim}; \mathcal{N}(n\mu,; n\sigma^2) \quad \text{for large } n$$

---

## Proof via Moment Generating Functions

This is the Stat 110 proof. It's clean, memorable, and illuminates _why_ the normal appears.

**Assume** $M(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ exists in a neighborhood of $0$.

**Step 1: Center and normalize.**

Let $Y_i = (X_i - \mu)/\sigma$, so $E[Y_i] = 0$, $\mathrm{Var}(Y_i) = 1$, MGF is $M_Y$. Then:

$$Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$$

**Step 2: MGF of $Z_n$.**

By independence and the i.i.d. structure:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = E!\left[e^{tZ_n}\right] = \left[M_Y!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n$$

**Step 3: Taylor expand $M_Y$ around $0$.**

Since $E[Y] = 0$ and $E[Y^2] = 1$:

$$M_Y(s) = 1 + s\underbrace{E[Y]}_{=,0} + \frac{s^2}{2}\underbrace{E[Y^2]}_{=,1} + O(s^3) = 1 + \frac{s^2}{2} + O(s^3)$$

Substituting $s = t/\sqrt{n}$:

$$M_Y!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O!\left(n^{-3/2}\right)$$

**Step 4: Raise to the $n$-th power.**

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left[1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O!\left(n^{-3/2}\right)\right]^n \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} e^{t^2/2}$$

using the standard limit $(1 + a/n + o(1/n))^n \to e^a$.

**Step 5: Recognize the limit.**

$e^{t^2/2}$ is exactly the MGF of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Since MGFs uniquely determine distributions and convergence of MGFs implies convergence in distribution:

$$Z_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1) \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _The hinge of this proof is Step 3. The Taylor expansion kills the first-order term because the mean is zero. The $s^2/2$ term — the variance term — is what survives. Everything of order $s^3$ and above vanishes faster than $1/n$ when raised to the $n$-th power. The normal is "selected" because it's the distribution whose MGF is $e^{t^2/2}$, i.e., the one whose log-MGF is purely quadratic. The CLT is the statement that all the higher-order terms get annihilated by the scaling._

## Proof via Characteristic Functions (the correct general proof)

The MGF proof requires the MGF to exist, which fails for heavy-tailed distributions. The right proof uses **characteristic functions**:

$$\varphi_X(t) = E[e^{itX}]$$

which always exists. The structure is identical: let $Y_i = (X_i - \mu)/\sigma$, then

$$\varphi_{Z_n}(t) = \left[\varphi_Y!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n$$

Taylor expand (using $E[Y]=0$, $E[Y^2]=1$, noting the $i^2 = -1$ sign):

$$\varphi_Y(s) = 1 + isE[Y] - \frac{s^2}{2}E[Y^2] + O(s^3) = 1 - \frac{s^2}{2} + O(s^3)$$

So:

$$\varphi_{Z_n}(t) = \left[1 - \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right]^n \to e^{-t^2/2}$$

And $e^{-t^2/2}$ is the characteristic function of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The conclusion follows from **Lévy's continuity theorem**: if characteristic functions converge pointwise to a function continuous at $0$, the distributions converge in distribution.

> _This version works for all distributions with finite variance — no MGF assumption. The Cauchy distribution has neither finite mean nor variance, and indeed the CLT fails for it. Characteristic functions are the natural language here, not MGFs. I should think of the MGF proof as the "training wheels" version._

---

## What $\xrightarrow{d}$ Really Means

**Convergence in distribution** is the weakest mode:

$$\text{a.s.} \implies \text{in probability} \implies \text{in distribution}$$

It does **not** say the random variables $Z_n$ themselves get close to $Z$ in any pathwise sense — only that their _laws_ converge. The CDFs converge at every continuity point of the limit CDF. The CLT lives at the bottom of this hierarchy, and that's appropriate: what's universal is the _shape_ of the limiting distribution, not the realizations.

---

## Rate of Convergence: Berry-Esseen

The CLT gives no rate. The **Berry-Esseen theorem** does:

$$\sup_{x \in \mathbb{R}} \left|P(Z_n \leq x) - \Phi(x)\right| \leq \frac{C \cdot E|Y_i|^3}{\sqrt{n}}$$

for absolute constant $C \leq 0.4748$ (sharpest known). The rate is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$, controlled by the **third absolute moment** — a measure of skewness.

- Symmetric distributions (zero skewness) converge faster.
- Skewed distributions need larger $n$.

The folk theorem "n = 30 is enough" is a rough proxy for when $O(1/\sqrt{n})$ error becomes negligible. For skewed distributions — say, Exponential — you need much larger $n$.

## Why the Normal? The Cumulant Explanation

This is the deep reason, not just the mechanical proof.

**Cumulants** are the coefficients of the cumulant generating function $\log M_X(t)$:

$$\log M_X(t) = \kappa_1 t + \frac{\kappa_2}{2!}t^2 + \frac{\kappa_3}{3!}t^3 + \cdots$$

where $\kappa_1 = \mu$, $\kappa_2 = \sigma^2$, $\kappa_3$ = third cumulant (related to skewness), etc. Cumulants are **additive** for independent r.v.'s.

Now track what happens to $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\sum(X_i - \mu)$:

- **Centering** kills $\kappa_1$
- **Scaling by $1/\sigma\sqrt{n}$** scales the $k$-th cumulant by $(\sigma\sqrt{n})^{-k}$, so $\kappa_k(Z_n) = \frac{n \cdot \kappa_k(X)}{\sigma^k n^{k/2}} = \frac{\kappa_k(X)}{\sigma^k n^{k/2-1}}$

For $k \geq 3$: $\kappa_k(Z_n) \to 0$ as $n \to \infty$ (since $k/2 - 1 > 0$).

The only distribution with $\kappa_1 = 0$, $\kappa_2 = 1$, and all higher cumulants zero is $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. Convolution + scaling drives all cumulants of order $\geq 3$ to zero. The Gaussian is the unique fixed point of this operation.

> _This is the most satisfying answer to "why normal?" that I know. The proof shows how; the cumulant argument shows why. Both are worth internalizing. The Gaussian isn't special because someone decided it was — it's the unique distribution that survives the annihilation of all higher-order structure._

## Applications

### Normal approximation to Binomial

$S_n \sim \mathrm{Bin}(n,p)$ — write it as a sum of i.i.d. $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(p)$, so $\mu = p$, $\sigma^2 = p(1-p)$:

$$\frac{S_n - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

**Continuity correction** (since $S_n$ is discrete): use $k + \tfrac{1}{2}$ as the cutoff when approximating $P(S_n \leq k)$.

### Normal approximation to Poisson

$S_n \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda)$ with large $\lambda$: $\mu = \sigma^2 = \lambda$, so

$$\frac{S_n - \lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

### The Delta Method

If $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$ and $g$ is differentiable at $\mu$ with $g'(\mu) \neq 0$:

$$\sqrt{n}\bigl(g(\bar{X}_n) - g(\mu)\bigr) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}!\left(0,; [g'(\mu)]^2\sigma^2\right)$$

**Proof idea:** Taylor expand $g(\bar{X}_n) \approx g(\mu) + g'(\mu)(\bar{X}_n - \mu)$ and apply CLT to the linear term. This is one of the most practically powerful consequences of the CLT — it gives asymptotic distributions for any smooth transformation of the sample mean. Variance-stabilizing transformations (like $\log$, $\arcsin\sqrt{\cdot}$) are built by choosing $g$ so that $[g'(\mu)]^2\sigma^2(\mu) = \text{const}$.

## Multivariate CLT

Let $\mathbf{X}_1, \ldots, \mathbf{X}_n$ be i.i.d. random vectors in $\mathbb{R}^k$ with mean $\boldsymbol{\mu}$ and covariance matrix $\boldsymbol{\Sigma}$.

$$\sqrt{n}\left(\bar{\mathbf{X}}_n - \boldsymbol{\mu}\right) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}_k(\mathbf{0}, \boldsymbol{\Sigma})$$

The proof uses the **Cramér-Wold device**: a sequence of random vectors converges in distribution iff every linear combination of its components converges. So check $\mathbf{t}^\top \mathbf{Z}_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \mathbf{t}^\top\boldsymbol{\Sigma}\mathbf{t})$ for all $\mathbf{t}$ via the univariate CLT, and you're done.

---

## Failure Modes

- **Infinite variance:** Cauchy has no finite mean or variance. The correct framework is **stable distributions** and the **generalized CLT** (Gnedenko). The scaling is $n^{1/\alpha}$ for $\alpha \in (0,2]$ instead of $\sqrt{n}$.
- **Dependence:** For dependent sequences, extensions exist (CLT for martingales, $m$-dependent sequences, mixing processes) but require additional conditions controlling how fast dependence decays.
- **Non-identical distributions:** The **Lindeberg-Feller theorem** gives the necessary and sufficient condition for CLT when the $X_i$ are independent but not identically distributed. The condition (Lindeberg condition) says no single term dominates the sum.

## Connection Back to LLN

||LLN|CLT|
|---|---|---|
|**Object**|$\bar{X}_n$|$(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n})$|
|**Limit**|$\mu$ — a constant|$\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ — a distribution|
|**Zoom scale**|None needed|$1/\sqrt{n}$|
|**Mode of convergence**|a.s. / in probability|in distribution|
|**Assumption**|Finite mean|Finite variance|

LLN is first-order. CLT is second-order. **Large deviation theory** is the next layer — it asks about the probability of events far outside the $\sqrt{n}$ window, i.e., exponentially unlikely deviations.
