
> _Entropy is one of those definitions that feels arbitrary until suddenly it doesn't. Why $-\sum p_i \log p_i$? Why not something else? The answer is that Shannon essentially proved this is the only function satisfying a small list of properties any reasonable measure of uncertainty should have. Once you accept those axioms, entropy is forced on you. And then it starts appearing everywhere — in coding theory, in statistical mechanics, in hypothesis testing, in the geometry of exponential families. This note is my attempt to understand entropy deeply, not just compute it._

## 1. Definition

### 1.1 Entropy of a Discrete Distribution

Let $X$ be a discrete r.v. with PMF $p(x) = \mathbb{P}(X = x)$ on a finite or countable support $\mathcal{X}$. The **entropy** of $X$ is:

$$H(X) = -\sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x)\log p(x) = \mathbb{E}[-\log p(X)]$$

with the convention $0 \log 0 = 0$ (since $x\log x \to 0$ as $x \to 0^+$).

> [!note] The Right Way to Remember This The form $H(X) = \mathbb{E}[-\log p(X)]$ is the one to remember. Entropy is the expected **surprise** or **self-information** $-\log p(X)$. When $X = x$ is rare ($p(x)$ small), $-\log p(x)$ is large — you're surprised. When $X = x$ is certain ($p(x) = 1$), $-\log p(x) = 0$ — no surprise at all. Entropy averages this surprise over all outcomes.

### 1.2 Units

|Base|Unit|Note|
|---|---|---|
|2|**bits**|$H(X) = 1$ bit means $X$ is a fair coin flip|
|$e$|**nats**|Used in analysis; cleaner formulas|
|10|**dits**|Rare|

The relationship: $H_2(X) = H_e(X) / \ln 2$. Everything scales by $\log_2 e = 1/\ln 2 \approx 1.443$.

## 2. The Axiomatic Derivation (Why This Formula?)

Shannon (1948) showed entropy is the **unique** function satisfying:

1. **Continuity:** $H(p_1,\ldots,p_n)$ is continuous in the $p_i$.
2. **Symmetry:** $H$ is invariant under permutation of outcomes.
3. **Maximality:** $H$ is maximized when $p_i = 1/n$ for all $i$ (uniform).
4. **Additivity / Recursion:** $H(p_1,\ldots,p_n) = H(p_1+p_2, p_3,\ldots,p_n) + (p_1+p_2),H!\left(\tfrac{p_1}{p_1+p_2}, \tfrac{p_2}{p_1+p_2}\right)$

The fourth axiom says: coarsening and then refining gives the same total entropy. Under these axioms, entropy must be $-C\sum p_i \log p_i$ for some constant $C > 0$. Setting $C$ fixes the base of the logarithm.

> [!tip] Why This is Satisfying The formula is not pulled from thin air — it is forced by requirements that any sane measure of uncertainty must satisfy. This is analogous to how the normal distribution is forced by the CLT, or how the Poisson is forced by the limit of rare events.

## 3. Basic Properties

### 3.1 Non-Negativity

$$H(X) \geq 0$$

**Proof.** Since $p(x) \in [0,1]$, we have $\log p(x) \leq 0$, so $-p(x)\log p(x) \geq 0$ for each term. $\square$

Equality iff $X$ is constant a.s. (one $p(x) = 1$, rest $0$): zero uncertainty means zero entropy.

### 3.2 Maximum Entropy: the Uniform Distribution

For $X$ supported on $n$ outcomes:

$$H(X) \leq \log n$$

with equality iff $X \sim \text{Uniform}{1,\ldots,n}$.

**Proof.** Let $u(x) = 1/n$ be the uniform PMF. Then:

$$H(X) = -\sum_x p(x)\log p(x) = -\sum_x p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{u(x)} + \sum_x p(x)\log\frac{1}{u(x)}$$

$$= -D_{\text{KL}}(p|u) + \log n \leq \log n$$

since $D_{\text{KL}}(p|u) \geq 0$ (by Jensen). Equality iff $p = u$. $\square$

> [!note] This proof shows the gap between $H(X)$ and $\log n$ is exactly $D_{\text{KL}}(p|u)$ — the KL divergence to the uniform. Maximum entropy distributions are those closest to uniform in the KL sense. This perspective generalizes: the max-entropy distribution subject to constraints is the one minimizing KL to the unconstrained maximum, which leads to exponential families.

### 3.3 Deterministic Transformation Loses Entropy

If $Y = f(X)$ for a deterministic function $f$:

$$H(Y) \leq H(X)$$

with equality iff $f$ is injective on the support of $X$. Applying a function can only lose information, never gain it.

## 4. Joint Entropy and Conditional Entropy

### 4.1 Joint Entropy

For a pair $(X, Y)$:

$$H(X, Y) = -\sum_{x,y} p(x,y)\log p(x,y) = \mathbb{E}[-\log p(X,Y)]$$

### 4.2 Conditional Entropy

$$H(Y \mid X) = \sum_x p(x),H(Y \mid X = x) = -\sum_{x,y} p(x,y)\log p(y\mid x) = \mathbb{E}[-\log p(Y\mid X)]$$

Conditional entropy is the average uncertainty in $Y$ after observing $X$. It's an expectation of a log-probability of the conditional distribution — the symmetry with the unconditional definition is exact.

### 4.3 Chain Rule for Entropy

$$H(X, Y) = H(X) + H(Y \mid X)$$

**Proof.**

$$H(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[-\log p(X,Y)] = \mathbb{E}[-\log(p(X)p(Y\mid X))] = \mathbb{E}[-\log p(X)] + \mathbb{E}[-\log p(Y\mid X)]$$

$$= H(X) + H(Y\mid X) \qquad \square$$

Extends to any number of variables:

$$H(X_1,\ldots,X_n) = \sum_{i=1}^n H(X_i \mid X_1,\ldots,X_{i-1})$$

### 4.4 Conditioning Reduces Entropy

$$H(Y \mid X) \leq H(Y)$$

with equality iff $X$ and $Y$ are independent.

**Proof.** $H(Y) - H(Y\mid X) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y) =: I(X;Y) \geq 0$ (mutual information, proved below). $\square$

Observing $X$ can only reduce (or maintain) uncertainty about $Y$, never increase it — the information-theoretic formalization of "data never hurts."

## 5. Mutual Information

### 5.1 Definition

The **mutual information** between $X$ and $Y$:

$$I(X; Y) = H(X) + H(Y) - H(X,Y) = H(X) - H(X\mid Y) = H(Y) - H(Y\mid X)$$

Equivalently:

$$I(X;Y) = \sum_{x,y} p(x,y)\log\frac{p(x,y)}{p(x)p(y)} = D_{\text{KL}}(p_{XY} ,|, p_X \otimes p_Y)$$

> [!note] Mutual information is the KL divergence between the joint distribution and the product of marginals. It measures how far $(X,Y)$ is from being independent — KL divergence to the "independence model."

### 5.2 Non-Negativity of Mutual Information

$$I(X;Y) \geq 0$$

with equality iff $X \perp Y$.

**Proof.** $I(X;Y) = D_{\text{KL}}(p_{XY}|p_X p_Y) \geq 0$ by the non-negativity of KL divergence (Jensen applied to $-\log$). $\square$

This simultaneously proves $H(Y\mid X) \leq H(Y)$ and gives the equality condition.

### 5.3 The Entropy Venn Diagram

$$H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y) - I(X;Y)$$

Draw a Venn diagram: two circles for $H(X)$ and $H(Y)$, their overlap is $I(X;Y)$, their union is $H(X,Y)$. For two variables, the picture is exact.

> [!warning] Unlike set Venn diagrams, conditional entropy can behave oddly with three or more variables — conditional entropy can exceed joint entropy in some extended senses. The diagram is exact for two variables only.

## 6. KL Divergence — The Parent of Everything

### 6.1 Definition

For two PMFs $p, q$ on the same support $\mathcal{X}$:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(p | q) = \sum_{x} p(x)\log\frac{p(x)}{q(x)} = \mathbb{E}_p!\left[\log\frac{p(X)}{q(X)}\right]$$

with $0\log(0/0) = 0$ and $p(x)\log(p(x)/0) = +\infty$ if $p(x) > 0$.

> [!warning] KL divergence is **not** a metric — it's not symmetric and doesn't satisfy the triangle inequality. But it is always non-negative.

### 6.2 Non-Negativity via Jensen

**Proof.** Apply Jensen to $g(t) = -\log t$ (convex) and the r.v. $q(X)/p(X)$ under $\mathbb{P} = p$:

$$D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) = \mathbb{E}_p!\left[-\log\frac{q(X)}{p(X)}\right] \geq -\log\mathbb{E}_p!\left[\frac{q(X)}{p(X)}\right] = -\log\sum_x p(x)\frac{q(x)}{p(x)} = -\log 1 = 0$$

Equality iff $q(X)/p(X)$ is constant $p$-a.s., i.e., $p = q$. $\square$

### 6.3 Entropy as KL to the Uniform

$$H(X) = \log|\mathcal{X}| - D_{\text{KL}}(p | u)$$

where $u$ is uniform on $\mathcal{X}$. So $H(X) \leq \log|\mathcal{X}|$ with equality iff $p = u$.

> [!note] Entropy is not a standalone object — it's always secretly a KL divergence to the uniform. The "amount of information" in $X$ is the distance from $p$ to maximum ignorance.

## 7. Cross-Entropy

### 7.1 Definition

The **cross-entropy** of $q$ relative to $p$:

$$H(p, q) = -\sum_x p(x)\log q(x) = \mathbb{E}_p[-\log q(X)]$$

### 7.2 Relation to KL and Entropy

$$H(p, q) = H(p) + D_{\text{KL}}(p|q)$$

**Proof.**

$$H(p,q) = -\mathbb{E}_p[\log q(X)] = -\mathbb{E}_p[\log p(X)] + \mathbb{E}_p!\left[\log\frac{p(X)}{q(X)}\right] = H(p) + D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) \qquad\square$$

Since $D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$: cross-entropy is always at least the true entropy, $H(p,q) \geq H(p)$.

> [!tip] Machine Learning Connection Cross-entropy is what you minimize in ML classification. Minimizing $H(p,q)$ over $q$ (with $p$ fixed as the true distribution) is equivalent to minimizing $D_{\text{KL}}(p|q)$, since $H(p)$ doesn't depend on $q$. So training a classifier by minimizing cross-entropy loss is the same as minimizing KL divergence to the true distribution. Jensen is quietly inside every neural network training loop.

## 8. Maximum Entropy Principle

### 8.1 The Unconstrained Case

Maximize $H(X)$ over all distributions on $n$ outcomes: the maximum is $\log n$, achieved uniquely by the uniform. Proved in §3.2.

### 8.2 Constrained Case — Exponential Families Emerge

Maximize $H(X)$ subject to $\mathbb{E}[f_k(X)] = \alpha_k$ for $k = 1,\ldots,m$ (given moment constraints). By Lagrange multipliers, the solution has the form:

$$p^*(x) = \frac{1}{Z(\lambda)}\exp!\left(\sum_{k=1}^m \lambda_k f_k(x)\right)$$

where $Z(\lambda) = \sum_x \exp(\sum_k \lambda_k f_k(x))$ is the **partition function** and $\lambda_k$ are Lagrange multipliers chosen to satisfy the constraints.

This is an **exponential family**:

|Constraint|MaxEnt Distribution|
|---|---|
|Support ${0,1,\ldots}$, mean $= \mu$|Geometric|
|Support ${1,\ldots,n}$, no constraint|Uniform|
|Support $\mathbb{R}$, mean $= \mu$, variance $= \sigma^2$|Normal|
|Support $[0,\infty)$, mean $= \mu$|Exponential|

> [!tip] This is why the normal distribution is everywhere: it is the maximum entropy distribution given only a mean and variance constraint — pure maximum ignorance subject to those two numbers. Maximum entropy gives a principled reason these distributions are "natural."

## 9. Data Compression — Why Entropy Matters

### 9.1 Shannon's Source Coding Theorem

If $X_1, X_2, \ldots \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} p$, then the minimum expected number of bits per symbol needed to encode the sequence is exactly $H(X)$ bits. Formally: for any uniquely decodable code, the expected code length $\bar L$ satisfies:

$$\bar L \geq H(X)$$

and there exist codes with $\bar L < H(X) + 1$.

Entropy is the fundamental limit of lossless compression. If $H(X) = 3$ bits, no compression scheme can do better than 3 bits per symbol on average. Huffman coding nearly achieves this.

### 9.2 The Surprise Interpretation

The optimal code assigns $-\log_2 p(x)$ bits to outcome $x$. Rare events get long codes; common events get short codes. Entropy is the expected code length under the optimal code. This makes $H(X) = \mathbb{E}[-\log_2 p(X)]$ completely natural — it's the expected optimal code length.

## 10. Examples

### 10.1 Bernoulli($p$) — Binary Entropy Function

$X \sim \text{Bern}(p)$:

$$H(X) = -p\log p - (1-p)\log(1-p) =: h(p)$$

- $h(0) = h(1) = 0$: no uncertainty when outcome is deterministic
- $h(1/2) = \log 2 = 1$ bit: maximum uncertainty for a binary variable
- $h$ is concave on $[0,1]$, symmetric around $p = 1/2$

### 10.2 Uniform on ${1,\ldots,n}$

$$H(X) = -\sum_{k=1}^n \frac{1}{n}\log\frac{1}{n} = \log n$$

Maximum entropy on $n$ outcomes, as expected.

### 10.3 Geometric($p$) on ${1, 2, 3, \ldots}$

$\mathbb{P}(X = k) = (1-p)^{k-1}p$.

$$H(X) = -\sum_{k=1}^\infty (1-p)^{k-1}p\log((1-p)^{k-1}p) = \frac{h(p)}{p}$$

where $h(p)$ is the binary entropy function. (Derivation: separate the log, use geometric series formulas for $\sum k (1-p)^{k-1}$.)

## 11. The Relationship Map

$$H(X) = \log|\mathcal{X}| - D_{\text{KL}}(p|u)$$

$$H(p,q) = H(p) + D_{\text{KL}}(p|q)$$

$$I(X;Y) = D_{\text{KL}}(p_{XY}|p_X p_Y)$$

$$H(X,Y) = H(X) + H(Y\mid X) \quad\text{(chain rule)}$$

$$I(X;Y) = H(X) - H(X\mid Y) = H(Y) - H(Y\mid X)$$

> [!note] Every information-theoretic quantity is either an entropy or a KL divergence in disguise. The KL divergence is the atom. Everything else is built from it and from $\log|\mathcal{X}|$.

## 13. Open Questions / Things to Sit With

- [ ] **Continuous entropy (differential entropy):** For continuous $X$ with PDF $f$, define $h(X) = -\int f(x)\log f(x),dx$. This is NOT the limit of the discrete entropy as the partition gets finer (that limit is $-\infty$). Differential entropy can be negative, and many discrete entropy properties break. Worth a separate note.
- [ ] **Rényi entropy:** $H_\alpha(X) = \frac{1}{1-\alpha}\log\sum_x p(x)^\alpha$. This is a one-parameter family: $H_1 = H$ (Shannon), $H_0 = \log|\text{support}|$, $H_\infty = -\log\max_x p(x)$ (min-entropy). Appears in cryptography.
- [ ] **The geometry of exponential families:** The MaxEnt distributions form an exponential family, and KL divergence is the Bregman divergence associated to the negative entropy. The geometry here (information geometry) is beautiful — geodesics in KL are $e$-geodesics and $m$-geodesics, Pythagoras theorem holds for KL projections.
- [ ] **Connection to statistical mechanics:** The Boltzmann entropy $S = k_B\log W$ is entropy of the uniform distribution over microstates. The Gibbs entropy $-k_B\sum p_i \log p_i$ is Shannon entropy. They coincide at equilibrium because equilibrium is maximum entropy. The partition function $Z(\beta)$ in statistical mechanics is the same object as the MGF of the energy.
