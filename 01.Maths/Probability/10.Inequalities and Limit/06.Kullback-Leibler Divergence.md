 

> _KL divergence is the central object of information theory, and once you see it clearly, you realize it was already hiding inside everything you knew. The non-negativity of KL is Jensen applied to $-\log$. Entropy is KL to the uniform. Mutual information is KL to the independence model. The maximum likelihood estimator minimizes KL to the true distribution. The exponential family is the solution to a KL projection. Fisher information is the second derivative of KL at zero. Every time I think I've found the boundary of where KL appears, it turns out to be further out. This note is about understanding KL as a geometric object — a way of measuring distance between distributions — and tracing all the threads that lead out from it._

## 1. Definition

### 1.1 Discrete case

Let $p$ and $q$ be PMFs on the same finite or countable support $\mathcal{X}$. The **Kullback-Leibler divergence** (KL divergence, relative entropy) of $p$ from $q$ is:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p | q) = \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} p(x) \log \frac{p(x)}{q(x)} $$

with the conventions:

- $0 \log(0/0) = 0$
- $0 \log(0/q(x)) = 0$ for $q(x) > 0$
- $p(x)\log(p(x)/0) = +\infty$ if $p(x) > 0$ — so $D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) = +\infty$ if $p$ is not absolutely continuous with respect to $q$

### 1.2 Continuous case

For PDFs $f, g$ on $\mathbb{R}$:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(f | g) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty f(x)\log\frac{f(x)}{g(x)},dx $$

Same conventions apply. Requires $f \ll g$ (absolute continuity: $g(x) = 0 \Rightarrow f(x) = 0$).

### 1.3 The expectation form

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p | q) = \mathbb{E}_p!\left[\log\frac{p(X)}{q(X)}\right] = \mathbb{E}_p[-\log q(X)] - \mathbb{E}_p[-\log p(X)] = H(p,q) - H(p) $$

where $H(p,q) = \mathbb{E}_p[-\log q(X)]$ is the cross-entropy. So:

$$ \boxed{D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) = H(p,q) - H(p)} $$

> _KL divergence is the excess surprise you experience when you use $q$ to encode data that actually comes from $p$. If you use the wrong distribution $q$, you pay $H(p,q)$ bits per symbol on average. The true minimum cost is $H(p)$. The excess is $D_{\text{KL}}(p|q)$. This is why KL appears in coding theory, and why minimizing KL is the right objective for learning._

## 2. Non-Negativity — The Fundamental Theorem

### 2.1 Statement

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p | q) \geq 0 $$

with equality iff $p = q$ (i.e., $p(x) = q(x)$ for all $x$ with $p(x) > 0$).

### 2.2 Proof via Jensen

Let $X \sim p$. Consider the random variable $Z = q(X)/p(X)$ (the likelihood ratio viewed under $p$). Apply Jensen to $g(t) = -\log t$ (convex on $t > 0$):

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) = \mathbb{E}_p!\left[-\log\frac{q(X)}{p(X)}\right] = \mathbb{E}_p[-\log Z] \geq -\log\mathbb{E}_p[Z] $$

Now compute $\mathbb{E}_p[Z]$:

$$ \mathbb{E}_p[Z] = \mathbb{E}_p!\left[\frac{q(X)}{p(X)}\right] = \sum_x p(x)\cdot\frac{q(x)}{p(x)} = \sum_x q(x) = 1 $$

Therefore:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) \geq -\log 1 = 0 \qquad \square $$

**Equality** iff $Z = q(X)/p(X)$ is constant $p$-a.s. Since $\mathbb{E}_p[Z] = 1$, that constant must be 1, i.e., $q(x) = p(x)$ for all $x$ in the support of $p$.

> _Three lines. The key identity is $\mathbb{E}_p[q(X)/p(X)] = 1$ — this is always true because it is just $\sum_x q(x) = 1$ rewritten. The quantity $q(X)/p(X)$ is the likelihood ratio (Radon-Nikodym derivative of $q$ w.r.t. $p$), and its expectation under $p$ is always 1. This normalization identity is doing all the work; Jensen gives the direction._

### 2.3 Alternative: the log-sum inequality

For $a_x, b_x > 0$:

$$ \sum_x a_x \log\frac{a_x}{b_x} \geq \left(\sum_x a_x\right)\log\frac{\sum_x a_x}{\sum_x b_x} $$

Setting $a_x = p(x)$, $b_x = q(x)$: $\sum p(x) = \sum q(x) = 1$, so the RHS is $0$. This gives $D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0$ directly. The log-sum inequality itself follows from convexity of $t\log t$.

## 3. KL is Not a Metric

### 3.1 Asymmetry

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) \neq D_{\text{KL}}(q|p) \quad \text{in general} $$

Example: $p = \text{Bern}(0.01)$, $q = \text{Bern}(0.5)$:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) \approx 0.645 \text{ nats}, \qquad D_{\text{KL}}(q|p) \approx 1.972 \text{ nats} $$

$D_{\text{KL}}(q|p)$ is larger because $q$ puts substantial mass ($0.5$) where $p$ has almost none ($0.01$) — the term $0.5\log(0.5/0.01)$ is large.

### 3.2 The meaning of direction

**Forward KL, $D_{\text{KL}}(p|q)$:** $p$ is truth, $q$ is approximation. Penalizes $q$ for having zero mass where $p$ has mass. Forces $q$ to cover all of $p$'s support. Minimizing forward KL gives **mean-seeking** behavior — $q$ spreads to cover all modes.

**Reverse KL, $D_{\text{KL}}(q|p)$:** Penalizes $q$ for putting mass where $p$ has none. Forces $q$ to stay inside $p$'s support. Minimizing reverse KL gives **mode-seeking** behavior — $q$ concentrates on one mode.

> _This distinction is not academic. Variational inference minimizes $D_{\text{KL}}(q|p_{\text{posterior}})$ (reverse KL) because the forward KL requires integrating against the intractable posterior. The consequence: variational posteriors underestimate uncertainty and collapse to modes. Understanding why this happens is understanding the geometry of KL._

### 3.3 A symmetric alternative

The **Jensen-Shannon divergence**:

$$ \text{JSD}(p, q) = \frac{1}{2}D_{\text{KL}}!\left(p,\Big|,\frac{p+q}{2}\right) + \frac{1}{2}D_{\text{KL}}!\left(q,\Big|,\frac{p+q}{2}\right) $$

is symmetric, bounded in $[0, \log 2]$, and $\sqrt{\text{JSD}}$ is a true metric. It appears in the theory of GANs (the original GAN objective is equivalent to minimizing JSD between the data distribution and the generator).

## 4. KL and All the Entropy Quantities

### 4.1 The full dictionary

$$ H(p) = \log|\mathcal{X}| - D_{\text{KL}}(p|u) \quad (u \text{ uniform}) $$

$$ H(p,q) = H(p) + D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) \quad \text{(cross-entropy)} $$

$$ I(X;Y) = D_{\text{KL}}(p_{XY} | p_X \otimes p_Y) \quad \text{(mutual information)} $$

Every information-theoretic quantity is a KL divergence or differs from one by a constant that doesn't depend on what you're optimizing.

### 4.2 Non-negativity cascade

$$ D_{\text{KL}} \geq 0 \implies I(X;Y) \geq 0 \implies H(Y\mid X) \leq H(Y) \implies H(X) \leq \log|\mathcal{X}| $$

One inequality (Jensen on $-\log$), four consequences in a chain.

## 5. KL and Maximum Likelihood

### 5.1 MLE minimizes KL

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} p^*$ (true, unknown). Fit parametric model ${q_\theta}$ by MLE:

$$ \hat\theta = \arg\max_\theta \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n \log q_\theta(X_i) \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} \arg\max_\theta \mathbb{E}_{p^*}[\log q_\theta(X)] $$

Now:

$$ \mathbb{E}_{p^*}[\log q_\theta(X)] = \underbrace{-H(p^_)}_{\text{const in }\theta} - D_{\text{KL}}(p^_|q_\theta) $$

So:

$$ \hat\theta \xrightarrow{n\to\infty} \arg\min_\theta D_{\text{KL}}(p^*|q_\theta) $$

**MLE is the KL projection of the true distribution onto the model family.**

> _This is why MLE is the right thing to do. It's not a heuristic — it is the procedure that finds the closest distribution to the truth (in the forward KL sense) within your model. If $p^_ \in {q_\theta}$, KL is zero at the optimum and MLE recovers $p^_$. If not, MLE finds the best approximation the model can offer._

### 5.2 Moment matching in exponential families

If ${q_\theta}$ is an exponential family with sufficient statistic $T(x)$:

$$ q_\theta(x) \propto \exp(\theta^\top T(x)) $$

then the KL projection condition $\nabla_\theta D_{\text{KL}}(p^*|q_\theta) = 0$ reduces to:

$$ \mathbb{E}_{q_\theta}[T(X)] = \mathbb{E}_{p^*}[T(X)] $$

The MLE matches the model's expected sufficient statistics to the empirical ones. This is the general form of moment matching, and it's why MLE in exponential families always reduces to matching moments.

## 6. Fisher Information as the Curvature of KL

### 6.1 The second-order expansion

For a smooth parametric family ${p_\theta}$, expand KL between nearby parameters:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p_\theta | p_{\theta + \epsilon}) = \frac{\epsilon^2}{2},\mathcal{I}(\theta) + O(\epsilon^3) $$

where the **Fisher information** is:

$$ \mathcal{I}(\theta) = \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}!\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta}\log p_\theta(X)\right)^2\right] = -\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}!\left[\frac{\partial^2}{\partial\theta^2}\log p_\theta(X)\right] $$

**Derivation sketch.** Let $\ell_\theta(x) = \log p_\theta(x)$. Expand:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p_\theta|p_{\theta+\epsilon}) = \mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\ell_\theta(X) - \ell_{\theta+\epsilon}(X)] \approx -\epsilon,\mathbb{E}[\ell'_\theta] - \frac{\epsilon^2}{2}\mathbb{E}[\ell''_\theta] $$

The first term: $\mathbb{E}_{p_\theta}[\ell'_\theta(X)] = \partial_\theta \sum_x p_\theta(x) = \partial_\theta 1 = 0$ (score function has mean zero). The second term gives $\frac{\epsilon^2}{2}\mathcal{I}(\theta)$.

> _Fisher information is the local curvature of KL divergence. It measures how fast distributions separate as you move in parameter space. Large $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ means parameters are easy to distinguish (nearby distributions are far in KL); small $\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ means they're hard to distinguish. The Cramér-Rao bound $\text{Var}(\hat\theta) \geq 1/\mathcal{I}(\theta)$ then says: the harder distributions are to distinguish, the harder the parameter is to estimate. This is the right intuition._

## 7. The Data-Processing Inequality

**Theorem.** If $X \to Y \to Z$ is a Markov chain, then:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p_X | q_X) \geq D_{\text{KL}}(p_Y | q_Y) \geq D_{\text{KL}}(p_Z | q_Z) $$

Processing data can only decrease KL divergence — information cannot be created.

**For mutual information:** In the Markov chain $X \to Y \to Z$:

$$ I(X;Y) \geq I(X;Z) $$

Any function of $Y$ contains at most as much information about $X$ as $Y$ itself.

> _Sufficient statistics are exactly those for which equality holds: $T(X)$ is sufficient for $\theta$ iff $I(\theta; T(X)) = I(\theta; X)$, i.e., no information about $\theta$ is lost by passing from $X$ to $T(X)$. The data-processing inequality makes "sufficiency" into an information-theoretic statement, not just a conditional independence statement._

## 8. Worked Examples

### 8.1 Two Bernoullis

$p = \text{Bern}(p_0)$, $q = \text{Bern}(q_0)$:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(\text{Bern}(p_0)|\text{Bern}(q_0)) = p_0\log\frac{p_0}{q_0} + (1-p_0)\log\frac{1-p_0}{1-q_0} $$

Check: equals 0 when $p_0 = q_0$ ✓. Blows up as $q_0 \to 0$ with $p_0 > 0$ ✓.

### 8.2 Two Gaussians

$p = N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$, $q = N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) = \log\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1} + \frac{\sigma_1^2 + (\mu_1-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2} - \frac{1}{2} $$

Equal variances $\sigma_1 = \sigma_2 = \sigma$:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(p|q) = \frac{(\mu_1-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma^2} $$

This is the squared Mahalanobis distance. KL between equal-variance Gaussians is proportional to squared mean difference — KL is literally a distance here.

**Derivation.** Write $\log(p/q) = \log(\sigma_2/\sigma_1) - \frac{(x-\mu_1)^2}{2\sigma_1^2} + \frac{(x-\mu_2)^2}{2\sigma_2^2}$. Take $\mathbb{E}_p$, use $\mathbb{E}_p[X] = \mu_1$, $\mathbb{E}_p[X^2] = \sigma_1^2 + \mu_1^2$.

### 8.3 Two Poissons

$p = \text{Pois}(\lambda)$, $q = \text{Pois}(\mu)$:

$$ D_{\text{KL}}(\text{Pois}(\lambda)|\text{Pois}(\mu)) = \lambda\log\frac{\lambda}{\mu} - \lambda + \mu $$

Check: at $\lambda = \mu$: $\lambda\log 1 - \lambda + \lambda = 0$ ✓.

## 11. Open Questions / Things to Sit With

- **Sanov's theorem:** The probability that the empirical distribution of $n$ iid samples falls in a set $\mathcal{Q}$ decays like $\exp(-n \cdot \inf_{q\in\mathcal{Q}} D_{\text{KL}}(q|p))$. KL is the rate function of large deviations for empirical measures. This is the deepest appearance of KL — not just a measure of closeness, but the exponential rate of distinguishability. When I study large deviations, this is the first theorem.
- **Information geometry:** The parameter space of a smooth model carries a Riemannian metric given by the Fisher information matrix. KL is the squared "distance" in this geometry to second order. Geodesics split into $e$-geodesics and $m$-geodesics (exponential and mixture), and a Pythagorean theorem holds for KL projections. Amari's work. Far future, but the seed is in §6.
- **$f$-divergences:** KL is $D_f$ with $f(t) = t\log t$. The family $D_f(p|q) = \sum_x q(x)f(p(x)/q(x))$ includes total variation ($f(t) = |t-1|/2$), $\chi^2$ ($f(t) = (t-1)^2$), Hellinger. All non-negative by convexity of $f$ (Jensen again). The Csiszár-Morimoto theorem unifies them.
- **GANs and JSD:** The original GAN minimax objective is equivalent to minimizing JSD between the data distribution and the generator. Later GANs (Wasserstein GAN) replace JSD with the Wasserstein distance, which has better gradient properties. The evolution of GAN objectives is a story about which divergence to minimize and why.
