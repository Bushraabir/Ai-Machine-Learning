## 1. Setup and the Naive Error

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim}$ some distribution with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2 < \infty$.

Define:

$$ S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X})^2 $$

**Known fact:** $\mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2$. So $S^2$ is unbiased for $\sigma^2$.

**The naive hope:** Since $\mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2$, maybe $\mathbb{E}[S] = \sigma$? After all, $S = \sqrt{S^2}$, and $\sqrt{\sigma^2} = \sigma$. So "just take square roots of both sides of $\mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2$."

**Why this fails:** Expectation is not a ring homomorphism. You cannot pass nonlinear functions through $\mathbb{E}$. Specifically:

$$ \mathbb{E}[\sqrt{S^2}] \neq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[S^2]} $$

in general. The function $g(x) = \sqrt{x}$ is **concave** on $[0,\infty)$. By Jensen:

$$ \mathbb{E}[\sqrt{S^2}] \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[S^2]} = \sqrt{\sigma^2} = \sigma $$

$$ \therefore \quad \mathbb{E}[S] \leq \sigma $$

with equality iff $S^2$ is constant a.s., i.e., $\text{Var}(S^2) = 0$. For any non-degenerate distribution and $n < \infty$, $\text{Var}(S^2) > 0$, so:

$$ \boxed{\mathbb{E}[S] < \sigma} $$

> _The sample standard deviation $S$ always underestimates $\sigma$. Not sometimes, not approximately — always, for any non-degenerate population and any finite $n$. Jensen is doing all the work, and concavity of $\sqrt{\cdot}$ is the villain._

---

## 2. Why $S^2$ is Unbiased — The Proof

Before going further, I should have the unbiasedness of $S^2$ cold, because it motivates the whole setup.

**Theorem.** $\mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2$.

**Proof.** Write $S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar{X})^2$. The key identity:

$$ \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X})^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - n\bar{X}^2 $$

Take expectation:

$$ \mathbb{E}!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2\right] = n(\sigma^2 + \mu^2) \quad \text{(since } \mathbb{E}[X_i^2] = \text{Var}(X_i) + (\mathbb{E}[X_i])^2 = \sigma^2 + \mu^2\text{)} $$

$$ \mathbb{E}[n\bar{X}^2] = n,\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}^2] = n!\left(\text{Var}(\bar{X}) + (\mathbb{E}[\bar{X}])^2\right) = n!\left(\frac{\sigma^2}{n} + \mu^2\right) = \sigma^2 + n\mu^2 $$

Subtracting:

$$ \mathbb{E}!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i-\bar{X})^2\right] = n(\sigma^2+\mu^2) - (\sigma^2 + n\mu^2) = (n-1)\sigma^2 $$

Dividing by $n-1$: $\mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2$. $\square$

> _The $n-1$ in the denominator is exactly what corrects for the fact that $\bar{X}$ is estimated from the same data — the deviations $(X_i - \bar{X})$ have only $n-1$ degrees of freedom, not $n$. Dividing by $n$ would give a biased-low estimator of $\sigma^2$._

---

## 3. The Exact Bias for Normal Data

For general distributions, Jensen gives us the sign of the bias ($\mathbb{E}[S] < \sigma$) but not its magnitude. For normal data, we can compute it exactly.

### 3.1 The chi-squared connection

Let $X_1,\ldots,X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(\mu,\sigma^2)$. Then:

$$ \frac{(n-1)S^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1} $$

This is a theorem — proof uses the fact that $(X_i - \bar{X})/\sigma$ are jointly normal with a covariance matrix of rank $n-1$, and a sum of squares of independent standard normals is chi-squared. Take it as given for now.

So:

$$ S^2 = \frac{\sigma^2}{n-1} W, \quad W \sim \chi^2_{n-1} $$

$$ S = \sigma\sqrt{\frac{W}{n-1}} $$

$$ \mathbb{E}[S] = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n-1}},\mathbb{E}[\sqrt{W}] $$

### 3.2 Computing $\mathbb{E}[\sqrt{W}]$ for $W \sim \chi^2_k$

The chi-squared distribution with $k$ degrees of freedom is $\text{Gamma}(k/2, 1/2)$ (shape-rate parametrization), so its PDF is:

$$ f_W(w) = \frac{w^{k/2-1}e^{-w/2}}{2^{k/2}\Gamma(k/2)}, \quad w > 0 $$

$$ \mathbb{E}[\sqrt{W}] = \int_0^\infty w^{1/2} \cdot \frac{w^{k/2-1}e^{-w/2}}{2^{k/2}\Gamma(k/2)},dw = \frac{1}{2^{k/2}\Gamma(k/2)}\int_0^\infty w^{(k+1)/2 - 1}e^{-w/2},dw $$

Recognize this as a Gamma integral. Substitute $u = w/2$:

$$ = \frac{1}{2^{k/2}\Gamma(k/2)}\cdot 2^{(k+1)/2}\Gamma!\left(\frac{k+1}{2}\right) = \frac{\sqrt{2},\Gamma!\left(\frac{k+1}{2}\right)}{\Gamma(k/2)} $$

### 3.3 The result

With $k = n-1$:

$$ \mathbb{E}[S] = \frac{\sigma}{\sqrt{n-1}}\cdot\frac{\sqrt{2},\Gamma(n/2)}{\Gamma!\left(\frac{n-1}{2}\right)} = \sigma \cdot \underbrace{\sqrt{\frac{2}{n-1}},\frac{\Gamma(n/2)}{\Gamma!\left(\frac{n-1}{2}\right)}}_{=:,c_n} $$

The constant $c_n < 1$ (by Jensen), and:

$$ \boxed{\mathbb{E}[S] = c_n \sigma, \qquad c_n = \sqrt{\frac{2}{n-1}},\frac{\Gamma(n/2)}{\Gamma!\left(\frac{n-1}{2}\right)}} $$

The **bias** is:

$$ \text{Bias}(S) = \mathbb{E}[S] - \sigma = (c_n - 1)\sigma < 0 $$

### 3.4 Numerical values of $c_n$

|$n$|$c_n$|$1 - c_n$ (relative bias)|
|---|---|---|
|2|$\sqrt{2/\pi} \approx 0.7979$|$\approx 20.2%$|
|5|$\approx 0.9400$|$\approx 6.0%$|
|10|$\approx 0.9727$|$\approx 2.7%$|
|25|$\approx 0.9896$|$\approx 1.0%$|
|100|$\approx 0.9975$|$\approx 0.25%$|

> _For $n=2$: the sample standard deviation underestimates $\sigma$ by about 20% on average. For $n=10$ it's still almost 3%. The bias vanishes as $n\to\infty$, but it's non-trivial for small samples. This matters in practice — small-sample inference with $S$ is not as clean as textbooks make it seem._

### 3.5 The unbiased estimator

If we want an exactly unbiased estimator of $\sigma$ (under normality), we use:

$$ \hat\sigma = \frac{S}{c_n} $$

This is called the **corrected sample standard deviation**. In practice, $c_n \approx 1$ for moderate $n$, so $S$ is used anyway — but the correction exists, and some software applies it.

## 4. Large $n$ Asymptotics: The Delta Method Perspective

For large $n$, the bias is approximately:

$$ \mathbb{E}[S] \approx \sigma!\left(1 - \frac{1}{4(n-1)}\right) $$

**Derivation via the delta method.** We have $\mathbb{E}[S^2] = \sigma^2$ and $\text{Var}(S^2) = \frac{2\sigma^4}{n-1}$ (for normal data; depends on the fourth cumulant in general).

Let $g(x) = \sqrt{x}$, so $g'(x) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{x}}$, $g''(x) = -\frac{1}{4x^{3/2}}$. By a second-order Taylor expansion around $\mu_{S^2} = \sigma^2$:

$$ \mathbb{E}[g(S^2)] \approx g(\sigma^2) + \frac{1}{2}g''(\sigma^2),\text{Var}(S^2) = \sigma + \frac{1}{2}\cdot\left(-\frac{1}{4\sigma^3}\right)\cdot\frac{2\sigma^4}{n-1} = \sigma - \frac{\sigma}{4(n-1)} $$

So $\text{Bias}(S) \approx -\frac{\sigma}{4(n-1)}$, confirming the bias is $O(1/n)$.

> _The delta method reframes the bias as coming from the curvature of $\sqrt{\cdot}$ at $\sigma^2$. The second derivative $g'' < 0$ (concavity) is what makes the bias negative. Jensen and the delta method are telling the same story — Jensen gives the sign, the delta method gives the rate._

---

## 5. The General Principle

This example is really a lesson about a general phenomenon:

**If $\hat\theta$ is unbiased for $\theta$, and $h$ is a nonlinear function, then $h(\hat\theta)$ is generally biased for $h(\theta)$.** The direction of the bias is determined by the convexity or concavity of $h$:

- $h$ convex: $\mathbb{E}[h(\hat\theta)] \geq h(\theta)$ — biased high
- $h$ concave: $\mathbb{E}[h(\hat\theta)] \leq h(\theta)$ — biased low

The magnitude of the bias is approximately $\frac{1}{2}h''(\theta),\text{Var}(\hat\theta)$ for large $n$.

> _So the sample variance $S^2$ is unbiased for $\sigma^2$. But $S$ is biased low for $\sigma$ (concave transformation). And $S^2$ is biased high for $\sigma^2$... wait, no, that's circular. Let me think again: $\log S$ is biased low for $\log\sigma$ (concave). $e^S$ is biased high for $e^\sigma$ (convex). The principle applies at every level of the composition._
