## 1. Cauchy-Schwarz Inequality

### 1.1 The "real" statement (inner product form)

Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, \mathbb{P})$ be a probability space. For any two r.v.'s $X, Y$ with finite second moments:

$$ |\mathbb{E}[XY]|^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2],\mathbb{E}[Y^2] $$

This is just the inner product inequality $|\langle u, v\rangle|^2 \leq |u|^2 |v|^2$ in the Hilbert space $L^2(\Omega, \mathbb{P})$, where the inner product is $\langle X, Y \rangle := \mathbb{E}[XY]$.

> _Every time I write this I feel like I should acknowledge: this is not a "probability theorem." It's a theorem about inner product spaces that probability inherits for free. That's why it's so powerful — it's not tied to any particular structure of the problem._

---

### 1.2 Proof I actually remember (the discriminant trick)

**Claim:** $(\mathbb{E}[XY])^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2]\mathbb{E}[Y^2]$.

**Proof.** For any $t \in \mathbb{R}$, consider:

$$ 0 \leq \mathbb{E}[(tX - Y)^2] = t^2,\mathbb{E}[X^2] - 2t,\mathbb{E}[XY] + \mathbb{E}[Y^2] $$

This is a quadratic in $t$ that is always $\geq 0$. For a quadratic $at^2 + bt + c \geq 0$ for all $t$, we need the discriminant to be non-positive:

$$ b^2 - 4ac \leq 0 \implies 4(\mathbb{E}[XY])^2 - 4,\mathbb{E}[X^2],\mathbb{E}[Y^2] \leq 0 $$

$$ \boxed{(\mathbb{E}[XY])^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2],\mathbb{E}[Y^2]} $$

**Equality** holds iff $\mathbb{E}[(tX-Y)^2] = 0$ for some $t$, i.e., $tX = Y$ almost surely — i.e., $X$ and $Y$ are a.s. linear multiples of each other. $\square$

---

### 1.3 The correlation form (what I use most)

Taking $X \leftarrow X - \mathbb{E}[X]$ and $Y \leftarrow Y - \mathbb{E}[Y]$, Cauchy-Schwarz immediately gives:

$$ |\text{Cov}(X,Y)|^2 \leq \text{Var}(X),\text{Var}(Y) $$

which means $|\text{Corr}(X,Y)| \leq 1$. This is the proof that correlation lives in $[-1,1]$. It's not obvious without C-S.

---

### 1.4 A useful rearrangement: bounding $\mathbb{E}[X]^2$ from below

Set $Y = 1$ (the constant r.v.). Then:

$$ (\mathbb{E}[X \cdot 1])^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2],\mathbb{E}[1^2] \implies \mathbb{E}[X]^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2] $$

So Cauchy-Schwarz gives Jensen ($f(x) = x^2$ is convex) as a special case. Or flipped:

$$ \mathbb{E}[X]^2 \leq \mathbb{E}[X^2] $$

> _This is the key inequality I need for the Second Moment Method. Write it on your hand._

---

### 1.5 Another form: $\mathbb{E}[X]\geq \frac{(\mathbb{E}[X])^2}{\mathbb{E}[X^2]}$ for $X \geq 0$

If $X \geq 0$, write $X = X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{{X > 0}}$ and apply C-S with $Y = \mathbf{1}_{{X > 0}}$:

$$ \mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{{X>0}}] \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]},\sqrt{\mathbb{P}(X>0)} $$

Squaring and rearranging:

$$ \boxed{\mathbb{P}(X > 0) \geq \frac{(\mathbb{E}[X])^2}{\mathbb{E}[X^2]}} $$

> _This is the Second Moment Method lower bound. The whole method lives in this one line. I should tattoo this._

## 2. The Second Moment Method

### 2.1 What is it, philosophically?

The **First Moment Method** says: $\mathbb{P}(X > 0) \leq \mathbb{E}[X]$ (Markov). It gives upper bounds on the probability that something exists.

The **Second Moment Method** gives _lower bounds_: it says that if $\mathbb{E}[X]$ is big AND $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ is not too much bigger than $(\mathbb{E}[X])^2$, then $X > 0$ with positive probability. In words: the count has enough "signal" relative to its "noise" to guarantee existence.

> _The ratio $\mathbb{E}[X^2]/(\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ measures the "spread" of $X$ relative to its mean. If this ratio is bounded, you're in business._

---

### 2.2 The Paley-Zygmund inequality (the full version)

**Theorem (Paley-Zygmund).** For $X \geq 0$ with $\mathbb{E}[X^2] < \infty$ and $0 < \theta < 1$:

$$ \mathbb{P}(X \geq \theta,\mathbb{E}[X]) \geq (1-\theta)^2,\frac{(\mathbb{E}[X])^2}{\mathbb{E}[X^2]} $$

**Proof.** Let $\mu = \mathbb{E}[X]$. Write:

$$ \mu = \mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X,\mathbf{1}_{{X < \theta\mu}}] + \mathbb{E}[X,\mathbf{1}_{{X \geq \theta\mu}}] $$

The first term is at most $\theta\mu \cdot 1 = \theta\mu$, so:

$$ \mathbb{E}[X,\mathbf{1}_{{X \geq \theta\mu}}] \geq (1-\theta)\mu $$

Now apply Cauchy-Schwarz to the left side:

$$ (1-\theta)\mu \leq \mathbb{E}[X,\mathbf{1}_{{X \geq \theta\mu}}] \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]},\sqrt{\mathbb{P}(X \geq \theta\mu)} $$

Square both sides and rearrange:

$$ \mathbb{P}(X \geq \theta\mu) \geq \frac{(1-\theta)^2\mu^2}{\mathbb{E}[X^2]} = (1-\theta)^2,\frac{(\mathbb{E}[X])^2}{\mathbb{E}[X^2]} \qquad \square $$

The $\theta \to 0$ case recovers our earlier bound $\mathbb{P}(X > 0) \geq (\mathbb{E}[X])^2/\mathbb{E}[X^2]$.

---

### 2.3 The standard setup (indicator variables)

The method is most powerful when $X = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ counts the number of events that occur.

**Computing $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$:**

$$ \mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}!\left[\left(\sum_i \mathbf{1}_{A_i}\right)^2\right] = \sum_i\sum_j \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{A_i}\mathbf{1}_{A_j}] = \sum_i\sum_j \mathbb{P}(A_i \cap A_j) $$

Split the diagonal from the off-diagonal:

$$ \mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \sum_{i \neq j} \mathbb{P}(A_i \cap A_j) $$

> _The diagonal terms $\mathbb{P}(A_i \cap A_i) = \mathbb{P}(A_i) = \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{A_i}]$, so summing them gives $\mathbb{E}[X]$. The off-diagonal sum is what I need to control._

**The ratio to control:**

$$ \frac{\mathbb{E}[X^2]}{(\mathbb{E}[X])^2} = \frac{\mathbb{E}[X] + \sum_{i\neq j}\mathbb{P}(A_i \cap A_j)}{(\mathbb{E}[X])^2} $$

The method works when this ratio stays bounded (ideally $\to 1$) as $n \to \infty$.

---

### 2.4 Canonical example: Birthday matching

> _This is the Stat 110 example that clicked everything for me._

$n$ people, $365$ days. Let $A_{ij}$ = event that persons $i$ and $j$ share a birthday. Let $X = \sum_{i < j}\mathbf{1}_{A_{ij}}$ count the number of matching pairs.

$$ \mathbb{E}[X] = \binom{n}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{365} \approx \frac{n^2}{730} $$

**First moment method (upper bound on no match):** $\mathbb{P}(\text{no match}) \leq 1$ trivially. More useful: by Markov, $\mathbb{P}(X = 0) \leq 1$ — useless. The first moment doesn't give us the birthday problem. We need a lower bound on $\mathbb{P}(X > 0)$.

**Second moment method:**

$$ \mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \sum_{(i,j)\neq(k,l)} \mathbb{P}(A_{ij} \cap A_{kl}) $$

Two cases for the pair of pairs: share one person, or disjoint.

- _Disjoint pairs_ (4 distinct people): $\mathbb{P}(A_{ij}\cap A_{kl}) = 1/365^2$
- _One shared person_ (3 distinct people, e.g., $i=k$): $\mathbb{P}(A_{ij}\cap A_{il}) = 1/365^2$ (all three share $i$'s birthday, probability $1/365^2$... wait, no. $A_{ij}$: $j$ matches $i$; $A_{il}$: $l$ matches $i$. Both require matching $i$'s birthday: $\mathbb{P} = (1/365)^2$. Same.)

So $\mathbb{P}(A_{ij}\cap A_{kl}) = 1/365^2$ in all cases. Thus:

$$ \mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \binom{n}{2}^2 \cdot \frac{1}{365^2} + O(n^3 \cdot \frac{1}{365^2}) $$

More carefully: $(\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \approx n^4/(730)^2$ and $\mathbb{E}[X^2]/(\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \to 1$ when $\mathbb{E}[X] \to \infty$. So when $n \gg \sqrt{365}$, $\mathbb{P}(X > 0) \to 1$. ✓

---

### 2.5 Warning: when the method fails

The second moment method fails when $\mathbb{E}[X^2] \gg (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$, i.e., when the events $A_i$ are highly _positively correlated_. In that case $X$ is "lumpy" — it's either very large or zero — and a large mean doesn't guarantee $X > 0$.

> _Classic failure case: $X$ is a single indicator, say $\mathbf{1}_A$ with $\mathbb{P}(A) = p$ small. Then $\mathbb{E}[X] = p$, $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = p$, so the bound gives $\mathbb{P}(X > 0) \geq p^2/p = p$. Which is exact! But if $p$ is small that lower bound is useless. The method doesn't fail — it's just tight._

The method _really_ fails when you have a few dominant terms that contribute most of $\mathbb{E}[X]$ but are correlated. In that case, break the sum up and handle dominant/non-dominant terms separately.

## 3. Connection Between the Two

Here is the line I want to remember:

> **Cauchy-Schwarz is the engine of the Second Moment Method.**

Specifically, the key inequality $\mathbb{P}(X > 0) \geq (\mathbb{E}[X])^2/\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ is a direct consequence of applying Cauchy-Schwarz with $Y = \mathbf{1}_{{X > 0}}$. The whole method is one application of one inequality.

The reason we compute $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ and not something else is because C-S naturally pairs $X$ with the $L^2$ norm $\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]}$. The "second moment" in the name is not a coincidence — it's $|X|_{L^2}^2$.
