## The Big Picture (before any formulas)

I keep thinking of this theorem as the _bridge_. On one side you have the abstract, measure-theoretic definition of probability — a number $P(A) \in [0,1]$ attached to an event. On the other side you have the real world: you flip a coin a thousand times and observe a frequency. The LLN is the thing that justifies why those two sides correspond.

Without LLN, saying "a fair coin has probability 1/2 of heads" is just a mathematical assertion with no empirical content. LLN says: run the experiment enough times, and the observed frequency _will_ converge to that number. That's not obvious — it's a theorem.

There are two versions, and they are genuinely different animals. I need to keep them straight.

## Setup

Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be **i.i.d.** random variables with mean $\mu = E[X_i]$ (and for the strong version, we'll need this to exist finitely).

Define the **sample mean**: $$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$

The question is: in what sense does $\bar{X}_n \to \mu$?

---

## Weak Law of Large Numbers (WLLN)

### Statement

$$\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} \mu \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

i.e., for every $\varepsilon > 0$: $$P!\left(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon\right) \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

This is **convergence in probability**.

### Proof (assuming finite variance)

Assume $\text{Var}(X_i) = \sigma^2 < \infty$. This is a stronger assumption than strictly necessary, but the proof is clean and worth knowing cold.

**Step 1: Compute the mean and variance of $\bar{X}_n$.**

$$E[\bar{X}_n] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i] = \mu$$

$$\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = \frac{\sigma^2}{n}$$

(Independence is used here — variance of a sum of independent r.v.'s is the sum of variances.)

**Step 2: Apply Chebyshev's inequality.**

For any $\varepsilon > 0$: $$P!\left(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon\right) \leq \frac{\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n)}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2}$$

**Step 3: Take the limit.**

$$\frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \to 0 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

Therefore $P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon) \to 0$. $\blacksquare$

> _Personal note: this proof is genuinely beautiful in its economy. Chebyshev does all the heavy lifting, and variance shrinking like $1/n$ is the whole game. The proof is so short I sometimes distrust it — but it's airtight._

### What if $\sigma^2 = \infty$?

The WLLN still holds under weaker conditions — just finite mean is enough. But the proof then requires the **characteristic function** (Fourier transform of the distribution) rather than Chebyshev. The key identity is:

$$E[e^{it\bar{X}_n}] = \left(\varphi_X!\left(\frac{t}{n}\right)\right)^n$$

and a Taylor expansion around $t=0$ shows this converges to $e^{i\mu t}$, the characteristic function of the point mass at $\mu$. I'll write that up separately when I cover characteristic functions properly.

## Strong Law of Large Numbers (SLLN)

### Statement

$$\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{a.s.} \mu \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

i.e.: $$P!\left(\lim_{n\to\infty} \bar{X}_n = \mu\right) = 1$$

This is **almost sure convergence** (= convergence with probability 1).

### Why this is genuinely stronger

The WLLN says: for any fixed $\varepsilon$, the _probability of being far from $\mu$_ goes to zero. It does **not** say that a single infinite sequence of trials will settle down.

More precisely: convergence in probability does **not** imply almost sure convergence. A classic counterexample is the "typewriter sequence" — a sequence of r.v.'s that converge in probability to 0 but oscillate forever almost surely.

The SLLN says the stronger thing: with probability 1, the actual infinite sequence $\bar{X}_1(\omega), \bar{X}_2(\omega), \bar{X}_3(\omega), \ldots$ converges to $\mu$. This is a statement about _paths_, not about probabilities of events at individual times.

$$\underbrace{P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon) \to 0}_{\text{WLLN}} \quad \text{vs.} \quad \underbrace{P!\left(\lim_{n\to\infty}\bar{X}_n = \mu\right) = 1}_{\text{SLLN}}$$

### Proof sketch (the $L^4$ moment approach — cleanest elementary proof)

A clean proof under the stronger assumption $E[X_i^4] < \infty$ uses the **fourth moment of $\bar{X}_n - \mu$** and **Borel-Cantelli**.

**Step 1:** Let $Y_i = X_i - \mu$ (center the r.v.'s). So $E[Y_i] = 0$, $E[Y_i^4] = \mu_4 < \infty$.

**Step 2:** Expand $\left(\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i\right)^4$ and take expectations. By independence, cross terms with an unpaired index vanish:

$$E!\left[\left(\sum_{i=1}^n Y_i\right)^4\right] = \sum_i E[Y_i^4] + 3\sum_{i \neq j} E[Y_i^2]E[Y_j^2] = O(n^2)$$

More precisely, this equals $n\mu_4 + 3n(n-1)\sigma^4 \leq Cn^2$ for some constant $C$.

**Step 3:** By Markov's inequality applied to the fourth power:

$$P(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon) = P!\left(\left|\sum Y_i\right|^4 > (n\varepsilon)^4\right) \leq \frac{E[(\sum Y_i)^4]}{n^4\varepsilon^4} \leq \frac{Cn^2}{n^4\varepsilon^4} = \frac{C}{n^2\varepsilon^4}$$

**Step 4:** Since $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{C}{n^2\varepsilon^4} < \infty$, by the **Borel-Cantelli Lemma**, the event ${|\bar{X}_n - \mu| > \varepsilon}$ occurs only finitely often almost surely.

Since this holds for every rational $\varepsilon > 0$, we get $\bar{X}_n \to \mu$ almost surely. $\blacksquare$

> _The full SLLN proof (requiring only $E|X| < \infty$) is significantly harder — it's one of those theorems where I feel like I understand the ideas but the bookkeeping is genuinely painful. The truncation argument (replacing $X_i$ with $X_i \mathbf{1}_{|X_i| \leq i}$) is the key trick. Kolmogorov's inequality replaces Chebyshev in that version._

## The Hierarchy of Convergence Modes

Worth drawing this out for myself:

$$\text{a.s. convergence} \implies \text{convergence in probability} \implies \text{convergence in distribution}$$

None of these arrows reverse in general. The SLLN $\implies$ WLLN direction follows from this hierarchy.

## Applications & Intuition Building

### Monte Carlo integration

If $g$ is a function and I want $\int_0^1 g(x),dx$, I can draw $U_1, \ldots, U_n \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and compute:

$$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n g(U_i) \xrightarrow{a.s.} E[g(U)] = \int_0^1 g(x),dx$$

The LLN is literally why Monte Carlo works. Beautiful.

### Consistent estimators

In statistics, an estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ is **consistent** for $\theta$ if $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta$. The LLN immediately gives us that the sample mean is a consistent estimator for the population mean.

### Law of Averages (the wrong version)

People often say "after 10 heads in a row, a tail is 'due'." This is **not** what LLN says. LLN says the _frequency_ converges — not that future outcomes compensate for past outcomes. Each flip is independent. The coin has no memory. The sequence "HHHHHHHHHH" followed by i.i.d. flips will eventually see frequency $\to 1/2$ not because tails "catch up" in a causal sense, but because 10 heads gets _diluted_ by millions of subsequent flips.

> _This misconception (the Gambler's Fallacy) is worth understanding deeply because it shows up everywhere — roulette, hot streaks in sports, etc._

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> **WLLN:** The probability of the sample mean being far from $\mu$ vanishes. Proved by Chebyshev + variance shrinking like $1/n$.  
> **SLLN:** With probability 1, the sample mean _actually converges_ to $\mu$. Proved by Borel-Cantelli + fourth moment bound. Strictly stronger.
