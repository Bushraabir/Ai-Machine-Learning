> _"The $t$-distribution is what honesty looks like. When you don't know $\sigma$ and have to estimate it, your uncertainty about that estimate inflates the tails. The $t$ quantifies exactly how much inflation."_

---

## The Big Picture

Here is the situation the $t$-distribution was invented to resolve.

You have $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and you want to make inference about $\mu$. If $\sigma$ were known, you'd use the $z$-statistic:

$$Z = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

exactly. But $\sigma$ is never known. You replace it with the sample standard deviation $S$:

$$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}}$$

and now $T$ is _not_ $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. The randomness of $S$ inflates the tails — $T$ is more spread out than a standard normal, and the degree of inflation depends on how well-estimated $S$ is, i.e., on $n$. The $t$-distribution with $n-1$ degrees of freedom captures this exactly.

William Gosset figured this out in 1908 while working at the Guinness brewery. He published under the pseudonym "Student" because Guinness didn't allow employees to publish. Hence: **Student's $t$-distribution**.

## Definition

### The Constructive Definition

If $Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ and $V \sim \chi^2_\nu$ are **independent**, then:

$$T = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{V/\nu}} \sim t_\nu$$

The parameter $\nu$ is the **degrees of freedom**.

This is the definition that reveals what the $t$-distribution _is_: a normal divided by an independent (scaled) chi. The denominator is random, and that randomness is what fattens the tails.

### Why This Is the Right Definition for Inference

In the one-sample setting: $X_1,\ldots,X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu,\sigma^2)$.

- Numerator: $\bar{X} - \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2/n)$, so $Z = (\bar{X}-\mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n}) \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.
- Denominator: by Cochran's theorem, $(n-1)S^2/\sigma^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$, and $S^2 \perp \bar{X}$.

So: $$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu}{S/\sqrt{n}} = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{\frac{(n-1)S^2/\sigma^2}{n-1}}} = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{V/(n-1)}} \sim t_{n-1}$$

The $\sigma^2$ cancels exactly — that's the point. You don't need to know $\sigma$.

> _The independence of $\bar{X}$ and $S^2$ is doing serious work here. Without Cochran's theorem guaranteeing $Z \perp V$, the ratio wouldn't have a $t$-distribution. This is a deeply non-obvious fact — in non-normal populations, $\bar{X}$ and $S^2$ are generally dependent._

## The PDF

**Claim:** $T \sim t_\nu$ has PDF:

$$f_\nu(t) = \frac{\Gamma!\left(\frac{\nu+1}{2}\right)}{\sqrt{\nu\pi};\Gamma!\left(\frac{\nu}{2}\right)} \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2}, \qquad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

**Proof:** Use the constructive definition $T = Z/\sqrt{V/\nu}$ with $Z \perp V$.

Condition on $V = v$. Given $V=v$, we have $T | V=v \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \nu/v)$, so:

$$f_{T|V}(t|v) = \sqrt{\frac{v}{\nu}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp!\left(-\frac{vt^2}{2\nu}\right)$$

The marginal PDF of $T$ is:

$$f_T(t) = \int_0^\infty f_{T|V}(t|v), f_V(v),dv$$

where $f_V(v) = \frac{v^{\nu/2-1}e^{-v/2}}{2^{\nu/2}\Gamma(\nu/2)}$ is the $\chi^2_\nu$ PDF. Substituting:

$$f_T(t) = \int_0^\infty \sqrt{\frac{v}{\nu}} \cdot \frac{e^{-vt^2/(2\nu)}}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \frac{v^{\nu/2-1}e^{-v/2}}{2^{\nu/2}\Gamma(\nu/2)},dv$$

$$= \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\nu},2^{\nu/2}\Gamma(\nu/2)} \int_0^\infty v^{(\nu+1)/2 - 1} \exp!\left(-\frac{v}{2}\left(1 + \frac{t^2}{\nu}\right)\right)dv$$

The integral is a Gamma integral. Let $\lambda = \frac{1}{2}!\left(1 + \frac{t^2}{\nu}\right)$, so the integral $= \Gamma!\left(\frac{\nu+1}{2}\right)/\lambda^{(\nu+1)/2}$:

$$f_T(t) = \frac{\Gamma!\left(\frac{\nu+1}{2}\right)}{\sqrt{2\pi\nu},2^{\nu/2}\Gamma(\nu/2)} \cdot \frac{2^{(\nu+1)/2}}{\left(1 + t^2/\nu\right)^{(\nu+1)/2}}$$

Simplify the powers of 2: $2^{(\nu+1)/2}/2^{\nu/2} = 2^{1/2} = \sqrt{2}$. Then $\sqrt{2}/\sqrt{2\pi} = 1/\sqrt{\pi}$:

$$\boxed{f_\nu(t) = \frac{\Gamma!\left(\frac{\nu+1}{2}\right)}{\sqrt{\nu\pi};\Gamma!\left(\frac{\nu}{2}\right)} \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2}} \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _The key move is conditioning on $V$ and recognizing the resulting integral as a Gamma function. This is a standard trick: when a distribution is defined as a ratio involving a $\chi^2$, condition on the denominator, do the integral, and you get a closed form. The same strategy proves the $F$-distribution PDF._

## The Heavy Tails, Made Precise

The $t_\nu$ PDF has **polynomial tails**: for large $|t|$,

$$f_\nu(t) \sim C_\nu \cdot |t|^{-(\nu+1)}$$

Compare this to the Gaussian, whose tails are $\sim e^{-t^2/2}$ — _exponential_ decay. The $t$-distribution tails decay polynomially, which is why they're called **heavy tails**.

**Consequence for moments:** $E[|T|^k] < \infty$ if and only if $k < \nu$.

- $\nu = 1$ (Cauchy): no finite moments at all — not even $E[|T|]$.
- $\nu = 2$: finite mean ($E[T] = 0$ by symmetry) but infinite variance.
- $\nu \geq 3$: finite variance $= \nu/(\nu-2)$.
- $\nu \geq 5$: finite kurtosis.

For $\nu \geq 2$:

$$E[T] = 0 \qquad \text{(by symmetry, when it exists)}$$

$$\mathrm{Var}(T) = \frac{\nu}{\nu - 2}, \qquad \nu > 2$$

The variance exceeds 1 for all finite $\nu$, reflecting the extra uncertainty from estimating $\sigma$. As $\nu \to \infty$, $\mathrm{Var}(T) \to 1$, consistent with convergence to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

## $t_\nu \to \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ as $\nu \to \infty$

**Claim:** As $\nu \to \infty$, $t_\nu \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$.

**Proof via the constructive definition:**

$T = Z/\sqrt{V/\nu}$ where $V \sim \chi^2_\nu$.

By the LLN applied to $V = \sum_{i=1}^\nu W_i^2$ (i.i.d. $\chi^2_1$ terms):

$$\frac{V}{\nu} = \frac{1}{\nu}\sum_{i=1}^\nu W_i^2 \xrightarrow{a.s.} E[W_1^2] = 1$$

Therefore $\sqrt{V/\nu} \xrightarrow{a.s.} 1$, and by Slutsky's theorem:

$$T = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{V/\nu}} \xrightarrow{d} \frac{Z}{1} = Z \sim \mathcal{N}(0,1) \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Alternatively, via the PDF directly:**

$$\left(1 + \frac{t^2}{\nu}\right)^{-(\nu+1)/2} = \exp!\left(-\frac{\nu+1}{2}\log!\left(1 + \frac{t^2}{\nu}\right)\right) \to \exp!\left(-\frac{t^2}{2}\right)$$

using $\log(1 + x/\nu) \approx x/\nu$ for large $\nu$. And the normalizing constant $\frac{\Gamma((\nu+1)/2)}{\sqrt{\nu\pi},\Gamma(\nu/2)} \to \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}$ by Stirling's approximation. So the PDF converges pointwise to $\phi(t)$.

> _The Slutsky proof is the one I think of first — it's the most conceptually direct. LLN kills the randomness in the denominator as $\nu \to \infty$, and what's left is just $Z$. The heavy tails of $t_\nu$ exist precisely because $V/\nu$ fluctuates when $\nu$ is small._

## Special Cases

### $\nu = 1$: The Cauchy Distribution

$$f_1(t) = \frac{1}{\pi(1 + t^2)}$$

This is the **Cauchy distribution**. It has:

- No finite mean (the integral $\int |t|/(1+t^2),dt$ diverges).
- No finite variance.
- Tails decaying as $t^{-2}$ — the heaviest possible for a $t$-distribution.

The Cauchy is $Z_1/|Z_2|$ for independent standard normals $Z_1, Z_2$, equivalently $\tan(\theta)$ for $\theta \sim \mathrm{Uniform}(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$.

It's the canonical example of a distribution where the LLN _fails_ — the sample mean of i.i.d. Cauchy r.v.'s does not converge to anything, because there is no population mean to converge to.

### $\nu = 2$

$$f_2(t) = \frac{1}{2}\left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2}\right)^{-3/2}$$

Finite mean (zero), infinite variance.

### $\nu \geq 30$ (practical rule of thumb)

At $\nu = 30$, the $t$-distribution is so close to $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$ that the difference is negligible in most applications. But for small $\nu$ — say, $\nu = 5$ or $\nu = 10$ — the tails are meaningfully heavier and using a $z$-table instead of a $t$-table would give anti-conservative confidence intervals.

## No MGF (and why that matters)

The $t_\nu$ distribution has **no moment generating function** for any $\nu$. This is because the polynomial tail decay means $E[e^{tT}] = \infty$ for all $t \neq 0$: the exponential function grows faster than any polynomial tail can suppress.

This is a substantive limitation: MGF-based proofs don't apply to $t$. The characteristic function $\varphi_T(t) = E[e^{itT}]$ does exist (it's bounded), but it doesn't have a clean closed form for general $\nu$.

For $\nu = 1$ (Cauchy): $\varphi(t) = e^{-|t|}$.

> _The absence of an MGF is a direct consequence of the heavy tails. Every moment $E[|T|^k]$ eventually blows up as $k$ exceeds $\nu$, and the MGF — which would require all moments to be finite and well-behaved — can't exist. This is a good example of why MGFs are a tool of convenience, not universality; characteristic functions are always there when MGFs aren't._

## Connection to the $F$-Distribution

$$T^2 \sim F_{1,\nu}$$

**Proof:** $T^2 = Z^2/(V/\nu)$. Now $Z^2 \sim \chi^2_1$ and $V \sim \chi^2_\nu$ independently. So:

$$T^2 = \frac{Z^2/1}{V/\nu} = \frac{\chi^2_1/1}{\chi^2_\nu/\nu} \sim F_{1,\nu}$$

by definition of the $F$-distribution. $\blacksquare$

This is why a two-sided $t$-test at level $\alpha$ is equivalent to an $F$-test at level $\alpha$ — squaring the $t$-statistic gives an $F_{1,\nu}$ statistic, and the rejection regions correspond exactly.

## The $t$-Distribution as a Scale Mixture of Normals

There is a beautiful representation: $T \sim t_\nu$ can be written as:

$$T \mid W \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1/W), \qquad W \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\nu/2,, \nu/2)$$

i.e., $T$ is a **normal distribution with a random variance**, where the precision $W$ follows a Gamma distribution. Marginalizing over $W$ gives the $t$-distribution.

**Why this matters:**

1. It explains the heavy tails intuitively: some draws of $W$ are very small (large variance), making extreme values of $T$ plausible.
2. It connects to **Bayesian statistics**: under a Gaussian likelihood with a Gamma prior on the precision, the marginal distribution of the data is $t$-distributed.
3. It's the basis of robust regression algorithms: by treating each observation's variance as a latent variable drawn from a Gamma, you get $t$-distributed errors, which downweight outliers automatically (the EM algorithm for this is the basis of IRLS with $t$-errors).

The representation also makes the PDF derivation feel less magical: you're just marginalizing a Gaussian with a random variance, and that integral is the Gamma integral that appeared in the proof.

> _This is the representation I keep coming back to. The $t$-distribution isn't just a quirky thing that happens when you don't know $\sigma$ — it's the natural distribution for a Gaussian model where you're uncertain about the scale. That's a deep structural fact with implications throughout Bayesian inference._

## Applications in Classical Statistics

### One-Sample $t$-Test

$H_0: \mu = \mu_0$ vs. $H_1: \mu \neq \mu_0$.

Test statistic: $T = \frac{\bar{X} - \mu_0}{S/\sqrt{n}} \sim t_{n-1}$ under $H_0$.

Reject at level $\alpha$ if $|T| > t_{n-1,,\alpha/2}$ (the upper $\alpha/2$ quantile of $t_{n-1}$).

### Two-Sample $t$-Test (equal variances)

$X_1,\ldots,X_m \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu_1,\sigma^2)$ and $Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu_2,\sigma^2)$, both groups with the same $\sigma^2$.

Pooled variance: $S_p^2 = \frac{(m-1)S_X^2 + (n-1)S_Y^2}{m+n-2} \sim \frac{\sigma^2}{m+n-2}\chi^2_{m+n-2}$

Test statistic: $$T = \frac{\bar{X} - \bar{Y}}{S_p\sqrt{1/m + 1/n}} \sim t_{m+n-2}$$

### Confidence Interval for $\mu$

$$\bar{X} \pm t_{n-1,,\alpha/2} \cdot \frac{S}{\sqrt{n}}$$

As $n \to \infty$, $t_{n-1,\alpha/2} \to z_{\alpha/2}$ and this reduces to the standard $z$-interval.

### Regression Coefficients

In a linear model $\mathbf{Y} = X\boldsymbol{\beta} + \boldsymbol{\varepsilon}$ with $\varepsilon_i \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)$, the OLS estimator satisfies:

$$\frac{\hat{\beta}_j - \beta_j}{\hat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\beta}_j)} \sim t_{n-p}$$

where $p$ is the number of predictors. Every standard error and $p$-value in a regression table comes from this $t$-distribution. Degrees of freedom $= n - p$ because $p$ parameters are estimated, consuming $p$ degrees of freedom from $n$.

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> $t_\nu = \mathcal{N}(0,1)/\sqrt{\chi^2_\nu/\nu}$ (independent). Heavy polynomial tails because the denominator is random; variance $= \nu/(\nu-2)$; no MGF.
