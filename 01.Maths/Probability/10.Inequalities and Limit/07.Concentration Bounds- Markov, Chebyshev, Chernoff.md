

> _The central question: how likely is a random variable to be far from its mean? The three bounds here — Markov, Chebyshev, Chernoff — form a hierarchy. Each uses more information about the distribution and buys you a sharper bound in return. Markov needs only a mean. Chebyshev needs a variance. Chernoff needs the full moment generating function. The cost of ignorance is exponential: Markov and Chebyshev give polynomial decay in the tail; Chernoff gives exponential decay. In repeated trials, exponential is the one that matters._

## 0. The Setup

We have a random variable $X$ (or a sum $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ of i.i.d. trials) and we want to bound:

$$\mathbb{P}(X \geq t) \quad \text{or} \quad \mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| \geq t)$$

for large $t$. The three bounds give progressively sharper answers at progressively higher cost.

|Bound|Input Required|Tail Decay|Tightness|
|---|---|---|---|
|Markov|Mean $\mu$|$O(1/t)$|Weak|
|Chebyshev|Mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$|$O(1/t^2)$|Moderate|
|Chernoff|Full MGF $M(s) = \mathbb{E}[e^{sX}]$|$e^{-\Theta(t)}$|Strong|

---

## 1. Markov's Inequality

### 1.1 Statement

For any **non-negative** random variable $X$ and $a > 0$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(X \geq a) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{a}}$$

### 1.2 Proof

Since $X \geq 0$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}] + \mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X < a}] \geq \mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}] \geq a \cdot \mathbb{P}(X \geq a)$$

Divide both sides by $a$. $\square$

### 1.3 Intuition

If the mean is $\mu$, then the probability of exceeding $k\mu$ is at most $1/k$. You can't have too much mass far above the mean without pulling the mean up with it.

### 1.4 Tightness

Markov is tight: the bound is achieved by a two-point distribution $X = 0$ with prob $1 - \mu/a$, $X = a$ with prob $\mu/a$.

### 1.5 Applying to Functions

Markov applies to any non-negative function of $X$. This is the key trick: for any increasing $\phi \geq 0$:

$$\mathbb{P}(X \geq a) = \mathbb{P}(\phi(X) \geq \phi(a)) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[\phi(X)]}{\phi(a)}$$

Chebyshev uses $\phi(x) = (x - \mu)^2$. Chernoff uses $\phi(x) = e^{sx}$. Both are Markov in disguise.

---

## 2. Chebyshev's Inequality

### 2.1 Statement

For any random variable $X$ with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, and any $k > 0$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| \geq k) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}}$$

Equivalently, writing $k = t\sigma$:

$$\mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| \geq t\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{t^2}$$

### 2.2 Proof

Apply Markov to the non-negative r.v. $(X - \mu)^2$ with threshold $k^2$:

$$\mathbb{P}(|X - \mu| \geq k) = \mathbb{P}((X-\mu)^2 \geq k^2) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2]}{k^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2} \qquad \square$$

### 2.3 Intuition

Chebyshev gives $O(1/t^2)$ decay. The LLN follows immediately: if $\bar{X}_n = (X_1 + \cdots + X_n)/n$ with $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \sigma^2/n$, then for any $\varepsilon > 0$:

$$\mathbb{P}(|\bar{X}_n - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{n\varepsilon^2} \to 0$$

This is a proof of the Weak Law of Large Numbers.

### 2.4 One-Sided Version (Cantelli's Inequality)

For the one-sided tail:

$$\mathbb{P}(X - \mu \geq k) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{\sigma^2 + k^2}$$

This is tighter than $\sigma^2/k^2$ for one-sided deviations and doesn't require symmetry.

### 2.5 Tightness

Chebyshev is tight: achieved by a three-point distribution putting mass $1/(2t^2)$ at $\mu \pm t\sigma$ and $1 - 1/t^2$ at $\mu$. So for a general distribution, the $1/t^2$ decay cannot be improved — but for specific distributions (e.g. normal, Binomial) you can do much better.

## 3. Chernoff Bounds

### 3.1 The Core Idea

Apply Markov to $e^{sX}$ for $s > 0$:

$$\mathbb{P}(X \geq a) = \mathbb{P}(e^{sX} \geq e^{sa}) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[e^{sX}]}{e^{sa}} = \frac{M_X(s)}{e^{sa}}$$

where $M_X(s) = \mathbb{E}[e^{sX}]$ is the MGF. This holds for every $s > 0$, so optimize over $s$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(X \geq a) \leq \min_{s > 0} , e^{-sa} M_X(s)}$$

Taking logs, the bound is $e^{-\sup_{s>0}(sa - \log M_X(s))}$. The function $\Lambda^*(a) = \sup_{s>0}(sa - \log M_X(s))$ is the **Legendre-Fenchel transform** of the log-MGF — the object at the heart of large deviations theory.

### 3.2 Why Exponential Decay?

For i.i.d. $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with $S_n = \sum X_i$:

$$M_{S_n}(s) = M_X(s)^n$$

So the Chernoff bound on $S_n \geq na$ (i.e., sample mean $\geq a$) becomes:

$$\mathbb{P}(S_n \geq na) \leq e^{-n \sup_{s>0}(sa - \log M_X(s))} = e^{-n\Lambda^*(a)}$$

The exponent grows linearly in $n$. This is exponential decay in the sample size — the Markov/Chebyshev bounds give $O(1/n)$ or $O(1/n^2)$; Chernoff gives $e^{-cn}$.

## 4. Chernoff Bounds for Binomial / Bernoulli Trials

This is the most important special case. Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bern}(p)$, $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$, $\mu = np$.

### 4.1 MGF of a Bernoulli

$$M_{X_i}(s) = \mathbb{E}[e^{sX_i}] = pe^s + (1-p) = 1 + p(e^s - 1)$$

### 4.2 MGF of the Binomial Sum

$$M_{S_n}(s) = (pe^s + 1 - p)^n$$

### 4.3 Upper Tail Bound

For $\delta > 0$ (i.e., threshold $(1+\delta)\mu$ above the mean), applying Markov to $e^{sS_n}$ and optimizing over $s$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(S_n \geq (1+\delta)\mu) \leq \left(\frac{e^\delta}{(1+\delta)^{(1+\delta)}}\right)^\mu}$$

This is the **exact Chernoff bound**. It is derived by setting $s = \log(1+\delta)$ (the optimal $s$).

**Cleaner forms** (weaker but simpler):

$$\mathbb{P}(S_n \geq (1+\delta)\mu) \leq e^{-\mu\delta^2/3} \quad \text{for } 0 < \delta \leq 1$$

$$\mathbb{P}(S_n \geq (1+\delta)\mu) \leq e^{-\mu\delta/3} \quad \text{for } \delta > 1$$

### 4.4 Lower Tail Bound

For $0 < \delta < 1$ (threshold below the mean):

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(S_n \leq (1-\delta)\mu) \leq \left(\frac{e^{-\delta}}{(1-\delta)^{(1-\delta)}}\right)^\mu}$$

Simpler form:

$$\mathbb{P}(S_n \leq (1-\delta)\mu) \leq e^{-\mu\delta^2/2} \quad \text{for } 0 < \delta < 1$$

### 4.5 Two-Sided Bound

Combining upper and lower:

$$\mathbb{P}(|S_n - \mu| \geq \delta\mu) \leq 2e^{-\mu\delta^2/3} \quad \text{for } 0 < \delta \leq 1$$

### 4.6 Derivation of the Simple Upper Bound

Starting from the exact bound, we want to show $\frac{e^\delta}{(1+\delta)^{1+\delta}} \leq e^{-\delta^2/3}$ for $\delta \in (0,1]$.

Take logs: need $\delta - (1+\delta)\log(1+\delta) \leq -\delta^2/3$.

Use the Taylor bound $\log(1+\delta) \geq \delta - \delta^2/2 + \delta^3/3 - \cdots$. For $\delta \in (0,1]$, one can verify $(1+\delta)\log(1+\delta) \geq \delta + \delta^2/3$, giving the result. $\square$

## 5. Worked Examples

### 5.1 Fair Coin Flips

$S_n \sim \text{Bin}(n, 1/2)$, $\mu = n/2$. What is $\mathbb{P}(S_n \geq 3n/4)$?

Here $(1+\delta)\mu = 3n/4$ gives $\delta = 1/2$.

**Markov:** $\mathbb{P}(S_n \geq 3n/4) \leq \frac{n/2}{3n/4} = \frac{2}{3}$. Useless.

**Chebyshev:** $\text{Var}(S_n) = n/4$. $\mathbb{P}(S_n \geq 3n/4) \leq \mathbb{P}(|S_n - n/2| \geq n/4) \leq \frac{n/4}{(n/4)^2} = \frac{4}{n}$. Polynomial decay.

**Chernoff:** $\mathbb{P}(S_n \geq 3n/4) \leq e^{-\mu\delta^2/3} = e^{-(n/2)(1/4)/3} = e^{-n/24}$. Exponential decay.

> [!note] For $n = 100$: Chebyshev gives $\leq 0.04$; Chernoff gives $\leq e^{-100/24} \approx 0.015$. For $n = 1000$: Chebyshev gives $\leq 0.004$; Chernoff gives $\leq e^{-1000/24} \approx 5 \times 10^{-19}$. The gap is catastrophic at large $n$ — Chernoff is the only bound that matters in asymptotics.

### 5.2 Polling

$n$ people polled, true fraction $p = 0.5$, want $\mathbb{P}(|\hat{p} - 0.5| \geq 0.03) \leq 0.05$.

Let $S_n = $ number of yes votes, $\mu = 0.5n$, threshold $\delta = 0.06$ (since $0.03 = \delta \cdot 0.5$).

Two-sided Chernoff: $2e^{-\mu\delta^2/3} = 2e^{-(0.5n)(0.0036)/3} = 2e^{-0.0006n} \leq 0.05$.

Solve: $n \geq \frac{\ln(40)}{0.0006} \approx 6109$.

> [!tip] Standard polling uses $n \approx 1000$ for $\pm 3%$ at 95% confidence — this comes from the CLT/normal approximation, which is tighter than Chernoff. Chernoff gives a rigorous but conservative bound; the CLT gives the asymptotically correct answer.

### 5.3 Load Balancing

$n$ jobs, each assigned uniformly to one of $n$ machines. Let $X$ = number of jobs on machine 1, $X \sim \text{Bin}(n, 1/n)$, $\mu = 1$.

$\mathbb{P}(X \geq c\log n / \log\log n)$ for large $c$? Chernoff with $\mu = 1$ gives:

$$\mathbb{P}(X \geq t) \leq \left(\frac{e}{t}\right)^t$$

for $t = c\log n / \log\log n$, this is $\leq 1/n^2$ for large enough $c$. Union bound over $n$ machines: maximum load is $O(\log n / \log\log n)$ with high probability. This is the classical balls-into-bins result.

## 6. The Hierarchy in One Picture

For $S_n \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$ and threshold $a = (1+\delta)\mu$:

$$\underbrace{\frac{\mu}{a}}_{\text{Markov}} \geq \underbrace{\frac{\sigma^2}{(a-\mu)^2}}_{\text{Chebyshev}} \geq \underbrace{e^{-\mu \delta^2/3}}_{\text{Chernoff}}$$

Each bound is a refinement of the previous. The Chernoff bound can be exponentially smaller.

> [!note] Why Does Exponential Decay Win? Markov uses $\mathbb{E}[X]$. Chebyshev uses $\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2]$. Chernoff uses $\mathbb{E}[e^{sX}]$ — which encodes all moments simultaneously via the Taylor series $e^{sX} = \sum_k (sX)^k/k!$. Using all moments simultaneously is what buys exponential tails.

## 7. Mill's Inequality (Normal Tail Bound)

For $Z \sim N(0,1)$, the tail satisfies:

$$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\left(\frac{1}{t} - \frac{1}{t^3}\right)e^{-t^2/2} \leq \mathbb{P}(Z \geq t) \leq \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \frac{1}{t} \cdot e^{-t^2/2}$$

The upper bound is the **Mill's inequality** (or Mills ratio bound):

$$\mathbb{P}(Z \geq t) \leq \frac{\phi(t)}{t}$$

where $\phi$ is the standard normal PDF. This is exponential in $t^2/2$ — the Chernoff bound applied to the Gaussian MGF.

---

## 8. Hoeffding's Inequality

A generalization of Chernoff for bounded random variables that doesn't require the Bernoulli structure.

If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with $a_i \leq X_i \leq b_i$ and $S_n = \sum X_i$, $\mu = \mathbb{E}[S_n]$:

$$\mathbb{P}(S_n - \mu \geq t) \leq \exp!\left(-\frac{2t^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (b_i - a_i)^2}\right)$$

For Bernoulli with $[0,1]$ range, $\sum(b_i - a_i)^2 = n$, giving $\mathbb{P}(S_n - \mu \geq t) \leq e^{-2t^2/n}$ — similar flavor to Chernoff but derived without needing the exact MGF.

## 9. Summary Table

|Inequality|Assumption|Bound on $\mathbb{P}(X \geq a)$|Decay Rate|
|---|---|---|---|
|Markov|$X \geq 0$, know $\mu$|$\mu / a$|$1/a$|
|Chebyshev|Know $\mu, \sigma^2$|$\sigma^2 / (a - \mu)^2$|$1/(a-\mu)^2$|
|Chernoff (general)|Know MGF $M(s)$|$\min_{s>0} e^{-sa}M(s)$|$e^{-\Lambda^*(a)}$|
|Chernoff (Binomial, upper)|$S_n \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$|$e^{-\mu\delta^2/3}$ for $\delta \leq 1$|$e^{-\Theta(\mu\delta^2)}$|
|Chernoff (Binomial, lower)|$S_n \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$|$e^{-\mu\delta^2/2}$ for $\delta < 1$|$e^{-\Theta(\mu\delta^2)}$|
|Hoeffding|Bounded: $X_i \in [a_i, b_i]$|$e^{-2t^2/\sum(b_i-a_i)^2}$|$e^{-\Theta(t^2/n)}$|

## 11. Open Questions / Things to Sit With

- [ ] **Large deviations:** The Legendre transform $\Lambda^_(a) = \sup_s(sa - \log M(s))$ is the rate function. The full Cramér theorem says $\mathbb{P}(\bar{X}_n \geq a) = e^{-n\Lambda^_(a) + o(n)}$ — Chernoff gives the exponent exactly. How does the sub-exponential prefactor behave?
- [ ] **Sub-Gaussian and Sub-Exponential:** A random variable is sub-Gaussian if its MGF is bounded by a Gaussian MGF — equivalently, if Chernoff-style tails hold with Gaussian rate. Extend Hoeffding to the sub-Gaussian setting.
- [ ] **Matrix Chernoff:** For sums of random PSD matrices, there are matrix analogues of the scalar Chernoff bound using the matrix MGF. Used in randomized linear algebra.
- [ ] **Second moment method:** The first moment method is Markov ($\mathbb{P}(X \geq 1) \leq \mathbb{E}[X]$). The second moment method bounds $\mathbb{P}(X = 0) \leq \text{Var}(X)/(\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ — useful in the probabilistic method. Chebyshev in disguise.
- [ ] **Paley-Zygmund:** The reverse of Markov: $\mathbb{P}(X \geq \theta\mathbb{E}[X]) \geq (1-\theta)^2 (\mathbb{E}[X])^2/\mathbb{E}[X^2]$. Gives lower bounds on tail probabilities.
