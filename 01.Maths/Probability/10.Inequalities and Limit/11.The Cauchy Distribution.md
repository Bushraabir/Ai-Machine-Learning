

> _The Cauchy distribution is probability's most important pathology. It looks like a normal distribution — symmetric, bell-shaped, unimodal — but it has no mean, no variance, no moment generating function. The sample mean of Cauchy random variables has the same distribution as a single observation. The Law of Large Numbers fails completely. The CLT fails completely. It is the canonical example of a heavy-tailed distribution, and understanding why it breaks everything is as important as understanding why the normal distribution fixes everything._

## 1. Definition

### 1.1 Standard Cauchy

The **standard Cauchy distribution** $X \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$ has PDF:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi(1 + x^2)}, \quad x \in \mathbb{R}$$

CDF:

$$F(x) = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{\pi}\arctan(x)$$

Quantile function:

$$F^{-1}(u) = \tan!\left(\pi!\left(u - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)\right)$$

### 1.2 General Cauchy

$X \sim \text{Cauchy}(\mu, \gamma)$ with **location** $\mu \in \mathbb{R}$ and **scale** $\gamma > 0$ has PDF:

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi\gamma\left[1 + \left(\frac{x-\mu}{\gamma}\right)^2\right]}$$

The standard Cauchy is $\text{Cauchy}(0, 1)$.

### 1.3 The $t_1$ Connection

$$\text{Cauchy}(0,1) = t_1$$

The Cauchy is the Student's $t$-distribution with 1 degree of freedom. More generally, $t_\nu$ has finite moments only up to order $\nu - 1$. The Cauchy ($\nu = 1$) has no finite moments at all — not even the mean.

## 2. The Ratio Representation

### 2.1 Statement

If $X, Y \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(0,1)$, then:

$$\frac{X}{Y} \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$$

### 2.2 Proof

Let $Z = X/Y$. Condition on $Y = y$: given $Y = y$, $Z = X/y \sim N(0, 1/y^2)$, so $Z \mid Y = y$ has PDF $f_{Z|Y}(z|y) = \frac{|y|}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y^2z^2/2}$.

Marginalize over $Y$:

$$f_Z(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} \frac{|y|}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y^2z^2/2} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y^2/2},dy = \frac{1}{\pi}\int_0^\infty y,e^{-y^2(1+z^2)/2},dy$$

Let $u = y^2(1+z^2)/2$, $du = y(1+z^2),dy$:

$$f_Z(z) = \frac{1}{\pi(1+z^2)}\int_0^\infty e^{-u},du = \frac{1}{\pi(1+z^2)} \qquad \square$$

> [!note] This representation explains intuitively why the Cauchy has no mean: it is a ratio of two mean-zero normals. The denominator can be arbitrarily close to zero, producing arbitrarily large values — and the tails are heavy enough that the expectation diverges.

### 2.3 Alternative Representation: Tangent of Uniform

If $U \sim \text{Uniform}(-\pi/2, \pi/2)$, then:

$$\tan(U) \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$$

**Proof.** The CDF of $\tan(U)$ at $x$ is $\mathbb{P}(\tan(U) \leq x) = \mathbb{P}(U \leq \arctan(x)) = \frac{\arctan(x) + \pi/2}{\pi} = \frac{1}{2} + \frac{\arctan(x)}{\pi}$, which is the Cauchy CDF. $\square$

This representation gives an easy simulation method: generate $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, return $\tan(\pi(U - 1/2))$.

## 3. No Mean, No Variance, No MGF

### 3.1 The Mean Does Not Exist

$$\mathbb{E}[|X|] = \frac{2}{\pi}\int_0^\infty \frac{x}{1+x^2},dx = \frac{1}{\pi}\left[\log(1+x^2)\right]_0^\infty = +\infty$$

The absolute expectation is infinite, so $\mathbb{E}[X]$ does not exist (the integral $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{x}{\pi(1+x^2)},dx$ is not absolutely convergent — it is $\infty - \infty$ in a precise sense, though the symmetric principal value is 0).

> [!warning] The Principal Value Trap $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{x}{\pi(1+x^2)},dx$ is sometimes written as 0 by symmetry. This is the **Cauchy principal value** $\lim_{R\to\infty}\int_{-R}^{R}$, not the actual expectation. The actual expectation requires absolute convergence, which fails. The "mean = 0 by symmetry" argument is wrong: the integral is $\infty - \infty$, which is undefined, not $0 - 0$.

### 3.2 All Moments Are Infinite

$$\mathbb{E}[|X|^k] = +\infty \quad \text{for all } k \geq 1$$

Since $\frac{|x|^k}{1+x^2} \sim |x|^{k-2}$ for large $x$, the integral diverges for all $k \geq 1$. The Cauchy has no finite moments of any order.

### 3.3 The MGF Does Not Exist

$$M(s) = \mathbb{E}[e^{sX}] = +\infty \quad \text{for all } s \neq 0$$

Since $e^{sx}/(1+x^2) \sim e^{sx}/x^2$ grows faster than any power, the integral diverges for every $s \neq 0$. The MGF is identically $+\infty$ on $(-\infty, 0) \cup (0, \infty)$.

> [!warning] This makes the MGF proof technique entirely useless for Cauchy. The right tool is the **characteristic function** $\varphi(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}]$, which always exists since $|e^{itX}| = 1$.

### 3.4 The Characteristic Function

For $X \sim \text{Cauchy}(\mu, \gamma)$:

$$\varphi(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}] = e^{i\mu t - \gamma|t|}$$

For the standard Cauchy $(\mu = 0, \gamma = 1)$:

$$\varphi(t) = e^{-|t|}$$

**Proof sketch.** Compute $\int_{-\infty}^\infty \frac{e^{itx}}{\pi(1+x^2)},dx$ by contour integration (residue at $x = i$ for $t > 0$): residue $= e^{-t}/(2i)$, giving $2\pi i \cdot e^{-t}/(2\pi i) = e^{-t}$. $\square$

> [!note] The characteristic function $e^{-|t|}$ is not differentiable at $t = 0$. This is directly related to the non-existence of the mean: differentiating the characteristic function at 0 gives the mean, so if the characteristic function is not differentiable at 0, the mean does not exist.

## 4. Stability: The LLN and CLT Both Fail

### 4.1 The Sample Mean Has the Same Distribution

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$. Then:

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{X_1 + \cdots + X_n}{n} \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$$

The sample mean has **exactly the same distribution** as a single observation, for every $n$.

**Proof via characteristic functions.** The characteristic function of $X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ is $\varphi(t)^n = e^{-n|t|}$. The characteristic function of $\bar{X}_n = (X_1 + \cdots + X_n)/n$ is:

$$\varphi_{\bar{X}_n}(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{it\bar{X}_n}] = \varphi(t/n)^n = e^{-n|t/n|} = e^{-|t|}$$

which is the characteristic function of Cauchy$(0,1)$. $\square$

### 4.2 The LLN Fails

The WLLN states $\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} \mathbb{E}[X]$ when $\mathbb{E}[X]$ is finite. For Cauchy:

- $\mathbb{E}[X]$ does not exist → LLN does not apply
- $\bar{X}_n \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$ for all $n$ → $\bar{X}_n$ does not converge in probability to anything

No matter how many observations you take, the sample mean does not stabilize. Averaging Cauchy data is completely futile.

### 4.3 The CLT Fails

The CLT requires finite variance. The Cauchy has infinite variance (in fact infinite mean). The conclusion $(\bar{X}_n - \mu)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n}) \to N(0,1)$ requires $\sigma < \infty$, which fails.

More precisely: the correct normalization for Cauchy sums is not $\sqrt{n}$ but $n$ itself. $S_n/n \sim \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$ — the sum grows linearly, not like $\sqrt{n}$.

> [!note] What Replaces the CLT? For heavy-tailed distributions, the CLT is replaced by the **Generalized CLT**: properly normalized sums converge to a **stable distribution**. The Cauchy is itself stable (see §5). The normal distribution is the stable distribution with index $\alpha = 2$; the Cauchy is stable with index $\alpha = 1$.

## 5. Stability

### 5.1 Definition

A distribution is **stable** if sums of i.i.d. copies have the same distribution type (up to location and scale). Formally, $X$ is stable if for any $n$, there exist $a_n > 0$ and $b_n$ such that:

$$X_1 + \cdots + X_n \overset{d}{=} a_n X + b_n$$

### 5.2 Cauchy is Stable with Index 1

For $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Cauchy}(0,1)$:

$$X_1 + \cdots + X_n \overset{d}{=} n \cdot \text{Cauchy}(0,1) \overset{d}{=} \text{Cauchy}(0, n)$$

So $a_n = n$, $b_n = 0$. The scaling is linear in $n$, not $\sqrt{n}$ as for the normal.

### 5.3 The Stable Family

The family of stable distributions is parametrized by the **stability index** $\alpha \in (0, 2]$:

- $\alpha = 2$: Normal distribution (CLT limit for finite-variance variables)
- $\alpha = 1$: Cauchy distribution
- $\alpha \in (1,2)$: Lévy alpha-stable distributions with infinite variance but finite mean
- $\alpha \in (0,1]$: Infinite mean and infinite variance

The Cauchy sits at the boundary $\alpha = 1$ between distributions with finite mean ($\alpha > 1$) and no mean ($\alpha \leq 1$).

## 6. Tail Behavior

### 6.1 Power Law Tails

For large $|x|$:

$$f(x) \sim \frac{1}{\pi x^2}, \quad \mathbb{P}(X > x) \sim \frac{1}{\pi x}$$

The tails decay as $1/x^2$ for the PDF and $1/x$ for the survival function. This is a **power law** with exponent 1 — the slowest possible decay that still integrates to 1.

Compare with the normal: $f_N(x) \sim e^{-x^2/2}/\sqrt{2\pi}$ — exponentially faster decay.

### 6.2 Why the Mean Diverges: A Direct Argument

$$\mathbb{E}[X^+] = \int_0^\infty \frac{x}{\pi(1+x^2)},dx \approx \int_1^\infty \frac{1}{\pi x},dx = \frac{1}{\pi}\log x\Big|_1^\infty = +\infty$$

The $1/x$ tail is exactly at the boundary of integrability — $\int_1^\infty x^{-p},dx$ converges iff $p > 1$, and the Cauchy tail $\sim 1/(\pi x)$ has $p = 1$.

### 6.3 Contrast: Pareto and Other Power Laws

The Pareto distribution $\mathbb{P}(X > x) = (x_m/x)^\alpha$ for $x \geq x_m$ has:

- Finite mean iff $\alpha > 1$
- Finite variance iff $\alpha > 2$

The Cauchy corresponds to $\alpha = 1$ in the Pareto family — right at the boundary of finite mean. This is not a coincidence: both have $1/x$ survival functions.

## 7. Why the Cauchy Looks Normal but Isn't

### 7.1 Visual Deception

The Cauchy PDF $\frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}$ and the standard normal PDF $\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-x^2/2}$ are both:

- Symmetric about 0
- Unimodal with mode at 0
- Bell-shaped

Near the center, they look similar. The difference is entirely in the tails: the Cauchy decays as $1/x^2$ while the normal decays as $e^{-x^2/2}$. In a histogram, rare but enormous values will eventually reveal the Cauchy's heavy tail — but it may take a very large sample.

### 7.2 The Empirical Mean Oscillates

For Cauchy data, the running sample mean $\bar{X}_n$ does not converge. It oscillates indefinitely, occasionally jumping to large values when an extreme observation occurs. The histogram of $\bar{X}_n$ for large $n$ looks like a Cauchy — because it is one.

### 7.3 Median and Interquartile Range Work Fine

The Cauchy has a well-defined **median** (= 0 for standard Cauchy) and **interquartile range** (= 2 for standard Cauchy, since $F^{-1}(3/4) - F^{-1}(1/4) = \tan(\pi/4) - \tan(-\pi/4) = 2$). Robust statistics — median, IQR, quantiles — work correctly for Cauchy. Only moment-based statistics (mean, variance, standard deviation) break.

## 8. Practical Appearances of the Cauchy

### 8.1 Ratio of Normals

Any time you divide two independent zero-mean normals, you get a Cauchy. Ratios appear in:

- Signal-to-noise estimates when the signal mean is uncertain
- Regression coefficient divided by standard error under misspecification
- Finance: ratio of two asset returns

### 8.2 Physics: Lorentzian / Breit-Wigner

The Cauchy distribution appears in physics as the **Lorentzian** line shape and the **Breit-Wigner** distribution for resonance peaks in particle physics. The parameter $\gamma$ is the half-width at half-maximum of the resonance.

### 8.3 Electrical Engineering: Shot Noise

Certain shot noise processes have Cauchy-distributed outputs. The heavy tails correspond to rare but large voltage spikes.

### 8.4 Counterexample Generator

The Cauchy is the standard counterexample for:

- LLN (fails: mean doesn't exist)
- CLT (fails: variance doesn't exist)
- MGF techniques (fails: MGF doesn't exist)
- Consistency of the sample mean (fails: $\bar{X}_n \sim \text{Cauchy}$ for all $n$)

## 12. Open Questions / Things to Sit With

- [ ] **Truncated Cauchy:** If you truncate the Cauchy to $[-M, M]$, all moments exist. How does the sample mean of the truncated Cauchy behave? The truncation removes the heavy tails — does the LLN recover?
- [ ] **Median as estimator:** Since the sample mean fails for Cauchy, use the sample median. The sample median of $n$ Cauchy$(0,1)$ observations is approximately $N(0, \pi^2/(4n))$ by the delta method applied to the quantile function. This is a striking result — the median is consistent and asymptotically normal even when the mean is not.
- [ ] **Stable distributions:** What is the full parametrization of the stable family? What are the characteristic functions for general $\alpha \in (0,2]$? The Lévy distribution ($\alpha = 1/2$, one-sided) is another closed-form special case.
- [ ] **Cauchy and Brownian motion:** The Cauchy distribution appears as the distribution of the first hitting time of a level by Brownian motion and as the distribution of Brownian motion at a random exponential time. Understand this connection.
- [ ] **Financial returns:** Mandelbrot argued empirical asset returns have power-law tails inconsistent with normality — possibly $\alpha \approx 1.5$ to $1.8$. The Cauchy ($\alpha = 1$) is too heavy-tailed, but the intuition is right. What is the right stable model for financial returns?
