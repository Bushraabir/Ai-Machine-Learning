
> The [[01.Probability|naive definition]] was always a special case. This is the real one — the one that works even when outcomes aren't equally likely, even when the sample space is infinite, even when the problem is genuinely hard.

---

## Why We Need This

The naive definition:

$$P(A) = \frac{\text{number of favourable outcomes}}{\text{number of total outcomes}}$$

is clean and intuitive, but it only works under two conditions — finite sample space, equally likely outcomes. The moment either of those breaks, you're stuck.

- What's the probability it rains tomorrow? The outcomes "rain" and "no rain" exist, but they're not equally likely.
- What's the probability a dart lands within 1cm of the bullseye? The sample space isn't even countable.

The naive definition can't touch either of these. We need something that works in full generality.

---

## The Setup — A Probability Space

A **probability space** has two components:

1. A **sample space** $S$ — the set of all possible outcomes
2. A **probability function** $P$ — a function that takes any event (subset of $S$) as input and returns a number

The probability function $P$ is not arbitrary. It has to satisfy two axioms — and _everything_ else follows from just those two.

---

>[!important] ## The Two Axioms
>
**Axiom 1:** $$P(\emptyset) = 0 \quad \text{and} \quad P(S) = 1$$
>
The impossible event has probability zero. Something must happen — the probability of the entire sample space is 1.
>
**Axiom 2:** 
For **disjoint** events $A_1, A_2, A_3, \ldots$ (events with no overlap): $$P\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{\infty} A_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{\infty} P(A_i)$$
>
If events can't happen simultaneously, the probability of any one of them happening is just the sum of their individual probabilities. No double-counting because there's no overlap.

That's it. Two axioms. The rest of probability is built on these.

---

## Events vs Probabilities — Don't Conflate Them

This is worth pausing on because it's easy to blur the distinction.

- **Events** are _sets_ — subsets of the sample space. They live in the world of outcomes.
- **Probabilities** are _numbers_ — values between 0 and 1. They live in the world of measurement.

$P$ is the bridge between the two. It maps a set to a number. Before the experiment runs, you don't know which event occurred — you assign probabilities to capture the uncertainty. After it runs, one specific outcome crystallises.

Keeping this distinction clear prevents a lot of confused reasoning later.

---

## What Falls Out of Just Two Axioms

It's striking how much you can derive from axioms this minimal. Every useful rule you'll ever use in probability is a theorem — a consequence of Axiom 1 and Axiom 2. Nothing else is assumed.

**Complement rule:** 
>[!important] $$P(A^c) = 1 - P(A)$$

_Why:_ $A$ and $A^c$ are disjoint and together cover all of $S$. By Axiom 2, $P(A) + P(A^c) = P(S) = 1$.

**Monotonicity:** If $A \subseteq B$, then $P(A) \leq P(B)$

_Why:_ Write $B = A \cup (B \cap A^c)$. These two pieces are disjoint. By Axiom 2, $P(B) = P(A) + P(B \cap A^c)$. Since probabilities are non-negative, $P(B) \geq P(A)$.

**Inclusion-Exclusion (two events):** 
>[!important]  $$P(A \cup B) = P(A) + P(B) - P(A \cap B)$$

_Why:_ $A \cup B$ can be split into disjoint pieces: $A \cap B^c$, $A \cap B$, and $A^c \cap B$. Adding $P(A) + P(B)$ counts the middle piece $A \cap B$ twice — you subtract it once.

None of these required any extra assumptions. Just the two axioms.

---

## How It Connects Back to the Naive Definition

The naive definition isn't wrong — it's a _special case_. When the sample space is finite and all outcomes are equally likely, you can assign each outcome probability $\frac{1}{|S|}$. This satisfies both axioms. The naive formula then follows directly from Axiom 2 by summing over the favourable outcomes.

So the non-naive definition strictly generalises the naive one. Every valid naive probability calculation is still valid — it just now has a rigorous foundation underneath it.
