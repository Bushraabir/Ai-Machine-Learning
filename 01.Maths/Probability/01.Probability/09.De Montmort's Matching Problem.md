> [!abstract]
> **Derangements and the Inclusion–Exclusion Principle**
>
> This problem is simple to state but surprisingly deep.  
> It leads to one of the most elegant results in probability:
>
> $$
> P(\text{no match}) \rightarrow \frac{1}{e}
> $$
>
> The result appears in combinatorics, probability theory, and algorithm analysis.

---

# The Derangement Problem

> [!quote]
> You have **n cards** labelled  
> $$
> 1,2,\ldots,n
> $$
>
> Shuffle them randomly. This means we choose a **permutation**
>
> $$
> \sigma
> $$
>
> uniformly from the $n!$ possible permutations.
>
> A **match** occurs if
>
> $$
> \sigma(i)=i
> $$
>
> meaning **card $i$ lands in position $i$**.

---

## Goal

We want the probability that **no match occurs**.

That means:

$$
\sigma(i) \ne i \quad \text{for every } i
$$

---

## Derangements

> [!definition]
> A **derangement** is a permutation where **no element stays in its original position**.

Example (for $n=4$)

| Position | Card |
|--------|------|
|1|2|
|2|3|
|3|4|
|4|1|

Here no card equals its position.

So this permutation is a **derangement**.

---

## Notation

Let

$$
D_n
$$

be the **number of derangements of $n$ elements**.

Total permutations:

$$
n!
$$

Therefore the probability of no match is

$$
p_n = P(\text{no match}) = \frac{D_n}{n!}
$$

---

# Events to Track

Define events:

$$
A_i
$$

where

$$
A_i = \{\sigma(i)=i\}
$$

Meaning:

- event $A_i$ happens if **position $i$ is correct**

Example:

| Event | Meaning |
|-----|-----|
|$A_1$|card 1 in position 1|
|$A_2$|card 2 in position 2|

---

## What We Want

We want **none of these events to happen**.

So the probability we want is

$$
P(\text{no match})
$$

which means

$$
P(A_1^c \cap A_2^c \cap \dots \cap A_n^c)
$$

Using complements:

$$
P(\text{no match}) =
1 - P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \dots \cup A_n)
$$

Or written formally:

$$
P\!\left(\bigcap_{i=1}^n A_i^c\right)
=
1 -
P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right)
$$

So the problem becomes computing:

$$
P(A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \dots \cup A_n)
$$

---

# Inclusion–Exclusion Principle

To compute the probability of a **union of events**, we use the **Inclusion–Exclusion Principle**.

---

## General Idea

For two events:

$$
P(A\cup B)=P(A)+P(B)-P(A\cap B)
$$

For three events:

$$
P(A\cup B\cup C)
=
P(A)+P(B)+P(C)
-
P(A\cap B)-P(A\cap C)-P(B\cap C)
+
P(A\cap B\cap C)
$$

Notice the **alternating signs**.

---

## General Formula

For many events:

$$
P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^{n} A_i\right)
=
\sum P(A_i)
-
\sum P(A_i \cap A_j)
+
\sum P(A_i \cap A_j \cap A_k)
-
\cdots
$$

The pattern continues with alternating signs.

---

# Master Inclusion–Exclusion Formula

A compact way to write it is:

$$
P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right)
=
\sum_{k=1}^{n}
(-1)^{k-1}
\sum_{|S|=k}
P\!\left(\bigcap_{i\in S}A_i\right)
$$

Where:

- $S$ is a subset of indices
- $|S|=k$ means the subset has **k elements**

---

# Computing the Intersection Probability

Now compute:

$$
P\!\left(\bigcap_{i\in S} A_i\right)
$$

where $|S|=k$.

This means **k specific positions are fixed**.

Example:

positions

$$
i_1,i_2,\dots,i_k
$$

are fixed.

So:

- those $k$ cards are fixed
- remaining cards = $n-k$

These remaining cards can be arranged in:

$$
(n-k)!
$$

ways.

Total permutations:

$$
n!
$$

Therefore

$$
P\!\left(\bigcap_{i\in S} A_i\right)
=
\frac{(n-k)!}{n!}
$$

>[!important] This probability depends **only on $k$**, not on which positions were chosen.
>
This symmetry simplifies the calculation.

---

# Counting the Number of Terms

How many subsets of size $k$ exist?

$$
\binom{n}{k}
$$

So

$$
\sum_{|S|=k}
P\!\left(\bigcap_{i\in S} A_i\right)
=
\binom{n}{k}
\frac{(n-k)!}{n!}
$$

Simplify:

$$
\binom{n}{k}
=
\frac{n!}{k!(n-k)!}
$$

Therefore

$$
\binom{n}{k}\frac{(n-k)!}{n!}
=
\frac{1}{k!}
$$

So every term becomes

$$
\frac{1}{k!}
$$

This is the key simplification.

---

# Final Probability Formula

Thus

$$
P(A_1\cup\dots\cup A_n)
=
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{(-1)^{k-1}}{k!}
$$

So

$$
P(\text{no match})
=
1-
\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{(-1)^{k-1}}{k!}
$$

which simplifies to

$$
\boxed{
p_n=
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{(-1)^k}{k!}
}
$$

---

# Derangement Formula

Multiply by $n!$:

$$
D_n=n!p_n
$$

So

$$
D_n
=
n!
\sum_{k=0}^{n}\frac{(-1)^k}{k!}
$$

Or

$$
D_n
=
\sum_{k=0}^{n}
(-1)^k
\frac{n!}{k!}
$$

---

# Connection to the Exponential Function

Recall the Taylor series:

$$
e^x=\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^k}{k!}
$$

For $x=-1$:

$$
e^{-1}
=
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k}{k!}
$$

Our formula for $p_n$ is the **partial sum of this series**.

Thus

$$
\lim_{n\to\infty}p_n=e^{-1}
$$

Numerically:

$$
e^{-1}\approx0.3679
$$

---

# Convergence Speed

Error term:

$$
p_n-e^{-1}
=
-\sum_{k=n+1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^k}{k!}
$$

Using the alternating series bound:

$$
|p_n-e^{-1}|
\le
\frac{1}{(n+1)!}
$$

This means convergence is **extremely fast**.

---

# Recurrence Relation

Another formula for derangements is

$$
D_n=(n-1)(D_{n-1}+D_{n-2})
$$

with

$$
D_1=0,\quad D_2=1
$$

### Explanation

Card $n$ cannot go to position $n$.

Suppose card $n$ goes to position $k$.

There are

$$
n-1
$$

choices for $k$.

Two possibilities exist.

**Case 1**

Card $k$ goes to position $n$.

Remaining elements form a derangement of

$$
n-2
$$

elements.

Contribution:

$$
(n-1)D_{n-2}
$$

---

**Case 2**

Card $k$ does not go to position $n$.

Remaining elements form a derangement of

$$
n-1
$$

elements.

Contribution:

$$
(n-1)D_{n-1}
$$

---

Adding both gives

$$
D_n=(n-1)(D_{n-1}+D_{n-2})
$$

---

# Expected Number of Matches

Define

$$
X=\sum_{i=1}^{n}\mathbf{1}_{A_i}
$$

where $\mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ is an indicator variable.

Then

$$
E[X]
=
\sum_{i=1}^{n}P(A_i)
$$

Since

$$
P(A_i)=\frac{1}{n}
$$

we obtain

$$
E[X]=1
$$

So the **expected number of matches is always 1**, regardless of $n$.

---

# Poisson Approximation

For large $n$:

$$
X\sim\text{Poisson}(1)
$$

Thus

$$
P(X=k)\approx\frac{e^{-1}}{k!}
$$

In particular

$$
P(X=0)\approx e^{-1}
$$

which matches the derangement probability.

---

---

> [!summary] When a large number of objects are randomly permuted, the probability that **none remain in their original position** approaches
>
> $$
> \frac{1}{e}
> $$
>
> This result arises from the inclusion–exclusion principle and connects combinatorics with the exponential function.
