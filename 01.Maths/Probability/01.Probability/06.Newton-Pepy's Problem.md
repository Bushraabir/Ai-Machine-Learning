
> Newton got the right answer. His intuition was wrong. That distinction matters — and it's the whole point of this problem.

---

## The History

In 1693, Samuel Pepys — diarist, naval administrator, and apparently an enthusiastic gambler — wrote to Isaac Newton asking for help with a bet. He wanted to know which of three wagers gave him the best odds.

The man who invented calculus thought about it and got the right answer. But then he gave Pepys an explanation for _why_ it was right — and that explanation was invalid.

even Newton wasn't immune to bad probabilistic intuition. Getting the correct number and having correct intuition are two different things. You need both.

---

## The Three Bets

> **A:** At least one 6 appears when **6 fair dice** are rolled. **B:** At least two 6's appear when **12 fair dice** are rolled. **C:** At least three 6's appear when **18 fair dice** are rolled.

Each bet is tripling the dice and raising the threshold by one. At a glance they look equivalent — each requires rolling sixes at a rate of $\frac{1}{6}$. You might guess the probabilities are the same.

They are not.

---

## The Naive Guess — and Why It Feels Reasonable

The expected number of 6's in each scenario:

- 6 dice: expected $6 \times \frac{1}{6} = 1$ six
- 12 dice: expected $12 \times \frac{1}{6} = 2$ sixes
- 18 dice: expected $18 \times \frac{1}{6} = 3$ sixes

Each bet is asking for exactly the expected value. They seem balanced — one might guess equal probabilities. Pepys himself thought B was the most likely. He was wrong on both counts.

---

## The Solution — Complement Each Time

The sample space has $6^6$, $6^{12}$, and $6^{18}$ equally likely outcomes respectively. The naive definition applies to all three — fair dice, finite space, equal likelihood.

Each one is an "at least" problem, so we use the complement.

---

### Event A — At least one 6 in 6 rolls

Complement: **no 6's at all.** Each die must show 1 through 5.

Getting no 6's in 6 rolls = sampling ${1,2,3,4,5}$ with replacement, 6 times:

$$P(A^c) = \frac{5^6}{6^6}$$

$$\boxed{P(A) = 1 - \frac{5^6}{6^6} \approx 0.6651}$$

---

### Event B — At least two 6's in 12 rolls

Complement: **zero 6's or exactly one 6.**

Getting zero 6's: $5^{12}$ ways.

Getting exactly one 6: choose which of the 12 dice shows a 6 ($\binom{12}{1} = 12$ ways), then the other 11 must avoid 6's ($5^{11}$ ways). Total: $12 \cdot 5^{11}$.

$$P(B^c) = \frac{5^{12} + 12 \cdot 5^{11}}{6^{12}}$$

$$\boxed{P(B) = 1 - \frac{5^{12} + 12 \cdot 5^{11}}{6^{12}} \approx 0.6187}$$

---

### Event C — At least three 6's in 18 rolls

Complement: **zero, one, or two 6's.**

- Zero 6's: $5^{18}$
- Exactly one 6: $\binom{18}{1} \cdot 5^{17} = 18 \cdot 5^{17}$
- Exactly two 6's: $\binom{18}{2} \cdot 5^{16} = 153 \cdot 5^{16}$

$$P(C^c) = \frac{5^{18} + 18 \cdot 5^{17} + 153 \cdot 5^{16}}{6^{18}}$$

$$\boxed{P(C) = 1 - \frac{5^{18} + 18 \cdot 5^{17} + 153 \cdot 5^{16}}{6^{18}} \approx 0.5973}$$

---

## The Answer

| Bet | Dice | Threshold | Probability |
| --- | ---- | --------- | ----------- |
| A   | 6    | ≥ 1 six   | ≈ **66.5%** |
| B   | 12   | ≥ 2 sixes | ≈ 61.9%     |
| C   | 18   | ≥ 3 sixes | ≈ 59.7%     |

**A has the highest probability.** Pepys should take bet A.

The probabilities are close — they don't collapse to the same value, but they don't diverge wildly either. What's interesting is that A is consistently better despite the intuition that all three are "balanced."

---

## Newton's Invalid Intuition

Newton gave Pepys a correct answer, then tried to explain _why_ with a shortcut. He imagined B and C tossing their dice in groups of six, and argued that A was best because it only needed one six in one group of six, while B needed at least one six in _each_ of its two groups of six, and C in each of its three groups.

This reasoning has a flaw: it assumes each group of six produces _at most_ one 6. But that's not guaranteed. If a group produces two 6's, Newton's argument collapses — the groups are not independent in the way he assumed. His framework doesn't match the actual problem.

As the book notes, using loaded dice could actually change the ordering of A, B, C entirely. Newton's intuition can't account for that — which is further evidence the intuition wasn't tracking the right thing.

---

## What This Problem Is Really Teaching

Two things:

**1. The complement is the move for "at least" problems.** Every one of these three was solved by flipping to "fails to reach the threshold." That pattern — count the complement, subtract from 1 — is something to reach for automatically now.

**2. Getting a right answer doesn't mean your reasoning is right.** Newton calculated correctly and reasoned incorrectly. The calculation and the intuition are separate things to check. This is why the book emphasises sanity checks and story proofs — you want to understand _why_ something is true, not just arrive at the right number.
