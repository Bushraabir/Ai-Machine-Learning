
> One of the most famous counterintuitive results in all of probability. Your gut will tell you one thing. The math will tell you something completely different. Trust the math.

---

## The Problem

There are $k$ people in a room. Assume:

- Each person's birthday is equally likely to be any of the **365 days** of the year (February 29 excluded)
- People's birthdays are **independent** — knowing one person's birthday tells you nothing about anyone else's

The question:

> What is the probability that **at least two people** share a birthday?

---

## Why Intuition Fails Here

Most people hear this and think: 365 days, so you'd need close to 183 people before a match becomes likely. Maybe even more.

The answer is **23**.

With just 23 people in a room, there is already a **better than 50% chance** of a shared birthday. By the time you reach 57 people, the probability exceeds **99%**. And at 100 people, it's essentially certain.

The reason intuition fails is that you're thinking about the wrong thing. You're asking: "what's the chance _someone_ shares _my_ birthday?" That's a hard thing to trigger. But the problem isn't asking that. It's asking whether _any_ two people among the group share _a_ birthday — any birthday at all. The number of pairs grows much faster than the number of people.

With $k$ people, there are $\binom{k}{2}$ pairs. With 23 people, that's $\binom{23}{2} = 253$ pairs. Each pair has roughly a $\frac{1}{365}$ chance of matching. 253 chances at a coincidence — suddenly 50% doesn't feel so surprising.

---

## The Solution — Work With the Complement

Counting directly is painful. You'd have to account for exactly one pair matching, or two pairs, or all three sharing a birthday, or... it spirals fast.

The complement is clean:

$$P(\text{at least one match}) = 1 - P(\text{no match at all})$$

**How many ways can $k$ people all have different birthdays?**

This is sampling 365 days _without replacement_, $k$ times — since no two people can share a birthday if there's no match. The count is:

$$365 \times 364 \times 363 \times \cdots \times (365 - k + 1)$$

**Total ways to assign birthdays to $k$ people** (with no restriction)?

Each person can have any of 365 birthdays independently — sampling _with replacement_. So:

$$365^k$$

Both counts assume the naive definition applies — all equally likely — which holds under our assumptions.

Therefore:

$$P(\text{no birthday match}) = \frac{365 \times 364 \times \cdots \times (365 - k + 1)}{365^k}$$

And:

$$\boxed{P(\text{at least one birthday match}) = 1 - \frac{365 \times 364 \times \cdots \times (365 - k + 1)}{365^k}}$$

---

## What the Numbers Actually Look Like

| People ($k$) | $P(\text{match})$ |
| ------------ | ----------------- |
| 10           | ~11.7%            |
| 20           | ~41.1%            |
| **23**       | **~50.7%**        |
| 30           | ~70.6%            |
| 40           | ~89.1%            |
| 57           | ~99.0%            |
| 70           | ~99.9%            |

The first $k$ where the probability crosses 50% is $k = 23$.

---

## The Structure of the Solution — Why This Is Clever

Notice what's actually happening here. The numerator counts ordered sequences of $k$ distinct birthdays — that's **sampling without replacement**. The denominator counts all ordered birthday sequences — that's **sampling with replacement**. The same problem uses both at once.

This is a pattern worth recognising. When you need to compute the probability of "all different", you're usually dividing a without-replacement count by a with-replacement count.

---

## The Pairs Intuition (More Carefully)

The rough argument: 23 people give $\binom{23}{2} = 253$ pairs, and each pair has probability $\frac{1}{365}$ of matching. So the expected number of matching pairs is about $\frac{253}{365} \approx 0.69$.

An expected value of 0.69 matching pairs doesn't directly give you the probability of at least one match, but it does suggest you're well into the range where a match is plausible. The exact calculation confirms it sits just above 50%.
