
> [!quote] The core question _k_ people walk into a room. What's the probability that **at least two of them share a birthday?**

---

## Why My Intuition Was Wrong

My first instinct: _365 days in a year, so you'd need ~183 people before a match becomes likely._

**The actual answer: just 23 people.**

With only 23 people in a room, there's already a **>50% chance** of a shared birthday. I was thinking about the wrong question entirely — I was imagining _"does someone share MY birthday?"_ But the problem asks about _any_ match between _any_ two people. That's a completely different beast.

> [!tip] The key insight I was anchoring on **people**, but I should have been thinking about **pairs**.
> 
> With _k_ people, there are $\binom{k}{2}$ pairs. With just 23 people, that's already **253 pairs** — each with a ~1/365 shot at a match. 253 chances feels a lot more plausible for a 50% hit rate.

---

##  The Solution

### Why direct counting is painful

To count directly, I'd need to account for _exactly_ one pair matching, or two, or three sharing a birthday, or... it spirals fast. Painful.

### Work with the complement instead

$$P(\text{at least one match}) = 1 - P(\text{no match at all})$$

This is so much cleaner.

---

###  Building the formula

**How many ways can _k_ people all have _different_ birthdays?**

This is sampling 365 days **without replacement** — no two people can share a day:

$$365 \times 364 \times 363 \times \cdots \times (365 - k + 1)$$

**Total ways to assign birthdays with no restriction?**

Each person independently picks any of 365 days — sampling **with replacement**:

$$365^k$$

**Therefore:**

$$P(\text{no match}) = \frac{365 \times 364 \times \cdots \times (365 - k + 1)}{365^k}$$

$$\boxed{P(\text{at least one match}) = 1 - \frac{365 \times 364 \times \cdots \times (365 - k + 1)}{365^k}}$$

##  The Pattern Worth Remembering

> [!important] Generalizable insight When computing P(all different), you're almost always dividing a **without-replacement** count by a **with-replacement** count.
> 
> $$P(\text{all different}) = \frac{\text{ordered sequences of } k \text{ distinct items}}{\text{all ordered sequences of } k \text{ items}}$$
> 
> The same problem uses both sampling models at once — that's the elegant structure here.

##  The Pairs Intuition (More Carefully)

The rough back-of-envelope argument:

- 23 people → $\binom{23}{2} = 253$ pairs
- Each pair: probability $\frac{1}{365}$ of matching
- Expected matching pairs: $\frac{253}{365} \approx 0.69$

An expected value of **0.69 matching pairs** doesn't directly give the probability of at least one match — but it does suggest we're squarely in the range where a match is plausible. The exact calculation confirms we land just above 50%.

---

##  Assumptions

- Birthdays are **uniformly distributed** across 365 days (no Feb 29)
- Birthdays are **independent** — knowing one person's birthday tells you nothing about another's
