# Two Dice — Sum of 11 vs Sum of 12

> **trap:** treating identical-looking outcomes as one thing when they're actually two separate events.

---

## The Question

> If we roll two fair dice, which is more likely — a sum of **11** or a sum of **12**?

First instinct for most people? They both seem equally rare, so... equal chance?

**Wrong.** And here's exactly why.

---

## The Mistake People Make

When you think about it casually:

- $12 = 6 + 6$
- $11 = 6 + 5$

Looks like one way each. So you write:

$$P(12) = P(11) = \frac{1}{6}$$

**This is the mistake.** You're not labelling the dice — you're treating them as one blob instead of two separate, distinguishable objects.

---

## Correct Approach — Label Your Dice

Call them **Die 1** and **Die 2**. Now list every outcome _carefully_:

---

###  Sum = 12

The only way to get 12:

| Die 1 | Die 2 | Total |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 6     | 6     | 12 ✓  |

→ **1 outcome**

---

### Sum = 11

The ways to get 11:

| Die 1 | Die 2 | Total |
| ----- | ----- | ----- |
| 6     | 5     | 11 ✓  |
| 5     | 6     | 11 ✓  |

→ **2 outcomes**

These are _not the same outcome_. Die 1 showing 6 while Die 2 shows 5 is a completely different physical result from Die 1 showing 5 while Die 2 shows 6. Same sum, different events.

---

## The Correct Probabilities

Total possible outcomes when rolling two dice = $6 \times 6 = 36$

$$\boxed{P(\text{sum} = 12) = \frac{1}{36}}$$

$$\boxed{P(\text{sum} = 11) = \frac{2}{36} = \frac{1}{18}}$$

**Sum of 11 is exactly twice as likely as sum of 12.**

---

## The Lesson to Burn Into Your Brain 

> **Always label and distinguish your objects — even when they look identical.**

A pair of dice looks symmetric. The numbers look the same. But the _dice themselves_ are separate objects sitting in separate positions in space. Die 1 and Die 2 are not the same die.

This is the same principle from the counting lectures:

- When in doubt → **label everything**
- Unlabelled = undercounted = wrong answer

The moment you label the dice, the "equal probability" illusion disappears.
