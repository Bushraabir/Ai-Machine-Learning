— a problem that looks like estimation but is secretly a lesson in thinking carefully about what "random" means_
>[!quote] You're a wildlife biologist. There are $N$ elk in a forest. You **don't know $N$** — that's the whole point. You capture $m$ of them, tag them, and release them back. You wait. Then you recapture a sample of $n$ elk, and you observe that exactly $k$ of those $n$ are tagged.
>
**Question:** What is a good estimate of $N$?

This is the **capture-recapture** problem, sometimes called the mark-recapture or Lincoln-Petersen method. In Stat 110 spirit, before we do any estimation, let's first ask the probability question that underlies it.

---

## The Probability Model (the clean setup)

Assume the $n$ recaptured elk are a **uniformly random sample** (without replacement) from the $N$ elk, of which exactly $m$ are tagged.

Let $X$ = number of tagged elk in the recapture sample.

Then $X$ follows a **Hypergeometric distribution**:

$$ P(X = k) = \frac{\binom{m}{k}\binom{N-m}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}} $$

for $k = \max(0,, n - (N-m)) \leq k \leq \min(m, n)$.

> **Why hypergeometric and not binomial?** Because sampling is _without replacement_ — the draws are not independent. If I told you 99 out of 100 elk were tagged, the probability the last one is tagged is 1, not the base rate. Dependence is baked in. The binomial would be the lazy wrong answer here.

---

## The Estimator — and the Beautiful Symmetry Argument

So now: given the data $(m, n, k)$, what should we guess for $N$?

The **Method of Moments** / **plug-in intuition** goes like this:

> "In my sample of $n$, the fraction tagged is $k/n$. In the whole population of $N$, the fraction tagged is $m/N$. These should be roughly equal."

$$ \frac{k}{n} \approx \frac{m}{N} $$

Solve for $N$:

$$ \boxed{\hat{N} = \frac{mn}{k}} $$

This is the **Lincoln-Petersen estimator**. It's almost offensively simple. But _why_ does it work? The symmetry argument is the real insight:

> In the hypergeometric model, $\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{nm}{N}$. So if we set the observed $k$ equal to $\mathbb{E}[X]$ and solve for $N$, we get exactly $\hat{N} = mn/k$. The estimator is just the **method of moments** applied to the hypergeometric mean.

Let me write that expectation derivation cleanly, because it's a good exercise.

---

## Computing $\mathbb{E}[X]$ for the Hypergeometric

> _I always re-derive this. It never gets old._

**Method 1 — Indicator RVs (the Stat 110 way, elegant)**

Let $I_j = \mathbf{1}[\text{the } j\text{-th recaptured elk is tagged}]$ for $j = 1, \ldots, n$.

Then $X = I_1 + I_2 + \cdots + I_n$.

By symmetry (all elk are equally likely to be in any position in the sample):

$$ P(I_j = 1) = \frac{m}{N} $$

for every $j$. So by linearity of expectation:

$$ \mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbb{E}[I_j] = n \cdot \frac{m}{N} = \frac{nm}{N} $$

No combinatorial mess. No conditional arguments. Just symmetry + linearity.

> **Note to self:** This is the move Blitzstein makes constantly — when in doubt, find the indicator RVs and invoke linearity. The individual $I_j$'s are _not_ independent here, but that doesn't matter. Linearity of expectation doesn't need independence. That's the whole point.

**Method 2 — Direct from the PMF (the hard way, but confirms it)**

$$ \mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{\min(m,n)} k \cdot \frac{\binom{m}{k}\binom{N-m}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}} $$

Use the identity $k \binom{m}{k} = m \binom{m-1}{k-1}$:

$$ = \frac{m}{\binom{N}{n}} \sum_{k=1}^{\min(m,n)} \binom{m-1}{k-1}\binom{N-m}{n-k} $$

Re-index $j = k-1$:

$$ = \frac{m}{\binom{N}{n}} \sum_{j=0}^{\min(m-1,,n-1)} \binom{m-1}{j}\binom{N-m}{n-1-j} $$

Vandermonde's identity: $\sum_{j=0}^{r} \binom{m-1}{j}\binom{N-m}{r-j} = \binom{N-1}{r}$ with $r = n-1$:

$$ = \frac{m \cdot \binom{N-1}{n-1}}{\binom{N}{n}} = m \cdot \frac{(N-1)!,n!,(N-n)!}{(N-1-n+1)!,(n-1)!, N!} = \frac{mn}{N} $$

Same answer. But Method 1 is _obviously_ better. Use Method 1.

---

## Is $\hat{N} = mn/k$ any good? Bias Discussion

Something that should bother you: $\hat{N} = mn/k$ is **not unbiased** for $N$.

Why? Because $\mathbb{E}[mn/X] \neq mn/\mathbb{E}[X]$ in general — Jensen's inequality (since $1/x$ is convex). So $\hat{N}$ slightly overestimates $N$ on average.

The **Chapman estimator** corrects for this:

$$ \hat{N}_{\text{Chapman}} = \frac{(m+1)(n+1)}{k+1} - 1 $$

This is approximately unbiased. In practice, when $k$ is not too small, the difference is negligible. But the fact that $k=0$ breaks the original estimator ($\hat{N} = \infty$... bad) motivates the Chapman form regardless.

---

## The Deeper Point — Naive Probability and Symmetry

What I really want to remember about this problem:

1. **The hypergeometric is the natural model** for sampling without replacement from a binary population. Every time you see "draw $n$ from $N$, of which $m$ are special," write hypergeometric immediately.
    
2. **Symmetry is your best friend.** The computation $P(I_j = 1) = m/N$ didn't need to know what position $j$ was, or what happened at any other draw. That's the power of exchangeability — all positions are symmetric, so the marginal probability is just the overall fraction.
    
3. **Linearity of expectation is unconditional.** This problem is a perfect reminder that you never need independence to apply linearity. You need it for $\text{Var}(X_1 + X_2) = \text{Var}(X_1) + \text{Var}(X_2)$, but never for expectations.
    
4. **Method of moments is secretly just matching population fractions.** The estimator $\hat{N} = mn/k$ is nothing but the proportion argument made rigorous.
    

---

## A Sanity Check

Say $N = 1000$, $m = 100$ tagged, $n = 50$ recaptured, $k = 5$ tagged in recapture.

$$\hat{N} = \frac{100 \cdot 50}{5} = 1000$$

We recover the truth exactly (of course, $k = \mathbb{E}[X]$ here). In real life $k$ fluctuates, and the estimator fluctuates with it. That's what makes this estimation and not oracle-querying.

---

## Variance of $X$ (for completeness)

$$ \text{Var}(X) = n \cdot \frac{m}{N} \cdot \frac{N-m}{N} \cdot \frac{N-n}{N-1} $$

The factor $\frac{N-n}{N-1}$ is the **finite population correction (FPC)**. When $n \ll N$, FPC $\approx 1$ and this collapses to the binomial variance $np(1-p)$. When $n = N$ (sample the whole population), FPC $= 0$ and $\text{Var}(X) = 0$ — of course, you know exactly how many tagged elk there are.

> The FPC is one of those things that's obvious in hindsight but easy to forget. If you're sampling a large fraction of the population, your estimate is much more precise than a binomial model would suggest.

---

_Last thought: there's something poetic about this problem. You can't see the whole forest. You tag a few elk, let them wander, and trust that randomness will carry information back to you in your second sample. The whole enterprise of statistics is right there in miniature — inference under uncertainty, from partial observation, using symmetry to say something true about something you can never directly count._
