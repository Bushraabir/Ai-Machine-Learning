> _"You rarely care about $X$ itself. You care about $g(X)$ — the cost, the distance, the transformed measurement. This is the machinery for that."_

I have a random variable $X$ with a known distribution. Now someone hands me a function $g$ and asks: what is the distribution of $Y = g(X)$?

This comes up constantly:

- $X$ is a Normal, and I need $X^2$ (Chi-squared)
- $X$ is Uniform$(0,1)$ and I need to simulate an Exponential
- $X, Y$ are independent Normals and I want $X/Y$ (Cauchy)
- $X$ is a waiting time and I want to work in log-scale

There are **three main techniques**, each suited to different situations:

| Technique                                       | When to use                                               |
| ----------------------------------------------- | --------------------------------------------------------- |
| **CDF Method**                                  | Always works. Start here.                                 |
| **PDF Method (Change of Variables / Jacobian)** | $g$ is monotone, continuous, differentiable               |
| **MGF Method**                                  | Identifying the _type_ of distribution of a sum/transform |

---

# PART I — The CDF Method

**The master technique. Works for discrete, continuous, or mixed.**

**Recipe:**

1. Write $F_Y(y) = P(Y \leq y) = P(g(X) \leq y)$
2. Rewrite the event ${g(X) \leq y}$ as an event on $X$
3. Evaluate using the known distribution of $X$
4. Differentiate to get $f_Y(y)$ if $Y$ is continuous

---

### Example 1: Linear Transformation

Let $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$. Find the distribution of $Y = aX + b$ with $a \neq 0$.

$$F_Y(y) = P(aX + b \leq y) = P!\left(X \leq \frac{y-b}{a}\right) = F_X!\left(\frac{y-b}{a}\right).$$

Differentiate (chain rule):

$$f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{|a|} f_X!\left(\frac{y-b}{a}\right) = \frac{1}{|a|} \cdot \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp!\left(-\frac{\left(\frac{y-b}{a} - \mu\right)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right).$$

Simplify: this is $N(a\mu + b,, a^2\sigma^2)$.

> **Key takeaway:** Normal distributions are closed under linear transformations. This is one of the reasons the Normal is so tractable.

### Example 2: $Y = X^2$ for $X \sim N(0,1)$

This is the Chi-squared with 1 degree of freedom.

For $y > 0$:

$$F_Y(y) = P(X^2 \leq y) = P(-\sqrt{y} \leq X \leq \sqrt{y}) = F_X(\sqrt{y}) - F_X(-\sqrt{y}) = 2F_X(\sqrt{y}) - 1.$$

Differentiate:

$$f_Y(y) = 2f_X(\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} = \frac{f_X(\sqrt{y})}{\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \frac{e^{-y/2}}{\sqrt{y}} = \frac{y^{1/2 - 1} e^{-y/2}}{2^{1/2},\Gamma(1/2)}.$$

This is $\text{Gamma}(1/2,, 1/2) = \chi^2(1)$. $\square$

> _Notice the trick at the non-monotone step: ${X^2 \leq y} = {-\sqrt{y} \leq X \leq \sqrt{y}}$. I had to unpack the absolute value carefully. With $g$ non-monotone, the CDF method handles it naturally — the Change of Variables formula would need modification._


### Example 3: $Y = e^X$ for $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ — The Log-Normal

For $y > 0$:

$$F_Y(y) = P(e^X \leq y) = P(X \leq \ln y) = \Phi!\left(\frac{\ln y - \mu}{\sigma}\right).$$

Differentiate:

$$f_Y(y) = \frac{1}{y\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp!\left(-\frac{(\ln y - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right), \qquad y > 0.$$

This is the **Log-Normal** distribution. It models things like stock prices, incomes, city sizes — phenomena that are products of many small multiplicative factors (take log → sum → CLT → Normal → exponentiate → Log-Normal).

# PART II — Change of Variables (The Jacobian Formula)

When $g$ is **strictly monotone** and differentiable, there's a direct formula for the PDF. This is the rigorous version of "substitution" from calculus, applied to densities.

---

## The One-Dimensional Formula

**Theorem.** Let $X$ have PDF $f_X$. Let $Y = g(X)$ where $g$ is strictly monotone and differentiable on the support of $X$. Then $Y$ has PDF

$$\boxed{f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \left|\frac{d}{dy} g^{-1}(y)\right|.}$$

Equivalently, writing $x = g^{-1}(y)$:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(x) \cdot \left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right|.$$

---

### Derivation (Don't just memorize — understand)

Suppose $g$ is **strictly increasing** (the decreasing case is symmetric).

$$F_Y(y) = P(Y \leq y) = P(g(X) \leq y) = P(X \leq g^{-1}(y)) = F_X(g^{-1}(y)).$$

Differentiate via chain rule:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y).$$

If $g$ is **strictly decreasing**, then $g^{-1}$ is also decreasing, so $\frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y) < 0$, and

$$F_Y(y) = P(g(X) \leq y) = P(X \geq g^{-1}(y)) = 1 - F_X(g^{-1}(y)),$$

giving $f_Y(y) = -f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)$.

Both cases are captured by the absolute value $\left|\frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y)\right|$. $\square$

> _The Jacobian $\left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right|$ is a **stretching factor**. It corrects for how much the transformation $g$ distorts volume. This is the same Jacobian from multivariable calculus — it's not a coincidence, it's the same idea._

---

### Example: Universality of the Uniform

**Claim.** If $X$ has continuous CDF $F_X$, then $U = F_X(X) \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$.

**Proof.** For $u \in (0,1)$:

$$P(U \leq u) = P(F_X(X) \leq u) = P(X \leq F_X^{-1}(u)) = F_X(F_X^{-1}(u)) = u.$$

This is the CDF of Uniform$(0,1)$. $\square$

**Corollary (Simulation).** If $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, then $X = F_X^{-1}(U)$ has CDF $F_X$.

This is how you simulate _any_ continuous distribution from a Uniform. For example, $X = -\frac{1}{\lambda}\ln(1-U) \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$.

> _This is one of my favorite theorems in all of probability. It says the Uniform$(0,1)$ is the universal raw material for randomness. Every computer's random number generator produces Uniforms for exactly this reason._

## The Multivariate Change of Variables

Now let $(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ have joint PDF $f_{\mathbf{X}}$. Define a transformation $\mathbf{Y} = g(\mathbf{X})$ where $g: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is differentiable and one-to-one.

**Theorem.** The joint PDF of $\mathbf{Y}$ is:

$$f_{\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{y}) = f_{\mathbf{X}}(g^{-1}(\mathbf{y})) \cdot |\det J|,$$

where $J$ is the **Jacobian matrix**:

$$J = \frac{\partial(x_1, \ldots, x_n)}{\partial(y_1, \ldots, y_n)} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial y_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial y_n} \ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ \frac{\partial x_n}{\partial y_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial x_n}{\partial y_n} \end{pmatrix}.$$

> _Convention: I compute the Jacobian of the inverse transformation $\mathbf{x} = g^{-1}(\mathbf{y})$. This matches Blitzstein and is the cleaner convention._

---

### Example: Sum and Difference of Normals

Let $X, Y \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} N(0,1)$. Find the joint distribution of $U = X+Y$, $V = X-Y$.

**Inverse transformation:** $X = (U+V)/2$, $Y = (U-V)/2$.

**Jacobian:**

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} \partial x/\partial u & \partial x/\partial v \ \partial y/\partial u & \partial y/\partial v \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} 1/2 & 1/2 \ 1/2 & -1/2 \end{pmatrix}, \qquad |\det J| = \left|-\frac{1}{4} - \frac{1}{4}\right| = \frac{1}{2}.$$

**Joint PDF of $(X,Y)$:** Since independent,

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi} e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}.$$

**Joint PDF of $(U,V)$:**

$$f_{U,V}(u,v) = f_{X,Y}!\left(\frac{u+v}{2}, \frac{u-v}{2}\right) \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{2\pi} e^{-\frac{(u+v)^2+(u-v)^2}{8}} \cdot \frac{1}{2}.$$

Expand: $(u+v)^2 + (u-v)^2 = 2u^2 + 2v^2$. So:

$$f_{U,V}(u,v) = \frac{1}{4\pi} e^{-(u^2+v^2)/4} = \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi}} e^{-u^2/4}}_{\sim, N(0,2)} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{4\pi}} e^{-v^2/4}}_{\sim, N(0,2)}.$$

The joint PDF factors, so $U \perp V$. Both are $N(0,2)$. $\square$

> _The sum and difference of independent standard Normals are themselves independent. This is a deep property of the Normal — it doesn't hold for most distributions._

# PART III — The MGF Method

When I want to **identify the distribution** of $Y = g(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ — especially sums — the MGF is the cleanest tool.

**Strategy:** Compute $M_Y(t) = E[e^{tY}]$. If it matches the MGF of a known distribution, we're done (by uniqueness of MGFs).

---

### Example: Sum of Independent Normals

Let $X_i \overset{\text{ind.}}{\sim} N(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$. Find the distribution of $S = \sum X_i$.

By independence:

$$M_S(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{X_i}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n e^{\mu_i t + \sigma_i^2 t^2/2} = \exp!\left(t\sum_i \mu_i + \frac{t^2}{2}\sum_i \sigma_i^2\right).$$

This is the MGF of $N!\left(\sum \mu_i,, \sum \sigma_i^2\right)$. $\square$

---

### Example: Sum of Independent Poissons

Let $X_i \overset{\text{ind.}}{\sim} \text{Pois}(\lambda_i)$. Then:

$$M_S(t) = \prod_i e^{\lambda_i(e^t - 1)} = e^{(\sum_i \lambda_i)(e^t-1)}.$$

This is $\text{Pois}(\sum_i \lambda_i)$. $\square$

# PART IV — Special Transformations Worth Knowing

## The Probability Integral Transform (revisited)

Already proved above: $F_X(X) \sim U(0,1)$.

---

## Box-Muller Transform

Given $U_1, U_2 \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} U(0,1)$, define:

$$Z_1 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1} \cos(2\pi U_2), \qquad Z_2 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1} \sin(2\pi U_2).$$

**Claim:** $Z_1, Z_2 \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} N(0,1)$.

_Proof sketch:_ Write in polar coordinates. $R^2 = Z_1^2 + Z_2^2 = -2\ln U_1 \sim \text{Expo}(1/2) = \chi^2(2)$. The angle $\Theta = 2\pi U_2 \sim U(0, 2\pi)$, independent of $R$. Converting back from polar to Cartesian gives the bivariate standard Normal. $\square$

> _This is how many software libraries generate Normals. Computers only have Uniforms; everything else is built from them._

---

## Order Statistics

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} f_X$ with CDF $F_X$. Let $X_{(1)} \leq X_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq X_{(n)}$ be the sorted values.

**PDF of the $k$-th order statistic $X_{(k)}$:**

$$f_{X_{(k)}}(x) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!} [F_X(x)]^{k-1}[1-F_X(x)]^{n-k} f_X(x).$$

**Intuition:** For $X_{(k)}$ to equal $x$, exactly $k-1$ of the other $n-1$ values must be $\leq x$, exactly $n-k$ must be $> x$, and one (the $k$-th) is at $x$. The combinatorial factor counts arrangements.

**Special case:** For $X_i \sim U(0,1)$, $X_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k, n-k+1)$.

## The Delta Method (First-Order Approximation)

For a differentiable function $g$ and a random variable $X$ with $E[X] = \mu$, $\text{Var}(X) = \sigma^2$:

$$g(X) \approx g(\mu) + g'(\mu)(X - \mu) \quad \text{(Taylor, first order)}.$$

Taking expectations and variance:

$$E[g(X)] \approx g(\mu), \qquad \text{Var}(g(X)) \approx [g'(\mu)]^2 \sigma^2.$$

**More precisely (for sequences):** If $\sqrt{n}(X_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} N(0, \sigma^2)$, then

$$\sqrt{n}(g(X_n) - g(\mu)) \xrightarrow{d} N(0,, [g'(\mu)]^2\sigma^2).$$

> _The Delta Method is the tool for propagating uncertainty through nonlinear transformations. It's used everywhere in statistics — confidence intervals for transformed parameters, asymptotic distributions of MLEs, etc._

# Which Method When?

```
Is g monotone and differentiable?
├── Yes → Change of Variables (Jacobian) — direct and clean
└── No  → CDF Method — always works, just unpack {g(X) ≤ y}

Is the question "what distribution is this sum?"
└── Yes → MGF Method — identify by matching known MGFs

Is g a complicated function of many variables?
└── Multivariate Jacobian, or condition and integrate
```
