## From Events to Random Variables

You already know independence for events: $A$ and $B$ are independent if

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)$$

equivalently, $P(A \mid B) = P(A)$ — knowing $B$ happened tells you nothing about $A$.

For random variables, the idea is the same but richer. $X$ and $Y$ are independent if knowing the value of $Y$ tells you nothing about the distribution of $X$ — for _any_ values, not just one pair.

---

## The Definition

Random variables $X$ and $Y$ are **independent** if for all $x, y \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$P(X \leq x,\ Y \leq y) = P(X \leq x), P(Y \leq y)$$

In CDF language: the **joint CDF factors** into the product of marginal CDFs:

$$F_{X,Y}(x, y) = F_X(x), F_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

This is the definition. Everything else follows from it.

### Equivalent Formulations

For **discrete** $X, Y$, independence $\iff$ the joint PMF factors:

$$P(X = x,\ Y = y) = P(X = x), P(Y = y) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

For **continuous** $X, Y$, independence $\iff$ the joint PDF factors:

$$f_{X,Y}(x, y) = f_X(x), f_Y(y) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

For **any** $X, Y$, independence $\iff$ for all (measurable) sets $A, B \subseteq \mathbb{R}$:

$$P(X \in A,\ Y \in B) = P(X \in A), P(Y \in B)$$

> **The "for all" is critical.** It is not enough that $P(X = x, Y = y) = P(X = x) P(Y = y)$ holds for one particular pair $(x, y)$. It must hold for every pair. Independence is a global property of the joint distribution, not a pointwise accident.

---

## What Independence Really Says

Independence means the joint distribution is completely determined by the marginals. If I give you $F_X$ and $F_Y$ separately, and $X \perp Y$, then you know everything about the pair $(X, Y)$.

If $X$ and $Y$ are _not_ independent, there is extra information in their joint distribution that cannot be recovered from the marginals alone. The marginals are insufficient summaries.

Conditional on $Y = y$: independence means

$$P(X \leq x \mid Y = y) = P(X \leq x) \quad \text{for all } x, y$$

The conditional distribution of $X$ given $Y = y$ is the same as the unconditional distribution of $X$, regardless of what $y$ is. Learning $Y$ is useless for predicting $X$.

## Independence vs Uncorrelatedness — The Critical Distinction

This is the most important distinction in this note.

**Uncorrelated:** $\text{Cov}(X, Y) = 0$, equivalently $\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$.

**Independent:** The full joint distribution factors.

$$\text{Independent} \implies \text{Uncorrelated}$$

$$\text{Uncorrelated} \not\implies \text{Independent}$$

The implication is one-way. Independence is strictly stronger.

### Proof that Independence $\implies$ Uncorrelated

If $X \perp Y$, then for discrete RVs:

$$\mathbb{E}[XY] = \sum_x \sum_y xy, P(X=x, Y=y) = \sum_x \sum_y xy, P(X=x) P(Y=y)$$

$$= \left(\sum_x x, P(X=x)\right)\left(\sum_y y, P(Y=y)\right) = \mathbb{E}[X], \mathbb{E}[Y]$$

So $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y] = 0$. $\square$

The factorization of the joint distribution allows the double sum to split into a product of single sums. That's the entire proof — and it shows why independence is such a powerful condition for expectation calculations.

### Counterexample: Uncorrelated but Dependent

Let $X \sim \text{Uniform}{-1, 0, 1}$ (each with probability $1/3$), and let $Y = X^2$.

$Y$ is a deterministic function of $X$ — they are maximally dependent. Yet:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = 0, \quad \mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X^3] = \frac{1}{3}((-1)^3 + 0^3 + 1^3) = 0$$

$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = \mathbb{E}[XY] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y] = 0 - 0 = 0$$

Zero covariance. Completely dependent. Covariance only captures **linear** dependence. The nonlinear relationship $Y = X^2$ is invisible to it.

> **Moral:** Uncorrelatedness is a statement about one number — the covariance. Independence is a statement about the entire joint distribution — infinitely many numbers. You cannot infer the latter from the former. Every time someone says "uncorrelated, therefore independent," they are making an error — unless they are in the special case of jointly normal random variables (more on this below).

## Independence and Functions

**Theorem.** If $X \perp Y$ and $g, h$ are (measurable) functions, then $g(X) \perp h(Y)$.

**Proof.** For any sets $A, B$:

$$P(g(X) \in A,\ h(Y) \in B) = P(X \in g^{-1}(A),\ Y \in h^{-1}(B))$$

$$= P(X \in g^{-1}(A)), P(Y \in h^{-1}(B)) = P(g(X) \in A), P(h(Y) \in B) \quad \square$$

Independence is preserved under functions. If $X$ and $Y$ are independent, then so are $X^2$ and $\sin(Y)$, or $e^X$ and $\log|Y|$, or any other measurable functions.

This also gives us: if $X \perp Y$, then $\mathbb{E}[g(X) h(Y)] = \mathbb{E}[g(X)], \mathbb{E}[h(Y)]$ for any $g, h$ — a far stronger statement than just $\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$.

## Variance of a Sum

For any $X, Y$:

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\text{Cov}(X, Y)$$

If $X \perp Y$, then $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$, so:

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$$

Variance is additive over independent random variables. This extends to any finite sum:

$$X_1, \ldots, X_n \text{ independent} \implies \text{Var}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i)$$

Note the contrast with expectation: $\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y]$ **always**, regardless of dependence. Variance additivity requires independence (or at least uncorrelatedness).

## Mutual Independence vs Pairwise Independence

For more than two random variables, there are two distinct notions.

**Pairwise independence:** $X_i \perp X_j$ for all $i \neq j$.

**Mutual independence:** For all subsets ${i_1, \ldots, i_k} \subseteq {1, \ldots, n}$ and all $x_{i_1}, \ldots, x_{i_k}$:

$$P(X_{i_1} \leq x_{i_1}, \ldots, X_{i_k} \leq x_{i_k}) = \prod_{j=1}^k P(X_{i_j} \leq x_{i_j})$$

The joint CDF factors for every subset, not just every pair.

$$\text{Mutually independent} \implies \text{Pairwise independent}$$ $$\text{Pairwise independent} \not\implies \text{Mutually independent}$$

### Counterexample: Pairwise but not Mutually Independent

Let $X_1, X_2 \sim \text{Bernoulli}(1/2)$ be independent, and let $X_3 = X_1 \oplus X_2$ (XOR, i.e., $X_3 = 1$ iff $X_1 \neq X_2$).

Check pairwise: $P(X_1 = 1, X_3 = 1) = P(X_1 = 1, X_2 = 0) = 1/4 = P(X_1=1)P(X_3=1)$. Similarly for all pairs. Pairwise independent. ✓

Check mutual: $P(X_1 = 1, X_2 = 1, X_3 = 1) = P(X_1 = 1, X_2 = 1, X_1 \oplus X_2 = 1) = 0$,

but $P(X_1=1)P(X_2=1)P(X_3=1) = 1/8 \neq 0$.

Not mutually independent. ✗

> Knowing $X_1$ and $X_2$ together tells you $X_3$ exactly — there is complete three-way dependence even though no pair is dependent. The joint structure is richer than pairwise relationships can see.

**When we say $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent in practice, we always mean mutually independent.** Pairwise is not enough for the standard results (variance additivity for $n > 2$, factoring joint distributions, etc.).

## i.i.d. — Independent and Identically Distributed

The workhorse assumption of statistics. $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are **i.i.d.** if:

1. They are mutually independent
2. They all have the same distribution: $F_{X_i} = F$ for all $i$

This is the setup for the Law of Large Numbers, the Central Limit Theorem, most of estimation theory, and most of hypothesis testing. It is almost never exactly true in applications and almost always assumed anyway. Knowing when it's a safe approximation vs. a dangerous one is most of applied statistics.

---

## The Jointly Normal Special Case

For jointly normal random variables, the equivalence flips:

$$X, Y \text{ jointly normal and uncorrelated} \iff X \perp Y$$

This is the one case where uncorrelated implies independent. It follows from the structure of the multivariate normal: the joint PDF is:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_X\sigma_Y\sqrt{1-\rho^2}} \exp\left(-\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)}\left[\frac{(x-\mu_X)^2}{\sigma_X^2} - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_X)(y-\mu_Y)}{\sigma_X\sigma_Y} + \frac{(y-\mu_Y)^2}{\sigma_Y^2}\right]\right)$$

When $\rho = \text{Corr}(X,Y) = 0$, the cross term vanishes and the PDF factors into $f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$ exactly. This is special to the normal — the covariance is the only parameter governing dependence, so zero covariance means no dependence.

> **Warning:** This requires _joint_ normality. $X$ normal and $Y$ normal does not make $(X, Y)$ jointly normal. There exist pairs of normal marginals with any dependence structure. "Both marginals are normal" is much weaker than "jointly normal."

## Conditional Independence

$X$ and $Y$ are **conditionally independent given $Z$**, written $X \perp Y \mid Z$, if:

$$P(X \leq x,\ Y \leq y \mid Z = z) = P(X \leq x \mid Z = z), P(Y \leq y \mid Z = z) \quad \forall, x, y, z$$

Knowing $Z$, $X$ and $Y$ carry no additional information about each other.

Conditional independence and (unconditional) independence are logically unrelated:

- $X \perp Y$ does **not** imply $X \perp Y \mid Z$
- $X \perp Y \mid Z$ does **not** imply $X \perp Y$

### Example: $X \perp Y$ but not $X \perp Y \mid Z$

$X, Y \sim \text{Bernoulli}(1/2)$ independent. Let $Z = X + Y$.

$X \perp Y$ unconditionally. ✓

But given $Z = 1$: $P(X = 1 \mid Z = 1) = 1/2$, yet $P(X = 1 \mid Z = 1, Y = 1) = 0 \neq 1/2$.

Conditioning on $Z$ creates dependence between $X$ and $Y$ — $Z$ is a **collider**. Knowing the sum and one summand determines the other.

### Example: $X \perp Y \mid Z$ but not $X \perp Y$

Let $Z \sim \text{Bernoulli}(1/2)$, and given $Z$, let $X \mid Z$ and $Y \mid Z$ be independent $\text{Bernoulli}(Z/2 + 1/4)$.

Given $Z$, $X$ and $Y$ are independent (both just coins with the same bias, but conditionally independent). Marginally, $X$ and $Y$ are correlated because they both depend on the shared $Z$. Conditioning on $Z$ blocks the path $X \leftarrow Z \rightarrow Y$ and restores independence.

> This is the **common cause** structure — $Z$ is a confounder. Conditioning on it makes $X$ and $Y$ independent. This is exactly the structure that Simpson's Paradox exploits, and that causal graphs formalize.

## Summary

| Property                           | Meaning                          | Strength                         |
| ---------------------------------- | -------------------------------- | -------------------------------- |
| Uncorrelated                       | $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$            | Weakest — one number             |
| Pairwise independent               | $X_i \perp X_j$ for all pairs    | Stronger — but not enough        |
| Mutually independent               | Joint distribution fully factors | Strong — the standard assumption |
| Conditionally independent $\mid Z$ | Independent within levels of $Z$ | Different axis entirely          |
