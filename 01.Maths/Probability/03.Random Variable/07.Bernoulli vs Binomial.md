## Why Bother Writing This Down?

Bernoulli and Binomial are the first distributions most people encounter. Because of that, they're often taught too fast, with too little rigour, and the deep structure gets buried under "just memorise the PMF."

What I actually want to understand:

1. What is a Bernoulli trial, precisely, as a random variable?
2. How does the Binomial emerge from summing Bernoullis, and what conditions make that sum work out so cleanly?
3. Where do the mean and variance formulas actually come from — not just stated, but _derived_?
4. What does the MGF tell us, and why is it the natural tool for proving the sum result?
5. What breaks if the trials aren't independent, or don't share the same $p$?

This is the note I'll come back to before anything involving indicator random variables, which is basically everything in Stat 110.

## § 1 — The Bernoulli Distribution: Precise Definition

### The model

A **Bernoulli trial** is a single experiment with exactly two outcomes. We encode them as numbers:

$$X = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{with probability } p \ 0 & \text{with probability } 1 - p =: q \end{cases}$$

where $p \in [0, 1]$ is called the **success probability**.

We write $X \sim \text{Bern}(p)$.

> [!NOTE] The choice to encode outcomes as 1 and 0 (rather than, say, 1 and −1) is not arbitrary. It makes the algebra work beautifully: $\mathbb{E}[X] = p$, $X^2 = X$, and indicator variables become additive. The encoding 1/−1 is used elsewhere (e.g. in the random walk model), but for counting purposes, 0/1 is canonical.

### PMF

$$\mathbb{P}(X = k) = p^k (1-p)^{1-k} \qquad k \in {0, 1}$$

This is a compact way to write both cases at once. Check: $k=1$ gives $p^1 q^0 = p$. $k=0$ gives $p^0 q^1 = q$. ✓

### The support

$X$ takes values in ${0, 1}$ only. The entire probability mass lives on two points. This seems trivial, but it means Bernoulli is the simplest possible non-degenerate discrete distribution.

---

## § 2 — Bernoulli: Mean, Variance, MGF

### Expected value

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = 1 \cdot p + 0 \cdot q = p$$

No surprises.

### Second moment and variance

A key property of indicator variables: since $X \in {0,1}$, we have $X^2 = X$ always (both $0^2 = 0$ and $1^2 = 1$). Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[X] = p$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = p - p^2 = p(1-p) = pq$$

> [!IMPORTANT] The fact that $X^2 = X$ is not a coincidence — it's the defining property of an **idempotent**, and it's the algebraic reason why indicator random variables are so well-behaved. Any time you see a ${0,1}$-valued variable, remember this trick immediately.

### Moment generating function

$$M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = e^{t \cdot 0} \cdot q + e^{t \cdot 1} \cdot p = q + pe^t$$

So:

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = q + pe^t = 1 - p + pe^t}$$

This is a weighted average of $1$ (contribution of $X=0$) and $e^t$ (contribution of $X=1$). Clean and memorable.

**Verify moments from MGF:**

$M_X'(t) = pe^t \implies M_X'(0) = p = \mathbb{E}[X]$ ✓

$M_X''(t) = pe^t \implies M_X''(0) = p = \mathbb{E}[X^2]$ ✓

$\text{Var}(X) = p - p^2 = pq$ ✓

## § 3 — The Binomial Distribution: Precise Definition

### The model

Run $n$ independent Bernoulli trials, each with the same success probability $p$. Let

$$X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bern}(p)$$

Define the **total number of successes**:

$$Y = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$$

We say $Y \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$.

> [!IMPORTANT] The two conditions **independent** and **identically distributed** are both load-bearing. If trials are dependent, the sum is not Binomial. If they have different $p_i$, the sum is not Binomial (it's Poisson-Binomial). Both conditions must hold. I'll return to what happens when they fail in § 7.

### PMF — Derivation from first principles

We want $\mathbb{P}(Y = k)$ for $k \in {0, 1, \ldots, n}$.

A specific sequence of outcomes with exactly $k$ successes and $n-k$ failures looks like:

$$(\underbrace{1, 1, \ldots, 1}_{k}, \underbrace{0, 0, \ldots, 0}_{n-k})$$

By independence, each such specific sequence has probability:

$$p^k \cdot (1-p)^{n-k} = p^k q^{n-k}$$

Now, how many such sequences are there? We need to choose which $k$ of the $n$ positions are successes — that's $\binom{n}{k}$ choices, and each gives a distinct sequence.

Since the sequences are mutually exclusive (different arrangements of outcomes):

$$\mathbb{P}(Y = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{P}(Y = k) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}} \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$$

### Sanity check — probabilities sum to 1

$$\sum_{k=0}^{n} \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k} = (p + q)^n = 1^n = 1 \quad \checkmark$$

by the Binomial Theorem. This is actually where the distribution gets its name — the PMF values are exactly the terms in the binomial expansion of $(p+q)^n$.

## § 4 — Binomial: Mean, Variance, MGF

### Method 1 — Direct computation (painful but instructive once)

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_{k=0}^{n} k \binom{n}{k} p^k q^{n-k}$$

Use the identity $k\binom{n}{k} = n\binom{n-1}{k-1}$:

$$= \sum_{k=1}^{n} n \binom{n-1}{k-1} p^k q^{n-k} = np \sum_{k=1}^{n} \binom{n-1}{k-1} p^{k-1} q^{n-k}$$

Substitute $j = k - 1$:

$$= np \sum_{j=0}^{n-1} \binom{n-1}{j} p^j q^{n-1-j} = np(p+q)^{n-1} = np$$

So $\mathbb{E}[Y] = np$. ✓

This works, but it's algebraically messy. There's a much better way.

---

### Method 2 — Linearity of expectation (the right way)

Since $Y = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$ and each $X_i \sim \text{Bern}(p)$:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \mathbb{E}!\left[\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\right] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{E}[X_i] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} p = np$$

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[Y] = np}$$

One line. Linearity of expectation requires no independence — it holds for any sum of random variables.

### Variance via independence

For variance, we do need independence. Since the $X_i$ are independent:

$$\text{Var}(Y) = \text{Var}!\left(\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{Var}(X_i) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} pq = npq$$

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(Y) = npq}$$

> [!NOTE] This is the step where independence actually does real work. For any random variables $X_i$ (independent or not): $$\text{Var}!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) + 2\sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$$ Independence kills the covariance terms. If the trials are dependent (say, drawing cards without replacement), those cross terms survive and Var$(Y) \neq npq$.

### MGF of the Binomial

Since $Y = \sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$ with independent $X_i$:

$$M_Y(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tY}] = \mathbb{E}!\left[e^{t\sum_{i=1}^n X_i}\right] = \mathbb{E}!\left[\prod_{i=1}^{n} e^{tX_i}\right]$$

By independence, the expectation of a product equals the product of expectations:

$$= \prod_{i=1}^{n} \mathbb{E}[e^{tX_i}] = \prod_{i=1}^{n} M_{X_i}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} (q + pe^t)$$

$$\boxed{M_Y(t) = (q + pe^t)^n}$$

This is just the Bernoulli MGF raised to the power $n$ — exactly what you'd expect from $n$ independent copies.

**Verify mean from MGF:**

$$M_Y'(t) = n(q + pe^t)^{n-1} \cdot pe^t$$

$$M_Y'(0) = n(q + p)^{n-1} \cdot p = n \cdot 1 \cdot p = np \quad \checkmark$$

## § 5 — The Relationship: "Binomial is a Sum of Bernoullis"

Let me state this cleanly as a theorem, because it's the core of everything.

**Theorem.** If $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bern}(p)$, then $Y = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$.

**Proof via MGFs.** We computed $M_Y(t) = (q + pe^t)^n$. This is exactly the MGF of a $\text{Bin}(n,p)$ distribution. Since MGFs uniquely determine distributions (when they exist in a neighbourhood of 0, which they do here), $Y \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$. $\blacksquare$

**Converse.** If $Y \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$, can we always write $Y = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ for iid Bernoullis? Yes — just take $X_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{trial } i \text{ is a success}]$. The construction is canonical.

> [!TIP] This decomposition — writing a Binomial as a sum of indicators — is one of the most useful tricks in all of probability. Blitzstein calls it "the Binomial as a sum of indicators" and uses it relentlessly. Whenever you see a Binomial, ask yourself: _what is the indicator random variable here, and what is it indicating?_

### The additive property

A direct consequence: if $Y_1 \sim \text{Bin}(n_1, p)$ and $Y_2 \sim \text{Bin}(n_2, p)$ are independent (same $p$!), then:

$$Y_1 + Y_2 \sim \text{Bin}(n_1 + n_2, p)$$

**Proof via MGFs:**

$$M_{Y_1 + Y_2}(t) = M_{Y_1}(t) \cdot M_{Y_2}(t) = (q+pe^t)^{n_1} \cdot (q+pe^t)^{n_2} = (q+pe^t)^{n_1+n_2}$$

This is the MGF of $\text{Bin}(n_1 + n_2, p)$. $\blacksquare$

> [!NOTE] The condition that both have the _same_ $p$ is essential here. $\text{Bin}(n_1, p_1) + \text{Bin}(n_2, p_2)$ with $p_1 \neq p_2$ is **not** Binomial. The MGFs multiply to $(q_1 + p_1 e^t)^{n_1}(q_2 + p_2 e^t)^{n_2}$, which doesn't factorise as a single $(q + pe^t)^N$ for any $q, p, N$.

## § 6 — Side-by-Side Comparison

| Property         | Bernoulli$(p)$            | Binomial$(n,p)$                        |
| ---------------- | ------------------------- | -------------------------------------- |
| **Parameters**   | $p \in [0,1]$             | $n \in \mathbb{Z}_{>0}$, $p \in [0,1]$ |
| **Support**      | ${0, 1}$                  | ${0, 1, \ldots, n}$                    |
| **PMF**          | $p^k(1-p)^{1-k}$          | $\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$           |
| **Mean**         | $p$                       | $np$                                   |
| **Variance**     | $pq$                      | $npq$                                  |
| **MGF**          | $q + pe^t$                | $(q + pe^t)^n$                         |
| **Relationship** | Special case: Bin$(1, p)$ | Sum of $n$ iid Bern$(p)$               |
| **Skewness**     | $\frac{1-2p}{\sqrt{pq}}$  | $\frac{1-2p}{\sqrt{npq}}$              |

> [!NOTE] Bernoulli$(p)$ = Binomial$(1, p)$. Every formula for the Binomial reduces to the Bernoulli when you plug in $n = 1$. They are not two separate distributions — Bernoulli is a special case. I find it helpful to always derive Bernoulli results first and then check that the Binomial formula recovers them at $n=1$.

## § 7 — What Breaks Without iid?

It's worth understanding precisely which properties fail when the iid assumption is violated.

### Case 1: Dependent trials, same marginal $p$

Example: draw $n$ cards without replacement from a deck with $Np$ red cards and $N(1-p)$ blue cards. Each draw is still Bern$(p)$ marginally, but the draws are negatively correlated.

- $\mathbb{E}[Y] = np$ still holds (linearity of expectation doesn't need independence).
- $\text{Var}(Y) = npq \cdot \frac{N-n}{N-1} < npq$ — the covariance terms are negative, so variance is _smaller_ than Binomial. This is the **Hypergeometric** distribution.
- The PMF is no longer Binomial.

### Case 2: Independent trials, different $p_i$

Each $X_i \sim \text{Bern}(p_i)$ independently, but $p_i$ vary. Then $Y = \sum X_i$ has:

- $\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum p_i$ (linearity)
- $\text{Var}(Y) = \sum p_i q_i$ (independence)
- PMF is the **Poisson-Binomial** distribution — complicated convolution, no closed form in general.
- MGF: $M_Y(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n (q_i + p_i e^t)$ — a product of distinct linear factors, not a perfect $n$th power.

> [!IMPORTANT] The Binomial is special precisely because all the $p_i$ are equal. That's what collapses the product MGF $\prod(q_i + p_i e^t)$ into the clean form $(q + pe^t)^n$. Equality of parameters = structural simplification. This pattern recurs throughout probability — equal parameters always produce the most tractable distributions.

## § 8 — The Normal Approximation (Where This All Leads)

The Central Limit Theorem says: for large $n$, a standardised Binomial converges in distribution to a standard Normal.

Precisely, if $Y \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$:

$$\frac{Y - np}{\sqrt{npq}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

**Why?** Because $Y = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ is a sum of iid random variables with mean $p$ and variance $pq$, so the CLT applies directly. The decomposition into iid Bernoullis is precisely what makes the CLT applicable.

The MGF proof: show that the MGF of $\frac{Y - np}{\sqrt{npq}}$ converges pointwise to $e^{t^2/2}$ (the standard Normal MGF). This requires Taylor-expanding the Bernoulli MGF and taking $n \to \infty$ carefully.

> [!TIP] This is why the mean $np$ and variance $npq$ matter so much — they're the centring and scaling constants for the CLT approximation. In practice, the approximation is reasonable when $np \geq 5$ and $nq \geq 5$. For small $p$ and large $n$, use the Poisson approximation instead (Poisson limit theorem).

## § 9 — Indicator Random Variables: The Real Reason This Matters

The Bernoulli distribution is the formalism behind **indicator random variables**, which Blitzstein considers the single most useful tool in discrete probability.

For any event $A$, define:

$$\mathbf{1}_A = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } A \text{ occurs} \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Then $\mathbf{1}_A \sim \text{Bern}(\mathbb{P}(A))$, and crucially:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_A] = \mathbb{P}(A)$$

This identity — **expectation of an indicator equals probability of the event** — is used constantly. It turns difficult probability calculations into expectation calculations, and expectation calculations are often much more tractable via linearity.

**Example: Expected number of fixed points in a random permutation.**

Let $Y$ = number of fixed points (positions $i$ where $\sigma(i) = i$) in a uniformly random permutation of ${1,\ldots,n}$. Write:

$$Y = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \mathbf{1}[\sigma(i) = i]$$

Each $\mathbf{1}[\sigma(i) = i] \sim \text{Bern}(1/n)$ (by symmetry, each position is fixed with probability $1/n$). By linearity:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{1}{n} = 1$$

No combinatorics needed. The answer is 1 regardless of $n$. This is the power of the indicator method.

> [!IMPORTANT] Notice the $X_i$ here are **not independent** (if $n-1$ positions are fixed, the last one must be too). But we didn't need independence for the expectation — linearity is unconditional. The Binomial structure breaks down (we can't say $Y \sim \text{Bin}(n, 1/n)$), but the mean calculation works fine. This distinction between "same mean as a Binomial" and "actually Binomial" is worth internalising.
