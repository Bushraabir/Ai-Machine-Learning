
> [!note] Why This Note Exists You have seen Bernoulli and Binomial separately. This note exists for one purpose: to make the structural relationship between them so clear that every formula in both notes becomes obvious rather than memorised.
> 
> The central fact is this: $$\text{Bin}(n, p) = \underbrace{\text{Bern}(p) + \text{Bern}(p) + \cdots + \text{Bern}(p)}_{n \text{ independent copies}}$$ Every comparison below is a precise unpacking of that single sentence.

---

## The Structural Relationship

Bernoulli is not a special case of Binomial in the way a square is a special case of a rectangle — something degenerate and simpler. It is the **atom** from which Binomial is built.

The correct mental model:

```
Bernoulli(p)     = one trial,  tracks success/failure
Binomial(n, p)   = n trials,   tracks count of successes
```

And the decomposition $X = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n$ is not notation — it is the proof engine for every result in both distributions. Mean, variance, MGF, CLT convergence — all fall out of it.

The special case direction also holds: $\text{Bin}(1, p) = \text{Bern}(p)$.

Use this as a sanity check on every Binomial formula. If it does not reduce to the Bernoulli result at $n = 1$, it is wrong.

## Side-by-Side Properties

|Property|Bernoulli$(p)$|Binomial$(n, p)$|How Binomial Emerges|
|---|---|---|---|
|Parameters|$p \in [0,1]$|$n \in \mathbb{N},\ p \in [0,1]$|One extra parameter: trial count|
|Support|${0, 1}$|${0, 1, \ldots, n}$|$n$ atoms instead of 2|
|PMF|$p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$|$\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$|$\binom{n}{k}$ counts arrangements|
|Mean|$p$|$np$|Linearity: $\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i] = np$|
|Variance|$p(1-p)$|$np(1-p)$|Independence: $\sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = np(1-p)$|
|MGF|$1 - p + pe^t$|$(1-p+pe^t)^n$|Independence: product of MGFs collapses to a power|
|Skewness|$\dfrac{1-2p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}$|$\dfrac{1-2p}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}$|Third cumulant scales as $n$, $\sigma^3$ as $n^{3/2}$: divide by $\sqrt{n}$|
|Excess Kurtosis|$\dfrac{1-6p(1-p)}{p(1-p)}$|$\dfrac{1-6p(1-p)}{np(1-p)}$|Fourth cumulant scales as $n$, $\sigma^4$ as $n^2$: divide by $n$|

The pattern is exact: every Binomial formula is the Bernoulli formula scaled by $n$ in the appropriate sense. Mean scales linearly. Variance scales linearly. Skewness scales as $1/\sqrt{n}$. Kurtosis scales as $1/n$. This is not coincidence — it is cumulant scaling, and it holds for the sum of any iid sequence.

## What Each Condition Buys You

The decomposition $X = \sum X_i$ is only as strong as the assumptions behind it. Here is what each assumption purchases:

|Assumption|What it gives you|
|---|---|
|Nothing (linearity alone)|$E[X] = np$ always holds|
|Independence|$\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p)$; MGF factorises as a product|
|Identical $p$|MGF product collapses to $(1-p+pe^t)^n$; PMF has closed form|
|Both (iid)|$X \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$ exactly|

This table is the most important thing in this note. It tells you exactly which step in any derivation requires which assumption — and therefore which step breaks first when you move to a more general setting.

Mean needs nothing. Variance needs independence. The Binomial structure itself needs both.

## Why the PMF Gains a Binomial Coefficient

The Bernoulli PMF $p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$ gives the probability of one specific sequence of outcomes.

When you sum $n$ Bernoullis and ask for $P(X = k)$, you are asking: across all possible orderings of $k$ successes and $n-k$ failures, what is the total probability?

Each specific ordering has probability $p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$ (by independence). There are $\binom{n}{k}$ such orderings (by combinatorics). They are mutually exclusive. So you add:

$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} \cdot p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$$

The $\binom{n}{k}$ is absent in Bernoulli because there is only one trial — there is only one arrangement of $k$ successes in 1 trial.

---

## Why the MGF Becomes a Power
For independent random variables, the MGF of a sum is the product of MGFs: $M_{X_1 + \cdots + X_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{X_i}(t)$
When all $X_i$ are identically distributed with MGF $M(t) = 1 - p + pe^t$, the product collapses: $\prod_{i=1}^n (1 - p + pe^t) = (1 - p + pe^t)^n$

The power $n$ is the entire content of the Bernoulli-to-Binomial transition in MGF language. If the $p_i$ differ across trials, the product does not collapse and you get the Poisson-Binomial — a strictly harder object with no closed-form PMF.

## Where the Skewness and Kurtosis Formulas Come From

Both distributions have the same numerator structure. The denominator changes because $\sigma$ depends on $n$.

For skewness: the third central moment of $X = \sum X_i$ scales as $n$ (cumulants are additive for independent variables). The denominator $\sigma^3 = [np(1-p)]^{3/2}$ scales as $n^{3/2}$. So:

$$\gamma_1(\text{Bin}) = \frac{n \cdot p(1-p)(1-2p)}{[np(1-p)]^{3/2}} = \frac{1-2p}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} = \frac{\gamma_1(\text{Bern})}{\sqrt{n}}$$

For kurtosis: the fourth cumulant scales as $n$, $\sigma^4$ scales as $n^2$:

$$\gamma_2(\text{Bin}) = \frac{\gamma_2(\text{Bern})}{n}$$

Both vanish as $n \to \infty$ — the distribution becomes symmetric and mesokurtic, converging to Normal. This is the Central Limit Theorem expressed at the level of moments.

## What Breaks Without iid

### Dependent trials, same marginal $p$ — Hypergeometric

Draw $n$ cards without replacement from a deck with $Np$ red cards. Each draw is still $\text{Bern}(p)$ marginally, but draws are negatively correlated.

- $E[X] = np$ — unchanged, linearity needs no independence
- $\text{Var}(X) = np(1-p) \cdot \dfrac{N-n}{N-1}$ — covariance terms survive and reduce variance
- PMF is not Binomial — this is the Hypergeometric distribution

The factor $\frac{N-n}{N-1}$ is the finite population correction. As $N \to \infty$, it tends to 1 and Hypergeometric converges to Binomial.

### Independent trials, unequal $p_i$ — Poisson-Binomial

Each $X_i \sim \text{Bern}(p_i)$ independently but with varying $p_i$.

- $E[X] = \sum p_i$ — holds
- $\text{Var}(X) = \sum p_i(1-p_i)$ — holds
- $M_X(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n (1 - p_i + p_i e^t)$ — does not collapse to a single power
- PMF: Poisson-Binomial distribution, no closed form

The Binomial is special precisely because equal parameters collapse the product. This pattern — equal parameters enabling closed forms — recurs across all of probability theory.

## Indicator Variables: The Bridge

The deepest reason to understand Bernoulli is not the distribution itself. It is what Bernoulli enables: indicator random variables.

For any event $A$:

$$\mathbf{1}_A \sim \text{Bern}(P(A)), \qquad E[\mathbf{1}_A] = P(A)$$

Expectation of an indicator equals the probability of the event. This converts probability calculations into expectation calculations, which are tractable via linearity.

Binomial arises naturally when you define $X = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ for iid events $A_i$ — the count of how many occur. The indicator representation is not just a derivation tool. It is the correct way to think about what a Binomial random variable is.

> [!warning] The Subtle Distinction If the events $A_i$ are not independent, $X = \sum \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ still has mean $\sum P(A_i)$ by linearity, but $X$ is not Binomial and its variance is not $np(1-p)$.
> 
> "Same mean as a Binomial" is not the same as "actually Binomial." Know which tool you are using and what it requires.

## The Path to CLT

The iid decomposition $X = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ is precisely the structure the Central Limit Theorem requires.

$$\frac{X - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

The centring constant $np$ and scaling constant $\sqrt{np(1-p)}$ are the mean and standard deviation derived from the indicator decomposition. The CLT is not an independent result sitting on top of the Binomial — it is the natural limit of the Bernoulli summation structure as $n$ grows.

The moment-level version of this: skewness $\sim 1/\sqrt{n} \to 0$ and excess kurtosis $\sim 1/n \to 0$. The distribution sheds asymmetry and heavy tails as it converges to Normal.

## The $n = 1$ Sanity Check

Every Binomial formula must reduce to the Bernoulli formula at $n = 1$:

|Formula|At $n=1$|Bernoulli|
|---|---|---|
|Mean: $np$|$p$|$p$|
|Variance: $np(1-p)$|$p(1-p)$|$p(1-p)$|
|MGF: $(1-p+pe^t)^n$|$1-p+pe^t$|$1-p+pe^t$|
|Skewness: $\frac{1-2p}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}$|$\frac{1-2p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}$|$\frac{1-2p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}$|
|PMF: $\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$|$p^k(1-p)^{1-k}$|$p^k(1-p)^{1-k}$|

If any formula fails this check, it is wrong.

## Connection to MSE and Risk

From the [[MSE and Risk]] note: for estimating $p$ from $n$ Bernoulli trials, the sample proportion $\hat{p} = X/n$ satisfies:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{p}) = \frac{p(1-p)}{n}$$

This is the $\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \sigma^2/n$ result applied to Bernoulli. The variance shrinks as $1/n$ — adding trials reduces estimation uncertainty. The Binomial is the probability model underlying every proportion estimate in statistics.

The Cramér-Rao lower bound for estimating $p$ from $n$ iid Bernoulli trials equals exactly $p(1-p)/n$ — the sample proportion achieves the bound. It is the uniformly minimum variance unbiased estimator (UMVUE) for $p$.
