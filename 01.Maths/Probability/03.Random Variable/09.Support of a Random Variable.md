## The Definition — Discrete Case

If $X$ is a **discrete** random variable, its support is:

$$\text{supp}(X) = {x \in \mathbb{R} : P(X = x) > 0}$$

This is the set of all atoms — every value that $X$ hits with positive probability. Anything outside this set is a ghost; it contributes nothing.

**The canonical example.** Fair die: $X \in {1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6}$, each with probability $\frac{1}{6}$. The support is exactly ${1,2,3,4,5,6}$. The value $3.7$ is not in the support. Neither is $0$ or $7$.

---

## The Definition — Continuous Case

If $X$ is **continuous** with PDF $f_X$, the support is:

$$\text{supp}(X) = {x \in \mathbb{R} : f_X(x) > 0}$$

Note carefully: we are **not** asking for $P(X = x) > 0$ here — that is always zero for a continuous RV. We ask where the _density_ lives, not where point masses live.

**Example.** $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$: $f_X(x) = 1$ for $x \in [0,1]$ and $0$ otherwise. So $\text{supp}(X) = [0,1]$.

> **Personal note:** The endpoints are subtle. $f_X(0) = 1 > 0$, so $0$ is in the support. Yet $P(X = 0) = 0$. This is exactly the measure-theoretic flavor leaking through — the support of a measure is about _density_, not point mass. Don't confuse the two levels.

---

## The Deeper Picture — What Support Really Means

The support is the _effective domain_ of the distribution. Think of it this way:

- The PMF or PDF **vanishes** outside the support. Writing your normalization integral over all of $\mathbb{R}$ is fine mathematically, but the support tells you where the real action is.
- Any **expectation** computation collapses to the support: $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sum_{x \in \text{supp}(X)} g(x) P(X=x)$ or $\int_{\text{supp}(X)} g(x) f_X(x), dx$.
- Any **conditioning** argument will fundamentally change the support. This is where beginners get burned. $X | X > 3$ has support ${4,5,6}$ for the die, not the original ${1,\ldots,6}$.

## Common Supports to Internalize

| Distribution            | Support             |
| ----------------------- | ------------------- |
| Bernoulli$(p)$          | ${0,1}$             |
| Binomial$(n,p)$         | ${0, 1, \ldots, n}$ |
| Geometric$(p)$          | ${1, 2, 3, \ldots}$ |
| Poisson$(\lambda)$      | ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$ |
| Uniform$(a,b)$          | $[a, b]$            |
| Exponential$(\lambda)$  | $(0, \infty)$       |
| Normal$(\mu, \sigma^2)$ | $(-\infty, \infty)$ |
| Beta$(\alpha,\beta)$    | $(0,1)$             |
| Gamma$(\alpha,\beta)$   | $(0,\infty)$        |

> The Normal is one of the few distributions with full support on $\mathbb{R}$. The Exponential has support on $(0,\infty)$ — not $[0,\infty)$ — because $f(0) = \lambda e^{0} = \lambda > 0$... wait. Actually $f(0) = \lambda > 0$, so $0$ _is_ in the support. The open vs. closed distinction doesn't matter measure-theoretically (a single point has Lebesgue measure zero), but it's worth being careful when writing.

---

## Why the Strict Inequality $> 0$ Matters

The definition uses **strictly** greater than zero. This is intentional and meaningful:

- In the discrete case: any $x$ with $P(X=x) = 0$ is irrelevant — the RV never takes that value.
- In the continuous case: $f_X(x) = 0$ at a point means the density assigns no mass in any neighborhood of $x$ (loosely).

What if $f_X(x) = 0$ at a single isolated point inside an otherwise positive-density interval? Measure-theoretically, a single point has Lebesgue measure zero, so it doesn't affect any integral. The support is still the "essentially positive" set. Formally, the support of the measure $\mu_X$ induced by $X$ is the _smallest closed set_ $S$ such that $\mu_X(S^c) = 0$.

> I won't dive into measure theory fully here, but this is the rigorous version. The closed-set characterization explains why $\text{supp}(\text{Uniform}(0,1)) = [0,1]$ and not $(0,1)$ — the closure of the open interval is the closed interval.

## Support and the Normalization Condition

For a discrete RV: $\sum_{x \in \text{supp}(X)} P(X = x) = 1$

For a continuous RV: $\int_{\text{supp}(X)} f_X(x), dx = 1$

These are equivalent to the usual normalization over all of $\mathbb{R}$, since $f_X \equiv 0$ outside the support anyway. But writing the bounds explicitly signals that you _know_ where the distribution lives. This matters when computing expectations, MGFs, or any transform.

---

## Support in the Context of Conditional Distributions

This is where the concept earns its keep. Suppose $X \sim \text{Geometric}(p)$ (number of trials until first success), so $\text{supp}(X) = {1,2,3,\ldots}$.

Now condition on $X > 3$. The conditional support is ${4, 5, 6, \ldots}$. And the conditional PMF must re-normalize over _this_ smaller support:

$$P(X = k \mid X > 3) = \frac{P(X = k, X > 3)}{P(X > 3)} = \frac{(1-p)^{k-1}p}{(1-p)^3}, \quad k \geq 4$$

The Geometric distribution's memoryless property actually tells us this is the same as $P(X = k-3)$ — beautiful. But the point stands: **conditioning reshapes the support**.

---

## A Subtlety: Transformations and Support

If $Y = g(X)$, the support of $Y$ is the _image of the support of $X$ under $g$_:

$$\text{supp}(Y) = g(\text{supp}(X)) = {g(x) : x \in \text{supp}(X)}$$

**Example.** $X \sim \text{Uniform}(-1, 1)$, $Y = X^2$. Then:

$$\text{supp}(X) = (-1,1), \quad \text{supp}(Y) = [0,1)$$

Note: $g(x) = x^2$ maps $(-1,1) \to [0,1)$, not $(0,1)$, since $g(0) = 0$. The support of $Y$ includes $0$.

This matters enormously when I derive the PDF of $Y$ via the change-of-variables formula — I must restrict the formula to $\text{supp}(Y)$.

---

## Edge Cases to Remember:

**1. Zero probability $\neq$ outside the support (continuous case).** For continuous RVs, $P(X = x) = 0$ for every $x$, yet $x$ can still be in the support. The support is about the _density_, not point masses.

**2. The support can be a strange set.** In principle, $\text{supp}(X)$ can be any measurable subset of $\mathbb{R}$. For standard distributions it's an interval or a countable set, but don't assume this in general.

**3. Writing integrals.** When computing $\mathbb{E}[X]$ for $X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)$: write $\int_0^\infty x \lambda e^{-\lambda x},dx$, not $\int_{-\infty}^\infty$. The latter technically works (the integrand is zero on $(-\infty,0)$), but writing the correct bounds shows I understand the support and prevents mistakes when the integrand is something more complex.

**4. Discrete distributions on $\mathbb{N}$ vs $\mathbb{N}_0$.** Geometric (starting from 1) has support ${1,2,\ldots}$. Poisson has support ${0,1,2,\ldots}$. This difference matters when I write a sum — starting at $k=0$ vs $k=1$ is not cosmetic.
