A **Random Variable (RV)** is a function that **assigns a numerical value to each outcome** of a random experiment.

- **Formal:**  
$$
X: \Omega \rightarrow \mathbb{R}
$$ 
where $$\Omega $$ is the sample space.

---

## Random Variables are of 2 types:

### 1. Discrete Random Variable

- Takes **countable values**.  
- Examples: number of heads in 10 coin flips, number of cars passing a checkpoint.  
- Associated with a **PMF (Probability Mass Function)**.

$$
p_X(x) = P(X = x)
$$

### 2. Continuous Random Variable

- Takes **uncountable values** (any real number in an interval).  
- Examples: height of a person, time to complete a task.  
- Associated with a **PDF (Probability Density Function)**.

$$
f_X(x) \ge 0, \quad \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x) dx = 1
$$

- Probabilities computed as **area under the curve**:

$$
P(a \le X \le b) = \int_a^b f_X(x) dx
$$

---

## Support

The **support** of a random variable \(X\) is the set of values with **non-zero probability** (discrete) or **non-zero density** (continuous):

$$
\text{Support}(X) = \{ x : p_X(x) > 0 \} \quad \text{or} \quad \{ x : f_X(x) > 0 \}
$$

---

## Examples

### Example 1: Discrete RV

- Toss a coin 3 times; let \(X\) = number of heads.  
- Support: \(\{0,1,2,3\}\)  
- PMF:  
$$
p_X(x) = \binom{3}{x} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^3
$$

### Example 2: Continuous RV

- Let \(X\) = height of students in a class (in cm), modeled as continuous.  
- PDF: Normal distribution with mean 170 cm, SD 10 cm.  
$$
f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi 10^2}} e^{-(x-170)^2/(2\cdot10^2)}
$$

- Probability of a student being 160–180 cm tall:  
$$
P(160 \le X \le 180) = \int_{160}^{180} f_X(x) dx
$$

---

## Relation to PMF / PDF / CDF

- **Discrete RV:** PMF → CDF  
$$
F_X(x) = \sum_{t \le x} p_X(t)
$$

more detailed in [[11.CDF vs PMF]]

- **Continuous RV:** PDF → CDF  
$$
F_X(x) = \int_{-\infty}^{x} f_X(t) dt
$$

---

## Key Insights (Stats 110 perspective)

- Random Variables **transform outcomes into numerical values**, allowing computation of probabilities, expectations, and variances.  
- Discrete vs continuous distinction is crucial for **choosing PMF or PDF**.  
- All probability distributions, moments, and functions are built on **random variables**.
