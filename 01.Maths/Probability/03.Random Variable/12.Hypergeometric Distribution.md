> [!note] Big Picture The Hypergeometric distribution is what the Binomial **becomes when you cannot replace**. It is the honest model for sampling from a finite population — the one every statistician reaches for when independence fails. Every quality control problem, every card game probability, every clinical trial with a fixed pool of patients — this is the model. The Binomial is an approximation; the Hypergeometric is the truth.


## 1. The Setup — What Problem Are We Modelling?

We have a **finite population** of $N$ objects, partitioned into exactly two types:

- $K$ objects of type "success" (tagged, defective, red, whatever)
- $N - K$ objects of type "failure"

We draw a sample of $n$ objects **without replacement**. Let $X$ be the number of successes in the sample.

The three parameters are:

$$N = \text{population size}, \quad K = \text{number of successes in population}, \quad n = \text{sample size}$$

with constraints $N, K, n \in \mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$, $K \leq N$, $n \leq N$.

> **Personal note:** The moment you see "without replacement," your brain should immediately switch from Binomial to Hypergeometric. The Binomial assumes each draw is independent — that the act of drawing one ball does not change the composition of the urn. Without replacement, it does. The Hypergeometric is the exact model; the Binomial is the limit when $N \to \infty$.

---

## 2. The PMF — Derivation from First Principles

### Counting Argument

We want $P(X = k)$: the probability of drawing exactly $k$ successes and $n-k$ failures in a sample of $n$.

**How many ways to choose the sample of size $n$ from $N$ objects?**

$$\binom{N}{n}$$

**How many of these give exactly $k$ successes?** We must choose $k$ from the $K$ successes AND $n-k$ from the $N-K$ failures — independently:

$$\binom{K}{k} \cdot \binom{N-K}{n-k}$$

By the naive definition of probability (equally likely outcomes, since we draw uniformly at random):

$$\boxed{P(X = k) = \frac{\binom{K}{k}\binom{N-K}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}}}$$

### Why This is "Honest"

In the Binomial, we would write $\binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}$, treating each draw as independent with probability $p = K/N$ of success. But the probability of success on the second draw **changes** after the first draw:

- If first draw was success: $P(\text{success on 2nd}) = \frac{K-1}{N-1}$
- If first draw was failure: $P(\text{success on 2nd}) = \frac{K}{N-1}$

The Hypergeometric PMF handles all of this via combinatorics — no need to track order or condition step-by-step. The counting argument absorbs the dependence automatically.

### Validity: Does It Sum to 1?

We need:

$$\sum_{k} \binom{K}{k}\binom{N-K}{n-k} = \binom{N}{n}$$

This is **Vandermonde's Identity**:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{r} \binom{m}{k}\binom{n}{r-k} = \binom{m+n}{r}$$

applied with $m = K$, $n = N-K$, $r = n$. This is a combinatorial identity worth knowing cold — it comes up everywhere. Proof: count the number of ways to choose $n$ objects from $N = K + (N-K)$ objects, either directly ($\binom{N}{n}$) or by splitting the population into two groups and summing over all possible splits. $\checkmark$

## 3. Support

The support is not simply ${0, 1, \ldots, n}$. We need $k$ to be feasible — both binomial coefficients must be nonzero:

$$\binom{K}{k} > 0 \implies k \leq K$$ $$\binom{N-K}{n-k} > 0 \implies n - k \leq N - K \implies k \geq n - (N-K)$$

Therefore:

$$\text{supp}(X) = {\max(0,, n-(N-K)),, \ldots,, \min(n,, K)}$$

> **Personal note:** This is one of the things I always get sloppy about. If I have $N=10$, $K=3$, $n=8$, then the minimum number of successes I can draw is $\max(0, 8-7) = 1$ — I **must** draw at least 1 success because there are only 7 failures and I'm drawing 8. The support shrinks from both ends based on the problem constraints. Always check this before writing an expectation sum.

## 4. Expectation 

### Method: The Absorption Identity

Direct computation from the definition is algebraically brutal. The clean approach uses the **absorption identity** for binomial coefficients:

$$k \binom{K}{k} = K \binom{K-1}{k-1}$$

_Proof:_ $k \cdot \frac{K!}{k!(K-k)!} = K \cdot \frac{(K-1)!}{(k-1)!(K-k)!} = K\binom{K-1}{k-1}$. $\checkmark$

Now:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k} k \cdot \frac{\binom{K}{k}\binom{N-K}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}}$$

Apply the absorption identity to factor out $K$:

$$= \frac{K}{\binom{N}{n}} \sum_{k=1}^{\min(n,K)} \binom{K-1}{k-1}\binom{N-K}{n-k}$$

Re-index: let $j = k-1$, so $k-1$ ranges from $0$ to $\min(n,K)-1$:

$$= \frac{K}{\binom{N}{n}} \sum_{j=0}^{\min(n-1,K-1)} \binom{K-1}{j}\binom{N-K}{n-1-j}$$

The sum is Vandermonde's identity with parameters $K-1$, $N-K$, $n-1$:

$$\sum_{j} \binom{K-1}{j}\binom{N-K}{n-1-j} = \binom{N-1}{n-1}$$

Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{K \cdot \binom{N-1}{n-1}}{\binom{N}{n}}$$

Now simplify $\frac{\binom{N-1}{n-1}}{\binom{N}{n}}$:

$$\frac{\binom{N-1}{n-1}}{\binom{N}{n}} = \frac{(N-1)!/(n-1)!(N-n)!}{N!/n!(N-n)!} = \frac{(N-1)! \cdot n!}{N! \cdot (n-1)!} = \frac{n}{N}$$

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{nK}{N} = n \cdot \frac{K}{N}}$$

### Intuition

Let $p = K/N$ be the proportion of successes in the population. Then $\mathbb{E}[X] = np$ — **exactly the same as the Binomial**. This makes complete sense: if I draw $n$ objects and a fraction $p$ of the population is tagged, I should expect $np$ tagged objects on average, regardless of whether I sample with or without replacement. The expected value cannot distinguish the two sampling schemes; the variance will.

### Alternative: Indicator RV Method

Write $X = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n$ where $X_i = \mathbf{1}[\text{i-th draw is success}]$.
By **symmetry**, each draw is equally likely to be any of the $N$ objects:
$\mathbb{E}[X_i] = P(\text{i-th draw is success})=\frac{K}{N}$

(This holds even without replacement — each position is marginally a uniform draw from the population by symmetry.)

By linearity:
$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X_i] = n \cdot \frac{K}{N}$$

This is the slick Stat 110 approach. No combinatorial gymnastics. Use this in exams.

## 5. Variance 

### Strategy: Compute $\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)]$ Then Assemble

**Step 1: Compute $\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)]$** using the absorption identity twice:

$$k(k-1)\binom{K}{k} = K(K-1)\binom{K-2}{k-2}$$

$$\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] = \frac{K(K-1)}{\binom{N}{n}} \sum_{k=2} \binom{K-2}{k-2}\binom{N-K}{n-k}$$

Re-index $j = k-2$, apply Vandermonde with $K-2$, $N-K$, $n-2$:

$$= \frac{K(K-1)\binom{N-2}{n-2}}{\binom{N}{n}}$$

Simplify $\frac{\binom{N-2}{n-2}}{\binom{N}{n}}$:

$$\frac{\binom{N-2}{n-2}}{\binom{N}{n}} = \frac{n(n-1)}{N(N-1)}$$

So:

$$\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] = \frac{n(n-1)K(K-1)}{N(N-1)}$$

**Step 2: Assemble the variance.**

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = \mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] + \mathbb{E}[X] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$$

$$= \frac{n(n-1)K(K-1)}{N(N-1)} + \frac{nK}{N} - \frac{n^2K^2}{N^2}$$

Let $p = K/N$. After algebra (expand, find common denominator $N^2(N-1)$, collect):

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = n \cdot \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{N-K}{N} \cdot \frac{N-n}{N-1}}$$

### The Finite Population Correction Factor

Writing $p = K/N$ and $q = 1-p = (N-K)/N$:

$$\text{Var}(X) = npq \cdot \underbrace{\frac{N-n}{N-1}}_{\text{FPC}}$$

The term $\frac{N-n}{N-1}$ is the **Finite Population Correction (FPC)**. Compare:

| Model                   | Variance                    |
| ----------------------- | --------------------------- |
| Binomial$(n,p)$         | $npq$                       |
| Hypergeometric$(N,K,n)$ | $npq \cdot \frac{N-n}{N-1}$ |

The FPC is always $\leq 1$ (since $n \leq N$ implies $N-n \leq N-1$). So **the Hypergeometric always has smaller or equal variance than the Binomial with the same $n$ and $p$.**

> **Why does the FPC reduce variance?** Without replacement, the draws are _negatively correlated_ — drawing a success makes the next draw less likely to be a success. Negative correlation reduces variance. The FPC captures exactly how much the draws "know about each other" through the shared population.

**Boundary cases:**

- $n = 1$: FPC $= \frac{N-1}{N-1} = 1$ — sampling one item, no dependence effect, Hyp $=$ Binom. $\checkmark$
- $n = N$: FPC $= 0$ — sampling the whole population, $X = K$ with certainty, zero variance. $\checkmark$

## 6. Indicator Variance Derivation (The Covariance Path)

Write $X = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ as before. Then:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) + 2\sum_{i < j} \text{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$$

Each $X_i \sim \text{Bern}(K/N)$, so $\text{Var}(X_i) = p(1-p) = pq$.

For $i \neq j$:

$$\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = \mathbb{E}[X_i X_j] - \mathbb{E}[X_i]\mathbb{E}[X_j]$$

$$\mathbb{E}[X_i X_j] = P(\text{both draw $i$ and draw $j$ are success}) = \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{K-1}{N-1}$$

(By symmetry — pick two positions; probability both are successes.)

$$\text{Cov}(X_i, X_j) = \frac{K(K-1)}{N(N-1)} - \frac{K^2}{N^2} = -\frac{K(N-K)}{N^2(N-1)} = -\frac{pq}{N-1}$$

Negative — confirms the intuition above.

Total:

$$\text{Var}(X) = n \cdot pq + 2\binom{n}{2}\left(-\frac{pq}{N-1}\right) = npq - \frac{n(n-1)pq}{N-1} = npq\left(1 - \frac{n-1}{N-1}\right) = npq\cdot\frac{N-n}{N-1}$$

This derivation is far more illuminating than brute-force computation. The FPC emerges from negative covariance between draws. See [[Covariance and Correlation]].

---

## 7. Standard Deviation

$$\sigma = \sqrt{n \cdot \frac{K}{N} \cdot \frac{N-K}{N} \cdot \frac{N-n}{N-1}}$$

No surprises. The FPC appears here too, shrinking the standard deviation relative to Binomial.

---

## 8. CDF

There is no closed form for the Hypergeometric CDF. It is computed by summing the PMF:

$$F(k) = P(X \leq k) = \sum_{j = \max(0, n-(N-K))}^{\lfloor k \rfloor} \frac{\binom{K}{j}\binom{N-K}{n-j}}{\binom{N}{n}}$$

In practice: use software. In theory: the CDF is a regularized incomplete hypergeometric function. Not worth memorizing — understand the PMF and compute the CDF by summation.

---

## 9. Moment Generating Function

$$M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = \frac{\binom{N-K}{n}}{\binom{N}{n}} {}_2F_1!\left(-n, -K;, N-K-n+1;, e^t\right)$$

where ${}_2F_1$ is the Gauss hypergeometric function. This is where the distribution gets its name — the PMF's normalizing sum is a hypergeometric series. The MGF is not used in practice for this distribution; moment calculations via the absorption/indicator methods are far more tractable.

> **Personal note:** Don't memorize this. Know that it exists, know where the name comes from. If someone asks for the MGF in an exam they're either being cruel or they want the series definition. Neither is productive.

## 10. The Binomial Approximation

When $N$ is large relative to $n$ (say, $n/N \leq 0.05$), sampling without replacement is nearly the same as sampling with replacement, and:

$$\text{Hypergeometric}(N, K, n) \approx \text{Binomial}!\left(n,, \frac{K}{N}\right)$$

**Why?** When $N \gg n$, removing one object barely changes the composition. The FPC:

$$\frac{N-n}{N-1} = \frac{1 - n/N}{1 - 1/N} \to 1 \text{ as } N \to \infty$$

So the variances converge. And the PMF:

$$\frac{\binom{K}{k}\binom{N-K}{n-k}}{\binom{N}{n}} \to \binom{n}{k}\left(\frac{K}{N}\right)^k\left(1-\frac{K}{N}\right)^{n-k}$$

as $N \to \infty$ with $K/N \to p$. This is a clean limit theorem worth proving once. Proof: expand each binomial coefficient as falling factorials and take $N \to \infty$.

**Rule of thumb:** Use Hypergeometric when $n/N > 0.05$. Use Binomial approximation when $n/N \leq 0.05$.

## 12. Worked Example

> [!example] Quality Control A shipment of $N = 20$ light bulbs contains $K = 5$ defective ones. An inspector draws $n = 4$ bulbs at random without replacement. Let $X$ = number of defective bulbs in the sample.
> 
> **Support:** $$\max(0,, 4 - 15) = 0, \quad \min(4,, 5) = 4$$ $$\text{supp}(X) = {0, 1, 2, 3, 4}$$
> 
> **PMF at $k=1$:** $$P(X=1) = \frac{\binom{5}{1}\binom{15}{3}}{\binom{20}{4}} = \frac{5 \cdot 455}{4845} = \frac{2275}{4845} \approx 0.4696$$
> 
> **Expectation:** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{4 \cdot 5}{20} = 1$$
> 
> **Variance:** $$\text{Var}(X) = 4 \cdot \frac{5}{20} \cdot \frac{15}{20} \cdot \frac{20-4}{20-1} = 4 \cdot \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{16}{19} = \frac{12}{19} \approx 0.632$$
> 
> **Compare to Binomial$(4, 0.25)$ variance:** $4 \cdot 0.25 \cdot 0.75 = 0.75 > 0.632$.
> 
> The FPC $= 16/19 \approx 0.842$ — sampling $4$ out of $20$ is significant enough to reduce variance meaningfully.

## 13. Why This Distribution Is Underrated

Most intro courses rush through the Hypergeometric on the way to the Binomial. But the Hypergeometric is the _correct_ model for finite populations, and the Binomial is the approximation. The Hypergeometric forces you to think about:

- **Dependence:** Without replacement means draws are not independent. The Hypergeometric is a distribution over _dependent_ indicators — the variance derivation via covariance makes this explicit.
- **Population size matters:** $n/N$ is the sampling fraction. Large sampling fractions fundamentally change the uncertainty calculus. Survey sampling, clinical trials with fixed enrollment pools, randomized controlled experiments — all require the FPC.
- **Vandermonde's Identity:** The PMF normalization is a beautiful combinatorial identity that recurs throughout probability theory.
- **Exponential family:** Unlike Binomial, Hypergeometric is **not** an exponential family (the support depends on the parameters). This is why certain standard statistical machinery breaks down and must be handled separately.
