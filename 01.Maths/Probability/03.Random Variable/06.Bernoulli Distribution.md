> [!note] Big Picture The Bernoulli distribution is the **atomic unit** of probability theory. Every model involving binary outcomes — coin flips, spam/not-spam, disease/healthy, click/no-click — begins here. Master this completely. Everything from Binomial to Logistic Regression to Neural Network output layers is built on top of it.


## What Problem Are We Modelling?

![[Pasted image 20260403163644.png]]

We have a single experiment with **exactly two possible outcomes**:
- **Success** — coded as $X = 1$
- **Failure** — coded as $X = 0$

The coding ${0, 1}$ is not arbitrary. It is chosen precisely so that algebraic expressions for expectation and variance come out cleanly. You'll see why below.

We define one parameter: $p = P(\text{success}) = P(X = 1), \qquad p \in [0, 1]$
And immediately: $P(\text{failure}) = P(X = 0) = 1 - p$

This is forced : the two probabilities must sum to 1. There is no other free parameter.

---

## PMF
- ### The Piecewise Form (Naive)
	The most honest way to write the PMF is: $P(X = x) = \begin{cases} p & \text{if } x = 1 \\ 1 - p & \text{if } x = 0 \end{cases}$

	This is perfectly correct but clunky — two cases, hard to manipulate algebraically.

- ### The Compact Form — Why $p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$?
	We want a **single expression** that automatically selects the right branch.

	Observe what happens when we write $p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$ and substitute:
		**When $x = 1$:** 
			$p^1 \cdot (1-p)^{1-1}$
			$= p^1 \cdot (1-p)^0$
			$= p \cdot 1$
			$= p \checkmark$
		**When $x = 0$:** 
			$p^0 \cdot (1-p)^{1-0}$
			$= 1 \cdot (1-p)^1$
			$= 1-p \checkmark$
	The exponents $x$ and $1-x$ act as a **binary switch**:
- When $x = 1$: the first factor is "on" ($p^1$), the second is "off" ($(1-p)^0 = 1$)
- When $x = 0$: the first factor is "off" ($p^0 = 1$), the second is "on" ($(1-p)^1$)

Therefore the compact PMF is
$$\boxed{P(X = x) = p^x(1-p)^{1-x}, \qquad x \in {0, 1}}$$

> [!tip] Why This Form Matters This compact form is not just aesthetic. It is **required** for:
> 
> - Writing the likelihood function for multiple observations: $\prod_{i=1}^n p^{x_i}(1-p)^{1-x_i}$
> - Taking the log-likelihood (which turns products into sums)
> - Deriving the MLE for $p$
> - Recognizing Bernoulli as a member of the [[Exponential Family]]

### Verification: Does It Sum to 1?
$\sum_{x \in {0,1}} p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$
$= (1-p) + p = 1 \checkmark$

##  Support
$\text{Support}(X) = {0, 1}$
Only two points carry probability mass. Everything else has probability zero and is excluded from the support by definition. See [[09.Support of a Random Variable]].

## Expectation
- ### From the Definition
	The expected value of a discrete RV is $\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{x} x \cdot P(X = x)$.
	$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{x \in {0,1}} x \cdot P(X = x)$
	$= 0 \cdot P(X = 0) + 1 \cdot P(X = 1)$
	$= 0 \cdot (1-p) + 1 \cdot p$
	$= 0 + p$
	$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X] = p}$
#### Intuition
The expected value equals exactly the probability of success. This is why the coding ${0, 1}$ (not ${-1, +1}$ or ${1, 2}$) is canonical — it makes $\mathbb{E}[X]$ directly interpretable as a probability.

---

### Variance
- ### Step 1: Compute $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$
	$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \sum_{x \in {0,1}} x^2 \cdot P(X = x)$
	$= 0^2 \cdot (1-p) + 1^2 \cdot p$
	$= 0 + p$
	$= p$
	Notice: for a ${0,1}$-valued variable, $x^2 = x$ for both values ($0^2 = 0$, $1^2 = 1$). So $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[X] = p$.
- ### Step 2: Apply the Variance Formula
	$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$
	$= p - p^2$
	$= p(1-p)$
	$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = p(1-p)}$

##### Intuition: Where Is Variance Maximized?
Take the derivative with respect to $p$ and set to zero:
	$\frac{d}{dp}[p(1-p)]$
	 $= 1 - 2p$
	 $= 0$
	 $\implies p = \frac{1}{2}$

At $p = \frac{1}{2}$: $\text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4}$, the maximum.
At $p = 0$ or $p = 1$: $\text{Var}(X) = 0$ — no uncertainty when the outcome is certain.
![[Pasted image 20260423160609.png]]
Maximum uncertainty at $p = 0.5$, zero uncertainty at the extremes. This curve is the foundation of **entropy** in information theory.

## Standard Deviation

$$\sigma = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$$
$$= \sqrt{p(1-p)}$$

No separate derivation needed — it is the square root of variance by definition. The maximum standard deviation is $\sqrt{1/4} = 0.5$, occurring at $p = 0.5$.

---

## Skewness
Skewness measures the asymmetry of a distribution:
$$\gamma_1 = \frac{\mathbb{E}[(X - \mu)^3]}{\sigma^3}$$
- ### Step 1: Compute $\mathbb{E}[(X - \mu)^3]$
	Let $\mu = p$. We need $\mathbb{E}[(X-p)^3]$:
	$\mathbb{E}[(X-p)^3] = (0-p)^3(1-p) + (1-p)^3 \cdot p$
	$= -p^3(1-p) + p(1-p)^3$
	Factor out $p(1-p)$:
	$= p(1-p)\left[-(p^2) + (1-p)^2\right]$
	$= p(1-p)\left[-p^2 + 1 - 2p + p^2\right]$
	$= p(1-p)(1 - 2p)$
- ### Step 2: Divide by $\sigma^3$
	$\sigma^3 = [p(1-p)]^{3/2}$

	$\gamma_1 = \frac{p(1-p)(1-2p)}{[p(1-p)]^{3/2}}$
	$= \frac{1-2p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}$
	$\boxed{\gamma_1 = \frac{1-2p}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}}$


| $p < 0.5$ | $1-2p > 0$     | Right-skewed — most mass at 0, long tail toward 1 |
| --------- | -------------- | ------------------------------------------------- |
| $p = 0.5$ | $\gamma_1 = 0$ | Perfectly symmetric                               |
| $p > 0.5$ | $1-2p < 0$     | Left-skewed — most mass at 1, long tail toward 0  |
see more[[02.Skewness]]

## Kurtosis (Excess)

Excess kurtosis measures "tailedness" relative to a Normal distribution:
$$\gamma_2 = \frac{\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^4]}{\sigma^4} - 3$$

The $-3$ subtracts the Normal's baseline kurtosis.
- ### Step 1: Compute $\mathbb{E}[(X-p)^4]$
	$\mathbb{E}[(X-p)^4] = (-p)^4(1-p) + (1-p)^4 \cdot p$
	$= p^4(1-p) + p(1-p)^4$
	$= p(1-p)\left[p^3 + (1-p)^3\right]$
	Expand $(1-p)^3 = 1 - 3p + 3p^2 - p^3$:$p^3 + (1-p)^3 = p^3 + 1 - 3p + 3p^2 - p^3 = 1 - 3p + 3p^2$
	$= 1 - 3p(1-p)$
	So:
	$\mathbb{E}[(X-p)^4] = p(1-p)\bigl[1 - 3p(1-p)\bigr]$
- ### Step 2: Divide by $\sigma^4$ and subtract 3
	$\sigma^4 = [p(1-p)]^2$
	$\frac{\mathbb{E}[(X-p)^4]}{\sigma^4}$
	$= \frac{p(1-p)[1-3p(1-p)]}{[p(1-p)]^2}$
	$= \frac{1 - 3p(1-p)}{p(1-p)}$

	$\gamma_2 = \frac{1 - 3p(1-p)}{p(1-p)} - 3$
	$= \frac{1 - 3p(1-p) - 3p(1-p)}{p(1-p)}$$
	$\boxed{\gamma_2 = \frac{1 - 6p(1-p)}{p(1-p)}}$

#### Intuition
At $p = 0.5$: $\gamma_2 = \frac{1 - 6(0.25)}{0.25} = \frac{-0.5}{0.25} = -2$.

Negative excess kurtosis means the Bernoulli at $p=0.5$ is **platykurtic** (lighter tails, flatter than Normal). A two-point distribution has no tails at all — makes sense.
see more[[03.Kurtosis]]

## CDF
The CDF is $F(x) = P(X \leq x)$. We evaluate at all real $x$:
- **Case 1: $x < 0$** No values in support are $\leq x$, so $F(x) = 0$.
- **Case 2: $0 \leq x < 1$** Only $X = 0$ is $\leq x$, so: $F(x) = P(X = 0) = 1-p$
- **Case 3: $x \geq 1$** Both $X = 0$ and $X = 1$ are $\leq x$, so: $$F(x) = P(X = 0) + P(X = 1) = (1-p) + p = 1$$
$$\boxed{F(x) = \begin{cases} 0 & x < 0 \ 1-p & 0 \leq x < 1 \ 1 & x \geq 1 \end{cases}}$$

## Moment Generating Function (MGF)
$$M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$$
- ### Derivation:
	$M_X(t) = \sum_{x \in {0,1}} e^{tx} \cdot P(X = x)$
	$= e^{t \cdot 0} \cdot (1-p) + e^{t \cdot 1} \cdot p$
	$= 1 \cdot (1-p) + e^t \cdot p$
	$\boxed{M_X(t) = 1 - p + pe^t}$

### Recovering Moments from the MGF
The $n$-th moment is $\mathbb{E}[X^n] = M_X^{(n)}(0)$ (the $n$-th derivative evaluated at $t=0$).
- **First derivative:** 
	$M_X'(t) = pe^t$ 
	$M_X'(0) = p = \mathbb{E}[X] \checkmark$
- **Second derivative:** 
	$M_X''(t) = pe^t$$
	$M_X''(0) = p = \mathbb{E}[X^2] \checkmark$
This confirms $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = p - p^2 = p(1-p)$.

> [!tip] Notice All derivatives of $M_X(t)$ equal $pe^t$, so **all moments of $X$ equal $p$**. This makes sense because $X^n = X$ for $X \in {0,1}$ and any $n \geq 1$.

see more[[05.Moment Generating Function (MGF)]],[[06.Generating Moments with the MGF]]

## Bernoulli as Exponential Family

Rewrite the PMF to expose the exponential family structure:
$P(X = x) = p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$
Take the exponential of the log:
$= \exp!\left(x\log p + (1-x)\log(1-p)\right)$
$= \exp!\left(x\log p - x\log(1-p) + \log(1-p)\right)$
$= \exp!\left(x\log\frac{p}{1-p} + \log(1-p)\right)$

Matching to $h(x)\exp(\eta T(x) - A(\theta))$:

| Component | Value                         | Meaning                             |
| --------- | ----------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| $h(x)$    | $1$                           | No base measure needed              |
| $\eta(p)$ | $\log\frac{p}{1-p}$           | The **log-odds** (logit) of $p$     |
| $T(x)$    | $x$                           | Identity — $x$ itself is sufficient |
| $A(p)$    | $-\log(1-p) = \log(1+e^\eta)$ | Log-partition function              |

The natural parameter $\eta = \log\frac{p}{1-p}$ is the **logit function** — the same function that defines logistic regression. When you model $\eta = \mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$, you recover logistic regression exactly. See [[Generalized Linear Models (GLMs)]].

## Worked Example

>  Biased Coin Toss a biased coin with $p = 0.7$ (probability of heads). Let $X = 1$ for heads, $X = 0$ for tails.
> 
> **PMF:** $P(X=1) = 0.7, \quad P(X=0) = 0.3$
> **Expectation:** $\mathbb{E}[X] = 0.7$ 
> **Variance:** $\text{Var}(X) = 0.7 \times 0.3 = 0.21$ 
> **Skewness:** $\gamma_1 = \frac{1 - 2(0.7)}{\sqrt{0.21}} = \frac{-0.4}{0.458} \approx -0.873$
>  Negative — left-skewed, as expected since $p > 0.5$ (most mass on heads).
> **MGF:** $M_X(t) = 0.3 + 0.7e^t$
