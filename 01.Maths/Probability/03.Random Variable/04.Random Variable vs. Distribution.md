
> _This distinction confused me for longer than I'd like to admit. Everyone uses the words interchangeably in casual speech, including professors. But they are genuinely different objects, and getting this straight changes how you read proofs._

---

## The Setup: Probability Space

Everything lives inside a **probability space** $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$:

- $\Omega$ — the **sample space**: the set of all possible outcomes $\omega$.
- $\mathcal{F}$ — a **$\sigma$-algebra** on $\Omega$: the collection of measurable events (subsets of $\Omega$ we're allowed to assign probabilities to).
- $P$ — a **probability measure**: a function $P : \mathcal{F} \to [0,1]$ satisfying Kolmogorov's axioms.

Most of the time in Stat 110, $\Omega$ stays in the background. But it's always there, and the distinction between a random variable and its distribution is precisely about whether you're looking at the function or just its output behaviour.

---

## Random Variable — The Function

**Definition.** A **random variable** $X$ is a measurable function:

$$X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$$

That's it. $X$ is a function. It takes an outcome $\omega \in \Omega$ and maps it to a real number $X(\omega)$.

"Measurable" means: for every Borel set $B \subseteq \mathbb{R}$, the preimage $X^{-1}(B) = {\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in B} \in \mathcal{F}$. In plain language: every event defined in terms of $X$ is a legitimate event we can assign probability to.

> _The word "random" is slightly misleading. $X$ itself is a completely deterministic function — given $\omega$, $X(\omega)$ is fixed with no randomness. The randomness comes from $\omega$ being drawn according to $P$. $X$ is just the lens through which we look at $\omega$._

**Example.** Roll two dice. $\Omega = {(i,j) : i,j \in {1,\ldots,6}}$, $|\Omega| = 36$. Define:

$$X(\omega) = X((i,j)) = i + j$$

$X$ is a random variable — a function from outcomes to numbers. The outcome $(3,4)$ gives $X = 7$. The outcome $(1,1)$ gives $X = 2$.

---

## Distribution — The Induced Measure

**Definition.** The **distribution** of $X$ (also called the **law** of $X$, written $\mathcal{L}(X)$) is the probability measure $\mu_X$ on $\mathbb{R}$ defined by:

$$\mu_X(B) = P(X^{-1}(B)) = P(\omega \in \Omega : X(\omega) \in B)$$

for every Borel set $B \subseteq \mathbb{R}$.

In words: the distribution is the **pushforward of $P$ through $X$**. It tells you how probability mass is distributed over the real line, without any reference to the underlying $\Omega$.

Concretely, the distribution is fully captured by the **CDF**:

$$F_X(x) = P(X \leq x) = P({\omega : X(\omega) \leq x})$$

Or by the **PMF** (discrete case) or **PDF** (continuous case) when they exist.

> _The distribution is what you can observe and compute from data. The random variable is the underlying mechanism. This is an important epistemological distinction — in practice you only ever see the distribution, never the $\omega$'s._

---

## The Core Distinction

||Random Variable|Distribution|
|---|---|---|
|**What it is**|A function $X: \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$|A measure $\mu_X$ on $\mathbb{R}$|
|**Lives on**|The probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$|The real line $(\mathbb{R}, \mathcal{B}(\mathbb{R}))$|
|**Captures**|The full mechanism|Only the marginal behaviour of $X$|
|**Notation**|$X(\omega)$, or just $X$|$F_X$, PMF/PDF, $\mathcal{L}(X)$|
|**Multiple RVs**|Can be defined jointly on same $\Omega$|Marginals lose joint information|

---

## Two Random Variables, Same Distribution

This is the clearest way to see that the objects are different.

**Example.** Let $\Omega = {H, T}$, $P(H) = P(T) = 1/2$. Define:

$$X(\omega) = \begin{cases} 1 & \omega = H \ 0 & \omega = T \end{cases} \qquad Y(\omega) = \begin{cases} 0 & \omega = H \ 1 & \omega = T \end{cases}$$

$X$ and $Y$ are **different functions** on $\Omega$ — for every $\omega$, $X(\omega) \neq Y(\omega)$. In fact $X + Y = 1$ always, so they are perfectly negatively correlated.

But they have the **same distribution**: both are Bernoulli$(1/2)$, i.e., $P(X = 1) = P(Y = 1) = 1/2$.

Same distribution, completely different random variables. The distribution cannot distinguish them. The random variable can.

> _This example shows why "same distribution" is a strictly weaker statement than "same random variable." Two RVs with the same distribution can have radically different joint behaviour._

---

## Same Random Variable, Different Contexts

Conversely: the same random variable $X$ carries information about its relationship with other variables defined on the same $\Omega$. The distribution $\mathcal{L}(X)$ throws this away.

If $X, Y$ are both defined on $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$, then $\mathcal{L}(X)$ and $\mathcal{L}(Y)$ say nothing about $\text{Cov}(X, Y)$, $P(X > Y)$, or any other joint quantity. For those you need the **joint distribution** $\mathcal{L}(X, Y)$, which is a measure on $\mathbb{R}^2$.

---

## Equal in Distribution vs. Almost Sure Equality

This distinction has formal notation:

$$X \overset{d}{=} Y \quad \iff \quad \mathcal{L}(X) = \mathcal{L}(Y) \quad \iff \quad F_X = F_Y$$

$$X = Y \text{ a.s.} \quad \iff \quad P(\omega : X(\omega) = Y(\omega)) = 1$$

Almost sure equality is much stronger. It requires the functions to agree on a set of full measure.

In the $X, Y$ example above: $X \overset{d}{=} Y$ (same distribution), but $X \neq Y$ almost surely (in fact $X(\omega) \neq Y(\omega)$ for every $\omega$, so $P(X = Y) = 0$).

The hierarchy:

$$X = Y \text{ everywhere} \implies X = Y \text{ a.s.} \implies X \overset{d}{=} Y$$

None of these implications reverses.

---

## Why This Matters for Proofs

**Scenario 1: "Let $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$."**

This specifies the distribution of $X$, nothing more. Any random variable with this PMF works. You can't say anything about $X$'s relationship to other variables without more information.

**Scenario 2: "$X_1, X_2 \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Geom}(p)$"**

This specifies that $X_1$ and $X_2$ are defined on the same probability space, have the same distribution, and are independent. Independence is a statement about the **joint** distribution $\mathcal{L}(X_1, X_2)$ — specifically that it factors as $\mathcal{L}(X_1) \otimes \mathcal{L}(X_2)$. This is strictly more information than just knowing each marginal.

**Scenario 3: The decomposition $\text{NBin}(r,p) = \sum_{i=1}^r X_i$, $X_i \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Geom}(p)$.**

When I write this in [[04.Negative Binomial Distribution]], I mean: there exist random variables $X_1, \ldots, X_r$ on a common probability space, i.i.d. Geom$(p)$, such that their sum has the NBin$(r,p)$ distribution. The equality is in distribution:

$$X_1 + \cdots + X_r \overset{d}{=} \text{NBin}(r,p)$$

I am not saying anything about a specific $\omega$.

> _In practice, people write $=$ when they mean $\overset{d}{=}$. This is usually fine, but when a proof breaks down, the first thing to check is whether an equality is in distribution or almost sure. They require completely different arguments._

---

## The Skorokhod Representation Theorem (A Taste)

A deep theorem in probability says: if $X_n \overset{d}{\to} X$ (convergence in distribution), then there **exist** random variables $\tilde{X}_n, \tilde{X}$ on a **common** probability space such that $\tilde{X}_n \overset{d}{=} X_n$, $\tilde{X} \overset{d}{=} X$, and $\tilde{X}_n \to \tilde{X}$ **almost surely**.

This theorem lets you convert distributional convergence (a weak statement) into almost sure convergence (a strong statement) by changing the underlying probability space. The distributions are preserved; the probability space is reconstructed.

It's a striking illustration of the random variable / distribution distinction — the theorem is precisely about the freedom to choose the underlying $\Omega$ without affecting distributional properties.

I haven't proved this yet. It requires more measure theory than Stat 110 assumes. But knowing it exists changes how you think about the CLT and other limit theorems.

---

## The Canonical Construction

Given any CDF $F$, you can always construct a random variable with that distribution. Take $\Omega = (0,1)$, $P = $ Lebesgue measure, and define:

$$X(\omega) = F^{-1}(\omega) = \inf{x : F(x) \geq \omega}$$

This is the **quantile function** (generalised inverse). Then $X$ has CDF $F$.

This is the **inverse CDF method** — also the basis for simulation. To sample from any distribution, draw $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ and return $F^{-1}(U)$.

> _This construction shows that every distribution "lives" on the same probability space $(0,1)$ with Lebesgue measure, if you want it to. The probability space is flexible — the distribution is the invariant._

---

## Quick Summary

A **random variable** is a function $X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R}$. It lives on a specific probability space and carries information about its relationship with all other random variables defined on that space.

A **distribution** is the pushforward measure $\mu_X$ on $\mathbb{R}$. It captures only the marginal behaviour of $X$ — how probability mass is spread over the real line. It is blind to the underlying $\Omega$ and to joint structure.

**Same distribution $\not\Rightarrow$ same random variable.** **Same random variable $\Rightarrow$ same distribution.**

When you write $X \sim F$, you are specifying a distribution. When you write $X(\omega)$, you are evaluating a function. Keep the two layers separate and proofs become much cleaner.
