## 1. The Central Question

You run a Poisson process PP($\lambda$) on $[0, T]$ and observe:

$$N(T) = n$$

You now know **exactly $n$ events happened**. Where are they?

> **Answer (The Fundamental Conditioning Theorem):**  
> Given $N(T) = n$, the $n$ arrival times $T_1 < T_2 < \cdots < T_n$ are distributed as the **order statistics** of $n$ iid Uniform$(0, T)$ random variables.

$$\boxed{(T_1, T_2, \ldots, T_n) \mid N(T) = n ;\overset{d}{=}; (U_{(1)}, U_{(2)}, \ldots, U_{(n)})}$$

where $U_1, \ldots, U_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Uniform}(0, T)$ and $U_{(1)} < U_{(2)} < \cdots < U_{(n)}$ are their order statistics.

---

## 2. What This Really Means

There are two equivalent interpretations — both are important:

### Interpretation A — Order Statistics

The joint density of arrival times, conditioned on count $n$, equals the joint density of sorted uniform draws.

### Interpretation B — Unordered Uniformity (Deeper)

**Before sorting**, the $n$ event locations are like $n$ iid Uniform$(0,T)$ draws — completely **unstructured**, scattered uniformly.

The ordering is just an artifact of how we index them chronologically.

```
Without conditioning:         With conditioning N(T) = n:
Events land randomly,         Events are like n uniform darts
with random count N(T)        thrown blindly at [0, T]

─────┼──┼────┼──┼──┼──▶       ─────┼──┼────┼──┼──┼──▶
  (random # of events)            (exactly n, uniform)
```

> **Intuition:** Conditioning on the count $n$ "uses up" all the Poisson randomness about _how many_ events occur. What's left — the _where_ — is maximally uniform.

---

## 3. Proof

We want the joint density of $(T_1, \ldots, T_n)$ given $N(T) = n$.

**Step 1 — Joint density of arrival times unconditionally.**

The $n$ arrival times of a PP($\lambda$) on $[0,T]$, given there are exactly $n$ of them, have joint density:

For $0 < t_1 < t_2 < \cdots < t_n < T$:

$$f(t_1, \ldots, t_n, N(T)=n) = \lambda^n e^{-\lambda T}$$

_(Each event contributes $\lambda$ from the rate; $e^{-\lambda T}$ is the no-event probability for the gaps.)_

**Step 2 — Marginal $P(N(T) = n)$.**

$$P(N(T) = n) = \frac{e^{-\lambda T}(\lambda T)^n}{n!}$$

**Step 3 — Conditional density.**

$$f(t_1,\ldots,t_n \mid N(T)=n) = \frac{\lambda^n e^{-\lambda T}}{\frac{e^{-\lambda T}(\lambda T)^n}{n!}} = \frac{n!}{T^n}$$

for $0 < t_1 < \cdots < t_n < T$.

**Step 4 — Recognize the distribution.**

The joint density of the order statistics $U_{(1)} < \cdots < U_{(n)}$ of $n$ iid Uniform$(0,T)$ is exactly:

$$f(u_1, \ldots, u_n) = \frac{n!}{T^n}, \quad 0 < u_1 < \cdots < u_n < T$$

**They match.** $\checkmark$

> **Key cancellation:** $\lambda$ completely vanishes from the conditional density. The rate is irrelevant once you condition on the count.

## 4. Conditioning on a Sub-interval

A more general and powerful result:

**Setup:** PP($\lambda$) on $[0, T]$. You observe $N(T) = n$. What is the distribution of $N(s)$ for $s < T$?

$$\boxed{N(s) \mid N(T) = n ;\sim; \text{Binomial}!\left(n,, \frac{s}{T}\right)}$$

**Proof:** Each of the $n$ events (uniform on $[0,T]$) independently falls in $[0,s]$ with probability $s/T$. Counting how many fall in $[0,s]$ is exactly a Binomial$(n, s/T)$.

**What this says geometrically:**

```
[0──────────s│────────────T]
 ←── s/T ──→│←── 1-s/T ──→

Each of n points independently hits [0,s] w.p. s/T
→ Binomial(n, s/T) points in [0,s]
```

**Corollary — Expected count in sub-interval:**

$$E[N(s) \mid N(T) = n] = n \cdot \frac{s}{T}$$

This is linear in $s$ — the events are spread proportionally across $[0,T]$.

## 5. Conditioning on the Past — The Memoryless Refresh

**Setup:** You are at time $s$ and know $N(s) = k$. What happens after $s$?

$$\boxed{N(t) - N(s) \mid N(s) = k ;\sim; \text{Poisson}(\lambda(t-s))}$$

The future is independent of the past, and the process **restarts fresh** from $s$.

This follows directly from the **independent increments** property of the Poisson process.

```
Past: known k events         Future: independent PP(λ)
─────────────────────┼──────────────────────────────▶
        [0, s]        s          [s, t]
     N(s) = k known              N(t)-N(s) ~ Poisson(λ(t-s))
```

> This is the **Markov property** of the Poisson process — the future depends on the past only through the current time, not through what happened before.

## 6. Conditioning on an Arrival — The Inspection Paradox

### Setup

You arrive at a bus stop at a **random time** $t^*$. Buses follow PP($\lambda$). What is the distribution of:

- $B$ = time since last bus (backward recurrence time)
- $F$ = time until next bus (forward recurrence time)

### Naive (Wrong) Answer

"Average interarrival time is $1/\lambda$, so I should wait $1/(2\lambda)$ on average."

### Correct Answer

$$E[F] = \frac{1}{\lambda}, \qquad E[B] = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

$$E[F + B] = \frac{2}{\lambda}$$

The **total interval** containing $t^*$ has expected length $\frac{2}{\lambda}$ — **twice** the average interarrival time!

### Why — Length-Biased Sampling

A random time $t^_$ is more likely to fall in a **long** interarrival interval than a short one. The interval containing $t^_$ is length-biased:

$$f_{\text{observed interval}}(\ell) \propto \ell \cdot f_{\text{interval}}(\ell)$$

For $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ interarrivals:

$$f_{\text{observed}}(\ell) \propto \ell \cdot \lambda e^{-\lambda \ell} \implies \text{Gamma}(2, \lambda)$$

So $E[\text{observed interval}] = \frac{2}{\lambda}$, split symmetrically: $E[F] = E[B] = \frac{1}{\lambda}$.

> **Paradox resolution:** You don't land uniformly among intervals — you land **preferentially in long ones**. The Poisson process's memoryless property then makes $F \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ despite this bias.

## 7. The Ballot Problem Connection

**Given $N(T) = n$ events uniform on $[0,T]$:**

What is the probability that event $k$ (the $k$-th in chronological order) falls in sub-interval $(a, b)$?

$$P(T_k \in (a,b) \mid N(T)=n) = P(U_{(k)} \in (a,b))$$

where $U_{(k)}$ is the $k$-th order statistic of $n$ iid Uniform$(0,T)$:

$$f_{U_{(k)}}(u) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!} \cdot \frac{u^{k-1}(T-u)^{n-k}}{T^n}$$

This is the **Beta distribution** scaled to $[0,T]$:

$$U_{(k)}/T \sim \text{Beta}(k, n-k+1)$$

$$E[T_k \mid N(T)=n] = \frac{k}{n+1} \cdot T$$

> **Beautiful result:** Given $n$ events in $[0,T]$, the expected time of the $k$-th event is exactly $\frac{k}{n+1}T$ — equally spaced! The events are "spread out" on average.

## 8. Summary Table of Conditioning Results

|Condition|Query|Distribution|
|---|---|---|
|$N(T) = n$|Location of all events|Order stats of $n$ iid Uniform$(0,T)$|
|$N(T) = n$|$N(s)$ for $s < T$|Binomial$(n, s/T)$|
|$N(T) = n$|Position of $k$-th event|Beta$(k, n-k+1)$ scaled to $[0,T]$|
|$N(s) = k$|$N(t) - N(s)$ for $t > s$|Poisson$(\lambda(t-s))$ — independent|
|Random $t^*$|Wait until next event $F$|$\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ (memoryless)|
|Random $t^*$|Total interval containing $t^*$|Gamma$(2, \lambda)$ (length-biased)|

## 9. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Insurance Claims

Claims arrive at rate $\lambda = 2$/day. You observe $N(30) = 54$ claims in 30 days.

**(a) Expected claims in first 10 days:** $$E[N(10) \mid N(30) = 54] = 54 \cdot \frac{10}{30} = 18$$

**(b) Probability of $\leq 15$ claims in first 10 days:** $$N(10) \mid N(30) = 54 \sim \text{Binomial}(54, 1/3)$$ $$P(N(10) \leq 15) = \sum_{k=0}^{15} \binom{54}{k} (1/3)^k (2/3)^{54-k}$$

**(c) Expected time of 10th claim:** $$E[T_{10} \mid N(30) = 54] = \frac{10}{55} \cdot 30 = \frac{300}{55} \approx 5.45 \text{ days}$$

### Example 2 — Uniform Order Statistics Simulation

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt

def verify_conditioning_theorem(lam=3, T=10, n=8, n_sim=5000):
    """
    Verify: arrival times | N(T)=n  ==  order stats of n Uniform(0,T)
    """
    # Method 1: Simulate actual PP and condition on count
    pp_arrivals = []
    for _ in range(n_sim * 20):  # oversample to get N(T)=n cases
        times = []
        t = 0
        while t < T:
            t += np.random.exponential(1/lam)
            if t < T:
                times.append(t)
        if len(times) == n:
            pp_arrivals.append(sorted(times))
        if len(pp_arrivals) >= n_sim:
            break
    
    # Method 2: Direct order statistics of Uniform(0,T)
    uniform_order_stats = np.sort(
        np.random.uniform(0, T, size=(n_sim, n)), axis=1
    )
    
    pp_arrivals = np.array(pp_arrivals[:n_sim])
    
    # Compare marginal distribution of 4th arrival time
    k = 3  # 4th order statistic (0-indexed)
    plt.figure(figsize=(10, 4))
    plt.hist(pp_arrivals[:, k], bins=40, alpha=0.6,
             density=True, label=f'PP conditioned on N({T})={n}')
    plt.hist(uniform_order_stats[:, k], bins=40, alpha=0.6,
             density=True, label=f'Order stat U_({k+1}) of {n} Uniform(0,{T})')
    plt.xlabel(f'T_{k+1} value')
    plt.title(f'Conditioning Theorem: T_{k+1} | N({T})={n}')
    plt.legend()
    plt.show()
    
    print(f"PP mean T_{k+1}: {pp_arrivals[:, k].mean():.4f}")
    print(f"Uniform mean U_({k+1}): {uniform_order_stats[:, k].mean():.4f}")
    print(f"Theoretical E[T_{k+1}|N={n}] = {(k+1)/(n+1)*T:.4f}")

verify_conditioning_theorem()
```

### Example 3 — Inspection Paradox Verification

```python
def inspection_paradox(lam=1.0, n_trials=100000):
    """
    Verify E[F] = 1/lambda despite the paradox intuition
    """
    forward_waits = []
    
    for _ in range(n_trials):
        # Generate a long PP
        arrivals = np.cumsum(np.random.exponential(1/lam, size=1000))
        # Pick a random observation time
        t_star = np.random.uniform(0, arrivals[-2])
        # Find next arrival after t_star
        next_arrival = arrivals[arrivals > t_star][0]
        forward_waits.append(next_arrival - t_star)
    
    print(f"E[F] empirical: {np.mean(forward_waits):.4f}")
    print(f"E[F] theoretical: {1/lam:.4f}")
    # Both ≈ 1/lambda — paradox confirmed

inspection_paradox()
```

## 13. The Unifying Mental Model

There are **two layers of randomness** in a Poisson process:

```
Layer 1 (How many?):   N(T) ~ Poisson(λT)
Layer 2 (Where?):      Locations | N(T)=n ~ n iid Uniform(0,T)
```

**Conditioning on Layer 1 completely decouples the layers.**

Once you fix $n$, the process becomes a **uniform scatter** — no rate, no Poisson, just $n$ uniform random points. This decomposition is what makes Poisson processes analytically tractable in virtually every applied setting.

> Every hard Poisson process conditioning problem becomes easy once you invoke this decomposition: fix the count, scatter uniformly, then apply Binomial/Beta/Multinomial logic.
