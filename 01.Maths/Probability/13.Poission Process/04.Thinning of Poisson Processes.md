## 1. The Setup

You have a Poisson process PP($\lambda$). Each arriving event is **independently kept** with probability $p$, or **discarded** with probability $1-p$.

This random filtering is called **thinning** (also: _independent marking_, _Bernoulli thinning_).

```
Original PP(λ):
────┼────┼──┼─────┼──┼────┼───┼──▶
    e₁   e₂  e₃    e₄  e₅   e₆   e₇

Each event: keep (↓) w.p. p, discard (✗) w.p. 1-p

    ✓    ✗   ✓     ✗   ✓    ✓    ✗

Kept stream PP(λp):
────┼───────┼──────────┼────┼──────▶
    e₁      e₃         e₅   e₆

Discarded stream PP(λ(1-p)):
─────────┼───────┼──────────────┼──▶
         e₂      e₄             e₇
```

---

## 2. The Theorem

**Theorem (Thinning / Decomposition):**

Let $N \sim \text{PP}(\lambda)$. Independently mark each event as type 1 with probability $p$ and type 2 with probability $1-p$. Define:

- $N_1(t)$ = number of type-1 events in $[0,t]$
- $N_2(t)$ = number of type-2 events in $[0,t]$

Then:

$$\boxed{N_1(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda p) \quad \text{and} \quad N_2(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda(1-p))}$$

$$\boxed{N_1 \perp N_2}$$

**The two thinned streams are independent Poisson processes.**

---

## 3. The Surprise — Independence

The rates $\lambda p$ and $\lambda(1-p)$ are expected. The **independence** is the deep result.

Intuitively: knowing that many events were kept (large $N_1$) tells you nothing about how many were discarded ($N_2$), because each decision was made independently coin flip by coin flip.

But this intuition is incomplete — $N_1$ and $N_2$ are **both functions of the same underlying process $N$**. Why doesn't knowing $N_1$ tell you something about $N = N_1 + N_2$ and hence about $N_2$?

The answer lies in the Poisson distribution's special property:

> **Key algebraic fact:** If $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu)$ and $N_1 \mid N \sim \text{Binomial}(N, p)$, then $N_1 \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu p)$ and $N_1 \perp N_2 = N - N_1$.

This is **unique to the Poisson distribution** — no other distribution has this property.

---

## 4. Proof

### Proof of Marginal Distributions

Fix time interval $[0,t]$. Let $N = N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$.

Each event is independently type 1 with probability $p$, so:

$$N_1 \mid N \sim \text{Binomial}(N, p)$$

Compute $P(N_1 = k)$ by conditioning on $N$:

$$P(N_1 = k) = \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} P(N_1 = k \mid N = n) \cdot P(N = n)$$

$$= \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^n}{n!}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-\lambda t} p^k (\lambda t)^k}{k!} \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \frac{(\lambda t)^{n-k}(1-p)^{n-k}}{(n-k)!}$$

Let $m = n - k$:

$$= \frac{e^{-\lambda t} (p\lambda t)^k}{k!} \sum_{m=0}^{\infty} \frac{(\lambda t (1-p))^m}{m!}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-\lambda t} (p\lambda t)^k}{k!} \cdot e^{\lambda t(1-p)}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-\lambda t p} (p\lambda t)^k}{k!}$$

$$\implies \boxed{N_1 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t p)} \checkmark$$

By symmetry: $N_2 \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t (1-p))$.

---

### Proof of Independence

Compute the joint PMF of $(N_1, N_2)$:

$$P(N_1 = j,, N_2 = k) = P(N_1 = j,, N = j+k)$$

$$= P(N_1 = j \mid N = j+k) \cdot P(N = j+k)$$

$$= \binom{j+k}{j} p^j (1-p)^k \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^{j+k}}{(j+k)!}$$

$$= \frac{(j+k)!}{j!,k!} p^j(1-p)^k \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^{j+k}}{(j+k)!}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-\lambda t p}(\lambda t p)^j}{j!} \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda t(1-p)}(\lambda t(1-p))^k}{k!}$$

$$= P(N_1 = j) \cdot P(N_2 = k) \checkmark$$

The joint PMF **factors** — $N_1$ and $N_2$ are independent. $\checkmark$

---

## 5. Generalization — $k$-Type Thinning

Each event is independently assigned to type $i$ with probability $p_i$, where $\sum_{i=1}^k p_i = 1$.

$$\boxed{N_1 \sim \text{PP}(\lambda p_1),; N_2 \sim \text{PP}(\lambda p_2),; \ldots,; N_k \sim \text{PP}(\lambda p_k)}$$

$$\boxed{N_1, N_2, \ldots, N_k \text{ mutually independent}}$$

**Proof:** Same as above using Multinomial instead of Binomial. The joint PMF factors into a product of Poisson PMFs.

**Example:** Customers at a store choose product A (40%), B (35%), C (25%). If customers arrive as PP(100/hour):

- Product A purchases: PP(40/hour)
- Product B purchases: PP(35/hour)
- Product C purchases: PP(25/hour)
- All three streams are **mutually independent**

---

## 6. Non-Constant Thinning — State-Dependent Marking

Thinning can be generalized: the retention probability $p(t)$ varies with time.

$$\text{Each event at time } t \text{ is kept with probability } p(t)$$

**Result:** The thinned process is still Poisson but with a **time-varying rate**:

$$N_1 \sim \text{PP}(\lambda(t) \cdot p(t)) \quad \text{(inhomogeneous PP)}$$

The rate at time $t$ becomes $\lambda \cdot p(t)$.

**Example:** A server processes requests at rate 100/sec. During peak hours (8am–6pm), each request has a $p(t) = 0.8$ chance of being a "complex" request. Complex requests form an inhomogeneous PP with rate $80$/sec during peak hours.

## 7. Duality with Superposition

Thinning and superposition are **exact inverses**:

```
                    SUPERPOSITION
         PP(λ₁) ──┐
                   ├──→  PP(λ₁ + λ₂)
         PP(λ₂) ──┘

                      THINNING
                              ┌──→  PP(λp)       [kept]
         PP(λ)  ──────────────┤
                              └──→  PP(λ(1-p))   [discarded]
```

**The full picture:**

||Superposition|Thinning|
|---|---|---|
|**Direction**|$n \to 1$ (merge)|$1 \to n$ (split)|
|**Rate**|$\lambda_1 + \lambda_2$|$\lambda p$, $\lambda(1-p)$|
|**Independence**|Input streams must be independent|Output streams become independent|
|**Key tool**|PGF factoring|Poisson-Binomial-Poisson identity|

**Round-trip consistency:**

Thin PP($\lambda$) with prob $p$ → get PP($\lambda p$) and PP($\lambda(1-p)$).  
Now superpose them → PP($\lambda p + \lambda(1-p)$) = PP($\lambda$). ✓

You recover the original rate exactly.

---

## 8. The Poisson-Binomial-Poisson Identity

This is the algebraic engine behind thinning. Worth stating cleanly:

**Theorem:** If $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu)$ and $X \mid N \sim \text{Binomial}(N, p)$, then:

$$X \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu p) \quad \text{and} \quad X \perp (N - X)$$

**This is unique to Poisson.** Check: if $N \sim \text{Binomial}(m, q)$ and $X \mid N \sim \text{Binomial}(N, p)$, then $X \sim \text{Binomial}(m, pq)$ but $X$ and $N-X$ are **not** independent.

The Poisson distribution is the **only** distribution closed under Binomial thinning with output independence.

---

## 9. Connection to the Conditioning Theorem

Thinning and conditioning are related through a beautiful symmetry.

**Conditioning theorem says:** Given $N(T) = n$, events are uniform on $[0,T]$.

**Thinning says:** Thin PP($\lambda$) by keeping each event with probability $p = s/T$ (i.e., keep if event falls in $[0,s]$).

Then:

- Kept stream = PP($\lambda \cdot s/T$) on $[0,T]$, which equals PP($\lambda$) on $[0,s]$
- This is exactly $N(s)$

So thinning by a **spatial indicator** ($\mathbf{1}[\text{event} \in [0,s]]$) recovers the sub-interval count, and the independence of the thinned streams recovers the **independent increments** property.

> **Deep link:** The independence of increments of a Poisson process **is** thinning independence, when the thinning is by time sub-interval membership.

## 10. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Network Packets

Packets arrive at a router as PP($\lambda = 1000$/sec).

- 60% are TCP packets
- 30% are UDP packets
- 10% are ICMP packets

TCP stream: PP(600/sec)  
UDP stream: PP(300/sec)  
ICMP stream: PP(100/sec)  
All **mutually independent**.

$P(\text{no ICMP in 10ms}) = e^{-100 \times 0.01} = e^{-1} \approx 0.368$

$P(\text{exactly 5 TCP and exactly 2 UDP in 10ms})$:

$$= P(N_{TCP}=5) \cdot P(N_{UDP}=2) \quad \text{(by independence)}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-6} \cdot 6^5}{5!} \cdot \frac{e^{-3} \cdot 3^2}{2!} \approx 0.1606 \times 0.2240 \approx 0.036$$

### Example 2 — Quality Control

Items produced as PP($\lambda = 50$/hour). Each is defective with probability $p = 0.04$.

- Defective items: PP($2$/hour)
- Good items: PP($48$/hour)
- Independent streams

$E[\text{time until first defective}] = 1/2$ hour = 30 minutes

$P(\text{0 defectives in 2 hours}) = e^{-2 \times 2} = e^{-4} \approx 0.018$

### Example 3 — Thinning Then Race

Cars arrive on a highway as PP($\lambda = 60$/min). Each is independently a truck w.p. 0.1 or car w.p. 0.9.

Truck stream: PP(6/min), Car stream: PP(54/min).

$P(\text{first vehicle is a truck}) = \frac{6}{60} = 0.1$ ← same as thinning probability. Always.

> **General rule:** $P(\text{first thinned event is type } i) = p_i$. The race outcome probability equals the thinning probability.

## 11. Common Mistakes

|Mistake|Correction|
|---|---|
|Assuming independence is obvious|$N_1$ and $N_2$ share the same parent $N$ — independence is a **theorem**, not automatic|
|Applying thinning without independence of marks|Marks must be independent of each other **and** of the process history|
|Confusing thinning probability with conditional probability|$p$ is the probability of keeping an event, not the probability given some condition|
|Forgetting thinned streams are full Poisson processes|Not just the right mean — full independent increments, stationary, etc.|

**[ ] Exercise 4 — Simulation**

```python
import numpy as np

def verify_thinning(lam=10, p=0.3, T=1.0, n_sim=50000):
    """
    Verify: thinned streams are independent Poisson processes
    """
    n1_counts, n2_counts = [], []
    
    for _ in range(n_sim):
        # Generate PP(lambda) on [0, T]
        n_total = np.random.poisson(lam * T)
        # Thin: each event independently kept w.p. p
        marks = np.random.binomial(1, p, size=n_total)
        n1_counts.append(marks.sum())
        n2_counts.append(n_total - marks.sum())
    
    n1_counts = np.array(n1_counts)
    n2_counts = np.array(n2_counts)
    
    print(f"E[N1] = {n1_counts.mean():.3f} | Theoretical: {lam*p*T:.3f}")
    print(f"E[N2] = {n2_counts.mean():.3f} | Theoretical: {lam*(1-p)*T:.3f}")
    print(f"Cov(N1,N2) = {np.cov(n1_counts, n2_counts)[0,1]:.4f} | Theoretical: 0")
    print(f"Corr(N1,N2) = {np.corrcoef(n1_counts, n2_counts)[0,1]:.4f} | Theoretical: 0")

verify_thinning()
# Covariance and correlation should be ≈ 0 — confirming independence
```

## 13. Key Formulas Cheatsheet

$$\boxed{N \sim \text{PP}(\lambda),; p\text{-thin} ;\Rightarrow; N_1 \sim \text{PP}(\lambda p) \perp N_2 \sim \text{PP}(\lambda(1-p))}$$

$$\boxed{k\text{-type thinning: } N_i \sim \text{PP}(\lambda p_i),\quad N_1 \perp N_2 \perp \cdots \perp N_k}$$

$$\boxed{N \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu),; X \mid N \sim \text{Bin}(N,p) ;\Rightarrow; X \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu p),; X \perp N-X}$$

$$\boxed{P(\text{first event is type } i) = p_i}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Thin then superpose: PP}(\lambda p) + \text{PP}(\lambda(1-p)) = \text{PP}(\lambda)}$$

## 14. Mental Model

> Imagine a factory line (the Poisson process) producing widgets at rate $\lambda$. At the end of the line, a quality inspector **independently flips a coin** for each widget: heads → keep it, tails → scrap it.
> 
> The kept widgets form their own independent assembly line at rate $\lambda p$. The scrapped widgets form their own independent scrap heap at rate $\lambda(1-p)$. The two piles have **no memory of each other** — because each decision was made independently.
> 
> The magic: the scrapping process has **no knowledge** of how many were kept, and vice versa. The independence emerges from the independence of the coin flips **and** the Poisson structure of the original process.
