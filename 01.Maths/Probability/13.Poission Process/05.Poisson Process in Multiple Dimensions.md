## 1. Motivation — Why Go Beyond 1D?

In 1D, the Poisson process models **events in time**. But many real phenomena are **spatially distributed**:

|Application|Space|
|---|---|
|Stars in the sky|2D sphere projection|
|Trees in a forest|2D plane|
|Defects in a material|3D volume|
|Neurons firing in a brain region|3D space + time = 4D|
|Wi-Fi access points in a city|2D plane|
|Cosmic ray hits on a detector|2D surface|

The 1D intuition extends cleanly — but the geometry becomes richer.

---

## 2. Definition — Spatial Poisson Process

Let $S \subseteq \mathbb{R}^d$ be a region in $d$-dimensional space. A **spatial Poisson process** (also: Poisson Point Process, PPP) with **intensity measure** $\Lambda$ is a random collection of points ${x_i} \subset S$ satisfying:

### Axiom 1 — Poisson Counts

For any bounded region $A \subseteq S$:

$$N(A) \sim \text{Poisson}(\Lambda(A))$$

where $\Lambda(A)$ is the **intensity measure** of $A$ (the expected number of points in $A$).

### Axiom 2 — Independent Scattering

For any **disjoint** regions $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_k \subseteq S$:

$$N(A_1),, N(A_2),, \ldots,, N(A_k) \quad \text{are mutually independent}$$

> These two axioms completely define the spatial Poisson process — no reference to ordering, interarrivals, or time.

---

## 3. Homogeneous vs. Inhomogeneous

### 3.1 Homogeneous (Stationary) PPP

The intensity measure is proportional to volume:

$$\Lambda(A) = \lambda \cdot |A|$$

where $|A|$ is the $d$-dimensional volume (area in 2D, length in 1D, volume in 3D) and $\lambda > 0$ is the **intensity** (points per unit volume).

$$\boxed{N(A) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda \cdot |A|)}$$

**Homogeneous = uniform density everywhere.** The process looks statistically the same from any location.

### 3.2 Inhomogeneous PPP

The intensity varies across space via an **intensity function** $\lambda(x) \geq 0$:

$$\Lambda(A) = \int_A \lambda(x), dx$$

$$\boxed{N(A) \sim \text{Poisson}!\left(\int_A \lambda(x), dx\right)}$$

Points are denser where $\lambda(x)$ is large.

```
Homogeneous (λ = const):        Inhomogeneous (λ(x) varies):

· · · · · · · · · ·            · · ·  ·· ···· ··  · ·
· · · · · · · · · ·            · ·  ·  ·   ·····  ·
· · · · · · · · · ·            ·  ·    ·  ·····  ·· ·
· · · · · · · · · ·            ·    ·   ···· ·  ·   ·
(uniform density)               (density peaks at center)
```

## 4. The $d$-Dimensional Setup

### Key Quantities by Dimension

| Dimension | Space | Volume element | $|A|$ for sphere/disk radius $r$ | |---|---|---|---| | 1D | $\mathbb{R}$ | $dx$ | $2r$ (interval) | | 2D | $\mathbb{R}^2$ | $dx,dy$ | $\pi r^2$ (disk) | | 3D | $\mathbb{R}^3$ | $dx,dy,dz$ | $\frac{4}{3}\pi r^3$ (ball) | | $d$D | $\mathbb{R}^d$ | $dx_1 \cdots dx_d$ | $\frac{\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2+1)} r^d$ |

### Count Distribution

For homogeneous PPP($\lambda$) in $d$ dimensions, in a ball of radius $r$ centred at origin:

$$N(B_r) \sim \text{Poisson}!\left(\lambda \cdot \frac{\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2+1)} r^d\right)$$

---

## 5. The Fundamental Conditioning Theorem in $d$D

**Theorem:** Given $N(A) = n$, the $n$ points in $A$ are distributed as $n$ iid draws from the **normalized intensity measure** on $A$:

$$\text{Each point} \mid N(A) = n \overset{iid}{\sim} f(x) = \frac{\lambda(x)}{\Lambda(A)}, \quad x \in A$$

**For homogeneous PPP:** Given $N(A) = n$, the $n$ points are iid **Uniform$(A)$**.

$$\boxed{(x_1, \ldots, x_n) \mid N(A) = n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Uniform}(A)}$$

> **This is the 1D conditioning theorem, verbatim, in $d$ dimensions.** The geometry of $A$ only affects the uniform distribution's support.

---

## 6. Superposition and Thinning in $d$D

Both theorems carry over without modification:

### Superposition

$$\text{PPP}(\lambda_1) + \text{PPP}(\lambda_2) = \text{PPP}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$$

(adding point patterns from independent processes → denser homogeneous PPP)

### Thinning

Each point of PPP($\lambda$) is independently retained with probability $p(x)$ (can depend on location):

$$\text{Retained points} \sim \text{PPP}(\lambda \cdot p(x))$$ $$\text{Removed points} \sim \text{PPP}(\lambda \cdot (1-p(x)))$$

Both independent. For homogeneous thinning ($p(x) = p$ constant):

$$\text{PPP}(\lambda) \xrightarrow{\text{thin } p} \text{PPP}(\lambda p) \perp \text{PPP}(\lambda(1-p))$$

## 7. Nearest Neighbour Distance

One of the most important derived quantities in spatial statistics.

**Setup:** Homogeneous PPP($\lambda$) in $\mathbb{R}^d$. Fix a point at the origin (or pick a random point). Let $R$ = distance to the **nearest other point**.

### In 2D

The probability that no point falls within radius $r$:

$$P(R > r) = P(N(B_r) = 0) = e^{-\lambda \pi r^2}$$

So the CDF and PDF of $R$:

$$\boxed{F_R(r) = 1 - e^{-\lambda \pi r^2}}$$

$$\boxed{f_R(r) = 2\lambda \pi r \cdot e^{-\lambda \pi r^2}, \quad r \geq 0}$$

This is a **Rayleigh distribution** (special case of Weibull).

$$E[R] = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\lambda}}, \qquad \text{Var}(R) = \frac{4 - \pi}{4\pi\lambda}$$

### In $d$D (General)

Let $\omega_d = \frac{\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2+1)}$ be the volume of the unit ball in $\mathbb{R}^d$.

$$P(R > r) = e^{-\lambda \omega_d r^d}$$

$$f_R(r) = \lambda \omega_d d \cdot r^{d-1} \cdot e^{-\lambda \omega_d r^d}$$

$$E[R] = \frac{\Gamma(1 + 1/d)}{\left(\lambda \omega_d\right)^{1/d}}$$

|Dimension|$f_R(r)$|Distribution name|
|---|---|---|
|1D|$2\lambda e^{-2\lambda r}$ (for $r>0$)|Exponential$(2\lambda)$|
|2D|$2\lambda\pi r \cdot e^{-\lambda\pi r^2}$|Rayleigh|
|3D|$4\lambda\pi r^2 \cdot e^{-\frac{4}{3}\lambda\pi r^3}$|Weibull|

> **1D check:** In 1D, nearest neighbour = min of left and right Exp($\lambda$) gaps. By superposition and race: $R \sim \text{Exp}(2\lambda)$. ✓

## 8. The Empty Space Function

**Definition:** $F(r) = P(\text{no point of PPP within distance } r \text{ of origin})$

For homogeneous PPP($\lambda$) in $\mathbb{R}^d$:

$$\boxed{F(r) = 1 - e^{-\lambda \omega_d r^d}}$$

Same formula as the nearest-neighbour CDF — because for a homogeneous PPP, these two quantities coincide (the process looks the same from any fixed point as from a typical random point).

This is used in **spatial statistics** to test whether an observed point pattern is consistent with a PPP (CSR — Complete Spatial Randomness).

## 9. Campbell's Theorem

A fundamental result for computing expectations of sums over PPP points.

**Theorem:** For PPP with intensity $\lambda(x)$ and any measurable function $f: S \to \mathbb{R}$:

$$\boxed{E!\left[\sum_{x_i \in \text{PPP}} f(x_i)\right] = \int_S f(x),\lambda(x),dx}$$

**Intuition:** Replace the random sum over points with an integral against the intensity measure.

**Example — Total signal power:**  
Transmitters distributed as PPP($\lambda$) in 2D. Each transmitter at location $x$ contributes signal power $f(x) = |x|^{-\alpha}$ (path-loss model). Expected total received power:

$$E!\left[\sum_i |x_i|^{-\alpha}\right] = \lambda \int_{\mathbb{R}^2} |x|^{-\alpha},dx = \lambda \int_0^\infty r^{-\alpha} \cdot 2\pi r,dr = 2\pi\lambda \int_0^\infty r^{1-\alpha},dr$$

(Converges for $\alpha > 2$.)

**Second moment — Campbell's extended theorem:**

$$\text{Var}!\left[\sum_{x_i} f(x_i)\right] = \int_S f(x)^2,\lambda(x),dx$$

## 10. Marked Poisson Process

Each point $x_i$ of the PPP is equipped with an independent random **mark** $m_i \sim F_m$ (a scalar, vector, or label).

**Result:** The marked process is itself a PPP on $S \times \mathcal{M}$ (space × mark space) with intensity:

$$\lambda(x, m) = \lambda(x) \cdot f_m(m)$$

**Examples:**

- Trees (PPP in 2D) with random heights (marks)
- Wi-Fi access points (PPP in 2D) with random transmission powers
- Stars (PPP in 3D) with random luminosities

**Key property:** Projecting back to $S$ (ignoring marks) recovers the original PPP. Thinning by mark value recovers the thinning theorem.

## 11. Stochastic Geometry — Voronoi and Delaunay

Given a PPP in $\mathbb{R}^2$, two canonical geometric structures arise:

### Voronoi Tessellation

Each point $x_i$ "owns" the region of space closer to it than to any other PPP point:

$$V_i = {y \in \mathbb{R}^2 : |y - x_i| \leq |y - x_j| ;\forall j \neq i}$$

The Voronoi cells of a PPP($\lambda$) form a random tessellation. Each cell has:

$$E[\text{area of cell}] = \frac{1}{\lambda}, \quad E[\text{number of sides}] = 6$$

### Delaunay Triangulation

The dual graph of the Voronoi tessellation — connects PPP points whose Voronoi cells share an edge. Used in mesh generation, spatial interpolation.

**Application in ML:** Voronoi diagrams of training data → nearest-neighbour classifiers; each Voronoi cell is the decision region for one training point.

## 12. 2D PPP — Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Stars in the Sky

Stars distributed as homogeneous PPP($\lambda = 0.01$/deg²) on a 2D projection.

**(a) Expected stars in a 10° × 10° patch:** $$E[N] = 0.01 \times 100 = 1$$

**(b) Probability of seeing exactly 3 stars in the patch:** $$P(N=3) = \frac{e^{-1} \cdot 1^3}{3!} = \frac{e^{-1}}{6} \approx 0.0613$$

**(c) Expected nearest-star distance (in degrees):** $$E[R] = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{\lambda}} = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{0.01}} = \frac{1}{0.2} = 5 \text{ degrees}$$

### Example 2 — Wi-Fi Access Points

Access points distributed as PPP($\lambda = 0.05$/m²) in a 2D office floor.

**(a) Probability of no access point within 2 metres:** $$P(R > 2) = e^{-\lambda \pi r^2} = e^{-0.05 \times \pi \times 4} = e^{-0.628} \approx 0.534$$

**(b) Given 20 access points in a 20m × 20m room, where are they?**

By conditioning theorem: each is iid Uniform on the 400m² room.  
Expected position of each: centre of the room $(10, 10)$.

### Example 3 — Defects in a Material

Defects in a 3D material follow PPP($\lambda = 10^{-6}$/mm³).

Volume of a 10mm × 10mm × 10mm cube: $|A| = 1000$ mm³.

$$N(A) \sim \text{Poisson}(10^{-6} \times 1000) = \text{Poisson}(10^{-3})$$

$$P(N = 0) = e^{-0.001} \approx 0.999 \quad \text{(almost surely defect-free)}$$

## 13. Python Implementation

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import poisson

# ── 1. Simulate 2D Homogeneous PPP ─────────────────────────────────────
def simulate_2d_ppp(lam, region=(0, 1, 0, 1)):
    """
    Simulate homogeneous PPP(lambda) on rectangle [x0,x1] x [y0,y1]
    """
    x0, x1, y0, y1 = region
    area = (x1 - x0) * (y1 - y0)
    n_points = np.random.poisson(lam * area)
    x = np.random.uniform(x0, x1, n_points)
    y = np.random.uniform(y0, y1, n_points)
    return x, y

# ── 2. Simulate 2D Inhomogeneous PPP (thinning method) ─────────────────
def simulate_2d_inhomogeneous_ppp(lam_func, lam_max, region=(0, 1, 0, 1)):
    """
    Simulate inhomogeneous PPP via thinning:
    1. Generate homogeneous PPP(lam_max)
    2. Thin each point: keep with prob lam_func(x,y) / lam_max
    """
    x0, x1, y0, y1 = region
    # Step 1: homogeneous envelope
    x, y = simulate_2d_ppp(lam_max, region)
    # Step 2: thin
    keep_prob = lam_func(x, y) / lam_max
    keep = np.random.uniform(size=len(x)) < keep_prob
    return x[keep], y[keep]

# ── 3. Nearest Neighbour Distance ───────────────────────────────────────
def nearest_neighbour_distances(x, y):
    """Compute nearest neighbour distance for each point"""
    from scipy.spatial import KDTree
    points = np.column_stack([x, y])
    tree = KDTree(points)
    dists, _ = tree.query(points, k=2)  # k=2: self + nearest
    return dists[:, 1]

# ── 4. Verify Nearest Neighbour Distribution ────────────────────────────
lam = 50  # points per unit area
x, y = simulate_2d_ppp(lam, region=(0, 10, 0, 10))
nn_dists = nearest_neighbour_distances(x, y)

r = np.linspace(0, 0.5, 200)
# Theoretical: f_R(r) = 2*pi*lambda*r * exp(-pi*lambda*r^2)
theoretical_pdf = 2 * np.pi * lam * r * np.exp(-np.pi * lam * r**2)

plt.figure(figsize=(12, 4))

plt.subplot(1, 3, 1)
plt.scatter(x, y, s=2, alpha=0.5)
plt.title(f'2D PPP(λ={lam})\nn={len(x)} points')
plt.xlabel('x'); plt.ylabel('y')

plt.subplot(1, 3, 2)
plt.hist(nn_dists, bins=40, density=True, alpha=0.7, label='Empirical')
plt.plot(r, theoretical_pdf, 'r-', lw=2, label='Rayleigh theory')
plt.xlabel('Nearest neighbour distance')
plt.title('NN Distance Distribution')
plt.legend()

# Inhomogeneous: Gaussian intensity
plt.subplot(1, 3, 3)
lam_func = lambda x, y: 200 * np.exp(-((x-5)**2 + (y-5)**2) / 4)
xi, yi = simulate_2d_inhomogeneous_ppp(lam_func, lam_max=200,
                                        region=(0, 10, 0, 10))
plt.scatter(xi, yi, s=2, alpha=0.5, c='darkgreen')
plt.title(f'Inhomogeneous PPP\n(Gaussian intensity, n={len(xi)})')
plt.xlabel('x'); plt.ylabel('y')

plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()

# ── 5. Verify Conditioning Theorem ─────────────────────────────────────
def verify_conditioning_2d(lam=5, n_target=10, n_sim=3000):
    """Given N(A)=n, points should be Uniform(A)"""
    x0, x1, y0, y1 = 0, 4, 0, 4
    conditional_x, conditional_y = [], []
    
    while len(conditional_x) < n_sim:
        x, y = simulate_2d_ppp(lam, region=(x0, x1, y0, y1))
        if len(x) == n_target:
            conditional_x.extend(x)
            conditional_y.extend(y)
    
    # Should be Uniform(0,4) x Uniform(0,4)
    print(f"E[x] = {np.mean(conditional_x[:n_sim*n_target]):.3f} | "
          f"Theoretical: {(x0+x1)/2:.3f}")
    print(f"E[y] = {np.mean(conditional_y[:n_sim*n_target]):.3f} | "
          f"Theoretical: {(y0+y1)/2:.3f}")

verify_conditioning_2d()
```

## 14. Connection to ML and CS229

### 14.1 Stochastic Geometry in Wireless Networks

Base stations: PPP($\lambda_b$) in 2D. Users: PPP($\lambda_u$) in 2D. Independent.

Coverage probability (probability a random user is covered by at least one base station within radius $r$):

$$P(\text{covered}) = 1 - e^{-\lambda_b \pi r^2}$$

This is the **empty space function** — the PPP model makes this analytically tractable.

### 14.2 Object Detection / Point Cloud Processing

LiDAR point clouds are often modelled as inhomogeneous PPPs. Detecting an object = testing $H_0$: PPP vs $H_1$: non-PPP clustering in a region.

### 14.3 Neural Processes and Spike Trains

Neuron spike trains: modelled as inhomogeneous PPP($\lambda(t)$) where $\lambda(t)$ is the **firing rate** (a function of stimulus). Learning $\lambda(t)$ from data = density estimation problem.

### 14.4 Gaussian Processes and PPP

The **Cox Process** (doubly stochastic Poisson process): $\lambda(x)$ is itself a random field (e.g., a log-Gaussian process). Used in Bayesian spatial statistics and GP-based generative models in PRML Ch.3/6.

## 17. Key Formulas Cheatsheet

$$\boxed{N(A) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda |A|) \quad \text{(homogeneous, } d\text{D)}}$$

$$\boxed{N(A) \sim \text{Poisson}!\left(\int_A \lambda(x),dx\right) \quad \text{(inhomogeneous)}}$$

$$\boxed{(x_1,\ldots,x_n) \mid N(A) = n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Uniform}(A)}$$

$$\boxed{P(R > r) = e^{-\lambda \omega_d r^d}, \quad \omega_d = \frac{\pi^{d/2}}{\Gamma(d/2+1)}}$$

$$\boxed{f_R(r) = \lambda \omega_d d, r^{d-1} e^{-\lambda \omega_d r^d}}$$

$$\boxed{E!\left[\sum_{x_i} f(x_i)\right] = \int f(x),\lambda(x),dx \quad \text{(Campbell's theorem)}}$$

## 18. Mental Model

> Imagine a cosmic dart-thrower standing outside all of space, throwing darts **uniformly at random** into $\mathbb{R}^d$ at rate $\lambda$ per unit volume. Each dart lands independently of all others, with no memory of where previous darts landed.
> 
> - Ask "how many darts in region $A$?" → Poisson($\lambda|A|$)
> - Ask "given $n$ darts in $A$, where are they?" → iid Uniform($A$)
> - Ask "how far to the nearest dart?" → depends on $d$-dimensional ball geometry
> 
> The geometry of space enters only through the **volume of regions and balls**. The Poisson randomness is purely in the count; the spatial structure is purely in the uniform scatter.
