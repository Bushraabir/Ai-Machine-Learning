## 1. The Statement

If you have $n$ **independent** Poisson processes:

$$N_1(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_1), \quad N_2(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_2), \quad \ldots, \quad N_n(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_n)$$

then their **merged (superposed) process** is also a Poisson process:

$$\boxed{N(t) = N_1(t) + N_2(t) + \cdots + N_n(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n)}$$

The merged process has rate $\Lambda = \sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i$.

---

## 2. Why This Is Non-Trivial

Summing two random processes is easy. The surprise is that **the result is still a Poisson process** — not just a process with the right mean, but one satisfying **all three Poisson process axioms**:

- Independent increments ✓
- Stationary increments ✓
- Poisson-distributed counts ✓

This closure property is special. Summing two Binomial processes, for example, does **not** generally give a Binomial process.

---

## 3. Proof — Three Routes

### Route 1: MGF / PGF (Cleanest)

The **Probability Generating Function** of $\text{Poisson}(\mu)$ is:

$$G(z) = E[z^N] = e^{\mu(z-1)}$$

For $N = N_1 + N_2$ with $N_1 \perp N_2$:

$$G_N(z) = E[z^{N_1 + N_2}] = E[z^{N_1}] \cdot E[z^{N_2}]$$

$$= e^{\lambda_1 t(z-1)} \cdot e^{\lambda_2 t(z-1)} = e^{(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)t(z-1)}$$

This is the PGF of $\text{Poisson}((\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)t)$. $\checkmark$

> **Key step:** Independence allows factoring $E[z^{N_1+N_2}] = E[z^{N_1}]E[z^{N_2}]$. The PGF uniquely determines the distribution.

---

### Route 2: Direct PMF Convolution

$$P(N_1(t) + N_2(t) = k) = \sum_{j=0}^{k} P(N_1 = j) \cdot P(N_2 = k-j)$$

$$= \sum_{j=0}^{k} \frac{e^{-\lambda_1 t}(\lambda_1 t)^j}{j!} \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda_2 t}(\lambda_2 t)^{k-j}}{(k-j)!}$$

$$= e^{-(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)t} \sum_{j=0}^{k} \frac{(\lambda_1 t)^j (\lambda_2 t)^{k-j}}{j!(k-j)!}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)t}}{k!} \sum_{j=0}^{k} \binom{k}{j} (\lambda_1 t)^j (\lambda_2 t)^{k-j}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)t} \cdot [(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)t]^k}{k!} \checkmark$$

by the **Binomial theorem**: $\sum_{j=0}^k \binom{k}{j} a^j b^{k-j} = (a+b)^k$.

---

### Route 3: Infinitesimal Definition (Most Rigorous)

Check the three axioms for $N(t) = N_1(t) + N_2(t)$:

**Independent increments:** For disjoint intervals $[s,t]$ and $[u,v]$:

- $N(t)-N(s) = [N_1(t)-N_1(s)] + [N_2(t)-N_2(s)]$, independent of
- $N(v)-N(u) = [N_1(v)-N_1(u)] + [N_2(v)-N_2(u)]$

Since $N_1 \perp N_2$ and each has independent increments, the sum inherits this. ✓

**Stationary increments:** Each component is stationary, so their sum is. ✓

**No simultaneous events:** $$P(\text{both } N_1 \text{ and } N_2 \text{ jump in } dt) = (\lambda_1 , dt)(\lambda_2 , dt) = \lambda_1 \lambda_2 (dt)^2 = o(dt) \checkmark$$

So at most one event occurs per infinitesimal interval. ✓

All three axioms satisfied $\Rightarrow$ $N$ is a Poisson process with rate $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2$. $\checkmark$

---

## 4. The Labeling / Type Probability

When you merge two streams, a natural question arises:

> **Given that an event just occurred in the merged process, what is the probability it came from stream $i$?**

$$\boxed{P(\text{event from stream } i) = \frac{\lambda_i}{\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n}}$$

**Proof for 2 streams:** In any infinitesimal interval $dt$:

$$P(\text{event from } N_1) = \lambda_1 , dt, \quad P(\text{event from } N_2) = \lambda_2 , dt$$

Conditioning on the merged event occurring:

$$P(\text{type 1} \mid \text{event}) = \frac{\lambda_1 , dt}{(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2) , dt} = \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_1 + \lambda_2}$$

This is **constant** — it does not depend on time, history, or how many events have occurred.

---

## 5. The Full Picture — Superposition + Thinning Duality

These two theorems are **inverses of each other**:

```
                    Thinning (p, 1-p)
          ┌──────────────────────────────────┐
          │                                  ▼
   PP(λ) ──┤                           PP(λp)  ⊥  PP(λ(1-p))
          │                                  │
          └──────────────────────────────────┘
                   Superposition (+)
```

|Operation|Input|Output|
|---|---|---|
|**Superposition**|$n$ independent PP's with rates $\lambda_i$|Single PP with rate $\sum \lambda_i$|
|**Thinning**|Single PP($\lambda$), keep each event w.p. $p$|Two **independent** PP's: PP($\lambda p$) and PP($\lambda(1-p)$)|

> **Deep insight:** Superposition and thinning together show that the Poisson process is **closed** under both merging and splitting — a hallmark of its mathematical elegance.

---

## 6. The Competition / Race Theorem

Two independent Poisson processes $N_1 \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_1)$ and $N_2 \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_2)$ are running simultaneously. What is the probability that the **first event overall** comes from $N_1$?

**Method 1 — Exponential race:**

Let $T_1 \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda_1)$ and $T_2 \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda_2)$ be the first arrival times, independent.

$$P(T_1 < T_2) = \int_0^\infty P(T_2 > t) \cdot f_{T_1}(t) , dt$$

$$= \int_0^\infty e^{-\lambda_2 t} \cdot \lambda_1 e^{-\lambda_1 t} , dt = \lambda_1 \int_0^\infty e^{-(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)t} , dt = \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}$$

$$\boxed{P(T_1 < T_2) = \frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_1 + \lambda_2}}$$

**Method 2 — Labeling theorem (1 line):**

The first event of the merged PP($\lambda_1 + \lambda_2$) is type 1 with probability $\frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_1+\lambda_2}$, by the labeling result above. Same answer.

> **Generalization:** For $n$ independent exponentials, $\min(T_1, \ldots, T_n) \sim \text{Exp}(\sum \lambda_i)$ and $P(T_i = \min) = \frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_j \lambda_j}$.

## 7. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — Call Center

Two call centers A and B independently receive calls at rates $\lambda_A = 4$/min and $\lambda_B = 6$/min. Calls are routed to a single queue.

- Merged arrival rate: $\Lambda = 10$/min
- $P(\text{next call is from A}) = 4/10 = 0.4$
- $P(\text{0 calls in 30 seconds}) = e^{-10 \times 0.5} = e^{-5} \approx 0.0067$
- $E[\text{time until 3rd call}] = 3/10 = 0.3$ min

### Example 2 — Competing Servers

Two servers each fail independently: Server 1 at rate $\lambda_1 = 0.1$/hour, Server 2 at rate $\lambda_2 = 0.2$/hour.

- $P(\text{Server 1 fails first}) = \frac{0.1}{0.3} = \frac{1}{3}$
- $E[\text{time to first failure}] = \frac{1}{0.3} \approx 3.33$ hours
- After Server 1 fails, Server 2 continues at rate 0.2/hour independently

### Example 3 — n Streams

Customers arrive from $n$ cities with rates $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$. Merged rate $\Lambda = \sum \lambda_i$.

Given a customer just arrived, the probability they are from city $k$:

$$P(\text{city } k) = \frac{\lambda_k}{\Lambda}$$

This is a **Categorical distribution** over cities — the arrival is like drawing a city label with these probabilities, independent of everything else.

## 8. Common Mistakes

|Mistake|Correction|
|---|---|
|Forgetting independence requirement|Superposition requires $N_1 \perp N_2$. Dependent processes don't merge cleanly.|
|Thinking rates add only for counts|The **full process** (interarrivals, order statistics) is Poisson at rate $\Lambda$, not just counts at one time.|
|Confusing superposition with thinning|Superposition: $n \to 1$ (merge). Thinning: $1 \to n$ (split). They are inverses.|
|Assuming labeled streams are dependent post-merge|After merging, the type labels are **independent** of the merged process's timing.|

```python
import numpy as np

def simulate_superposition(lambda1, lambda2, T, n_sim=10000):
    """Verify superposition: merged rate should be lambda1 + lambda2"""
    counts = []
    for _ in range(n_sim):
        # Generate arrivals from two independent streams
        n1 = np.random.poisson(lambda1 * T)
        n2 = np.random.poisson(lambda2 * T)
        counts.append(n1 + n2)
    
    empirical_mean = np.mean(counts)
    theoretical_mean = (lambda1 + lambda2) * T
    
    print(f"Empirical mean: {empirical_mean:.4f}")
    print(f"Theoretical mean: {theoretical_mean:.4f}")
    return counts

counts = simulate_superposition(lambda1=3, lambda2=5, T=1)
# Should verify: empirical ≈ 8
```

## 11. Key Formulas Cheatsheet

$$\boxed{\sum_{i=1}^n \text{PP}(\lambda_i) = \text{PP}!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\right) \quad \text{(if mutually independent)}}$$

$$\boxed{P(\text{event is type } i) = \frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_j \lambda_j}}$$

$$\boxed{P(T_i = \min_j T_j) = \frac{\lambda_i}{\sum_j \lambda_j}, \quad \min_j T_j \sim \text{Exp}!\left(\sum_j \lambda_j\right)}$$

$$\boxed{G_{\text{Poisson}(\mu)}(z) = e^{\mu(z-1)}, \quad G_{N_1+N_2}(z) = G_{N_1}(z) \cdot G_{N_2}(z)}$$

## 12. Mental Model

> Imagine $n$ people independently and randomly throwing darts at the same timeline. Superposition asks: what does the **combined dart board** look like? Answer: it's exactly what you'd get if **one person** threw darts at rate $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 + \cdots + \lambda_n$ — the sources become completely indistinguishable except for the fixed probability labels.

The merged process has **no memory of where events came from** — only the rate changes.
