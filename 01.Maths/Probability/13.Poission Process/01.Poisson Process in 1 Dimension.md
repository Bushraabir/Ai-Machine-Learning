## 1. The Core Intuition

A **Poisson Process** on the real line (time axis) models **random arrivals** — events that occur:

- **Independently** of each other
- At a **constant average rate** $\lambda$ (events per unit time)
- Such that **no two events happen simultaneously**

> **Physical picture:** Buses arriving at a stop, photons hitting a detector, emails in your inbox, radioactive decay — all modeled as Poisson processes.

```
Time axis:
─────┼──────────┼────┼───────────────┼───┼──────────▶
     t₁         t₂   t₃              t₄  t₅
     
Interarrivals: X₁  X₂   X₃              X₄  X₅  ~ Exp(λ) iid
```

## 2. Three Equivalent Definitions

These are **not three different things** — they are three lenses on the same object. Mastering all three = mastery of the Poisson process.

---

### Definition 1 — Counting Process Definition

A counting process ${N(t), t \geq 0}$ is a **Poisson Process** with rate $\lambda > 0$ if:

1. $N(0) = 0$
2. **Independent increments:** For $0 \leq s < t \leq u < v$: $$N(t) - N(s) \perp N(v) - N(u)$$
3. **Stationary increments:** $N(t+s) - N(s) \sim N(t) - N(0)$ (distribution depends only on length)
4. **Poisson counts:** $$N(t) - N(s) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda(t-s))$$

So the number of arrivals in any interval of length $\ell$ is: $$\boxed{N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda \ell)}$$

$$P(N(t) = k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^k}{k!}, \quad k = 0,1,2,\ldots$$

---

### Definition 2 — Interarrival Definition (Constructive)

Let $X_1, X_2, X_3, \ldots \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ be the **waiting times between consecutive arrivals**.

Define arrival times (partial sums): $$T_n = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$$

Then $N(t) = \max{n : T_n \leq t}$ defines a Poisson process.

> **Why Exponential?** The only memoryless continuous distribution. This is what makes the Poisson process "stationary" — the process has no memory of when the last event occurred.

$$\boxed{P(X > t + s \mid X > s) = P(X > t)}$$

---

### Definition 3 — Infinitesimal Definition

For a small interval $[t, t+dt]$:

$$P(N(t+dt) - N(t) = 1) = \lambda , dt + o(dt)$$ $$P(N(t+dt) - N(t) = 0) = 1 - \lambda , dt + o(dt)$$ $$P(N(t+dt) - N(t) \geq 2) = o(dt)$$

This says: in a tiny window, at most one event can occur, with probability proportional to $\lambda , dt$.

> This is the definition used to **derive** the Poisson PMF from first principles (Stat 110 style).

## 3. Key Distributions

### 3.1 Number of Arrivals in $[0, t]$

$$N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$$

$$E[N(t)] = \lambda t, \qquad \text{Var}[N(t)] = \lambda t$$

### 3.2 Waiting Time to $n$-th Arrival

$$T_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$$

$$f_{T_n}(t) = \frac{\lambda^n t^{n-1} e^{-\lambda t}}{(n-1)!}, \quad t > 0$$

$$E[T_n] = \frac{n}{\lambda}, \qquad \text{Var}[T_n] = \frac{n}{\lambda^2}$$

### 3.3 Waiting Time to First Arrival

$$T_1 \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda), \quad E[T_1] = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

---

## 4. The Superposition and Thinning Theorems

These are the **power tools** of Poisson processes.

### 4.1 Superposition (Merging)

If $N_1(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_1)$ and $N_2(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_2)$ are **independent**, then:

$$N_1(t) + N_2(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$$

> Merging two independent Poisson streams gives a faster Poisson stream.

### 4.2 Thinning (Splitting)

Take a $\text{PP}(\lambda)$. Independently mark each arrival as **type A** with probability $p$, or **type B** with probability $1-p$.

Then: $$N_A(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda p), \qquad N_B(t) \sim \text{PP}(\lambda(1-p))$$

And crucially: $N_A \perp N_B$.

> **Intuition:** Randomizing a Poisson process gives independent Poisson processes — the randomness "decomposes cleanly."

---

## 5. Conditioning — The Uniform Order Statistics Property

This is the **deepest and most beautiful** fact about the Poisson process.

**Theorem:** Given $N(t) = n$, the $n$ arrival times $T_1, T_2, \ldots, T_n$ are distributed as the **order statistics** of $n$ iid $\text{Uniform}(0, t)$ random variables.

$$\boxed{(T_1, \ldots, T_n) \mid N(t) = n ;\overset{d}{=}; (U_{(1)}, \ldots, U_{(n)})}$$

where $U_{(1)} < U_{(2)} < \cdots < U_{(n)}$ are sorted draws from $\text{Uniform}(0, t)$.

**Consequence:** If you know exactly $n$ events happened in $[0, t]$, each event's location is a **uniform draw** on $[0, t]$, independent of the others (before sorting).

> **Intuition:** Conditioning on the count completely removes the "Poisson randomness" — all that remains is uniformly scattered points.

## 6. Worked Examples

### Example 1 — No Arrivals

Buses arrive at rate $\lambda = 3$/hour. What is the probability of waiting more than 30 minutes for the first bus?

$$P(T_1 > 0.5) = e^{-\lambda \cdot 0.5} = e^{-1.5} \approx 0.223$$

### Example 2 — Counting Arrivals

Emails arrive at rate $\lambda = 5$/hour. What is the probability of receiving exactly 3 emails in the next 30 minutes?

$$\lambda t = 5 \times 0.5 = 2.5$$ $$P(N = 3) = \frac{e^{-2.5}(2.5)^3}{3!} = \frac{e^{-2.5} \cdot 15.625}{6} \approx 0.2138$$

### Example 3 — Thinning

Customers arrive at rate $\lambda = 10$/hour. Each is independently a "premium" customer with probability $p = 0.2$.

- Premium customers arrive as $\text{PP}(2)$ per hour
- Regular customers arrive as $\text{PP}(8)$ per hour
- These two streams are **independent**

### Example 4 — Uniform Order Statistics (Stat 110 Classic)

Given that exactly 1 person arrives between 12:00 and 13:00, what is the distribution of their arrival time?

$$T_1 \mid N(1) = 1 \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$$

The arrival time is uniformly distributed across the hour — no "bunching" near start or end.

## 7. Derivation of Poisson PMF from Definition 3

Let $p_k(t) = P(N(t) = k)$.

From the infinitesimal definition, write the **forward Kolmogorov equations**:

$$p_0'(t) = -\lambda p_0(t)$$ $$p_k'(t) = \lambda p_{k-1}(t) - \lambda p_k(t), \quad k \geq 1$$

**Solve for $p_0$:** $$p_0(t) = e^{-\lambda t}$$

**Solve recursively using integrating factor:** $$p_k(t) = \frac{(\lambda t)^k e^{-\lambda t}}{k!} \checkmark$$

## 8. Poisson Process vs. Poisson Distribution

||Poisson Distribution|Poisson Process|
|---|---|---|
|**Type**|Discrete RV|Stochastic process|
|**Input**|Fixed parameter $\mu$|Rate $\lambda$, time $t$|
|**Output**|Count $N$|Count $N(t)$ for all $t$|
|**Relationship**|$N \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu)$|$N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$|
|**Extra structure**|None|Interarrivals, order statistics|

> The Poisson distribution is a **snapshot**. The Poisson process is the **full movie**.

---

## 9. Poisson Process on a Line vs. Time

The 1D Poisson process applies equally to:

- **Time axis:** Events at random moments
- **Spatial axis:** Points scattered on a line (e.g., defects on a wire, stars projected onto a line)

For spatial: replace $t$ with length $\ell$, and $\lambda$ = events per unit length.

$$N(\ell) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda \ell)$$

## 12. Key Formulas Cheatsheet

$$\boxed{N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t)}$$

$$\boxed{P(N(t)=k) = \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^k}{k!}}$$

$$\boxed{X_i \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Exp}(\lambda), \quad E[X_i] = \frac{1}{\lambda}}$$

$$\boxed{T_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n,\lambda), \quad E[T_n] = \frac{n}{\lambda}}$$

$$\boxed{(T_1,\ldots,T_n) \mid N(t) = n ;\overset{d}{=}; U_{(1:n)} \sim \text{Uniform}(0,t)}$$

$$\boxed{\text{PP}(\lambda_1) \perp \text{PP}(\lambda_2) \Rightarrow \text{merged} \sim \text{PP}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)}$$

$$\boxed{\text{PP}(\lambda) \xrightarrow{\text{thin } p} \text{PP}(\lambda p) \perp \text{PP}(\lambda(1-p))}$$

## 13. Mental Model

> Think of the Poisson process as **nature randomly throwing darts at the time axis**, where:
> 
> - The rate $\lambda$ controls the **density** of darts
> - No two darts land at exactly the same spot
> - Each dart is thrown **independently** of all others
> - Zooming into any sub-interval gives you the **same structure**, just scaled

This self-similarity and independence is what makes the Poisson process the **canonical model for randomness in time**.
