Then I saw the same pattern in city populations, income distributions, protein interaction frequencies, and earthquake magnitudes. At that point it stops being a coincidence and starts being a structural property of how complex systems organize themselves. The mathematics is simple. The reason it's everywhere is not."_

## The Distribution

Let $X$ take values in ${1, 2, \ldots, n}$ (finite support) or ${1, 2, 3, \ldots}$ (infinite support). We say $X \sim \text{Zipf}(s, n)$ if:

$$P(X = k) = \frac{k^{-s}}{H_{n,s}}, \qquad k = 1, 2, \ldots, n$$

where $s > 0$ is the **exponent** (also called the shape or skewness parameter) and $H_{n,s}$ is the normalizing constant — the **generalized harmonic number**:

$$H_{n,s} = \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{1}{j^s} = 1 + \frac{1}{2^s} + \frac{1}{3^s} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n^s}$$

For the **infinite support** case, we need $s > 1$ for the sum to converge (by the $p$-series test from real analysis):

$$H_{\infty, s} = \sum_{j=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{j^s} = \zeta(s)$$

the **Riemann zeta function**. So $P(X = k) = k^{-s}/\zeta(s)$ for $k \geq 1$, valid when $s > 1$.

> _Notice that $s = 1$ is the canonical "Zipf's law" exponent — the rank-frequency relationship George Zipf observed in English text. But at $s = 1$, the infinite-support version diverges: $\sum_{k=1}^\infty 1/k = \infty$ (the harmonic series). So the pure $s=1$ law only makes sense with finite support. When people say "Zipf's law" loosely, they usually mean the finite-$n$ version with $s$ near 1._

## Properties

### Moments

For the infinite support case ($s > 1$), the $r$-th moment is:

$$E[X^r] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k^r \cdot \frac{k^{-s}}{\zeta(s)} = \frac{\zeta(s-r)}{\zeta(s)}$$

This is **finite only when $s - r > 1$**, i.e., $r < s - 1$.

Consequently:

- **Mean** $E[X] = \zeta(s-1)/\zeta(s)$, finite iff $s > 2$.
- **Variance** finite iff $s > 3$.
- For $1 < s \leq 2$: the mean is **infinite**. The distribution is so heavy-tailed that no first moment exists.

This is striking. I'm so used to distributions where the mean "obviously" exists. Zipf with $s \leq 2$ has no mean — a realization that should make me uncomfortable in a productive way.

**For finite support**, all moments exist (finite sum of finite terms), but they blow up as $n \to \infty$ when $s$ is small.

### The Power Law Tail

The defining feature: $P(X \geq k) \sim Ck^{-(s-1)}$ as $k \to \infty$ (for infinite support, $s > 1$). This is a **power-law tail** — much heavier than exponential tails (like Geometric or Poisson) which decay as $e^{-ck}$.

**Intuitive comparison of tail heaviness:**

|Distribution|$P(X \geq k)$ decay|Has all moments?|
|---|---|---|
|Geometric$(p)$|$\sim (1-p)^k$ (exponential)|Yes|
|Poisson$(\lambda)$|faster than exponential|Yes|
|Zipf$(s)$, $s>1$|$\sim Ck^{-(s-1)}$ (power law)|Only finitely many|
|Pareto|power law|Same phenomenon|

Power law tails are the mathematical signature of **rare but catastrophic events** — the occasional word that appears a million times, the city that dwarfs all others, the earthquake that releases more energy than all smaller ones combined.

## Why Zipf's Law Appears Everywhere: A Sketch

This is the question I keep coming back to. A few mechanisms that generate Zipf-like distributions:

**1. Preferential attachment (Yule-Simon process).** New items arrive and choose an existing category $k$ with probability proportional to how many items already chose $k$ (the "rich get richer" mechanism). This generates power-law distributions. Used to explain city size growth, citation networks, degree distributions in scale-free graphs.

**2. Optimization under constraints.** If rational agents minimize a communication cost that is proportional to the information content of messages, and messages are drawn i.i.d., the optimal code length gives a rank-frequency relation that approximates Zipf. This is Mandelbrot's argument for language.

**3. Mixtures of exponentials.** A mixture of exponential distributions where the rate parameter has a power-law distribution produces a power-law marginal. Mathematically: if $X \mid \lambda \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$ and $\lambda \sim \text{Pareto}(s)$, then $X$ has a power-law distribution.

**4. Products of random variables.** By the central limit theorem, sums of i.i.d. random variables converge to Normal. Products of positive i.i.d. random variables (or equivalently, sums of their logs) converge to Log-Normal, which approximates power laws over finite ranges.

None of these mechanisms is definitive — there's no consensus "why Zipf." What's agreed is that power laws are **attractors** in certain generative processes, just as the Normal is an attractor under addition.

> _The analogy to the CLT is imprecise but useful. The CLT tells me that summation produces Gaussians. Various "multiplicative" or "preferential" processes produce power laws. The deep reason Zipf is everywhere might be that those processes are as ubiquitous as summation._

## Simulation

### Method 1: Inverse CDF (Exact)

For finite support ${1, \ldots, n}$, I can sample exactly using the inverse CDF method, which I know from [[Inverse CDF Method]].

**Setup:** Compute the CDF:

$$F(k) = P(X \leq k) = \frac{1}{H_{n,s}} \sum_{j=1}^{k} j^{-s}$$

**Algorithm:**

1. Compute and store the CDF values $F(1), F(2), \ldots, F(n)$ (precomputation — $O(n)$ time and space).
2. Draw $U \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$.
3. Return $k^* = \min{k : F(k) \geq U}$ — binary search in the CDF table, $O(\log n)$ per draw.

**Correctness:** $P(k^* = k) = P(F(k-1) < U \leq F(k)) = F(k) - F(k-1) = P(X = k)$. Exact by construction.

**Pseudocode:**

```python
import numpy as np

def zipf_sample_inverse_cdf(s, n, size=1):
    # Precompute CDF
    k_vals = np.arange(1, n+1)
    pmf = k_vals**(-s)
    pmf /= pmf.sum()           # normalize by H_{n,s}
    cdf = np.cumsum(pmf)
    
    # Sample via inverse CDF
    U = np.random.uniform(size=size)
    # searchsorted finds min index k s.t. cdf[k] >= U
    indices = np.searchsorted(cdf, U)
    return k_vals[indices]     # k_vals[0] = 1, so index 0 -> k=1
```

This is exact and efficient for moderate $n$. For very large $n$ (e.g., $n = 10^9$), storing the CDF is infeasible.

### Method 2: Numpy's Built-In (for Quick Experiments)

NumPy has `numpy.random.zipf(a, size)` which samples from the infinite Zipf distribution with exponent $a = s$, using the rejection sampling algorithm of Devroye (1986). Note: NumPy uses the convention $P(X=k) \propto k^{-a}$, requires $a > 1$.

```python
samples = np.random.zipf(a=2.0, size=10000)
```

**Caution:** For $a$ close to 1 (heavy tails), the expected value is infinite and samples will occasionally be astronomically large — don't be surprised by a sample of $10^7$ when $a = 1.1$.

---

### Method 3: Rejection Sampling (for Large $n$, Continuous Approximation)

For large $n$, I can use the fact that $k^{-s}$ is approximated by the continuous Pareto distribution, and use a Pareto as the envelope.

**Pareto proposal:** $Y \sim \text{Pareto}(s-1, 1)$ has density $f_Y(y) = (s-1)y^{-s}$ for $y \geq 1$. Then $\lfloor Y \rfloor$ has PMF roughly $\propto k^{-s}$. Formalizing this into a proper rejection sampler requires care — see Devroye's _Non-Uniform Random Variate Generation_ (1986), Chapter X.

The key bound: $k^{-s} \leq \int_{k-1}^{k} x^{-s},dx \cdot C$ for an appropriate constant $C$, giving an envelope.

> _I won't derive the full Devroye sampler here — it's the kind of thing where the proof is in a textbook and I trust it works. What matters conceptually: for heavy-tailed distributions, rejection sampling with a continuous heavy-tailed envelope is often the practical approach, because the inverse CDF table is too large to precompute._

## Empirical Verification: The Log-Log Plot

The canonical diagnostic for power-law behavior is the **log-log rank-frequency plot**.

If $P(X = k) \propto k^{-s}$, then $\log P(X=k) = -s \log k + C$, which is **linear** on a log-log scale with slope $-s$.

**Procedure:**

1. Generate $N$ samples from Zipf$(s, n)$.
2. Count empirical frequencies $\hat{f}(k) = (\text{count of } k)/N$.
3. Plot $\log k$ vs $\log \hat{f}(k)$. Fit a line — slope should be $\approx -s$.

```python
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from collections import Counter

N = 100_000
s_true = 1.5
n = 1000

samples = zipf_sample_inverse_cdf(s_true, n, size=N)
counts = Counter(samples)

k_vals = np.array(sorted(counts.keys()))
freqs  = np.array([counts[k] for k in k_vals]) / N

# Log-log plot
log_k    = np.log(k_vals)
log_freq = np.log(freqs)

# OLS slope estimate
slope = np.polyfit(log_k, log_freq, 1)[0]
print(f"Estimated exponent: {-slope:.3f} (true: {s_true})")

plt.figure()
plt.scatter(log_k, log_freq, s=5, alpha=0.6)
plt.xlabel("log rank $k$")
plt.ylabel("log frequency")
plt.title(f"Zipf($s={s_true}$, $n={n}$) — estimated $s={-slope:.2f}$")
plt.tight_layout()
plt.show()
```

On a log-log scale, the Zipf PMF should look like a straight line. Deviations at the tail (large $k$) are expected — counts become sparse and noisy.

**A subtlety I keep getting confused about:** The rank-frequency plot (Zipf's original framing) plots rank $r$ on the $x$-axis vs frequency $f(r)$ on the $y$-axis, where rank 1 = most common word. This is _not_ the same as plotting $k$ vs $P(X=k)$ when the support is not ${1,\ldots,n}$ in sorted order. For simulated data, these coincide. For word frequency data, rank 1 corresponds to the most frequent word, which has some empirical frequency $f$, not PMF value $P(X=1)$.

## Moment Estimation and the Traps of Heavy Tails

Suppose I've generated samples $X_1, \ldots, X_N \sim \text{Zipf}(s, n)$ and want to estimate $s$.

**Method 1: Log-log regression.** Estimate the slope of the log-log rank-frequency plot (as above). Simple but biased — OLS on the log scale is not maximum likelihood for the Poisson-noise model that underlies count data.

**Method 2: Maximum Likelihood.** For the finite-support Zipf, the log-likelihood is:

$$\ell(s) = -s \sum_{i=1}^N \log X_i - N \log H_{n,s}$$

Taking the derivative and setting to zero:

$$\frac{d\ell}{ds} = -\sum_{i=1}^N \log X_i - N \cdot \frac{d}{ds}\log H_{n,s} = 0$$

$$\Rightarrow \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^N \log X_i = -\frac{H_{n,s}'}{H_{n,s}}, \quad H_{n,s}' = \frac{d H_{n,s}}{ds} = -\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{\log j}{j^s}$$

There's no closed form — solve numerically (Newton-Raphson or `scipy.optimize`). The MLE is consistent and asymptotically efficient.

**The trap: sample mean is unreliable when $s \leq 2$.**

When the true mean is infinite (or very large), the sample mean $\bar{X}_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum X_i$ does **not** converge to a finite limit as $N \to \infty$ — the LLN doesn't apply without a finite mean. Instead, the sample mean grows with $N$ (slowly, as a power of $N$). This means:

- Do **not** estimate $s$ from the sample mean using $E[X] = \zeta(s-1)/\zeta(s)$ when $s$ is suspected to be $\leq 2$ — the estimator is inconsistent.
- The MLE (based on $\sum \log X_i$, not $\sum X_i$) is fine — $\log X$ has finite mean for all $s > 1$.

> _This is the moment I realize why heavy-tailed distributions require different tools. Everything I learned about estimation assumes finite moments — LLN, CLT, method of moments. All of it breaks for $s \leq 2$. This isn't a technicality; it's empirically relevant. Pareto-distributed income data has $s$ estimated around 1.5–2 depending on the country and year — right in the regime where the mean barely exists or doesn't exist at all. "Average income" statistics for such distributions are misleading in a deep, mathematically precise sense._

## Connection to the Pareto Distribution

The **Pareto distribution** is the continuous analogue: $Y \sim \text{Pareto}(\alpha, x_m)$ has PDF $f(y) = \alpha x_m^\alpha y^{-(\alpha+1)}$ for $y \geq x_m$. The tail: $P(Y \geq y) = (x_m/y)^\alpha$ — a power law.

The relationship to Zipf: if I discretize the Pareto — i.e., $X = \lceil Y \rceil$ for $Y \sim \text{Pareto}(s-1, 1)$ — then $X$ has a distribution approximately Zipf$(s)$ for large values. More precisely:

$$P(X = k) = P(k-1 < Y \leq k) = (k-1)^{-(s-1)} - k^{-(s-1)} \approx (s-1)k^{-s} \text{ for large } k$$

which is proportional to $k^{-s}$. So Pareto is the continuous version, Zipf is the discrete version. This is the same relationship as Exponential (continuous) vs Geometric (discrete).

## Connections to Other Topics

- **[[Birth-Death Chain]]:** The Glauber dynamics for certain statistical physics models produce Zipf-like stationary distributions for system observables.
- **[[Markov Chain Convergence Theorem]]:** Simulating from Zipf via MCMC (as an alternative to inverse CDF) — when would I ever do this? If the state space is structured (e.g., words in a language with transition probabilities), I might want a chain whose stationary distribution is Zipf. This connects to the [[Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm]] note.
- **[[Stationary Distributions]]:** Zipf arises as the stationary distribution of certain random walks on $\mathbb{Z}_{>0}$ with drift toward low values.
- **Information Theory:** The entropy of Zipf$(s, n)$ is $H(X) = s \cdot E[\log X] + \log H_{n,s}$. For $s$ near 1 (nearly uniform on log scale), entropy is high. Zipf with $s=1$ is the **maximum-entropy distribution on the integers subject to a constraint on $E[\log X]$** — analogous to how the Exponential is maximum-entropy given $E[X]$.
