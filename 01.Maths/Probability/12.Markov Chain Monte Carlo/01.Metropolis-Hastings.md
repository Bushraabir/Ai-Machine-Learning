> _"The problem MCMC solves is this: I have a distribution $\pi$ I want to sample from, but I can only evaluate $\pi$ up to a normalizing constant I cannot compute. The normalizing constant might be an integral over a million-dimensional space — completely intractable. Metropolis-Hastings sidesteps this entirely. It constructs a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is $\pi$, using only ratios $\pi(y)/\pi(x)$ — the normalizing constant cancels. Then it runs the chain until it mixes, and treats the samples as approximate draws from $\pi$. The entire algorithm is detailed balance, engineered on purpose."_

---

## The Problem

I want to sample from a distribution $\pi$ on state space $\mathcal{S}$. The distribution is known up to a normalizing constant:

$$\pi(x) = \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x)}{Z}, \qquad Z = \sum_{x \in \mathcal{S}} \tilde{\pi}(x)$$

where I can evaluate $\tilde{\pi}(x)$ for any $x$, but $Z$ is **intractable** — computing it requires summing over all of $\mathcal{S}$, which might be exponentially large or a high-dimensional integral.

**Examples where this happens:**

- **Bayesian posterior:** $\pi(\theta \mid \text{data}) \propto \mathcal{L}(\text{data} \mid \theta) \cdot \pi_0(\theta)$. I can evaluate the likelihood and prior, but the normalizing constant is $\int \mathcal{L} \cdot \pi_0 , d\theta$ — often analytically and computationally intractable.
- **Boltzmann distribution:** $\pi(x) \propto e^{-E(x)/T}$ where $E(x)$ is the energy of configuration $x$. The partition function $Z = \sum_x e^{-E(x)/T}$ sums over exponentially many configurations.
- **Ising model:** $\pi(\sigma) \propto \exp!\left(\beta \sum_{\langle i,j \rangle} \sigma_i \sigma_j\right)$ over spin configurations $\sigma \in {-1,+1}^N$. The $2^N$ term sum for $Z$ is intractable for large $N$.

The key insight: **to check detailed balance, I only need ratios $\pi(y)/\pi(x) = \tilde{\pi}(y)/\tilde{\pi}(x)$, and $Z$ cancels.** This is the entire trick.

---

## The Algorithm

### Ingredients

- **Target distribution:** $\pi$ (known up to constant $Z$).
- **Proposal kernel:** $Q(x, y)$ — a transition matrix I can sample from. At state $x$, I propose moving to $y$ with probability $Q(x,y)$. This is a Markov chain I choose; it does not have to have stationary distribution $\pi$.
- **Acceptance probability:** a function $A(x, y) \in [0,1]$ to be determined.

### One Step of the Algorithm

At current state $X_n = x$:

1. **Propose:** Sample $y \sim Q(x, \cdot)$.
2. **Accept or reject:** With probability $A(x,y)$, set $X_{n+1} = y$. With probability $1 - A(x,y)$, set $X_{n+1} = x$ (stay).

The resulting transition probabilities:

$$T(x,y) = Q(x,y) \cdot A(x,y) \quad \text{for } y \neq x$$ $$T(x,x) = Q(x,x) + \sum_{y \neq x} Q(x,y)(1 - A(x,y)) \quad \text{(stay probability)}$$

### The Metropolis-Hastings Acceptance Ratio

Choose:

$$\boxed{A(x,y) = \min!\left(1,, \frac{\pi(y) Q(y,x)}{\pi(x) Q(x,y)}\right) = \min!\left(1,, \frac{\tilde{\pi}(y) Q(y,x)}{\tilde{\pi}(x) Q(x,y)}\right)}$$

The second equality holds because $Z$ cancels in the ratio. I never need to compute $Z$.

## Why This Works: Detailed Balance

**Theorem.** The Metropolis-Hastings chain with acceptance ratio $A(x,y)$ as above satisfies detailed balance with respect to $\pi$:

$$\pi(x) T(x,y) = \pi(y) T(y,x) \quad \text{for all } x \neq y$$

**Proof.** Fix $x \neq y$. Write:

$$\pi(x) T(x,y) = \pi(x) Q(x,y) A(x,y) = \pi(x) Q(x,y) \min!\left(1, \frac{\pi(y)Q(y,x)}{\pi(x)Q(x,y)}\right)$$

**Case 1:** $\pi(y)Q(y,x) \leq \pi(x)Q(x,y)$.

Then $A(x,y) = \frac{\pi(y)Q(y,x)}{\pi(x)Q(x,y)}$, so:

$$\pi(x) T(x,y) = \pi(x) Q(x,y) \cdot \frac{\pi(y)Q(y,x)}{\pi(x)Q(x,y)} = \pi(y)Q(y,x)$$

In this case, $A(y,x) = \min(1, \pi(x)Q(x,y)/\pi(y)Q(y,x)) = 1$ (since $\pi(x)Q(x,y) \geq \pi(y)Q(y,x)$), so:

$$\pi(y) T(y,x) = \pi(y) Q(y,x) \cdot 1 = \pi(y) Q(y,x)$$

Equal. ✓

**Case 2:** $\pi(y)Q(y,x) > \pi(x)Q(x,y)$. By symmetry of the argument (swap $x \leftrightarrow y$), the same cancellation gives $\pi(x)T(x,y) = \pi(x)Q(x,y) = \pi(y)T(y,x)$. ✓

In both cases, $\pi(x) T(x,y) = \pi(y) T(y,x)$. $\blacksquare$

Since detailed balance holds, $\pi$ is stationary for the Metropolis-Hastings chain — by the theorem from [[Detailed Balance and Reversibility]].

> _The min in the acceptance ratio is not arbitrary — it's the unique choice (up to some flexibility) that satisfies detailed balance while keeping $A(x,y) \in [0,1]$. Here's why: detailed balance requires $\pi(x)Q(x,y)A(x,y) = \pi(y)Q(y,x)A(y,x)$, which means $A(x,y)/A(y,x) = \pi(y)Q(y,x)/\pi(x)Q(x,y)$. I need to find $A(x,y), A(y,x) \in [0,1]$ satisfying this ratio constraint. The solution that maximizes acceptance (minimizes rejection, hence mixes fastest) is to set the larger of $A(x,y), A(y,x)$ equal to 1 and the smaller to the ratio. That gives exactly $\min(1, \cdot)$. Any other valid choice would accept less often and mix more slowly._

## The Metropolis-Hastings Ratio

Define the **MH ratio** (also called the **Hastings ratio**):

$$r(x,y) = \frac{\tilde{\pi}(y) Q(y,x)}{\tilde{\pi}(x) Q(x,y)}$$

Then $A(x,y) = \min(1, r(x,y))$. Reading this ratio:

- **$\tilde{\pi}(y)/\tilde{\pi}(x)$:** How much more probable is the proposed state $y$ than the current state $x$ under the target? If $y$ has higher probability, the ratio $> 1$ — accept for sure. If $y$ has lower probability, the ratio $< 1$ — accept with that probability.
- **$Q(y,x)/Q(x,y)$:** A correction for asymmetric proposals. If the proposal is more likely to go $x \to y$ than $y \to x$, divide by that excess to prevent the chain from over-visiting $y$.

The correction factor $Q(y,x)/Q(x,y)$ is why Metropolis-Hastings generalizes the original Metropolis algorithm (which required symmetric proposals, making this factor 1).

## Special Cases

### Case 1: Metropolis Algorithm (Symmetric Proposals)

If $Q(x,y) = Q(y,x)$ for all $x,y$ — the proposal is symmetric — then $Q(y,x)/Q(x,y) = 1$ and the ratio simplifies to:

$$A(x,y) = \min!\left(1, \frac{\tilde{\pi}(y)}{\tilde{\pi}(x)}\right)$$

This is the original **Metropolis algorithm** (1953). The rule: always accept uphill moves ($\tilde\pi(y) > \tilde\pi(x)$); accept downhill moves with probability $\tilde\pi(y)/\tilde\pi(x)$.

**Example: symmetric random walk proposal.** On $\mathcal{S} = \mathbb{Z}$, propose $y = x + \epsilon$ where $\epsilon \sim \text{Uniform}{-1, +1}$. This is symmetric ($Q(x,y) = Q(y,x) = 1/2$ for adjacent $x,y$). The MH acceptance is $\min(1, \tilde\pi(y)/\tilde\pi(x))$.

### Case 2: Independent Metropolis-Hastings

If $Q(x,y) = Q(y)$ — the proposal doesn't depend on the current state — then:

$$A(x,y) = \min!\left(1, \frac{\tilde\pi(y)/Q(y)}{\tilde\pi(x)/Q(x)}\right) = \min!\left(1, \frac{w(y)}{w(x)}\right)$$

where $w(x) = \tilde\pi(x)/Q(x)$ are **importance weights**. This is **importance sampling** in Markov chain form. It works well when the proposal $Q$ is a good approximation to $\pi$ — the weights $w(x)$ are nearly constant and acceptance rates are high.

### Case 3: Gibbs Sampling

For a distribution $\pi(x_1, \ldots, x_d)$ over a $d$-dimensional state space, **Gibbs sampling** updates one coordinate at a time: at each step, pick coordinate $i$, and sample $x_i$ from its conditional distribution $\pi(x_i \mid x_{-i})$.

**Claim:** Gibbs sampling is a special case of Metropolis-Hastings with acceptance probability always 1.

**Proof.** The Gibbs proposal for coordinate $i$: $Q((x_1,\ldots,x_d), (x_1,\ldots,y_i,\ldots,x_d)) = \pi(y_i \mid x_{-i})$.

The MH ratio:

$$r = \frac{\pi(y_i, x_{-i}) \cdot \pi(x_i \mid y_{-i}=x_{-i})}{\pi(x_i, x_{-i}) \cdot \pi(y_i \mid x_{-i})}$$

Note $\pi(y_i, x_{-i}) = \pi(y_i \mid x_{-i}) \cdot \pi(x_{-i})$ and $\pi(x_i, x_{-i}) = \pi(x_i \mid x_{-i}) \cdot \pi(x_{-i})$:

$$r = \frac{\pi(y_i \mid x_{-i}) \cdot \pi(x_{-i}) \cdot \pi(x_i \mid x_{-i})}{\pi(x_i \mid x_{-i}) \cdot \pi(x_{-i}) \cdot \pi(y_i \mid x_{-i})} = 1$$

So $A = \min(1,1) = 1$: Gibbs always accepts. $\blacksquare$

This makes Gibbs extremely efficient per step — no wasted proposals. The cost is that it requires sampling from each conditional distribution, which may itself be hard. When conditionals are tractable (as in many conjugate Bayesian models), Gibbs is the algorithm of choice.

> _Gibbs sampling is why conjugate priors matter so much in practice. If the prior is conjugate to the likelihood, every conditional in a hierarchical model is a recognizable distribution — Beta, Gamma, Normal — and Gibbs sampling becomes a sequence of trivial draws. Non-conjugate models require Metropolis steps within the Gibbs sweep, producing "Metropolis-within-Gibbs" or "hybrid MCMC." The choice of algorithm is determined by which conditionals are tractable._

## Convergence: When Does the Chain Mix?

For the MH chain to be useful, it must converge to $\pi$. The conditions from [[Markov Chain Convergence Theorem]]:

**Irreducibility:** The chain must be able to reach any state from any other. This depends on the proposal $Q$ — if $Q(x,y) = 0$ for some pair $(x,y)$, the chain might get stuck. Standard choices (random walk proposals on connected graphs, or proposals with full support) ensure irreducibility.

**Aperiodicity:** The chain is aperiodic as long as there is positive probability of staying ($T(x,x) > 0$), which happens whenever there is positive probability of rejection. For the MH chain, $T(x,x) \geq \sum_y Q(x,y)(1-A(x,y)) > 0$ unless every proposal is accepted with probability 1 — which happens only if the proposal already has stationary distribution $\pi$. In all non-trivial cases, there is some rejection, hence a self-loop, hence aperiodicity.

Under these conditions: the MH chain is irreducible, aperiodic, and has stationary distribution $\pi$. By the convergence theorem:

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \pi \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

for any initial distribution $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$.

## The Mixing Time and Practical Considerations

Convergence is guaranteed, but **how fast** is it? The convergence theorem gives no rate. The rate is controlled by the spectral gap $1 - |\lambda_2|$ of $T$, as in [[Mixing Times and Spectral Gap]]. In practice, the mixing time depends on:

**The proposal distribution $Q$:**

- Too narrow (proposals close to current state): high acceptance, but slow exploration. The chain takes small steps and mixes slowly.
- Too wide (proposals far from current state): fast exploration in principle, but proposals often land in low-$\pi$ regions and are rejected. High rejection rate means the chain barely moves.
- The optimal tradeoff (in high dimensions, for Gaussian $\pi$ and Gaussian proposals) is to set the proposal variance such that the acceptance rate is approximately **23.4%** — a classical result in MCMC theory.

**The geometry of $\pi$:**

- **Multimodal distributions:** If $\pi$ has multiple modes separated by low-probability regions, the chain can get trapped in one mode for an exponentially long time. Local proposals rarely cross the low-probability gap.
- **High-dimensional distributions:** In $d$ dimensions, local random-walk proposals explore a $d$-dimensional ball of radius $\epsilon$, but $\pi$ might be concentrated on a lower-dimensional manifold. The effective mixing time scales poorly with $d$ for naive proposals.

**Standard diagnostics:**

- **Trace plots:** Plot $X_n$ vs $n$ for each coordinate. If the chain is mixing well, the trace should look like stationary noise. If it wanders slowly, mixing is poor.
- **$\hat{R}$ statistic (Gelman-Rubin):** Run multiple chains from different starting points; compare within-chain variance to between-chain variance. $\hat{R} \approx 1$ indicates convergence.
- **Autocorrelation:** Compute $\text{Corr}(X_n, X_{n+k})$ as a function of lag $k$. Slow decay indicates slow mixing.

> _The 23.4% acceptance rate result is one of the most cited numbers in computational statistics. It comes from Roberts, Gelman, and Gilks (1997): for a $d$-dimensional target with i.i.d. components and a Gaussian random-walk proposal, the optimal acceptance rate as $d \to \infty$ is $2\Phi(-\sqrt{2} \cdot 2.38^2/2) \approx 0.234$, where $\Phi$ is the standard normal CDF. The optimal proposal standard deviation scales as $2.38/\sqrt{d}$ per coordinate. This tells me something important: in high dimensions, I should be rejecting about 3 out of 4 proposals. If I'm accepting too many, my proposals are too small; if too few, too large._

## Why the Normalizing Constant Doesn't Matter

This deserves its own moment. The MH ratio is:

$$r(x,y) = \frac{\tilde\pi(y) Q(y,x)}{\tilde\pi(x) Q(x,y)} = \frac{(\pi(y) \cdot Z) \cdot Q(y,x)}{(\pi(x) \cdot Z) \cdot Q(x,y)} = \frac{\pi(y) Q(y,x)}{\pi(x) Q(x,y)}$$

$Z$ appears in both numerator and denominator and cancels. The acceptance probability depends only on the **ratio** of $\pi$ values, not their absolute values.

This is deeply useful because in Bayesian inference:

$$\frac{\pi(\theta \mid \text{data})}{\pi(\theta' \mid \text{data})} = \frac{\mathcal{L}(\text{data} \mid \theta) \pi_0(\theta)}{\mathcal{L}(\text{data} \mid \theta') \pi_0(\theta')}$$

The ratio of posteriors equals the ratio of (likelihood $\times$ prior) — no normalization required. I can run MCMC on any posterior as long as I can evaluate the likelihood and prior pointwise, regardless of model complexity.

> _This is the reason MCMC transformed Bayesian statistics in the 1990s. Before MCMC, Bayesian inference was restricted to models with conjugate priors — because otherwise the posterior normalization constant was intractable and there was no way to sample from the posterior. MCMC lifted this restriction entirely. Any likelihood, any prior, any hierarchical model — as long as I can evaluate $\mathcal{L} \cdot \pi_0$ pointwise, I can run MCMC. The price is computational (chains must run long enough to mix), but the gain in modeling flexibility is enormous. It fundamentally changed what statistical models are tractable._

## A Worked Example: Sampling from the Ising Model

The Ising model on an $N$-node graph has states $\sigma \in {-1,+1}^N$ with distribution:

$$\pi(\sigma) = \frac{1}{Z} \exp!\left(\beta \sum_{\langle i,j \rangle} \sigma_i \sigma_j\right)$$

$Z$ involves a sum over $2^N$ configurations — completely intractable for large $N$.

**Gibbs sampling for the Ising model (also called Glauber dynamics):**

At each step:

1. Pick a random site $i \in {1, \ldots, N}$.
2. Compute the conditional distribution of $\sigma_i$ given all other spins $\sigma_{-i}$:

$$\pi(\sigma_i = +1 \mid \sigma_{-i}) = \frac{e^{\beta \sigma_i \sum_{j \sim i} \sigma_j}}{e^{\beta \sum_{j \sim i} \sigma_j} + e^{-\beta \sum_{j \sim i} \sigma_j}}\Bigg|_{\sigma_i = +1} = \text{sigmoid}!\left(2\beta \sum_{j \sim i} \sigma_j\right)$$

where the sum is over neighbors $j$ of $i$. This is just a Bernoulli conditional — trivially sampleable.

3. Draw $\sigma_i$ from this Bernoulli and update.

Since Gibbs is a special case of MH (acceptance probability 1), this chain has stationary distribution $\pi$ — the Ising distribution. The normalizing constant $Z$ never appears. Running this chain for many steps produces (approximate) samples from the Ising model at inverse temperature $\beta$.

The mixing behavior is physically meaningful: near the **critical temperature** $\beta_c$, the Ising model exhibits a phase transition, and the Glauber dynamics chain mixes exponentially slowly — the mixing time diverges as $\beta \to \beta_c$. This is called **critical slowing down**, and it's a major challenge for MCMC near phase transitions.

## Connection to Simulated Annealing

**Simulated annealing** is Metropolis-Hastings applied to optimization. Instead of sampling from a fixed $\pi$, I want to find the global maximum of $f(x)$.

Idea: sample from $\pi_T(x) \propto e^{f(x)/T}$ (Boltzmann distribution at "temperature" $T$) and slowly decrease $T \to 0$. As $T \to 0$, $\pi_T$ concentrates all its mass on the global maximum of $f$. If I cool slowly enough (a logarithmic cooling schedule), the chain tracks the evolving distribution and converges to the maximum.

The Metropolis acceptance ratio at temperature $T$:

$$A(x,y) = \min!\left(1, e^{(f(y)-f(x))/T}\right)$$

At high $T$: $A \approx 1$ — accept almost everything, explore freely. At low $T$: $A \approx 0$ for downhill moves — the chain increasingly concentrates on high-$f$ regions. The temperature schedule controls the tradeoff between exploration (high $T$) and exploitation (low $T$).

Simulated annealing is Metropolis-Hastings with a time-varying target — technically a non-homogeneous Markov chain. The convergence theory is more subtle (the chain is no longer time-homogeneous), but the algorithm is widely used in combinatorial optimization (TSP, circuit layout, protein folding).

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> Metropolis-Hastings constructs a Markov chain with stationary distribution $\pi$ by using the acceptance ratio $A(x,y) = \min(1, \tilde\pi(y)Q(y,x)/\tilde\pi(x)Q(x,y))$, which satisfies detailed balance by design — and $Z$ cancels in the ratio so the normalizing constant is never needed. Gibbs sampling is the special case where the proposal is the exact conditional, making $A = 1$ always. Convergence is guaranteed under irreducibility and aperiodicity; mixing speed depends on the proposal geometry and the spectral gap.
