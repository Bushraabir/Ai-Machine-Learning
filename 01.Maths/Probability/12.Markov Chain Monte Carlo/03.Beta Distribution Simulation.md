
> _"The Beta distribution is the distribution on $[0,1]$, in the same way the Normal is the distribution on $\mathbb{R}$. It's what I reach for whenever the unknown is a probability — a bias of a coin, a click-through rate, a proportion. The parameters $a$ and $b$ aren't just shape parameters; they're counts. $a-1$ successes and $b-1$ failures, accumulated as prior evidence. Every time I update a Beta prior with new Binomial data, I get another Beta. That's conjugacy, and it's why the Beta is the backbone of Bayesian inference for proportions."_

## The Distribution

$X \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)$ with $a, b > 0$ has PDF:

$$f(x) = \frac{x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1}}{B(a,b)}, \qquad x \in (0,1)$$

where the **Beta function** $B(a,b)$ is the normalizing constant:

$$B(a,b) = \int_0^1 x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1},dx$$

**The fundamental identity** connecting $B$ to the Gamma function:

$$B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)}$$

_Proof sketch:_ Consider $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a,1)$ and $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b,1)$ independent. Let $T = X + Y$ and $W = X/(X+Y)$. By computing the joint density of $(T, W)$ via the change-of-variables Jacobian, one finds $T \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b, 1)$ and $W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, and they are **independent**. The normalization constant falls out as $\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)/\Gamma(a+b)$. This isn't just a computational trick — it's the theorem that makes the Gamma-Beta relationship structural.

> _This proof is in B&H Chapter 8 and it's one of my favourite change-of-variables arguments. The independence of $T$ and $W$ — the total and the fraction — is genuinely surprising. I'd expect them to be correlated. They're not._

## Shape Behaviour

The parameters $a$ and $b$ control the shape completely. Let me internalize the cases:

|$a$, $b$|Shape|Intuition|
|---|---|---|
|$a = b = 1$|Uniform$(0,1)$|No information, all probabilities equally likely|
|$a, b > 1$|Unimodal, interior mode|Evidence for an interior probability|
|$a < 1$, $b < 1$|U-shaped (bimodal at 0 and 1)|Evidence for extremes|
|$a = 1$, $b > 1$|Monotone decreasing|Skewed toward 0|
|$a > 1$, $b = 1$|Monotone increasing|Skewed toward 1|
|$a = b$|Symmetric about $1/2$|Symmetric uncertainty|
|$a, b \to \infty$, $a/b$ fixed|Concentrates near $a/(a+b)$|Many observations, sharp posterior|

**Mode:** For $a, b > 1$, the mode is $(a-1)/(a+b-2)$. Found by differentiating the log-PDF:

$$\frac{d}{dx}\log f(x) = \frac{a-1}{x} - \frac{b-1}{1-x} = 0 \implies x^* = \frac{a-1}{a+b-2}$$

For $a = b = 1$ (Uniform), any point in $(0,1)$ is a mode. For $a < 1$ or $b < 1$, the density blows up at the boundary — the "mode" is at the edge.

## Moments

$$E[X] = \frac{a}{a+b}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{ab}{(a+b)^2(a+b+1)}$$

**Derivation of the mean** — elegant via the Beta function identity:

$$E[X] = \int_0^1 x \cdot \frac{x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1}}{B(a,b)},dx = \frac{B(a+1,b)}{B(a,b)} = \frac{\Gamma(a+1)\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(a+b+1)} = \frac{a}{a+b}$$

using $\Gamma(a+1) = a\Gamma(a)$. Clean.

**Higher moments:** $E[X^r] = B(a+r,b)/B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a+r)\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(a+b+r)}$. So all moments are expressible as ratios of Gamma functions — which for integer $a,b$ collapse to ratios of factorials.

## The Order Statistics Connection

This is one of my favourite facts in all of probability.

**Theorem.** Let $U_{(1)} \leq U_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq U_{(n)}$ be the order statistics of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$ random variables. Then:

$$U_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k, n-k+1)$$

**Proof.** The PDF of $U_{(k)}$ is:

$$f_{U_{(k)}}(x) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!} x^{k-1}(1-x)^{n-k}, \qquad x \in (0,1)$$

This follows from a combinatorial argument: $f_{U_{(k)}}(x),dx \approx P(U_{(k)} \in (x, x+dx))$, which requires exactly $k-1$ of the $n$ uniforms to fall in $[0,x)$ (probability $x^{k-1}$), one to fall in $(x, x+dx)$ (probability $dx$, $n$ choices), and $n-k$ to fall in $(x+dx, 1]$ (probability $(1-x)^{n-k}$), times the multinomial count $n!/((k-1)! \cdot 1! \cdot (n-k)!)$. This is exactly $\text{Beta}(k, n-k+1)$.

**Why this matters for intuition:** $U_{(k)}$ is the $k$-th smallest uniform. It concentrates near $k/(n+1)$ (the expected value: $k/(k + n-k+1) = k/(n+1)$). The Beta$(k, n-k+1)$ is the distribution of "where the $k$-th order statistic lands." With large $n$, it concentrates tightly around $k/(n+1)$.

This gives me a way to simulate Beta$(a,b)$ for positive integer $a,b$: generate $a+b-1$ uniforms, sort them, and return the $a$-th one. Wildly impractical for large $a+b$, but illuminating.

> _The order statistics connection is the reason Beta$(1, 1)$ is Uniform — the "first" (and only) order statistic of 1 Uniform is just that Uniform itself. And Beta$(1, n)$ is the minimum of $n$ Uniforms — it concentrates near 0, decaying as $(1-x)^{n-1}$, exactly as a minimum should._

## Bayesian Conjugacy: Beta-Binomial

This is the most important property of the Beta in applied work.

**Setup.** I observe $X \mid p \sim \text{Binomial}(n, p)$ — $n$ Bernoulli trials with success probability $p$. I place a prior $p \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)$.

**Posterior:**

$$p \mid X = k \sim \text{Beta}(a + k,; b + n - k)$$

**Proof.** By Bayes:

$$\pi(p \mid X=k) \propto P(X=k \mid p) \cdot \pi(p) = \binom{n}{k} p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \cdot \frac{p^{a-1}(1-p)^{b-1}}{B(a,b)}$$

$$\propto p^{a+k-1}(1-p)^{b+n-k-1}$$

which is the Beta$(a+k, b+n-k)$ kernel. The normalizing constant is $B(a+k, b+n-k)$ — I don't even need to compute it; I just recognize the kernel. $\blacksquare$

**The interpretation:** $a$ and $b$ act as **pseudo-counts** — $a-1$ prior successes and $b-1$ prior failures. After observing $k$ successes in $n$ trials, I add them to get $a+k$ and $b + (n-k)$.

- Prior mean: $a/(a+b)$.
- Posterior mean: $(a+k)/(a+b+n)$ — a weighted average of the prior mean and the MLE $k/n$, with weight proportional to evidence.
- As $n \to \infty$: posterior mean $\to k/n$ — the data overwhelms the prior (consistency).

> _This is the template I should have in my head for every conjugate Bayesian update. The parameters have a semantic: they're counts. A Beta(1,1) prior (Uniform) says "I've seen zero successes and zero failures — I have no data." A Beta(10, 10) prior says "I've seen 9 successes and 9 failures — I mildly believe $p \approx 1/2$." The prior is data, expressed as a distribution._

## Simulation

### Method 1: Gamma Ratio (Standard Algorithm)

From the Gamma-Beta relationship: if $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, 1)$ and $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b, 1)$ independently, then:

$$W = \frac{X}{X+Y} \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)$$

**Algorithm:**

1. Draw $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, 1)$.
2. Draw $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b, 1)$.
3. Return $W = X/(X+Y)$.

This is the standard algorithm, and it's what `numpy.random.beta` uses under the hood (via the Gamma sampler, which itself uses Marsaglia-Tsang's method or Ahrens-Dieter depending on shape).

```python
import numpy as np

def beta_via_gamma(a, b, size=1):
    X = np.random.gamma(a, scale=1.0, size=size)
    Y = np.random.gamma(b, scale=1.0, size=size)
    return X / (X + Y)
```

**Why this works:** From the proof of $B(a,b) = \Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)/\Gamma(a+b)$ — the independence and distributional identity are theorems, not heuristics.

**Numerical caution:** When $a$ or $b$ is very small (say $< 0.1$), the Gamma sample can be extremely close to zero, making $X/(X+Y)$ numerically unstable. Use `numpy.random.beta` directly in such cases — it has numerical safeguards.

---

### Method 2: numpy's Built-In

```python
samples = np.random.beta(a=2.0, b=5.0, size=10_000)
```

Internally uses the Gamma ratio method. For $a, b < 1$, it switches to a different algorithm (Johnk's method or a boundary-aware variant) to avoid numerical issues near 0 and 1.

---

### Method 3: Inverse CDF (for Specific Cases)

The Beta CDF is the **regularized incomplete beta function** $I_x(a,b)$:

$$F(x) = I_x(a,b) = \frac{B(x;,a,b)}{B(a,b)}, \qquad B(x;,a,b) = \int_0^x t^{a-1}(1-t)^{b-1},dt$$

There is no closed form in general, but `scipy.special.betaincinv` computes the inverse:

```python
from scipy.special import betaincinv
import numpy as np

def beta_inverse_cdf(a, b, size=1):
    U = np.random.uniform(size=size)
    return betaincinv(a, b, U)
```

This is exact (modulo floating-point), but slower than the Gamma method. I'd use it when I specifically need to control the uniform draws (e.g., for quasi-Monte Carlo with a low-discrepancy sequence).

---

### Method 4: Order Statistics (Pedagogical, Not Practical)

For integer $a, b$ with $a + b$ small:

```python
def beta_order_stat(a, b, size=1):
    # Beta(a, b) = (a)-th order stat of a+b-1 Uniforms
    n = a + b - 1
    U = np.random.uniform(size=(size, n))
    U_sorted = np.sort(U, axis=1)
    return U_sorted[:, a-1]   # 0-indexed: a-th smallest
```

Correct, but $O(n \log n)$ per sample vs $O(1)$ for the Gamma method. Only use this to build intuition.

## Empirical Verification

### 1. Histogram vs PDF

```python
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import beta as beta_dist

a, b = 2.0, 5.0
N = 50_000
samples = np.random.beta(a, b, size=N)

x = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
pdf = beta_dist.pdf(x, a, b)

plt.figure(figsize=(7, 4))
plt.hist(samples, bins=60, density=True, alpha=0.5, label='Simulated')
plt.plot(x, pdf, 'r-', lw=2, label=f'Beta({a},{b}) PDF')
plt.xlabel('x'); plt.ylabel('Density')
plt.legend(); plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
```

### 2. Verifying the Mean and Variance

```python
a, b = 3.0, 7.0
N = 100_000
samples = np.random.beta(a, b, size=N)

print(f"Sample mean:     {samples.mean():.4f}  |  True: {a/(a+b):.4f}")
print(f"Sample variance: {samples.var():.4f}   |  True: {a*b/((a+b)**2*(a+b+1)):.4f}")
```

By LLN, sample mean $\to$ true mean. By a similar law for second moments, sample variance $\to$ true variance. For $N = 10^5$, agreement to 3 decimal places is typical.

### 3. Verifying Conjugacy via Simulation

```python
# Prior: p ~ Beta(2, 2)
# Data:  X | p ~ Binomial(20, p)
# Posterior should be Beta(2 + k, 2 + 20 - k)

a_prior, b_prior = 2.0, 2.0
n_trials = 20

# Simulate the prior predictive: draw p, then draw X | p
p_samples = np.random.beta(a_prior, b_prior, size=50_000)
X_samples  = np.random.binomial(n_trials, p_samples)

# Condition on X = 7 (for example)
k = 7
mask = (X_samples == k)
p_posterior_approx = p_samples[mask]   # approximate posterior via rejection

# Theoretical posterior
a_post = a_prior + k
b_post = b_prior + n_trials - k

x = np.linspace(0, 1, 300)
pdf_post = beta_dist.pdf(x, a_post, b_post)

plt.figure(figsize=(7, 4))
plt.hist(p_posterior_approx, bins=50, density=True, alpha=0.5,
         label='Simulated posterior (ABC)')
plt.plot(x, pdf_post, 'r-', lw=2,
         label=f'Beta({a_post},{b_post}) posterior')
plt.xlabel('p'); plt.title(f'Posterior after observing k={k} in n={n_trials} trials')
plt.legend(); plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
```

This is **approximate Bayesian computation (ABC)** in miniature — simulate from the joint, condition on the observed data. It's inefficient (most samples are discarded) but it verifies conjugacy visually without needing any analytic calculation.

> _This simulation is beautiful because it doesn't assume conjugacy — it just simulates from the joint and filters. The fact that the histogram matches the Beta posterior exactly (up to Monte Carlo noise) is a non-trivial empirical confirmation that the math is right._

## The Beta-Binomial Distribution (Marginal)

If $p \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$ and $X \mid p \sim \text{Binomial}(n,p)$, the **marginal** distribution of $X$ is:

$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} \frac{B(a+k,, b+n-k)}{B(a,b)}, \qquad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n$$

This is the **Beta-Binomial distribution**. It has overdispersion relative to the Binomial — meaning $\text{Var}(X) > np(1-p)$ when $p$ is random. Specifically:

$$E[X] = n \cdot \frac{a}{a+b}, \qquad \text{Var}(X) = n \cdot \frac{ab}{(a+b)^2} \cdot \frac{a+b+n}{a+b+1}$$

The extra factor $\frac{a+b+n}{a+b+1} > 1$ is the **overdispersion**. This comes from averaging over random $p$ — uncertainty about $p$ inflates the variance.

**When is Beta-Binomial useful?** Any time the success probability is not fixed but varies by individual/group — e.g., click-through rates across users, defect rates across batches. Using a plain Binomial would understate the variance and give overconfident uncertainty estimates.

## Special Cases Worth Memorizing

|Parameters|Distribution|
|---|---|
|$\text{Beta}(1,1)$|$\text{Uniform}(0,1)$|
|$\text{Beta}(1/2, 1/2)$|Arcsine distribution (related to occupation time of Brownian motion)|
|$\text{Beta}(k, n-k+1)$|$U_{(k)}$, the $k$-th order stat of $n$ Uniforms|
|$\text{Beta}(a, 1)$|$f(x) = ax^{a-1}$; Power distribution|
|$\text{Beta}(1, b)$|$f(x) = b(1-x)^{b-1}$; reflected Power distribution|

The **arcsine distribution** Beta$(1/2, 1/2)$ has PDF $f(x) = 1/(\pi\sqrt{x(1-x)})$ — it's U-shaped and blows up at both endpoints. It appears in the arc-sine laws for random walks (how long does a random walk spend positive?). Unexpected that a distribution on $[0,1]$ concentrates at the extremes.

## Connection to the Dirichlet Distribution

The Dirichlet is the multivariate Beta. If $(X_1, \ldots, X_k) \sim \text{Dirichlet}(\alpha_1, \ldots, \alpha_k)$, then:

- Each marginal $X_i \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha_i, \sum_{j \neq i} \alpha_j)$.
- The Dirichlet is conjugate to the Multinomial, exactly as Beta is conjugate to Binomial.
- Used in topic models (LDA), Bayesian mixture models, and any model with a random probability vector over $k \geq 3$ categories.

**Simulation:** $(X_1, \ldots, X_k) \sim \text{Dirichlet}(\alpha)$ by drawing $Y_i \sim \text{Gamma}(\alpha_i, 1)$ independently and returning $Y_i / \sum_j Y_j$. Same Gamma-ratio trick, generalized.
