

> _"Normal-Normal conjugacy is the result I reach for more than any other in Bayesian inference. It's clean, it's closed-form, and the posterior has a beautiful geometric interpretation: the posterior mean is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean and the data mean. More information — higher precision — pulls the estimate closer to you. Less information — lower precision — lets the prior dominate. Every time I derive this I'm struck by how the algebra forces exactly the right answer. The math isn't just computing; it's explaining why Bayesian updating works the way it does."_

---

## Setup

I observe $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ where:

- $\sigma^2$ is **known** (the observation variance — I'll relax this later).
- $\mu$ is **unknown** — the parameter I want to infer.

I place a **Normal prior** on $\mu$:

$$\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \tau_0^2)$$

where $\mu_0$ is my prior mean and $\tau_0^2$ is my prior variance (how uncertain I am about $\mu$ before seeing data).

**Claim.** The posterior is also Normal:

$$\mu \mid X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \tau_n^2)$$

with parameters I will now derive.

---

## Derivation

### Step 1: Write the posterior kernel

By Bayes' theorem:

$$\pi(\mu \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto \underbrace{f(\mathbf{x} \mid \mu)}_{\text{likelihood}} \cdot \underbrace{\pi(\mu)}_{\text{prior}}$$

**Likelihood** (data $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ given $\mu$):

$$f(\mathbf{x} \mid \mu) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{(x_i - \mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right) \propto \exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n (x_i - \mu)^2\right)$$

**Prior:**

$$\pi(\mu) \propto \exp!\left(-\frac{(\mu - \mu_0)^2}{2\tau_0^2}\right)$$

**Posterior kernel:**

$$\pi(\mu \mid \mathbf{x}) \propto \exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i-\mu)^2 - \frac{(\mu-\mu_0)^2}{2\tau_0^2}\right)$$

### Step 2: Complete the square in $\mu$

Expand the exponent. Collect all terms involving $\mu$:

$$-\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \mu)^2 - \frac{(\mu - \mu_0)^2}{2\tau_0^2}$$

$$= -\frac{1}{2\sigma^2}\left(n\mu^2 - 2\mu\sum_i x_i + \sum_i x_i^2\right) - \frac{1}{2\tau_0^2}\left(\mu^2 - 2\mu\mu_0 + \mu_0^2\right)$$

Drop constants in $\mu$ (they go into the normalizing constant):

$$\propto -\frac{\mu^2}{2}\left(\frac{n}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}\right) + \mu\left(\frac{\sum_i x_i}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\mu_0}{\tau_0^2}\right)$$

This is a quadratic in $\mu$ of the form $-\frac{A}{2}\mu^2 + B\mu$, which completes to:

$$-\frac{A}{2}\left(\mu - \frac{B}{A}\right)^2 + \text{const}$$

So the posterior is $\mathcal{N}(B/A,; 1/A)$ with:

$$A = \frac{n}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}, \qquad B = \frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\mu_0}{\tau_0^2}$$

where $\bar{x} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i x_i$ is the sample mean.

### Step 3: Read off the posterior parameters

**Posterior precision** (precision = reciprocal variance):

$$\boxed{\frac{1}{\tau_n^2} = \frac{n}{\sigma^2} + \frac{1}{\tau_0^2}}$$

**Posterior mean:**

$$\boxed{\mu_n = \tau_n^2!\left(\frac{n\bar{x}}{\sigma^2} + \frac{\mu_0}{\tau_0^2}\right)}$$

**Posterior:**

$$\mu \mid \mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n,; \tau_n^2) \qquad \blacksquare$$

## The Precision Perspective

The cleanest way to remember this is in terms of **precision** $\lambda = 1/\sigma^2$ (inverse variance).

Define:

- $\lambda_0 = 1/\tau_0^2$ — prior precision.
- $\lambda_{\text{data}} = n/\sigma^2$ — data precision (total information from $n$ observations).
- $\lambda_n = \lambda_0 + \lambda_{\text{data}}$ — posterior precision.

Then:

$$\frac{1}{\tau_n^2} = \lambda_n = \lambda_0 + \frac{n}{\sigma^2}$$

$$\mu_n = \frac{\lambda_0 \mu_0 + \lambda_{\text{data}} \bar{x}}{\lambda_n} = \frac{\lambda_0}{\lambda_n}\mu_0 + \frac{\lambda_{\text{data}}}{\lambda_n}\bar{x}$$

The posterior mean is a **precision-weighted average** of the prior mean and the data mean. The weights sum to 1 and are proportional to how much information each source contributes.

**Sequentially:** If I observe one data point at a time, I can update iteratively:

$$\lambda_n = \lambda_{n-1} + \frac{1}{\sigma^2}, \qquad \mu_n = \frac{\lambda_{n-1}\mu_{n-1} + x_n/\sigma^2}{\lambda_n}$$

Each new observation adds precision $1/\sigma^2$ and pulls the mean toward $x_n$ with weight proportional to $1/\sigma^2$. This is the **Kalman filter** in one dimension.

> _The precision perspective is the one I should tattoo on my brain. Precisions add. Variances don't. When I have two independent sources of information about the same quantity, the total precision is the sum of individual precisions. This is why Normal-Normal conjugacy is so tractable — the parameter space for precision is additive, and addition is the simplest operation. The moment I think in precisions instead of variances, every formula simplifies._

## Limiting Cases

### As $n \to \infty$ (infinite data):

$$\tau_n^2 = \frac{\tau_0^2 \sigma^2}{n\tau_0^2 + \sigma^2} \to 0, \qquad \mu_n \to \bar{x}$$

The posterior collapses to a point mass at the MLE $\bar{x}$. The prior is overwhelmed by data — **consistency**.

### As $\tau_0^2 \to \infty$ (flat/uninformative prior):

$$\mu_n \to \bar{x}, \qquad \tau_n^2 \to \sigma^2/n$$

The posterior becomes $\mathcal{N}(\bar{x}, \sigma^2/n)$ — the sampling distribution of the MLE. Bayesian inference with an uninformative prior recovers the frequentist answer.

### As $\tau_0^2 \to 0$ (infinitely informative prior):

$$\mu_n \to \mu_0, \qquad \tau_n^2 \to 0$$

The posterior collapses to the prior — the data can't move an infinitely confident prior. This is a pathology worth remembering: prior precision $\to \infty$ means no amount of data updates the belief.

### Single observation ($n = 1$):

$$\mu_1 = \frac{\mu_0/\tau_0^2 + x_1/\sigma^2}{1/\tau_0^2 + 1/\sigma^2}$$

A precision-weighted average of $\mu_0$ and $x_1$. If $\tau_0^2 = \sigma^2$ (prior as uncertain as one observation), $\mu_1 = (\mu_0 + x_1)/2$ — equal weight. Perfect symmetry.

## The Posterior Predictive Distribution

Once I've updated my belief about $\mu$, I often want to predict a **new** observation $X_{n+1}$.

**Theorem.** The posterior predictive distribution is:

$$X_{n+1} \mid X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n,; \sigma^2 + \tau_n^2)$$

**Proof.** By the law of total expectation and total variance:

$$X_{n+1} \mid \mathbf{x} = (X_{n+1} \mid \mu, \mathbf{x}) \text{ marginalized over } (\mu \mid \mathbf{x})$$

Since $X_{n+1} \mid \mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ and $\mu \mid \mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \tau_n^2)$:

$$E[X_{n+1} \mid \mathbf{x}] = E[E[X_{n+1} \mid \mu, \mathbf{x}]] = E[\mu \mid \mathbf{x}] = \mu_n$$

$$\text{Var}(X_{n+1} \mid \mathbf{x}) = E[\text{Var}(X_{n+1} \mid \mu)] + \text{Var}(E[X_{n+1} \mid \mu]) = \sigma^2 + \tau_n^2$$

Since the marginal of a joint Normal is Normal (and the sum of two independent Normals is Normal):

$$X_{n+1} \mid \mathbf{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_n,; \sigma^2 + \tau_n^2) \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Interpretation:** The predictive variance $\sigma^2 + \tau_n^2$ has two parts:

- $\sigma^2$: irreducible observation noise — even if I knew $\mu$ exactly, new observations still vary.
- $\tau_n^2$: parametric uncertainty — I don't know $\mu$ exactly, which adds spread.

As $n \to \infty$: $\tau_n^2 \to 0$ and the predictive collapses to $\mathcal{N}(\bar{x}, \sigma^2)$ — parametric uncertainty vanishes, only observation noise remains.

## Unknown Variance: The Normal-Inverse-Gamma Model

When $\sigma^2$ is also unknown, the conjugate prior for $(\mu, \sigma^2)$ jointly is the **Normal-Inverse-Gamma**:

$$\sigma^2 \sim \text{Inv-Gamma}(\alpha_0, \beta_0), \qquad \mu \mid \sigma^2 \sim \mathcal{N}!\left(\mu_0, \frac{\sigma^2}{\kappa_0}\right)$$

The joint prior is $\text{NIG}(\mu_0, \kappa_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0)$. After observing $n$ data points:

$$\mu_n = \frac{\kappa_0 \mu_0 + n\bar{x}}{\kappa_0 + n}$$

$$\kappa_n = \kappa_0 + n$$

$$\alpha_n = \alpha_0 + \frac{n}{2}$$

$$\beta_n = \beta_0 + \frac{1}{2}\sum_{i=1}^n(x_i - \bar{x})^2 + \frac{\kappa_0 n(\bar{x} - \mu_0)^2}{2(\kappa_0 + n)}$$

The marginal posterior of $\mu$ (integrating out $\sigma^2$) is a **Student-$t$ distribution** — heavier tails than Normal because $\sigma^2$ is uncertain.

I won't derive the full NIG update here — it's a longer computation and belongs in [[Normal-Inverse-Gamma Model]]. But the key point: the four parameters $(\mu_0, \kappa_0, \alpha_0, \beta_0)$ each have clean interpretations as pseudo-data:

- $\mu_0$: prior mean of $\mu$.
- $\kappa_0$: prior "sample size" — how many observations worth of confidence in $\mu_0$.
- $\alpha_0, \beta_0$: prior shape and scale for $\sigma^2$ — encodes prior belief about the noise level.

> _The NIG model is the honest version of Normal-Normal conjugacy. In practice, I almost never know $\sigma^2$. The NIG gives me a tractable joint posterior at the cost of four hyperparameters instead of two. The Student-$t$ marginal for $\mu$ is the right answer when $\sigma^2$ is uncertain — wider tails, more honest about what I don't know._

## Connection to the Kalman Filter

The Kalman filter is Normal-Normal conjugacy applied sequentially to a dynamic state.

**Model:**

$$X_t = \mu_t + \epsilon_t, \quad \epsilon_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) \quad \text{(observation)}$$ $$\mu_t = \mu_{t-1} + \eta_t, \quad \eta_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, Q) \quad \text{(state evolution)}$$

At each time step:

**Predict:** The prior on $\mu_t$ given observations up to $t-1$:

$$\mu_t \mid x_{1:t-1} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{t-1|t-1},; \tau_{t-1|t-1}^2 + Q)$$

The prediction step increases uncertainty by $Q$ — the state has evolved randomly.

**Update:** After observing $x_t$, apply Normal-Normal conjugacy:

$$\frac{1}{\tau_{t|t}^2} = \frac{1}{\tau_{t|t-1}^2} + \frac{1}{\sigma^2}$$

$$\mu_{t|t} = \frac{\mu_{t|t-1}/\tau_{t|t-1}^2 + x_t/\sigma^2}{1/\tau_{t|t-1}^2 + 1/\sigma^2}$$

This is exactly the $n=1$ Normal-Normal update, applied at each time step. The Kalman filter is nothing more than iterated Bayesian updating with Normal distributions — elegant in its simplicity, powerful in its generality.

The **Kalman gain** $K_t = \tau_{t|t-1}^2 / (\tau_{t|t-1}^2 + \sigma^2) \in (0,1)$ controls how much the new observation moves the estimate:

$$\mu_{t|t} = \mu_{t|t-1} + K_t(x_t - \mu_{t|t-1})$$

High $K_t$ (uncertain prior, precise observations): move a lot toward $x_t$. Low $K_t$ (precise prior, noisy observations): barely move.

## Simulation and Verification

```python
import numpy as np
import matplotlib.pyplot as plt
from scipy.stats import norm

# --- Parameters ---
mu_true = 3.0       # true mean (unknown to the model)
sigma2  = 1.0       # known observation variance
mu0     = 0.0       # prior mean
tau0_sq = 2.0       # prior variance

n_obs   = 20
np.random.seed(42)
data = np.random.normal(mu_true, np.sqrt(sigma2), size=n_obs)

# --- Sequential Bayesian update ---
mu_post  = mu0
tau2_post = tau0_sq

mu_history  = [mu_post]
tau2_history = [tau2_post]

for x in data:
    # Precision update
    prec_post = 1/tau2_post + 1/sigma2
    tau2_post = 1/prec_post
    # Mean update
    mu_post   = tau2_post * (mu_history[-1]/tau2_history[-1] + x/sigma2)
    mu_history.append(mu_post)
    tau2_history.append(tau2_post)

# --- Batch formula (should match final sequential) ---
xbar     = data.mean()
tau2_n   = 1 / (1/tau0_sq + n_obs/sigma2)
mu_n     = tau2_n * (mu0/tau0_sq + n_obs*xbar/sigma2)

print(f"Sequential posterior: mean={mu_history[-1]:.6f}, var={tau2_history[-1]:.6f}")
print(f"Batch posterior:      mean={mu_n:.6f},            var={tau2_n:.6f}")
# Should be identical up to floating point

# --- Plot posterior evolution ---
mu_vals = np.linspace(-1, 6, 500)

plt.figure(figsize=(8, 4))
for i, step in enumerate([0, 1, 5, 10, 20]):
    lw = 2.5 if step == 20 else 1
    label = f'n={step}' + (' (posterior)' if step==20 else '')
    plt.plot(mu_vals,
             norm.pdf(mu_vals, mu_history[step], np.sqrt(tau2_history[step])),
             lw=lw, label=label)

plt.axvline(mu_true, color='k', ls='--', lw=1, label=f'True μ={mu_true}')
plt.xlabel('μ'); plt.ylabel('Density')
plt.title('Normal-Normal Posterior — Sequential Updates')
plt.legend(); plt.tight_layout(); plt.show()
```

**What to look for:**

- The posterior at $n=0$ is the prior — wide, centred at $\mu_0 = 0$.
- As $n$ increases, the posterior narrows and shifts toward $\mu_{\text{true}} = 3$.
- Sequential and batch formulas give identical results (up to floating point).
- The posterior mean is always between $\mu_0$ and $\bar{x}$ — the precision-weighted average.

## Why the Normal is Self-Conjugate: The MGF Argument

There's a deeper reason Normal-Normal conjugacy works: the Normal family is closed under multiplication of densities (up to normalization), because the product of two Gaussian kernels is another Gaussian kernel.

**Lemma.** For $a, b > 0$ and $\mu_1, \mu_2 \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\exp!\left(-\frac{a(\mu - \mu_1)^2}{2}\right) \cdot \exp!\left(-\frac{b(\mu - \mu_2)^2}{2}\right) = C \cdot \exp!\left(-\frac{(a+b)(\mu - \mu^*)^2}{2}\right)$$

where $\mu^* = (a\mu_1 + b\mu_2)/(a+b)$ and $C$ is a constant in $\mu$.

**Proof.** Expand both squares, collect $\mu^2$ and $\mu$ terms:

$$-\frac{a+b}{2}\mu^2 + (a\mu_1 + b\mu_2)\mu + \text{const} = -\frac{a+b}{2}\left(\mu - \frac{a\mu_1 + b\mu_2}{a+b}\right)^2 + \text{const}$$

This is completing the square. The result is a Gaussian in $\mu$ with precision $a+b$ and mean $\mu^*$. $\blacksquare$

In the Normal-Normal update: the likelihood contributes precision $n/\sigma^2$ and mean $\bar{x}$; the prior contributes precision $1/\tau_0^2$ and mean $\mu_0$. The lemma gives the posterior directly — no integration required. The algebra is the proof.

## Connections to Other Topics

**[[Beta Distribution & Simulation]]:** Beta-Binomial is the discrete analogue. Beta is conjugate to Binomial; Normal is conjugate to Normal (known variance). Both update by adding pseudo-counts / pseudo-precision. Both have the posterior mean as a weighted average of prior and data.

**[[Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm]]:** When $\sigma^2$ is unknown and the prior is non-conjugate, I can't get a closed-form posterior. MCMC (specifically Gibbs sampling on the NIG model, or MH for non-conjugate priors) is the fallback. Conjugacy is what makes closed-form inference possible — when it fails, I reach for [[Metropolis-Hastings Algorithm]].

**[[Markov Chain Convergence Theorem]]:** The Kalman filter is the Bayesian optimal estimator for a linear-Gaussian state space model. Its convergence (the steady-state Kalman gain) is related to the fixed point of the Riccati equation — an algebraic analogue of the chain's stationary distribution.

**[[Central Limit Theorem]]:** For large $n$, the posterior $\mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \tau_n^2)$ approximates the sampling distribution of the MLE $\bar{x} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2/n)$. This is the Bernstein-von Mises theorem in this simple case: under mild conditions, the posterior converges to a Normal centred at the MLE as $n \to \infty$, regardless of the prior.

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> Normal-Normal conjugacy: prior $\mathcal{N}(\mu_0, \tau_0^2)$ plus $n$ observations with known variance $\sigma^2$ gives posterior $\mathcal{N}(\mu_n, \tau_n^2)$ where **precisions add** ($1/\tau_n^2 = 1/\tau_0^2 + n/\sigma^2$) and **the posterior mean is the precision-weighted average** of prior mean and sample mean — iterated one observation at a time, this is the Kalman filter.
