
## 1. The Core Problem It Solves

You want to sample from a **joint distribution** $p(x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_n)$ that is:

- Too complex to sample from directly (no closed form, high-dimensional), or
- Only known up to a normalizing constant: $p(\mathbf{x}) \propto \tilde{p}(\mathbf{x})$

**Gibbs Sampling** sidesteps this by exploiting the fact that even when the joint is intractable, the **full conditionals** $p(x_i \mid x_{\setminus i})$ are often tractable.

$$\text{Joint: intractable} \quad \Longrightarrow \quad \text{Conditionals: tractable}$$

---

## 2. The Algorithm

### Setup

- Variables: $\mathbf{x} = (x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_d)$
- Full conditional of variable $i$: $p(x_i \mid x_1, \ldots, x_{i-1}, x_{i+1}, \ldots, x_d)$, written $p(x_i \mid \mathbf{x}_{\setminus i})$

### Pseudocode

```
Initialize: x^(0) = (x_1^(0), ..., x_d^(0))   ← random or prior draw

For t = 1, 2, ..., T:
    Sample x_1^(t)  ~ p(x_1  | x_2^(t-1), x_3^(t-1), ..., x_d^(t-1))
    Sample x_2^(t)  ~ p(x_2  | x_1^(t),   x_3^(t-1), ..., x_d^(t-1))
    Sample x_3^(t)  ~ p(x_3  | x_1^(t),   x_2^(t),   ..., x_d^(t-1))
    ...
    Sample x_d^(t)  ~ p(x_d  | x_1^(t),   x_2^(t),   ..., x_{d-1}^(t))

Return: {x^(t)} after burn-in B steps
```

> **Key**: Each variable is updated **one at a time**, using the **most recent values** of all others (systematic scan).

---

## 3. Why It Works — The MCMC Lens

Gibbs sampling is a **special case of Metropolis-Hastings** with acceptance rate always = 1.

Each update step defines a Markov chain transition:

$$T(x_i^{new} \mid \mathbf{x}) = p(x_i^{new} \mid \mathbf{x}_{\setminus i})$$

This satisfies **detailed balance** with respect to the target joint $p(\mathbf{x})$:

$$p(\mathbf{x}) \cdot T(\mathbf{x}' \mid \mathbf{x}) = p(\mathbf{x}') \cdot T(\mathbf{x} \mid \mathbf{x}')$$

**Proof sketch (2D case):**

$$p(x_1, x_2) \cdot p(x_1' \mid x_2) = p(x_1, x_2) \cdot \frac{p(x_1', x_2)}{p(x_2)}$$ $$= p(x_1', x_2) \cdot \frac{p(x_1, x_2)}{p(x_2)} = p(x_1', x_2) \cdot p(x_1 \mid x_2) \checkmark$$

Therefore: the **stationary distribution** of the chain **is** the target joint $p(\mathbf{x})$.

## 4. Concrete Example — Bivariate Gaussian

**Target:**

$$p(x_1, x_2) = \mathcal{N}\left(\begin{bmatrix}0\0\end{bmatrix}, \begin{bmatrix}1 & \rho \ \rho & 1\end{bmatrix}\right)$$

**Full conditionals** (analytically tractable):

$$x_1 \mid x_2 \sim \mathcal{N}(\rho x_2,\ 1 - \rho^2)$$ $$x_2 \mid x_1 \sim \mathcal{N}(\rho x_1,\ 1 - \rho^2)$$

**Python Implementation:**

```python
import numpy as np

def gibbs_bivariate_gaussian(rho=0.8, n_samples=5000, burn_in=500):
    samples = []
    x1, x2 = 0.0, 0.0  # initialization
    
    for t in range(n_samples + burn_in):
        # Sample x1 | x2
        x1 = np.random.normal(loc=rho * x2, scale=np.sqrt(1 - rho**2))
        # Sample x2 | x1
        x2 = np.random.normal(loc=rho * x1, scale=np.sqrt(1 - rho**2))
        
        if t >= burn_in:
            samples.append((x1, x2))
    
    return np.array(samples)

samples = gibbs_bivariate_gaussian(rho=0.9)
# samples[:, 0] ≈ N(0,1), samples[:, 1] ≈ N(0,1), corr ≈ 0.9
```

## 5. Bayesian Inference Use Case — LDA Flavor

In **Latent Dirichlet Allocation** and Bayesian models, you often need:

$$p(\theta, z \mid x) \propto p(x \mid z) \cdot p(z \mid \theta) \cdot p(\theta)$$

Gibbs cycles between:

1. $p(\theta \mid z, x)$ — often Dirichlet (conjugate)
2. $p(z \mid \theta, x)$ — often Categorical

This is **collapsed Gibbs sampling** when $\theta$ is analytically integrated out, sampling only $z$.

## 6. Convergence Diagnostics

|Diagnostic|What it checks|Tool|
|---|---|---|
|**Trace plot**|Chain mixing visually|`matplotlib`|
|**Autocorrelation**|Lag-k correlation of samples|`statsmodels.graphics.tsaplots`|
|**Effective Sample Size (ESS)**|How many iid samples the chain is worth|`arviz.ess()`|
|**R-hat (Gelman-Rubin)**|Convergence across multiple chains|`arviz.rhat()`|
|**Burn-in removal**|Discard early non-stationary samples|Manual / `arviz`|

**Rule of thumb:** Run $\geq 2$ chains. If $\hat{R} < 1.01$ and ESS $> 400$, trust your estimates.

## 7. Critical Failure Modes

### 7.1 Slow Mixing (High Correlation)

If $\rho \approx 1$ in a Gaussian, the chain takes tiny correlated steps — ESS collapses.

**Fix:** Use **reparameterization**, or switch to Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC).

### 7.2 Multimodal Distributions

If $p(\mathbf{x})$ has multiple modes separated by low-probability regions, the chain gets **trapped in one mode**.

```
Mode A ████████       ░░░░░░░░   Mode B ████████
          ↑ chain stuck here
```

**Fix:** Simulated Tempering, Parallel Tempering, or Normalizing Flows.

### 7.3 Deterministic Scan vs. Random Scan

- **Systematic (deterministic) scan**: Update $x_1, x_2, \ldots, x_d$ in fixed order. Simpler, commonly used.
- **Random scan**: Pick $x_i$ uniformly at random. Easier to prove detailed balance; preferred theoretically.

## 8. Gibbs vs. Metropolis-Hastings

|Property|Gibbs|Metropolis-Hastings|
|---|---|---|
|Acceptance step|Always accept (rate = 1)|Explicit accept/reject|
|Proposal distribution|Full conditional (exact)|Arbitrary (Gaussian, etc.)|
|Requires full conditional|Yes (must be tractable)|No|
|Efficiency|High when conditionals are easy|Flexible but tuning-heavy|
|Generalizes to|HMC, Collapsed Gibbs|NUTS, MALA|

**Insight:** Gibbs = MH with a proposal of $q(x_i' \mid \mathbf{x}) = p(x_i' \mid \mathbf{x}_{\setminus i})$. The acceptance ratio is always 1.

## 11. Key Formulas Cheatsheet

$$\boxed{p(x_i^{(t)} \mid \mathbf{x}_{\setminus i}^{(t)}) = \frac{p(\mathbf{x})}{\int p(\mathbf{x}), dx_i}}$$

$$\text{ESS} = \frac{T}{1 + 2\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \rho_k} \quad \text{(Geyer's formula)}$$

$$\hat{R} = \sqrt{\frac{\hat{V}}{W}} \xrightarrow{T \to \infty} 1 \quad \text{(Gelman-Rubin)}$$

---

## 12. Mental Model / Intuition

> Think of Gibbs sampling as **coordinate descent for distributions** — instead of minimizing a loss function one variable at a time, you're sampling from the conditional distribution one variable at a time. The stationary point of descent becomes the stationary distribution of the Markov chain.
