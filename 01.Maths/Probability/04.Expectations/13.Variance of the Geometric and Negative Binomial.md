

> _The Geometric is the discrete analogue of the Exponential â€” memoryless, elegant, and slightly annoying to differentiate. The Negative Binomial is just the Geometric's grown-up version. Derive one carefully and you get the other for free._

---

## Setup and Notation

Let $p \in (0,1)$, $q = 1 - p$.

**Geometric($p$):** Number of trials until the first success (inclusive). $$X \sim \text{Geom}(p), \quad P(X = k) = q^{k-1}p, \quad k = 1, 2, 3, \ldots$$

**Negative Binomial($r, p$):** Number of trials until the $r$-th success. $$X \sim \text{NBin}(r, p), \quad P(X = n) = \binom{n-1}{r-1} p^r q^{n-r}, \quad n = r, r+1, \ldots$$

The NBin is a sum of $r$ i.i.d. Geom($p$) random variables. This is the key structural fact â€” it drives everything.

## Variance of the Geometric

### Step 1 â€” Compute $E[X]$

We need $E[X] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k , q^{k-1} p$.

Factor out $p$: $$E[X] = p \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k , q^{k-1}$$

Recognize this as the derivative of a geometric series. Recall: $$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^k = \frac{1}{1-q} = \frac{1}{p}$$

Differentiate both sides with respect to $q$: $$\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k , q^{k-1} = \frac{1}{(1-q)^2} = \frac{1}{p^2}$$

Therefore: $$\boxed{E[X] = p \cdot \frac{1}{p^2} = \frac{1}{p}}$$

### Step 2 â€” Compute $E[X^2]$

Use the identity $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$, so I need $E[X^2]$.

Write $X^2 = X(X-1) + X$, so: $$E[X^2] = E[X(X-1)] + E[X]$$

Now compute $E[X(X-1)]$: $$E[X(X-1)] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k(k-1), q^{k-1} p = p \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1), q^{k-1}$$

Differentiate $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^k = \frac{1}{p}$ twice with respect to $q$:

First derivative: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k, q^{k-1} = \frac{1}{p^2}$

Second derivative: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1), q^{k-2} = \frac{2}{p^3}$

Multiply by $q$: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1), q^{k-1} = \frac{2q}{p^3}$

So: $$E[X(X-1)] = p \cdot \frac{2q}{p^3} = \frac{2q}{p^2}$$

Therefore: $$E[X^2] = \frac{2q}{p^2} + \frac{1}{p} = \frac{2q}{p^2} + \frac{p}{p^2} = \frac{2q + p}{p^2}$$

Since $p + q = 1$: $$E[X^2] = \frac{q + 1}{p^2}$$

### Step 3 â€” Variance

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \frac{q+1}{p^2} - \frac{1}{p^2} = \frac{q}{p^2}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(\text{Geom}(p)) = \frac{q}{p^2} = \frac{1-p}{p^2}}$$

## Alternative: Variance via the Memoryless Property

There's a slicker derivation using first-step analysis that Blitzstein loves.

Let $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$. Condition on the first trial:

$$E[X] = p \cdot 1 + q \cdot E[1 + X] = p + q(1 + E[X])$$ $$E[X] = p + q + qE[X] \implies pE[X] = 1 \implies E[X] = \frac{1}{p} \checkmark$$

For the second moment, condition similarly: $$E[X^2] = p \cdot 1 + q \cdot E[(1+X)^2]$$ $$= p + q \cdot E[1 + 2X + X^2]$$ $$= p + q(1 + 2E[X] + E[X^2])$$ $$= p + q + \frac{2q}{p} + qE[X^2]$$

Solve for $E[X^2]$: $$E[X^2](1 - q) = 1 + \frac{2q}{p} = \frac{p + 2q}{p}$$ $$E[X^2] \cdot p = \frac{p + 2q}{p}$$ $$E[X^2] = \frac{p + 2q}{p^2} = \frac{1 + q}{p^2}$$

Same answer. The memoryless approach is cleaner â€” saves you from differentiating twice â€” and it's more conceptually honest about _why_ the Geometric is what it is.

## Variance of the Negative Binomial

### The Key Structural Observation

If $X \sim \text{NBin}(r, p)$, then we can write: $$X = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_r$$

where $X_1, \ldots, X_r \stackrel{\text{i.i.d.}}{\sim} \text{Geom}(p)$.

This is not just a trick â€” it's what the NBin _is_. Each $X_i$ counts the number of trials between the $(i-1)$-th and $i$-th success.

### Expectation (immediate)

$$E[X] = rE[X_1] = \frac{r}{p}$$

### Variance (immediate from independence)

Since the $X_i$ are **independent**:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \text{Var}(X_1 + \cdots + X_r) = \sum_{i=1}^r \text{Var}(X_i) = r \cdot \frac{q}{p^2}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(\text{NBin}(r,p)) = \frac{rq}{p^2} = \frac{r(1-p)}{p^2}}$$

That's it. Once you know the Geometric variance and the independence of the inter-success waiting times, the NBin variance is one line.

## Summary Table

|Distribution|PMF|$E[X]$|$\text{Var}(X)$|
|---|---|---|---|
|$\text{Geom}(p)$|$q^{k-1}p$|$\dfrac{1}{p}$|$\dfrac{q}{p^2}$|
|$\text{NBin}(r,p)$|$\dbinom{n-1}{r-1}p^r q^{n-r}$|$\dfrac{r}{p}$|$\dfrac{rq}{p^2}$|

Note the perfect scaling: NBin is just $r$ copies of Geom, and the variance scales linearly. This is **only** because of independence â€” if the trials weren't independent this would fail immediately.

---

## Why $\text{Var}(X) > (E[X])^2 \cdot q$?

A sanity check worth internalizing. For the Geometric:

$$\frac{\text{Var}(X)}{(E[X])^2} = \frac{q/p^2}{1/p^2} = q$$

So the coefficient of variation squared is $q = 1-p$. As $p \to 0$ (very rare successes, very long waits), $q \to 1$ and the relative variability approaches 1 â€” the distribution becomes highly spread relative to its mean. As $p \to 1$, $q \to 0$ and the distribution concentrates near 1. Makes intuitive sense.

## The Differentiation Trick â€” Worth Memorizing

The core technical tool used above is worth isolating:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} x^k = \frac{1}{1-x}, \quad |x| < 1$$

Differentiate $n$ times with respect to $x$:

$$\sum_{k=n}^{\infty} k(k-1)\cdots(k-n+1), x^{k-n} = \frac{n!}{(1-x)^{n+1}}$$

For $n=1$: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k x^{k-1} = \frac{1}{(1-x)^2}$

For $n=2$: $\displaystyle\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1) x^{k-2} = \frac{2}{(1-x)^3}$

This generates all moments of any distribution with a geometric-series MGF. Keep it in your back pocket â€” it shows up constantly.
