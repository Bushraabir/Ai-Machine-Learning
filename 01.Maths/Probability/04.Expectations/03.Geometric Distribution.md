> [!note] Big Picture You are flipping a biased coin, over and over. Each flip is independent. $p \in (0,1)$ is the probability of **success** on any single flip. You stop the moment you get your first success.
> 
> **How long do you wait?**
> 
> That waiting time is exactly the Geometric$(p)$ random variable. Simple to state — but the moment you pull on the thread, the memoryless property, the connection to exponential distributions, and the entire structure of waiting-time distributions starts showing up.

---

## 1. The Two Conventions — Choose One and Commit

> [!warning] Two Conventions Exist **Type 1 (Blitzstein / Stat 110):** $X$ = number of _failures_ before the first success. Support: ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$
> 
> **Type 2 (many textbooks):** $X$ = number of _trials_ until (and including) the first success. Support: ${1, 2, 3, \ldots}$
> 
> The two differ by exactly 1: if $X$ is Type 1 and $Y$ is Type 2, then $Y = X + 1$.
> 
> **This note uses Type 1 throughout** — it makes the algebra cleaner, and it's the convention in Stat 110, which is the primary source.

$$X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$$

$X$ counts the number of **failures before the first success**, in a sequence of independent Bernoulli$(p)$ trials.

---

## 2. PMF — Derivation from First Principles

### The logic

Suppose $X = k$. This means:

- Trials $1, 2, \ldots, k$ are all **failures** — each with probability $1-p$
- Trial $k+1$ is a **success** — with probability $p$
- By independence, multiply

$$P(X = k) = \underbrace{(1-p)(1-p)\cdots(1-p)}_{k \text{ failures}} \cdot \underbrace{p}_{\text{success}}$$

$$\boxed{P(X = k) = (1-p)^k \cdot p, \qquad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots}$$

Let $q := 1-p$ for cleaner notation. Then $P(X = k) = q^k p$.

### Verification — sum to 1

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^k p = p \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^k = p \cdot \frac{1}{1-q} = p \cdot \frac{1}{p} = 1 \checkmark$$

using the geometric series $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} r^k = \frac{1}{1-r}$ for $|r| < 1$.

> [!tip] Why "Geometric"? The PMF values $p,\ qp,\ q^2p,\ q^3p, \ldots$ form a **geometric sequence** — each term is a constant multiple $q$ of the previous. The distribution is named after the series that validates it. It's the same word for the same reason.

### Shape of the PMF

The PMF is **strictly decreasing** in $k$ for all $p \in (0,1)$:

$$\frac{P(X=k+1)}{P(X=k)} = \frac{q^{k+1}p}{q^k p} = q < 1$$

The mode is always at $k=0$. The most likely outcome is zero failures — you succeed on the very first trial. As $p \to 0$ (rare success), the distribution flattens and spreads out; as $p \to 1$ (near-certain success), the distribution collapses onto $k=0$.

## 3. CDF — Complement Method

### Direct sum (painful)

$$P(X \leq k) = \sum_{j=0}^{k} q^j p = p \cdot \frac{1-q^{k+1}}{1-q} = 1 - q^{k+1}$$

### Complement method (elegant — burn this reflex in)

Instead of summing successes up to $k$, compute the complement:

$$P(X > k) = P(\text{first } k+1 \text{ trials are all failures}) = q^{k+1}$$

The first $k+1$ trials are all failures with probability $q^{k+1}$, by independence.

Therefore:

$$\boxed{P(X \leq k) = 1 - q^{k+1} = 1 - (1-p)^{k+1}, \qquad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots}$$

> [!tip] Complement Reflex For geometric and exponential-type calculations, **always try the complement first**. $P(X > k)$ has a direct physical interpretation (all trials fail) that $P(X \leq k)$ does not. The complement route avoids summing a geometric series by reading the answer directly from the physics.

### CDF staircase

The CDF jumps at each integer $k \geq 0$, with jump size $P(X=k) = q^k p$:

```
F(k)
  1  |                              ●────
     |                    ●────────○
     |          ●────────○
 1-q |●────────○
     |
  0  ●
     ─────────────────────────────────── k
     0         1         2         3
```

Each step is smaller than the last (by factor $q$), consistent with the decreasing PMF.

## 4. Expectation — Three Derivations

### Derivation 1: Geometric series differentiation (the calculus route)

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k \cdot q^k p = p \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k q^k$$

We need $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k q^k$. Start from the geometric series:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^k = \frac{1}{1-q}$$

Differentiate both sides with respect to $q$:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k q^{k-1} = \frac{1}{(1-q)^2}$$

Multiply both sides by $q$:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k q^k = \frac{q}{(1-q)^2} = \frac{q}{p^2}$$

Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = p \cdot \frac{q}{p^2} = \frac{q}{p}$$

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{1-p}{p}}$$

### Derivation 2: Story proof — memorylessness (the Stat 110 route)

No calculus. One equation.

On the first trial, two things can happen:

- **Success** (probability $p$): $X = 0$ failures. We're done immediately.
- **Failure** (probability $q$): we've accumulated 1 failure. By the memoryless property (proven in §6), the remaining wait is distributed as a fresh $\text{Geom}(p)$.

This gives the recursion:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = p \cdot 0 + q \cdot (1 + \mathbb{E}[X])$$

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = q + q,\mathbb{E}[X]$$

$$\mathbb{E}X = q$$

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{q}{p} \checkmark$$

> [!tip] Why This Proof is Profound It uses no calculus, no summation formulas — only the **physical structure** of the process and memorylessness. This is a _story proof_: the equation mirrors what actually happens on the first trial. The algebra is just bookkeeping. This style of reasoning recurs constantly in Stat 110 and should be your first instinct.

### Derivation 3: Tail sum formula

For any non-negative integer-valued RV: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} P(X > k)$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} P(X > k) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^{k+1} = q \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^k = q \cdot \frac{1}{1-q} = \frac{q}{p} \checkmark$$

Three derivations, one answer. Each illuminates a different tool.

### Intuition

| $p$    | $\mathbb{E}[X] = q/p$ | Interpretation                              |
| ------ | --------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| $1$    | $0$                   | Success guaranteed — no failures expected   |
| $1/2$  | $1$                   | Expect 1 failure before first success       |
| $1/10$ | $9$                   | Rare success — expect 9 failures first      |
| $1/n$  | $n-1$                 | Very rare — expect roughly $n$ trials total |

For small $p$: $\mathbb{E}[X] = (1-p)/p \approx 1/p$. Waiting time scales as $1/p$ — the rarer the event, the longer you wait.

## 5. Variance — Full Derivation

### Step 1: Compute $\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)]$ (factorial moment)

The factorial moment avoids the clutter of $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ directly:

$$\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] = p\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k(k-1) q^k$$

The $k=0$ and $k=1$ terms vanish. For $k \geq 2$, differentiate the geometric series twice.

Start from $\sum q^k = \frac{1}{1-q}$.

First derivative: $\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k q^{k-1} = \frac{1}{(1-q)^2}$

Second derivative: $\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1)q^{k-2} = \frac{2}{(1-q)^3}$

Multiply by $q^2$:

$$\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1) q^k = \frac{2q^2}{(1-q)^3} = \frac{2q^2}{p^3}$$

Therefore:

$$\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] = p \cdot \frac{2q^2}{p^3} = \frac{2q^2}{p^2}$$

### Step 2: Recover $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] + \mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{2q^2}{p^2} + \frac{q}{p}$$

### Step 3: Apply variance formula

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = \frac{2q^2}{p^2} + \frac{q}{p} - \frac{q^2}{p^2}$$

$$= \frac{q^2}{p^2} + \frac{q}{p} = \frac{q^2 + qp}{p^2} = \frac{q(q+p)}{p^2} = \frac{q \cdot 1}{p^2}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = \frac{1-p}{p^2}}$$

### Intuition

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{q}{p^2} = \frac{q}{p} \cdot \frac{1}{p} = \mathbb{E}[X] \cdot \frac{1}{p}$$

The variance is the mean multiplied by $1/p$. Since $1/p \geq 1$ always, the variance always exceeds the mean — the geometric distribution is **overdispersed** relative to the Poisson (where mean = variance). For small $p$, the variance scales as $1/p^2$ while the mean scales as $1/p$ — the distribution becomes increasingly spread out relative to its mean.

## 6. The Memoryless Property — The Theorem That Makes Geometric Special

### Statement

If $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$, then for all $m, n \geq 0$:

$$\boxed{P(X \geq m+n \mid X \geq m) = P(X \geq n)}$$

The conditional distribution of _additional_ failures, given that $m$ failures have already occurred, is still $\text{Geom}(p)$.

### Proof

$$P(X \geq m+n \mid X \geq m) = \frac{P(X \geq m+n \text{ and } X \geq m)}{P(X \geq m)} = \frac{P(X \geq m+n)}{P(X \geq m)}$$

Using $P(X \geq k) = q^k$:

$$= \frac{q^{m+n}}{q^m} = q^n = P(X \geq n) \checkmark$$

### What it means, physically

"Given that I've already waited through $m$ failures, the probability distribution of _how much longer I still have to wait_ is the same as if I hadn't waited at all."

The coin has no memory. Past failures are irrelevant to future outcomes. The process resets after every failure.

### The characterisation theorem

The converse is equally important:

**Theorem.** If $X$ takes values in ${0,1,2,\ldots}$ and satisfies $P(X \geq m+n \mid X \geq m) = P(X \geq n)$ for all $m,n \geq 0$, then $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ for some $p$.

The geometric is the **unique** discrete memoryless distribution.

**Proof sketch.** Let $f(n) = P(X \geq n)$. The memoryless condition gives:

$$f(m+n) = f(m) \cdot f(n) \quad \text{for all } m,n \geq 0$$

This is **Cauchy's functional equation** on the integers. The only solutions with $f(0)=1$, $f$ non-increasing, are $f(n) = q^n$ for some $q \in [0,1]$. This means $P(X \geq n) = q^n$, which gives $P(X = k) = q^k - q^{k+1} = q^k(1-q) = q^k p$. This is $\text{Geom}(p)$. $\blacksquare$

> [!warning] Gambler's Fallacy The memoryless property is the mathematical proof that the **gambler's fallacy is a fallacy**.
> 
> After 10 consecutive tails, the next flip is still heads with probability $p$. The sequence of 10 tails changes nothing about the future. The coin does not "owe" you a head. The universe does not balance.
> 
> Mathematically: $P(\text{head on flip 11} \mid \text{10 tails in a row}) = p$.
> 
> **Don't anthropomorphise the coin.**

## 7. MGF — Full Derivation

$$M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} e^{tk} \cdot q^k p = p \sum_{k=0}^{\infty}(qe^t)^k$$

This is a geometric series in $qe^t$, valid when $qe^t < 1$, i.e., $t < -\ln q = -\ln(1-p)$:

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = \frac{p}{1-(1-p)e^t}, \qquad t < -\ln(1-p)}$$

### Recover $\mathbb{E}[X]$ from MGF

$$M_X'(t) = \frac{p \cdot (1-p)e^t}{[1-(1-p)e^t]^2}$$

At $t=0$:

$$M_X'(0) = \frac{p \cdot q}{(1-q)^2} = \frac{pq}{p^2} = \frac{q}{p} = \mathbb{E}[X] \checkmark$$

### Recover $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ from MGF

Differentiate again (using the quotient rule):

$$M_X''(0) = \frac{q(1+q)}{p^2}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = M_X''(0) - [M_X'(0)]^2 = \frac{q(1+q)}{p^2} - \frac{q^2}{p^2} = \frac{q+q^2-q^2}{p^2} = \frac{q}{p^2} \checkmark$$

## 8. Geometric as Exponential Family

Write the PMF:

$$P(X=k) = q^k p = p \cdot \exp(k \log q)$$

Matching to $h(x)\exp(\eta T(x) - A(\theta))$:

| Component | Value                | Meaning                                      |
| --------- | -------------------- | -------------------------------------------- |
| $h(k)$    | $1$                  | No base measure needed                       |
| $\eta(p)$ | $\log(1-p) = \log q$ | Natural parameter                            |
| $T(k)$    | $k$                  | Sufficient statistic — the count of failures |
| $A(p)$    | $-\log p$            | Log-partition: $\log\frac{1}{p}$             |

Verify: $h(k)\exp(\eta T(k) - A(p)) = 1 \cdot \exp(k\log q + \log p) = p \cdot q^k$ ✓

The sufficient statistic is $T(k) = k$ — the total number of failures is all you need to estimate $p$. You don't need to know the positions of the failures, only how many there were.

## 9. Connection to Negative Binomial

The Geometric is the $r=1$ special case of the Negative Binomial.

If we ask "how many failures before the $r$-th success?", the answer is $\text{NegBin}(r,p)$.

$$\text{Geom}(p) = \text{NegBin}(1, p)$$

More importantly: if $X_1, \ldots, X_r \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Geom}(p)$, then:

$$X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_r \sim \text{NegBin}(r, p)$$

**Proof via MGFs:**

$$M_{X_1+\cdots+X_r}(t) = \left(\frac{p}{1-qe^t}\right)^r$$

This is the MGF of $\text{NegBin}(r,p)$. The Negative Binomial is to the Geometric what the Binomial is to the Bernoulli — the sum of $r$ independent copies.

## 10. Connection to Exponential Distribution

The Geometric is the **discrete analogue** of the Exponential distribution. Both share the memoryless property — one on ${0,1,2,\ldots}$, the other on $[0,\infty)$.

### The limiting argument

Let each trial take time $\Delta t$, and set $p = \lambda \Delta t$ (probability of success in a small time window). Let $X_{\Delta t}$ be the number of failures before success.

The total waiting time is $T_{\Delta t} = X_{\Delta t} \cdot \Delta t$ (time per failure × number of failures).

As $\Delta t \to 0$ (continuous time limit):

$$T_{\Delta t} \xrightarrow{d} \text{Exp}(\lambda)$$

**Why:** The MGF of $T_{\Delta t}$ converges to $\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - s}$, which is the MGF of $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$.

### The analogy

| Property          | Geometric$(p)$      | Exponential$(\lambda)$  |
| ----------------- | ------------------- | ----------------------- |
| Support           | ${0,1,2,\ldots}$    | $[0,\infty)$            |
| Memoryless?       | ✅ (unique discrete) | ✅ (unique continuous)   |
| Mean              | $q/p$               | $1/\lambda$             |
| Variance          | $q/p^2$             | $1/\lambda^2$           |
| "Success rate"    | $p$ per trial       | $\lambda$ per unit time |
| Sum of $r$ copies | NegBin$(r,p)$       | Gamma$(r,\lambda)$      |

The Poisson process is the bridge: it generates Exponential inter-arrival times in continuous time, and Geometric waiting counts in discrete time.

## 12. Worked Example

> [!example] Defective Parts A machine produces defective parts with probability $p = 0.1$. Parts are inspected one by one. Let $X$ = number of non-defective parts inspected before the first defective one (treating "defective" as "success").
> 
> $X \sim \text{Geom}(0.1)$, $q = 0.9$.
> 
> **PMF:** $$P(X=k) = (0.9)^k (0.1)$$
> 
> **Expected failures before first defect:** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{0.9}{0.1} = 9$$
> 
> **Variance:** $$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{0.9}{0.01} = 90$$
> 
> **Probability of finding first defect by the 5th inspection ($X \leq 4$):** $$P(X \leq 4) = 1 - (0.9)^5 = 1 - 0.59049 = 0.40951$$
> 
> **Memoryless check:** Given that the first 5 parts were all fine, what's the probability the next 3 are also fine? $$P(X \geq 8 \mid X \geq 5) = P(X \geq 3) = (0.9)^3 = 0.729$$ Same as $P(X \geq 3)$ from the start. The conditioning on 5 prior successes changes nothing.
