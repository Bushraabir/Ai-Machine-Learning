
> _The simplest "waiting time" distribution — yet surprisingly rich. Every time I think I've squeezed everything out of it, something else appears._

---

## Setup & Motivation

I keep coming back to this mental picture: you're flipping a (possibly biased) coin, over and over. Each flip is independent. $p \in (0,1)$ is the probability of **success** (Heads, say) on any single flip. You stop the moment you get your first success.

**How long do you wait?**

That waiting time is exactly the Geometric$(p)$ random variable. Simple to state, but the moment you start pulling on the thread, the whole structure of discrete distributions starts showing up.

> [!note] Two conventions in the wild **Type 1 (Blitzstein & Hwang convention):** $X =$ number of _failures_ before the first success. So $X \in {0, 1, 2, \ldots}$. **Type 2:** $X =$ number of _trials_ until (and including) the first success. So $X \in {1, 2, 3, \ldots}$.
> 
> I'll use **Type 1** (failures before first success) throughout — it's what Blitzstein uses in Stat 110 and it makes the algebra cleaner. The two differ by exactly 1, so converting is trivial.

---

## Definition

$$X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$$

means $X$ counts the number of **failures before the first success**, in a sequence of independent Bernoulli$(p)$ trials.

---

## PMF

For $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$:

$$\boxed{P(X = k) = (1-p)^k \cdot p}$$

**Sanity check (always do this):** Does this actually sum to 1?

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (1-p)^k p = p \cdot \frac{1}{1-(1-p)} = p \cdot \frac{1}{p} = 1 \checkmark$$

That's just the geometric series $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} r^k = \frac{1}{1-r}$ for $r = 1-p \in (0,1)$. I love how the name of the distribution and the series that validates it are the same word — it's not a coincidence.

---

## CDF

$$P(X \leq k) = 1 - (1-p)^{k+1}, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$$

**Proof:** It's cleaner to compute the complement.

$$P(X > k) = P(\text{first } k+1 \text{ trials are all failures}) = (1-p)^{k+1}$$

So $P(X \leq k) = 1 - (1-p)^{k+1}$. $\square$

> _I find the complement route almost always cleaner for geometric/exponential-type calculations. Burn this reflex in._

---

## Expectation

$$E[X] = \frac{1-p}{p}$$

**Proof (via geometric series differentiation — my favourite approach):**

$$E[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k \cdot (1-p)^k p$$

Let $q = 1-p$. Then:

$$E[X] = p \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k \cdot q^k$$

I need $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k q^k$. Start from $\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} q^k = \frac{1}{1-q}$ and differentiate both sides w.r.t. $q$:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k q^{k-1} = \frac{1}{(1-q)^2}$$

Multiply both sides by $q$:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k q^k = \frac{q}{(1-q)^2}$$

So:

$$E[X] = p \cdot \frac{q}{(1-q)^2} = p \cdot \frac{(1-p)}{p^2} = \frac{1-p}{p}$$

$\square$

> [!tip] Intuition If $p = 1/2$, we expect 1 failure before the first success. If $p$ is tiny (rare event), we wait a _long_ time. $E[X] = (1-p)/p \approx 1/p$ for small $p$. Makes sense.

---

## Variance

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{1-p}{p^2}$$

**Proof:** Use $\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$. Compute $E[X^2]$ by differentiating the geometric series twice (or use the identity $E[X(X-1)] = E[X^2] - E[X]$ to avoid messy algebra).

$$E[X(X-1)] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k(k-1)(1-p)^k p = p \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1) q^k$$

Differentiate $\sum q^k = \frac{1}{1-q}$ twice:

$$\sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1) q^{k-2} = \frac{2}{(1-q)^3} \implies \sum_{k=2}^{\infty} k(k-1) q^k = \frac{2q^2}{(1-q)^3}$$

So:

$$E[X(X-1)] = p \cdot \frac{2q^2}{(1-q)^3} = \frac{2q^2}{p^2}$$

Then:

$$E[X^2] = E[X(X-1)] + E[X] = \frac{2q^2}{p^2} + \frac{q}{p}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \frac{2q^2}{p^2} + \frac{q}{p} - \frac{q^2}{p^2} = \frac{q^2}{p^2} + \frac{q}{p} = \frac{q(q+p)}{p^2} = \frac{q}{p^2} = \frac{1-p}{p^2}$$

$\square$

---

## The Memoryless Property

This is the theorem that makes the geometric distribution truly special. It's the **only** discrete memoryless distribution.

**Theorem.** If $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$, then for all $m, n \geq 0$:

$$P(X \geq m + n \mid X \geq m) = P(X \geq n)$$

**Proof:**

$$P(X \geq m+n \mid X \geq m) = \frac{P(X \geq m+n)}{P(X \geq m)} = \frac{(1-p)^{m+n}}{(1-p)^m} = (1-p)^n = P(X \geq n)$$

$\square$

**What this actually means:** If I've already suffered $m$ failures, the conditional distribution of _additional_ failures is still $\text{Geom}(p)$. The process has no memory. The coin doesn't "remember" that you've been losing.

> [!warning] Gambler's fallacy connection The memoryless property is the mathematical reason the gambler's fallacy is a fallacy. After 10 tails in a row, the probability of tails on the next flip is still $p$. The coin has no memory. **Don't anthropomorphise the coin.**

**Converse (important!):** If $X$ takes values in ${0,1,2,\ldots}$ and satisfies the memoryless property, then $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ for some $p$. The geometric distribution is _characterised_ by memorylessness among discrete distributions.

_(The continuous analogue is the Exponential — same story, different support.)_

---

## MGF

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = \frac{p}{1 - (1-p)e^t}, \quad t < -\ln(1-p)$$

**Derivation:**

$$M_X(t) = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} e^{tk}(1-p)^k p = p \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \left[(1-p)e^t\right]^k = \frac{p}{1-(1-p)e^t}$$

valid when $(1-p)e^t < 1$, i.e., $t < -\ln(1-p)$.

---

## Story Proof of $E[X] = q/p$ (Stat 110 style)

Blitzstein loves story proofs. Here's one for the mean that doesn't touch a single summation:

On the first trial:

- With probability $p$: first trial is a success, $X = 0$ failures.
- With probability $q = 1-p$: first trial is a failure. Now by memorylessness, we're back to the start, but we've already accumulated 1 failure.

So: $E[X] = p \cdot 0 + q \cdot (1 + E[X])$

$$E[X] = q + q \cdot E[X] \implies E[X](https://claude.ai/chat/1-q) = q \implies E[X] = \frac{q}{p} \checkmark$$

> _This is the kind of argument that makes me want to be a probabilist. One line, zero calculus, complete proof._

## Connection to Negative Binomial

If I ask: "How many failures before the $r$-th success?", the answer is $\text{NegBin}(r, p)$.

$$\text{Geom}(p) = \text{NegBin}(1, p)$$

And if $X_1, \ldots, X_r \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Geom}(p)$, then $X_1 + \cdots + X_r \sim \text{NegBin}(r, p)$. (Prove via MGFs — it's a one-liner.)

---

## Connection to Exponential Distribution

The geometric is the discrete analogue of the exponential. Both are memoryless. As $p \to 0$ and we rescale time (think of each trial as taking time $\Delta t$, with $p = \lambda \Delta t$), the geometric converges to an exponential. This is the standard Poisson process construction.

$$\text{Geom}(p) \xrightarrow{p \to 0, \text{ rescaled}} \text{Exp}(\lambda)$$
