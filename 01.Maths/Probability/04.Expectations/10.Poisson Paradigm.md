
> _This is the most powerful heuristic in discrete probability. It is the reason the Poisson distribution is not just a curiosity but a universal tool. Once you internalise it, you see Poisson approximations everywhere — and more importantly, you know when to trust them and when to be suspicious._

---

## The Statement

**Poisson Paradigm (informal).** Suppose events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ each occur with small probability $p_i = P(A_i)$, and are "nearly independent" (or at least weakly dependent). Let:

$$X = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{A_i} \qquad \lambda = E[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n p_i$$

Then $X$ is approximately $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$.

This is deliberately informal. The paradigm is a guiding principle — it tells you _what to try_. The rigorous version (Chen-Stein) tells you whether to trust it.

> _Blitzstein calls this the "Poisson Paradigm" and treats it as a way of thinking, not a theorem. I think that's exactly right. It's a lens, not a formula._

---

## Why It Should Be True: The Heuristic

Recall from [[05.Indicator Random Variables]] that $X = \sum \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ is a sum of Bernoulli random variables with small parameters $p_i$. Now:

- Each $\mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ is Bernoulli$(p_i)$, with $p_i$ small.
- If the $A_i$ were **independent**, then $X = \sum \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ would be a sum of independent Bernoullis — a generalised Binomial, and by the Poisson limit theorem from [[12.Poisson vs Binomial]], it would converge to $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$ as the $p_i \to 0$.
- If the $A_i$ are **weakly dependent**, the dependence should wash out in the sum, and the limit should still hold.

The paradigm is therefore the generalisation of the Poisson limit from i.i.d. Bernoullis to the case of non-identically distributed, weakly dependent indicators.

## The Rigorous Version: Chen-Stein Method

The Chen-Stein method gives an explicit **total variation bound** that makes the paradigm precise.

**Setup.** For each $i$, let $B_i \subseteq {1, \ldots, n}$ be a "dependency neighbourhood" of $i$ — the set of indices $j$ such that $A_i$ and $A_j$ are dependent (or non-negligibly correlated). Typically $i \in B_i$.

**Theorem (Chen-Stein).** With $X$, $\lambda$, $B_i$ as above:

$$d_{TV}!\left(\mathcal{L}(X),, \text{Pois}(\lambda)\right) \leq \frac{1}{\lambda}\left(b_1 + b_2\right)$$

where:

$$b_1 = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j \in B_i} p_i p_j \qquad \text{(within-neighbourhood marginal products)}$$

$$b_2 = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j \in B_i,, j \neq i} E[\mathbf{1}_{A_i} \mathbf{1}_{A_j}] = \sum_{i=1}^n \sum_{j \in B_i,, j \neq i} P(A_i \cap A_j) \qquad \text{(within-neighbourhood joint probabilities)}$$

**Intuition:**

- $b_1$ captures the size of the dependency neighbourhoods — large $B_i$ means more potential dependence.
- $b_2$ captures the actual strength of pairwise dependence within neighbourhoods.
- If events are independent, $B_i = {i}$, $b_2 = 0$, and $b_1 = \sum p_i^2 = \lambda \cdot \bar{p}$ (mean individual probability). The bound becomes $\sum p_i^2 / \lambda = \bar{p}$, which is small when all $p_i$ are small.

> _The Chen-Stein method works by constructing a coupling — a Poisson random variable on the same probability space as $X$ — and bounding how often they disagree. I haven't worked through the coupling argument fully. It's on the list._

## The Four Ingredients for Applying the Paradigm

When I see a problem and suspect Poisson, I run through these:

1. **Name the events.** What are the $A_i$'s? What does each one mean?
2. **Compute $p_i = P(A_i)$.** Usually a symmetry or counting argument.
3. **Compute $\lambda = \sum p_i$.** This is $E[X]$ by [[05.Indicator Random Variables]].
4. **Assess dependence.** Are the $A_i$'s nearly independent? Is each $p_i$ small? If yes to both, assert $X \approx \text{Pois}(\lambda)$ and use the Poisson PMF/CDF.

The dependence check is the hardest step and the one most often skipped carelessly. Don't skip it.

## Example 1: The Birthday Problem (Revisited)

**Setup.** $n$ people, each birthday uniform on ${1, \ldots, 365}$. Let $A_{ij}$ = event that persons $i$ and $j$ share a birthday, for $1 \leq i < j \leq n$.

**Step 1.** There are $\binom{n}{2}$ events.

**Step 2.** $p_{ij} = P(A_{ij}) = 1/365$ for all pairs.

**Step 3.** $\lambda = \binom{n}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{365}$.

For $n = 23$: $\lambda = \binom{23}{2}/365 = 253/365 \approx 0.693$.

**Step 4.** Dependence: $A_{ij}$ and $A_{ik}$ share person $i$, so they are dependent (they both involve person $i$'s birthday). But the dependence is mild — knowing persons $i$ and $j$ share a birthday gives a little information about person $i$'s birthday, which is weakly informative about whether persons $i$ and $k$ share a birthday too. For small $p$, this is a second-order effect.

**Conclusion:** $X \approx \text{Pois}(0.693)$.

$$P(\text{at least one shared birthday}) \approx 1 - P(X = 0) \approx 1 - e^{-0.693} \approx 1 - \frac{1}{2} = 0.5$$

The exact answer for $n = 23$ is $\approx 0.5073$. The Poisson approximation gives $0.5$. Excellent.

> [[03.The Birthday Problem|The Birthday Problem]] is usually solved by the complement method — computing $P(\text{no shared birthday})$ exactly. The Poisson paradigm gives the same answer in two lines. For larger $n$ or more complex birthday variants, the Poisson approach scales far better._

## Example 2: Runs in a Random Sequence

**Setup.** Flip a fair coin $n$ times. A **run of heads** of length $k$ starting at position $i$ means flips $i, i+1, \ldots, i+k-1$ are all heads and (if $i > 1$) flip $i-1$ is tails, and (if $i+k \leq n$) flip $i+k$ is tails. Let $X$ = number of such runs.

For simplicity: let $A_i$ = event that there is a run of at least $k$ heads starting at position $i$, for $i = 1, \ldots, n-k+1$.

**Step 2.** $p_i = P(A_i) = (1/2)^k$ for all $i$ (just need $k$ consecutive heads; the boundary conditions are a small correction).

**Step 3.** $\lambda = (n - k + 1) \cdot (1/2)^k \approx n/2^k$.

**Step 4.** Dependence: $A_i$ and $A_j$ are independent when $|i - j| \geq k$ (non-overlapping windows). They are dependent only for $|i-j| < k$ — a neighbourhood of size $2k - 1$ around each $i$.

Since $k$ is fixed and $p_i = 1/2^k$ is small, the Chen-Stein bound is of order $k \cdot (1/2^k)^2 \cdot n / \lambda = k/2^k$, which is small for large $k$.

**Conclusion:** $X \approx \text{Pois}(n/2^k)$.

$$P(\text{no run of length } k) \approx e^{-n/2^k}$$

This sets $e^{-n/2^k} \approx 1/2$ when $n \approx 2^k \ln 2$, giving the well-known result that you need about $2^k \ln 2$ flips to expect to see a run of $k$ heads. For $k = 10$: about $710$ flips.

## Example 3: Fixed Points of a Random Permutation

From [[05.Indicator Random Variables]]: $X$ = number of fixed points of a uniform random permutation of ${1, \ldots, n}$.

$A_i = {\sigma(i) = i}$, $p_i = 1/n$, $\lambda = 1$.

Are the $A_i$ independent? No — they are negatively correlated (if many positions are fixed, fewer are available for others). But the dependence is weak for large $n$.

**Poisson paradigm:** $X \approx \text{Pois}(1)$.

$$P(X = 0) \approx e^{-1} \approx 0.3679$$

The exact answer (by inclusion-exclusion) is $\sum_{k=0}^n (-1)^k / k! \to e^{-1}$. The paradigm gives the right answer and the right intuition — the Poisson approximation is exact in the limit.

This is actually the **derangements problem** resolved by the Poisson paradigm in one line.

## Example 4: Triangles in a Random Graph

**Setup.** Erdős–Rényi random graph $G(n, p)$: $n$ vertices, each edge present independently with probability $p$. Let $X$ = number of triangles.

**Step 1.** Index triangles by triples ${i,j,k}$. There are $\binom{n}{3}$ of them.

**Step 2.** $P(A_{{i,j,k}}) = p^3$ (all three edges must be present).

**Step 3.** $\lambda = \binom{n}{3} p^3$.

**Step 4.** Two triangles sharing an edge are dependent; triangles sharing no edge are independent. The dependency neighbourhood of triangle $T$ consists of all triangles sharing at least one edge with $T$ — there are $O(n)$ of these.

**Conclusion:** If $p = c/n$ (sparse graph), then $\lambda = \binom{n}{3}(c/n)^3 \to c^3/6$, and $X \approx \text{Pois}(c^3/6)$.

$$P(\text{no triangles}) \approx e^{-c^3/6}$$

This is a classical result in random graph theory, and the Poisson paradigm delivers it almost effortlessly.

> _This example is why combinatorialists care about the Poisson paradigm. The exact distribution of the triangle count is intractable. The Poisson approximation gives a clean, useful answer with quantified error._

## When the [[10.Poisson Paradigm]] Fails

The paradigm breaks down when:

**1. Events are strongly positively dependent.** If $A_i$ occurring makes $A_j$ much more likely, the sum $X$ is more dispersed than Poisson. $\text{Var}(X) > E[X]$ — overdispersion. The Negative Binomial is a better model (see [[04.Negative Binomial Distribution]]).

**2. $\lambda$ is large with each $p_i$ not small.** If $\lambda = 1000$ because there are $1000$ events each with $p_i = 1$, then $X = 1000$ deterministically. Nothing about this is Poisson.

**3. Events have a hard structural constraint.** If $X$ is constrained to be at most $n$ (like a Binomial), the Poisson's unbounded support is wrong. The approximation fails in the right tail.

**4. Strong negative dependence.** If events are mutually exclusive in large groups, $X$ is more concentrated than Poisson. The variance is too small.

> _The paradigm is a tool for nearly independent, rare events. "Nearly independent" does real work — don't gloss over it. In the Chen-Stein bound, positive dependence inflates $b_2$, which inflates the error. Check the sign of the dependence before applying the paradigm._

## The Paradigm as a Modelling Philosophy

Beyond specific problems, the Poisson paradigm encodes a modelling philosophy:

**Step 1: Decompose $X$ into indicators.** Write $X = \sum \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$. This always works — every count is a sum of indicators.

**Step 2: Use linearity to get $E[X]$.** $E[X] = \sum p_i$. No independence needed.

**Step 3: Check if Poisson applies.** Small $p_i$, weak dependence, no structural upper bound? Yes → Poisson. Otherwise → think harder.

**Step 4: Use the Poisson to answer distributional questions.** $P(X = 0)$, $P(X \geq k)$, etc. These would be painful to compute from the joint distribution of all the $A_i$'s; they're trivial from $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$.

The paradigm converts hard distributional questions into easy Poisson calculations, at the cost of an approximation error that Chen-Stein quantifies.
