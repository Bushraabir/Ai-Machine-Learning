

> _The Binomial sits at the centre of a web of limits. Push $p$ small and $n$ large — you get Poisson. Push $n$ large with $p$ fixed — you get Normal. These are not just approximations; they are structural theorems about what the Binomial becomes under different asymptotic regimes. Understanding which limit applies when is one of the most practically important skills in probability._

---

## Setup

$X \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$. Two parameters, two independent limiting regimes:

$$\text{Regime 1: } n \to \infty,; p = \frac{\lambda}{n} \to 0,; np = \lambda \text{ fixed} \implies \text{Pois}(\lambda)$$

$$\text{Regime 2: } n \to \infty,; p \text{ fixed},; np = \mu,; np(1-p) = \sigma^2 \implies \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$$

These are genuinely different limits. They live in different parts of the $(n, p)$ parameter space and require different proof techniques. Getting them confused is a real mistake.

---

## Limit 1: Binomial $\to$ Poisson

_(Full proof in [[12.Poisson vs Binomial]] and [[09.Poisson Distribution]]. Reproduced here for completeness.)_

**Theorem.** Let $X_n \sim \text{Bin}(n, \lambda/n)$ for fixed $\lambda > 0$. Then for each $k \geq 0$:

$$P(X_n = k) \to \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

i.e., $X_n \xrightarrow{d} \text{Pois}(\lambda)$.

**Proof via MGFs:**

$$M_{X_n}(t) = \left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n} + \frac{\lambda}{n}e^t\right)^n = \left(1 + \frac{\lambda(e^t-1)}{n}\right)^n \to e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$$

which is the $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$ MGF. $\square$

**When to use:** $n$ large, $p$ small, $np$ moderate. The regime of rare events.

**Error:** $d_{TV}(\text{Bin}(n,p), \text{Pois}(np)) \leq 2np^2 = 2\lambda p$ (Le Cam).

## Limit 2: Binomial $\to$ Normal (The CLT)

**Theorem (De Moivre–Laplace, 1733/1812).** Let $X_n \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$ with $p \in (0,1)$ fixed. Then:

$$\frac{X_n - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

This is the oldest instance of the Central Limit Theorem — proved for the Binomial by De Moivre (1733) a century before Laplace gave the general version.

**Proof via MGFs:**

Let $Y_i = \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ be i.i.d. Bernoulli$(p)$, so $X_n = \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$. Standardise:

$$Z_n = \frac{X_n - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (Y_i - p)}{\sqrt{n} \cdot \sqrt{p(1-p)}}$$

Let $W_i = (Y_i - p)/\sqrt{p(1-p)}$, so $E[W_i] = 0$, $\text{Var}(W_i) = 1$, and $Z_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}\sum W_i$.

The MGF of $W_i$:

$$M_{W_i}(t) = E[e^{tW_i}] = pe^{t(1-p)/\sqrt{p(1-p)}} + (1-p)e^{-tp/\sqrt{p(1-p)}}$$

Expand to second order using $e^x = 1 + x + x^2/2 + O(x^3)$:

$$M_{W_i}(t) = 1 + 0 \cdot t + \frac{t^2}{2} \cdot 1 + O(t^3/\sqrt{n}) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2} + O!\left(\frac{t^3}{\sqrt{n}}\right)$$

(The $O(t^3/\sqrt{n})$ term arises because $W_i$ has bounded third moment, so the remainder is controlled.)

Then:

$$M_{Z_n}(t) = \left[M_{W_i}!\left(\frac{t}{\sqrt{n}}\right)\right]^n = \left[1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O!\left(\frac{t^3}{n^{3/2}}\right)\right]^n \to e^{t^2/2}$$

using $(1 + x_n/n)^n \to e^x$ when $x_n \to x$. The limit $e^{t^2/2}$ is the MGF of $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$. $\square$

> _The MGF proof is clean but hides the geometric intuition. De Moivre's original proof used Stirling's approximation directly on the Binomial PMF — much more painful but illuminating. The Stirling proof is in the to-do list._

## The Two Regimes, Side by Side

Let me be precise about where each approximation lives.

$$\text{Bin}(n,p) \approx \begin{cases} \text{Pois}(np) & \text{if } p \to 0,; n \to \infty,; np = O(1) \ \mathcal{N}(np,; np(1-p)) & \text{if } p \text{ fixed},; n \to \infty \end{cases}$$

The crossover between regimes is roughly $p \sim 1/\sqrt{n}$:

- If $p \ll 1/\sqrt{n}$: Poisson regime. $np \to 0$ or stays bounded.
- If $p \gg 1/\sqrt{n}$: Normal regime. $np \to \infty$ and the CLT kicks in.
- If $p \sim 1/\sqrt{n}$: both approximations are rough; need exact Binomial or a finer analysis.

> _I find it helpful to think of the $(n,p)$ parameter space as a plane, with the Poisson regime near the $p=0$ axis and the Normal regime in the bulk. The transition zone is where neither approximation is great._

## Why the Two Limits Are Different Objects

This is the conceptually important point, and it confused me for a while.

**The Poisson limit** is a limit of the **PMF** — a discrete distribution converges to another discrete distribution. The support stays ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$.

**The Normal limit** is a limit of a **standardised** random variable — the distribution of $X_n$ itself does not converge (its mean $np \to \infty$). Only after subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation does the limit exist, and it is a **continuous** distribution.

These are different modes of convergence being used for different purposes:

||Poisson Limit|Normal Limit|
|---|---|---|
|**What converges**|$P(X_n = k)$ pointwise|$P(Z_n \leq z)$ for all $z$|
|**Standardisation needed?**|No|Yes — must centre and scale|
|**Limit distribution**|Discrete: $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$|Continuous: $\mathcal{N}(0,1)$|
|**What's fixed**|$\lambda = np$|$p$ (and hence $\mu = np \to \infty$)|
|**Mode of convergence**|Pointwise PMF convergence|Convergence in distribution (CDF)|

> _The Normal limit throws away the discreteness of the Binomial entirely. The Poisson limit preserves it. This is not a flaw — it reflects the fact that in the Normal regime ($p$ fixed, $n$ large), $X_n$ takes $n+1$ values spread over an interval of width $\sim \sqrt{n}$, and the spacing $1/\sqrt{n}$ between consecutive values becomes negligible. The discreteness washes out._

## Continuity Correction

Because the Binomial is discrete and the Normal is continuous, naively approximating $P(X \leq k)$ by $\Phi((k - np)/\sqrt{np(1-p)})$ underuses the fact that $X$ only takes integer values.

The **continuity correction** fixes this:

$$P(X \leq k) \approx \Phi!\left(\frac{k + 1/2 - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)$$

$$P(X = k) \approx \Phi!\left(\frac{k + 1/2 - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right) - \Phi!\left(\frac{k - 1/2 - np}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}\right)$$

The $+1/2$ accounts for the fact that the integer $k$ corresponds to the interval $[k - 1/2, k + 1/2)$ on the real line.

**Example.** $X \sim \text{Bin}(100, 0.5)$. Compute $P(X \leq 55)$.

Without correction: $\Phi!\left(\frac{55 - 50}{5}\right) = \Phi(1) \approx 0.8413$.

With correction: $\Phi!\left(\frac{55.5 - 50}{5}\right) = \Phi(1.1) \approx 0.8643$.

Exact: $P(X \leq 55) \approx 0.8644$. The continuity correction is dramatically better.

> _I used to think the continuity correction was a pedantic adjustment. It's not — it can change the third significant figure. For hypothesis testing, that matters. Always use it when approximating discrete distributions with the Normal._

## The Berry-Esseen Theorem: Quantifying the Normal Approximation

The CLT says $Z_n \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$ but says nothing about the rate. The Berry-Esseen theorem fixes this.

**Theorem (Berry-Esseen).** Let $X = \sum_{i=1}^n Y_i$ with $Y_i$ i.i.d., $E[Y_i] = 0$, $\text{Var}(Y_i) = \sigma^2$, $E[|Y_i|^3] = \rho < \infty$. Then:

$$\sup_z \left|P!\left(\frac{X}{\sigma\sqrt{n}} \leq z\right) - \Phi(z)\right| \leq \frac{C\rho}{\sigma^3 \sqrt{n}}$$

where $C \leq 0.4748$ (best known constant).

For the Binomial, $Y_i = \mathbf{1}_{A_i} - p$, so $\sigma^2 = p(1-p)$ and $\rho = E[|Y_i|^3] \leq 1$.

$$\sup_z \left|P(Z_n \leq z) - \Phi(z)\right| \leq \frac{C}{(p(1-p))^{3/2}\sqrt{n}} \leq \frac{0.4748}{\sqrt{np(1-p)}}$$

So the error in the Normal approximation is $O(1/\sqrt{n})$, uniform over all $z$.

**Practical implication:** The approximation is good to $O(1/\sqrt{n})$. For $n = 100$, $p = 0.5$: error $\leq 0.4748/\sqrt{25} \approx 0.095$. Not tiny. For $n = 10000$: error $\leq 0.0047$. Very good.

> _Berry-Esseen is the rigorous justification for the "rule of thumb" $np \geq 5$ and $n(1-p) \geq 5$ — it ensures $\sqrt{np(1-p)}$ is large enough that the $1/\sqrt{n}$ error is acceptably small. The rule of thumb is conservative; Berry-Esseen tells you exactly how conservative._

## The Local Limit Theorem

The CLT (Berry-Esseen) is about the CDF — the error in $P(Z_n \leq z)$. The **local limit theorem** is about the PMF directly.

**Theorem (Local CLT for Binomial).** For $X \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$ and $k$ in the bulk of the distribution:

$$P(X = k) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi np(1-p)}} \exp!\left(-\frac{(k-np)^2}{2np(1-p)}\right) \cdot \left(1 + O(n^{-1/2})\right)$$

This is proved using Stirling's approximation:

$$n! \approx \sqrt{2\pi n}, n^n e^{-n}$$

applied to $\binom{n}{k} = n!/(k!(n-k)!)$. The resulting algebra is the calculation De Moivre originally did.

The local CLT is stronger than the global CLT — it approximates individual probabilities, not just cumulative ones.

## Why This All Matters

The Binomial has two faces:

- **The rare-event face:** when $p$ is small, it counts rare successes. The Poisson captures this.
- **The averaging face:** when $n$ is large and $p$ is fixed, it is a sum of many i.i.d. terms. The Normal captures this.

The De Moivre-Laplace theorem is historically the first CLT — every subsequent CLT (for general i.i.d. sums, for dependent variables, for martingales) is a generalisation of the statement proved here for the Binomial. Understanding this case deeply is understanding the source of the entire CLT family.

> _Every time I prove the CLT for the Binomial, I feel like I'm watching probability being invented in real time. De Moivre proved this in 1733 to solve gambling problems. He had no idea he was proving the most important theorem in statistics._

## The Full Picture

$$\text{Bin}(n,p) \xrightarrow{p \to 0,, np \to \lambda} \text{Pois}(\lambda) \xrightarrow{?} \text{Normal?}$$

Does the Poisson itself converge to Normal? Yes — as $\lambda \to \infty$:

$$\frac{\text{Pois}(\lambda) - \lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}} \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$$

This follows from the CLT applied to the decomposition $\text{Pois}(\lambda) = \sum_{i=1}^n \text{Pois}(\lambda/n)$ (additivity). So:

$$\text{Bin}(n,p) \longrightarrow \text{Pois}(\lambda) \longrightarrow \mathcal{N}(\lambda, \lambda)$$

The Binomial, Poisson, and Normal form a hierarchy of limits. The Poisson is an intermediate object between the finite Binomial and the continuous Normal.
