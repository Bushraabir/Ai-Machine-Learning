For a **discrete** random variable $X$ with PMF $p$, the **expected value** (expectation, mean) is:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{x} x \cdot P(X = x) = \sum_x x \cdot p(x)$$

where the sum is over all $x$ in the support of $X$.

This is a weighted average of the possible values of $X$, weighted by their probabilities. It exists (is finite) when $\sum_x |x| \cdot p(x) < \infty$ — absolute convergence is required so the sum doesn't depend on order.

For a **continuous** random variable $X$ with PDF $f$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} x, f(x), dx$$

Same idea. Weighted average, now with a density instead of point masses.

> $\mathbb{E}[X]$ need not be a value $X$ can actually take. The expected number of children per family might be 2.3. The expected value is a property of the distribution, not a prediction of a single outcome.

## Existence and Absolute Convergence

$\mathbb{E}[X]$ is defined only when $\sum_x |x| p(x) < \infty$ (discrete) or $\int |x| f(x), dx < \infty$ (continuous). If this fails, the expectation does not exist.

### The St. Petersburg Paradox

A coin is flipped until heads. If heads first appears on flip $k$, you win $2^k$ dollars. What is a fair price to pay?

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{winnings}] = \sum_{k=1}^\infty 2^k \cdot \frac{1}{2^k} = \sum_{k=1}^\infty 1 = \infty$$

The expected value is infinite. No finite price is "fair" in the expectation sense. Yet no rational person would pay $1,000,000 to play. This is not a contradiction — it is a reminder that $\mathbb{E}[X] = \infty$ is a mathematical statement, not a practical recommendation. Expected value is not the whole story (variance, risk aversion, utility — all matter in practice).

The St. Petersburg game is a rare example of an RV with no finite expectation. In most of probability, we work with RVs where $\mathbb{E}[X]$ exists and is finite.

## LOTUS — The Law of the Unconscious Statistician

This is the theorem that makes computing $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ not require finding the distribution of $g(X)$ first.

**Theorem (LOTUS).** If $X$ is a discrete RV with PMF $p$ and $g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$, then:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x), p(x)$$

If $X$ is continuous with PDF $f$:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x), f(x), dx$$

The sum/integral is over the distribution of $X$, not of $g(X)$.

**Why this is nontrivial:** To compute $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$, you might think you need to find the PMF of $X^2$ first, then take its expectation. LOTUS says: don't bother. Just square the values of $X$ and weight by $p(x)$. The distribution of $X$ is all you need.

### Proof (discrete case)

Let $Y = g(X)$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_y y \cdot P(Y = y) = \sum_y y \sum_{x:, g(x) = y} p(x) = \sum_y \sum_{x:, g(x)=y} g(x), p(x) = \sum_x g(x), p(x) \quad \square$$

The key step: group together all $x$ values that map to the same $y = g(x)$, then ungroup. The grouping is just reindexing the sum.

> LOTUS is called "unconscious" because you can use it without thinking about what distribution $g(X)$ has — you proceed as if you're computing $\mathbb{E}[X]$ but with $g(x)$ in place of $x$. The "unconscious" statistician does it without realizing they're skipping a step.

## Linearity of Expectation

This is the most important theorem in probability. I will state it carefully and then say why it deserves that status.

**Theorem.** For any random variables $X, Y$ (on the same probability space) and constants $a, b, c$:

$$\mathbb{E}[aX + bY + c] = a\mathbb{E}[X] + b\mathbb{E}[Y] + c$$

More generally, for any $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ and constants $a_1, \ldots, a_n$:

$$\mathbb{E}\left[\sum_{i=1}^n a_i X_i\right] = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i \mathbb{E}[X_i]$$

**This holds regardless of whether $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent.**

### Proof (two discrete RVs)

$$\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \sum_x \sum_y (x + y), P(X=x, Y=y)$$

$$= \sum_x \sum_y x, P(X=x, Y=y) + \sum_x \sum_y y, P(X=x, Y=y)$$

$$= \sum_x x \sum_y P(X=x, Y=y) + \sum_y y \sum_x P(X=x, Y=y)$$

$$= \sum_x x, P(X=x) + \sum_y y, P(Y=y) = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y] \quad \square$$

The marginal PMFs $P(X=x) = \sum_y P(X=x, Y=y)$ are the only thing used. No factoring of the joint distribution was needed. Independence plays no role.

> **Why this is remarkable:** $\mathbb{E}[X+Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y]$ even when $X$ and $Y$ are the same random variable ($X = Y$), giving $\mathbb{E}[2X] = 2\mathbb{E}[X]$. Even when they are perfectly correlated. Even when $Y = -X$, giving $\mathbb{E}[0] = 0$. The theorem doesn't know or care about dependence. Expectation is a linear functional on random variables, full stop.

## Indicator Random Variables — The Killer Application of Linearity

The indicator of event $A$:

$$\mathbf{1}_A = \begin{cases} 1 & \text{if } A \text{ occurs} \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases}$$

Key fact: $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_A] = P(A)$.

_Proof:_ $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_A] = 1 \cdot P(A) + 0 \cdot P(A^c) = P(A)$. $\square$

This trivial observation, combined with linearity, is the engine behind most clever expectation computations.

**The strategy:** To compute $\mathbb{E}[X]$ for a count-type random variable, write $X = \sum_{i} \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ where each $A_i$ is an event. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_i \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{A_i}] = \sum_i P(A_i)$$

No joint distributions. No combinatorial identities. Just $n$ marginal probabilities.

### Example 1 — Binomial Mean

$X \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$. Write $X = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ where $A_i = {\text{trial } i \text{ succeeds}}$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n P(A_i) = np$$

Compare with the direct computation: $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k=0}^n k \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$

which requires the identity $k\binom{n}{k} = n\binom{n-1}{k-1}$ and is genuinely unpleasant. The indicator method takes three lines.

### Example 2 — Hypergeometric Mean (back to ELK)

[[15.The ELK Problem|Seen this before]] . $X = \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbf{1}[\text{draw } j \text{ is tagged}]$. By symmetry, $P(\text{draw } j \text{ tagged}) = m/N$. So $\mathbb{E}[X] = nm/N$. Two lines.

### Example 3 — Expected Number of Fixed Points (Derangements)

A random permutation of ${1, \ldots, n}$. Let $X$ = number of fixed points (positions $i$ where $\sigma(i) = i$).

Let $\mathbf{1}_i = \mathbf{1}[\sigma(i) = i]$. By symmetry, $P(\sigma(i) = i) = 1/n$ for each $i$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n P(\sigma(i) = i) = n \cdot \frac{1}{n} = 1$$

The expected number of fixed points is **1**, for any $n$. The individual indicators are highly dependent (they're not even close to independent — knowing $\sigma(1) = 1$ changes the probabilities for all others). Linearity doesn't care.

## Expectations of Named Distributions

These are worth memorizing — not by rote, but by deriving them once using indicators, then knowing the answer cold.

| Distribution                   | $\mathbb{E}[X]$ | Derivation method     |
| ------------------------------ | --------------- | --------------------- |
| $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$          | $p$             | Direct                |
| $\text{Bin}(n,p)$              | $np$            | Indicators            |
| $\text{Geom}(p)$               | $1/p$           | Direct or story       |
| $\text{NegBin}(r,p)$           | $r/p$           | Sum of Geometrics     |
| $\text{HGeom}(m, N-m, n)$      | $nm/N$          | Indicators + symmetry |
| $\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$      | $\lambda$       | Direct from PMF       |
| $\text{Uniform}(a,b)$          | $(a+b)/2$       | Symmetry              |
| $\text{Normal}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ | $\mu$           | Symmetry              |
| $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$         | $1/\lambda$     | Integration by parts  |

### Geometric Mean — the "story" derivation

$X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$: number of trials until first success.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = 1 + (1-p)\mathbb{E}[X]$$

Why? On the first trial: with probability $p$, you succeed and $X = 1$. With probability $1-p$, you fail, and by the memoryless property, you're back where you started — you still need $\mathbb{E}[X]$ more trials. So:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = p \cdot 1 + (1-p)(1 + \mathbb{E}[X]) \implies p\mathbb{E}[X] = 1 \implies \mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{1}{p}$$

This is the **first-step analysis** / recursive expectation method. Beautiful. Requires the memoryless property — and in fact characterizes it.

## Expectation Does Not Commute with Nonlinear Functions — Jensen's Inequality

**Theorem (Jensen).** If $g$ is convex and $\mathbb{E}[X]$ exists:

$$g(\mathbb{E}[X]) \leq \mathbb{E}[g(X)]$$

If $g$ is concave, the inequality reverses.

Convex functions curve upward — by convexity, $g$ lies above its tangent line at $\mathbb{E}[X]$, and averaging over the distribution of $X$ pushes $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ above $g(\mathbb{E}[X])$.

### Consequences

- $\mathbb{E}[X^2] \geq (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ (take $g(x) = x^2$, convex) — this is exactly $\text{Var}(X) \geq 0$
- $\mathbb{E}[\sqrt{X}] \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X]}$ (take $g(x) = \sqrt{x}$, concave)
- $\mathbb{E}[e^X] \geq e^{\mathbb{E}[X]}$ (exponential is convex)
- $\mathbb{E}[\log X] \leq \log \mathbb{E}[X]$ (log is concave) — AM-GM as a special case
- The ELK estimator $\hat{N} = mn/k$ is biased upward because $\mathbb{E}[1/X] \geq 1/\mathbb{E}[X]$ (Jensen, $g(x) = 1/x$ convex for $x > 0$)

Jensen is the reason you cannot casually pass expectations through nonlinear functions. $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = g(\mathbb{E}[X])$ holds **if and only if** $g$ is linear (or $X$ is degenerate).

## The Tail Sum Formula

For a non-negative integer-valued RV $X$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k=1}^\infty P(X \geq k) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P(X > k)$$

**Proof:**

$$\sum_{k=1}^\infty P(X \geq k) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \sum_{n=k}^\infty P(X=n) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \sum_{k=1}^n P(X=n) = \sum_{n=1}^\infty n, P(X=n) = \mathbb{E}[X] \quad \square$$

The swap of summation order is justified by absolute convergence (assuming $\mathbb{E}[X] < \infty$).

For non-negative continuous $X$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^\infty P(X > t), dt$$

**Proof:** $\int_0^\infty P(X > t), dt = \int_0^\infty \int_t^\infty f(x), dx, dt = \int_0^\infty \int_0^x dt, f(x), dx = \int_0^\infty x f(x), dx = \mathbb{E}[X] \quad \square$

(Fubini's theorem to swap the order of integration.)

The tail sum formula is useful when $P(X > t)$ has a clean closed form but the PMF/PDF does not — or when it converts a messy sum into a cleaner one.

## Conditional Expectation 

The **conditional expectation** $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = y]$ is a number (for each fixed $y$). The object $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ — leaving $Y$ as a random variable — is itself a random variable. This is the beginning of a very deep story.

The **Law of Total Expectation (Adam's Law)**:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]]$$

This is a tower property — you can compute $\mathbb{E}[X]$ by first conditioning on $Y$, computing the conditional expectation, and then averaging over $Y$. It is to expectation what LOTP is to probability.

These deserve their own note. But the seed is here.
