
> _These two distributions are so entangled that I keep needing to re-examine where one ends and the other begins. They're not just "related" — they are the same object viewed at different scales, connected by conditioning, and separated only by what you choose to treat as fixed vs. random._

---

## The Surface-Level Answer

||Binomial$(n,p)$|Poisson$(\lambda)$|
|---|---|---|
|**$n$ (number of trials)**|Fixed, known|Infinite / not specified|
|**$p$ (success probability)**|Fixed, known|Implicit: $p = \lambda/n$ small|
|**Support**|${0, 1, \ldots, n}$|${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$|
|**Parameters**|Two: $n, p$|One: $\lambda = np$|
|**Mean**|$np$|$\lambda$|
|**Variance**|$np(1-p)$|$\lambda$|
|**Use when**|$n$ small or $p$ not small|$n$ large, $p$ small, $np = \lambda$ moderate|

This table is correct but shallow. The real story is about _why_ they're related, when the approximation is valid, and what is lost and gained in the limit.

## The Deep Relationship: Three Perspectives

### Perspective 1 — The Poisson IS the Binomial at Infinity

The Poisson limit theorem (proved in [[09.Poisson Distribution]]) says:

$$\text{Bin}(n, \lambda/n) \xrightarrow{d} \text{Pois}(\lambda) \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

But let me unpack what this is really saying.

A $\text{Bin}(n,p)$ random variable counts successes in $n$ **fixed** Bernoulli$(p)$ trials. As $n \to \infty$ with $p = \lambda/n \to 0$, two things happen simultaneously:

- The number of opportunities ($n$) grows without bound.
- Each individual opportunity becomes vanishingly rare ($p \to 0$).
- But the _expected count_ $np = \lambda$ stays fixed.

The Poisson is what you get when you take this regime to its logical extreme: infinitely many opportunities, each individually negligible, with a fixed expected total. This is precisely the regime of **rare events in large populations**.

> _The Binomial asks: "I have $n$ chances; how many successes?" The Poisson asks: "Rare things happen at rate $\lambda$; how many happened?" The first fixes the number of trials. The second fixes the rate. In the limit they agree._

---

### Perspective 2 — Conditioning Converts One to the Other

This is the result that surprised me most (proved in [[09.Poisson Distribution]]):

**Theorem.** Let $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$, $Y \sim \text{Pois}(\mu)$, independent. Then:

$$X \mid X + Y = n ;\sim; \text{Bin}!\left(n,, \frac{\lambda}{\lambda + \mu}\right)$$

So: start with two Poissons, condition on their sum being fixed — you get a Binomial. The total count is now fixed (it's $n$), and each event is independently type-1 with probability $\lambda/(\lambda+\mu)$.

The reverse: start with $\text{Bin}(n, p)$ and let $n$ become random and Poisson-distributed — you recover a Poisson marginal.

**Theorem.** Let $N \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$ and, given $N = n$, let $X \mid N \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$. Then marginally:

$$X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda p)$$

**Proof:**

$$P(X = k) = \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} P(X = k \mid N = n) P(N = n) = \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^n}{n!}$$

$$= \frac{e^{-\lambda}(\lambda p)^k}{k!} \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \frac{(\lambda(1-p))^{n-k}}{(n-k)!} = \frac{e^{-\lambda}(\lambda p)^k}{k!} \cdot e^{\lambda(1-p)} = \frac{e^{-\lambda p}(\lambda p)^k}{k!}$$

which is $\text{Pois}(\lambda p)$. $\square$

> _In other words: if the number of trials is itself Poisson-distributed, and each trial succeeds with probability $p$, then the number of successes is Poisson with rate $\lambda p$. The Binomial-inside-Poisson structure collapses back to a Poisson. This is the Poisson's self-consistency under randomisation of $n$._

---

### Perspective 3 — MGFs Make the Limit Transparent

The MGF of $\text{Bin}(n, \lambda/n)$ is:

$$M_n(t) = \left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n} + \frac{\lambda}{n}e^t\right)^n = \left(1 + \frac{\lambda(e^t - 1)}{n}\right)^n$$

Taking $n \to \infty$:

$$M_n(t) \to e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$$

which is exactly the $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$ MGF. The limit in the exponent is just $(1 + x/n)^n \to e^x$ with $x = \lambda(e^t - 1)$.

The MGF proof is three lines and makes clear exactly where the exponential comes from.

## What the Approximation Costs: Variance

The most important practical difference is the variance.

$$\text{Var}(\text{Bin}(n,p)) = np(1-p) = \lambda(1-p) < \lambda$$

$$\text{Var}(\text{Pois}(\lambda)) = \lambda$$

The Binomial has **strictly smaller variance** than the Poisson with the same mean. The gap is $\lambda p = \lambda^2/n$, which is small when $p$ is small.

Intuitively: the Binomial is **bounded above by $n$** — it cannot exceed the number of trials. This upper bound suppresses the right tail and reduces variance. The Poisson has no upper bound, so its right tail is heavier, and its variance equals its mean.

When $p$ is small, $\lambda p \approx 0$ and the variance gap disappears. But for moderate $p$ (say $p = 0.1$), the difference matters:

$$\text{Var}(\text{Bin}(100, 0.1)) = 9 \qquad \text{vs.} \qquad \text{Var}(\text{Pois}(10)) = 10$$

Not huge, but not zero. The Poisson approximation slightly overestimates dispersion.

## When to Use Which

### Use Binomial when:

- $n$ is small (say $n \leq 30$) regardless of $p$.
- $p$ is not small (say $p \geq 0.1$) — the approximation error in variance is non-negligible.
- You care about the upper bound — the Binomial cannot exceed $n$; the Poisson can take any non-negative integer value.
- You're computing exact probabilities and $n$ is manageable.

### Use Poisson when:

- $n$ is large and $p$ is small, with $\lambda = np$ moderate.
- The "number of trials" is not well-defined or effectively infinite (e.g., radioactive decay — there are $\sim 10^{23}$ atoms, each decaying with tiny probability).
- You want additivity: independent Poisson counts sum to Poisson. Independent Binomials only sum to Binomial if they share the same $p$.
- You're modelling arrivals, events per unit time/area, counts with no natural upper bound.

> _The "no natural upper bound" criterion is underappreciated. If I ask "how many cars pass this corner in an hour," the answer can in principle be arbitrarily large — there's no fixed $n$. That's Poisson territory. If I ask "how many of these 50 patients recover," there's a hard upper bound of 50. That's Binomial territory._

## The Approximation Error: Quantified

The **total variation distance** between $\text{Bin}(n,p)$ and $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$ with $\lambda = np$ satisfies (Le Cam's theorem):

$$d_{TV}(\text{Bin}(n,p),, \text{Pois}(\lambda)) \leq 2\sum_{i=1}^n p_i^2 = 2np^2 = \frac{2\lambda^2}{n} = 2\lambda p$$

So the approximation error is bounded by $2\lambda p = 2np^2$. This vanishes when $p \to 0$ with $\lambda = np$ fixed. This is the rigorous version of "use Poisson when $p$ is small."

**Example:** $n = 100$, $p = 0.02$, $\lambda = 2$.

$$d_{TV} \leq 2 \times 100 \times (0.02)^2 = 2 \times 0.04 = 0.08$$

The total variation error is at most 8%. Acceptable for most purposes. For $p = 0.005$, $n = 400$, $\lambda = 2$: bound is $2 \times 0.005 = 0.01$ — excellent approximation.

## A Concrete Comparison

Let $\lambda = 3$. Compare $\text{Bin}(100, 0.03)$ and $\text{Pois}(3)$:

|$k$|$\text{Bin}(100, 0.03)$|$\text{Pois}(3)$|
|---|---|---|
|0|0.0476|0.0498|
|1|0.1471|0.1494|
|2|0.2252|0.2240|
|3|0.2275|0.2240|
|4|0.1706|0.1680|
|5|0.1013|0.1008|
|6|0.0496|0.0504|

The agreement is excellent. Now try $\text{Bin}(10, 0.3)$ vs $\text{Pois}(3)$:

|$k$|$\text{Bin}(10, 0.3)$|$\text{Pois}(3)$|
|---|---|---|
|0|0.0282|0.0498|
|1|0.1211|0.1494|
|2|0.2335|0.2240|
|3|0.2668|0.2240|
|4|0.2001|0.1680|

The approximation is poor — as expected, since $p = 0.3$ is not small and Le Cam's bound gives $d_{TV} \leq 2 \times 10 \times 0.09 = 1.8$ (vacuous — the bound exceeds 1).

## The Structural Summary

$$\boxed{\text{Bin}(n,p) \xrightarrow{n \to \infty,; np = \lambda} \text{Pois}(\lambda) \xrightarrow{\text{condition on total}} \text{Bin}(n, p')}$$

The Binomial and Poisson are not two separate distributions that happen to be numerically close. They are different parameterisations of the same underlying idea — counting rare successes — at different levels of abstraction:

- **Binomial:** finite, concrete, $n$ and $p$ both specified.
- **Poisson:** infinite, idealized, only the rate $\lambda$ specified.

The Poisson is what you get when you stop caring about $n$ and $p$ individually and only care about their product.
