## The Statement

For any random variables $X$ and $Y$ defined on the same probability space, and any constants $a, b, c \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$\mathbb{E}[aX + bY + c] = a,\mathbb{E}[X] + b,\mathbb{E}[Y] + c$$

Extending to any finite collection $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ and constants $a_1, \ldots, a_n$:

$$\mathbb{E}!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n a_i X_i\right] = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i,\mathbb{E}[X_i]$$

**No assumptions on the joint distribution of $X_1, \ldots, X_n$. No independence. No identical distribution. Nothing.**

Expectation is a linear functional on random variables, in exactly the same sense that integration is a linear functional on functions. This is not a coincidence — expectation _is_ integration, with respect to a probability measure.

## The Proof

### Additivity — $\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y]$

For discrete $X, Y$ with joint PMF $p_{X,Y}$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \sum_x \sum_y (x + y), p_{X,Y}(x, y)$$

$$= \sum_x \sum_y x, p_{X,Y}(x,y) + \sum_x \sum_y y, p_{X,Y}(x,y)$$

$$= \sum_x x \underbrace{\sum_y p_{X,Y}(x,y)}_{P(X=x)} + \sum_y y \underbrace{\sum_x p_{X,Y}(x,y)}_{P(Y=y)}$$

$$= \sum_x x,P(X=x) + \sum_y y,P(Y=y) = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y] \qquad \square$$

**Where independence would have been needed:** nowhere. The step $\sum_y p_{X,Y}(x,y) = P(X=x)$ is marginalization — it holds for any joint distribution. Independence would give us $p_{X,Y}(x,y) = p_X(x),p_Y(y)$, but we never used that factoring.

### Homogeneity — $\mathbb{E}[aX] = a,\mathbb{E}[X]$

$$\mathbb{E}[aX] = \sum_x ax,P(X=x) = a\sum_x x,P(X=x) = a,\mathbb{E}[X] \qquad \square$$

Constant factors pull out of the sum directly. Trivial, but worth stating.

### The Full Result

Combining: $\mathbb{E}[aX + bY + c] = a,\mathbb{E}[X] + b,\mathbb{E}[Y] + \mathbb{E}[c] = a,\mathbb{E}[X] + b,\mathbb{E}[Y] + c$.

The $n$-variable case follows by induction, or directly by the same marginalization argument applied to a joint PMF over $n$ variables.

## Why Independence Is Not Needed — The Deep Reason

It is worth understanding _why_ independence plays no role, not just observing that it doesn't.

Expectation is defined as:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_\omega X(\omega), P({\omega})$$

summing over outcomes $\omega$ in the sample space. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[X + Y] = \sum_\omega (X(\omega) + Y(\omega)), P({\omega}) = \sum_\omega X(\omega),P({\omega}) + \sum_\omega Y(\omega),P({\omega}) = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y]$$

The proof is just the linearity of finite sums (or integrals). $X(\omega)$ and $Y(\omega)$ are both just numbers for each $\omega$, and numbers distribute over addition trivially. Independence is a condition on the _joint distribution_ of $(X, Y)$ — it affects how probability is spread over the sample space. But expectation is a weighted sum, and weighted sums are always additive. The distribution structure is irrelevant to the linearity.

> **The slogan:** Linearity of expectation holds because addition distributes over addition. That's the whole proof. Independence is a condition about joint distributions; expectation's linearity lives entirely at the level of the sample space, below where joint distributions live.

## The Contrast With Variance

The analogous statement for variance is:

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2,\text{Cov}(X, Y)$$

Variance additivity — $\text{Var}(X+Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$ — holds **if and only if** $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$, which requires at least uncorrelation (and independence is sufficient but stronger than necessary).

Why does variance need this but expectation doesn't? Because variance is a nonlinear functional of the distribution:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$$

The square is nonlinear. Nonlinearity interacts with the joint distribution, and the joint distribution is where independence (or its absence) lives. Expectation is linear, so it never touches the joint structure.

||Needs independence?|What it requires|
|---|---|---|
|$\mathbb{E}[X+Y] = \mathbb{E}[X] + \mathbb{E}[Y]$|**Never**|Nothing|
|$\text{Var}(X+Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$|**Yes**|$\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$|
|$\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$|**Yes**|At minimum uncorrelated; independence sufficient|
|$\mathbb{E}[g(X)h(Y)] = \mathbb{E}[g(X)]\mathbb{E}[h(Y)]$|**Yes**|Independence|

The last two rows — multiplicative expectations — do require independence (or at least some form of it). Linearity is about sums, not products. The moment you write a product, you're outside the reach of linearity alone.

## The Indicator Method — Linearity's Killer Application

The strategy: write a complex random variable as a sum of simple ones.

**Canonical form:** If $X$ counts how many events in a collection ${A_1, \ldots, A_n}$ occur:

$$X = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$$

then by linearity:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{A_i}] = \sum_{i=1}^n P(A_i)$$

The $A_i$ can be arbitrarily dependent. All that matters is each individual $P(A_i)$.

### Gallery of Applications

**Binomial:** $X \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$. $X = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{A_i}$ where $A_i = {\text{trial } i \text{ succeeds}}$. $P(A_i) = p$ for all $i$. $\mathbb{E}[X] = np$.

**Hypergeometric:** $X \sim \text{HGeom}(m, N-m, n)$. $X = \sum_{j=1}^n \mathbf{1}_{A_j}$ where $A_j = {\text{draw } j \text{ is tagged}}$. By symmetry, $P(A_j) = m/N$. $\mathbb{E}[X] = nm/N$. The draws are dependent — doesn't matter.

**Coupon Collector:** $n$ coupon types, each draw uniform. Let $T$ = number of draws to collect all types. Write $T = T_1 + T_2 + \cdots + T_n$ where $T_k$ = additional draws needed to get the $k$-th new type after having $k-1$ distinct types. $T_k \sim \text{Geom}((n-k+1)/n)$, so $\mathbb{E}[T_k] = n/(n-k+1)$. The $T_k$ are independent (each is a fresh geometric waiting time). By linearity:

$$\mathbb{E}[T] = \sum_{k=1}^n \frac{n}{n-k+1} = n\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{1}{j} = n, H_n$$

where $H_n = \sum_{j=1}^n 1/j$ is the $n$-th harmonic number. For $n = 100$: $\mathbb{E}[T] = 100 \cdot H_{100} \approx 100 \cdot 5.187 = 518.7$ draws. Obtained by decomposing $T$ into a sum, then applying linearity. Without linearity, this computation is brutal.

**Birthday Problem (expected matches):** $n$ people, each birthday uniform on 365 days. Let $X$ = number of matching pairs. There are $\binom{n}{2}$ pairs. For each pair $(i,j)$, let $A_{ij} = {\text{persons } i \text{ and } j \text{ share a birthday}}$. $P(A_{ij}) = 1/365$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \binom{n}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{365}$$

The pairs are dependent (if $i$ matches $j$ and $j$ matches $k$, then $i$ matches $k$). Irrelevant.

**Fixed points of a random permutation:** $\sigma$ is a uniformly random permutation of ${1,\ldots,n}$. Let $X$ = number of fixed points. $X = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbf{1}[\sigma(i) = i]$. By symmetry, $P(\sigma(i) = i) = 1/n$. $\mathbb{E}[X] = 1$ for all $n$. The indicators are highly dependent. Irrelevant.

## A Failure Mode to Avoid

Linearity applies to sums. It does **not** apply to products, ratios, or other nonlinear combinations.

$$\mathbb{E}[XY] \neq \mathbb{E}[X],\mathbb{E}[Y] \quad \text{in general}$$

$$\mathbb{E}!\left[\frac{X}{Y}\right] \neq \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{\mathbb{E}[Y]} \quad \text{in general}$$

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \neq g(\mathbb{E}[X]) \quad \text{for nonlinear } g \quad \text{(Jensen)}$$

Each of these failures has a name and a counterexample. The ELK estimator bias is $\mathbb{E}[1/X] \neq 1/\mathbb{E}[X]$. The covariance term in variance is $\mathbb{E}[XY] \neq \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$ when dependent.

The theorem is precisely about **linear** combinations. It earns that word.

## Expectation as a Linear Functional — The Abstract View

For those who want to see the algebra clearly: the space of random variables with finite expectation on a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ forms a vector space. The map $X \mapsto \mathbb{E}[X]$ is a linear functional on this space:

$$\mathbb{E}[aX + bY] = a,\mathbb{E}[X] + b,\mathbb{E}[Y]$$

It is also:

- **Positive:** $X \geq 0 \implies \mathbb{E}[X] \geq 0$
- **Normalized:** $\mathbb{E}[1] = 1$

These three properties (linearity, positivity, normalization) characterize expectation completely as an abstract object. Every measure-theoretic probability course defines expectation this way — as the unique positive normalized linear functional. The PMF/PDF formula is a theorem, not a definition.

You don't need measure theory to use this. But knowing that $\mathbb{E}$ is linear _by construction_ — not as a coincidence — makes it feel natural rather than lucky.

---

_What I want to hold permanently: this theorem asks for nothing and delivers everything. No independence, no identical distribution, no conditions on the joint structure whatsoever. The proof is just that a sum of weighted averages is a weighted average of sums — i.e., that addition distributes over addition. The entire content of the theorem is that expectation is a sum (or integral), and sums are linear. Every time you decompose a complex random variable into a sum of simple ones and pass the expectation through, you're using this theorem. It is the reason the indicator method works. It is the reason the coupon collector computation is three lines instead of a page. It is the most load-bearing assumption-free fact in all of probability._
