
> _The natural generalisation of geometric â€” once you understand geometric deeply, negative binomial is almost free. The name is terrible though. It has nothing negative about it._

---

## Motivation

In [[03.Geometric Distribution]], I was waiting for the **first** success. Now ask a more general question:

> How many **failures** occur before the **$r$-th success**?

That's it. That's the negative binomial. If $r = 1$, we're back to geometric. Everything generalises cleanly.

---

## Setup

As before: independent Bernoulli$(p)$ trials, $p \in (0,1)$ fixed, each trial succeeds with probability $p$ and fails with probability $q = 1-p$.

**$X \sim \text{NBin}(r, p)$** means $X$ = number of failures before the $r$-th success, where $r \in {1, 2, 3, \ldots}$ is a fixed positive integer.

Support: $X \in {0, 1, 2, \ldots}$.

---

## PMF

For $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$:

$$\boxed{P(X = k) = \binom{k + r - 1}{r - 1} p^r (1-p)^k}$$

**Derivation (this is a counting argument, not a formula to memorise blindly):**

The event ${X = k}$ means exactly $k$ failures and $r$ successes occurred, with the **last trial being the $r$-th success**. So in the first $k + r - 1$ trials, there are exactly $r-1$ successes (and $k$ failures) arranged in any order, and then trial $k+r$ is a guaranteed success.

- Choose which $r-1$ of the first $k+r-1$ trials are successes: $\binom{k+r-1}{r-1}$ ways.
- Probability of any such sequence: $p^{r-1} \cdot q^k \cdot p = p^r q^k$.

$$P(X = k) = \binom{k+r-1}{r-1} p^r q^k$$

$\square$

> _The combinatorial argument is the proof. Stare at it until the last-trial-must-be-a-success constraint feels obvious â€” it's what pins down the count._

**Sanity check:** Does this sum to 1?

$$\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{k+r-1}{r-1} q^k p^r = p^r \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{k+r-1}{k} q^k = p^r \cdot \frac{1}{(1-q)^r} = p^r \cdot \frac{1}{p^r} = 1 \checkmark$$

This uses the **negative binomial series** (which is where the distribution gets its name):

$$\frac{1}{(1-q)^r} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{k+r-1}{k} q^k$$

This is the generalisation of the geometric series to higher powers. Proof via Taylor expansion of $(1-q)^{-r}$ â€” worth doing once.

---

## Why "Negative Binomial"?

The name comes from the generalised binomial theorem applied to negative integer exponents:

$$(1-q)^{-r} = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{-r}{k}(-q)^k = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} \binom{k+r-1}{k} q^k$$

The coefficient $\binom{-r}{k}(-1)^k = \binom{k+r-1}{k}$ â€” a "negative" binomial coefficient. It's a historical name. Don't let it confuse you.

---

## Expectation

$$E[X] = \frac{rq}{p}$$

**Proof 1 â€” Linearity (the clean way):**

If $X_1, \ldots, X_r \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Geom}(p)$, then $X = X_1 + \cdots + X_r \sim \text{NBin}(r,p)$.

_(I'll prove this decomposition rigorously below.)_

By linearity:

$$E[X] = r \cdot E[X_1] = r \cdot \frac{q}{p} = \frac{rq}{p}$$

$\square$

> _This is why I always prove the sum-of-geometrics decomposition first. It makes everything else a one-liner._

**Proof 2 â€” Direct series (ugly but honest):**

$$E[X] = \sum_{k=0}^{\infty} k \binom{k+r-1}{r-1} p^r q^k$$

Use the identity $k \binom{k+r-1}{r-1} = r \binom{k+r-1}{r}$ (easy to verify by expanding factorials), then reindex:

$$E[X] = r \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \binom{k+r-1}{r} p^r q^k = \frac{rq}{p} \sum_{j=0}^{\infty} \binom{j+r}{r} p^{r+1} q^j = \frac{rq}{p} \cdot 1 = \frac{rq}{p}$$

$\square$

---

## Variance

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{rq}{p^2}$$

**Proof:** Again, use the decomposition $X = X_1 + \cdots + X_r$ with $X_i \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Geom}(p)$. By independence:

$$\text{Var}(X) = r \cdot \text{Var}(X_1) = r \cdot \frac{q}{p^2} = \frac{rq}{p^2}$$

$\square$

---

## MGF

$$M_X(t) = \left(\frac{p}{1 - qe^t}\right)^r, \quad t < -\ln q$$

**Derivation:** Use the geometric MGF $M_{X_i}(t) = \frac{p}{1-qe^t}$ and independence:

$$M_X(t) = \prod_{i=1}^{r} M_{X_i}(t) = \left(\frac{p}{1-qe^t}\right)^r$$

$\square$

This is the slickest proof of the MGF â€” no direct summation needed once you have the geometric MGF and the decomposition.

---

## The Key Decomposition Theorem

This is the theorem that makes the whole thing click.

**Theorem.** Let $X_1, \ldots, X_r \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Geom}(p)$. Then:

$$X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_r \sim \text{NBin}(r, p)$$

**Proof (via story):** $X_i$ = number of failures before the $i$-th success, measured _relative to the $(i-1)$-th success_. By independence and memorylessness of each geometric, these are i.i.d. The total $X_1 + \cdots + X_r$ counts total failures before the $r$-th success overall. That's exactly $\text{NBin}(r,p)$. $\square$

**Proof (via MGFs):** The MGF of $\sum X_i$ is $\prod M_{X_i}(t) = \left(\frac{p}{1-qe^t}\right)^r$, which is the NBin$(r,p)$ MGF. By uniqueness of MGFs, $\sum X_i \sim \text{NBin}(r,p)$. $\square$

> _The story proof is more illuminating. The MGF proof is more rigorous. I like having both â€” use the story to understand, use MGFs to prove things to a sceptic._

---

## Memoryless Property (Inherited)

The negative binomial is **not** memoryless in the same strong sense as the geometric. But each waiting period _between_ successive successes is i.i.d. Geom$(p)$ and hence memoryless. The memorylessness lives in the building blocks, not the sum.

## Special Cases

| Parameters               | Distribution                        |
| ------------------------ | ----------------------------------- |
| $r = 1$                  | $\text{Geom}(p)$                    |
| $r$ arbitrary, $p \to 1$ | Concentrates at 0                   |
| $r$ arbitrary, $p \to 0$ | Spreads out; mean $rq/p \to \infty$ |

## Connection to Binomial (a nice duality)

There's a beautiful complementary relationship between the binomial and negative binomial:

$$P(\text{NBin}(r,p) \geq k) = P(\text{Bin}(k+r-1, p) \leq r-1)$$

In words: "at least $k$ failures before $r$-th success" $\iff$ "at most $r-1$ successes in $k+r-1$ trials." Both events describe the same underlying sequence of coin flips, just phrased differently. Prove it by thinking carefully about what the sample space looks like â€” it's a pure counting/event argument, no algebra needed.

> _This duality is the kind of thing that shows up in actuarial problems and Bayesian inference. Worth internalising._

---

## Negative Binomial as a Poisson Mixture (Advanced)

There's a completely different story for the negative binomial â€” it arises as a **Poisson-Gamma mixture**:

If $\lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(r, p/(1-p))$ and $X \mid \lambda \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, then marginally $X \sim \text{NBin}(r, p)$.

This is the entry point into Bayesian modelling and overdispersed count data. The Poisson has $\text{Var} = \text{Mean}$; the negative binomial has $\text{Var} = \text{Mean} + \text{Mean}^2/r > \text{Mean}$, so it handles overdispersion that Poisson can't.

I haven't proved this properly yet â€” it requires integrating out $\lambda$ using the Gamma integral. Put this on the list.
