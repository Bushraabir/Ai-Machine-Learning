## The Problem LOTUS Solves

You know the distribution of $X$. You want $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ for some function $g$.

The naive approach: find the distribution of $Y = g(X)$, then compute $\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_y y,P(Y=y)$.

This works but is often painful. Finding the distribution of $g(X)$ requires inverting $g$, grouping preimages, and recomputing an entire PMF or PDF. For something like $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ or $\mathbb{E}[e^X]$, this is unnecessary suffering.

**LOTUS says: don't bother finding the distribution of $g(X)$. Just use the distribution of $X$ directly.**

---

## The Theorem

**Theorem (LOTUS — Discrete).** Let $X$ be a discrete random variable with PMF $p_X$, and $g : \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ any function. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x), p_X(x)$$

**Theorem (LOTUS — Continuous).** Let $X$ be a continuous random variable with PDF $f_X$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x), f_X(x), dx$$

In both cases: **sum or integrate $g(x)$ against the distribution of $X$, not the distribution of $g(X)$.**

---

## The Proof — Discrete Case

Let $Y = g(X)$. By definition:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_y y, P(Y = y)$$

Now expand $P(Y = y) = P(g(X) = y) = \sum_{x:, g(x) = y} P(X = x)$:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_y y \sum_{x:, g(x) = y} p_X(x)$$

Since $y = g(x)$ for all $x$ in the inner sum, substitute:

$$= \sum_y \sum_{x:, g(x) = y} g(x), p_X(x)$$

The double sum $\sum_y \sum_{x:,g(x)=y}$ ranges over all $x$ exactly once — every $x$ belongs to exactly one group ${x : g(x) = y}$ for $y = g(x)$. So:

$$= \sum_x g(x), p_X(x) \qquad \square$$

**The key step:** regrouping. The sum over $y$ and then over preimages of $y$ is just a reindexing of the sum over all $x$. Every $x$ appears exactly once. The distribution of $Y$ was an intermediate scaffolding that collapses away.

> The proof works for any $g$ — injective, many-to-one, non-monotone, discontinuous. The preimage grouping handles everything. $g$ doesn't need any regularity at all.

---

## The Continuous Case — Why It's Not Obvious

For continuous $X$, the proof requires more care because $P(X = x) = 0$ for every $x$ — you can't just sum over preimages.

The rigorous proof uses measure theory: expectation is the Lebesgue integral $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int g,dP_X$, where $P_X$ is the pushforward measure. The LOTUS formula is the change-of-variables theorem for Lebesgue integrals.

Without measure theory, the clean argument: let $Y = g(X)$ with PDF $f_Y$. Then by definition $\mathbb{E}[Y] = \int y f_Y(y), dy$. Using the change-of-variables formula for PDFs (valid when $g$ is smooth and invertible on pieces):

$$\int y, f_Y(y), dy = \int g(x), f_X(x), dx$$

This requires justification for general $g$, but the formula is what it is. For the purposes of Stat 110 and almost all applications: accept LOTUS for continuous RVs and know the discrete proof cold.

## LOTUS for Two Variables

For jointly distributed $(X, Y)$:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X, Y)] = \sum_x \sum_y g(x, y), p_{X,Y}(x, y) \quad \text{(discrete)}$$

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X, Y)] = \int\int g(x,y), f_{X,Y}(x,y), dx, dy \quad \text{(continuous)}$$

Again: use the joint distribution of $(X, Y)$, not the distribution of $g(X,Y)$. The same grouping argument works.

## Why It's Called "Unconscious"

The name is a joke at the statistician's expense. An "unconscious" statistician — one not paying attention — might just write:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x), p_X(x)$$

without thinking about whether this is valid, without deriving the distribution of $g(X)$ first. The theorem says: that instinct is correct. The unconscious statistician was right all along.

More precisely: the instinct to substitute $g(x)$ into the expectation formula and weight by $p_X(x)$ is the correct procedure, even though it looks like you're computing the expectation of $g(X)$ as if it were the expectation of $X$ but with $g(x)$ in place of $x$. That substitution is exactly what LOTUS licenses.

## The Applications — Where LOTUS Actually Lives

LOTUS is most useful when $g$ is nonlinear and the distribution of $g(X)$ is messy or unknown. Here are the canonical uses:

### Computing $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ — The Gateway to Variance

For any $X$ with known PMF or PDF:

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \sum_x x^2, p_X(x)$$

This is LOTUS with $g(x) = x^2$. The distribution of $X^2$ is never needed.

Then variance falls out:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$$

**Without LOTUS, variance would require finding the distribution of $(X - \mu)^2$, which is miserable.** With LOTUS, it's a sum/integral you already know how to set up.

### $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = 1^2 \cdot p + 0^2 \cdot (1-p) = p$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = p - p^2 = p(1-p)$$

### $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty k^2 \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$$

Write $k^2 = k(k-1) + k$, so $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] + \mathbb{E}[X]$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] = \sum_{k=2}^\infty k(k-1)\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^2 \sum_{k=2}^\infty \frac{\lambda^{k-2}}{(k-2)!} = \lambda^2$$

So $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \lambda^2 + \lambda$, and:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \lambda^2 + \lambda - \lambda^2 = \lambda$$

The Poisson has equal mean and variance. Both equal $\lambda$. This is a characterizing property of the Poisson distribution.

### $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, PDF $f(x) = 1$ on $[0,1]$

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \int_0^1 x^2 \cdot 1, dx = \frac{1}{3}$$

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{1}{2}, \quad \text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{12}$$

### Moment Generating Functions — LOTUS as Infrastructure

The **moment generating function** of $X$:

$$M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = \sum_x e^{tx}, p_X(x) \quad \text{(discrete)}$$

This is LOTUS with $g(x) = e^{tx}$. The entire theory of MGFs rests on LOTUS. Without it, $\mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$ would require the distribution of $e^{tX}$, which is both unnatural and painful.

## LOTUS vs Linearity — What Each Covers

|Situation|Tool|Requires|
|---|---|---|
|$\mathbb{E}[aX + b]$|Linearity|Nothing|
|$\mathbb{E}[X + Y]$|Linearity|Nothing|
|$\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ for nonlinear $g$|LOTUS|Distribution of $X$|
|$\mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)]$|LOTUS (joint)|Joint distribution of $(X,Y)$|
|$\mathbb{E}[g(X)h(Y)]$ with $X \perp Y$|LOTUS + independence|Independence|

Linearity handles sums. LOTUS handles everything else. They are complementary tools, not competing ones.

Note: $\mathbb{E}[g(X)h(Y)] = \mathbb{E}[g(X)],\mathbb{E}[h(Y)]$ when $X \perp Y$ — this is LOTUS applied to the joint distribution, then factoring using independence. Independence is what allows the joint LOTUS integral to split into a product of single integrals.

---

## The One Thing LOTUS Cannot Fix

LOTUS computes $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ easily. But:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \neq g(\mathbb{E}[X]) \quad \text{for nonlinear } g$$

LOTUS tells you how to compute the left side. Jensen's inequality tells you the relationship between the two sides. LOTUS and Jensen are complementary: LOTUS is a computational tool, Jensen is a comparison theorem.

The unconscious statistician who writes $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = g(\mathbb{E}[X])$ is wrong — that's not LOTUS, that's Jensen's inequality forgotten. LOTUS says use $p_X$, not that you can pull $\mathbb{E}$ through $g$.



---

_The thing to carry: LOTUS is a license. It says you are allowed to write $\sum_x g(x) p_X(x)$ and call it $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ without going through the distribution of $g(X)$. The proof is just reindexing a sum. The unconscious statistician who did this without thinking was accidentally right. Now you know why — and you can do it on purpose._
