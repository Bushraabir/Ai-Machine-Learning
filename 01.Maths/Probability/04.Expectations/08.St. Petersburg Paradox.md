> _The first time I saw this, I thought the resolution was obvious. Then I read more carefully and realised it isn't. This problem has bothered mathematicians, economists, and philosophers for three hundred years. It should bother you too._

---

## The Game

A casino offers you the following game:

> Flip a fair coin repeatedly until you get Heads. If Heads first appears on flip $k$, you win $2^k$ dollars.

**How much would you pay to play?**

Formally: let $T$ = the flip on which Heads first appears. Then $T \sim \text{Geom}(1/2)$ (type 2, number of flips including the success). The payout is $X = 2^T$.

---

## The Calculation

$$E[X] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} 2^k \cdot P(T = k) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} 2^k \cdot \frac{1}{2^k} = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} 1 = \infty$$

The expected payout is **infinite**.

So a rational expected-value maximiser should pay _any finite price_ to play this game. Yet essentially no one would pay more than $20–$30. This gap between the mathematical answer and human intuition is the paradox.

> _The series $\sum_{k=1}^\infty 1$ is the starkest possible divergence — every term contributes exactly 1 to the expectation, and there are infinitely many terms. The game gives you a tiny probability of an astronomical payout at every level, and these balance out perfectly._

---

## Why It's Actually a Paradox

Let me be precise about what makes this a genuine paradox and not just a curiosity.

A **paradox** here means: a mathematically valid argument leads to a conclusion that is strongly inconsistent with rational behaviour. There are two horns:

1. **Accept the math:** Then you should pay any finite amount to play. But no one would pay $1,000 to play. Are humans irrational?
2. **Trust the intuition:** Then something must be wrong with using expected value as the decision criterion. But expected value is the foundation of decision theory. What replaces it?

Neither horn is comfortable. That's the paradox.

---

## Proposed Resolutions

### Resolution 1 — Utility Theory (Bernoulli, 1738)

Daniel Bernoulli's original paper proposed that people don't maximise **expected monetary value** — they maximise **expected utility**, where utility is a concave function of wealth.

The canonical choice: $u(w) = \ln(w)$.

Starting from wealth $w_0$, the expected utility of playing is:

$$E[u(w_0 + X)] = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2^k} \ln(w_0 + 2^k)$$

**Does this converge?**

$$\sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{\ln(w_0 + 2^k)}{2^k} \leq \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{\ln(2^{k+1})}{2^k} = \ln 2 \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \frac{k+1}{2^k} < \infty$$

Yes — the series converges. With log utility, the game has a finite certainty equivalent, explaining why people won't pay arbitrary amounts.

For $w_0 = 0$, the certainty equivalent $c$ satisfies $\ln c = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{\ln 2^k}{2^k} = \ln 2 \sum_{k=1}^\infty \frac{k}{2^k} = 2\ln 2$, giving $c = 4$ dollars. So log-utility agents value the game at just $4.

> _Bernoulli's insight was genuinely revolutionary — the idea that rational agents maximise expected utility rather than expected value is the foundation of modern decision theory and all of microeconomics. But it doesn't fully resolve the paradox, as we'll see._

**Problem with this resolution:** For _any_ unbounded utility function $u$, you can construct a modified St. Petersburg game that produces infinite expected utility. Just replace the payout $2^k$ with $u^{-1}(2^k)$. The paradox regenerates itself. Utility theory kicks the can down the road.

---

### Resolution 2 — No Casino Can Pay

Real casinos have finite wealth $W$. If the casino can only pay up to $W$, then the game terminates at round $N = \lfloor \log_2 W \rfloor$, paying $\min(2^k, W)$.

The expected value becomes:

$$E[X_W] = \sum_{k=1}^{N} 2^k \cdot \frac{1}{2^k} + W \cdot \sum_{k=N+1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2^k} = N + W \cdot \frac{1}{2^N} = N + 1$$

So if the casino has $W = 2^{40}$ dollars (more than global GDP), the expected payout is only about $41. **The expected value is logarithmic in the casino's wealth.**

This is compelling as a practical resolution — infinity is not physically realisable. But it feels philosophically unsatisfying: the paradox is a statement about the mathematics of expectation, and we shouldn't need physics to resolve a math problem.

---

### Resolution 3 — Probability Weighting

The event "winning $2^{40}$ dollars" has probability $1/2^{40} \approx 10^{-12}$. Humans systematically **underweight** tiny probabilities (or treat them as effectively zero). Once you cap the probabilities you take seriously at some threshold $\varepsilon$, the sum truncates and the expectation is finite.

This is descriptively accurate — it matches how people actually behave — but it's not a normative resolution. It says humans are boundedly rational, not that expected value is wrong.

---

### Resolution 4 — Samuelson: Accept the Divergence

Paul Samuelson argued that infinite expected value series that diverge _too slowly_ simply should not be used as decision criteria. The St. Petersburg series diverges as slowly as possible ($\sum 1$). Samuelson's position: expected value is a valid criterion only when the expectation converges. When it doesn't, the criterion breaks down and we need a different framework.

This is mathematically honest but philosophically austere — it restricts the domain of expected utility theory rather than fixing it.

---

### Resolution 5 — Ergodicity Economics (Peters, 2011)

Ole Peters argues the real issue is conflating **ensemble average** with **time average**. Expected value $E[X]$ averages over many parallel universes (ensemble). But a single player playing repeatedly cares about the **geometric mean** of returns, which captures what actually happens to their wealth over time.

For multiplicative processes, the time-average growth rate is:

$$g = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n} \ln \frac{W_n}{W_0}$$

This quantity is finite and can be negative even when $E[X] = \infty$. Peters claims the St. Petersburg paradox disappears once you use the correct (time-average) criterion.

> _This is the most modern take and it's genuinely interesting, but it's also contested. The ergodicity framework handles multiplicative gambles well but doesn't obviously apply here since you're paying a fixed entry fee, not staking your entire wealth. I need to think about this more carefully._

## The Mathematical Lesson

Strip away the economics. The St. Petersburg paradox is really teaching us something about **when $E[X]$ is a useful summary statistic**.

If $X \geq 0$ and $E[X] = \infty$, then by the **law of large numbers**:

$$\frac{X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n}{n} \xrightarrow{P} \infty$$

The sample mean diverges. But this says nothing about a _single play_. With overwhelming probability, a single play of the St. Petersburg game pays very little. Let's make this precise.

**Claim:** $P(X \geq n) = P(2^T \geq n) = P(T \geq \log_2 n) = \frac{1}{2^{\lfloor \log_2 n \rfloor}} \approx \frac{1}{n}$.

So $X$ has a **heavy tail**: $P(X \geq x) \sim 1/x$. This puts it on the boundary of having a finite expectation — and indeed the expectation is infinite. The high expectation is driven entirely by rare, extreme events.

**Median of $X$:** $P(X \leq 2) = P(T = 1) = 1/2$. So the **median payout is $2**. Half the time you win $2 or less. The expectation is infinite; the median is $2. This is the most visceral way to see the paradox.

> _Expected value is not a robust measure of central tendency for heavy-tailed distributions. The St. Petersburg distribution has so much mass in the tail that the mean is pulled to infinity while the typical outcome (median) is just $2. This is an extreme version of the mean/median divergence that shows up throughout statistics._

---

## Connection to $E[X] < \infty$ iff $\sum P(X \geq n) < \infty$

There's a beautiful theorem that ties this together:

**Theorem.** For a non-negative integer-valued random variable $X$:

$$E[X] = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(X \geq n)$$

**Proof:**

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} P(X \geq n) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \sum_{k=n}^{\infty} P(X=k) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} \sum_{n=1}^{k} P(X=k) = \sum_{k=1}^{\infty} k \cdot P(X=k) = E[X]$$

(switching the order of summation — valid since all terms are non-negative by Tonelli's theorem). $\square$

For St. Petersburg: $P(X \geq n) \approx 1/n$, so $\sum P(X \geq n) \approx \sum 1/n = \infty$. The harmonic series strikes again.

This is a genuinely useful formula — often easier to compute $\sum P(X \geq n)$ than $\sum k P(X = k)$ directly.

---

## A Rigorous Version of the Paradox

To summarise the tension precisely:

**Theorem (St. Petersburg).** Let $T \sim \text{Geom}(1/2)$ (type 2) and $X = 2^T$. Then:

1. $E[X] = \infty$
2. $P(X \leq 2) = 1/2$ — median payout is $2
3. For any $\varepsilon > 0$: $P(X \geq n) \geq \varepsilon$ for infinitely many $n$ — the tail is genuinely heavy
4. For any fixed finite entry fee $c$: $P(X < c) > 0$ — there is always a positive probability of losing money

The paradox: (1) says pay anything; (2), (3), (4) say be very cautious. Standard expected value theory gives the "wrong" answer.

---

## What I Actually Think

The resolution that feels most mathematically honest to me is a combination of **Resolutions 2 and 4**: in any physical realisation of the game, the expectation is finite (logarithmic in the casino's resources), and in the idealised mathematical version, the expected value criterion simply breaks down for distributions with no finite mean. The infinite expectation is a feature of the mathematics, not the real world.

The utility theory resolution is historically important and practically useful, but philosophically unsatisfying because the paradox regenerates for any unbounded utility function.

The ergodicity point is the deepest and I don't fully understand it yet.

> _The real lesson might be this: expected value is a tool, and like all tools, it has a domain of applicability. When $E[X] = \infty$, you've left that domain. Don't be surprised when the tool gives nonsense. Find a better tool._
