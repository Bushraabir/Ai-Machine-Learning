> _"The Gamma is the distribution you get when you stop asking 'did it happen?' and start asking 'how long until it happens $n$ times?' Everything flows from that story."_

---

## Why this note exists

The Gamma distribution is the backbone of a surprising amount of probability. The Exponential is a special case. The Chi-squared is a special case. The Beta _comes from_ two Gammas (see [[Beta Transformations]]). The Normal's square is a Gamma. Almost every distribution I care about either is a Gamma or is built from one.

The transformations here are what let me move between all of them. Know these cold.

---

## Definition and PDF

$X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$ has PDF

$$f(x) = \frac{\lambda^a}{\Gamma(a)} x^{a-1} e^{-\lambda x}, \quad x > 0$$

where $a > 0$ is the **shape** and $\lambda > 0$ is the **rate**.

The normalizing constant uses the **Gamma function**:

$$\Gamma(a) = \int_0^\infty t^{a-1} e^{-t},dt$$

**Key facts I must know:**

- $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ for positive integers $n$
- $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$
- $\Gamma(a+1) = a,\Gamma(a)$ — the functional equation, proved by integration by parts

**Special cases:**

- $\text{Gamma}(1, \lambda) = \text{Expo}(\lambda)$
- $\text{Gamma}(n/2, 1/2) = \chi^2_n$ (chi-squared with $n$ degrees of freedom)

---

## The Gamma Function Identity: $\Gamma(a+1) = a,\Gamma(a)$

**Proof.** Integrate by parts with $u = t^a$, $dv = e^{-t}dt$:

$$\Gamma(a+1) = \int_0^\infty t^a e^{-t},dt = \Big[-t^a e^{-t}\Big]_0^\infty + \int_0^\infty a t^{a-1} e^{-t},dt = 0 + a,\Gamma(a) \qquad \blacksquare$$

The boundary term vanishes since $e^{-t}$ dominates any polynomial.

## Transformation 1: Scaling

> **Proposition.** If $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$, then $cX \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda/c)$ for $c > 0$.

**Proof.** Let $Y = cX$, so $x = y/c$, $dx = dy/c$:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(y/c) \cdot \frac{1}{c} = \frac{\lambda^a}{\Gamma(a)} \left(\frac{y}{c}\right)^{a-1} e^{-\lambda y/c} \cdot \frac{1}{c} = \frac{(\lambda/c)^a}{\Gamma(a)} y^{a-1} e^{-(\lambda/c)y} \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Consequence:** If $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$, then $\lambda X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, 1)$.

> _So WLOG I can always work with rate 1 and scale at the end. This is why all my Gamma computations use $\text{Gamma}(a,1)$._

## Transformation 2: Summation (Additive Reproduction)

> **Theorem.** If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent with $X_i \sim \text{Gamma}(a_i, \lambda)$, then
> 
> $$X_1 + \cdots + X_n \sim \text{Gamma}(a_1 + \cdots + a_n,; \lambda)$$

**Proof via MGF.** The MGF of $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$ for $t < \lambda$:

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = \int_0^\infty e^{tx} \frac{\lambda^a}{\Gamma(a)} x^{a-1} e^{-\lambda x},dx = \frac{\lambda^a}{\Gamma(a)} \int_0^\infty x^{a-1} e^{-(\lambda - t)x},dx$$

Substitute $u = (\lambda - t)x$:

$$= \frac{\lambda^a}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a)}{(\lambda-t)^a} = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right)^a$$

For the sum $S = \sum X_i$, by independence:

$$M_S(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{X_i}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda-t}\right)^{a_i} = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda-t}\right)^{\sum a_i}$$

This is the MGF of $\text{Gamma}(\sum a_i, \lambda)$. By uniqueness of MGFs, $S \sim \text{Gamma}(\sum a_i, \lambda)$. $\blacksquare$

**Special case:** $n$ independent $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$ random variables sum to $\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$.

> _This is the "waiting for the $n$-th event" story. If each inter-arrival is $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$, the time until the $n$-th arrival is $\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$. Poisson process waiting times._

## Transformation 3: The Gamma–Beta Relationship

This is the deepest transformation. Full proof is in [[Beta Transformations]], but I'll record the statement and the key idea here.

> **Theorem.** Let $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, 1)$ and $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b, 1)$ be independent. Then:
> 
> $$W = \frac{X}{X+Y} \sim \text{Beta}(a, b), \qquad T = X+Y \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b, 1)$$
> 
> and $W \perp T$.

**Key step:** The Jacobian of $(x,y) \mapsto (w,t)$ where $x = wt$, $y=(1-w)t$ is $|J| = t$, and the joint PDF factors as $f_W(w) \cdot f_T(t)$.

This is the construction that:

1. Proves the Beta function identity $B(a,b) = \Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)/\Gamma(a+b)$
2. Gives a way to _simulate_ Beta random variables from Gammas
3. Explains why the Beta lives on $(0,1)$ — it's a ratio normalized by the total

## Transformation 4: Gamma and the Chi-Squared

> **Definition.** $\chi^2_n = \text{Gamma}(n/2,, 1/2)$.

> **Theorem.** If $Z \sim N(0,1)$, then $Z^2 \sim \chi^2_1 = \text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2)$.

**Proof.** Let $Y = Z^2$. For $y > 0$:

$$F_Y(y) = P(Z^2 \leq y) = P(-\sqrt{y} \leq Z \leq \sqrt{y}) = 2\Phi(\sqrt{y}) - 1$$

Differentiate:

$$f_Y(y) = 2\phi(\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{y}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-y/2} = \frac{(1/2)^{1/2}}{\Gamma(1/2)} y^{1/2 - 1} e^{-y/2}$$

using $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$. This is $\text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2) = \chi^2_1$. $\blacksquare$

> **Corollary.** If $Z_1, \ldots, Z_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(0,1)$, then $\sum Z_i^2 \sim \chi^2_n$ by the additive property.

## Transformation 5: The Poisson Connection

> **Theorem.** Let $T_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$ be the waiting time for the $n$-th Poisson event. Then
> 
> $$P(T_n > t) = P(N(t) \leq n-1)$$
> 
> where $N(t) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda t)$.

**Proof.** The $n$-th event occurs after time $t$ if and only if fewer than $n$ events have occurred by time $t$:

$$P(T_n > t) = P(N(t) < n) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{e^{-\lambda t}(\lambda t)^k}{k!} \qquad \blacksquare$$

This is the **Gamma–Poisson relationship**. It lets me convert between a CDF I can't compute in closed form (Gamma CDF) and one I can (Poisson PMF, summed).

> _In practice: if I need $P(\text{Gamma}(n,\lambda) > t)$ and $n$ is an integer, I compute a Poisson tail sum instead. Huge computational shortcut._

## Moments

For $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$:

$$E[X] = \frac{a}{\lambda}, \qquad \text{Var}(X) = \frac{a}{\lambda^2}$$

More generally, the $r$-th moment:

$$E[X^r] = \frac{\Gamma(a+r)}{\Gamma(a),\lambda^r}$$

**Proof of mean.** Raise and lower the shape parameter by 1:

$$E[X] = \int_0^\infty x \cdot \frac{\lambda^a}{\Gamma(a)} x^{a-1} e^{-\lambda x},dx = \frac{\lambda^a}{\Gamma(a)} \int_0^\infty x^a e^{-\lambda x},dx = \frac{\lambda^a}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a+1)}{\lambda^{a+1}} = \frac{a}{\lambda} \qquad \blacksquare$$

## Simulation Takeaways

- To simulate $\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$ for integer $n$: sum $n$ independent $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$ samples
- To simulate $\text{Beta}(a,b)$: simulate $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a,1)$, $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b,1)$, return $X/(X+Y)$
- These aren't tricks — they're the distributional identities made algorithmic

## Summary of Key Transformations

|Transformation|Input|Output|
|---|---|---|
|Scaling|$X \sim \text{Gamma}(a,\lambda)$|$cX \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda/c)$|
|Standardizing|$X \sim \text{Gamma}(a,\lambda)$|$\lambda X \sim \text{Gamma}(a,1)$|
|Summation|$X_i \sim \text{Gamma}(a_i, \lambda)$ indep.|$\sum X_i \sim \text{Gamma}(\sum a_i, \lambda)$|
|Expo special case|$X_i \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$ indep.|$\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \sim \text{Gamma}(n,\lambda)$|
|Gamma → Beta|$X \sim \Gamma(a,1), Y \sim \Gamma(b,1)$ indep.|$X/(X+Y) \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$|
|Normal → Chi-sq|$Z \sim N(0,1)$|$Z^2 \sim \chi^2_1 = \text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2)$|
|Gamma–Poisson|$T_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n,\lambda)$|$P(T_n > t) = P(\text{Pois}(\lambda t) \leq n-1)$|
