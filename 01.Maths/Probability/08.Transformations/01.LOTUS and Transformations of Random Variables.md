> _Two things that look like the same problem but are completely different operations. One is a theorem you should memorize and feel. The other is a procedure you reach for only when you actually need the full distribution — and that's rarer than you think._

---

## The Setup — Two Very Different Questions

Let $X$ be a random variable and $g: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ a measurable function. You might want either:

1. **Just the expectation** $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ — without ever finding the distribution of $g(X)$
2. **The full distribution of $Y = g(X)$** — the PMF, PDF, or CDF of the new random variable

These are _not_ the same task. And the first is answered by a theorem so clean it should feel like cheating.

---

## LOTUS — The Law of the Unconscious Statistician

### Statement

**Discrete case.** If $X$ is a discrete random variable with PMF $p_X$, then for any function $g$:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x), p_X(x)$$

**Continuous case.** If $X$ is continuous with PDF $f_X$, then:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} g(x), f_X(x), dx$$

### The Punchline

You integrate (or sum) $g(x)$ against **the distribution of $X$** — not against the distribution of $g(X)$. The distribution of $Y = g(X)$ never appears.

> _This is the theorem that lives in the name. The "unconscious statistician" computes $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ without thinking — they just plug $g$ into the integral over $X$'s distribution, as if by reflex. The name is a gentle joke: many people use this formula without realizing it even needs a proof._

### Why It Needs a Proof (It's Not Obvious)

The definition of expectation is: $$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_y y, p_Y(y) \quad \text{or} \quad \int y, f_Y(y), dy$$

LOTUS claims this equals $\sum_x g(x), p_X(x)$. These are _different sums over different spaces_. Why should they agree? The left side sums over values of $Y$; the right side sums over values of $X$. That they're equal is the content of the theorem.

---

## Proof of LOTUS (Discrete Case)

This is the version worth knowing cold. The continuous case follows from measure theory — the discrete case is where you see the idea.

**Setup.** $X$ takes values in some countable set $\mathcal{X}$. Let $Y = g(X)$, taking values in $\mathcal{Y} = g(\mathcal{X})$.

**The PMF of $Y$.** For each $y \in \mathcal{Y}$: $$p_Y(y) = P(g(X) = y) = \sum_{\substack{x \in \mathcal{X} \ g(x) = y}} p_X(x)$$

**Start from the definition of $\mathbb{E}[Y]$:** $$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} y, p_Y(y)$$

**Substitute the expression for $p_Y(y)$:** $$= \sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} y \sum_{\substack{x \in \mathcal{X} \ g(x) = y}} p_X(x)$$

**Since $g(x) = y$ on the inner sum, replace $y$ with $g(x)$:** $$= \sum_{y \in \mathcal{Y}} \sum_{\substack{x \in \mathcal{X} \ g(x) = y}} g(x), p_X(x)$$

**The double sum partitions $\mathcal{X}$ — every $x$ appears in exactly one inner sum (the one where $y = g(x)$). So we can collapse:** $$= \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} g(x), p_X(x) \qquad \square$$

> _The move is: the partition ${g^{-1}(y) : y \in \mathcal{Y}}$ is a partition of $\mathcal{X}$, so summing over $y$ first and then over the fiber $g^{-1}(y)$ is the same as summing directly over $\mathcal{X}$. That's it. That's the whole proof._

The double sum collapsing to a single sum over $\mathcal{X}$ is the "aha" — you're just reindexing, and LOTUS falls out.

## Why You Almost Never Need the Distribution of $g(X)$

The most common mistake early on: computing $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ by first finding the PMF of $X^2$, then summing.

**The wrong way (for $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$, find $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$):**

$X^2$ takes values $0$ and $1$. $P(X^2 = 0) = 1-p$, $P(X^2 = 1) = p$. So $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = 0\cdot(1-p) + 1 \cdot p = p$.

That works, but it was unnecessary work. By LOTUS: $$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \sum_x x^2, p_X(x) = 0^2(1-p) + 1^2 \cdot p = p$$

One line. And for harder problems, the distribution of $g(X)$ might be a pain to derive — LOTUS lets you sidestep it entirely.

**A case where the distribution really would be annoying.** Let $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$. Find $\mathbb{E}[e^X]$.

Without LOTUS: find the density of $Y = e^X$, then integrate $y, f_Y(y)$.

- $Y$ takes values in $(1, e)$, CDF of $Y$ is $P(e^X \leq y) = P(X \leq \ln y) = \ln y$ for $y \in (1,e)$
- $f_Y(y) = 1/y$ on $(1,e)$
- $\mathbb{E}[Y] = \int_1^e y \cdot \frac{1}{y}, dy = \int_1^e 1, dy = e - 1$

With LOTUS: $$\mathbb{E}[e^X] = \int_0^1 e^x \cdot 1, dx = e^x\Big|_0^1 = e - 1$$

One line.

> _LOTUS is the answer to: "why would I ever bother finding the distribution of $g(X)$?" And the answer is: only when you need more than just the expectation — when you need quantiles, probabilities, or the full distributional shape._

## When You DO Need the Distribution of $g(X)$

The full distribution of $Y = g(X)$ is necessary when:

- You need $P(g(X) \leq t)$ for some specific $t$ — a probability, not just an expectation
- You need quantiles of $Y$
- You need the variance or higher moments _and_ the distribution simplifies the computation (though often LOTUS handles $\mathbb{E}[g(X)^2]$ too)
- You need to identify what named distribution $g(X)$ follows — sometimes the distribution of a transformation is recognizable and useful

---

## The Three Methods for Finding the Distribution of $Y = g(X)$

### Method 1 — CDF Method (Most General)

Find the CDF of $Y$ directly: $$F_Y(y) = P(Y \leq y) = P(g(X) \leq y)$$ then differentiate to get $f_Y$ if $Y$ is continuous.

This always works. The challenge is solving ${x : g(x) \leq y}$ — which can be a union of intervals if $g$ is not monotone.

**Example.** $X \sim N(0,1)$, find the distribution of $Y = X^2$.

$P(Y \leq y) = P(X^2 \leq y) = P(-\sqrt{y} \leq X \leq \sqrt{y}) = \Phi(\sqrt{y}) - \Phi(-\sqrt{y}) = 2\Phi(\sqrt{y}) - 1$ for $y > 0$.

Differentiate: $f_Y(y) = \frac{d}{dy}[2\Phi(\sqrt{y}) - 1] = 2\phi(\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi y}}, e^{-y/2}$

This is $\chi^2_1$ — the chi-squared distribution with 1 degree of freedom. You'd never get that from LOTUS.

### Method 2 — Change of Variables / Jacobian (For Monotone, Smooth $g$)

If $g$ is strictly monotone and differentiable, and $X$ has PDF $f_X$:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \left|\frac{d}{dy} g^{-1}(y)\right|$$

The $|\text{Jacobian}|$ factor corrects for the stretching or compressing of probability mass under the map $g$.

**Intuition.** Probability is preserved: $f_X(x)|dx| = f_Y(y)|dy|$. Solving for $f_Y(y)$: $$f_Y(y) = f_X(x) \cdot \left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right| = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot |(g^{-1})'(y)|$$

**When $g$ is not monotone** — say $g(x) = x^2$ — you need to sum over all branches: $$f_Y(y) = \sum_{x_i : g(x_i) = y} f_X(x_i) \cdot \frac{1}{|g'(x_i)|}$$

This is the multi-branch Jacobian formula. For $Y = X^2$ with $X$ symmetric, both $x = \sqrt{y}$ and $x = -\sqrt{y}$ contribute.

### Method 3 — MGF/Characteristic Function (When You Want to Identify the Distribution)

If $g$ has nice enough structure, compute the MGF $M_Y(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tY}] = \mathbb{E}[e^{t, g(X)}]$ — which is itself just LOTUS — and recognize the result.

This is especially powerful for sums: $Y = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n$ with independent $X_i$, because $M_Y(t) = \prod M_{X_i}(t)$.

## The Comparison Table

| Task                               | Tool                                                 | When                                                         |
| ---------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$                 | **LOTUS**                                            | Always — never find the distribution of $g(X)$ just for this |
| $P(g(X) \leq t)$                   | **CDF method**                                       | Always works, messy if $g$ not monotone                      |
| $f_Y$ from $f_X$, $g$ monotone     | **Change of variables**                              | Fast and clean                                               |
| $f_Y$ from $f_X$, $g$ not monotone | **CDF + differentiate** or **multi-branch Jacobian** | Need to identify all preimage branches                       |
| Identify the distribution of $Y$   | **MGFs** or **CDF method**                           | When you want to say "$Y \sim$ [something named]"            |

## The Variance Formula — A LOTUS Application You'll Use Constantly

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$$

The term $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ is LOTUS with $g(x) = x^2$. You never need the distribution of $X^2$.

Similarly, the second moment formula for any $g$: $$\mathbb{E}[g(X)^2] = \int g(x)^2, f_X(x), dx$$

And more generally, for any two functions $g, h$: $$\mathbb{E}[g(X)h(X)] = \int g(x)h(x), f_X(x), dx$$

## Example Collection

> _These are the ones to work through once by hand and then never forget._

**1. $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, find $\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)]$.**

By LOTUS: $\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty k(k-1) \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} = \sum_{k=2}^\infty \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{(k-2)!} = \lambda^2 \sum_{j=0}^\infty \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^j}{j!} = \lambda^2$

So $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - \lambda^2 = \mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] + \mathbb{E}[X] - \lambda^2 = \lambda^2 + \lambda - \lambda^2 = \lambda$.

The Poisson variance falls out _cleanly_ via LOTUS + the falling factorial trick. Trying to compute $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$ directly is messier.

**2. $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$, find $\mathbb{E}[X^n]$.**

$\mathbb{E}[X^n] = \int_0^1 x^n \cdot 1, dx = \frac{1}{n+1}$

The moments of the uniform are just $\frac{1}{n+1}$. LOTUS makes this trivial.

**3. $X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)$, find $\mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$ for $t < \lambda$.**

$\mathbb{E}[e^{tX}] = \int_0^\infty e^{tx} \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, dx = \lambda \int_0^\infty e^{-(λ-t)x}, dx = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}$

This _is_ the MGF of the Exponential, derived in one line via LOTUS.

## A Note on Measure Theory (For Later)

In the full measure-theoretic framework, LOTUS is not actually a theorem with a special name — it's almost a definition. If $Y = g(X)$, then for any measurable $h$:

$$\mathbb{E}[h(Y)] = \mathbb{E}[h(g(X))]$$

because $h(Y)$ and $h(g(X))$ are literally the same random variable. LOTUS (with $h = \text{id}$) is then:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int g, d\mu_X$$

where $\mu_X$ is the pushforward measure of $X$ — which is just a formal way of saying "integrate $g$ against $X$'s distribution." The statement becomes transparent at this level, which is exactly why the name "unconscious statistician" exists: at the elementary level it requires thought; at the measure-theoretic level it's definitional.

## Pitfalls and Things Not to Mix Up

> _For the exam or any proof, keep these straight._

1. **LOTUS ≠ linearity of expectation.** Linearity says $\mathbb{E}[aX + b] = a\mathbb{E}[X] + b$. LOTUS is for general nonlinear $g$. They're different tools. (Linearity doesn't require any computation; LOTUS does.)
    
2. **$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \neq g(\mathbb{E}[X])$ in general.** Jensen's inequality quantifies the gap. LOTUS computes the left side correctly; the right side is a common error.
    
3. **Change of variables requires monotonicity or branch-counting.** Don't apply the Jacobian formula to $g(x) = x^2$ without accounting for both $\pm\sqrt{y}$ branches.
    
4. **The CDF method works always; the Jacobian method is a shortcut.** When in doubt, use CDF + differentiate. The Jacobian is faster when $g$ is smooth and monotone.
    
5. **LOTUS needs absolute convergence.** Formally, $\mathbb{E}[g(X)]$ requires $\mathbb{E}[|g(X)|] < \infty$ for the sum/integral to be well-defined. Don't apply it blindly to heavy-tailed distributions.
