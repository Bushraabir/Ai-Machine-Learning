## Why I care about this

Before diving in: the Beta distribution lives on $(0,1)$, which makes it the natural home for **probabilities**, proportions, and anything that needs to be bounded. Every time I think "what's a prior over a probability?" — Beta. And the transformations here are what let me move between Beta, Gamma, and the Uniform in a principled way.

Blitzstein hammers this in Stat 110: **know the stories, then the transformations follow**.

---

## Setup: What is the Beta distribution?

$X \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)$ means $X$ has PDF

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{B(a,b)} x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1}, \quad x \in (0,1)$$

where the **Beta function** is the normalizing constant:

$$B(a,b) = \int_0^1 x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1},dx$$

The key fact (proved via Gamma):

$$\boxed{B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)}}$$

I should _know_ this identity cold. It comes up everywhere.

---

## The Fundamental Transformation: Beta from Gammas

> **Theorem.** Let $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, 1)$ and $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b, 1)$ be independent. Then
> 
> $$W = \frac{X}{X+Y} \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)$$
> 
> and $W$ is independent of $X + Y \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b, 1)$.

### Proof (full, rigorous)

Let $T = X + Y$. We want the joint distribution of $(W, T)$ via the change of variables

$$X = WT, \quad Y = (1-W)T$$

**Step 1: Jacobian.** The transformation $(x,y) \mapsto (w,t)$ is

$$x = wt, \quad y = (1-w)t$$

Compute the Jacobian $\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(w,t)}$:

$$J = \begin{vmatrix} t & w \ -t & 1-w \end{vmatrix} = t(1-w) + wt = t$$

So $|J| = t$.

**Step 2: Joint PDF of $(X,Y)$.** Since $X, Y$ independent:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{x^{a-1}e^{-x}}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{y^{b-1}e^{-y}}{\Gamma(b)}, \quad x,y > 0$$

**Step 3: Substitute.** With $x = wt$, $y = (1-w)t$, $dx,dy = t,dw,dt$:

$$f_{W,T}(w,t) = \frac{(wt)^{a-1}e^{-wt}}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{((1-w)t)^{b-1}e^{-(1-w)t}}{\Gamma(b)} \cdot t$$

Collect terms:

$$= \frac{w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1} \cdot t^{a+b-1} e^{-t}}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}$$

**Step 4: Factor.** This factors as a product of a function of $w$ alone and a function of $t$ alone:

$$f_{W,T}(w,t) = \underbrace{\left[\frac{w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1}}{B(a,b)}\right]}_{\text{Beta}(a,b)\text{ PDF in }w} \cdot \underbrace{\left[\frac{t^{a+b-1}e^{-t}}{\Gamma(a+b)}\right]}_{\text{Gamma}(a+b,1)\text{ PDF in }t}$$

using $B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)}$.

**Conclusion.** $W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, $T \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b,1)$, and they are **independent**. $\blacksquare$

> _Personal note: The independence is the shocking part. I wouldn't have guessed that $W = X/(X+Y)$ "forgets" the total $X+Y$. This is the same flavor of miracle as the Gamma–Poisson relationship._

## Corollary: $\text{Beta}(1,1) = \text{Uniform}(0,1)$

Since $\text{Gamma}(1,1) = \text{Expo}(1)$, if $X, Y \sim \text{Expo}(1)$ independent, then

$$\frac{X}{X+Y} \sim \text{Beta}(1,1) = \text{Uniform}(0,1)$$

This is a great sanity check. The Beta interpolates between the Uniform (both shape params = 1) and more concentrated distributions.

## The Beta Function Identity (proved via the transformation)

> **Corollary.** $B(a,b) = \dfrac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)}$

**Proof.** We know $W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$ must integrate to 1:

$$\int_0^1 \frac{w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1}}{B(a,b)},dw = 1$$

So $\int_0^1 w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1},dw = B(a,b)$.

On the other hand, integrate the joint PDF over all $w \in (0,1)$ and $t > 0$:

$$\int_0^\infty \int_0^1 f_{W,T}(w,t),dw,dt = 1$$

Separating and using that each marginal integrates to 1 forces $\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b) = B(a,b),\Gamma(a+b)$. $\blacksquare$

## Order Statistics of the Uniform: Another Beta

> **Theorem.** Let $U_1, \ldots, U_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Uniform}(0,1)$. The $k$-th order statistic $U_{(k)}$ satisfies
> 
> $$U_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k,, n-k+1)$$

### Proof

The PDF of $U_{(k)}$ is derived by the "sandwich" argument: the probability that $U_{(k)} \in (x, x+dx)$ is the probability that exactly $k-1$ observations fall below $x$, one falls in $(x,x+dx)$, and $n-k$ fall above $x+dx$. There are $\binom{n}{1}\binom{n-1}{k-1}$ ways to assign roles:

$$f_{U_{(k)}}(x) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!} x^{k-1}(1-x)^{n-k}, \quad x \in (0,1)$$

Recognizing the Beta PDF with $a = k$, $b = n-k+1$:

$$U_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k, n-k+1) \qquad \blacksquare$$

> **Why I love this.** It connects the abstract Gamma-construction Beta to something completely concrete: sorting uniforms. The median of $n$ uniforms (when $n$ is odd, $k = (n+1)/2$) is Beta-distributed. This is a great fact to keep in my back pocket.

## Symmetry of the Beta

> **Proposition.** If $X \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, then $1 - X \sim \text{Beta}(b,a)$.

**Proof.** Let $Y = 1 - X$. The PDF of $Y$ at $y \in (0,1)$:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(1-y) \cdot 1 = \frac{(1-y)^{a-1} y^{b-1}}{B(a,b)} = \frac{y^{b-1}(1-y)^{a-1}}{B(b,a)}$$

using $B(a,b) = B(b,a)$ (by symmetry of the definition). So $Y \sim \text{Beta}(b,a)$. $\blacksquare$

## Moments

For $X \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$:

$$E[X] = \frac{a}{a+b}$$

$$E[X^2] = \frac{a(a+1)}{(a+b)(a+b+1)}$$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{ab}{(a+b)^2(a+b+1)}$$

**Proof of the mean.** Using the Beta function:

$$E[X] = \frac{1}{B(a,b)} \int_0^1 x \cdot x^{a-1}(1-x)^{b-1},dx = \frac{B(a+1,b)}{B(a,b)} = \frac{\Gamma(a+1)\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a+b+1)\Gamma(a)} = \frac{a}{a+b} \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _The mean is just the "weight" of $a$ relative to $a+b$. Makes intuitive sense when thinking about Beta as a prior: if I've seen $a$ successes and $b$ failures, my posterior mean for $p$ is $a/(a+b)$._
