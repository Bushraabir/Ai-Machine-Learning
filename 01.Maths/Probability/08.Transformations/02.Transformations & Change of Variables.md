> _The whole game is this: you know the distribution of $X$, and you want the distribution of $Y = g(X)$. Everything else is just technique._

---

## Why This Matters (To Me)

I keep running into this everywhere — mgfs, convolutions, order statistics, the whole works. The machinery here is the same machinery that underpins most of the slick derivations in Stat 110. So it's worth doing this properly, once, with full rigor, so I never have to fumble through it again.

The core tension: probability is fundamentally about _sets_ (events), not _values_. When we change variables, we're really asking: what event in the new space corresponds to ${Y \leq y}$? That's the whole conceptual heart of it.

---

## Part I — The Discrete Case (Warm-Up)

Let $X$ be a discrete r.v. with PMF $p_X$, and let $Y = g(X)$ for some function $g$.

$$p_Y(y) = P(Y = y) = \sum_{{x ,:, g(x) = y}} p_X(x)$$

Nothing surprising — you pool all the $x$-values that map to $y$ under $g$. If $g$ is injective, there's at most one such $x$ for each $y$, and this simplifies cleanly.

**Example I want to remember:** If $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ and $Y = X^2$, then since $X$ takes values in ${1, 2, 3, \ldots}$, squaring is injective here, so $p_Y(k^2) = p_X(k)$ for each positive integer $k$. Done.

---

## Part II — The Continuous Case via CDF

This is where the real action is.

**Setup.** Let $X$ be a continuous r.v. with CDF $F_X$ and PDF $f_X$. Let $Y = g(X)$.

**The universal method: find $F_Y$ first, then differentiate.**

$$F_Y(y) = P(Y \leq y) = P(g(X) \leq y)$$

Now I have to think about what the set ${g(X) \leq y}$ looks like in terms of $X$. This depends on $g$.

**Case 1: $g$ strictly increasing.**

$g(X) \leq y \iff X \leq g^{-1}(y)$, so $F_Y(y) = F_X(g^{-1}(y))$. Differentiating:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \frac{d}{dy}, g^{-1}(y)$$

**Case 2: $g$ strictly decreasing.**

$g(X) \leq y \iff X \geq g^{-1}(y)$, so $F_Y(y) = 1 - F_X(g^{-1}(y))$. Differentiating (noting $\frac{d}{dy}g^{-1}(y) < 0$ now):

$$f_Y(y) = -f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \frac{d}{dy}, g^{-1}(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y)) \cdot \left|\frac{d}{dy}, g^{-1}(y)\right|$$

---

## Part III — The Change of Variables Formula

Combining both cases into one clean theorem:

> **Theorem (Change of Variables, 1D).** Let $X$ be continuous with PDF $f_X$, supported on interval $I$. Let $g: I \to \mathbb{R}$ be differentiable and strictly monotone. Let $Y = g(X)$. Then:
> 
> $$\boxed{f_Y(y) = f_X!\left(g^{-1}(y)\right) \cdot \left|\frac{d}{dy}, g^{-1}(y)\right|}$$
> 
> Equivalently: $f_Y(y) = f_X(x) \cdot \left|\dfrac{dx}{dy}\right|$ where $x = g^{-1}(y)$.

**Why the absolute value?** The Jacobian factor $|dx/dy|$ accounts for how $g$ stretches or compresses probability mass. Think of it as conservation: $f(x)|dx| = f(y)|dy|$ — probability in an infinitesimal interval is preserved under the transformation. If $g$ expands a region of the $x$-axis, the density must _fall_ proportionally so mass is conserved.

## Part IV — Worked Example: The Log-Normal

Let $Z \sim N(0,1)$, and $X = e^Z$. What's the distribution of $X$?

$g(z) = e^z$ is strictly increasing. We have $g^{-1}(x) = \ln x$ for $x > 0$, and

$$\frac{d}{dx} \ln x = \frac{1}{x}$$

So by the formula:

$$f_X(x) = f_Z(\ln x) \cdot \frac{1}{x} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}, e^{-(\ln x)^2/2} \cdot \frac{1}{x}, \qquad x > 0$$

This is the **Log-Normal** distribution. Two lines. The machinery does the work.

It comes up everywhere — stock prices, income distributions, anything driven by multiplicative noise. The reason: if $X$ is log-normal then $\ln X$ is Gaussian, which happens when growth is proportional to current size (i.e., percentage changes are roughly iid).

## Part V — The Non-Monotone Case

What if $g$ isn't monotone? Say $Y = X^2$ with $X$ continuous on all of $\mathbb{R}$.

For $y > 0$, both $\sqrt{y}$ and $-\sqrt{y}$ satisfy $g(x) = y$, so $g^{-1}$ is multi-valued. I can't apply the theorem directly.

**Fix:** work from the CDF, and break the domain into monotone pieces.

$$F_Y(y) = P(X^2 \leq y) = P(-\sqrt{y} \leq X \leq \sqrt{y}) = F_X(\sqrt{y}) - F_X(-\sqrt{y})$$

Differentiating:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} + f_X(-\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}, \qquad y > 0$$

**General principle:** if $g$ is piecewise monotone with inverse branches $h_1, \ldots, h_k$:

$$f_Y(y) = \sum_{i=1}^{k} f_X(h_i(y)) \cdot |h_i'(y)|$$

---

## Part VI — Chi-Squared as an Immediate Corollary

Let $Z \sim N(0,1)$, $Y = Z^2$. Using the formula above with symmetry $\varphi(-t) = \varphi(t)$:

$$f_Y(y) = \frac{2}{2\sqrt{y}},\varphi(\sqrt{y}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{y}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}},e^{-y/2} = \frac{1}{2^{1/2},\Gamma(1/2)},y^{1/2 - 1},e^{-y/2}, \qquad y > 0$$

That's $\text{Gamma}(1/2,, 1/2)$ — which is exactly $\chi^2_1$ by definition. The chi-squared distribution just _falls out_ of the machinery. I should remember this derivation cold.

## Part VII — Multivariable Change of Variables

Now the full story. Let $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ have joint PDF $f_{\mathbf{X}}$, and let $\mathbf{Y} = g(\mathbf{X})$ where $g: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is a diffeomorphism (smooth bijection with smooth inverse).

> **Theorem (Multivariate COV).** Under the above:
> 
> $$f_{\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{y}) = f_{\mathbf{X}}!\left(g^{-1}(\mathbf{y})\right) \cdot \left|\det J_{g^{-1}}(\mathbf{y})\right|$$
> 
> where the Jacobian matrix of $g^{-1}$ is:
> 
> $$\left(J_{g^{-1}}\right)_{ij} = \frac{\partial x_i}{\partial y_j}$$

Equivalently: $f_{\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{y}),|d\mathbf{y}| = f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x}),|d\mathbf{x}|$, since $|d\mathbf{x}| = |\det J|,|d\mathbf{y}|$.

**Why the determinant?** Because $|\det J|$ is the factor by which the linear approximation of $g^{-1}$ scales $n$-dimensional volume. Probability is volume under the density, and the Jacobian determinant precisely captures how that volume is distorted. This is the $n$-dimensional version of $|dx/dy|$.

## Part VIII — The Gaussian Miracle (Box-Muller)

Let $X, Y \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(0,1)$. Transform to polar coordinates:

$$R = \sqrt{X^2 + Y^2}, \qquad \Theta = \arctan(Y/X)$$

The joint PDF of $(X,Y)$ is:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi},e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}$$

Inverse transform: $x = r\cos\theta$, $y = r\sin\theta$. Jacobian:

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}, \qquad |\det J| = r$$

So:

$$f_{R,\Theta}(r,\theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi},e^{-r^2/2} \cdot r = \underbrace{re^{-r^2/2}}_{\text{density of }R} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{1}{2\pi}}_{\text{density of }\Theta}$$

This **factors**, proving $R \perp \Theta$, with $\Theta \sim \text{Unif}(0, 2\pi)$ and $R^2 \sim \text{Expo}(1/2)$ (i.e., $R^2 \sim \chi^2_2$). Beautiful.

**Practical upshot — Box-Muller:** To sample $Z_1, Z_2 \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(0,1)$, generate $U_1, U_2 \sim \text{Unif}(0,1)$ and set:

$$Z_1 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1}\cos(2\pi U_2), \qquad Z_2 = \sqrt{-2\ln U_1}\sin(2\pi U_2)$$

Exact, not approximate. The whole thing is just this theorem run backwards.

## Common Pitfalls I Keep Making

1. **Forgetting to restrict the support.** $f_Y$ is only nonzero on the _image_ of $g$. Always figure out what values $Y$ can take before writing down the formula.
    
2. **Forgetting the Jacobian.** I've written $f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y))$ without the derivative factor before. That's wrong and will never integrate to 1.
    
3. **Assuming monotonicity without checking.** If $g$ is not monotone on its full domain, the single-branch formula gives the wrong answer. Sketch the function.
    
4. **Getting the Jacobian direction wrong.** It's $|dx/dy| = |\frac{d}{dy} g^{-1}(y)|$, not $|dy/dx|$. I differentiate the _inverse_ map.

## The CDF Method vs. The Formula

I always have a choice:

|Method|When to use|
|---|---|
|**CDF method**|$g$ non-monotone, domain issues, or I'm being careful|
|**Change of variables formula**|$g$ is a smooth bijection on a clean domain|

The formula is _derived from_ the CDF method, so when in doubt, go back to basics. There's no shame in it.
