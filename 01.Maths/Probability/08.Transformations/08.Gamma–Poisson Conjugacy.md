

> _"The Beta-Binomial pair asks: what is the probability of success? The Gamma-Poisson pair asks: at what rate do events arrive? Same Bayesian skeleton, different story."_

---

## The Setup

In [[Bayes Billiards]] we put a prior on a probability $p \in (0,1)$. Here the unknown is a **rate** $\lambda \in (0, \infty)$ â€” how fast a Poisson process is firing. We observe counts and update.

**The model:**

$$\lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(a, b) \qquad \text{(prior on the rate)}$$

$$X \mid \lambda \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda) \qquad \text{(one observation)}$$

We want the posterior $f_{\lambda \mid X}(\lambda \mid X = k)$.

This is the Poisson analogue of [[Bayes Billiards]]. The punchline: the posterior is again a Gamma â€” the Gamma is **conjugate** to the Poisson.

---

## Why Gamma as a Prior on $\lambda$?

Two reasons:

1. **Support.** $\lambda > 0$, and so does $\text{Gamma}(a,b)$. The Uniform on $(0,1)$ won't do here â€” we need a distribution on the positive reals.
    
2. **Conjugacy.** As we'll prove, the posterior lands back in the Gamma family. This is the algebraic miracle that makes the update clean.
    

The hyperparameters $a, b$ have interpretations I'll make precise after the proof.

> _Convention note: I'm using $\text{Gamma}(a,b)$ with $b$ as the **rate** parameter throughout this note, so the PDF is $f(\lambda) = \frac{b^a}{\Gamma(a)}\lambda^{a-1}e^{-b\lambda}$. This matches Blitzstein. Don't mix up rate vs. scale._

---

## The Posterior: Full Derivation

By Bayes' theorem:

$$f_{\lambda \mid X=k}(\lambda) \propto P(X = k \mid \lambda) \cdot f_\lambda(\lambda)$$

**Likelihood:**

$$P(X = k \mid \lambda) = \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!}$$

**Prior:**

$$f_\lambda(\lambda) = \frac{b^a}{\Gamma(a)}\lambda^{a-1}e^{-b\lambda}$$

**Multiply and drop constants in $\lambda$:**

$$f_{\lambda \mid X=k}(\lambda) \propto e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k \cdot \lambda^{a-1}e^{-b\lambda} = \lambda^{a+k-1} e^{-(b+1)\lambda}$$

Recognize the kernel: $\lambda^{(a+k)-1}e^{-(b+1)\lambda}$ is exactly the kernel of $\text{Gamma}(a+k,; b+1)$.

$$\boxed{\lambda \mid X = k ;\sim; \text{Gamma}(a + k,; b + 1)}$$

$\blacksquare$

---

## The General Case: $n$ Observations

Now suppose $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ and let $S = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$.

Since $X_i \mid \lambda$ are independent Poissons, the joint likelihood factors:

$$P(X_1 = x_1,\ldots,X_n = x_n \mid \lambda) = \prod_{i=1}^n \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^{x_i}}{x_i!} \propto e^{-n\lambda}\lambda^S$$

Multiplying by the prior kernel $\lambda^{a-1}e^{-b\lambda}$:

$$f_{\lambda \mid \mathbf{X}}(\lambda) \propto \lambda^{a+S-1}e^{-(b+n)\lambda}$$

$$\boxed{\lambda \mid X_1,\ldots,X_n ;\sim; \text{Gamma}(a + S,; b + n)}$$

where $S = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$ is the total observed count. $\blacksquare$

> _The shape absorbs the total count. The rate absorbs the number of observations. Perfectly symmetric and clean._

---

## Interpreting the Hyperparameters

The posterior $\text{Gamma}(a+S, b+n)$ reveals exactly what $a$ and $b$ mean in the prior $\text{Gamma}(a,b)$:

|Hyperparameter|Interpretation|
|---|---|
|$a$|Prior pseudo-counts â€” equivalent to having seen $a$ events already|
|$b$|Prior pseudo-time â€” equivalent to having watched for $b$ time units|
|$a/b$|Prior mean of $\lambda$|

After seeing $S$ events in $n$ observations, update: $a \to a+S$, $b \to b+n$.

This is **Bayesian updating as bookkeeping**: just add the observed counts and observations to the prior counts.

---

## Posterior Mean and Shrinkage

$$E[\lambda \mid X_1,\ldots,X_n] = \frac{a+S}{b+n}$$

The MLE of $\lambda$ is $\hat\lambda_{\text{MLE}} = S/n = \bar X$.

Rewrite the posterior mean as a convex combination:

$$\frac{a+S}{b+n} = \frac{b}{b+n} \cdot \frac{a}{b} + \frac{n}{b+n} \cdot \frac{S}{n}$$

$$= \underbrace{\frac{b}{b+n}}_{\text{prior weight}} \cdot \underbrace{\frac{a}{b}}_{\text{prior mean}} ;+; \underbrace{\frac{n}{b+n}}_{\text{data weight}} \cdot \underbrace{\bar X}_{\text{MLE}}$$

The posterior mean is a **convex combination** of the prior mean and the MLE. As $n \to \infty$, the data weight $\to 1$ and the posterior mean $\to \bar X$. The prior washes out.

> _This shrinkage formula appears in every conjugate family â€” Beta-Binomial, Gamma-Poisson, Normal-Normal. It's the mathematical expression of "data eventually dominates belief." Internalize the pattern, not just the formula._

---

## The Marginal Distribution of $X$: Negative Binomial

What is $P(X = k)$ unconditionally, integrating $\lambda$ out entirely?

$$P(X = k) = \int_0^\infty P(X=k \mid \lambda), f_\lambda(\lambda),d\lambda = \int_0^\infty \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} \cdot \frac{b^a}{\Gamma(a)}\lambda^{a-1}e^{-b\lambda},d\lambda$$

$$= \frac{b^a}{k!,\Gamma(a)} \int_0^\infty \lambda^{a+k-1} e^{-(b+1)\lambda},d\lambda = \frac{b^a}{k!,\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a+k)}{(b+1)^{a+k}}$$

$$= \frac{\Gamma(a+k)}{\Gamma(a),k!} \cdot \left(\frac{b}{b+1}\right)^a \cdot \left(\frac{1}{b+1}\right)^k$$

Setting $p = b/(b+1)$, so $1-p = 1/(b+1)$:

$$\boxed{X ;\sim; \text{NegBin}!\left(a,; \frac{b}{b+1}\right)}$$

$$P(X = k) = \binom{a+k-1}{k} p^a (1-p)^k, \quad k = 0,1,2,\ldots$$

> _The Negative Binomial is the Poisson with a Gamma-distributed rate. This is the canonical model for **overdispersed count data** â€” situations where the variance exceeds the mean, which is impossible under a Poisson but natural once you admit uncertainty in $\lambda$._

**Proof that $\text{Var}(X) > E[X]$.** By the law of total variance:

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[\text{Var}(X\mid\lambda)] + \text{Var}(E[X\mid\lambda]) = E[\lambda] + \text{Var}(\lambda) = \frac{a}{b} + \frac{a}{b^2} > \frac{a}{b} = E[X]$$

The extra variance is entirely from **uncertainty about $\lambda$**. Lovely. $\blacksquare$

## The Predictive Distribution

Given $X_1,\ldots,X_n$ with $S = \sum X_i$, the posterior is $\text{Gamma}(a+S, b+n)$. What does the next observation look like?

By the same Gamma-Poisson marginalization with updated hyperparameters:

$$X_{n+1} \mid X_1,\ldots,X_n ;\sim; \text{NegBin}!\left(a+S,; \frac{b+n}{b+n+1}\right)$$

The predictive mean:

$$E[X_{n+1} \mid X_1,\ldots,X_n] = E\left[E[X_{n+1}\mid\lambda] \mid X_1,\ldots,X_n\right] = E[\lambda \mid X_1,\ldots,X_n] = \frac{a+S}{b+n}$$

The predictive mean is the posterior mean of $\lambda$. This is always true when $E[X\mid\lambda] = \lambda$.

---

## Jeffreys Prior for the Poisson

What's the "least informative" prior for $\lambda$? The **Jeffreys prior** is $\pi(\lambda) \propto \sqrt{I(\lambda)}$ where $I(\lambda)$ is the Fisher information.

For $X \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, compute the score:

$$\frac{\partial}{\partial\lambda}\log P(X=k;\lambda) = \frac{\partial}{\partial\lambda}(k\log\lambda - \lambda) = \frac{k}{\lambda} - 1 = \frac{X}{\lambda} - 1$$

Fisher information:

$$I(\lambda) = E\left[\left(\frac{X}{\lambda}-1\right)^2\right] = \frac{\text{Var}(X)}{\lambda^2} = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

So the Jeffreys prior is:

$$\pi(\lambda) \propto \sqrt{\frac{1}{\lambda}} = \lambda^{-1/2} = \lambda^{1/2 - 1}e^{-0\cdot\lambda}$$

This is the kernel of $\text{Gamma}(1/2, 0)$ â€” an **improper prior** (doesn't integrate to a finite constant). But the posterior after observing $S$ events in $n$ trials is:

$$\lambda \mid \mathbf{X} \sim \text{Gamma}(S + 1/2,; n)$$

which is proper as long as $n > 0$.

> _Improper priors make me a little uncomfortable, but they're standard. The rule to always check: even if the prior is improper, verify the posterior is proper. Here it is. Good._

## Comparison: Beta-Binomial vs Gamma-Poisson

||Beta-Binomial|Gamma-Poisson|
|---|---|---|
|Unknown|$p \in (0,1)$|$\lambda \in (0,\infty)$|
|Prior|$\text{Beta}(a,b)$|$\text{Gamma}(a,b)$|
|Likelihood|$\text{Bin}(n,p)$|$\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$|
|Sufficient statistic|$k = \sum x_i$|$S = \sum x_i$|
|Posterior|$\text{Beta}(a+k,; b+n-k)$|$\text{Gamma}(a+S,; b+n)$|
|Marginal of $X$|$\text{Unif}{0,\ldots,n}$ (when $a=b=1$)|$\text{NegBin}(a,; b/(b+1))$|
|Posterior mean|$\frac{a+k}{a+b+n}$|$\frac{a+S}{b+n}$|
|Jeffreys prior|$\text{Beta}(1/2,1/2)$|$\text{Gamma}(1/2, 0)$ â€” improper|

Same skeleton throughout.

## The General Mechanism: Exponential Family Conjugacy

Both the Beta-Binomial and Gamma-Poisson are instances of a single theorem. For any **exponential family** likelihood:

$$f(x \mid \eta) = h(x)\exp!\left(\eta, T(x) - A(\eta)\right)$$

the conjugate prior is:

$$\pi(\eta \mid \chi, \nu) \propto \exp!\left(\eta, \chi - \nu, A(\eta)\right)$$

and the posterior after $n$ observations with sufficient statistic sum $\sum T(x_i)$ is:

$$\pi!\left(\eta ;\middle|; \chi + \textstyle\sum T(x_i),; \nu + n\right)$$

The update rule: **add the sufficient statistic to $\chi$, add $n$ to $\nu$**. That's it.

For Poisson: $\eta = \log\lambda$, $T(x) = x$, $A(\eta) = e^\eta = \lambda$. For Binomial: $\eta = \log(p/(1-p))$, $T(x) = x$, $A(\eta) = n\log(1+e^\eta)$.

> _Come back to this properly when doing exponential families. For now: the pattern is the whole point. Both conjugacies are the same theorem wearing different clothes._
