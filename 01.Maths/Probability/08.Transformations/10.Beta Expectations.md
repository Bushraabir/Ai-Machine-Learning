

> _Once you have $B(a,b) = \Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)/\Gamma(a+b)$, computing moments of the Beta is almost embarrassingly easy. The whole game is recognising that the integral you're staring at is just another Beta function in disguise. This note is short because the proofs are short — and that's the point._

---

## 0. Setup

$W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, so: $$f_W(w) = \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}, w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1}, \quad 0 < w < 1$$

All moments live in $[0,1]$ — which should pass the sanity check immediately since $W$ is supported on $(0,1)$.

---

## 1. The $n$-th Moment (Raw)

$$\mathbb{E}[W^n] = \int_0^1 w^n \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}, w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1}, dw$$

$$= \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)} \int_0^1 w^{(a+n)-1}(1-w)^{b-1}, dw$$

The integral is exactly $B(a+n,, b)$. So:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[W^n] = \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a+n),\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b+n)}}$$

$$= \frac{\Gamma(a+n)}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a+b+n)}$$

> _The trick is always the same: absorb the extra $w^n$ into the exponent, recognise a Beta integral, apply the identity. You will use this move constantly._

This has a clean form using the **rising factorial** (Pochhammer symbol) $a^{(n)} = a(a+1)\cdots(a+n-1) = \Gamma(a+n)/\Gamma(a)$:

$$\mathbb{E}[W^n] = \frac{a^{(n)}}{(a+b)^{(n)}}$$

For $n=1,2$ this gives everything we need below.

---

## 2. Mean

Set $n=1$:

$$\mathbb{E}[W] = \frac{\Gamma(a+1)}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a+b+1)} = a \cdot \frac{1}{a+b}$$

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[W] = \frac{a}{a+b}}$$

**Sanity checks:**

- $a = b$: mean is $1/2$ ✓ (symmetric distribution, symmetric mean)
- $a \gg b$: mean $\to 1$ ✓ (weight pushed toward 1)
- $\text{Beta}(1,1) = \text{Uniform}(0,1)$: mean $= 1/2$ ✓

**Intuition from the Gamma construction.** Recall $W = X/(X+Y)$ where $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a,1)$, $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b,1)$ independent. We know $\mathbb{E}[X] = a$ and $\mathbb{E}[Y] = b$. Naively you might guess $\mathbb{E}[W] \approx a/(a+b)$ — and it's _exactly_ right. (This is not obvious from Jensen since $g(x,y) = x/(x+y)$ is nonlinear, but the Gamma structure makes it work out cleanly.)

---

## 3. Second Moment

Set $n=2$:

$$\mathbb{E}[W^2] = \frac{\Gamma(a+2)}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a+b+2)} = a(a+1) \cdot \frac{1}{(a+b)(a+b+1)}$$

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[W^2] = \frac{a(a+1)}{(a+b)(a+b+1)}}$$

---

## 4. Variance

$$\text{Var}(W) = \mathbb{E}[W^2] - (\mathbb{E}[W])^2 = \frac{a(a+1)}{(a+b)(a+b+1)} - \frac{a^2}{(a+b)^2}$$

Let $s = a+b$ for cleanliness. Then:

$$\text{Var}(W) = \frac{a(a+1)}{s(s+1)} - \frac{a^2}{s^2} = \frac{a}{s}\left[\frac{a+1}{s+1} - \frac{a}{s}\right]$$

$$= \frac{a}{s} \cdot \frac{s(a+1) - a(s+1)}{s(s+1)} = \frac{a}{s} \cdot \frac{as + s - as - a}{s(s+1)} = \frac{a}{s} \cdot \frac{b}{s(s+1)}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(W) = \frac{ab}{(a+b)^2(a+b+1)}}$$

**Reading the formula:**

- Numerator $ab$: variance is maximised when $a = b$ (most spread), killed when either parameter $\to \infty$ relative to the other.
- Denominator grows as $(a+b)^3$ for large $a+b$: adding more "pseudodata" concentrates the distribution. This is the Bayesian intuition — more prior observations, tighter posterior.
- For $\text{Beta}(1,1)$: $\text{Var} = 1/(4 \cdot 3) = 1/12$. Check: $\text{Var}(\text{Uniform}(0,1)) = 1/12$ ✓

## 6. Beta-Binomial: Where This Actually Lives

_This is why I care about these moments._

Suppose $W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$ and $X \mid W \sim \text{Binomial}(n, W)$. This is the **Beta-Binomial model** — the Beta is a prior on the unknown probability $p$.

**By Adam's Law (LTE):** $$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid W]] = \mathbb{E}[nW] = n \cdot \frac{a}{a+b}$$

**By Eve's Law (LTVE):** $$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid W)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid W])$$ $$= \mathbb{E}[nW(1-W)] + \text{Var}(nW)$$ $$= n\left(\mathbb{E}[W] - \mathbb{E}[W^2]\right) + n^2,\text{Var}(W)$$ $$= n\left(\frac{a}{a+b} - \frac{a(a+1)}{(a+b)(a+b+1)}\right) + n^2 \cdot \frac{ab}{(a+b)^2(a+b+1)}$$

The first term simplifies (factor out $a/(a+b)$):

$$\frac{a}{a+b}\left(1 - \frac{a+1}{a+b+1}\right) = \frac{a}{a+b}\cdot\frac{b}{a+b+1} = \frac{ab}{(a+b)(a+b+1)}$$

So: $$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{nab}{(a+b)(a+b+1)} + \frac{n^2 ab}{(a+b)^2(a+b+1)} = \frac{nab}{(a+b)(a+b+1)}\left(1 + \frac{n}{a+b}\right) \cdot \frac{a+b}{a+b}$$

Actually, let me write it cleanly. Set $s = a+b$:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = \frac{nab(n+s)}{s^2(s+1)}}$$

Compare to $\text{Binomial}(n,p)$ with fixed $p = a/s$: that has variance $np(1-p) = nab/s^2$. The Beta-Binomial variance has the extra factor $(n+s)/(s+1) > 1$ — it's **overdispersed** relative to the Binomial. The uncertainty in $W$ inflates the variance. More prior "data" (large $s$) reduces this inflation.

---

## 7. The Bayesian Posterior Mean

_One more thing worth recording here._

In the Beta-Binomial model: observe $X = k$ successes in $n$ trials. The posterior is:

$$W \mid X=k \sim \text{Beta}(a+k,, b+n-k)$$

Posterior mean: $$\mathbb{E}[W \mid X=k] = \frac{a+k}{a+b+n}$$

This is a **weighted average** of the prior mean $a/(a+b)$ and the MLE $k/n$:

$$\frac{a+k}{a+b+n} = \frac{a+b}{a+b+n}\cdot\frac{a}{a+b} + \frac{n}{a+b+n}\cdot\frac{k}{n}$$

As $n \to \infty$, weight shifts entirely to the data. As $a+b \to \infty$, the prior dominates. The moments of the Beta are _doing_ Bayesian inference.
