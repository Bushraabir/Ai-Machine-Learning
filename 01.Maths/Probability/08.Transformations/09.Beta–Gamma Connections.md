> _The moment I first saw this identity it felt like a cheat code. Two distributions that look completely unrelated turn out to be inseparable siblings. Write this down carefully — it's one of those facts that unlocks a dozen other proofs._

---

## 0. The Cast

Recall the key players before diving in.

**Gamma function** (the continuous factorial): $$\Gamma(a) = \int_0^\infty t^{a-1} e^{-t}, dt, \quad a > 0$$

This satisfies $\Gamma(a+1) = a,\Gamma(a)$ and $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ for positive integers. Think of it as the analytic extension of factorial that makes everything below _work_.

**Beta function:** $$B(a,b) = \int_0^1 t^{a-1}(1-t)^{b-1}, dt, \quad a,b > 0$$

**Gamma distribution** $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, 1)$: $$f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(a)} x^{a-1} e^{-x}, \quad x > 0$$

**Beta distribution** $W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$: $$f_W(w) = \frac{1}{B(a,b)} w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1}, \quad 0 < w < 1$$

The normalizing constant $B(a,b)$ is exactly what we need to compute — and the Gamma distributions will hand it to us for free.

---

## 1. The Key Identity

$$\boxed{B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a),\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)}}$$

This is _the_ fact. Everything else is either a proof or a consequence.

---

## 2. Proof via Independent Gamma RVs

_This is my favourite proof. It's a trick, but the trick is natural once you see it._

Let $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a,1)$ and $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b,1)$ be **independent**. Define the transformation:

$$W = \frac{X}{X+Y}, \qquad T = X + Y$$

**Claim:** $W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, $T \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b, 1)$, and $W \perp T$.

### 2.1 Setting Up the Change of Variables

The map $(x,y) \mapsto (w,t)$ is: $$x = wt, \qquad y = (1-w)t$$

with domain $x,y > 0$ mapping to $0 < w < 1$, $t > 0$.

**Jacobian:** $$\frac{\partial(x,y)}{\partial(w,t)} = \begin{vmatrix} t & w \ -t & 1-w \end{vmatrix} = t(1-w) + wt = t$$

So $|J| = t$.

### 2.2 Joint Density of $(X,Y)$

By independence: $$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{x^{a-1}e^{-x}}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{y^{b-1}e^{-y}}{\Gamma(b)}$$

### 2.3 Joint Density of $(W,T)$

Substitute $x = wt$, $y=(1-w)t$ and multiply by $|J|=t$:

$$f_{W,T}(w,t) = \frac{(wt)^{a-1}e^{-wt}}{\Gamma(a)} \cdot \frac{((1-w)t)^{b-1}e^{-(1-w)t}}{\Gamma(b)} \cdot t$$

$$= \frac{1}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}, w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1}, t^{a+b-1} e^{-t}$$

This **factors** into a function of $w$ alone times a function of $t$ alone — so $W \perp T$ immediately.

Reading off: $$f_T(t) = \frac{t^{a+b-1}e^{-t}}{\Gamma(a+b)}, \quad t > 0 \implies T \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b,1)$$

$$f_W(w) \propto w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1}, \quad 0 < w < 1 \implies W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$$

### 2.4 Extracting the Identity

Since $f_{W,T}$ must integrate to 1:

$$\int_0^1\int_0^\infty \frac{w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1} t^{a+b-1}e^{-t}}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}, dt, dw = 1$$

$$\frac{1}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)} \underbrace{\int_0^1 w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1},dw}_{B(a,b)} \cdot \underbrace{\int_0^\infty t^{a+b-1}e^{-t},dt}_{\Gamma(a+b)} = 1$$

Therefore: $$B(a,b),\Gamma(a+b) = \Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)$$

$$\therefore \quad B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a),\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)} \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _Notice what happened: we never had to evaluate $B(a,b)$ directly. The normalisation of two Gamma densities did all the work. This is the probabilistic method at its finest — embed your integral in a probability space and let normalisation finish the job._

## 3. Alternative Proof: Direct Convolution

_A more analytic route, less elegant but worth knowing. Connects to Blitzstein & Hwang §8.2._

We know $T = X+Y \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b,1)$ via convolution (the MGF argument, or direct computation). So:

$$f_T(t) = \int_0^t \frac{u^{a-1}e^{-u}}{\Gamma(a)}\cdot\frac{(t-u)^{b-1}e^{-(t-u)}}{\Gamma(b)},du = \frac{e^{-t}}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}\int_0^t u^{a-1}(t-u)^{b-1},du$$

Substitute $u = tw$:

$$= \frac{e^{-t}t^{a+b-1}}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)} \int_0^1 w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1},dw = \frac{e^{-t}t^{a+b-1}}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}, B(a,b)$$

But we also know $f_T(t) = \dfrac{t^{a+b-1}e^{-t}}{\Gamma(a+b)}$. Matching:

$$\frac{B(a,b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)} = \frac{1}{\Gamma(a+b)} \implies B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)} \qquad \blacksquare$$

## 4. Immediate Corollaries

### 4.1 Beta Normalising Constant is Now Explicit

We can write down the Beta PDF with full confidence:

$$f_W(w) = \frac{\Gamma(a+b)}{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}, w^{a-1}(1-w)^{b-1}$$

No more hand-wavy "proportional to." The constant is _computed_.

### 4.2 Integer Parameters: Clean Combinatorial Form

For positive integers $a,b$:

$$B(a,b) = \frac{(a-1)!,(b-1)!}{(a+b-1)!} = \frac{1}{(a+b-1)\binom{a+b-2}{a-1}}$$

### 4.3 Order Statistics Connection

If $U_{(k)}$ is the $k$-th order statistic of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$ RVs, then:

$$U_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k,, n-k+1)$$

The PDF is: $$f_{U_{(k)}}(u) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!}, u^{k-1}(1-u)^{n-k}$$

The normalising constant checks out via: $B(k, n-k+1) = \dfrac{(k-1)!(n-k)!}{n!}$. ✓

_This is worth meditating on. The $k$-th order statistic "lives" at position $k/(n+1)$ on average — and the Beta distribution captures the full uncertainty around that position. Beautiful._

### 4.4 Symmetry

$$B(a,b) = B(b,a)$$

Obvious from the Gamma formula (multiplication commutes). Also obvious geometrically: substitute $t \mapsto 1-t$ in the integral definition.

## 5. The Aggregation Property (Bonus)

_This fell out of the proof as a gift — don't skip it._

> If $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a,1)$, $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b,1)$ independent, then $X + Y \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b,1)$.

Proof: We showed $T \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b,1)$ above. This generalises: Gamma distributions with the same rate parameter are **closed under addition**. The shape parameters add. The rate stays fixed.

This is the Gamma analogue of "sum of Poissons is Poisson" — and there's a deep reason: Poisson processes.

---

## 6. Key Intuition to Carry Forward

The substitution $W = X/(X+Y)$, $T = X+Y$ is doing something profound:

- $T$ captures the **total size** (scale information)
- $W$ captures the **proportion** (shape/direction information)

The fact that $W \perp T$ says: knowing the total tells you nothing about how it splits. This is a characterisation of the Gamma distribution — if $W$ and $T$ are independent under this split, then $X$ and $Y$ _must_ be Gamma. (The Lukacs theorem.)
