> _"Bayes' first proof of his own theorem was a billiards table. He didn't think in terms of priors and likelihoods â€” he thought geometrically. The abstraction came later."_

---

## The Setup

This is one of the oldest problems in Bayesian inference, essentially the argument Thomas Bayes used in his posthumous 1763 paper. It's a beautiful way to see where the Beta distribution _comes from_ before you even know what Bayesian inference is.

**The story:**

Throw a ball $W$ onto a billiards table of length 1. It lands at some position $p \sim \text{Unif}(0,1)$ â€” we don't observe $p$. Now throw $n$ more balls. Each lands to the left of $p$ with probability $p$, independently. We observe $X =$ number of balls landing left of $p$.

**Question:** Given $X = k$, what do we know about $p$?

This is Bayesian inference before the formalism. The "prior" on $p$ is $\text{Unif}(0,1) = \text{Beta}(1,1)$. The "likelihood" is Binomial. And the posterior â€” this is the punchline â€” is Beta.

---

## The Formal Model

$$p \sim \text{Unif}(0,1) = \text{Beta}(1,1)$$

$$X \mid p \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$$

We want: $f_{p \mid X}(p \mid X = k)$.

---

## The Posterior: Full Derivation

By Bayes' theorem for continuous-discrete mixtures:

$$f_{p \mid X}(p \mid X = k) = \frac{P(X = k \mid p) \cdot f_p(p)}{P(X = k)}$$

**Numerator:**

$$P(X = k \mid p) \cdot f_p(p) = \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \cdot 1$$

**Denominator:** Integrate over $p$:

$$P(X = k) = \int_0^1 \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k},dp = \binom{n}{k} B(k+1,, n-k+1)$$

Using $B(a,b) = \frac{\Gamma(a)\Gamma(b)}{\Gamma(a+b)}$ and $\Gamma(m) = (m-1)!$ for integers:

$$B(k+1, n-k+1) = \frac{k!,(n-k)!}{(n+1)!} = \frac{1}{(n+1)\binom{n}{k}}$$

So:

$$P(X = k) = \binom{n}{k} \cdot \frac{1}{(n+1)\binom{n}{k}} = \frac{1}{n+1}$$

> _Stop and stare at this. No matter what $k$ is, $P(X = k) = 1/(n+1)$. Each of the $n+1$ possible values $0, 1, \ldots, n$ is equally likely. This is not obvious at all from the setup â€” it's a consequence of the Uniform prior._

**The posterior:**

$$f_{p \mid X = k}(p) = \frac{\binom{n}{k} p^k(1-p)^{n-k}}{1/(n+1)} = (n+1)\binom{n}{k}, p^k (1-p)^{n-k}$$

Recognize this: it's proportional to $p^k(1-p)^{n-k} = p^{(k+1)-1}(1-p)^{(n-k+1)-1}$, which is the kernel of a $\text{Beta}(k+1, n-k+1)$ density.

$$\boxed{p \mid X = k ;\sim; \text{Beta}(k+1,; n-k+1)}$$

$\blacksquare$

---

## Why $P(X = k) = \frac{1}{n+1}$: A Cleaner Argument

There's a slicker way to see this without integrating. Consider $n+1$ balls thrown uniformly and independently onto $[0,1]$. Label one of them $W$ (our "threshold") and the rest $B_1, \ldots, B_n$. By symmetry, all $(n+1)!$ orderings of the $n+1$ balls are equally likely.

The event $X = k$ is the event that exactly $k$ of the $B_i$'s land to the left of $W$, which is the same as $W$ being the $(k+1)$-th smallest among all $n+1$ balls. There are $n+1$ ranks, each equally likely by symmetry.

$$P(X = k) = \frac{1}{n+1}, \quad k = 0, 1, \ldots, n \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _This is the argument I want to remember. No integration required â€” just symmetry. Blitzstein would approve._

---

## The Beta-Binomial Conjugacy (General Case)

The Billiards problem is a special case of a general conjugacy:

> **Theorem (Beta-Binomial Conjugacy).** If
> 
> $$p \sim \text{Beta}(a, b), \qquad X \mid p \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$$
> 
> then
> 
> $$p \mid X = k ;\sim; \text{Beta}(a + k,; b + n - k)$$

### Proof

$$f_{p \mid X=k}(p) \propto P(X=k \mid p) \cdot f_p(p) = \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k} \cdot \frac{p^{a-1}(1-p)^{b-1}}{B(a,b)}$$

$$\propto p^{a+k-1}(1-p)^{b+n-k-1}$$

This is the kernel of $\text{Beta}(a+k, b+n-k)$. $\blacksquare$

**Interpretation:**

|Quantity|Meaning|
|---|---|
|Prior $\text{Beta}(a,b)$|$a-1$ prior "successes", $b-1$ prior "failures"|
|Observe $k$ successes in $n$ trials|Update counts|
|Posterior $\text{Beta}(a+k, b+n-k)$|$a+k-1$ total successes, $b+n-k-1$ failures|

The Beta is the **conjugate prior** for the Binomial. The prior and posterior live in the same family â€” you just add the data counts to the shape parameters.

The Billiards problem is the case $a = b = 1$ (Uniform prior).

---

## The Posterior Mean and Shrinkage

From [[Beta Transformations]], $E[\text{Beta}(a,b)] = a/(a+b)$. So:

$$E[p \mid X = k] = \frac{a+k}{a+b+n}$$

In the Billiards case ($a=b=1$):

$$E[p \mid X = k] = \frac{k+1}{n+2}$$

Compare to the MLE $\hat{p}_{\text{MLE}} = k/n$. The Bayesian estimate is _shrunk toward $1/2$_ (the prior mean). As $n \to \infty$, the posterior mean $\to k/n$: the data dominates.

This is **Laplace's rule of succession**: having seen $k$ successes in $n$ trials, the probability the next trial succeeds is $(k+1)/(n+2)$.

> _Classic example: a coin has come up heads every time in $n$ flips. The MLE says $p = 1$. Laplace says $p = (n+1)/(n+2) < 1$. The Bayesian hedge is never 100% certain._

---

## Connection to Order Statistics

From [[Beta Transformations]]: if $U_1, \ldots, U_{n+1} \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Unif}(0,1)$, then $U_{(k+1)} \sim \text{Beta}(k+1, n-k+1)$.

The Billiards posterior **is** an order statistic. Given that $W$ is the $(k+1)$-th order statistic among $n+1$ Uniform draws, its distribution is $\text{Beta}(k+1, n-k+1)$. The two perspectives (Bayesian update and order statistic) are the same thing.

---

## Predictive Distribution

**What is $P(\text{next ball lands left of } p \mid X = k)$?**

Let $Y = \mathbf{1}[\text{next ball left of } p]$. Then:

$$P(Y = 1 \mid X = k) = E[p \mid X = k] = \frac{k+1}{n+2}$$

This is the posterior predictive probability. It's also Laplace's rule again.

## Summary

|Object|Expression|
|---|---|
|Prior|$p \sim \text{Beta}(1,1) = \text{Unif}(0,1)$|
|Likelihood|$X \mid p \sim \text{Bin}(n,p)$|
|Marginal of $X$|$X \sim \text{Unif}{0,1,\ldots,n}$, i.e. $P(X=k) = 1/(n+1)$|
|Posterior|$p \mid X=k \sim \text{Beta}(k+1, n-k+1)$|
|Posterior mean|$(k+1)/(n+2)$|
|Predictive probability|$(k+1)/(n+2)$|

---

## Things to not forget

- The marginal $P(X=k) = 1/(n+1)$ is the cleanest result and best proved by symmetry, not integration.
- The Beta-Binomial conjugacy is the engine. Billiards is just the $a=b=1$ case.
- Posterior = prior + data. Literally: shape params add.
- As $n \to \infty$, the posterior concentrates at the true $p$ â€” Bayesian and frequentist estimates agree in the limit.
