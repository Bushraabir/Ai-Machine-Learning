

> _Convolution is what happens when you add two independent random variables and ask for the resulting distribution. The operation looks intimidating in the continuous case — an integral of a product of two shifted functions — but it's the same idea as counting paths in the discrete case. Learn both. The discrete one builds the intuition._

---

## The Core Question

If $X$ and $Y$ are **independent** random variables, what is the distribution of $Z = X + Y$?

This is one of the most fundamental questions in probability. The answer is the **convolution** of their distributions.

> The independence assumption is load-bearing. Without it, you'd need the full joint distribution of $(X, Y)$, not just the marginals. Convolution is precisely what independence buys you.

---

## Discrete Case First

Let $X$ and $Y$ be independent, taking values in $\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$. Then:

$$ P(Z = k) = P(X + Y = k) = \sum_{j=0}^{k} P(X = j), P(Y = k - j) $$

**Why?** Condition on $X = j$. Given $X = j$, we need $Y = k - j$. Since $X \perp Y$, this is $P(X=j) P(Y = k-j)$. Sum over all valid $j$. That's it.

This is literally **polynomial multiplication**. If you define generating functions $G_X(s) = \sum_k P(X=k)s^k$ and $G_Y(s) = \sum_k P(Y=k)s^k$, then:

$$ G_{X+Y}(s) = G_X(s) \cdot G_Y(s) $$

Convolution in PMF-space = multiplication in generating function space. This is why PGFs are so powerful for sums.

### Example: Sum of Two Dice

Let $X, Y \sim \text{Uniform}{1, \ldots, 6}$ independently. Then $P(Z = k) = \sum_j P(X=j)P(Y=k-j)$. You're counting the number of ways to write $k$ as an ordered pair $(j, k-j)$ with both in ${1,\ldots,6}$. The classic triangular histogram shape comes directly from this count.

## Continuous Case

Let $X$ and $Y$ be independent continuous RVs with PDFs $f_X$ and $f_Y$. The PDF of $Z = X + Y$ is:

$$ f_Z(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_X(x), f_Y(z - x), dx = (f_X * f_Y)(z) $$

This is the **convolution** of $f_X$ and $f_Y$.

### Derivation from First Principles

Don't just accept this. Derive it via the CDF approach.

$$ F_Z(z) = P(X + Y \leq z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P(Y \leq z - x), f_X(x), dx = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} F_Y(z-x), f_X(x), dx $$

where we used independence to write $P(X \in dx, Y \leq z - x) = f_X(x)dx \cdot F_Y(z-x)$.

Now differentiate with respect to $z$ (under the integral, which is justified by dominated convergence in nice cases):

$$ f_Z(z) = \frac{d}{dz} F_Z(z) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} f_Y(z - x), f_X(x), dx $$

Done. $\blacksquare$

> **Geometric interpretation:** $f_Z(z)$ is the "overlap" between $f_X$ and the **reflected-and-shifted** $f_Y$. Fix $z$. Flip $f_Y$ around zero to get $f_Y(-\cdot)$, then shift it right by $z$ to get $f_Y(z - \cdot)$. The integral is the inner product of $f_X$ and this shifted-reflected $f_Y$. As you slide $z$, the overlap changes — that's the shape of the convolution.

## The MGF/CF Method (Almost Always Easier)

For **independent** $X, Y$:

$$ M_{X+Y}(t) = E[e^{t(X+Y)}] = E[e^{tX}] E[e^{tY}] = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t) $$

So if you can identify the product of MGFs as the MGF of a known distribution, you're done — no integral needed.

This works with the **characteristic function** $\phi(t) = E[e^{itX}]$ even when the MGF doesn't exist. The CF always exists.

> **Strategy:** Always try the MGF method first. Only resort to the convolution integral when the MGF approach doesn't close nicely or you specifically need the PDF.

## Key Examples Worked Out

### 1. Poisson + Poisson

Let $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$, $Y \sim \text{Pois}(\mu)$, independent.

**Via MGF:** $M_X(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$, $M_Y(t) = e^{\mu(e^t - 1)}$.

$$ M_{X+Y}(t) = e^{(\lambda + \mu)(e^t - 1)} $$

This is the MGF of $\text{Pois}(\lambda + \mu)$. So $X + Y \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda + \mu)$. $\square$

**Directly via PMF:** $$ P(X + Y = k) = \sum_{j=0}^k \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^j}{j!} \cdot \frac{e^{-\mu}\mu^{k-j}}{(k-j)!} = \frac{e^{-(\lambda+\mu)}}{k!} \sum_{j=0}^k \binom{k}{j} \lambda^j \mu^{k-j} = \frac{e^{-(\lambda+\mu)}(\lambda+\mu)^k}{k!} $$

Binomial theorem at the last step. Beautiful. This is the **reproductive property** of the Poisson.

### 2. Normal + Normal

Let $X \sim N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$, $Y \sim N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$, independent.

**Via MGF:** $M_X(t) = e^{\mu_1 t + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_1^2 t^2}$, similarly for $Y$.

$$ M_{X+Y}(t) = e^{(\mu_1+\mu_2)t + \frac{1}{2}(\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)t^2} $$

This is $N(\mu_1 + \mu_2,\ \sigma_1^2 + \sigma_2^2)$.

> Note: **variances add, not standard deviations**. This is the fact that saves you from a lot of wrong answers. $\text{SD}(X+Y) \neq \text{SD}(X) + \text{SD}(Y)$ in general.

### 3. Gamma + Gamma (same rate)

Let $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$, $Y \sim \text{Gamma}(b, \lambda)$, independent. (Using shape-rate parameterization.)

**Via MGF:** $M_X(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right)^a$ for $t < \lambda$.

$$ M_{X+Y}(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right)^{a+b} $$

So $X + Y \sim \text{Gamma}(a+b, \lambda)$. The rate must match for this to work.

**Corollary:** Since $\text{Exp}(\lambda) = \text{Gamma}(1, \lambda)$, the sum of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Exp}(\lambda)$ RVs is $\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$. This is the waiting time for the $n$-th arrival in a Poisson process.

### 4. Uniform + Uniform (Convolution Integral Forced)

Let $X, Y \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ independently. Here the MGF method works but doesn't simplify to a named distribution, so let's do the integral.

$$ f_Z(z) = \int_0^1 f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(z-x), dx = \int_0^1 \mathbf{1}[0 \leq z-x \leq 1], dx = \int_0^1 \mathbf{1}[z-1 \leq x \leq z], dx $$

We need $x \in [0,1]$ and $x \in [z-1, z]$ simultaneously. There are two cases:

**Case $0 \leq z \leq 1$:** The intersection is $[0, z]$, so $f_Z(z) = z$.

**Case $1 < z \leq 2$:** The intersection is $[z-1, 1]$, so $f_Z(z) = 2 - z$.

Hence: $$ f_Z(z) = \begin{cases} z & 0 \leq z \leq 1 \ 2 - z & 1 < z \leq 2 \ 0 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$

This is the **triangular distribution** on $[0,2]$. Makes complete sense — the convolution of two box functions is a tent. By the CLT, summing more and more uniforms gives a shape that approaches Gaussian.

## Convolution and the Indicator-Function Trick

When the supports are bounded (like Uniform), you often end up integrating an indicator. The key skill is correctly intersecting the indicator constraints.

**Template:** $$ f_Z(z) = \int f_X(x) \cdot \mathbf{1}[z - x \in \text{supp}(Y)], f_Y(z-x), dx $$

The indicator carves out the valid range of $x$. Always draw the constraints on a number line and find the intersection. This is where most errors happen — getting the cases wrong.

## $n$-fold Convolution

For i.i.d. $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with PDF $f$, the PDF of $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ is the $n$-fold convolution:

$$ f_{S_n} = f * f * \cdots * f \quad (n \text{ times}) $$

In Fourier/CF space, this is just $\hat{f}(\xi)^n$ — exponentiation. This is exactly why the CLT is most naturally proved using characteristic functions.

---

## The Fourier Perspective

The **Fourier transform** of $f_Z = f_X * f_Y$ is:

$$ \widehat{f_Z}(\xi) = \widehat{f_X}(\xi) \cdot \widehat{f_Y}(\xi) $$

Convolution in the original domain = **pointwise multiplication** in the Fourier domain. This is the fundamental theorem of convolution, and it's the reason CFs/MGFs are so effective.

The CF of $X+Y$ (independent) is $\phi_X(t) \cdot \phi_Y(t)$ — this is literally the above with $\xi = t$, up to a sign convention. So the MGF factorization trick isn't a cute trick; it's an instance of a deep structural fact.

## When Convolution Fails (or Gets Complicated)

|Situation|Problem|
|---|---|
|$X, Y$ not independent|Need the full joint distribution; can't just convolve marginals|
|Distributions with different supports but weird overlap|The indicator constraints get messy; just be careful|
|Heavy-tailed distributions (Cauchy)|MGF doesn't exist; must use CF|
|$X - Y$ instead of $X + Y$|Convolve $f_X$ with the **reflection** $f_Y(-\cdot)$; i.e., $f_Z(z) = \int f_X(x) f_Y(x-z) dx$|

> For $Z = X - Y$ where $X, Y$ independent: this is the same as $X + (-Y)$, and $-Y$ has PDF $f_{-Y}(y) = f_Y(-y)$. So $f_Z(z) = (f_X * f_{-Y})(z) = \int f_X(x) f_Y(x-z) dx$. The **cross-correlation** rather than autocorrelation.

## Reproductive Properties Summary

These are worth memorizing not as facts but as corollaries of the MGF method:

|Distribution|Parameter|Sum of independent copies|
|---|---|---|
|$\text{Pois}(\lambda_i)$|rate|$\text{Pois}(\sum \lambda_i)$|
|$N(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)$|mean, variance|$N(\sum \mu_i, \sum \sigma_i^2)$|
|$\text{Gamma}(a_i, \lambda)$|shape (same rate!)|$\text{Gamma}(\sum a_i, \lambda)$|
|$\text{Binomial}(n_i, p)$|trials (same $p$!)|$\text{Binomial}(\sum n_i, p)$|
|$\chi^2(k_i)$|df|$\chi^2(\sum k_i)$|
|$\text{Cauchy}$|location, scale|Cauchy (use CF — MGF doesn't exist)|

The constraint "same rate / same $p$" is essential. You can't add $\text{Gamma}(2, 1)$ and $\text{Gamma}(3, 5)$ and get a Gamma without doing real work.
