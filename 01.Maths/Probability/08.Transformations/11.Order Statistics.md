

> _Order statistics are one of those topics where the setup looks annoying — sorting random variables, tracking ranks — and then suddenly everything clicks and you realise the Beta distribution has been lurking here all along. The connection to Beta is not a coincidence; it's the whole story._

---

## 0. Setup

Let $U_1, U_2, \ldots, U_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Uniform}(0,1)$. Sort them:

$$U_{(1)} \leq U_{(2)} \leq \cdots \leq U_{(n)}$$

$U_{(k)}$ is the **$k$-th order statistic** — the $k$-th smallest value. These are no longer independent (obviously: knowing $U_{(1)}$ tells you something about $U_{(2)}$), and they are no longer Uniform.

> _We start with Uniforms because the CDF is trivial: $F(u) = u$. Everything for general continuous RVs follows by the universality of the Uniform (probability integral transform). So Uniform is the right place to build the theory._

---

## 1. The CDF of $U_{(k)}$

**Event:** ${U_{(k)} \leq u}$ means **at least $k$ of the $n$ variables fall in $[0,u]$**.

Each $U_i \leq u$ with probability $u$, independently. So the count $#{i : U_i \leq u} \sim \text{Binomial}(n, u)$.

$$\boxed{F_{U_{(k)}}(u) = P(U_{(k)} \leq u) = \sum_{j=k}^{n} \binom{n}{j} u^j (1-u)^{n-j}}$$

This is $P(\text{Bin}(n,u) \geq k)$ — a tail probability of the Binomial. Useful for computation, but the PDF is cleaner.

---

## 2. The PDF of $U_{(k)}$

Differentiate the CDF. There's a slicker route: use a **heuristic counting argument** that gives the right answer and is perfectly rigorous in the limit.

**Heuristic.** For small $du > 0$, $P(U_{(k)} \in (u, u+du))$ is the probability that:

- exactly $k-1$ of the $n$ variables fall in $[0,u]$,
- exactly $1$ falls in $(u, u+du)$,
- the remaining $n-k$ fall in $(u+du, 1]$.

Multinomial count times the probabilities:

$$\frac{n!}{(k-1)!,1!,(n-k)!} \cdot u^{k-1} \cdot du \cdot (1-u)^{n-k}$$

So:

$$\boxed{f_{U_{(k)}}(u) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!}, u^{k-1}(1-u)^{n-k}, \quad 0 < u < 1}$$

**Recognition:** This is $\text{Beta}(k,, n-k+1)$.

The normalising constant checks out via [[Beta-Gamma Connections]]:

$$B(k,, n-k+1) = \frac{\Gamma(k),\Gamma(n-k+1)}{\Gamma(n+1)} = \frac{(k-1)!,(n-k)!}{n!} \qquad \checkmark$$

> _So $U_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k, n-k+1)$. This is the theorem to remember. The multinomial argument is the proof. Everything else — mean, variance, joint density — flows from here._

---

## 3. Mean and Variance of $U_{(k)}$

Immediate from [[Beta Expectations]]:

$$\mathbb{E}[U_{(k)}] = \frac{k}{n+1}$$

$$\text{Var}(U_{(k)}) = \frac{k(n-k+1)}{(n+1)^2(n+2)}$$

**The mean formula $k/(n+1)$ is beautiful.** The $n$ order statistics divide $[0,1]$ into $n+1$ equal-length intervals _on average_. They are evenly spaced in expectation.

Intuition: by symmetry, the $n+1$ gaps $U_{(1)}, U_{(2)}-U_{(1)}, \ldots, 1-U_{(n)}$ are exchangeable (they are — this is the **spacing** result below), so each has mean $1/(n+1)$, and $\mathbb{E}[U_{(k)}] = k \cdot 1/(n+1)$.

**Variance:** peaks at $k = (n+1)/2$ (middle order statistic is most uncertain), goes to 0 at the extremes as $n \to \infty$ relative to fixed $k$.

---

## 4. Joint Density of All Order Statistics

**Theorem.** The joint PDF of $(U_{(1)}, \ldots, U_{(n)})$ is:

$$f_{U_{(1)},\ldots,U_{(n)}}(u_1,\ldots,u_n) = n!, \quad 0 < u_1 < u_2 < \cdots < u_n < 1$$

**Proof.** There are $n!$ orderings of $(U_1,\ldots,U_n)$. Each ordering is equally likely by symmetry ($U_i$ are i.i.d.). Each ordering corresponds to exactly one point in the simplex ${0 < u_1 < \cdots < u_n < 1}$, which has volume $1/n!$. So the joint density on this simplex must be $n!$ (to integrate to 1). $\blacksquare$

> _This is one of those proofs that feels like cheating but isn't. The symmetry argument is completely rigorous. The $n!$ is just the Jacobian of "undo the sorting."_

Marginalise to recover the single-variable PDF:

$$f_{U_{(k)}}(u_k) = \int \cdots \int n!, du_1 \cdots \hat{du_k} \cdots du_n$$

The inner integrals over $u_1 < \cdots < u_{k-1} < u_k$ contribute $u_k^{k-1}/(k-1)!$ and over $u_k < u_{k+1} < \cdots < u_n < 1$ contribute $(1-u_k)^{n-k}/(n-k)!$. Multiply through to get $f_{U_{(k)}}$ — consistent with §2. ✓

---

## 5. Joint Density of Two Order Statistics

For $j < k$:

$$f_{U_{(j)}, U_{(k)}}(s, t) = \frac{n!}{(j-1)!(k-j-1)!(n-k)!}, s^{j-1}(t-s)^{k-j-1}(1-t)^{n-k}$$

for $0 < s < t < 1$.

**How to read this:** it's a Multinomial coefficient times the probability of:

- $j-1$ points in $[0,s)$: contributes $s^{j-1}$
- exactly one point at $s$ (the $j$-th order stat): contributes $ds$
- $k-j-1$ points in $(s,t)$: contributes $(t-s)^{k-j-1}$
- exactly one point at $t$ (the $k$-th order stat): contributes $dt$
- $n-k$ points in $(t,1]$: contributes $(1-t)^{n-k}$

Same multinomial heuristic as before, now with two "special" positions.

---

## 6. Spacings

Define the **spacings**:

$$D_0 = U_{(1)}, \quad D_k = U_{(k+1)} - U_{(k)} \text{ for } k=1,\ldots,n-1, \quad D_n = 1 - U_{(n)}$$

These are the $n+1$ gaps between consecutive order statistics (including the endpoints).

**Theorem.** $(D_0, D_1, \ldots, D_n) \overset{d}{=} \left(\frac{E_0}{S},\frac{E_1}{S},\ldots,\frac{E_n}{S}\right)$ where $E_0,\ldots,E_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Expo}(1)$ and $S = \sum_{i=0}^n E_i$.

**Corollary (Exchangeability).** The spacings $(D_0,\ldots,D_n)$ are **exchangeable** — any permutation has the same joint distribution. In particular:

$$\mathbb{E}[D_k] = \frac{1}{n+1} \quad \text{for all } k$$

which recovers $\mathbb{E}[U_{(k)}] = k/(n+1)$ immediately.

**Proof sketch of theorem.** Let $E_0,\ldots,E_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Expo}(1)$ and set $S_k = E_0 + \cdots + E_{k-1}$, $S = S_{n+1}$. The normalised partial sums $S_k/S$ have the same distribution as $U_{(k)}$ (this follows from the Poisson process construction: conditional on $n$ arrivals in $[0,1]$, the arrival times are the order statistics of $n$ i.i.d. Uniforms). $\blacksquare$

> _The Exponential/Poisson process interpretation is the deepest way to understand order statistics of the Uniform. Park this and come back to it when we cover Poisson processes._

## 7. General Continuous Distributions

Everything above was for Uniform. For general $X_1,\ldots,X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} F$ (continuous), the $k$-th order statistic $X_{(k)}$ has PDF:

$$f_{X_{(k)}}(x) = \frac{n!}{(k-1)!(n-k)!}, [F(x)]^{k-1}[1-F(x)]^{n-k} f(x)$$

**Proof.** Let $U_i = F(X_i) \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Uniform}(0,1)$ (universality of the Uniform). Then $X_{(k)} = F^{-1}(U_{(k)})$. Apply the change-of-variables: $f_{U_{(k)}}(u) \cdot |du/dx| = f_{U_{(k)}}(F(x)) \cdot f(x)$, and substitute. $\blacksquare$

**Special cases worth remembering:**

|Distribution|$X_{(n)}$ (maximum)|$X_{(1)}$ (minimum)|
|---|---|---|
|$\text{Expo}(\lambda)$|complicated|$\text{Expo}(n\lambda)$|
|$\text{Uniform}(0,1)$|$\text{Beta}(n,1)$|$\text{Beta}(1,n)$|

The minimum of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Expo}(\lambda)$ is $\text{Expo}(n\lambda)$: competing exponentials, the first to fire wins, and the minimum rate is $n\lambda$. This is the memoryless property doing work.

## 8. The Beta–Order Statistic Connection: Full Picture

$$U_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k,, n-k+1)$$

|Parameter|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$a = k$|number of points **at or below** $U_{(k)}$|
|$b = n-k+1$|number of points **at or above** $U_{(k)}$|
|$a + b = n+1$|total "pseudocount" (including $U_{(k)}$ itself)|

The Beta parameters count the points that the $k$-th order statistic separates. This is the combinatorial meaning.

**Bayesian reading.** In the Beta-Binomial model with $\text{Beta}(1,1) = \text{Uniform}$ prior: after observing $k-1$ successes and $n-k$ failures in $n-1$ trials (i.e., $k-1$ points below and $n-k$ points above), the posterior is $\text{Beta}(k, n-k+1)$. The $k$-th order statistic _is_ the Bayesian posterior for the unknown quantile, starting from a Uniform prior.

## 9. Quick Derivations to Have Ready

**Maximum $U_{(n)}$:** $\text{Beta}(n,1)$, PDF $= n u^{n-1}$ on $(0,1)$. Mean $= n/(n+1)$. ✓ (largest of $n$ uniforms, pushed toward 1)

**Minimum $U_{(1)}$:** $\text{Beta}(1,n)$, PDF $= n(1-u)^{n-1}$ on $(0,1)$. Mean $= 1/(n+1)$. ✓

**Median of $2m+1$ Uniforms:** $U_{(m+1)} \sim \text{Beta}(m+1, m+1)$, symmetric about $1/2$. Mean $= 1/2$, variance $= (m+1)^2/[(2m+2)^2(2m+3)] \to 0$ as $m \to \infty$. The sample median concentrates.
