> _This one took me an embarrassingly long time to really sit with. The formula looks like a monster at first. But once you see it as a quadratic form inside an exponential — and you trace back where every piece comes from — it's actually quite beautiful. Don't memorize it. Derive it._

---


## Setup & Motivation

We want a joint distribution for $(X, Y)$ such that:

- Every linear combination $aX + bY$ is normal
- Marginally, $X \sim N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$ and $Y \sim N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$
- The dependence is captured by a single parameter $\rho = \text{Corr}(X, Y)$

This is exactly what the **Bivariate Normal (BVN)** gives us. It is the "right" generalization of the univariate normal to two dimensions, and it will generalize further to the multivariate normal via the covariance matrix.

---

## The Joint PDF

Let $(X, Y)$ be Bivariate Normal with parameters $\mu_1, \mu_2 \in \mathbb{R}$, $\sigma_1, \sigma_2 > 0$, and $\rho \in (-1, 1)$. Then the joint PDF is:

$$ f(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \sqrt{1-\rho^2}} \exp!\left( -\frac{1}{2(1-\rho^2)} \left[ \frac{(x-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_1)(y-\mu_2)}{\sigma_1 \sigma_2} + \frac{(y-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2} \right] \right) $$

> The expression in the exponent is a **quadratic form** in $(x - \mu_1, y - \mu_2)$. This is the key structural insight — everything is governed by the inverse of the covariance matrix $\Sigma$.

---

## Where This Comes From — The Derivation I Actually Like

_Don't just accept the formula. Here's the derivation via the conditional approach, which also gives you the conditional distribution for free._

### Step 1: Start with independent normals

Let $Z_1, Z_2 \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} N(0,1)$. Define:

$$ X = \mu_1 + \sigma_1 Z_1 $$ $$ Y = \mu_2 + \sigma_2(\rho Z_1 + \sqrt{1-\rho^2}, Z_2) $$

**Check the marginals:**

- $X \sim N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$ ✓ (obvious)
- $Y \sim N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2(\rho^2 + (1-\rho^2))) = N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$ ✓

**Check the correlation:** $$ \text{Cov}(X, Y) = \text{Cov}(\sigma_1 Z_1,\ \sigma_2(\rho Z_1 + \sqrt{1-\rho^2}, Z_2)) = \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \rho \cdot \text{Var}(Z_1) = \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \rho $$ $$ \Rightarrow \text{Corr}(X, Y) = \frac{\sigma_1 \sigma_2 \rho}{\sigma_1 \sigma_2} = \rho \quad \checkmark $$

So the construction is consistent. Now derive the joint PDF by deriving the Jacobian of the inverse transformation from $(X, Y)$ to $(Z_1, Z_2)$, and multiplying by the joint PDF of $(Z_1, Z_2)$.

### Step 2: Invert the transformation

$$ Z_1 = \frac{x - \mu_1}{\sigma_1}, \qquad Z_2 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{1-\rho^2}}\left(\frac{y - \mu_2}{\sigma_2} - \rho \cdot \frac{x - \mu_1}{\sigma_1}\right) $$

### Step 3: Compute the Jacobian

The Jacobian of the map $(x,y) \mapsto (z_1, z_2)$ is:

$$ J = \begin{vmatrix} \partial z_1/\partial x & \partial z_1/\partial y \ \partial z_2/\partial x & \partial z_2/\partial y \end{vmatrix} = \begin{vmatrix} 1/\sigma_1 & 0 \ -\rho/(\sigma_1\sqrt{1-\rho^2}) & 1/(\sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho^2}) \end{vmatrix} = \frac{1}{\sigma_1 \sigma_2 \sqrt{1-\rho^2}} $$

### Step 4: Apply change of variables

$$ f_{X,Y}(x,y) = f_{Z_1,Z_2}(z_1, z_2) \cdot |J| $$

Since $Z_1, Z_2$ are independent standard normals:

$$ f_{Z_1, Z_2}(z_1, z_2) = \frac{1}{2\pi} e^{-(z_1^2 + z_2^2)/2} $$

Now substitute $z_1, z_2$ back in terms of $x, y$. The exponent becomes:

$$ z_1^2 + z_2^2 = \frac{(x-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} + \frac{1}{1-\rho^2}\left(\frac{y-\mu_2}{\sigma_2} - \rho \frac{x-\mu_1}{\sigma_1}\right)^2 $$

Expand the second term:

$$ = \frac{(x-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} + \frac{1}{1-\rho^2}\left[\frac{(y-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2} - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_1)(y-\mu_2)}{\sigma_1\sigma_2} + \frac{\rho^2(x-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2}\right] $$

$$ = \frac{1}{1-\rho^2}\left[\frac{(x-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2}(1-\rho^2+\rho^2) - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_1)(y-\mu_2)}{\sigma_1\sigma_2} + \frac{(y-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2}\right] $$

$$ = \frac{1}{1-\rho^2}\left[\frac{(x-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_1)(y-\mu_2)}{\sigma_1\sigma_2} + \frac{(y-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2}\right] $$

Multiply everything together, and you get exactly the joint PDF stated above. $\blacksquare$

## The Matrix Form (Preview of Multivariate Normal)

Let $\mathbf{x} = (x, y)^\top$, $\boldsymbol{\mu} = (\mu_1, \mu_2)^\top$, and define the **covariance matrix**:

$$ \Sigma = \begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1^2 & \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 \ \rho \sigma_1 \sigma_2 & \sigma_2^2 \end{pmatrix} $$

Then: $$ \det(\Sigma) = \sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 - \rho^2 \sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2 = \sigma_1^2 \sigma_2^2(1-\rho^2) $$

$$ \Sigma^{-1} = \frac{1}{\sigma_1^2\sigma_2^2(1-\rho^2)}\begin{pmatrix}\sigma_2^2 & -\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 \ -\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 & \sigma_1^2\end{pmatrix} $$

The exponent in the BVN PDF is precisely: $$ -\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu})^\top \Sigma^{-1} (\mathbf{x} - \boldsymbol{\mu}) $$

And the normalizing constant $\frac{1}{2\pi\sigma_1\sigma_2\sqrt{1-\rho^2}} = \frac{1}{(2\pi)^{2/2}\sqrt{\det(\Sigma)}} = \frac{1}{(2\pi)|\Sigma|^{1/2}}$.

So the BVN PDF in full matrix form is: $$ f(\mathbf{x}) = \frac{1}{(2\pi)|\Sigma|^{1/2}} \exp!\left(-\frac{1}{2}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})^\top\Sigma^{-1}(\mathbf{x}-\boldsymbol{\mu})\right) $$

> This is _exactly_ the multivariate normal PDF. The BVN is just the $n=2$ case. Every piece — the determinant, the inverse, the quadratic form — generalizes perfectly.

## Key Properties (Proven, Not Just Listed)

### 1. Marginals are Normal

$$ X \sim N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2), \quad Y \sim N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2) $$

_Proof:_ Integrate out $y$ from the joint PDF, or note it directly from the construction in Step 1.

> ⚠️ **Converse fails.** Marginals can each be normal without the joint being bivariate normal. You need to add the condition that every linear combination is normal (or equivalently, that the joint has the BVN form). This is a classic mistake — don't make it.

### 2. Conditional Distribution

$$ Y \mid X = x \sim N!\left(\mu_2 + \rho\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}(x - \mu_1),\ \sigma_2^2(1-\rho^2)\right) $$

_Proof:_ Write $f(y \mid x) = f(x,y)/f_X(x)$. The calculation is just algebra with the exponent. The conditional mean is **linear in $x$** — this is the key fact underlying linear regression.

The conditional variance $\sigma_2^2(1-\rho^2)$ is constant (doesn't depend on $x$). This is **homoskedasticity** built into the model.

> The conditional mean $E[Y | X=x] = \mu_2 + \rho\frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}(x - \mu_1)$ is exactly the **linear regression line** of $Y$ on $X$. This is not a coincidence — it's the reason why the bivariate normal is the natural probability model behind OLS.

### 3. Uncorrelated $\Leftrightarrow$ Independent (BVN only!)

If $(X,Y)$ is BVN, then: $$ \rho = 0 \iff X \perp Y $$

_Proof:_ If $\rho = 0$, the joint PDF factors as $f_X(x) \cdot f_Y(y)$. Independence implies uncorrelatedness always; the other direction holds **only** under joint normality.

### 4. Linear combinations are Normal

For any $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$: $$ aX + bY \sim N(a\mu_1 + b\mu_2,\ a^2\sigma_1^2 + 2ab\rho\sigma_1\sigma_2 + b^2\sigma_2^2) $$

_Proof:_ Use the construction from Step 1. $aX + bY$ is a linear combination of $Z_1, Z_2$, which are independent normals.

## The Shape: Level Sets

The set ${(x,y) : f(x,y) = c}$ for some constant $c$ is given by:

$$ \frac{(x-\mu_1)^2}{\sigma_1^2} - \frac{2\rho(x-\mu_1)(y-\mu_2)}{\sigma_1\sigma_2} + \frac{(y-\mu_2)^2}{\sigma_2^2} = \text{const} $$

This is an **ellipse** centered at $(\mu_1, \mu_2)$, rotated when $\rho \neq 0$. The axes of the ellipse are the eigenvectors of $\Sigma$.

- When $\rho = 0$: the ellipse aligns with the coordinate axes (axes = $x$-axis and $y$-axis)
- When $\rho > 0$: the ellipse tilts toward the first/third quadrant
- As $\rho \to \pm 1$: the ellipse degenerates to a line — the distribution becomes singular

---

## Edge Cases and Warnings

| Case                                 | What happens                                                                                                                                    |
| ------------------------------------ | ----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| $\rho = 0$                           | $X, Y$ independent. PDF factors.                                                                                                                |
| $\rho = \pm 1$                       | $\det(\Sigma) = 0$. **No density exists.** $Y = \mu_2 \pm \frac{\sigma_2}{\sigma_1}(X - \mu_1)$ a.s. — the distribution is supported on a line. |
| $\sigma_1 \to 0$ or $\sigma_2 \to 0$ | Same degeneracy as above.                                                                                                                       |

> When $|\rho| = 1$, the BVN is **degenerate** — we need to work with the distribution purely through characteristic functions or the multivariate normal framework. The PDF doesn't exist in the classical sense.
