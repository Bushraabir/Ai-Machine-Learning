You're sitting in a courtroom. The prosecution presents DNA evidence:

> "The probability of a random innocent person matching this DNA profile is 1 in a million."

The defense attorney stands up and says:

> "There are 8 million people in this city. That means roughly 8 people share this DNA. My client is just one of those 8. The probability he's guilty given the match is only 1 in 8 — that's just 12.5%! Reasonable doubt!"

**Sounds reasonable. It is wrong. Here's why.**

## The Formal Setup

Let me define the events cleanly:

$$ M = \text{"the defendant's DNA matches the sample"} $$ $$ G = \text{"the defendant is guilty"} $$

The prosecutor told us: $$ P(M \mid G^c) = 10^{-6} $$

What we _actually_ care about — what the jury needs — is: $$ P(G \mid M) = ;? $$

These are **not** the same thing. Confusing them is the **Prosecutor's Fallacy** (its own entry). But the defense attorney is making a different — and subtler — mistake.

---

## The Defense Attorney's Error

The defense attorney's argument implicitly computes:

$$ P(G \mid M) = \frac{1}{8} $$

by reasoning: "8 people match, so each has equal probability $\frac{1}{8}$."

This would be valid **only if** the prior probability of guilt is uniform over all 8 million people, i.e., if the defendant was chosen **uniformly at random** from the city with no other evidence.

But that's not what happened.

The defendant wasn't plucked randomly from the population. He was **already a suspect** — perhaps identified through witnesses, security footage, motive, opportunity. The prior $P(G)$ is **not** $\frac{1}{8{,}000{,}000}$.

---

## The Correct Framework — Bayes' Theorem

Let's use Bayes properly. By LOTP (Law of Total Probability):

$$ P(G \mid M) = \frac{P(M \mid G), P(G)}{P(M \mid G), P(G) + P(M \mid G^c), P(G^c)} $$

Now plug in what we know:

- $P(M \mid G) = 1$ — if guilty, their DNA certainly matches
- $P(M \mid G^c) = 10^{-6}$ — given innocent, the chance of matching is 1 in a million
- $P(G)$ — **this is the key variable the defense attorney ignores**

Let's see what happens under different priors:

### Scenario A: Weak Prior — Defendant was selected nearly randomly

$$ P(G) = \frac{1}{8{,}000{,}000} $$

$$ P(G \mid M) = \frac{1 \cdot \frac{1}{8{,}000{,}000}}{1 \cdot \frac{1}{8{,}000{,}000} + 10^{-6} \cdot \frac{8{,}000{,}000 - 1}{8{,}000{,}000}} $$

$$ \approx \frac{10^{-6}/8}{10^{-6}/8 + 10^{-6}} = \frac{1/8}{1/8 + 1} = \frac{1}{9} \approx 11 $$

Here the defense attorney's estimate is roughly right — **but only because the prior is uniform**, which is an unrealistic assumption.

### Scenario B: Strong Prior — Defendant was identified via other evidence

Suppose $P(G) = 0.6$ before the DNA match (based on witness testimony, security footage, etc.)

$$ P(G \mid M) = \frac{1 \cdot 0.6}{1 \cdot 0.6 + 10^{-6} \cdot 0.4} = \frac{0.6}{0.6 + 0.0000004} \approx 0.9999993 $$

The DNA match, **on top of other evidence**, makes guilt nearly certain. The defense attorney's "1 in 8" argument evaporates completely.

---

## What the Fallacy Actually Is

The defense attorney's fallacy is the error of **ignoring the prior** — treating $P(G \mid M)$ as if the defendant were a randomly selected member of the population, when in fact the act of being a _defendant_ already carries significant prior information.

More precisely:

> **The defense attorney implicitly assumes $P(G) = P(\text{random person is guilty})$ when computing $P(G \mid M)$, ignoring all evidence that led to the suspect being identified in the first place.**

This is a failure to respect the **full posterior**, not just the likelihood ratio.

---

## The Symmetry with the Prosecutor's Fallacy

These two fallacies form a pair — each is the mirror image of the other:

| Fallacy                | The Error                                                    | Direction of Bias |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ | ----------------- |
| **Prosecutor's**       | $P(M \mid G^c)$ is small, therefore $P(G^c \mid M)$ is small | Overstates guilt  |
| **Defense Attorney's** | Ignores prior; treats match as one-of-many in population     | Understates guilt |

The prosecutor transposes the conditional. The defense attorney ignores what made this person a defendant.

Neither is doing Bayes correctly.

---

## A Cleaner Way to See It — Likelihood Ratios

The Bayes factor (likelihood ratio) approach is often cleaner for courtroom reasoning:

$$ \underbrace{\frac{P(G \mid M)}{P(G^c \mid M)}}_{\text{posterior odds}} = \underbrace{\frac{P(M \mid G)}{P(M \mid G^c)}}_{\text{likelihood ratio}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(G)}{P(G^c)}}_{\text{prior odds}} $$

The DNA evidence gives a likelihood ratio of:

$$ \frac{1}{10^{-6}} = 1{,}000{,}000 $$

So the DNA evidence **multiplies the prior odds by one million**. Whether that makes the posterior odds compelling depends entirely on what the prior odds were.

The defense attorney wants you to use prior odds of $\frac{1}{8{,}000{,}000}$, giving posterior odds of about $\frac{1}{8}$. The prosecutor might reasonably argue for a much higher prior based on other evidence, making the posterior odds enormous.

**The DNA evidence itself doesn't determine guilt — it updates whatever prior the jury should already have.**

---

## The Deeper Lesson

This fallacy appears everywhere outside courtrooms:

- **Medical testing**: "Only 1 in 10,000 people have this disease, so even with a positive test result, the patient is probably fine" — this ignores what made the doctor order the test.
- **Rare event detection**: Treating a rare match as uninformative by comparing to the population, when the prior for the specific case is much stronger.

The key takeaway from Blitzstein's framing (STAT 110, Lecture 5-ish):

> _Conditional probabilities are not symmetric. The event you condition on matters enormously. Before you flip $P(A \mid B)$ to $P(B \mid A)$, ask yourself whether you have any justification whatsoever for that transposition._

---

## Summary:
> The defense attorney's fallacy uses a population-level base rate as the prior for a specific defendant, ignoring all evidence that elevated that individual to suspect status in the first place.
