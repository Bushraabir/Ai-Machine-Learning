— the moment you realize that a trend in every subgroup can reverse when you combine them, and that "controlling for a variable" is not always the right thing to do_


---

## The Paradox, Stated

**Simpson's Paradox:** A trend or association that appears in several subgroups of data can _disappear or reverse_ when the subgroups are combined into a single aggregate.

This is not a statistical error. It is not a rounding artifact. It is a mathematically exact phenomenon, and it happens because the subgroups are not sampled in equal proportions.

---

## A Concrete Example — The Classic Drug Trial

Two treatments for kidney stones: **Treatment A** and **Treatment B**.

### Small stones:

||Recovered|Total|Success rate|
|---|---|---|---|
|Treatment A|81|87|**93%**|
|Treatment B|234|270|**87%**|

A beats B for small stones.

### Large stones:

||Recovered|Total|Success rate|
|---|---|---|---|
|Treatment A|192|263|**73%**|
|Treatment B|55|80|**69%**|

A beats B for large stones.

### Combined (ignoring stone size):

||Recovered|Total|Success rate|
|---|---|---|---|
|Treatment A|273|350|**78%**|
|Treatment B|289|350|**83%**|

**B beats A overall.**

A is better for small stones. A is better for large stones. B is better overall. This is not a trick — these are real numbers from a real 1986 medical study (Charig et al.).

---

## Why Does This Happen? — The Weighted Average Argument

The key: **the mixing proportions differ between groups.**

Treatment A was predominantly given to patients with _large_ stones (the harder cases) — 263 out of 350. Treatment B was predominantly given to patients with _small_ stones (the easier cases) — 270 out of 350.

The overall success rate is a **weighted average** of the subgroup rates, where the weights are the group sizes:

$$P(\text{success} \mid A) = P(\text{success} \mid A, \text{small}) \cdot P(\text{small} \mid A) + P(\text{success} \mid A, \text{large}) \cdot P(\text{large} \mid A)$$

$$= 0.93 \cdot \frac{87}{350} + 0.73 \cdot \frac{263}{350} = 0.93 \cdot 0.249 + 0.73 \cdot 0.751 \approx 0.78$$

$$P(\text{success} \mid B) = 0.87 \cdot \frac{270}{350} + 0.69 \cdot \frac{80}{350} = 0.87 \cdot 0.771 + 0.69 \cdot 0.229 \approx 0.83$$

Treatment A is dragged down by being applied to harder cases. Treatment B is buoyed by being applied to easier cases. The aggregate hides this entirely.

> This is just **LOTP in action** — total probability as a weighted average over a partition. The paradox is what happens when those weights differ systematically between the two groups being compared.

---

## The Mathematical Skeleton

Strip away the story. The paradox says: it is possible to have

$$\frac{a_1}{b_1} > \frac{c_1}{d_1} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{a_2}{b_2} > \frac{c_2}{d_2}$$

and yet

$$\frac{a_1 + a_2}{b_1 + b_2} < \frac{c_1 + c_2}{d_1 + d_2}$$

A minimal numerical example:

$$\frac{1}{2} > \frac{0}{1} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{3}{5} > \frac{3}{10}$$

but

$$\frac{1+3}{2+5} = \frac{4}{7} \approx 0.571 < \frac{0+3}{1+10} = \frac{3}{11} \approx 0.273$$

Wait — let me construct it properly.

$$\frac{3}{4} > \frac{1}{2} \quad \text{and} \quad \frac{1}{5} > \frac{0}{4}$$

but

$$\frac{3+1}{4+5} = \frac{4}{9} \approx 0.44 < \frac{1+0}{2+4} = \frac{1}{6} \approx 0.17$$

Hmm, that doesn't flip. The point is that the flip requires the group with the lower rate to have much larger weight in one arm. Let me just keep the kidney stone numbers — they are the cleanest real example.

The algebraic condition for the reversal: let $w_A^S = P(\text{small} \mid A)$ and $w_B^S = P(\text{small} \mid B)$. The reversal occurs when $w_B^S \gg w_A^S$ — i.e., when the group with the higher subgroup rate (small stones, where both treatments do well) is heavily overrepresented in arm B. The confound tilts the aggregate.

## The Causal Lens — When Should You Condition?

This is where Simpson's Paradox stops being just a math curiosity and becomes a question about **causal inference**.

There are two different questions:

1. **"Which treatment has a better success rate in this dataset?"** — descriptive/associational
2. **"Which treatment should I prescribe to a new patient?"** — causal/interventional

For question 2, the answer is **Treatment A**, despite its lower aggregate rate. Here's why: stone size is a **confounder** — it affects both which treatment doctors chose to give and the probability of recovery. When you condition on stone size, you remove the confounding and see the true treatment effect.

The aggregate rate mixes two effects:

- The effect of treatment on recovery _(what we want)_
- The effect of doctor selection bias on who gets which treatment _(noise we don't want)_

Conditioning on stone size blocks the back-door path: $\text{Treatment} \leftarrow \text{Stone Size} \rightarrow \text{Recovery}$. The subgroup analysis is causally correct. The aggregate is causally misleading.

> **Pearl's language:** The aggregate comparison is an _associational_ quantity, $P(\text{recovery} \mid \text{treatment} = A)$. What we actually want for a decision is the _interventional_ quantity, $P(\text{recovery} \mid \text{do}(\text{treatment} = A))$. These coincide only when there is no confounding. Simpson's Paradox is precisely the failure of the associational quantity to track the interventional one.

---

## But Sometimes You Should NOT Condition — The Reversal Trap

Here is the dangerous flip side. Not every variable you could condition on is a confounder. Sometimes conditioning _creates_ a spurious reversal rather than fixing one.

### Example: Collider Bias

Suppose you study the relationship between talent and hard work among professional athletes. You find that among pros, talent and hard work are **negatively correlated** — the most talented athletes tend to work out less.

Does this mean talent makes you lazy?

No. The population of professional athletes is a **selected subset** — you've conditioned on "made it to the pros," which is a **collider**: both talent and hard work independently cause someone to become a pro. Conditioning on a collider induces a spurious negative correlation between its causes, even if talent and hard work are independent in the general population.

The causal graph:

$$\text{Talent} \rightarrow \text{Pro} \leftarrow \text{Hard Work}$$

Conditioning on $\text{Pro}$ opens a path between Talent and Hard Work that didn't exist before. This is the **Berkson's Paradox** — conditioning on a collider introduces association between independent causes.

> **The lesson:** Whether to condition on a variable is a _causal_ question, not a statistical one. Conditioning on a confounder (like stone size) fixes Simpson's Paradox. Conditioning on a collider (like "being a pro") creates a new spurious association. The data alone cannot tell you which is which — you need a causal model (a DAG).

---

## The LOTP Connection

Every instance of Simpson's Paradox is just LOTP with adversarially chosen weights.

$$P(B \mid A) = \sum_i P(B \mid A, C_i) P(C_i \mid A)$$ $$P(B \mid A^c) = \sum_i P(B \mid A^c, C_i) P(C_i \mid A^c)$$

It is entirely possible that:

$$P(B \mid A, C_i) > P(B \mid A^c, C_i) \quad \forall i$$

and yet:

$$P(B \mid A) < P(B \mid A^c)$$

if the weights $P(C_i \mid A)$ and $P(C_i \mid A^c)$ differ enough in the wrong direction.

The aggregate comparison $P(B \mid A)$ vs $P(B \mid A^c)$ is a _weighted average_ of the conditional comparisons, and the weights depend on the _conditional distribution of $C$ given $A$_ — which differs between the two arms. That's the whole mechanism, full stop.

## Another Classic Example — UC Berkeley Admissions (1973)

UC Berkeley was sued for gender bias in graduate admissions. The aggregate data showed men were admitted at a higher rate than women.

||Applied|Admitted|Rate|
|---|---|---|---|
|Men|8442|3738|**44%**|
|Women|4321|1494|**35%**|

Looks like bias. But when broken down by department, most individual departments showed either no bias or a _slight bias in favor of women_.

What happened? Women applied disproportionately to **more competitive departments** (English, History) with low admission rates for everyone. Men applied more to **less competitive departments** (Engineering, Chemistry) with high admission rates. The aggregate masked completely different application patterns.

The confound: **department choice**, which is correlated with both gender and admission probability.

Here the causal question is subtle — is department choice itself affected by gender discrimination (e.g., women steered away from engineering)? If so, conditioning on department might _over-correct_. This is exactly the Pearl point: the right answer depends on the causal graph, not just the numbers.

---

## How to Detect It in Practice

Simpson's Paradox lurks whenever:

1. You have a **confounding variable** $C$ that is correlated with both the treatment $A$ and the outcome $B$
2. The distribution of $C$ differs between the groups being compared
3. You are looking at an aggregate comparison without conditioning on $C$

Red flags in data:

- Unequal group sizes across subgroups
- Known or suspected confounders that weren't controlled for
- Results that seem "too good" or "too bad" in one direction

---

## Summary of the Key Distinctions

|Situation|Variable type|Should you condition?|
|---|---|---|
|Stone size in drug trial|**Confounder** (causes both treatment assignment and outcome)|**Yes** — conditioning removes bias|
|"Being a pro" in talent/work study|**Collider** (caused by both variables)|**No** — conditioning introduces spurious association|
|Department in Berkeley|**Mediator or confounder?**|**Depends on the causal graph**|

The statistics cannot answer this question. The causal graph can.


---

_What I keep coming back to: Simpson's Paradox is not really a paradox. It's a reminder that a conditional probability $P(B \mid A)$ is not the same as a causal effect of $A$ on $B$. The aggregate comparison conflates the effect of the treatment with the effect of whatever determined who received the treatment. Every time someone shows you a statistic without telling you how the groups were formed, Simpson's Paradox is waiting in the wings._
