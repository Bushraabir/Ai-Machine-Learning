
> _I keep coming back to this example because it's the one that made Bayes feel visceral rather than mechanical. The arithmetic is elementary. What's not elementary is accepting what the arithmetic says._

---

## The Setup

You test positive for a rare disease. Your doctor tells you the test is **99% accurate**. You spiral. But wait — what does "99% accurate" even mean? And more importantly: _what is the actual probability you have the disease?_

Let's be precise. Define:

- $D$ = event that you **have** the disease
- $T^+$ = event that you **test positive**

We're given: $$P(T^+ \mid D) = 0.99 \qquad \text{(sensitivity — true positive rate)}$$ $$P(T^- \mid D^c) = 0.99 \qquad \text{(specificity — true negative rate)}$$

So equivalently, $P(T^+ \mid D^c) = 0.01$ — the false positive rate.

Now the crucial ingredient most people forget to ask about: $$P(D) = 0.001 \qquad \text{(the disease affects 1 in 1000 people)}$$

**Question:** Given a positive test, what is $P(D \mid T^+)$?

---

## The Calculation via Bayes' Theorem

By **Bayes' Theorem**:

$$P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{P(T^+ \mid D), P(D)}{P(T^+)}$$

We expand the denominator using the **Law of Total Probability** (LOTP), partitioning on $D$ and $D^c$:

$$P(T^+) = P(T^+ \mid D),P(D) + P(T^+ \mid D^c),P(D^c)$$

Plugging in: $$P(T^+) = (0.99)(0.001) + (0.01)(0.999)$$ $$= 0.00099 + 0.00999 = 0.01098$$

Therefore: $$P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{(0.99)(0.001)}{0.01098} = \frac{0.00099}{0.01098} \approx \boxed{0.0902}$$

**About 9%.** Not 99%. Not even close.

---

## Why This Is So Disorienting

The intuitive error is conflating $P(T^+ \mid D)$ with $P(D \mid T^+)$. This is sometimes called the **confusion of the inverse** or, in legal contexts, the **prosecutor's fallacy**.

Think about what's happening in the denominator. The false positive rate is 1%, and the disease prevalence is 0.1%. For every true positive (a sick person who tests positive), there are roughly **ten false positives** (healthy people who also test positive):

$$\frac{P(T^+ \mid D^c), P(D^c)}{P(T^+ \mid D), P(D)} = \frac{(0.01)(0.999)}{(0.99)(0.001)} \approx \frac{0.00999}{0.00099} \approx 10.09$$

The disease is rare enough that the false positive pool swamps the true positive pool. The test is excellent, but **rarity wins**.

---

## The Natural Frequency Reframe

Blitzstein and Hwang (and Gigerenzen before them) suggest thinking in **natural frequencies** instead of probabilities. It makes the arithmetic transparent:

Imagine **100,000 people** tested:

||Has Disease ($D$)|No Disease ($D^c$)|Total|
|---|---|---|---|
|Tests $+$|$99$|$999$|$1098$|
|Tests $-$|$1$|$98901$|$98902$|
|**Total**|**100**|**99,900**|**100,000**|

Of the 1098 who test positive, only 99 actually have the disease: $$P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{99}{1098} \approx 9.02 $$

Same answer. But now you can _see_ it. The 999 false positives are right there in the table, outnumbering the true positives.

> _I find this table extraordinarily clarifying. Every time I'm confused by a conditional probability calculation, I ask: can I draw the $2 \times 2$ table? If yes, do that first._

## The General Principle: Bayes' Theorem

For completeness, the general form. Let $H$ be a hypothesis and $E$ be evidence:

$$\boxed{P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid H), P(H)}{P(E)}}$$

With LOTP expansion of $P(E)$ over a partition ${H_1, H_2, \ldots, H_n}$:

$$P(E) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(E \mid H_i), P(H_i)$$

The terms have names worth memorizing:

| Term          | Name                    | Meaning                                     |
| ------------- | ----------------------- | ------------------------------------------- |
| $P(H)$        | **Prior**               | Belief in $H$ before seeing evidence        |
| $P(E \mid H)$ | **Likelihood**          | How probable the evidence is if $H$ is true |
| $P(E)$        | **Marginal likelihood** | Normalizing constant                        |
| $P(H \mid E)$ | **Posterior**           | Updated belief after seeing evidence        |

Bayes is literally just the definition of conditional probability rearranged: $$P(H \mid E) = \frac{P(H \cap E)}{P(E)} = \frac{P(E \mid H), P(H)}{P(E)}$$

The magic is entirely in how you use it.

---

## Odds Form of Bayes (The Elegant Version)

There's a slicker way to state this. Define the **prior odds** and **posterior odds**:

$$\underbrace{\frac{P(D \mid T^+)}{P(D^c \mid T^+)}}_{\text{posterior odds}} = \underbrace{\frac{P(T^+ \mid D)}{P(T^+ \mid D^c)}}_{\text{likelihood ratio (Bayes factor)}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(D)}{P(D^c)}}_{\text{prior odds}}$$

In our example: $$\text{prior odds} = \frac{0.001}{0.999} \approx \frac{1}{999}$$ $$\text{likelihood ratio} = \frac{0.99}{0.01} = 99$$ $$\text{posterior odds} = \frac{99}{999} \approx \frac{1}{10.09}$$

Posterior probability: $\frac{1/10.09}{1 + 1/10.09} \approx 0.09$. ✓

The **Bayes factor** of 99 is saying: seeing a positive test makes the disease hypothesis 99 times more likely _relative to what it was_. Starting from 1:999 odds, that gets you to about 1:10 — still pretty unlikely.

> _The odds form is underrated. Once you internalize it, you can update probabilities in your head just by tracking how much each piece of evidence multiplies the odds. Sequential Bayesian updating becomes trivial._

## Connection to Stat 110

In lecture, Blitzstein frames this with **prior** and **posterior** as the central vocabulary. The key move is always:

1. Identify what you _want_ ($P(H \mid E)$)
2. Identify what you _know_ ($P(E \mid H)$, $P(H)$)
3. Apply Bayes + LOTP

He also connects this to the **symmetry of the joint distribution**: $P(D, T^+) = P(T^+ \mid D) P(D) = P(D \mid T^+) P(T^+)$. Bayes is just reading this equation in the other direction.

---

## What This Means in Practice

The lesson isn't "tests are useless." It's that **a positive test should change your probability, not determine it**. In our case:

$$P(D) = 0.001 \xrightarrow{\text{positive test}} P(D \mid T^+) \approx 0.09$$

That's a **90-fold increase** in probability. Significant! But the base rate was so low that even a 90-fold increase doesn't get us past 10%.

Actionable conclusion: **Order a second test.** Now your prior is $P(D) \approx 0.09$, and if that also comes back positive:

$$P(D \mid T^+_1, T^+_2) \approx \frac{(0.99)(0.09)}{(0.99)(0.09) + (0.01)(0.91)} \approx \frac{0.0891}{0.0982} \approx 0.908$$

Now we're talking. Two positives pushes us past 90%.

## A Personal Note on Intuition

> _The thing that tripped me up initially: I kept thinking the test's accuracy "should" dominate. It feels like 99% should mean something closer to 99%. But probability doesn't care about what feels right — it cares about the full joint distribution. The prior isn't a nuisance parameter. It's load-bearing._
> 
> _After enough examples like this, I've started thinking of Bayes as a machine that converts between two "views" of the same joint probability — you can enter from the $P(E \mid H)$ door or the $P(H \mid E)$ door, and the machine gets you from one to the other. LOTP handles the normalization. That's all there is to it._
