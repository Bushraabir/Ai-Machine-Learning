> [!quote] You test positive for a rare disease. Your doctor tells you the test is **99% accurate**. You spiral. But wait — what does "99% accurate" even mean? And more importantly: _what is the actual probability you have the disease?_

#  Bayes' Theorem & Medical Testing

> _When intuition fails, probability speaks._

---
![[Pasted image 20260402142237.png]]
## Setting Up the Problem

Let's be precise. Define:

- $D$ = event that you **have** the disease
- $T^+$ = event that you **test positive**

$$P(T^+ \mid D) = 0.99 \qquad \text{(sensitivity — true positive rate)}$$ $$P(T^- \mid D^c) = 0.99 \qquad \text{(specificity — true negative rate)}$$

So equivalently, $P(T^+ \mid D^c) = 0.01$ — the **false positive rate**.

Now the crucial ingredient most people forget to ask about:

$$P(D) = 0.001 \qquad \text{(the disease affects 1 in 1000 people)}$$

> [!question] **The Central Question** Given a positive test, what is $P(D \mid T^+)$?

---

## Calculation via Bayes' Theorem

By **Bayes' Theorem**:

$$P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{P(T^+ \mid D), P(D)}{P(T^+)}$$

We expand the denominator using the **Law of Total Probability (LOTP)**, partitioning on $D$ and $D^c$:

$$P(T^+) = P(T^+ \mid D),P(D) + P(T^+ \mid D^c),P(D^c)$$

Plugging in:

$$P(T^+) = (0.99)(0.001) + (0.01)(0.999)$$ $$= 0.00099 + 0.00999 = 0.01098$$

Therefore:

$$P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{(0.99)(0.001)}{0.01098} = \frac{0.00099}{0.01098} \approx \boxed{0.0902}$$

> [!success] **About 9%. Not 99%. Not even close.**

---

## Why This Is So Disorienting

The intuitive error is conflating $P(T^+ \mid D)$ with $P(D \mid T^+)$. This is sometimes called the **confusion of the inverse** or, in legal contexts, the **prosecutor's fallacy**.

Think about what's happening in the denominator. The false positive rate is 1%, and the disease prevalence is 0.1%. For every true positive (a sick person who tests positive), there are roughly **ten false positives** (healthy people who also test positive):

$$\frac{P(T^+ \mid D^c), P(D^c)}{P(T^+ \mid D), P(D)} = \frac{(0.01)(0.999)}{(0.99)(0.001)} \approx \frac{0.00999}{0.00099} \approx 10.09$$

> The disease is rare enough that the false positive pool **swamps** the true positive pool. The test is excellent, but **rarity wins**.

---

## The Natural Frequency Reframe

Blitzstein and Hwang (and Gigerenzen before them) suggest thinking in **natural frequencies** instead of probabilities. It makes the arithmetic transparent.

Imagine **100,000 people** tested:

|             | Has Disease $D$ | No Disease $D^c$ | Total       |
| ----------- | --------------- | ---------------- | ----------- |
| **Tests +** | 99              | 999              | 1,098       |
| **Tests -** | 1               | 98,901           | 98,902      |
| **Total**   | **100**         | **99,900**       | **100,000** |
Of the $1{,}098$ who test positive, only $99$ actually have the disease:

$$P(D \mid T^+) = \frac{99}{1098} \approx 9.02$$

Same answer. But now you can _see_ it. The **999 false positives** are right there in the table, outnumbering the true positives.

> [!tip] _I find this table extraordinarily clarifying. Every time I'm confused by a conditional probability calculation, I ask: can I draw the $2 \times 2$ table? If yes, do that first._


---

##  Odds Form of Bayes (The Elegant Version)

There's a slicker way to state this. Define the **prior odds** and **posterior odds**:

$$\underbrace{\frac{P(D \mid T^+)}{P(D^c \mid T^+)}}_{\text{posterior odds}} = \underbrace{\frac{P(T^+ \mid D)}{P(T^+ \mid D^c)}}_{\text{likelihood ratio (Bayes factor)}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(D)}{P(D^c)}}_{\text{prior odds}}$$

In our example:

$$\text{prior odds} = \frac{0.001}{0.999} \approx \frac{1}{999}$$ $$\text{likelihood ratio} = \frac{0.99}{0.01} = 99$$ $$\text{posterior odds} = \frac{99}{999} \approx \frac{1}{10.09}$$

Posterior probability: $\dfrac{1/10.09}{1 + 1/10.09} \approx 0.09$ ✓

The **Bayes factor** of $99$ is saying: seeing a positive test makes the disease hypothesis $99\times$ more likely _relative to what it was_. Starting from $1{:}999$ odds, that gets you to about $1{:}10$ — still pretty unlikely.

> [!tip] _The odds form is underrated. Once you internalize it, you can update probabilities in your head just by tracking how much each piece of evidence multiplies the odds. Sequential Bayesian updating becomes trivial._

---

## What This Means in Practice

The lesson isn't "tests are useless." It's that **a positive test should change your probability, not determine it**. In our case:

$$P(D) = 0.001 \xrightarrow{\text{positive test}} P(D \mid T^+) \approx 0.09$$

That's a **90-fold increase** in probability. Significant! But the base rate was so low that even a 90-fold increase doesn't get us past 10%.

> [!example] **Actionable Conclusion: Order a Second Test** Now your prior is $P(D) \approx 0.09$, and if that also comes back positive:
> 
> $$P(D \mid T^+_1, T^+_2) \approx \frac{(0.99)(0.09)}{(0.99)(0.09) + (0.01)(0.91)} \approx \frac{0.0891}{0.0982} \approx 0.908$$
> 
> Now we're talking. **Two positives pushes us past 90%.**

But probability doesn't care about what feels right — it cares about the full joint distribution. The prior isn't a nuisance parameter. **It's load-bearing.**_
