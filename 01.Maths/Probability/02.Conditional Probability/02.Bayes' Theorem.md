
> _The most controversial theorem in statistics. Not because anyone doubts the proof — the proof is trivial. Because of what it implies about how knowledge should be updated. Every Bayesian vs. frequentist argument ultimately traces back to this one equation._

---

## The Theorem

**Theorem (Bayes, 1763).** Let $A$ and $B$ be events with $P(B) > 0$. Then:

$$\boxed{P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A), P(A)}{P(B)}}$$

**Proof.** By definition of conditional probability:

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}$$

and equally:

$$P(B \mid A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(A)} \implies P(A \cap B) = P(B \mid A), P(A)$$

Substitute:

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A), P(A)}{P(B)} \qquad \square$$

The proof takes four lines. The implications take careers.

## The Language

The theorem has a canonical vocabulary that is worth learning exactly, because it structures how you think:

$$\underbrace{P(A \mid B)}_{\text{posterior}} = \frac{\underbrace{P(B \mid A)}_{\text{likelihood}} \cdot \underbrace{P(A)}_{\text{prior}}}{\underbrace{P(B)}_{\text{marginal likelihood / evidence}}}$$

- **Prior $P(A)$:** your belief in $A$ before observing $B$. What you bring to the problem.
- **Likelihood $P(B \mid A)$:** how probable is the data $B$ if $A$ is true. What the model says.
- **Posterior $P(A \mid B)$:** your updated belief in $A$ after observing $B$. What you leave with.
- **Marginal likelihood $P(B)$:** the total probability of the observed data, averaged over all hypotheses. A normalising constant.

The posterior is proportional to the likelihood times the prior:

$$P(A \mid B) \propto P(B \mid A) \cdot P(A)$$

The denominator $P(B)$ is just whatever makes the posterior sum/integrate to 1.

> _The language of prior and posterior is the language of learning. You start somewhere (prior), you see data (likelihood), you update (posterior). This is the Bayesian loop. Run it again with the posterior as the new prior. Repeat._

---

## The Law of Total Probability (The Engine)

The denominator $P(B)$ is almost always computed via the **law of total probability**. Let $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ be a partition of $\Omega$ (mutually exclusive, exhaustive). Then:

$$P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(B \mid A_i), P(A_i)$$

Substituting into Bayes:

$$P(A_j \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A_j), P(A_j)}{\sum_{i=1}^n P(B \mid A_i), P(A_i)}$$

This is the **full form of Bayes' theorem** — with a partition of hypotheses and a single observation. It is the workhorse form for all discrete applications.

> _The denominator forces you to consider all competing hypotheses simultaneously. This is not a bug — it's the feature. You cannot update on evidence without accounting for all the ways the evidence could have arisen._

## The Fundamental Insight: Inverting Conditional Probability

Bayes' theorem is fundamentally about **inversion**: you know $P(B \mid A)$ but want $P(A \mid B)$. These are not the same thing and confusing them is one of the most common errors in reasoning.

**$P(B \mid A) \neq P(A \mid B)$ in general.**

The classic confusion: "the test is 99% accurate" means $P(\text{positive} \mid \text{disease}) = 0.99$. It says nothing directly about $P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive})$, which is what you actually want to know.

The prosecutor's fallacy, the base rate fallacy, most medical test misinterpretation — all are failures to perform this inversion correctly. Bayes' theorem is the correct procedure for doing the inversion.

---

## Example 1: The Medical Test (Base Rate Neglect)

A disease affects 1% of the population. A test has:

- Sensitivity: $P(\text{pos} \mid \text{disease}) = 0.99$
- Specificity: $P(\text{neg} \mid \text{no disease}) = 0.95$, so $P(\text{pos} \mid \text{no disease}) = 0.05$.

You test positive. What is $P(\text{disease} \mid \text{positive})$?

Let $D$ = has disease, $+$ = tests positive.

**Prior:** $P(D) = 0.01$, $P(D^c) = 0.99$.

**Denominator** (law of total probability):

$$P(+) = P(+ \mid D),P(D) + P(+ \mid D^c),P(D^c) = 0.99 \times 0.01 + 0.05 \times 0.99 = 0.0099 + 0.0495 = 0.0594$$

**Posterior:**

$$P(D \mid +) = \frac{0.99 \times 0.01}{0.0594} = \frac{0.0099}{0.0594} \approx 0.167$$

A positive test on a 99%-accurate test still only gives you a 16.7% posterior probability of disease — because the disease is rare, and false positives swamp true positives.

> _This result shocks almost everyone the first time. It shocked me. The prior $P(D) = 0.01$ is not a nuisance to be ignored — it is doing 83% of the work. Ignoring the base rate and concluding "the test is 99% accurate so I probably have the disease" is the base rate fallacy, and it is extremely common, including among doctors._

**The 2×2 table intuition.** In a population of 10,000:

- 100 have the disease. Of these, 99 test positive (true positives).
- 9,900 don't. Of these, 495 test positive (false positives).
- Total positives: 594. Fraction with disease: 99/594 ≈ 16.7%.

The table makes the arithmetic visceral. The rare disease means false positives vastly outnumber true positives, even with a good test.

## Example 2: The Prosecutor's Fallacy

A defendant's DNA matches the crime scene. The match probability is $P(\text{match} \mid \text{innocent}) = 1/1{,}000{,}000$.

The prosecutor argues: "The probability that an innocent person would match is one in a million — therefore the defendant is almost certainly guilty."

This is the fallacy. The prosecutor is treating $P(\text{match} \mid \text{innocent})$ as if it were $P(\text{innocent} \mid \text{match})$.

By Bayes:

$$P(\text{innocent} \mid \text{match}) = \frac{P(\text{match} \mid \text{innocent}), P(\text{innocent})}{P(\text{match})}$$

If the city has 1 million potential suspects, $P(\text{innocent}) \approx 999{,}999/1{,}000{,}000$, and the expected number of innocents who would match is $\approx 1$. So the defendant is one of roughly 2 people who match — the actual perpetrator plus one false match. The posterior probability of guilt is around 50%, not 99.9999%.

Context — prior information, the size of the suspect pool, other evidence — is indispensable. Bayes' theorem forces you to account for it.

## Example 3: Sequential Updating

Suppose I flip a coin of unknown bias $p$. My prior is $p \sim \text{Uniform}[0,1]$. I observe Heads, then Heads, then Tails.

**After Heads 1:**

$$f(p \mid H_1) \propto P(H_1 \mid p) \cdot f(p) = p \cdot 1 = p$$

Normalised: $f(p \mid H_1) = 2p$ (Beta$(2,1)$ distribution).

**After Heads 2:**

$$f(p \mid H_1, H_2) \propto p \cdot 2p = 2p^2$$

Normalised: $f(p \mid H_1, H_2) = 3p^2$ (Beta$(3,1)$).

**After Tails:**

$$f(p \mid H_1, H_2, T) \propto (1-p) \cdot 3p^2 = 3p^2(1-p)$$

Normalised: Beta$(3,2)$, with density $12p^2(1-p)$.

The posterior mean is $E[p \mid \text{data}] = 3/(3+2) = 3/5$ — which is just the observed frequency of heads. The uniform prior and Beta posteriors form a **conjugate pair** with the Binomial likelihood.

> _Sequential updating is the proof that Bayesian inference is logically consistent: it doesn't matter whether you process all three observations at once or one at a time. The posterior is the same. This is the coherence property that frequentist procedures don't have in general._

## The Odds Form

Bayes' theorem has an elegant reformulation in terms of **odds** that is often more intuitive.

Define the **prior odds** of $A$ vs $A^c$:

$$\text{Prior odds} = \frac{P(A)}{P(A^c)}$$

and the **likelihood ratio** (Bayes factor):

$$\text{LR} = \frac{P(B \mid A)}{P(B \mid A^c)}$$

Then:

$$\underbrace{\frac{P(A \mid B)}{P(A^c \mid B)}}_{\text{posterior odds}} = \underbrace{\frac{P(B \mid A)}{P(B \mid A^c)}}_{\text{likelihood ratio}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(A)}{P(A^c)}}_{\text{prior odds}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Posterior odds} = \text{Likelihood ratio} \times \text{Prior odds}}$$

**Proof.** Write Bayes for $A$ and $A^c$, divide:

$$\frac{P(A \mid B)}{P(A^c \mid B)} = \frac{P(B \mid A),P(A)}{P(B \mid A^c),P(A^c)}$$

The $P(B)$ denominators cancel. $\square$

This form is beautiful because:

1. The normalising constant $P(B)$ disappears entirely.
2. It shows that each piece of evidence **multiplies** the odds by the likelihood ratio.
3. Sequential updating means multiplying by each likelihood ratio in turn — Bayesian inference as a product of likelihood ratios applied to the prior odds.

> _In the medical example: prior odds of disease are $0.01/0.99 \approx 1:99$. Likelihood ratio of a positive test is $0.99/0.05 = 19.8$. Posterior odds: $19.8/99 \approx 1:5$, i.e. $\approx 1/6 \approx 16.7%$. Same answer, no denominator calculation needed._

## Bayes' Theorem for Continuous Distributions

When $A$ is replaced by a continuous parameter $\theta$ with prior density $f(\theta)$ and $B$ by data $x$ with likelihood $L(\theta) = f(x \mid \theta)$:

$$f(\theta \mid x) = \frac{f(x \mid \theta), f(\theta)}{f(x)} = \frac{f(x \mid \theta), f(\theta)}{\int f(x \mid \theta'), f(\theta'), d\theta'}$$

or in the proportional form (almost always preferred):

$$f(\theta \mid x) \propto f(x \mid \theta), f(\theta)$$

**Posterior $\propto$ Likelihood $\times$ Prior.**

This is the master equation of Bayesian statistics. Every Bayesian model is an instance of this formula with different choices of likelihood and prior.

The integral $\int f(x \mid \theta) f(\theta) d\theta$ is the **marginal likelihood** — often analytically intractable, which is why MCMC exists.

---

## Conjugate Priors

A prior $f(\theta)$ is **conjugate** to a likelihood $f(x \mid \theta)$ if the posterior $f(\theta \mid x)$ is in the same distributional family as the prior. This gives closed-form posteriors and makes sequential updating trivial.

Key conjugate pairs:

|Likelihood|Conjugate Prior|Posterior|
|---|---|---|
|Binomial$(n,p)$|Beta$(\alpha,\beta)$|Beta$(\alpha + k,, \beta + n - k)$|
|Poisson$(\lambda)$|Gamma$(\alpha,\beta)$|Gamma$(\alpha + \sum x_i,, \beta + n)$|
|Normal$(\mu, \sigma^2)$, $\sigma^2$ known|Normal$(\mu_0, \tau^2)$|Normal (updated mean and variance)|

The Beta-Binomial pair is the canonical example: observe $k$ heads in $n$ flips, update Beta$(\alpha,\beta)$ to Beta$(\alpha+k, \beta+n-k)$. The parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ act like "prior pseudocounts" of successes and failures.

> _Conjugate priors are computationally convenient but philosophically optional. The prior should reflect your actual beliefs, not be chosen for mathematical tractability. In modern Bayesian practice (with MCMC), you can use any prior you want. Conjugacy is a historical artefact of the pre-computational era — beautiful, but not sacred._

## The Philosophical Divide

Bayes' theorem is mathematically uncontroversial. The controversy is about $P(A)$ — the **prior**.

**Bayesian position:** Probabilities are **degrees of belief**. The prior $P(A)$ represents your subjective uncertainty about $A$ before seeing data. Different analysts can have different priors, and that is fine — as data accumulates, posteriors converge regardless of the prior (under mild conditions). The prior is honest about the subjectivity that is always present in inference.

**Frequentist position:** Probabilities are **long-run frequencies**. The prior $P(A)$ is meaningless if $A$ is not a repeatable event — what is the "probability" that the universe is flat? Inference should be based only on data, not on subjective prior beliefs.

**My current view:** The Bayesian framework is more logically coherent for single-event inference and for updating beliefs sequentially. The frequentist framework is operationally useful when you genuinely have repeated experiments and want procedures with guaranteed long-run properties. They answer different questions. The war between them is partly a war about what "probability" means and partly a war about what "inference" is for.

> _I don't think I have to pick a side permanently. Use Bayes when you have a prior and care about the posterior. Use frequentist methods when you care about Type I error rates and long-run guarantees. Know which question you're answering._
