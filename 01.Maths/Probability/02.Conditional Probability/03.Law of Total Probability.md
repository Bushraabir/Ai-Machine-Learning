
> _This is the theorem that makes conditional probability useful. On its own, conditioning is just a definition. The law of total probability is what lets you compute things with it — by decomposing a hard probability into a weighted average of easier ones. I use this in almost every non-trivial probability calculation I do._

---

## The Theorem

**Theorem (Law of Total Probability).** Let $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ be a **partition** of $\Omega$ — that is:

- Mutually exclusive: $A_i \cap A_j = \emptyset$ for $i \neq j$
- Exhaustive: $A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n = \Omega$
- Each $P(A_i) > 0$

Then for any event $B$:

$$\boxed{P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(B \mid A_i), P(A_i)}$$

**Proof.**

$$B = B \cap \Omega = B \cap \left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right) = \bigcup_{i=1}^n (B \cap A_i)$$

Since the $A_i$ are mutually exclusive, so are the $B \cap A_i$. By countable additivity:

$$P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(B \cap A_i) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(B \mid A_i), P(A_i) \qquad \square$$

The last step uses $P(B \cap A_i) = P(B \mid A_i) P(A_i)$, which is just the definition of conditional probability rearranged.

> _The proof is two lines. The content is entirely in choosing the right partition ${A_i}$. That choice is the art. A good partition makes each $P(B \mid A_i)$ easy to compute. A bad partition makes everything harder. This is the main skill to develop._

## The Continuous Version

When the partition is uncountable — say, conditioning on the value of a continuous random variable $Y$ with density $f_Y$ — the sum becomes an integral:

$$P(B) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P(B \mid Y = y), f_Y(y), dy$$

And for random variables, this becomes the **law of total expectation** (see [[03.Law of Total Probability]]):

$$E[X] = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} E[X \mid Y = y], f_Y(y), dy = E[E[X \mid Y]]$$

LOTP and LOTE are the same theorem — one for probabilities, one for expectations. LOTP is the special case $X = \mathbf{1}_B$.

---

## The Geometry

The partition ${A_1, \ldots, A_n}$ slices $\Omega$ into non-overlapping pieces. The event $B$ intersects each slice differently. LOTP says: the probability of $B$ is a **weighted average** of its conditional probabilities in each slice, weighted by the probability of each slice.

$$P(B) = \sum_i \underbrace{P(B \mid A_i)}_{\text{probability of } B \text{ in slice } i} \cdot \underbrace{P(A_i)}_{\text{weight of slice } i}$$

This is not merely algebraic — it is saying that the unconditional probability is the mixture of the conditional probabilities. Knowing which slice you're in simplifies the problem; LOTP averages over your uncertainty about which slice you're actually in.

## The Standard Partitions

In practice, the partition almost always takes one of three forms:

**Form 1: Binary partition** ${A, A^c}$.

$$P(B) = P(B \mid A), P(A) + P(B \mid A^c), P(A^c)$$

Use when there is a natural yes/no condition (disease or not, red card or not, etc.).

**Form 2: Partition by the value of a discrete RV** $Y \in {y_1, \ldots, y_n}$.

$$P(B) = \sum_i P(B \mid Y = y_i), P(Y = y_i)$$

Use when conditioning on $Y$ makes $B$ easy to analyse.

**Form 3: Partition by a continuous RV** $Y$.

$$P(B) = \int P(B \mid Y = y), f_Y(y), dy$$

Use in continuous models.

> _The binary partition is the one I reach for first. If $A$ and $A^c$ both give tractable conditionals, you're done. Only if neither works cleanly do I look for a finer partition._

## Example 1: Two Coins

I have two coins: coin 1 is fair ($p = 1/2$), coin 2 is biased ($p = 3/4$). I pick one uniformly at random and flip it. What is the probability of Heads?

**Partition:** $A_1 = {\text{chose coin 1}}$, $A_2 = {\text{chose coin 2}}$.

$$P(H) = P(H \mid A_1), P(A_1) + P(H \mid A_2), P(A_2) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} + \frac{3}{4} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{3}{8} = \frac{5}{8}$$

Simple, but notice: the answer $5/8$ is strictly between $1/2$ and $3/4$. LOTP is producing a **convex combination** of the conditional probabilities. The unconditional probability is always between the min and max of the conditionals.

## Example 2: Gamblers' Ruin (Recursive LOTP)

A gambler starts with $$k$ and bets $$1$ each round against a casino. Wins with probability $p$, loses with probability $q = 1-p$. Game ends when they reach $$N$ (win) or $$0$ (ruin). Let $P_k$ = probability of winning starting from $k$.

**Partition** at the first step:  win first bet and lose first bet:

$$P_k = p \cdot P_{k+1} + q \cdot P_{k-1}, \quad k = 1, \ldots, N-1$$

with boundary conditions $P_0 = 0$, $P_N = 1$.

This is a second-order linear recurrence. For $p \neq q$, the solution is:

$$P_k = \frac{1 - (q/p)^k}{1 - (q/p)^N}$$

For $p = q = 1/2$: $P_k = k/N$ (by L'Hôpital or a separate argument).

> _This is LOTP applied recursively to set up a functional equation. The partition has only two elements (win or lose the first bet), but applying it at each state generates the full recurrence. This technique — condition on the first step, write a recurrence — is one of the most powerful patterns in discrete probability._

## Example 3: The Law of Total Probability as a Denominator

Recall from [[02.Bayes' Theorem]]: the denominator in Bayes is always computed via LOTP.

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A), P(A)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B \mid A), P(A)}{P(B \mid A),P(A) + P(B \mid A^c),P(A^c)}$$

Bayes' theorem without LOTP is incomplete — you cannot compute the normalising constant. LOTP is the computational engine inside Bayes.

Every Bayesian calculation has the structure: **Bayes in the numerator, LOTP in the denominator.**

## Example 4: Random Sums

Let $N \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$ and, given $N = n$, let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Bern}(p)$. Let $S = X_1 + \cdots + X_N$ (with $S = 0$ when $N = 0$).

**Compute $P(S = k)$.**

Condition on $N$:

$$P(S = k) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} P(S = k \mid N = n), P(N = n) = \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \binom{n}{k} p^k (1-p)^{n-k} \cdot \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^n}{n!}$$

This is exactly the calculation from [[09.Poisson Distribution]] showing $S \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda p)$. LOTP is doing all the work — conditioning on $N$ makes $S \mid N = n$ a Binomial, which is tractable. Then LOTP averages over $N$.

> _This pattern — condition on the random index, compute the conditional distribution, apply LOTP — is the standard technique for random sums. The Poisson-Binomial calculation is its cleanest instance._

## Example 5: First Step Analysis (Geometric Mean)

Let $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ (type 2). Derive $E[X]$ using LOTP applied to $E[X]$ directly (this previews the law of total expectation).

Partition on the outcome of the first trial:

$$E[X] = E[X \mid \text{success}], p + E[X \mid \text{failure}], q$$

Given success on the first trial: $X = 1$, so $E[X \mid \text{success}] = 1$.

Given failure on the first trial: we've used one trial and are back to the start by memorylessness, so $E[X \mid \text{failure}] = 1 + E[X]$.

$$E[X] = p \cdot 1 + q \cdot (1 + E[X]) = p + q + q,E[X] = 1 + q,E[X]$$

$$E[X](1 - q) = 1 \implies E[X] = \frac{1}{p}$$

This is the same story proof from [[03.Geometric Distribution]], now written explicitly as LOTP applied to expectations. The memoryless property is what makes the "back to the start" step valid.

## The Adam and Eve Laws

Blitzstein calls LOTE the "Adam Law" and the law of total variance the "Eve Law." LOTP is Adam's law applied to indicator variables. The full hierarchy:

**Adam's Law (LOTE):** $$E[X] = E[E[X \mid Y]]$$

**Eve's Law (LOTV):** $$\text{Var}(X) = E[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)] + \text{Var}(E[X \mid Y])$$

LOTP is the special case of Adam's Law with $X = \mathbf{1}_B$:

$$P(B) = E[\mathbf{1}_B] = E[E[\mathbf{1}_B \mid Y]] = E[P(B \mid Y)]$$

So LOTP, LOTE, and LOTV are all the same theorem at different levels of generality. Learn LOTP first, then LOTE, then LOTV — each one contains the previous as a special case.

## Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Not using a partition.**

$$P(B) \neq P(B \mid A) + P(B \mid A^c)$$

This is wrong. The correct formula is:

$$P(B) = P(B \mid A),P(A) + P(B \mid A^c),P(A^c)$$

The weights $P(A)$ and $P(A^c)$ are mandatory. Dropping them is the most common error.

**Mistake 2: Choosing a partition that doesn't cover $\Omega$.**

If $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ don't exhaust $\Omega$, the formula gives $\sum_i P(B \cap A_i) \neq P(B)$. Always check exhaustiveness.

**Mistake 3: Using LOTP when the conditioning event has probability zero.**

$P(B \mid A_i)$ requires $P(A_i) > 0$. In continuous settings, conditioning on ${Y = y}$ (a zero-probability event) requires measure-theoretic conditional expectation, not just the definition of conditional probability. The formula still works, but the justification is different.

> _Mistake 1 is by far the most common. I've made it. On an exam, if your answer doesn't look like a weighted average of conditional probabilities, something is wrong._
