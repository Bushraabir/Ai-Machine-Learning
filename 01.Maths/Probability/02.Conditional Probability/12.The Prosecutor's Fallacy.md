>[!quote] In a criminal trial, the prosecutor presents DNA evidence and states: _"The probability of this DNA match occurring by chance in an innocent person is 1 in 1,000,000. Therefore, there is only a 1 in 1,000,000 chance the defendant is innocent."_
**What is wrong with this reasoning?**
## The Core Confusion

The prosecutor's fallacy is a specific, named instance of conflating a conditional with its transpose. In court, it goes like this:

- The prosecution presents evidence $E$ (say, a DNA match).
- The expert witness states: _"The probability of this match if the defendant is innocent is 1 in a million."_
- The jury hears: _"The probability that the defendant is innocent is 1 in a million."_

Formally, the fallacy is:

$$P(E \mid \text{innocent}) \approx 0 \implies P(\text{innocent} \mid E) \approx 0$$

This inference is **not valid**. It ignores the prior. It is exactly the error Bayes' rule exists to correct.

---

## The Fallacy in Symbols

Let $I$ = "defendant is innocent", $E$ = "evidence observed (e.g. DNA match)".

What the expert gives us: $$P(E \mid I) = \varepsilon \quad \text{(very small)}$$

What Bayes' rule says we actually need: $$P(I \mid E) = \frac{P(E \mid I)P(I)}{P(E)}$$

Expanding the denominator by LOTP: $$P(I \mid E) = \frac{\varepsilon \cdot P(I)}{\varepsilon \cdot P(I) + P(E \mid I^c)\cdot P(I^c)}$$

The fallacy amounts to treating $P(I \mid E)$ as though $P(I)$ and $P(E \mid I^c)$ don't exist — as if the prior were uniform and the likelihood ratio were the whole story.

---

## Why the Error is So Easy to Make

The statement $P(E \mid I) = 10^{-6}$ _sounds_ like it says something about guilt. It doesn't. It says something about the _generative model under innocence_ — how surprising the evidence would be in a world where the defendant didn't do it.

The question a jury must answer is the _posterior_: given everything we know, how probable is guilt? That requires:

1. **A prior** $P(I)$ — before seeing the DNA, how likely was this specific person to be the perpetrator?
2. **The likelihood under guilt** $P(E \mid I^c)$ — how probable is the evidence if they _did_ do it?

Both are typically left out of expert testimony, and both matter enormously.

---

## A Worked Example: The DNA Database Trawl

This is the case that makes students genuinely uncomfortable.

**Setup.** A crime occurs. Police have a DNA sample from the scene. They trawl a database of $N = 1{,}000{,}000$ people. The DNA test has a false positive rate of $\Pr(\text{match} \mid \text{innocent}) = 10^{-6}$.

One person — call him $X$ — matches. He is arrested. The prosecutor argues: _"The probability of a false match is 1 in a million. He must be guilty."_

**Let's actually compute $P(X \text{ is innocent} \mid X \text{ matched})$.**

Assume the true perpetrator is in the database (worst case for the defendant). So exactly one person is guilty. The prior probability that $X$ specifically is guilty, before seeing any match:

$$P(X \text{ is guilty}) = \frac{1}{N} = 10^{-6}$$

Now apply LOTP. There are $N = 10^6$ people. Expected number of matches:

- From the guilty person: $1$ (they always match, assume $\Pr(\text{match} \mid \text{guilty}) = 1$)
- From the innocent $N-1$ people: $(N-1) \times 10^{-6} \approx 1$ false positive

So we expect roughly **2 matches total**. $X$ is one of approximately 2 people who matched. Therefore:

$$P(X \text{ is innocent} \mid X \text{ matched}) \approx \frac{1}{2}$$

The probability of innocence is approximately **50%** — not 1 in a million. The prosecutor was off by a factor of $500{,}000$.

**The lesson.** When you trawl a large database, the false positive rate must be multiplied by the database size. The prior probability of guilt for any single person is $1/N$, which exactly cancels the small false-positive rate when $N \sim 1/\varepsilon$. This is sometimes called the **database trawl problem** or the **birthday-problem cousin** — rare events become likely when you look at enough trials.

---

## The Formal Theorem: What the Fallacy Ignores

State it cleanly. We always have:

$$\frac{P(I \mid E)}{P(I^c \mid E)} = \underbrace{\frac{P(E \mid I)}{P(E \mid I^c)}}_{\Lambda\text{ (Bayes factor)}} \times \underbrace{\frac{P(I)}{P(I^c)}}_{\text{prior odds}}$$

The prosecutor's fallacy is equivalent to claiming that $\Lambda$ alone determines $P(I \mid E)$. But:

- If $P(I) \approx 1$ (the person was chosen from a huge population with no other evidence), $\Lambda = 10^6$ might still leave posterior odds roughly $1:1$.
- If $P(I) \approx 0$ (caught at the scene, fingerprints everywhere), even a weak $\Lambda = 10$ makes $\Pr(I \mid E)$ very small.

**The Bayes factor is not the posterior. It is the update multiplicative factor. These are very different things.**

---

## The Transposed Conditional: A Taxonomy

The prosecutor's fallacy is the most famous instance of a general error. Worth cataloguing:

| Context            | Fallacy                                                | Correct statement                                                         |
| ------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------ | ------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Criminal trial     | $\Pr(E \mid I) \small$ implies guilt                   | Need $\Pr(I \mid E)$ via Bayes                                            |
| Medical testing    | Positive test $\Rightarrow$ disease                    | $\Pr(D \mid T^+)$ depends on prevalence                                   |
| Hypothesis testing | $p < 0.05 \Rightarrow$ $H_0$ false                     | $p$-value is $\Pr(\text{data} \mid H_0)$, not $\Pr(H_0 \mid \text{data})$ |
| Machine learning   | High accuracy $\Rightarrow$ reliable on minority class | Accuracy ignores class imbalance                                          |

The third row is particularly painful: the entire frequentist $p$-value framework is vulnerable to exactly this confusion. A $p$-value of 0.01 does **not** mean there is a 1% probability the null hypothesis is true. It means: _if the null were true, data this extreme would occur 1% of the time._ These are different.

## Real Cases

**Sally Clark (UK, 1999).** Convicted of murdering her two infant sons. Expert witness Sir Roy Meadow claimed the probability of two cot deaths in one family was $1/73{,}000{,}000$. He obtained this by squaring $1/8500$ (the rate of a single cot death in affluent families) — treating the deaths as independent. But:

- The events are _not_ independent (genetic and environmental factors are shared).
- More importantly, he gave $\Pr(\text{two cot deaths} \mid \text{innocent})$ and the jury treated it as $\Pr(\text{innocent} \mid \text{two cot deaths})$.
- The correct computation must also account for the prior probability of a mother murdering two children, which is also extremely rare.

Sally Clark was convicted, spent three years in prison, was eventually acquitted, and died of acute alcohol poisoning four years later. The Royal Statistical Society issued a public statement condemning the use of statistics in her trial.

**R v. Adams (UK, 1996).** A Bayesian defence. The defence actually tried to walk the jury through Bayes' rule. The Court of Appeal rejected it, ruling that Bayesian reasoning was "inappropriate" for juries to use when assessing non-statistical evidence. This remains controversial.

---

## The Defence Fallacy (The Other Side)

There is a symmetric error made by defence attorneys, sometimes called the **defence attorney's fallacy**:

> _"One in a million people match this DNA profile. There are 60 million people in this country. Therefore there are 60 other potential suspects. My client is just one of 61."_

This ignores _all other evidence_. If the defendant was also found at the crime scene, knew the victim, had motive, and left fingerprints, the prior $\Pr(I)$ is already very low. Bayes' rule accounts for _all_ evidence jointly, not just the DNA.

Both fallacies are failures to properly compute the posterior. One ignores the prior (prosecutor), one ignores the likelihood of other evidence (defence).

---

## Connection to $p$-Values and Why This Matters for You

When you encounter a paper that says "$p = 0.03$, therefore the effect is real," that is a soft version of the prosecutor's fallacy. The $p$-value is:

$$p = P(\text{test statistic} \geq t_{\text{obs}} \mid H_0 \text{ true})$$

To get $P(H_0 \text{ true} \mid \text{data})$, you need:

- A prior on $H_0$
- The likelihood under $H_1$

This is exactly what Bayesian hypothesis testing provides. The frequentist framework deliberately avoids specifying a prior — which is philosophically motivated but means the $p$-value literally cannot tell you the probability that your null is false. Every time someone interprets it that way, they are committing a version of the prosecutor's fallacy.

## Summary

| Aspect                 | Prosecutor's Claim                    | Correct Calculation                       |
| ---------------------- | ------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| **Quantity stated**    | $P(E \mid I)$                         | Need $P(I \mid E)$                        |
| **What's missing**     | Prior $P(I)$, base rates              | Full Bayes' rule                          |
| **Direction of error** | Always overstates guilt               | Depends on prior                          |
| **Fix**                | Apply Bayes' rule with explicit prior | Posterior = likelihood × prior / evidence |
The fallacy is seductive because $P(E \mid I)$ is _easy to compute and easy to state_. The posterior $P(I \mid E)$ requires committing to a prior, which feels subjective. But refusing to specify a prior doesn't make it go away — it just means you're implicitly using a flat prior and pretending otherwise.
