## Why This Problem Actually Matters

I used to think Gambler's Ruin was just a cute puzzle. It isn't.

It's the prototype for an entire class of problems: _what is the probability that a stochastic process, starting at some interior point, eventually hits one boundary before the other?_ That question appears in sequential hypothesis testing, DNA sequence analysis, queueing theory, option pricing, and the study of random walks on graphs. If you understand this derivation — really understand it, not just memorise the answer — you understand the backbone of a significant chunk of applied probability.

Also, it's one of Blitzstein's favourite Stat 110 examples for good reason. The first-step analysis trick used here reappears everywhere.

---

## § 1 — Setting Up the Model Precisely

**The state space.** Two players, A and B, share a fixed total of $N$ dollars. At any moment, let the state be $i = $ A's current fortune, so $i \in {0, 1, 2, \ldots, N}$.

**The dynamics.** At each round, independently of all previous rounds: $$X_{n+1} = \begin{cases} X_n + 1 & \text{with probability } p \ X_n - 1 & \text{with probability } q := 1 - p \end{cases}$$

**The absorbing states.** States $0$ and $N$ are absorbing: once reached, the process stays there. State $0$ = A is ruined. State $N$ = A has won everything.

**The question.** Starting from $X_0 = i$, define

$$P_i := \mathbb{P}(\text{A wins} \mid X_0 = i) = \mathbb{P}(\text{process hits } N \text{ before } 0 \mid X_0 = i)$$

We want a closed-form expression for $P_i$ for every $i \in {0, 1, \ldots, N}$.

> [!NOTE] This is a finite-state Markov chain. The transient states are ${1, 2, \ldots, N-1}$ and the two absorbing states are ${0, N}$. Absorption is guaranteed because the chain is irreducible on the transient states and those states are all transient — the process must eventually leave them. I'll take this for granted here; the proof uses the fact that from any transient state, there's a positive probability path to an absorbing state in finitely many steps.

## § 2 — First-Step Analysis: Deriving the Recurrence

This is the key technique. Instead of thinking about the whole trajectory at once, we condition on just the _first_ step and use the Markov property.

For $1 \leq i \leq N-1$:

$$P_i = \mathbb{P}(\text{hit } N \text{ before } 0 \mid X_0 = i)$$

Condition on $X_1$:

$$P_i = \mathbb{P}(X_1 = i+1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{hit } N \text{ before } 0 \mid X_1 = i+1) + \mathbb{P}(X_1 = i-1) \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{hit } N \text{ before } 0 \mid X_1 = i-1)$$

By the **Markov property**, the future evolution depends only on the current state, not on how we got there. So $\mathbb{P}(\text{hit } N \text{ before } 0 \mid X_1 = j) = P_j$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{P_i = p \cdot P_{i+1} + q \cdot P_{i-1}} \qquad \text{for } i = 1, 2, \ldots, N-1$$

with boundary conditions:

$$P_0 = 0 \qquad P_N = 1$$

This is a **second-order linear recurrence** — the discrete analogue of a second-order ODE. Since it's linear with constant coefficients, we solve it exactly as we would an ODE: find the characteristic equation, get the general solution, then apply boundary conditions to pin down the constants.

---

## § 3 — Solving the Recurrence: Full Algebra

### Step 1 — Rewrite in standard form

The recurrence is: $$P_i = p \cdot P_{i+1} + q \cdot P_{i-1}$$

Since $p + q = 1$, multiply the left side by $(p + q) = 1$: $$p \cdot P_i + q \cdot P_i = p \cdot P_{i+1} + q \cdot P_{i-1}$$

Rearrange: $$p(P_{i+1} - P_i) = q(P_i - P_{i-1})$$

### Step 2 — Introduce the differences

Define $d_i := P_i - P_{i-1}$ for $i = 1, 2, \ldots, N$. Then the relation above becomes:

$$p \cdot d_{i+1} = q \cdot d_i$$

$$\therefore \quad d_{i+1} = \frac{q}{p} \cdot d_i$$

This says the sequence $(d_i)$ is **geometric** with common ratio $r := q/p$.

### Step 3 — Write out the geometric sequence

$$d_1 = d_1$$ $$d_2 = r \cdot d_1$$ $$d_3 = r^2 \cdot d_1$$ $$\vdots$$ $$d_i = r^{i-1} \cdot d_1$$

### Step 4 — Telescope to get $P_i$

Since $P_0 = 0$, we can recover $P_i$ by summing the differences:

$$P_i = P_0 + \sum_{k=1}^{i} d_k = \sum_{k=1}^{i} r^{k-1} \cdot d_1 = d_1 \sum_{k=0}^{i-1} r^k$$

Now we split into two cases depending on whether $r = 1$.

---

## § 4 — Case 1: Fair Game ($p = q = \tfrac{1}{2}$, so $r = 1$)

When $r = 1$, all differences are equal: $d_k = d_1$ for all $k$.

The sum becomes:

$$P_i = d_1 \cdot \sum_{k=0}^{i-1} 1 = i \cdot d_1$$

Apply the boundary condition $P_N = 1$:

$$1 = N \cdot d_1 \implies d_1 = \frac{1}{N}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{P_i = \frac{i}{N}} \qquad \text{(Fair game)}$$

**What this is saying.** Your probability of winning is exactly your share of the total wealth. If you bring $30 to a table where the total money in play is $100, you will — on average — win with probability 30%. This is a beautiful result: no matter how the game plays out round by round, the long-run probability reduces to a simple ratio.

> [!IMPORTANT] Notice the asymmetry baked into this formula. Even though the game is _fair_ round-by-round (zero expected gain per step), the _outcome_ is not symmetric in terms of survival. If you have $1 and your opponent has $99, you win with probability only 1%. Fairness in each step does not mean fairness in the long run when players have unequal resources. This is the core message of the whole theorem.

---

## § 5 — Case 2: Biased Game ($p \neq q$, so $r \neq 1$)

When $r \neq 1$, the sum is a standard finite geometric series:

$$\sum_{k=0}^{i-1} r^k = \frac{1 - r^i}{1 - r}$$

So:

$$P_i = d_1 \cdot \frac{1 - r^i}{1 - r}$$

Now apply $P_N = 1$:

$$1 = d_1 \cdot \frac{1 - r^N}{1 - r} \implies d_1 = \frac{1 - r}{1 - r^N}$$

Substitute back:

$$P_i = \frac{1-r}{1-r^N} \cdot \frac{1-r^i}{1-r}$$

$$\boxed{P_i = \frac{1 - r^i}{1 - r^N}} \qquad r = \frac{q}{p}, \quad r \neq 1$$

### Sanity checks

Let me verify this actually satisfies what it should:

- **$P_0$:** $\frac{1 - r^0}{1 - r^N} = \frac{0}{1-r^N} = 0$ ✓
- **$P_N$:** $\frac{1 - r^N}{1 - r^N} = 1$ ✓
- **Limit as $r \to 1$:** Apply L'Hôpital (or Taylor expand): $\frac{1 - r^i}{1 - r^N} \to \frac{i}{N}$ as $r \to 1$ ✓ — recovers the fair game case continuously.

> [!NOTE] The continuity at $r = 1$ is reassuring. There's no discontinuous "phase transition" between fair and biased games — the biased formula smoothly collapses to the fair formula as $p \to \frac{1}{2}$.

### The exponential effect of small biases

This is the part that always surprises me. Let's be concrete. Set $N = 100$, so total wealth is $100. You start with $i = 50$ — exactly half the money. Seems fair, right?

| Win prob $p$ | $r = q/p$ | $P_{50}$ (your win probability) |
| ------------ | --------- | ------------------------------- |
| 0.50         | 1.000     | 0.500                           |
| 0.51         | 0.961     | 0.880                           |
| 0.49         | 1.041     | 0.119                           |
| 0.55         | 0.818     | 0.999                           |
| 0.45         | 1.222     | 0.001                           |

Starting with **half the money**, a 1% disadvantage per round drops your survival probability from 50% to about 12%. A 5% disadvantage makes ruin near-certain. The exponential structure of $r^i$ is brutal.

## § 6 — Expected Duration of the Game

While we're here, let's also derive the expected duration. This requires the same first-step analysis, applied to a different function.

Let $D_i = \mathbb{E}[\text{number of rounds} \mid X_0 = i]$, with $D_0 = D_N = 0$.

### Step 1 — Write the recurrence

Conditioning on the first step:

$$D_i = 1 + p \cdot D_{i+1} + q \cdot D_{i-1} \qquad \text{for } 1 \leq i \leq N-1$$

The "1" accounts for the current round. This is an **inhomogeneous** second-order recurrence (the $+1$ on the right is the forcing term).

### Step 2 — Find the particular solution

We need a particular solution to $D_i - p \cdot D_{i+1} - q \cdot D_{i-1} = 1$.

**For the fair game** ($p = q = \frac{1}{2}$): The homogeneous part has general solution $A + Bi$ (since $r=1$ gives a repeated root). Try a particular solution of the form $D_i^{(p)} = Ci^2$.

Substituting: $$Ci^2 = 1 + \frac{1}{2}C(i+1)^2 + \frac{1}{2}C(i-1)^2$$ $$Ci^2 = 1 + \frac{C}{2}(i^2 + 2i + 1) + \frac{C}{2}(i^2 - 2i + 1)$$ $$Ci^2 = 1 + Ci^2 + C$$

This gives $0 = 1 + C$, so $C = -1$.

Particular solution: $D_i^{(p)} = -i^2$.

### Step 3 — General solution

$$D_i = A + Bi + (-i^2) = A + Bi - i^2$$

### Step 4 — Apply boundary conditions

$D_0 = 0$: $\quad A = 0$

$D_N = 0$: $\quad BN - N^2 = 0 \implies B = N$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{D_i = i(N - i)} \qquad \text{(Fair game)}$$

This is a downward parabola in $i$, symmetric about $i = N/2$, with maximum value $N^2/4$ at $i = N/2$.

**Interpretation.** The longest games happen when the players start with equal wealth. If one player is much richer, the game ends quickly — the poor player gets wiped out fast. Equal wealth maximises the duration because neither absorbing state is "nearby." The maximum expected duration scales as $N^2$, which grows much faster than $N$ — doubling the total wealth _quadruples_ the expected game length.

> [!TIP] If you want the biased-game formula for $D_i$, the algebra is similar but messier. The particular solution in that case is $D_i^{(p)} = \frac{i}{q - p}$ (when $p \neq q$), giving after boundary conditions: $$D_i = \frac{1}{q-p}\left[i - N \cdot \frac{1-r^i}{1-r^N}\right] \qquad r = \frac{q}{p}$$ I'll leave verifying the boundary conditions as an exercise. The key thing to notice is that when $p < q$ (unfavorable game), this grows much faster — the game can still take a very long time even when ruin is nearly certain, because the walk wanders around a lot before hitting 0.

## § 7 — The Martingale Proof (The "Right" Way to See It)

Once you know martingales, this whole problem has a cleaner proof that's also more illuminating.

### 7a — Fair game via $X_n$

**Claim.** When $p = q = \frac{1}{2}$, the wealth process $(X_n)$ is a martingale with respect to $\mathcal{F}_n = \sigma(X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_n)$.

**Proof.** $$\mathbb{E}[X_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = X_n \cdot \mathbb{P}(\text{no change}) + (X_n + 1) \cdot p + (X_n - 1) \cdot q = X_n + (p - q) = X_n$$

since $p = q$. ✓

Now let $T = \min{n : X_n \in {0, N}}$ be the stopping time. Since the process is bounded ($0 \leq X_n \leq N$) and $T$ is finite almost surely (the process must eventually absorb), the **Optional Stopping Theorem** applies:

$$\mathbb{E}[X_T] = \mathbb{E}[X_0] = i$$

But at time $T$, we know $X_T \in {0, N}$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X_T] = N \cdot \mathbb{P}(X_T = N) + 0 \cdot \mathbb{P}(X_T = 0) = N \cdot P_i$$

Therefore $N \cdot P_i = i$, giving $P_i = i/N$. $\blacksquare$

> [!IMPORTANT] Notice what we just did: **we derived Gambler's Ruin without solving any recurrence**. The whole proof is: "wealth is a martingale → OST → done." This is why martingales are so powerful. The recurrence proof works from the bottom up; the martingale proof works from the top down, using structure.

---

### 7b — Biased game via $(q/p)^{X_n}$

For the biased case, $X_n$ itself is not a martingale (it has a drift of $p - q$ per step). But we can find a function of $X_n$ that _is_ a martingale.

**Claim.** When $p \neq q$, the process $Y_n = \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n}$ is a martingale.

**Proof.** Let $r = q/p$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y_{n+1} \mid \mathcal{F}_n] = p \cdot r^{X_n + 1} + q \cdot r^{X_n - 1}$$

$$= r^{X_n} \left[ p \cdot r + q \cdot r^{-1} \right]$$

$$= r^{X_n} \left[ p \cdot \frac{q}{p} + q \cdot \frac{p}{q} \right]$$

$$= r^{X_n} \left[ q + p \right] = r^{X_n} = Y_n$$

So $Y_n$ is a martingale. ✓

Apply the Optional Stopping Theorem to $Y_n$ (same conditions apply — bounded, finite stopping time):

$$\mathbb{E}[Y_T] = \mathbb{E}[Y_0] = r^i$$

But $X_T \in {0, N}$:

$$r^i = r^N \cdot P_i + r^0 \cdot (1 - P_i) = r^N \cdot P_i + 1 - P_i$$

$$r^i - 1 = P_i(r^N - 1)$$

$$\boxed{P_i = \frac{r^i - 1}{r^N - 1} = \frac{1 - r^i}{1 - r^N}} \quad \checkmark$$

Same formula. The two proofs are completely consistent — but the martingale proof reveals _why_ the formula has the form it does. It's because $(q/p)^{X_n}$ is the natural "harmonic function" for this walk.

---

### 7c — What about the duration? (Optional Stopping, again)

**For the fair game**, the process $M_n = X_n^2 - n$ is a martingale (you can verify this directly). Apply OST:

$$\mathbb{E}[X_T^2 - T] = \mathbb{E}[X_0^2 - 0] = i^2$$

So:

$$\mathbb{E}[X_T^2] - \mathbb{E}[T] = i^2$$

Now $\mathbb{E}[X_T^2] = N^2 \cdot P_i + 0 \cdot (1-P_i) = N^2 \cdot \frac{i}{N} = Ni$.

Therefore:

$$Ni - \mathbb{E}[T] = i^2 \implies \mathbb{E}[T] = Ni - i^2 = i(N - i) \quad \checkmark$$

Again, same formula — derived in two lines using the right martingale.

> [!NOTE] **The general pattern.** For a random walk with a drift, there's a systematic way to find martingales. If $f$ is "harmonic" for the generator of the walk — meaning $\mathbb{E}[f(X_1) \mid X_0 = i] = f(i)$ — then $f(X_n)$ is a martingale. For the fair walk, the harmonic functions are $f(i) = 1$ (trivial) and $f(i) = i$ (gives the win probability). For the biased walk, they are $f(i) = 1$ and $f(i) = (q/p)^i$. This is deeply connected to the theory of harmonic functions for Markov chains, which generalises to continuous-time diffusions and Brownian motion.

## § 8 — What "Ruin is Certain" Means as $N \to \infty$

A natural question: what happens if we remove the upper boundary and let the opponent have infinite wealth? This models a gambler vs. an infinitely rich casino.

Take the formula $P_i = \frac{1 - r^i}{1 - r^N}$ (biased case) and let $N \to \infty$.

**If $r > 1$ (i.e. $p < q$, unfavorable game):**

$$r^N \to \infty \implies P_i = \frac{1 - r^i}{1 - r^N} \to \frac{1 - r^i}{-r^N} \to 0$$

Ruin is certain.

**If $r < 1$ (i.e. $p > q$, favorable game):**

$$r^N \to 0 \implies P_i = \frac{1 - r^i}{1 - r^N} \to \frac{1 - r^i}{1} = 1 - r^i = 1 - \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^i$$

There is a positive probability of _never_ being ruined — specifically, $P(\text{survive forever}) = 1 - (q/p)^i$.

**If $r = 1$ (fair game):**

$$P_i = \frac{i}{N} \to 0 \text{ as } N \to \infty$$

Ruin is certain even in a fair game against an infinitely rich opponent.

> [!IMPORTANT] This last point is the gut-punch result. A **fair game against a casino with infinite resources = certain ruin**. There is no strategy that changes this. The only variable is _when_ you go broke, not _whether_. This is the mathematical reason why "systems" like Martingale betting or any other strategy fail in the long run — against an infinite house, the absorbing state 0 is unavoidable.

## § 9 — The Casino Calculation (Made Concrete)

You walk into a casino with $100. The house edge per bet is $p_{\text{house}} = 0.51$, so $p_{\text{you}} = 0.49$.

Assume for simplicity the casino has $M$ dollars and total wealth is $N = 100 + M$.

$$r = \frac{q}{p} = \frac{0.51}{0.49} \approx 1.0408$$

$$P_{100} = \frac{1 - (1.0408)^{100}}{1 - (1.0408)^{N}}$$

For $M = 10{,}000$ (total $N = 10{,}100$):

$$(1.0408)^{100} \approx 56.9 \qquad (1.0408)^{10{,}100} \approx \text{astronomically large}$$

$$P_{100} \approx \frac{1 - 56.9}{1 - \infty} \approx \frac{-55.9}{-\infty} \approx 0$$

In practice, with $N$ large: $P(\text{win}) \approx 1 - r^i = 1 - (1.0408)^{100}$... wait, that's the infinite-wealth limit, which gives a negative number — confirming ruin is certain.

The exact formula gives approximately **$P(\text{ruin}) > 99.8%$**.

## § 10 — Connection to the Big Picture

Here's the chain of ideas this feeds into, and what to study next:

```
Gambler's Ruin
     ↓
First-step analysis (recurrence relations for Markov chains)
     ↓
Harmonic functions for Markov chains
     ↓
Optional Stopping Theorem (discrete time)
     ↓
Doob's martingale convergence theorems
     ↓
Brownian motion as the limit of scaled random walks
     ↓
Dirichlet problem (harmonic analysis / PDEs)
     ↓
Itô calculus and stochastic differential equations
```

The Gambler's Ruin is literally the first step in this entire tower. The "first-step analysis" recurrence is the discrete analogue of solving a boundary value ODE. The martingale proof is the discrete analogue of the maximum principle for harmonic functions. When you later see Brownian motion hit a boundary, or an SDE absorbed at zero, you'll recognise the same structure.

## Quick Reference

| Quantity                            | Fair ($p = q = \frac{1}{2}$) | Biased ($p \neq q$, $r = q/p$)                               |
| ----------------------------------- | ---------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------ |
| Win probability $P_i$               | $\dfrac{i}{N}$               | $\dfrac{1 - r^i}{1 - r^N}$                                   |
| Ruin probability                    | $\dfrac{N-i}{N}$             | $1 - P_i$                                                    |
| Expected duration $D_i$             | $i(N-i)$                     | $\dfrac{1}{q-p}!\left[i - N\cdot\dfrac{1-r^i}{1-r^N}\right]$ |
| Win prob vs. $\infty$-rich opponent | $0$                          | $0$ if $p < \frac{1}{2}$; $;1-(q/p)^i$ if $p > \frac{1}{2}$  |
