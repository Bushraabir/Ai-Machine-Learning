We already know Bayes' Rule:

$$
P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A)\, P(A)}{P(B)}
$$

But in practice, we're almost never reasoning in a vacuum. There is usually some **background information** \(E\) already conditioning everything — for example: a patient being over 60, a coin coming from a specific batch, or prior observed data.

## Theorem (Bayes' Rule with Background Evidence)
For events $A$, $B$, $E$ with $P(B \cap E) > 0$:
$$
\boxed{
P(A \mid B, E) = \frac{P(B \mid A, E)\, P(A \mid E)}{P(B \mid E)}
}
$$
where:
$$
P(A \mid B, E) := P(A \mid B \cap E)
$$

This notation means we are conditioning on both \(B\) and \(E\) occurring simultaneously.

## Proof:

One of those results where the proof is almost insultingly short. Everything follows from the **definition of conditional probability**, applied twice.

**Step 1.** Definition of $P(A \mid B, E)$:

$$P(A \mid B, E) = \frac{P(A \cap B \cap E)}{P(B \cap E)}$$

**Step 2.** Expand the numerator by conditioning on $A \cap E$:

$$P(A \cap B \cap E) = P(B \mid A, E), P(A \cap E) = P(B \mid A, E), P(A \mid E), P(E)$$

**Step 3.** Expand the denominator:

$$P(B \cap E) = P(B \mid E), P(E)$$

**Step 4.** Divide and cancel:

$$P(A \mid B, E) = \frac{P(B \mid A, E), P(A \mid E), \cancel{P(E)}}{P(B \mid E), \cancel{P(E)}} = \frac{P(B \mid A, E), P(A \mid E)}{P(B \mid E)} \qquad \blacksquare$$

> [!note] _The $P(E)$ factors cancel perfectly. That's the whole trick. We never used anything deeper than the definition of conditional probability. Bayes inside a conditioned universe is Bayes, full stop._

---

## How to Read the Formula

Think of $E$ as **shrinking the sample space** to $\Omega_E = E$. In that smaller universe, every probability inherits $E$ as background — and Bayes holds with exactly the same structure:

| Ordinary Bayes             | Bayes with Extra Conditioning            |
| :------------------------- | :--------------------------------------- |
| $P(A)$ — prior on $A$      | $P(A \mid E)$ — prior on $A$, given $E$  |
| $P(B \mid A)$ — likelihood | $P(B \mid A, E)$ — likelihood, given $E$ |
| $P(B)$ — marginal          | $P(B \mid E)$ — marginal, given $E$      |
| $P(A \mid B)$ — posterior  | $P(A \mid B, E)$ — posterior, given $E$  |

> [!tip] _$E$ is not a nuisance. It's the totality of everything you knew before seeing $B$. It's load-bearing._

## Conditional Independence Simplifies Everything

In sequential updating, if $B_2 \perp B_1 \mid A$ — the new evidence is independent of old evidence, given the hypothesis — then:

$$P(B_2 \mid A, B_1) = P(B_2 \mid A)$$

and the formula simplifies to:

$$P(A \mid B_1, B_2) = \frac{P(B_2 \mid A) P(A \mid B_1)}{P(B_2 \mid B_1)}$$

> [!tip] _This is the **naïve Bayes** assumption — and it's the workhorse of an enormous amount of practical inference. Conditional independence is the assumption that makes Bayesian updating tractable at scale._
