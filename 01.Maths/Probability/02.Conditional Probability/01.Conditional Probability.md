
> _"Conditioning is the soul of statistics. Every time you learn something, you should update. The universe doesn't change — your information does."_

---

## Before the Definition — The Right Frame of Mind

Here is the situation. I have a probability space $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$. An experiment is run. I don't know the outcome, but someone tells me that event $B$ occurred. Now I want to reason about event $A$.

The critical insight is this:

> **Learning that $B$ occurred doesn't change the world. It changes my information about the world. Conditional probability is the mathematics of updated belief.**

The whole sample space $\Omega$ shrinks — in my mind — to $B$. Everything outside $B$ is now impossible. I renormalize. That's it. That's the whole idea.

Hold this picture before reading the formula. The formula should feel like the _obvious_ way to implement this picture, not like a definition pulled from thin air.

---

## The Definition

**Definition.** Let $(\Omega, \mathcal{F}, P)$ be a probability space. Let $B \in \mathcal{F}$ with $P(B) > 0$. The **conditional probability of $A$ given $B$** is

>[!important] $$\boxed{P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}.}$$

The restriction $P(B) > 0$ is essential — conditioning on a probability-zero event is undefined (the denominator would be zero, and the whole framework collapses).

---

## Why This Formula — The Derivation from First Principles

I want to _motivate_ the definition, not just state it. Here are two ways to arrive at it.

### Motivation 1: The Frequentist Picture

Imagine running the experiment $N$ times, where $N$ is enormous. Let:

- $n(A)$ = number of times $A$ occurs
- $n(B)$ = number of times $B$ occurs
- $n(A \cap B)$ = number of times both $A$ and $B$ occur

Among the runs where $B$ occurred, the fraction where $A$ also occurred is:

$$\frac{n(A \cap B)}{n(B)} = \frac{n(A \cap B)/N}{n(B)/N} \approx \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}.$$

So $P(A \mid B)$ is the long-run frequency of $A$ among those trials where $B$ happened. The formula is _forced_ on us by this frequentist interpretation.

### Motivation 2: The Axiomatic Picture

When we condition on $B$, we want a new probability measure $Q$ on $\Omega$ that:

1. Assigns zero probability to everything outside $B$ — those outcomes are impossible
2. Agrees with $P$ on the relative likelihood of outcomes _inside_ $B$
3. Is a valid probability measure (so $Q(B) = 1$)

The unique function satisfying all three is:

$$Q(A) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)}.$$

_Check:_ $Q(\Omega) = Q(B) + Q(B^c) = \frac{P(B)}{P(B)} + \frac{P(\emptyset)}{P(B)} = 1$. Countable additivity follows from $P$'s additivity. Positivity is clear. This is the unique such measure. $\square$

> _This second motivation is deeper. It says conditional probability isn't a trick — it's the mathematically forced answer to "what is a probability measure on the restricted universe $B$?"_

## Immediate Consequences — What the Definition Gives for Free

### The Multiplication Rule

Rearrange the definition:

$$P(A \cap B) = P(A \mid B), P(B).$$

This is the **Multiplication Rule** — the probability that both $A$ and $B$ occur equals the probability $B$ occurs times the conditional probability of $A$ given $B$. Extend it:

$$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(A \mid B \cap C), P(B \mid C), P(C).$$

In general, for events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$:

$$P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = P(A_1), P(A_2 \mid A_1), P(A_3 \mid A_1 \cap A_2) \cdots P(A_n \mid A_1 \cap \cdots \cap A_{n-1}).$$

_Proof:_ Telescope — each term is defined because we condition on events of positive probability (assuming we proceed in order). $\square$

> _I think of this as peeling an onion. The probability of $n$ things all happening is computed by: first thing happens, then second given first, then third given both, etc._

---

### $P(,\cdot \mid B)$ is a Legitimate Probability Measure

This is worth stating explicitly. For fixed $B$ with $P(B) > 0$, the function $A \mapsto P(A \mid B)$ satisfies all the Kolmogorov axioms:

1. **Non-negativity:** $P(A \mid B) \geq 0$ — clear, since numerator and denominator are non-negative.
2. **Normalization:** $P(\Omega \mid B) = \frac{P(\Omega \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B)}{P(B)} = 1$.
3. **Countable additivity:** For disjoint $A_1, A_2, \ldots$:

$$P!\left(\bigcup_i A_i ;\Big|; B\right) = \frac{P!\left(\bigcup_i A_i \cap B\right)}{P(B)} = \frac{\sum_i P(A_i \cap B)}{P(B)} = \sum_i P(A_i \mid B).$$

**Why this matters:** Every theorem about unconditional probability — the Law of Total Probability, Bayes' Theorem, any inequality — holds inside the conditional world too. Conditioning on $B$ just replaces $P$ with $P(,\cdot \mid B)$ throughout.

## The Intuition, Carefully

Let me be precise about what conditional probability is _doing_ geometrically.

Think of $\Omega$ as a rectangle, with area representing probability. $A$ and $B$ are blobs inside it.

$$\underbrace{P(A \mid B)}_{\text{fraction of } B \text{ covered by } A} = \frac{\overbrace{P(A \cap B)}^{\text{overlap of } A \text{ and } B}}{\underbrace{P(B)}_{\text{total size of } B}}.$$

When I learn $B$ occurred:

- I **zoom in** on $B$, ignoring the rest of $\Omega$
- I **renormalize** so $B$ has total probability 1
- $A$'s probability is now its _share_ of $B$

This renormalization is the entire content of the formula. Nothing more, nothing less.

>[!tip] ## Common Mistakes and Confusions
>
>### Mistake 1: Confusing $P(A \mid B)$ with $P(B \mid A)$
>
These are _completely different_ quantities. This confusion is so common it has a name: the **Prosecutor's Fallacy**.
>
> "The probability that an innocent person has this DNA match is 1 in a million."  
> _(translated incorrectly as)_  
> "The probability this person is innocent, given the DNA match, is 1 in a million."
>
The first is $P(\text{match} \mid \text{innocent}) = 10^{-6}$. The second is $P(\text{innocent} \mid \text{match})$, which depends on the prior probability of innocence (base rate). These are related by Bayes' Theorem — they are not the same. → See [[02.Bayes' Theorem]]
>
>### Mistake 2: Assuming Conditioning Always Reduces Probability
>
$P(A \mid B)$ can be greater than, less than, or equal to $P(A)$. Conditioning on $B$ can _increase_ the probability of $A$ (if $B$ is evidence for $A$), decrease it (if $B$ is evidence against $A$), or leave it unchanged (if $A \perp B$).
>
Example: Roll a fair die.
>
>- $A = {6}$, $B = {4, 5, 6}$
>- $P(A) = 1/6$, but $P(A \mid B) = 1/3 > P(A)$
>
Conditioning on $B$ (the roll is at least 4) _raises_ the probability of rolling a 6.
>
>### Mistake 3: Forgetting the Base Rate
>
The base rate — the unconditional $P(A)$ — always matters when computing conditional probabilities via Bayes. Ignoring it produces absurd conclusions.
