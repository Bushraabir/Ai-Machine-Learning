##  Definition

Two events $A$ and $B$ are **independent** if:

$$\boxed{P(A \cap B) = P(A), P(B)}$$

That's it. That's the definition. Everything else is consequence.

The conditional probability interpretation — which is often given as the definition in introductory treatments — is:

$$P(A \mid B) = P(A) \qquad \text{(assuming } P(B) > 0\text{)}$$

**These are equivalent** (when $P(B) > 0$), since:

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(A \cap B)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(A),P(B)}{P(B)} = P(A)$$

But the product definition $P(A \cap B) = P(A) P(B)$ is **primary** — it works even when $P(B) = 0$, and it's symmetric in $A$ and $B$ without any conditional probability machinery.

> _Blitzstein is emphatic about this: use the product rule as the definition. The conditional probability version is a theorem, not the definition. This matters because it keeps the definition clean and avoids division-by-zero edge cases._

>[!important] We write $A \perp B$ to denote independence.

## Immediate Consequences

>[!formula] **Proposition.** If $A \perp B$, then:
>
>1. $A \perp B^c$
>2. $A^c \perp B$
>3. $A^c \perp B^c$

**Proof of (1).** We want to show $P(A \cap B^c) = P(A)P(B^c)$.

$$P(A \cap B^c) = P(A) - P(A \cap B) = P(A) - P(A),P(B) = P(A)(1 - P(B)) = P(A),P(B^c)$$

$\blacksquare$

(2) and (3) follow by symmetric arguments, or by applying (1) twice.)

> _This is a sanity check that the definition is doing the right thing. If knowing $B$ occurred doesn't change the probability of $A$, then knowing $B$ didn't occur shouldn't change it either. The complement of irrelevant information is still irrelevant._

>[!important] ## Independence vs Disjointness 
>
**Disjoint** (mutually exclusive): $A \cap B = \emptyset$, i.e. $P(A \cap B) = 0$.
**Independent**: $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$.
>
These are **almost never the same thing** (unless one of the events has probability zero).


![[Pasted image 20260403104713.png]]

If $A$ and $B$ are disjoint and both have positive probability:

$$P(A \cap B) = 0 \neq P(A),P(B) > 0$$

So disjoint events with positive probability are **always dependent**. Knowing $A$ occurred _rules out_ $B$ entirely — that's as dependent as it gets.

![[independence_vs_disjointness.png]]

>1. **One Flip = Disjoint (Not Independent)**
		If you flip **one coin one time**, getting "Heads" and "Tails" are **disjoint** events.
>
>		- **Why disjoint?** They cannot both happen at once
>		- **Why NOT independent?** They are actually **highly dependent**. If I tell you the coin landed on Heads, you know with 100% certainty it is _not_ Tails. One event completely rules out the other.
>
>2. **Two Flips = Independent (Not Disjoint)**
>		If you flip **two different coins** (or one coin twice), the result of "Coin 1" and the result of "Coin 2" are **independent**.
>
>		- **Why independent?** If Coin 1 is Heads, it has zero effect on the probability of Coin 2 being Heads.
>		- **Why NOT disjoint?** They _can_ both happen at the same time. You can absolutely have both Coin 1 and Coin 2 land on Heads.

---

**The Rule of Thumb:**

- **Disjoint** = They are "mutually exclusive" (It's either one or the other).
- **Independent** = They don't care about each other (One happening doesn't change the odds of the other).

## Pairwise vs Mutual Independence

Here is where things get genuinely interesting. Independence does not scale naïvely.

![[pairindependence_vs_mutualindependence.png]]
### Pairwise Independence

Events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ are **pairwise independent** if:

$$P(A_i \cap A_j) = P(A_i),P(A_j) \qquad \text{for all } i \neq j$$

### Mutual Independence

Events $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ are **mutually independent** if for **every subset** $S \subseteq {1, 2, \ldots, n}$:

$$P!\left(\bigcap_{i \in S} A_i\right) = \prod_{i \in S} P(A_i)$$

**Mutual independence implies pairwise independence. The converse is false.**

---

### The Classic Counterexample (Bernstein)

Let $(X, Y)$ be chosen uniformly from ${(0,0), (1,0), (0,1), (1,1)}$ — equivalently, flip two fair coins.

Define:

- $A$ = "first coin is heads" = ${(1,0),(1,1)}$, so $P(A) = 1/2$
- $B$ = "second coin is heads" = ${(0,1),(1,1)}$, so $P(B) = 1/2$
- $C$ = "exactly one head" = ${(1,0),(0,1)}$, so $P(C) = 1/2$

**Check pairwise independence:**

$$P(A \cap B) = P({(1,1)}) = \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{2} = P(A),P(B) \checkmark$$

$$P(A \cap C) = P({(1,0)}) = \frac{1}{4} = P(A),P(C) \checkmark$$

$$P(B \cap C) = P({(0,1)}) = \frac{1}{4} = P(B),P(C) \checkmark$$

**Check mutual independence:**

$$P(A \cap B \cap C) = P(\emptyset) = 0 \neq \frac{1}{8} = P(A),P(B),P(C) \quad \times$$

So $A, B, C$ are **pairwise independent but not mutually independent.**

> _This example is beautiful and slightly unsettling. Knowing any one of $A$, $B$, $C$ tells you nothing about any other one individually — but knowing any two of them tells you everything about the third. The joint structure can carry dependence that the pairwise structure completely hides._

## Conditional Independence

Events $A$ and $B$ are **conditionally independent given $E$** (written $A \perp B \mid E$) if:

$$P(A \cap B \mid E) = P(A \mid E)P(B \mid E)$$

Equivalently (when $P(B \cap E) > 0$):

$$P(A \mid B, E) = P(A \mid E)$$

**Conditional independence does not imply independence. Independence does not imply conditional independence.**

Both directions can fail. Here's a quick intuition for each:

**Independent but conditionally dependent.** Two fair coin flips $A$ and $B$ are independent. But condition on the event $E$ = "they show the same face" — now $A$ and $B$ are completely determined by each other. Conditioning created dependence.

**Conditionally independent but marginally dependent.** The two-bag example from [[05.Bayes' Rule with Extra Conditioning]]: draws $R_1$ and $R_2$ are conditionally independent given the bag, but marginally dependent because the bag is unknown. The latent variable (bag identity) is doing all the work.

## Independence and Multiplication

The product rule for independent events extends cleanly:

**Proposition.** If $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ are mutually independent, then:

$$P(A_1 \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = \prod_{i=1}^n P(A_i)$$

And complements of mutually independent events are also mutually independent, so:

$$P(A_1^c \cap A_2 \cap \cdots \cap A_n) = P(A_1^c) \prod_{i=2}^n P(A_i)$$

This is the engine behind many calculations involving repeated independent trials.

**Example.** A system has $n$ components, each failing independently with probability $p$. The system fails if **any** component fails. What's the probability the system survives?

$$P(\text{all survive}) = \prod_{i=1}^n (1-p) = (1-p)^n$$

Complementarily: $P(\text{system fails}) = 1 - (1-p)^n$.

This is the prototype for the **complement + independence** strategy: instead of counting all the ways things can go wrong, compute the probability everything goes right.

---

## A Worked Example: The Birthday-style Calculation

**Problem.** Each of $n$ people independently sends a message successfully with probability $p$. What is the probability that **at least one** message gets through?

Let $S_i$ = "person $i$'s message succeeds." Then $S_1, \ldots, S_n$ are mutually independent.

$$P(\text{at least one succeeds}) = 1 - P(\text{none succeed}) = 1 - \prod_{i=1}^n P(S_i^c) = 1 - (1-p)^n$$

For $p = 0.1$, $n = 10$: $1 - (0.9)^{10} \approx 0.651$.

For $p = 0.1$, $n = 20$: $1 - (0.9)^{20} \approx 0.878$.

> _The complement trick is so useful it should be a reflex. Whenever you see "at least one," immediately think: $1 - P(\text{none})$. The independence then lets you factor the "none" event into a product._

## Independence in Terms of the Joint Distribution

The cleanest way to state independence for a finite sample space: $A \perp B$ if and only if the **indicator random variables** $\mathbf{1}_A$ and $\mathbf{1}_B$ satisfy:

$$P(\mathbf{1}_A = a, \mathbf{1}_B = b) = P(\mathbf{1}_A = a), P(\mathbf{1}_B = b) \qquad \text{for all } a, b \in {0,1}$$

This is the bridge to **independence of random variables**, which is the same idea but extended to the full joint distribution.

## Summary

| Concept                     | Definition                                              |
| --------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------- |
| $A \perp B$                 | $P(A \cap B) = P(A)P(B)$                                |
| $A \perp B \mid E$          | $P(A \cap B \mid E) = P(A \mid E)P(B \mid E)$           |
| Pairwise independence       | Product rule holds for all pairs                        |
| Mutual independence         | Product rule holds for all subsets                      |
| Disjoint $\neq$ independent | Disjoint events with positive prob are always dependent |

The hierarchy: **mutual independence $\Rightarrow$ pairwise independence**, but not conversely.  
And: **independence $\not\Rightarrow$ conditional independence**, and **conditional independence $\not\Rightarrow$ independence**.
