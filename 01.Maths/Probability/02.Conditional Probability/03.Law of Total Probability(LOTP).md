

> [!quote] The most important conditioning theorem in the course. Everything — Bayes, LOTE, LOTV — descends from this. Understand it deeply.

## 1 · Setup: What Is a Partition?

A collection ${A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n}$ is a **partition** of $\Omega$ if and only if:

| Condition                | Meaning                                                                |
| :----------------------- | :--------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Mutually exclusive**   | $A_i \cap A_j = \emptyset$ for $i \neq j$ — slices don't overlap       |
| **Exhaustive**           | $A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \cdots \cup A_n = \Omega$ — slices cover everything |
| **Positive probability** | $P(A_i) > 0$ for all $i$ — each slice is reachable                     |

Think of it as cutting a pie into non-overlapping pieces that together make the whole pie. Every outcome in $\Omega$ belongs to exactly one $A_i$.

---

## 2 · The Theorem (Discrete Case)

> [!theorem] Law of Total Probability (Discrete) Let ${A_1, \ldots, A_n}$ be a partition of $\Omega$. Then for any event $B$: $$\boxed{P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^{n} P(B \mid A_i), P(A_i)}$$

This is a **weighted average** of conditional probabilities. The weight on $P(B \mid A_i)$ is exactly $P(A_i)$ — how likely that slice is to occur.

---

## 3 · Proof:

$$B = B \cap \Omega = B \cap \left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n A_i\right) = \bigcup_{i=1}^n (B \cap A_i)$$

Since the $A_i$ are mutually exclusive, so are the $B \cap A_i$. By **countable additivity**:

$$P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(B \cap A_i)$$

Apply the definition of conditional probability $P(B \cap A_i) = P(B \mid A_i),P(A_i)$:

$$P(B) = \sum_{i=1}^n P(B \mid A_i), P(A_i) \qquad \blacksquare$$

> [!note] What's actually happening geometrically The partition ${A_1, \ldots, A_n}$ slices $\Omega$ into non-overlapping pieces. The event $B$ intersects each slice differently. LOTP says: the probability of $B$ is a **weighted average** of its conditional probabilities in each slice, weighted by the probability of each slice.
> 
> $$P(B) = \sum_i \underbrace{P(B \mid A_i)}_{\text{prob of } B \text{ in slice } i} \cdot \underbrace{P(A_i)}_{\text{weight of slice } i}$$
> 
> The unconditional probability is the mixture of the conditional probabilities. LOTP averages over your uncertainty about _which slice you're actually in_.

## 4 · The Three Standard Partitions

In practice, the partition almost always takes one of three forms. Recognise them on sight.


### Form 1 · Binary partition ${A, A^c}$

![[Pasted image 20260402124831.png]]
		
$$P(B) = P(B \mid A),P(A) + P(B \mid A^c),P(A^c)$$

> _This is the one I reach for first. If $A$ and $A^c$ both give tractable conditionals, you're done. Only if neither works cleanly do I look for a finer partition._

Use when there's a natural **yes/no** condition:

1) disease or not, 
2) red card or not, 
3) heads or tails.

---

### Form 2 · Partition by a discrete RV $Y \in {y_1, \ldots, y_n}$


![[Pasted image 20260402132903.png]]


$$P(B) = \sum_i P(B \mid Y = y_i), P(Y = y_i)$$

Use when conditioning on $Y$ makes $B$ easy to analyse. The discrete RV becomes the "case selector."

---

### Form 3 · Partition by a continuous RV $Y$

![[Pasted image 20260402133418.png]]

$$P(B) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P(B \mid Y = y), f_Y(y), dy$$

Use in continuous models. Each "partition element" is now an infinitesimal slice $[y, y+dy]$.

---

## §5 · Why the Integral Appears in the Continuous Case

This is the conceptual crux — don't gloss over it.

When conditioning on a **continuous** $Y$:

- There are **uncountably many** possible values $y$
- $P(Y = y) = 0$ for every single $y$ — you cannot condition using the definition directly
- Instead you condition via the **PDF** $f_Y(y)$

Think of the range of $Y$ divided into infinitesimal slices $[y,, y + dy]$:

| Quantity                            | Expression                     |
| :---------------------------------- | :----------------------------- |
| Prob. that $Y$ lands in the slice   | $\approx f_Y(y),dy$            |
| Prob. of $B$ given that slice       | $\approx P(B \mid Y = y)$      |
| **Contribution of slice to $P(B)$** | $P(B \mid Y=y)\cdot f_Y(y),dy$ |

Add up contributions over **all** slices $\Rightarrow$ integrate:

$$\boxed{P(B) = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} P(B \mid Y=y), f_Y(y), dy}$$

> [!important] One-sentence intuition The integral is simply a **continuous weighted sum** — the same idea as LOTP, but uncountably many terms forces a sum into an integral.

**The parallel is exact:**

$$\text{Discrete:}\quad P(B) = \sum_i P(B \mid A_i),P(A_i)$$ $$\text{Continuous:}\quad P(B) = \int P(B \mid Y=y),f_Y(y),dy$$

Both say: overall probability = **weighted average of conditional probabilities**.

---

## 6 · Why This Also Gives LOTE

**Expectation is _also_ a weighted average** — the argument is identical.

**Discrete:** $$E[X] = \sum_i E[X \mid Y=y_i], P(Y=y_i)$$

**Continuous:** $$E[X] = \int E[X \mid Y=y], f_Y(y), dy$$

> [!important] LOTP and LOTE are the same theorem
> 
> - **LOTP** acts on probabilities (events)
> - **LOTE** acts on expectations (random variables)
> - LOTP is the special case $X = \mathbf{1}_B$: then $E[X \mid Y=y] = P(B \mid Y=y)$

## 7 · Examples

### Example 1 · Two Coins (Warm-Up)

Two coins: coin 1 fair ($p = 1/2$), coin 2 biased ($p = 3/4$). Pick one uniformly at random and flip. Find $P(H)$.

**Partition:** $A_1 = {\text{chose coin 1}}$, $A_2 = {\text{chose coin 2}}$.

$$P(H) = P(H \mid A_1),P(A_1) + P(H \mid A_2),P(A_2) = \frac{1}{2}\cdot\frac{1}{2} + \frac{3}{4}\cdot\frac{1}{2} = \frac{1}{4} + \frac{3}{8} = \frac{5}{8}$$

> [!note] Notice The answer $5/8$ sits strictly between $1/2$ and $3/4$. LOTP is producing a **convex combination** of the conditional probabilities. The unconditional probability always lies between the min and max of the conditionals.

---

### Example 2 · Gambler's Ruin (Recursive LOTP)

A gambler starts with $$k$, bets $$1$ per round. Wins with prob $p$, loses with prob $q = 1-p$. Game ends at $$N$ (win) or $$0$ (ruin). Let $P_k$ = prob of winning from $k$.

**Partition at the first step:** win or lose the first bet.

$$P_k = p\cdot P_{k+1} + q\cdot P_{k-1}, \quad k = 1, \ldots, N-1$$

**Boundary conditions:** $P_0 = 0$, $P_N = 1$.

**Solution** (for $p \neq q$): $$P_k = \frac{1 - (q/p)^k}{1 - (q/p)^N}$$

**For $p = q = 1/2$:** $P_k = k/N$.

> [!note] Pattern to internalise **Condition on the first step → write a recurrence.** The partition has only two elements, but applying LOTP at each state generates the full recurrence. This is one of the most powerful patterns in discrete probability. Memorise the structure: $$P_k = p \cdot (\text{value after win}) + q \cdot (\text{value after loss})$$

---

### Example 3 · LOTP as the Denominator Inside Bayes

From [[02.Bayes' Theorem]]: the denominator is _always_ LOTP.

$$P(A \mid B) = \frac{P(B \mid A),P(A)}{P(B)} = \frac{P(B \mid A)P(A)}{P(B \mid A)P(A) + P(B \mid A^c)P(A^c)}$$

> Bayes without LOTP is incomplete — you cannot compute the normalising constant. **LOTP is the computational engine inside Bayes.**
> 
> Every Bayesian calculation: **Bayes in the numerator, LOTP in the denominator.**

---

### Example 4 · Random Sum — [[12.Poisson vs Binomial]]

Let $N \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$ and, given $N = n$, let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} \text{Bern}(p)$. Set $S = X_1 + \cdots + X_N$ (with $S = 0$ when $N = 0$).

**Compute $P(S = k)$** by conditioning on $N$:

$$P(S = k) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} P(S = k \mid N = n),P(N = n) = \sum_{n=k}^{\infty} \binom{n}{k}p^k(1-p)^{n-k}\cdot\frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^n}{n!}$$

This sum evaluates to $S \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda p)$.

> [!note] Pattern: conditioning on the random index Conditioning on $N$ makes $S \mid N = n$ a Binomial, which is tractable. Then LOTP averages over $N$. This is the canonical technique for random sums.

---

### Example 5 · First-Step Analysis — $E[X]$ for Geometric

Let $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ (type 2). Derive $E[X]$ using LOTP applied to expectations.

**Partition on the first trial:**

$$E[X] = E[X \mid \text{success}],p + E[X \mid \text{failure}],q$$

- Given success: $X = 1$, so $E[X \mid \text{success}] = 1$
- Given failure: used one trial, back to start by **memorylessness**, so $E[X \mid \text{failure}] = 1 + E[X]$

$$E[X] = p\cdot 1 + q\cdot(1 + E[X]) = 1 + q,E[X]$$

$$E[X](1 - q) = 1 \implies \boxed{E[X] = \frac{1}{p}}$$

> The memorylessness of the Geometric is what makes the "back to the start" step valid. Without it, the recursion doesn't close.

## 8 · The Full Hierarchy: [[02.Adam's Law (Law of Total Expectation)|Adam]], [[08.Eve's Law (Law of Total Variance)|Eve]], and [[08.Continuous Bayes' Rule and LOTP|LOTP]]

Blitzstein calls LOTE the **Adam Law** and the law of total variance the **Eve Law**. LOTP is a special case of both.

$$\text{Adam's Law (LOTE):} \quad E[X] = E!\left[E[X \mid Y]\right]$$

$$\text{Eve's Law (LOTV):} \quad \operatorname{Var}(X) = E!\left[\operatorname{Var}(X \mid Y)\right] + \operatorname{Var}!\left(E[X \mid Y]\right)$$

**LOTP** is Adam's Law with $X = \mathbf{1}_B$:

$$P(B) = E[\mathbf{1}_B] = E!\left[E[\mathbf{1}_B \mid Y]\right] = E!\left[P(B \mid Y)\right]$$

> [!important] The containment chain $$\text{LOTP} \subset \text{Adam (LOTE)} \subset \text{Eve (LOTV)}$$ Each theorem contains the previous as a special case. Learn them in order — each one you understand makes the next one obvious.

## 9 · Common Mistakes

> [!warning] Mistake 1 · Dropping the weights — most common by far $$P(B) \neq P(B \mid A) + P(B \mid A^c) \qquad \textbf{WRONG}$$ $$P(B) = P(B \mid A),P(A) + P(B \mid A^c),P(A^c) \qquad \textbf{CORRECT}$$ If your answer doesn't look like a weighted average of conditional probabilities, something went wrong.

> [!warning] Mistake 2 · Partition doesn't cover $\Omega$ If $A_1, \ldots, A_n$ don't exhaust $\Omega$, you get $\sum_i P(B \cap A_i) \neq P(B)$. Always verify exhaustiveness before applying LOTP.

> [!warning] Mistake 3 · Conditioning on a zero-probability event naively $P(B \mid A_i)$ requires $P(A_i) > 0$. In continuous settings, conditioning on ${Y = y}$ (a measure-zero event) requires **measure-theoretic conditional expectation**, not the elementary definition. The formula still works, but the justification is different. Note this and move on — it's handled properly in Stat 210.

## 10 · Summary — The Shape of the Theorem

| Setting           | Formula                                 | When to use                        |
| :---------------- | :-------------------------------------- | :--------------------------------- |
| Binary partition  | $P(B \mid A)P(A) + P(B \mid A^c)P(A^c)$ | Natural yes/no condition           |
| Discrete RV $Y$   | $\sum_i P(B \mid Y=y_i),P(Y=y_i)$       | Conditioning on $Y$ simplifies $B$ |
| Continuous RV $Y$ | $\int P(B \mid Y=y),f_Y(y),dy$          | Continuous models                  |
| Expectation form  | $E[X] = E[E[X \mid Y]]$                 | Adam's Law — operator form         |

> [!tip] How to recognise when LOTP applies You're asked for an unconditional probability (or expectation), but the problem has hidden structure — a random mechanism, a random index, an unknown parameter. **Condition on whatever makes the problem simple.** Then LOTP glues the pieces back together automatically.
