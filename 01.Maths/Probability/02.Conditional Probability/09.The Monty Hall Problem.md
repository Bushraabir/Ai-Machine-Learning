— the problem that has humiliated PhDs, sparked letters to newspapers, and remains the cleanest lesson in conditional probability I know_

---

## The Problem

You're on a game show. There are **3 doors**. Behind one is a car. Behind the other two are goats. You pick door 1. The host, Monty Hall, **who knows where the car is**, opens one of the other two doors to reveal a goat — say he opens door 3. He then offers you the choice: **stick with door 1, or switch to door 2**.

**Should you switch?**

The instinctive answer is: _it doesn't matter, it's 50-50 now._ This is wrong. You should switch. Switching wins with probability $\mathbf{2/3}$.

This result is so counterintuitive that when Marilyn vos Savant published it in 1990, she received roughly 10,000 letters — many from professional mathematicians — telling her she was wrong. She was right. They were not.

Let's understand exactly why, from multiple angles, until it becomes _obvious_.

---

## Solution 1 — Sample Space (Naive Probability, Direct)

Enumerate. The car is equally likely behind any door, probability $1/3$ each. You pick door 1. Three cases:

|Car behind|Monty opens|Stick wins?|Switch wins?|
|---|---|---|---|
|Door 1|Door 2 or 3 (his choice)|✓|✗|
|Door 2|Door 3 (forced)|✗|✓|
|Door 3|Door 2 (forced)|✗|✓|

Each row has probability $1/3$. Stick wins in 1 out of 3 cases. Switch wins in 2 out of 3.

$$P(\text{win} \mid \text{switch}) = \frac{2}{3}, \qquad P(\text{win} \mid \text{stick}) = \frac{1}{3}$$

> The key observation: when you initially pick the wrong door (probability $2/3$), Monty is _forced_ to open the only other goat door, so the car is guaranteed to be behind the door you'd switch to. You only lose by switching when you picked correctly from the start, which happens with probability $1/3$.

---

## Solution 2 — Conditional Probability (The Full Bayes Treatment)

Let's be rigorous. Define events:

- $C_i$ = car is behind door $i$, for $i = 1, 2, 3$
- $M_3$ = Monty opens door 3

We want $P(C_1 \mid M_3)$ (probability car is behind door 1, given Monty opened door 3) — this is the probability that _sticking_ wins.

**Prior:** $P(C_1) = P(C_2) = P(C_3) = 1/3$.

**Likelihoods** — this is where Monty's behavior matters:

$$P(M_3 \mid C_1) = \frac{1}{2}$$ Monty can open either door 2 or door 3 (both goats). By symmetry, $1/2$.

$$P(M_3 \mid C_2) = 1$$ Monty cannot open door 2 (car there), cannot open door 1 (your pick). He must open door 3.

$$P(M_3 \mid C_3) = 0$$ Monty cannot open door 3 — that would reveal the car.

**LOTP to get the marginal:** $$P(M_3) = P(M_3 \mid C_1)P(C_1) + P(M_3 \mid C_2)P(C_2) + P(M_3 \mid C_3)P(C_3)$$ $$= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 1 \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 0 \cdot \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{6} + \frac{1}{3} = \frac{1}{2}$$

**Bayes' theorem:**

$$P(C_1 \mid M_3) = \frac{P(M_3 \mid C_1) P(C_1)}{P(M_3)} = \frac{\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3}}{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{1}{3}$$

$$P(C_2 \mid M_3) = \frac{P(M_3 \mid C_2) P(C_2)}{P(M_3)} = \frac{1 \cdot \frac{1}{3}}{\frac{1}{2}} = \frac{2}{3}$$

So:

$$\boxed{P(\text{win} \mid \text{switch}) = P(C_2 \mid M_3) = \frac{2}{3}}$$

> **What Bayes tells us that the table argument doesn't:** The update is entirely driven by the likelihood ratio $P(M_3 \mid C_1) / P(M_3 \mid C_2) = \frac{1/2}{1} = \frac{1}{2}$. Door 2 is twice as likely to have the car as door 1 after Monty opens door 3, because Monty was twice as likely to open door 3 if the car were behind door 2 (he had no choice) than if it were behind door 1 (he had a choice). The information in Monty's action is asymmetric.

---

## The Crux: Why Intuition Fails

The wrong reasoning: _"There are two doors left, one has the car, so each has probability $1/2$."_

This would be correct if Monty **picked a door uniformly at random** and happened to reveal a goat. That is a different problem. In that version, the answer genuinely is $1/2$.

The error is ignoring the **dependence between Monty's action and the car's location.** Monty is not choosing randomly — he is choosing with full knowledge. His action carries information. Specifically:

> If you picked wrong (prob $2/3$), Monty's hand is forced — he must open the one remaining goat door. The switch door is the car with certainty.  
> If you picked right (prob $1/3$), Monty has a free choice. The switch door is a goat with certainty.

Monty's opening of door 3 is not a symmetric event. It is more likely to occur when the car is behind door 2 than when it's behind door 1. That asymmetry is the entire content of the problem.

---

## The "Random Monty" Variant — Why it Actually is 50-50

It's worth solving this explicitly to make the contrast sharp.

**Variant:** Monty picks one of the two remaining doors _uniformly at random_. He opens it and it happens to show a goat. Now should you switch?

Likelihoods now: $$P(M_3 \mid C_1) = \frac{1}{2}, \quad P(M_3 \mid C_2) = \frac{1}{2}, \quad P(M_3 \mid C_3) = \frac{1}{2}$$

Wait — $P(M_3 \mid C_3) = 1/2$? Yes, because Monty picks randomly, so he might open door 3 even if the car is there. But we're conditioning on him opening door 3 and revealing a goat, so we need to be more careful.

Let $G_3$ = door 3 is opened and shows a goat.

$$P(G_3 \mid C_1) = \frac{1}{2}, \quad P(G_3 \mid C_2) = \frac{1}{2}, \quad P(G_3 \mid C_3) = 0$$

Now Bayes: $$P(C_1 \mid G_3) = \frac{\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3}}{\frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + 0} = \frac{1/6}{1/3} = \frac{1}{2}$$

$$P(C_2 \mid G_3) = \frac{1}{2}$$

In this version, it genuinely is $50$-$50$. Switching doesn't help. The difference: Monty's equal likelihood of opening door 3 regardless of whether the car is behind door 1 or door 2 means his action contains no asymmetric information. There's nothing to update on.

> **This is the cleanest demonstration that the answer to Monty Hall depends critically on the host's strategy, not just on what you observe.** Two scenarios with identical observations ($k=3$ opened, goat revealed) yield completely different posterior probabilities because the _data-generating process_ differs.

## Generalization: $n$ Doors

There are $n$ doors, car behind one, you pick door 1, Monty opens $n-2$ goat doors (all of the other goat doors except one), leaving your door and one other.

$$P(\text{win} \mid \text{switch}) = \frac{n-1}{n} \cdot \frac{1}{n-2} \cdot (n-2) = \frac{n-1}{n}$$

More cleanly: you win by switching iff your initial pick was wrong, which has probability $\frac{n-1}{n}$. When your initial pick is wrong, Monty is forced to leave the car door closed — switching always wins in that case. So:

$$P(\text{win} \mid \text{switch}) = \frac{n-1}{n}$$

For $n=3$: $2/3$. For $n=100$: $99/100$. As $n \to \infty$, switching is essentially certain to win.

> With 100 doors, you pick one, Monty opens 98 goat doors, leaving yours and one other. The remaining door is almost surely the car. The original problem scaled up until the answer is impossible to get wrong.

---

## Simulation Argument (for the skeptic in me)

If I still felt uneasy, here's how I'd think about running this:

Play the game $N$ times, always switching.

- Fraction of games where initial pick was correct: $\approx 1/3$. In these, switching loses.
- Fraction where initial pick was wrong: $\approx 2/3$. In these, Monty's forced, switching wins.

Win rate by switching $\approx 2/3$. This is not a proof but it's the sanity check that the answer isn't absurd.

---

## What This Problem Is Really About

Monty Hall is a lesson in three things:

**1. Conditional probability changes the sample space.** After Monty opens door 3, we don't have a uniform distribution over ${C_1, C_2}$. We have a posterior, and the posterior is not uniform because the prior wasn't updated symmetrically.

**2. The data-generating process is part of the model.** The probability of an observation depends not just on what you see but on _how it was generated_. Monty's knowledge and strategy are part of the probability model. Change the strategy, change the answer — even with identical observations.

**3. Information is asymmetric when the agent has knowledge.** Monty's action is a function of the car's location. That functional dependence is exactly what Bayes captures through the likelihood. When $P(\text{observation} \mid \text{hypothesis})$ differs across hypotheses, the observation has discriminatory power. That's what posterior updating means.

---

_The thing that stays with me: the answer is $2/3$ not because of some trick or technicality. It's $2/3$ because when you picked your door, you were probably wrong. Monty can't change that. All he does is make the alternative door carry all the probability mass of your initial mistake. Every time you switch, you're essentially betting that you were wrong the first time — and you probably were._
