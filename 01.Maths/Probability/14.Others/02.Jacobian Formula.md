> [!note] Big Picture The Jacobian formula is the answer to one question: **if I know the distribution of $X$, what is the distribution of $Y = g(X)$?** It is the fundamental tool for transforming continuous distributions. Every time you derive the distribution of a ratio, a product, a square root, or any nonlinear function of a random variable — you are using this. Get the intuition for _why_ it works, not just the formula.

---

## 1. The Problem — Why We Need This

Suppose $X$ has a known PDF $f_X(x)$. Define $Y = g(X)$ for some function $g$. We want $f_Y(y)$.

**The naive wrong answer:** $f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y))$.

This is wrong. It ignores the fact that $g$ stretches or compresses regions of the real line, and probability mass must be conserved. The Jacobian corrects for this stretching.

> **Personal note:** I made this mistake on a problem set early on — just substituted $x = g^{-1}(y)$ into $f_X$ and forgot the correction factor. The resulting "PDF" didn't integrate to 1. That failure is the best way to remember why the Jacobian exists.

---

## 2. The 1D Case — Derivation from First Principles

### Setup

Let $g: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be **strictly monotone and differentiable** on $\text{supp}(X)$. Then $g$ is invertible on this domain. Write $h = g^{-1}$, so $x = h(y)$.

### The CDF Method (The Honest Derivation)

**Case 1: $g$ is strictly increasing.**

$$F_Y(y) = P(Y \leq y) = P(g(X) \leq y) = P(X \leq g^{-1}(y)) = F_X(h(y))$$

Differentiate both sides with respect to $y$:

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(h(y)) \cdot h'(y)$$

Since $g$ is increasing, $h' = (g^{-1})' > 0$, so this is positive. ✓

**Case 2: $g$ is strictly decreasing.**

$$F_Y(y) = P(Y \leq y) = P(g(X) \leq y) = P(X \geq g^{-1}(y)) = 1 - F_X(h(y))$$

Differentiate:

$$f_Y(y) = -f_X(h(y)) \cdot h'(y)$$

Since $g$ is decreasing, $h' < 0$, so $-h'(y) > 0$. The formula stays positive. ✓

### The Unified Formula

Both cases are captured by:

$$\boxed{f_Y(y) = f_X(h(y)) \cdot |h'(y)|}$$

where $h = g^{-1}$ and the absolute value handles both monotone cases simultaneously.

**Equivalently**, since $h'(y) = \frac{dx}{dy}$:

$$\boxed{f_Y(y) = f_X(x) \cdot \left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right|, \quad \text{where } x = g^{-1}(y)}$$

This is the **1D Jacobian formula**. The term $\left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right|$ is the (absolute value of the) Jacobian.

---

## 3. The Geometric Intuition — What the Jacobian Is Doing

Forget the formula for a moment. Think about what a PDF actually says:

$$P(Y \in (y, y + dy)) \approx f_Y(y), dy$$

The probability of $Y$ landing in a tiny interval of width $dy$ is $f_Y(y),dy$. But $Y = g(X)$, so:

$$P(Y \in (y, y+dy)) = P(X \in (x, x+dx))$$

where $x = g^{-1}(y)$ and $dx$ is the corresponding interval in $X$-space. That probability is:

$$f_X(x),|dx|$$

Setting these equal:

$$f_Y(y),dy = f_X(x),|dx|$$

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(x) \cdot \left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right|$$

The Jacobian $\left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right|$ is the **ratio of infinitesimal lengths**: how much the interval in $x$-space shrinks or stretches when mapped to $y$-space. If $g$ compresses a region (small $|dx|$ for a given $|dy|$), then $f_Y$ must be larger there to preserve the total probability.

```
x-space:   [────────────] dx = 2
                ↓ g = x²
y-space:   [──] dy = 0.5

The region shrank. So f_Y must be larger than f_X there.
|dx/dy| = 2/0.5 = 4  →  f_Y = 4 · f_X
```

> **The slogan:** Probability is conserved. If $g$ squeezes an interval, the density there must grow proportionally.

## 4. The Non-Monotone Case — Handle With Care

If $g$ is **not** monotone, $g^{-1}$ is multi-valued — there are multiple $x$ values mapping to the same $y$. We must sum over all inverse images.

**Formula:**

$$f_Y(y) = \sum_{x ,:, g(x) = y} f_X(x) \cdot \left|\frac{1}{g'(x)}\right|$$

equivalently written as:

$$f_Y(y) = \sum_{i} f_X(h_i(y)) \cdot |h_i'(y)|$$

where $h_1, h_2, \ldots$ are the branches of $g^{-1}$.

### Example: $Y = X^2$, $X \sim \text{Normal}(0,1)$

$g(x) = x^2$ is not monotone on $\mathbb{R}$ — both $x$ and $-x$ map to the same $y = x^2$.

Two branches: $h_1(y) = \sqrt{y}$, $h_2(y) = -\sqrt{y}$, for $y > 0$.

$$h_1'(y) = \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}, \quad h_2'(y) = -\frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}$$

$$f_Y(y) = f_X(\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} + f_X(-\sqrt{y}) \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}}$$

Since $f_X$ is symmetric: $f_X(\sqrt{y}) = f_X(-\sqrt{y}) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y/2}$

$$f_Y(y) = 2 \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}e^{-y/2} \cdot \frac{1}{2\sqrt{y}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot y^{-1/2} e^{-y/2}, \quad y > 0$$

This is the $\text{Chi-Squared}(1)$ distribution — which is $\text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2)$. A beautiful result from one Jacobian calculation.

> **Personal note:** The non-monotone case is where students lose marks. Always ask: _how many preimages does $y$ have?_ Draw $g(x)$ first if in doubt.

## 5. The Multivariate Case — The Full Jacobian

### Setup

Let $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)$ have joint PDF $f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x})$. Define a transformation:

$$\mathbf{Y} = g(\mathbf{X}), \quad \text{i.e.,} \quad (Y_1, \ldots, Y_n) = g(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$$

where $g: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}^n$ is a **bijection** (one-to-one and onto) with inverse $\mathbf{h} = g^{-1}$.

### The Jacobian Matrix

Define the **Jacobian matrix of $\mathbf{h}$** as the $n \times n$ matrix of partial derivatives:

$$J = \frac{\partial(x_1, \ldots, x_n)}{\partial(y_1, \ldots, y_n)} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial y_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial x_1}{\partial y_n} \ \vdots & \ddots & \vdots \ \frac{\partial x_n}{\partial y_1} & \cdots & \frac{\partial x_n}{\partial y_n} \end{pmatrix}$$

### The Formula

$$\boxed{f_{\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{y}) = f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{y})) \cdot |\det(J)|}$$

where $|\det(J)|$ is the **absolute value of the determinant of the Jacobian matrix**.

The 1D formula $\left|\frac{dx}{dy}\right|$ is exactly this — the $1 \times 1$ "determinant."

### Why the Determinant?

In $\mathbb{R}^n$, the Jacobian determinant measures how the transformation $g$ scales $n$-dimensional volume. If $g$ maps a small box of volume $dV_x$ in $\mathbf{x}$-space to a box of volume $dV_y$ in $\mathbf{y}$-space, then:

$$dV_x = |\det(J)| \cdot dV_y$$

The probability conservation argument generalizes:

$$f_{\mathbf{Y}}(\mathbf{y}), dV_y = f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{x}), dV_x = f_{\mathbf{X}}(\mathbf{h}(\mathbf{y})) \cdot |\det(J)| \cdot dV_y$$

Cancel $dV_y$ and you have the formula. $\checkmark$

> **Note:** You can equivalently define $J$ as the Jacobian of the _forward_ transformation $g$ (i.e., $\partial y_i / \partial x_j$) and use $1/|\det J_g|$. Both conventions appear in textbooks. I prefer $\partial x / \partial y$ — derivatives of the _inverse_ — because the formula $f_Y = f_X \cdot |J|$ then has $|J|$ naturally as a correction factor, not its reciprocal.

## 6. A Canonical Multivariate Example — Polar Coordinates

Let $(X, Y)$ have joint PDF $f_{X,Y}(x,y)$. Transform to polar coordinates:

$$R = \sqrt{X^2 + Y^2}, \quad \Theta = \arctan(Y/X)$$

Inverse: $X = R\cos\Theta$, $Y = R\sin\Theta$.

Jacobian matrix $\partial(x,y)/\partial(r,\theta)$:

$$J = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{\partial x}{\partial r} & \frac{\partial x}{\partial \theta} \ \frac{\partial y}{\partial r} & \frac{\partial y}{\partial \theta} \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -r\sin\theta \ \sin\theta & r\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}$$

$$\det(J) = \cos\theta \cdot r\cos\theta - (-r\sin\theta)\cdot\sin\theta = r\cos^2\theta + r\sin^2\theta = r$$

$$|\det(J)| = r$$

So:

$$f_{R,\Theta}(r, \theta) = f_{X,Y}(r\cos\theta,, r\sin\theta) \cdot r$$

**Application — Normal to Rayleigh.** If $X, Y \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(0,1)$:

$$f_{X,Y}(x,y) = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-(x^2+y^2)/2}$$

Then:

$$f_{R,\Theta}(r,\theta) = \frac{1}{2\pi}e^{-r^2/2} \cdot r = \frac{r}{2\pi}e^{-r^2/2}$$

Marginalizing over $\Theta \in [0, 2\pi)$:

$$f_R(r) = r e^{-r^2/2}, \quad r > 0$$

This is the **Rayleigh distribution**. The extra factor of $r$ in the Jacobian is what produces it — without the Jacobian, you'd get the wrong answer entirely. This calculation also sits at the heart of the Box-Muller transform for generating Normal samples. See [[Simulation Methods]].

## 7. Procedure — How to Apply the Jacobian Formula

**1. Identify the transformation.** Write $y = g(x)$ explicitly.

**2. Find the inverse.** Solve for $x = h(y) = g^{-1}(y)$.

**3. Check monotonicity (1D) or bijectivity (multivariate).** If $g$ is not monotone, identify branches.

**4. Compute the Jacobian.**

- 1D: differentiate $h(y)$ with respect to $y$. Take absolute value.
- Multivariate: form the matrix $\partial x_i / \partial y_j$. Compute $|\det|$.

**5. Identify the new support.** $\text{supp}(Y) = g(\text{supp}(X))$.

**6. Write the answer.** $$f_Y(y) = f_X(h(y)) \cdot |h'(y)|, \quad y \in \text{supp}(Y)$$

**7. Verify.** Check that $f_Y$ integrates to 1 (or at least check that it is non-negative and bounded on the correct support). This catches sign errors and forgotten absolute values.

## 8. Common Mistakes

**Forgetting the Jacobian entirely.** Writing $f_Y(y) = f_X(g^{-1}(y))$. Wrong — doesn't integrate to 1 in general.

**Wrong direction of the Jacobian.** Computing $|dy/dx|$ instead of $|dx/dy|$. The formula uses the derivative of the _inverse_ with respect to $y$. If you compute the forward derivative, take its reciprocal.

**Forgetting the support.** $f_Y$ is zero outside $g(\text{supp}(X))$. A common error: writing $f_Y(y)$ without specifying that it holds only for $y > 0$ (or whatever the correct domain is), then getting confused when normalization fails.

**Non-monotone $g$ with only one branch.** If $g(x) = x^2$ and $X$ can be negative, there are _two_ preimages for each $y > 0$. Missing one branch gives you half the correct answer.

**Sign errors in the determinant.** In multivariate problems, compute $\det(J)$ carefully. If it comes out negative, take the absolute value — densities are non-negative.

## 9. Log-Transform — A Worked Example

> [!example] $X \sim \text{Exponential}(\lambda)$, find the PDF of $Y = \log X$.
> 
> **Transformation:** $g(x) = \log x$, defined on $x > 0$.
> 
> **Inverse:** $x = h(y) = e^y$, for $y \in \mathbb{R}$.
> 
> **Jacobian:** $h'(y) = e^y$, so $|h'(y)| = e^y$.
> 
> **Support:** $\text{supp}(X) = (0,\infty)$, so $\text{supp}(Y) = (-\infty, \infty)$.
> 
> **PDF of $X$:** $f_X(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x}$ for $x > 0$.
> 
> **Apply the formula:** $$f_Y(y) = f_X(e^y) \cdot e^y = \lambda e^{-\lambda e^y} \cdot e^y = \lambda e^y e^{-\lambda e^y}$$
> 
> $$f_Y(y) = \lambda e^{y - \lambda e^y}, \quad y \in \mathbb{R}$$
> 
> This is the **Gumbel distribution** (a special case). The log of an Exponential has full support on $\mathbb{R}$ — the Jacobian factor $e^y$ is what transforms the decaying exponential into the correct Gumbel shape.

## 10. Connection to Other Topics

- **Change of variables in integration:** The Jacobian formula is the probabilistic version of the substitution rule $\int f(g(x)) g'(x), dx = \int f(u), du$. The probability density version just carries the absolute value and the inverse direction.
- **Order statistics:** Deriving the joint PDF of order statistics $(X_{(1)}, \ldots, X_{(n)})$ from an iid sample uses a multivariate Jacobian — the transformation is the sorting map, whose Jacobian is $n!$ (the number of permutations).
- **Simulation:** The inverse CDF method and Box-Muller both rely on Jacobian calculations implicitly.
- **Exponential Family:** When reparametrizing exponential family distributions (e.g., from mean to natural parameter), the Jacobian of the reparametrization appears in the Fisher information transformation.
- **Multivariate Normal:** Rotating a standard Normal vector $\mathbf{Z}$ via $\mathbf{X} = \mu + L\mathbf{Z}$ (where $LL^\top = \Sigma$) — the Jacobian is $|\det(L^{-1})| = |\det(\Sigma)|^{-1/2}$, which is exactly the normalizing constant in the multivariate Normal PDF.
