Variance measures how spread out a distribution is around its mean.

More precisely: it is the expected squared deviation from the mean.

$$\text{Var}(X) = E\left[(X - \mu)^2\right] \quad \text{where } \mu = E[X]$$

The standard deviation is just the square root of variance:

$$\text{SD}(X) = \sqrt{\text{Var}(X)}$$

SD lives in the same units as $X$. Variance lives in squared units.

---

## Why Not Just Use Expected Deviation

The most natural idea for measuring spread is $E[|X - \mu|]$, the mean absolute deviation. So why do we use squared deviation instead?

Several reasons:

1. Absolute value is not differentiable at zero. Squaring is smooth everywhere, which makes calculus-based optimization work cleanly. This matters in regression, MLE, and essentially all of modern statistics.
    
2. Squaring penalizes large deviations more than small ones. A deviation of 2 contributes 4 to variance, not 2. This is often what we actually want — we care more about extreme errors than small ones.
    
3. Variance has elegant algebraic properties (linearity-adjacent rules, the shortcut formula, decomposition into parts) that absolute deviation does not.
    
4. Squared deviation connects directly to the $L^2$ norm in linear algebra, which underlies projection, least squares, and PCA.
    

The cost: variance is sensitive to outliers precisely because it squares deviations. This is a real tradeoff, not a free lunch.

---

## The Shortcut Formula

The definition $E[(X-\mu)^2]$ is rarely what you compute directly. Expand it:

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2 - 2\mu X + \mu^2]$$

$$= E[X^2] - 2\mu E[X] + \mu^2$$

$$= E[X^2] - 2\mu^2 + \mu^2$$

$$= E[X^2] - (E[X])^2$$

This is the identity you use in practice:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2}$$

Memorize this. Almost every variance calculation uses it.

A useful way to remember it: "expected square minus square of expected."

---

## Key Properties

**1. Variance is always non-negative**

$\text{Var}(X) \geq 0$, since it is an expectation of a squared quantity.

$\text{Var}(X) = 0$ if and only if $X$ is a constant (no randomness at all).

**2. Scaling**

$$\text{Var}(aX) = a^2 \text{Var}(X)$$

Squaring is why the $a$ becomes $a^2$. This is not the same as linearity.

**3. Shifting**

$$\text{Var}(X + c) = \text{Var}(X)$$

Adding a constant shifts the entire distribution but does not change its spread. Variance is translation-invariant.

**4. Independence gives additivity**

If $X$ and $Y$ are independent:

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$$

If they are not independent, there is a correction term involving covariance:

$$\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y) + 2\text{Cov}(X, Y)$$

**5. Variance is not linear**

$\text{Var}(X + Y) \neq \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$ in general. This trips people up constantly. Expectation is linear. Variance is not.

## Variance of Common Distributions

|Distribution|Mean|Variance|
|---|---|---|
|$\text{Bernoulli}(p)$|$p$|$p(1-p)$|
|$\text{Binomial}(n,p)$|$np$|$np(1-p)$|
|$\text{Geometric}(p)$|$1/p$|$(1-p)/p^2$|
|$\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$|$\lambda$|$\lambda$|
|$\text{Uniform}(a,b)$|$(a+b)/2$|$(b-a)^2/12$|
|$\text{Exponential}(\lambda)$|$1/\lambda$|$1/\lambda^2$|
|$\text{Normal}(\mu, \sigma^2)$|$\mu$|$\sigma^2$|

For Poisson: mean equals variance. This is a diagnostic — if your count data has variance much larger than the mean, Poisson is not the right model (look at negative binomial).

---

## Worked Example: Bernoulli

Let $X \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$. Then $X \in {0, 1}$.

$E[X] = p$

$E[X^2] = 0^2 \cdot (1-p) + 1^2 \cdot p = p$ (since $X^2 = X$ for a 0/1 variable)

$$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = p - p^2 = p(1-p)$$

Note that variance is maximized at $p = 0.5$ (maximum uncertainty) and goes to zero at $p = 0$ or $p = 1$ (no uncertainty). This makes intuitive sense.

---

## Worked Example: Uniform(0,1)

$E[X] = 1/2$

$E[X^2] = \int_0^1 x^2 dx = 1/3$

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{3} - \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^2 = \frac{1}{3} - \frac{1}{4} = \frac{1}{12}$$

## Why Variance Matters: The Real Reasons

**1. Law of Large Numbers depends on it**

The LLN says sample means converge to the true mean. The rate of convergence is governed by variance. Smaller variance means you need fewer samples to get a good estimate.

**2. Central Limit Theorem uses it explicitly**

$$\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma / \sqrt{n}} \to N(0,1)$$

The $\sigma$ in the denominator is the standard deviation. Without knowing variance, you cannot quantify uncertainty in the CLT approximation.

**3. Bias-Variance Tradeoff in ML**

For any estimator $\hat{\theta}$ of a parameter $\theta$:

$$\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^2 + \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})$$

Every ML model you will ever train is navigating this tradeoff. Regularization reduces variance at the cost of introducing bias. Understanding variance at the probability level is what makes this tradeoff legible.

**4. Risk and uncertainty quantification**

In finance, engineering, and decision theory, variance is the formal measure of risk. A strategy with high expected return but high variance may be worse than one with lower return and lower variance, depending on your risk tolerance. This is the foundation of Markowitz portfolio theory.

**5. Signal vs Noise decomposition**

In regression and ANOVA, total variance of an outcome is decomposed into explained variance (signal) and residual variance (noise). $R^2$ is literally the fraction of variance explained.

---

## Variance vs Standard Deviation: When to Use Which

Use variance when:

- Doing algebra or derivations (cleaner to work with)
- Computing MSE or loss functions
- Decomposing into additive components (ANOVA, bias-variance)

Use standard deviation when:

- Interpreting results to humans (same units as the data)
- Computing confidence intervals
- Describing a distribution intuitively ("within 2 standard deviations")

## Connection to Covariance and Correlation

Covariance generalizes variance to two variables:

$$\text{Cov}(X, Y) = E[(X - \mu_X)(Y - \mu_Y)] = E[XY] - E[X]E[Y]$$

Note that $\text{Cov}(X, X) = \text{Var}(X)$. Variance is covariance with itself.

Correlation normalizes covariance:

$$\text{Corr}(X, Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{SD}(X)\text{SD}(Y)} \in [-1, 1]$$

Correlation is dimensionless. It tells you the direction and strength of linear relationship, but not the scale.

---

## Law of Total Variance

This is the variance analog of the Law of Total Expectation:

$$\text{Var}(Y) = E[\text{Var}(Y|X)] + \text{Var}(E[Y|X])$$

Read it as: total variance = average within-group variance + variance of group means.

The first term is unexplained variance (within groups). The second term is explained variance (between groups). This decomposition is foundational in hierarchical models, random effects models, and ANOVA.

## Common Mistakes

- Confusing $\text{Var}(aX + b)$ with $a\text{Var}(X) + b$. The constant $b$ disappears, and $a$ gets squared: $\text{Var}(aX + b) = a^2\text{Var}(X)$.
    
- Assuming $\text{Var}(X + Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$ without checking independence.
    
- Forgetting that variance is in squared units. If $X$ is in meters, variance is in meters-squared. Always report SD for interpretability.
    
- Using $E[X^2] = (E[X])^2$. This is only true if $\text{Var}(X) = 0$, i.e., $X$ is constant. In general, $E[X^2] \geq (E[X])^2$ by Jensen's inequality (since $x^2$ is convex).

## Personal Insight

Variance is fundamentally an answer to the question: how much information does one draw from this distribution give me about the mean? Low variance means one sample is highly informative. High variance means you need many samples before your estimate stabilizes.

This is why variance appears everywhere: in confidence intervals (uncertainty of an estimate), in ML (model stability), in signal processing (noise level), in physics (thermal fluctuation). It is the mathematical formalization of uncertainty about location.

Every time I compute variance, I am asking: if I observe this random variable once, how far from the center should I expect to land?
