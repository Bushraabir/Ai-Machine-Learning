

> [!note] Core Definition A distribution belongs to the **exponential family** if its PDF/PMF can be written in the form:
> 
> $$f(x \mid \theta) = h(x) \exp!\bigl(\eta(\theta), T(x) - A(\theta)\bigr)$$
> 
> This is the **canonical form**. Every term has a precise role.

---

## Anatomy of the Formula

|Component|Name|Role|
|---|---|---|
|$h(x)$|**Base measure**|The part that depends only on $x$, not $\theta$. Sets the "shape floor."|
|$\eta(\theta)$|**Natural parameter**|Reparametrizes $\theta$ into the exponential family's native coordinate|
|$T(x)$|**Sufficient statistic**|The only function of $x$ that matters for estimating $\theta$|
|$A(\theta)$|**Log-partition function**|Normalization constant (in log space). Ensures the distribution integrates to 1|

> [!important] The Log-Partition Function $A(\theta)$ $A(\theta)$ is not arbitrary ‚Äî it is **completely determined** by $h$, $\eta$, and $T$:
> 
> $$A(\theta) = \log \int h(x)\exp(\eta(\theta),T(x)), dx$$
> 
> Its derivatives generate the moments of $T(x)$: $$\mathbb{E}[T(X)] = A'(\theta), \qquad \text{Var}(T(X)) = A''(\theta)$$
> 
> This is a profound fact ‚Äî **all moments come for free** once you know $A(\theta)$.

## Member Distributions

> [!example] The Family Tree

### Bernoulli ‚Äî $X \sim \text{Bern}(p)$

$$P(X = x) = p^x(1-p)^{1-x}$$

Rewrite:

$$= \exp!\left(x \log\frac{p}{1-p} + \log(1-p)\right)$$

|Component|Value|
|---|---|
|$h(x)$|$1$|
|$\eta(p)$|$\log\frac{p}{1-p}$ (the **log-odds**)|
|$T(x)$|$x$|
|$A(p)$|$-\log(1-p) = \log(1 + e^\eta)$|

---

### Poisson ‚Äî $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$

$$P(X = x) = \frac{\lambda^x e^{-\lambda}}{x!} = \frac{1}{x!}\exp(x\log\lambda - \lambda)$$

|Component|Value|
|---|---|
|$h(x)$|$\frac{1}{x!}$|
|$\eta(\lambda)$|$\log \lambda$|
|$T(x)$|$x$|
|$A(\lambda)$|$\lambda$|

---

### Normal ‚Äî $X \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^2)$ (known $\sigma^2$)

$$f(x) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\exp!\left(-\frac{(x-\mu)^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

Expand the square:

$$= \underbrace{\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}\exp!\left(-\frac{x^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)}_{h(x)} \cdot \exp!\left(\frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}x - \frac{\mu^2}{2\sigma^2}\right)$$

|Component|Value|
|---|---|
|$h(x)$|$\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}\sigma}e^{-x^2/2\sigma^2}$|
|$\eta(\mu)$|$\frac{\mu}{\sigma^2}$|
|$T(x)$|$x$|
|$A(\mu)$|$\frac{\mu^2}{2\sigma^2}$|

---

### Exponential ‚Äî $X \sim \text{Exp}(\lambda)$

$$f(x) = \lambda e^{-\lambda x} = \exp(\log\lambda - \lambda x)$$

|Component|Value|
|---|---|
|$h(x)$|$1$|
|$\eta(\lambda)$|$-\lambda$|
|$T(x)$|$x$|
|$A(\lambda)$|$-\log\lambda$|

> [!warning] The Cauchy Exception The Cauchy distribution $f(x) = \frac{1}{\pi(1+x^2)}$ **cannot** be written in exponential family form. Its heavy tails and undefined moments mean it resists the algebraic structure the family requires. This is why the Cauchy is a canonical _counterexample_ in statistics ‚Äî it has no mean, no variance, and no sufficient statistic.

## Why This Matters ‚Äî The Big Payoffs

### 1. Sufficient Statistics

> [!abstract] Fisher-Neyman Factorization $T(x)$ is a **sufficient statistic** for $\theta$: it captures _all the information_ in $x$ about $\theta$.
> 
> No matter how many data points $x_1, \ldots, x_n$ you collect, you only need $\sum T(x_i)$ to estimate $\theta$.  
> For Poisson: just keep the sample sum $\sum x_i$, discard everything else.

### 2. Conjugate Priors (Bayesian Statistics)

> [!abstract] Conjugacy Every exponential family distribution has a **conjugate prior** ‚Äî a prior that, when updated with data, yields a posterior _of the same family_.
> 
> |Likelihood|Conjugate Prior|
> |---|---|
> |Bernoulli/Binomial|Beta|
> |Poisson|Gamma|
> |Normal (unknown $\mu$)|Normal|
> |Exponential|Gamma|
> 
> This is why Bayesian inference is analytically tractable for exponential family distributions ‚Äî the math closes cleanly.

### 3. Maximum Likelihood Estimation (MLE)

> [!abstract] MLE Has a Clean Form For iid data $x_1, \ldots, x_n$, the MLE condition for exponential family distributions reduces to:
> 
> $$\hat{\mathbb{E}}[T(X)] = \bar{T} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n T(x_i)$$
> 
> The MLE **moment-matches** the sufficient statistic. No complicated optimization needed.

### 4. Generalized Linear Models (GLMs)

> [!abstract] GLMs are Built on This Logistic regression, Poisson regression, linear regression ‚Äî all GLMs assume the response comes from an exponential family distribution.  
> The **natural parameter** $\eta$ is what the linear predictor $\mathbf{w}^\top \mathbf{x}$ models directly.  
> This is the bridge between probability theory and machine learning.

---

## The Natural Parametrization

When we write $\eta = \eta(\theta)$, we can go further and treat $\eta$ **itself** as the parameter:

$$f(x \mid \eta) = h(x)\exp(\eta, T(x) - A(\eta))$$

This is the **natural** (or **canonical**) parametrization. It linearizes the problem ‚Äî the parameter enters the exponent _linearly_ in $\eta$. This is why GLMs, variational inference, and information geometry all prefer this form.

## Self-Test

> [!question] Exercises
> 
> 1. Write $\text{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$ in exponential family form. Identify all four components.
> 2. Why does the Uniform$(0, \theta)$ distribution (unknown upper bound) **fail** to be in the exponential family?
> 3. For Bernoulli, compute $A''(\eta)$ and verify it equals $\text{Var}(X)$.
> 4. If $X_1, \ldots, X_n \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$, what is the sufficient statistic for $\lambda$? What does this mean practically?

> [!success]- Answers
> 
> 1. $f(x \mid \alpha, \beta) = \frac{1}{\Gamma(\alpha)\beta^\alpha} x^{\alpha-1} e^{-x/\beta}$. So: $h(x) = x^{\alpha-1}/\Gamma(\alpha)$, $\eta = -1/\beta$, $T(x) = x$, $A = \alpha\log\beta + \log\Gamma(\alpha)$. (Treating $\alpha$ as fixed, $\beta$ as the parameter.)
> 2. The support $[0, \theta]$ **depends on the parameter** $\theta$. Exponential family requires the support to be fixed (independent of $\theta$). The Fisher-Neyman factorization breaks down when the support shifts.
> 3. $A(\eta) = \log(1 + e^\eta)$. $A'(\eta) = \frac{e^\eta}{1+e^\eta} = p = \mathbb{E}[X]$. $A''(\eta) = \frac{e^\eta}{(1+e^\eta)^2} = p(1-p) = \text{Var}(X)$. ‚úÖ
> 4. $T = \sum_{i=1}^n x_i$ (the sample sum). Practically: you don't need to store all $n$ observations ‚Äî just their sum. The sample mean carries all the information about $\lambda$.
