> [!note] Big Picture The Delta Method answers one question: **if $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta} - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, what is the asymptotic distribution of $g(\hat{\theta})$?** It is the workhorse of asymptotic statistics — every time you transform an estimator (take its log, square it, take a ratio), you need the Delta Method to propagate uncertainty through that transformation. It is Taylor expansion dressed in probability theory clothing.

---

## 1. The Problem — Why We Need This

Suppose we have an estimator $\hat{\theta}_n$ that is asymptotically Normal:

$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$

Now we care about $g(\hat{\theta}_n)$ for some smooth function $g$. What is its asymptotic distribution?

**The naive wrong answer:** "$g(\hat{\theta}_n)$ is approximately $\mathcal{N}(g(\theta), \sigma^2)$."

Wrong on two counts — the variance is not simply $\sigma^2$, and the centering requires care. The transformation $g$ distorts both the center and the spread. The Delta Method tells us exactly how.

> **Personal note:** I first encountered this when trying to find the asymptotic distribution of the sample coefficient of variation $\hat{\sigma}/\bar{X}$. You can't just say "it's Normal with variance $\sigma^2/n$" — you have to propagate through the ratio function. That's the Delta Method. Once I had the need, the formula became obvious.

---

## 2. Heuristic Derivation — Taylor Expansion

The idea is disarmingly simple. If $g$ is differentiable at $\theta$, Taylor expand around $\theta$:

$$g(\hat{\theta}_n) = g(\theta) + g'(\theta)(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) + \frac{1}{2}g''(\theta)(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)^2 + \cdots$$

When $\hat{\theta}_n \to \theta$ (which it does, by consistency), the higher-order terms are $o_p(|\hat{\theta}_n - \theta|)$ and vanish asymptotically. So:

$$g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta) \approx g'(\theta)(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)$$

Multiply both sides by $\sqrt{n}$:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta)) \approx g'(\theta) \cdot \sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)$$

Since $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, and $g'(\theta)$ is a constant:

$$g'(\theta) \cdot \sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,, [g'(\theta)]^2 \sigma^2)$$

---

## 3. The Delta Method — Statement

> [!theorem] Delta Method Let $\hat{\theta}_n$ be a sequence of estimators satisfying: $$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$$ Let $g: \mathbb{R} \to \mathbb{R}$ be differentiable at $\theta$ with $g'(\theta) \neq 0$. Then: $$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta)) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,, [g'(\theta)]^2 \sigma^2)$$

Equivalently, in the language of approximate distributions:

$$g(\hat{\theta}_n) \overset{\cdot}{\sim} \mathcal{N}!\left(g(\theta),, \frac{[g'(\theta)]^2 \sigma^2}{n}\right)$$

**The punchline in one line:**

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(g(\hat{\theta})) \approx [g'(\theta)]^2 \cdot \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})}$$

The variance gets multiplied by the square of the derivative. This is the propagation of uncertainty formula from physics, made rigorous.

---

## 4. Rigorous Proof — Via Slutsky and CMT

The heuristic is convincing but hand-wavy. Here is the clean proof.

**Setup.** Write $\hat{\theta}_n - \theta = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \cdot \sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)$. Since $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) = O_p(1)$, we have $\hat{\theta}_n - \theta = O_p(n^{-1/2}) \to 0$ in probability. So $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta$.

**Taylor expansion with remainder.** By the mean value theorem, there exists $\tilde{\theta}_n$ between $\hat{\theta}_n$ and $\theta$ such that:

$$g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta) = g'(\tilde{\theta}_n)(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)$$

Therefore:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta)) = g'(\tilde{\theta}_n) \cdot \sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)$$

**Convergence of $g'(\tilde{\theta}_n)$.** Since $\tilde{\theta}_n$ is squeezed between $\hat{\theta}_n$ and $\theta$, and $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta$, we get $\tilde{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta$. By continuity of $g'$ at $\theta$:

$$g'(\tilde{\theta}_n) \xrightarrow{p} g'(\theta)$$

**Apply Slutsky's Theorem.** We have:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta)) = \underbrace{g'(\tilde{\theta}_n)}_{\xrightarrow{p} g'(\theta)} \cdot \underbrace{\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)}_{\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0,\sigma^2)}$$

By Slutsky: if $A_n \xrightarrow{p} a$ and $B_n \xrightarrow{d} B$, then $A_n B_n \xrightarrow{d} aB$. Therefore:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta)) \xrightarrow{d} g'(\theta) \cdot \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2) = \mathcal{N}(0,, [g'(\theta)]^2\sigma^2) \qquad \blacksquare$$

> **Personal note:** The mean value theorem makes this rigorous — no sloppy "higher order terms vanish." The remainder is exactly $g'(\tilde{\theta}_n)$, not an approximation, and Slutsky handles the convergence cleanly. This proof is the one to write in an exam.

---

## 5. The Case $g'(\theta) = 0$ — Second-Order Delta Method

When $g'(\theta) = 0$, the first-order term vanishes and the leading term is second order. The usual Delta Method gives the degenerate result $\mathcal{N}(0, 0)$ — useless.

Go to second order in the Taylor expansion:

$$g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta) \approx \frac{1}{2}g''(\theta)(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)^2$$

Multiply by $n$:

$$n(g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta)) \approx \frac{g''(\theta)}{2} \cdot n(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)^2 = \frac{g''(\theta)}{2} \cdot \left[\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)\right]^2$$

Since $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$, we have $\left[\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_n - \theta)\right]^2 \xrightarrow{d} \sigma^2 \chi^2(1)$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{n(g(\hat{\theta}_n) - g(\theta)) \xrightarrow{d} \frac{g''(\theta)\sigma^2}{2} \cdot \chi^2(1)}$$

The rate of convergence changes from $\sqrt{n}$ to $n$, and the limiting distribution is no longer Normal — it is a scaled Chi-Squared. This is a genuinely different regime.

> **Personal note:** This case is subtle and easy to miss. If you're ever computing the Delta Method and the derivative vanishes at the true parameter value, stop — the whole first-order analysis collapses. You need the second-order method, and your confidence intervals based on the $\mathcal{N}(0, [g'(\theta)]^2\sigma^2)$ approximation are wrong.

## 6. The Multivariate Delta Method

Let $\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_n \in \mathbb{R}^k$ satisfy:

$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_n - \boldsymbol{\theta}) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(\mathbf{0}, \Sigma)$$

Let $g: \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}$ be differentiable at $\boldsymbol{\theta}$. Define the **gradient vector**:

$$\nabla g(\boldsymbol{\theta}) = \left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial \theta_1}, \ldots, \frac{\partial g}{\partial \theta_k}\right)^\top$$

Then:

$$\boxed{\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_n) - g(\boldsymbol{\theta})) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}!\left(0,; \nabla g(\boldsymbol{\theta})^\top \Sigma, \nabla g(\boldsymbol{\theta})\right)}$$

The asymptotic variance is the **quadratic form** $\nabla g^\top \Sigma \nabla g$.

**For $g: \mathbb{R}^k \to \mathbb{R}^m$ (vector-valued $g$):** Define the $m \times k$ Jacobian matrix $D g(\boldsymbol{\theta})$ with $(i,j)$-entry $\partial g_i / \partial \theta_j$. Then:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\boldsymbol{\theta}}_n) - g(\boldsymbol{\theta})) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}!\left(\mathbf{0},; Dg(\boldsymbol{\theta}), \Sigma, Dg(\boldsymbol{\theta})^\top\right)$$

The 1D case is the special case $k = m = 1$: $Dg = g'(\theta)$, $\Sigma = \sigma^2$, giving $[g'(\theta)]^2 \sigma^2$.

## 7. Variance Stabilizing Transformations

One of the most powerful applications of the Delta Method is _designing_ $g$ to make the asymptotic variance **constant** — independent of $\theta$. This is called a **variance stabilizing transformation (VST)**.

**Goal:** Find $g$ such that $[g'(\theta)]^2 \sigma^2(\theta) = c$ (a constant).

This requires:

$$g'(\theta) = \frac{\text{const}}{\sigma(\theta)} \implies g(\theta) = \int \frac{1}{\sigma(\theta)},d\theta$$

### Example: Binomial Proportion

$\hat{p} = X/n$ where $X \sim \text{Binomial}(n,p)$. Then $\hat{p}$ satisfies:

$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{p} - p) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, p(1-p))$$

Here $\sigma^2(p) = p(1-p)$, so $\sigma(p) = \sqrt{p(1-p)}$. The VST requires:

$$g(p) = \int \frac{dp}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}} = 2\arcsin(\sqrt{p})$$

This is the **arcsine transformation**. By the Delta Method:

$$g'(p) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{p(1-p)}}, \quad [g'(p)]^2 \cdot p(1-p) = 1$$

$$\sqrt{n}\left(2\arcsin(\sqrt{\hat{p}}) - 2\arcsin(\sqrt{p})\right) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$

The asymptotic variance is exactly 1, independent of $p$. This is why the arcsine transformation is used in meta-analysis and whenever $p$ is close to 0 or 1 (where the Normal approximation to the Binomial is worst).

### Example: Poisson Rate

$\bar{X}_n$ from a Poisson$(\lambda)$ sample: $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \lambda) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \lambda)$.

VST: $g(\lambda) = \int \frac{d\lambda}{\sqrt{\lambda}} = 2\sqrt{\lambda}$.

$$\sqrt{n}(2\sqrt{\bar{X}_n} - 2\sqrt{\lambda}) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$

Square root stabilizes the Poisson. Used routinely in count data analysis.

## 8. Application — Asymptotic Confidence Intervals

The Delta Method gives a principled way to build CIs for $g(\theta)$ from a CI for $\theta$.

If $\hat{\theta}_n \overset{\cdot}{\sim} \mathcal{N}(\theta, \sigma^2/n)$, then by the Delta Method:

$$g(\hat{\theta}_n) \overset{\cdot}{\sim} \mathcal{N}!\left(g(\theta),; \frac{[g'(\theta)]^2 \sigma^2}{n}\right)$$

An approximate 95% CI for $g(\theta)$ is:

$$g(\hat{\theta}_n) \pm 1.96 \cdot \frac{|g'(\hat{\theta}_n)| \cdot \hat{\sigma}}{\sqrt{n}}$$

where we plug in $\hat{\theta}_n$ for the unknown $\theta$ in $g'(\theta)$ — justified by the continuous mapping theorem.

> **Personal note:** In practice, $g'(\theta)$ is unknown. We estimate it at $\hat{\theta}_n$. This is consistent because $\hat{\theta}_n \xrightarrow{p} \theta$ and $g'$ is continuous. Don't be sloppy here — write "by CMT, $g'(\hat{\theta}_n) \xrightarrow{p} g'(\theta)$, so plugging in $\hat{\theta}_n$ is justified."

## 9. Application — MLE and the Fisher Information

One of the canonical settings: the MLE $\hat{\theta}_\text{MLE}$ satisfies (under regularity conditions):

$$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_\text{MLE} - \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, I(\theta)^{-1})$$

where $I(\theta) = -\mathbb{E}[\partial^2 \log f(X;\theta)/\partial\theta^2]$ is the Fisher information.

By the Delta Method, for any smooth $g$:

$$\sqrt{n}(g(\hat{\theta}_\text{MLE}) - g(\theta)) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}!\left(0,; \frac{[g'(\theta)]^2}{I(\theta)}\right)$$

This is the **invariance of the MLE** combined with the Delta Method — the MLE of $g(\theta)$ is $g(\hat{\theta}_\text{MLE})$, and its asymptotic variance follows from the Delta Method. The asymptotic Cramér-Rao bound for $g(\theta)$ is $[g'(\theta)]^2 / I(\theta)$, and the MLE achieves it. See [[Fisher Information and Cramér-Rao]].

## 10. Worked Examples

> [!example] Log Transformation of a Sample Mean
> 
> $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim}$ some distribution with mean $\mu > 0$ and variance $\sigma^2$. Let $g(\mu) = \log \mu$.
> 
> By CLT: $\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}_n - \mu) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^2)$.
> 
> $g'(\mu) = 1/\mu$, so $[g'(\mu)]^2 = 1/\mu^2$.
> 
> **Delta Method:** $$\sqrt{n}(\log\bar{X}_n - \log\mu) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}!\left(0,; \frac{\sigma^2}{\mu^2}\right)$$
> 
> The asymptotic variance is $\sigma^2/\mu^2$ — the squared coefficient of variation. Makes sense: relative precision of $\bar{X}_n$ is what determines precision of $\log\bar{X}_n$.

---

> [!example] Ratio of Two Sample Means
> 
> $X_1,\ldots,X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} (\mu_X, \sigma_X^2)$ and $Y_1,\ldots,Y_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} (\mu_Y, \sigma_Y^2)$, independent. We want the asymptotic distribution of $\hat{\theta} = \bar{X}_n / \bar{Y}_n$.
> 
> Let $\boldsymbol{\theta} = (\mu_X, \mu_Y)^\top$, $g(\mu_X, \mu_Y) = \mu_X/\mu_Y$.
> 
> By CLT and independence: $$\sqrt{n}\left(\begin{pmatrix}\bar{X}_n \ \bar{Y}_n\end{pmatrix} - \begin{pmatrix}\mu_X \ \mu_Y\end{pmatrix}\right) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}!\left(\mathbf{0}, \begin{pmatrix}\sigma_X^2 & 0 \ 0 & \sigma_Y^2\end{pmatrix}\right)$$
> 
> Gradient of $g$: $$\nabla g = \begin{pmatrix} 1/\mu_Y \ -\mu_X/\mu_Y^2 \end{pmatrix}$$
> 
> Asymptotic variance via multivariate Delta Method: $$\nabla g^\top \Sigma \nabla g = \frac{\sigma_X^2}{\mu_Y^2} + \frac{\mu_X^2 \sigma_Y^2}{\mu_Y^4} = \frac{1}{\mu_Y^2}\left(\sigma_X^2 + \frac{\mu_X^2}{\mu_Y^2}\sigma_Y^2\right)$$
> 
> **Result:** $$\sqrt{n}\left(\frac{\bar{X}_n}{\bar{Y}_n} - \frac{\mu_X}{\mu_Y}\right) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}!\left(0,; \frac{\sigma_X^2}{\mu_Y^2} + \frac{\mu_X^2\sigma_Y^2}{\mu_Y^4}\right)$$
> 
> This is the standard result for the ratio estimator — used in survey sampling, econometrics, and relative risk estimation.

## 12. Connections to Other Topics

- **[[Central Limit Theorem]]:** The Delta Method presupposes asymptotic Normality of $\hat{\theta}_n$. The CLT is what typically provides this — the Delta Method is one step downstream.
- **[[Slutsky's Theorem]]:** The rigorous proof of the Delta Method uses Slutsky. Know both.
- **[[Continuous Mapping Theorem]]:** Used to justify plugging in $\hat{\theta}_n$ for $\theta$ in $g'(\theta)$ when constructing confidence intervals.
- **[[Fisher Information and Cramér-Rao]]:** The Delta Method + MLE asymptotics gives the asymptotic efficiency of $g(\hat{\theta}_\text{MLE})$.
- **[[Jacobian Formula — Change of Variables]]:** The Delta Method is the asymptotic/distributional counterpart of the Jacobian formula. The Jacobian gives the exact distribution of $g(X)$; the Delta Method gives the approximate distribution for large $n$.
- **[[Bootstrap]]:** The Bootstrap is an alternative to the Delta Method when $g$ is complex or $g'$ is hard to compute. They should give the same answer asymptotically under standard conditions.
