## Starting Point

Every time you build an estimator, you are making a decision under uncertainty. You observe data, compute some function of it, and use that as your guess for an unknown quantity. The question is: how do you measure how good that guess is?

You need a loss function. And you need a way to average that loss over the randomness in your data.

That averaging is risk.

---

## Loss Functions

A loss function $L(\theta, \hat{\theta})$ measures the cost of estimating $\theta$ with $\hat{\theta}$.

Common choices:

|Loss|Formula|Name|
|---|---|---|
|Squared error|$(\theta - \hat{\theta})^2$|L2 loss|
|Absolute error|$|\theta - \hat{\theta}|
|0-1 loss|$\mathbf{1}[\hat{\theta} \neq \theta]$|Classification loss|
|Log loss|$-\log p_\theta(x)$|Cross-entropy|

The choice of loss function is a modeling decision. It encodes what kind of errors you care about and how much.

Squared error penalizes large errors more than small ones (quadratically). Absolute error treats all errors proportionally. The 0-1 loss only cares whether you are exactly right or wrong.

---

## Risk: The Definition

Risk is the expected loss under the true distribution:

$$R(\theta, \hat{\theta}) = E_\theta\left[L(\theta, \hat{\theta}(X))\right]$$

The expectation is over the randomness in the data $X$, which was drawn from a distribution parameterized by $\theta$.

Risk is a function of $\theta$. This is important. For a given estimator, its risk can be high for some values of $\theta$ and low for others.

This creates a problem: if risk depends on $\theta$, and $\theta$ is unknown, how do you compare two estimators? This question does not have one answer. Different answers give you different theories of statistics.

---

## Mean Squared Error

When the loss is squared error, risk becomes MSE:

$$\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = E\left[(\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2\right]$$

This is the most commonly used risk in statistics and ML. The reasons are the same as why variance uses squared deviation: differentiability, connection to $L^2$ geometry, and elegant algebraic decomposition.

---

## The Bias-Variance Decomposition of MSE

Add and subtract $E[\hat{\theta}]$:

$$\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = E\left[(\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2\right]$$

$$= E\left[(\hat{\theta} - E[\hat{\theta}] + E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta)^2\right]$$

Expand and note the cross term vanishes (since $E[\hat{\theta} - E[\hat{\theta}}] = 0$):

$$= E\left[(\hat{\theta} - E[\hat{\theta}])^2\right] + (E[\hat{\theta}] - \theta)^2$$

$$\boxed{\text{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = \text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) + \text{Bias}(\hat{\theta})^2}$$

This is the exact same decomposition as the bias-variance tradeoff note, but now derived from first principles at the estimator level rather than the model level.

MSE is the sum of variance (how much the estimator fluctuates across datasets) and squared bias (how far the estimator is from the truth on average).

---

## Unbiased Estimators and Why MSE Still Matters

An unbiased estimator has $E[\hat{\theta}] = \theta$, so $\text{Bias} = 0$.

For an unbiased estimator: $\text{MSE} = \text{Var}(\hat{\theta})$.

Unbiasedness sounds ideal. But an unbiased estimator with very high variance can have worse MSE than a biased estimator with low variance. The biased estimator is more reliably close to $\theta$ even though it is systematically off-center.

This is not a pathological edge case. It is the norm in high-dimensional statistics.

Example: the sample mean $\bar{X}$ is unbiased for $\mu$. The James-Stein estimator shrinks $\bar{X}$ toward zero, introducing bias — but in dimension $d \geq 3$, it has strictly lower MSE than $\bar{X}$ for every value of $\mu$. This result (Stein's paradox, 1956) was shocking when it appeared. An inadmissible estimator — one that is uniformly dominated in MSE by another — can be unbiased.

The lesson: unbiasedness is a local property (the estimator is centered at the truth). MSE is a global property (how spread out and how centered the estimator is). MSE is the right criterion when you care about accuracy, not just absence of systematic error.

---

## Bayes Risk

One way to handle the fact that risk depends on $\theta$ is to put a prior $\pi(\theta)$ on $\theta$ and average:

$$r(\pi, \hat{\theta}) = \int R(\theta, \hat{\theta}) \pi(\theta) d\theta = E_\pi\left[R(\theta, \hat{\theta})\right]$$

This is Bayes risk. The estimator that minimizes Bayes risk is the Bayes estimator.

For squared error loss, the Bayes estimator is the posterior mean:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{Bayes}} = E[\theta | X]$$

This is the single most important fact in Bayesian estimation. The posterior mean minimizes expected squared error loss under any prior. If you want to minimize MSE and you have a prior, compute the posterior mean.

For absolute error loss, the Bayes estimator is the posterior median.

For 0-1 loss, the Bayes estimator is the posterior mode (MAP estimate).

The loss function determines which summary of the posterior is optimal.

---

## Minimax Risk

The other way to handle risk depending on $\theta$ is the minimax approach. Instead of averaging over $\theta$, you guard against the worst case:

$$\hat{\theta}_{\text{minimax}} = \arg\min_{\hat{\theta}} \sup_\theta R(\theta, \hat{\theta})$$

The minimax estimator minimizes the maximum possible risk over all $\theta$.

This is a frequentist, worst-case approach. It makes no assumptions about $\theta$ beyond what the model specifies. It is appropriate when you have no prior information and the cost of being wrong in the worst case is high.

Minimax estimators often coincide with Bayes estimators under a least-favorable prior — the prior that makes the problem hardest.

---

## MSE in Regression: Connection to Loss Minimization

In regression, you observe $(x_i, y_i)$ pairs and fit $\hat{f}$.

Empirical risk minimization with squared loss:

$$\hat{f} = \arg\min_f \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - f(x_i))^2$$

This is ordinary least squares. The population version is:

$$f^* = \arg\min_f E\left[(Y - f(X))^2\right]$$

The solution to the population problem is $f^*(x) = E[Y | X = x]$, the conditional expectation. This is why the conditional mean is the best predictor under squared loss — it is not an assumption, it is a theorem.

If you change the loss to absolute error, the optimal predictor becomes the conditional median. If you use quantile loss, you get conditional quantiles. The loss function determines what you are estimating.

## MSE vs MAE in Practice

|Property|MSE|MAE|
|---|---|---|
|Outlier sensitivity|High (squares large errors)|Low (linear in errors)|
|Differentiability|Everywhere|Not at zero|
|Optimal predictor|Conditional mean|Conditional median|
|Gradient for optimization|Simple (linear in residuals)|Subgradient needed|
|Interpretation|Average squared error|Average absolute error|

Use MSE when:

- Large errors are disproportionately costly
- You want to estimate the conditional mean
- You need clean gradients for optimization

Use MAE when:

- Your data has outliers you do not want to dominate the fit
- You want to estimate the conditional median
- Robustness matters more than optimality under Gaussian noise

Huber loss interpolates between them: quadratic for small residuals, linear for large ones. It is differentiable everywhere and robust to outliers.

## MSE Decomposition for Prediction

In the prediction setting (as in the bias-variance tradeoff note), the full decomposition at a point $x$ is:

$$E[(Y - \hat{f}(x))^2] = \sigma^2 + \text{Bias}^2(\hat{f}(x)) + \text{Var}(\hat{f}(x))$$

The three terms:

- $\sigma^2$: irreducible noise — the variance of $Y$ around $f(x)$ that no model can remove
- $\text{Bias}^2$: systematic error from model misspecification
- $\text{Var}$: estimation error from finite data

MSE in estimation (parameter estimation) is the two-term version. MSE in prediction is the three-term version. The extra term is the irreducible noise floor.

---

## Admissibility

An estimator $\hat{\theta}_1$ dominates $\hat{\theta}_2$ if:

$$R(\theta, \hat{\theta}_1) \leq R(\theta, \hat{\theta}_2) \quad \forall \theta$$

with strict inequality for at least one $\theta$.

An estimator is admissible if no other estimator dominates it.

An inadmissible estimator is one you should never use — there exists a strictly better alternative for every value of $\theta$.

Stein's result: the sample mean is inadmissible under squared error loss in dimension $d \geq 3$. The James-Stein shrinkage estimator dominates it. This does not mean you always use James-Stein in practice, but it means the philosophical foundation of "just use the MLE/sample mean" is shakier than it appears.

---

## Cramér-Rao Lower Bound

For any unbiased estimator $\hat{\theta}$:

$$\text{Var}(\hat{\theta}) \geq \frac{1}{I(\theta)}$$

where $I(\theta)$ is the Fisher information:

$$I(\theta) = E\left[\left(\frac{\partial}{\partial\theta} \log p(X|\theta)\right)^2\right]$$

Since for an unbiased estimator $\text{MSE} = \text{Var}$, this gives a lower bound on the MSE achievable by any unbiased estimator. An estimator that achieves this bound is efficient.

The MLE is asymptotically efficient — it achieves the Cramér-Rao bound as $n \to \infty$.

This tells you: if you insist on unbiasedness, the best MSE you can possibly achieve is $1/I(\theta)$. If you are willing to accept bias, you can potentially do better (lower MSE) through shrinkage.

## Personal Insight

MSE is not just a convenient metric. It is the bridge between probability theory and statistical decision theory. When you write down MSE and decompose it, you are forced to separate two fundamentally different sources of error: structural error (bias, from your model being wrong about the world) and stochastic error (variance, from having limited data).

Every practical decision in ML — regularization strength, model complexity, ensemble size, prior choice in Bayesian methods — is ultimately a decision about how to navigate this decomposition.

Risk is the formalization of the question: not "did my model work on this dataset?" but "how will my model behave across all datasets I might have seen?" That shift in perspective — from a single dataset to a distribution over datasets — is the transition from fitting to generalization.

## Exercises

1. Show that the posterior mean minimizes Bayes risk under squared error loss. That is, prove $E[\theta | X] = \arg\min_{\hat{\theta}} E_\theta[((\hat{\theta} - \theta)^2 | X]$. (Hint: treat it as a calculus problem — differentiate with respect to $\hat{\theta}$.)
    
2. The MLE for the variance of a Normal distribution is $\hat{\sigma}^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2$. Show this is biased. Compute its MSE and compare to the unbiased estimator $S^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum(x_i - \bar{x})^2$. Which has lower MSE?
    
3. For a Normal location model $X \sim N(\theta, 1)$, compute the MSE of the shrinkage estimator $\hat{\theta}_c = cX$ for $c \in (0,1)$. Find the value of $c$ that minimizes MSE as a function of $\theta$. Why is this not a practical estimator?
    
4. Prove that the conditional mean $f^*(x) = E[Y|X=x]$ minimizes $E[(Y - f(X))^2]$ over all measurable functions $f$. Use the law of total expectation.
    
5. State the Cramér-Rao bound for $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ with $\sigma^2$ known and $\mu$ unknown. Verify that $\bar{X}$ achieves it.
