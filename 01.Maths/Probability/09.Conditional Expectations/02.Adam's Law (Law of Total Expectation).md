

> _Adam's Law is deceptively simple to state and endlessly useful in practice. The whole skill is learning to ask the right question: "what should I condition on to make this expectation tractable?" Once you pick the right conditioning variable, the law does the rest. This note is about building that instinct._

---

## 0. Statement

Let $X$ and $Y$ be random variables on the same probability space with $\mathbb{E}|X| < \infty$. Then:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]] = \mathbb{E}[X]}$$

Or equivalently, if $Y$ takes values $y_1, y_2, \ldots$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_i \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = y_i], P(Y = y_i)$$

In the continuous case:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = y], f_Y(y), dy$$

> _Recall from [[Conditional Expectation]]: $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ is a random variable — a function of $Y$. Adam's Law says: take the expectation of that random variable (averaging over the randomness of $Y$), and you recover $\mathbb{E}[X]$ unconditionally. The two layers of randomness — $X$ given $Y$, then $Y$ itself — collapse back into one._

---

## 1. Proof

**Discrete case.** Let $X, Y$ be jointly discrete.

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]] = \sum_y \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = y], P(Y = y)$$

$$= \sum_y \left(\sum_x x, P(X = x \mid Y = y)\right) P(Y = y)$$

$$= \sum_y \sum_x x, \frac{P(X=x,, Y=y)}{P(Y=y)} \cdot P(Y=y)$$

$$= \sum_x x \sum_y P(X=x,, Y=y)$$

$$= \sum_x x, P(X = x) = \mathbb{E}[X] \qquad \blacksquare$$

The key step is swapping the order of summation and recognising $\sum_y P(X=x,Y=y) = P(X=x)$ (marginalisation).

**Continuous case.** Replace sums with integrals and $P(Y=y)$ with $f_Y(y),dy$; the argument is identical. $\blacksquare$

---

## 2. The More General Form

Adam's Law holds with more conditioning on both sides:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z]$$

**Reading this:** condition on $Z$ throughout. Given $Z$, the inner expectation conditions additionally on $Y$; the outer expectation averages over $Y$. The net effect: conditioning on $Z$ alone.

This is the **Tower Property**: the coarser conditioning always wins.

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}] \mid \mathcal{H}] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{H}] \quad \text{whenever } \mathcal{H} \subseteq \mathcal{G}$$

(in the $\sigma$-algebra language of Stat 210 — file this away for later).

---

## 3. The Instinct: What to Condition On

Adam's Law is a tool. The skill is choosing $Y$ to condition on. Two heuristics:

**Heuristic 1: Condition on the "random parameter."**

If the problem has a hierarchical structure — some latent variable $\Theta$ that governs the distribution of $X$ — condition on $\Theta$. Given $\Theta$, $X$ is usually a standard distribution with known mean.

**Heuristic 2: Condition on the "first step" or "first outcome."**

In sequential problems (games, random walks, waiting times), condition on the first thing that happens. Express the conditional expectation in terms of the unconditional one, then solve the equation.

Both patterns appear below.

---

## 4. Examples

### 4.1 Geometric Mean (First-Step Analysis)

$X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$. Condition on $A = {\text{first trial success}}$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid A], p + \mathbb{E}[X \mid A^c],(1-p) = 1 \cdot p + (1 + \mathbb{E}[X])(1-p)$$

Solve: $\mathbb{E}[X] = 1/p$. ✓

_Pattern: memorylessness makes $\mathbb{E}[X \mid A^c] = 1 + \mathbb{E}[X]$ (start over after one wasted trial). Set up the equation, solve._

### 4.2 Negative Binomial Mean

$X = $ number of trials until $r$-th success, $p$ = success probability. Think of $X = X_1 + \cdots + X_r$ where $X_i \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ i.i.d. (waiting times between consecutive successes).

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X_1 + \cdots + X_r] = r \cdot \frac{1}{p} = \frac{r}{p}$$

No conditioning needed here — linearity of expectation does it. But the same first-step argument works if you condition on the first trial.

### 4.3 Poisson-Gamma (Negative Binomial) Model

$\Lambda \sim \text{Gamma}(r, \beta)$, $X \mid \Lambda \sim \text{Poisson}(\Lambda)$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Lambda]] = \mathbb{E}[\Lambda] = \frac{r}{\beta}$$

_Because $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Lambda = \lambda] = \lambda$ (Poisson mean), so $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Lambda] = \Lambda$._

### 4.4 Beta-Binomial Mean

$W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, $X \mid W \sim \text{Binomial}(n, W)$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid W]] = \mathbb{E}[nW] = n \cdot \frac{a}{a+b}$$

_Because $\mathbb{E}[X \mid W = w] = nw$, so $\mathbb{E}[X \mid W] = nW$, then Adam's Law applies._

### 4.5 Random Sum

$N$ is a non-negative integer-valued RV, $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ are i.i.d. with mean $\mu$, independent of $N$. Let $S = X_1 + \cdots + X_N$ (Wald's Identity setup).

$$\mathbb{E}[S] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[S \mid N]] = \mathbb{E}[N\mu] = \mu, \mathbb{E}[N]$$

**Proof of the inner step:** $$\mathbb{E}[S \mid N = n] = \mathbb{E}[X_1 + \cdots + X_n] = n\mu$$ so $\mathbb{E}[S \mid N] = N\mu$, then Adam's Law gives $\mathbb{E}[S] = \mu,\mathbb{E}[N]$. $\blacksquare$

> _This is Wald's Identity. It's used constantly in sequential analysis and insurance models. The key move: given $N=n$, the sum has $n$ terms each with mean $\mu$. Linearity finishes it._

### 4.6 Order Statistics Mean (Quick)

$U_{(k)}$ is the $k$-th order statistic of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$. From [[Order Statistics]], $U_{(k)} \sim \text{Beta}(k, n-k+1)$, so $\mathbb{E}[U_{(k)}] = k/(n+1)$.

Alternatively: by symmetry of spacings (which itself uses Adam's Law on the Poisson process representation), all $n+1$ gaps are exchangeable with mean $1/(n+1)$, so $\mathbb{E}[U_{(k)}] = k/(n+1)$. Two routes, same answer.

## 5. Adam's Law and Variance: The Connection to Eve's Law

Adam's Law computes $\mathbb{E}[X]$. Eve's Law decomposes $\text{Var}(X)$. They are companions:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)]}_{\text{unexplained}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y])}_{\text{explained}}$$

Adam's Law is hidden inside Eve's Law twice:

- $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid Y]]$ (Adam's Law)
- $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]]$ (Adam's Law)

Eve's Law is what you get when you apply Adam's Law to the variance decomposition. See [[Conditional Expectation]] §3 for the full proof.

---

## 6. A Subtlety: Iterated Conditioning

A common mistake is thinking Adam's Law means you can always "peel off" conditioning layers freely. The tower property is precise:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z] \qquad \checkmark$$

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Z] \mid Y, Z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z] \qquad \checkmark$$

The second line: if you already know $Z$, adding $Y$ to the outer conditioning doesn't change a function of $Z$ alone — "taking out what is known."

**What fails:**

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y] \mid Z] \neq \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z] \quad \text{in general}$$

Unless $Y$ and $Z$ are related in a special way (e.g., $Z$ is a function of $Y$, or $X \perp Z \mid Y$).

> _The tower property requires the conditioning on the outside to be a subset of the conditioning on the inside. When that nesting condition fails, you can't simplify._

## 7. Summary of Key Computations

|Setting|Condition on|Result|
|---|---|---|
|$X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$|First trial outcome|$\mathbb{E}[X] = 1/p$ via recurrence|
|$X\mid\Lambda \sim \text{Pois}(\Lambda)$|$\Lambda$|$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\Lambda]$|
|$X\mid W \sim \text{Bin}(n,W)$|$W$|$\mathbb{E}[X] = n\mathbb{E}[W]$|
|Random sum $S = \sum_{i=1}^N X_i$|$N$|$\mathbb{E}[S] = \mathbb{E}[N],\mathbb{E}[X_1]$|
|Any hierarchical model|Latent parameter|Reduces to known mean|
