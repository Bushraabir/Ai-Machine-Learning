

> Conditional variance is the quantity that Eve's Law decomposes. But before you can use Eve's Law fluently, you have to be comfortable with what $\text{Var}(X \mid Y)$ actually is — because it has the same type ambiguity as conditional expectation: sometimes a number, sometimes a random variable, and conflating the two is the source of most mistakes.

## 0. Definition

### Conditioning on an Event

For an event $A$ with $P(A) > 0$, the conditional variance of $X$ given $A$ is just the ordinary variance computed under the conditional distribution:

$$\text{Var}(X \mid A) = \mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid A] - (\mathbb{E}[X \mid A])^2$$

This is a **number**.

### Conditioning on a Random Variable

For a random variable $Y$, define:

$$\text{Var}(X \mid Y) = \mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid Y] - (\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y])^2 = \mathbb{E}!\left[(X - \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2 ,\Big|, Y\right]$$

This is a **random variable** — a function of $Y$, just like $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$. More explicitly: for each value $Y = y$, compute the conditional variance of $X$ given $Y = y$ as a number, then let $Y$ vary. The result is $\text{Var}(X\mid Y) = h(Y)$ where $h(y) = \text{Var}(X \mid Y=y)$.

> [!note] Type Ambiguity The type ambiguity here is identical to what we saw for $\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]$ in [[Conditional Expectation]].
> 
> - $\text{Var}(X\mid Y=y)$ is a **number**.
> - $\text{Var}(X\mid Y)$ is a **random variable**.
> 
> In Eve's Law, $\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$ takes the expectation of that random variable — averaging the conditional variance over the randomness of $Y$.

## 1. Eve's Law (Law of Total Variance)

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y])}$$

This was proved in [[Conditional Expectation]] §3. The two terms:

$$\underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)]}_{\text{average within-group variance}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y])}_{\text{variance of group means}}$$

- **First term**: on average (over $Y$), how spread out is $X$ within each $Y$-group? This is the noise $Y$ cannot explain.
- **Second term**: how much do the group means themselves vary as $Y$ changes? This is the variation $Y$ does explain.

$$\text{Total variance} = \text{unexplained} + \text{explained}$$

> [!tip] Big Picture This is ANOVA. This is $R^2$. This is the bias-variance tradeoff. It's all the same identity.

### 1.1 Proof

Start from $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ and apply [[Adams Law|Adam's Law]] to each piece:

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid Y]]$$

Write $\mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid Y] = \text{Var}(X\mid Y) + (\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \mathbb{E}[(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2]$$

Also $(\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = (\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]])^2$ by Adam's Law. Subtracting:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2] - (\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]])^2}_{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])} \qquad \blacksquare$$

## 2. Key Properties

### 2.1 Non-Negativity

$$\text{Var}(X \mid Y) \geq 0 \text{ a.s.}$$

since it's defined as a conditional expectation of a squared quantity. Therefore both terms in Eve's Law are non-negative, giving the bound:

$$\text{Var}(X) \geq \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$$

Conditioning on average reduces variance. The conditional mean has less variance than $X$ itself.

### 2.2 When Does Conditioning Eliminate All Variance?

$$\text{Var}(X\mid Y) = 0 \text{ a.s.} \iff X \text{ is a.s. a function of } Y$$

If $X = g(Y)$ for some $g$, then given $Y = y$, $X = g(y)$ is deterministic — zero conditional variance. Then Eve's Law gives:

$$\text{Var}(X) = 0 + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]) = \text{Var}(g(Y)) = \text{Var}(X) \qquad \checkmark$$

### 2.3 When Does $Y$ Explain Nothing?

$$X \perp Y \implies \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]) = \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X]) = 0$$

because $\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] = \mathbb{E}[X]$ (constant) when $X \perp Y$. Eve's Law then gives:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + 0 = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X)] = \text{Var}(X) \qquad \checkmark$$

Independence means $Y$ explains zero variance in $X$.

### 2.4 Linearity of Conditional Variance (Fails in General)

> [!warning] Unlike conditional expectation, conditional variance is **not linear**: $$\text{Var}(X + Z \mid Y) \neq \text{Var}(X\mid Y) + \text{Var}(Z\mid Y) \quad \text{in general}$$

The correct formula includes a covariance term:

$$\text{Var}(X + Z \mid Y) = \text{Var}(X\mid Y) + \text{Var}(Z\mid Y) + 2,\text{Cov}(X, Z\mid Y)$$

where $\text{Cov}(X,Z\mid Y) = \mathbb{E}[XZ\mid Y] - \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]\mathbb{E}[Z\mid Y]$.

If $X \perp Z \mid Y$ (conditional independence given $Y$), then $\text{Cov}(X,Z\mid Y) = 0$ and variances add. But unconditional independence is not enough.

## 3. Eve's Law with Extra Conditioning

The version with background conditioning on $Z$:

$$\text{Var}(X \mid Z) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y, Z) \mid Z] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z)$$

**Proof.** Apply Eve's Law in the conditional universe given $Z = z$, then let $z$ vary. $\blacksquare$

This is the version used in multi-level models — see §4.3 below.

## 4. Worked Examples

### 4.1 Beta-Binomial

$W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, $X\mid W \sim \text{Binomial}(n,W)$, $s = a+b$.

$$\text{Var}(X\mid W) = nW(1-W) \quad \text{(random variable, function of } W\text{)}$$ $$\mathbb{E}[X\mid W] = nW \quad \text{(random variable)}$$

**Unexplained variance:**

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid W)] = n(\mathbb{E}[W] - \mathbb{E}[W^2]) = n!\left(\frac{a}{s} - \frac{a(a+1)}{s(s+1)}\right) = \frac{nab}{s(s+1)}$$

**Explained variance:**

$$\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid W]) = \text{Var}(nW) = n^2,\text{Var}(W) = \frac{n^2 ab}{s^2(s+1)}$$

**Total:**

$$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{nab}{s(s+1)} + \frac{n^2 ab}{s^2(s+1)} = \frac{nab(s+n)}{s^2(s+1)}$$

As $s = a+b \to \infty$ (strong prior, $W$ concentrates at $p = a/s$), $\text{Var}(X) \to np(1-p)$ — recovering the Binomial variance. ✓

### 4.2 Random Sum (Wald's Second Identity)

$N$ non-negative integer-valued; $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ i.i.d. with mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$, independent of $N$; $S = X_1 + \cdots + X_N$.

Conditional on $N = n$: $S = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ is a sum of $n$ i.i.d. terms, so:

$$\mathbb{E}[S\mid N] = N\mu, \qquad \text{Var}(S\mid N) = N\sigma^2$$

**Unexplained variance:** $\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(S\mid N)] = \sigma^2,\mathbb{E}[N]$

**Explained variance:** $\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[S\mid N]) = \text{Var}(N\mu) = \mu^2,\text{Var}(N)$

**Total (Wald's second identity):**

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(S) = \sigma^2,\mathbb{E}[N] + \mu^2,\text{Var}(N)}$$

Reading it:

- If $N$ is deterministic ($\text{Var}(N) = 0$): $\text{Var}(S) = n\sigma^2$ — standard variance of a fixed sum.
- If $\mu = 0$: $\text{Var}(S) = \sigma^2,\mathbb{E}[N]$ — variance grows with expected number of terms, regardless of how random $N$ is.
- Extra variance from random $N$ is $\mu^2,\text{Var}(N)$ — each extra term contributes mean $\mu$, so uncertainty in the count propagates as $\mu^2,\text{Var}(N)$.

### 4.3 Three-Level Hierarchy

$\Theta \sim \text{prior}$, $\Lambda \mid \Theta \sim p(\lambda\mid\theta)$, $X\mid\Lambda \sim \text{Pois}(\Lambda)$.

**Step 1.** Apply Eve's Law conditioning on $\Lambda$:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid\Lambda)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid\Lambda]) = \mathbb{E}[\Lambda] + \text{Var}(\Lambda)$$

since $\mathbb{E}[X\mid\Lambda] = \Lambda$ and $\text{Var}(X\mid\Lambda) = \Lambda$ (Poisson).

**Step 2.** To get $\text{Var}(\Lambda)$, apply Eve's Law again conditioning on $\Theta$:

$$\text{Var}(\Lambda) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(\Lambda\mid\Theta)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[\Lambda\mid\Theta])$$

Each application of Eve's Law strips one level of the hierarchy — the Tower Property for variance, applied recursively.

### 4.4 Bivariate Normal

$(X,Y)$ bivariate normal with parameters $\mu_X, \mu_Y, \sigma_X, \sigma_Y, \rho$. From [[Linear Regression]] §5:

$$\text{Var}(Y\mid X) = \sigma_Y^2(1-\rho^2)$$

This is **constant** — does not depend on $X$. Homoskedasticity is automatic for bivariate normals.

**Verify Eve's Law:**

$$\text{Var}(Y) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(Y\mid X)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X])$$ $$\sigma_Y^2 = \sigma_Y^2(1-\rho^2) + \text{Var}!\left(\mu_Y + \rho\frac{\sigma_Y}{\sigma_X}(X-\mu_X)\right) = \sigma_Y^2(1-\rho^2) + \rho^2\sigma_Y^2 \qquad \checkmark$$

## 5. Common Confusions

> [!warning] $\text{Var}(X\mid Y) \neq \text{Var}(Y)$ The left side is the variance of $X$ given $Y$ — a function of $Y$. The right side is the unconditional variance of $Y$ — a number. These are completely different objects.

> [!warning] $\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] \neq \text{Var}(X)$ The left side is the **unexplained** variance — only part of $\text{Var}(X)$. You must add the explained variance $\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$ to recover $\text{Var}(X)$.

## 6. Geometric Interpretation

From [[Geometric Interpretation of Conditional Expectation]], Eve's Law is the **Pythagorean theorem in $L^2$**:

$$|\tilde{X}|^2 = |\tilde{X} - \text{proj}_{V_Y}(\tilde{X})|^2 + |\text{proj}_{V_Y}(\tilde{X})|^2$$

where $\tilde{X} = X - \mathbb{E}[X]$ and the projection is $\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] - \mathbb{E}[X]$.

|Term|Meaning|
|---|---|
|$\|\tilde{X}\|^2 = \text{Var}(X)$|Total length squared|
|$\|\text{proj}\|^2 = \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$|**Explained**: length of the projection|
|$\|\tilde{X} - \text{proj}\|^2 = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$|**Unexplained**: length of the residual|

$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$ is the squared distance from $\tilde{X}$ to the subspace $V_Y$. The conditional variance, averaged over $Y$, measures how far $X$ is from being a function of $Y$.

---

## 7. Summary

|Quantity|Type|Meaning|
|---|---|---|
|$\text{Var}(X\mid Y=y)$|Number|Variance of $X$ in the slice $Y=y$|
|$\text{Var}(X\mid Y)$|Random variable (function of $Y$)|Variance of $X$ as a function of the slice|
|$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$|Number|Average conditional variance — **unexplained**|
|$\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$|Number|Variance of conditional mean — **explained**|
|$\text{Var}(X)$|Number|Sum of the above two — **Eve's Law**|
