

> _This is the note that reframes everything. Once you see conditional expectation as orthogonal projection in a Hilbert space, every identity we've proved — Adam's Law, Eve's Law, the regression formula, the Tower Property — becomes a geometric theorem. The algebra didn't lie, but the geometry explains why it had to come out that way. I wish I had this picture from day one._

---

## 0. The Space

Define the space:

$$L^2 = {X : \Omega \to \mathbb{R} \mid \mathbb{E}[X^2] < \infty}$$

Elements of $L^2$ are random variables with finite second moment. We give this space an **inner product**:

$$\langle X, Y \rangle = \mathbb{E}[XY]$$

**Verification that this is an inner product:**

- Symmetry: $\langle X, Y\rangle = \mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[YX] = \langle Y,X\rangle$ ✓
- Linearity: $\langle aX + bZ, Y\rangle = \mathbb{E}[(aX+bZ)Y] = a\mathbb{E}[XY] + b\mathbb{E}[ZY]$ ✓
- Non-negativity: $\langle X,X\rangle = \mathbb{E}[X^2] \geq 0$ ✓
- Definiteness: $\langle X,X\rangle = 0 \iff \mathbb{E}[X^2] = 0 \iff X = 0$ a.s. ✓

_(Technically we work with equivalence classes of a.s.-equal RVs to make definiteness exact — but don't lose sleep over this.)_

The induced **norm** is:

$$|X| = \sqrt{\langle X,X\rangle} = \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]}$$

This is the **$L^2$ norm**, or the root mean square. With this structure, $L^2$ is a **Hilbert space** — a complete inner product space. Infinite-dimensional, but all the Euclidean geometry you know works here.

## 1. Orthogonality

Two random variables $X, Y \in L^2$ are **orthogonal** if:

$$X \perp_2 Y \iff \langle X, Y\rangle = 0 \iff \mathbb{E}[XY] = 0$$

This is a statement about second moments — **not** the same as independence ($X \perp Y$). Independence implies orthogonality of centred variables, but not conversely.

**Centering and orthogonality.** It is often more natural to work with centred variables. Define:

$$\tilde{X} = X - \mathbb{E}[X], \quad \tilde{Y} = Y - \mathbb{E}[Y]$$

Then:

$$\langle \tilde{X}, \tilde{Y}\rangle = \mathbb{E}[\tilde{X}\tilde{Y}] = \text{Cov}(X,Y)$$

So $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = 0$ (uncorrelatedness) is **orthogonality of the centred versions** in $L^2$.

The norm of a centred variable:

$$|\tilde{X}|^2 = \mathbb{E}[\tilde{X}^2] = \text{Var}(X)$$

Standard deviation is the $L^2$ norm of the centred variable. Correlation is the **cosine of the angle** between the centred variables:

$$\rho(X,Y) = \frac{\text{Cov}(X,Y)}{\text{SD}(X),\text{SD}(Y)} = \frac{\langle \tilde{X},\tilde{Y}\rangle}{|\tilde{X}||\tilde{Y}|} = \cos\theta$$

where $\theta$ is the angle between $\tilde{X}$ and $\tilde{Y}$ in $L^2$. This is exactly the formula for cosine in Euclidean geometry. **Correlation is cosine.**

> _$\rho = 1$: vectors point the same direction ($Y$ is a positive linear function of $X$). $\rho = -1$: opposite directions. $\rho = 0$: right angle — uncorrelated, orthogonal centred variables. The $|\rho| \leq 1$ bound is the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality: $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$._

## 2. Projection

Let $V \subseteq L^2$ be a **closed subspace** — think of it as a "flat" (a hyperplane, a line, a plane through the origin) inside the Hilbert space.

**Theorem (Projection).** For any $Y \in L^2$, there exists a unique element $\hat{Y} \in V$ such that:

$$|Y - \hat{Y}| = \min_{Z \in V} |Y - Z|$$

This $\hat{Y}$ is the **orthogonal projection** of $Y$ onto $V$. It is characterised by the **orthogonality condition**:

$$Y - \hat{Y} \perp_2 Z \quad \text{for all } Z \in V$$

i.e. $\mathbb{E}[(Y - \hat{Y})Z] = 0$ for all $Z \in V$.

**Why this characterises it.** For any $Z \in V$:

$$|Y - Z|^2 = |Y - \hat{Y} + \hat{Y} - Z|^2 = |Y - \hat{Y}|^2 + |\hat{Y} - Z|^2 + 2\langle Y - \hat{Y}, \hat{Y} - Z\rangle$$

The cross term vanishes by the orthogonality condition ($Y - \hat{Y} \perp_2 \hat{Y} - Z$ since $\hat{Y} - Z \in V$). So:

$$|Y - Z|^2 = |Y - \hat{Y}|^2 + |\hat{Y} - Z|^2 \geq |Y - \hat{Y}|^2$$

with equality iff $Z = \hat{Y}$. This is the **Pythagorean theorem** in $L^2$. $\blacksquare$

## 3. Conditional Expectation IS Projection

**Theorem.** $\mathbb{E}[Y \mid X]$ is the orthogonal projection of $Y$ onto:

$$V_X = {g(X) : g \text{ measurable}, \mathbb{E}[g(X)^2] < \infty}$$

the subspace of all (square-integrable) functions of $X$.

**Proof.** We need to show $\mathbb{E}[Y \mid X] \in V_X$ and $Y - \mathbb{E}[Y\mid X] \perp_2 Z$ for all $Z \in V_X$.

The first part is clear: $\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X] = g(X)$ for $g(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y\mid X=x]$, so it's in $V_X$.

For the second: take any $Z = h(X) \in V_X$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y\mid X])h(X)] = \mathbb{E}[Yh(X)] - \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X]h(X)]$$

For the second term, use "taking out what is known":

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X]h(X)] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[Yh(X)\mid X]] = \mathbb{E}[Yh(X)]$$

So $\mathbb{E}[(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y\mid X])h(X)] = 0$ for all $h(X) \in V_X$ — the residual is orthogonal to the subspace. $\blacksquare$

> _The "taking out what is known" property from [[Conditional Expectation]] is doing all the work. Geometrically it's saying: projections are self-adjoint. The projection of $Y$ onto $V_X$, dotted against anything in $V_X$, equals $Y$ dotted against that thing._

## 4. Every Identity as Geometry

### 4.1 Adam's Law: Projection Preserves Mass

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[Y \mid X]] = \mathbb{E}[Y]$$

**Geometrically.** The constant random variable $\mathbf{1} \in V_X$ (since $\mathbf{1} = g(X)$ with $g \equiv 1$). Take $h(X) = 1$ in the orthogonality condition:

$$\mathbb{E}[(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y\mid X]) \cdot 1] = 0 \implies \mathbb{E}[Y] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X]]$$

Adam's Law is orthogonality with respect to the constant vector $\mathbf{1}$. $\blacksquare$

### 4.2 Eve's Law: The Pythagorean Theorem

$$\text{Var}(Y) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(Y\mid X)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X])$$

**Geometrically.** Work with centred $\tilde{Y} = Y - \mathbb{E}[Y]$. Its projection onto $\widetilde{V}_X$ (centred functions of $X$) is $\widetilde{\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X]} = \mathbb{E}[Y\mid X] - \mathbb{E}[Y]$.

The Pythagorean theorem in $L^2$ says:

$$|\tilde{Y}|^2 = |\tilde{Y} - \text{proj}|^2 + |\text{proj}|^2$$

$$\underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\tilde{Y}^2]}_{\text{Var}(Y)} = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[(Y - \mathbb{E}[Y\mid X])^2]}_{\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(Y\mid X)]} + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[(\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X] - \mathbb{E}[Y])^2]}_{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X])}$$

Eve's Law is the Pythagorean theorem. $\blacksquare$

```
        Y
       /|
      / |
     /  |  ← residual Y - Ŷ (orthogonal to V_X)
    /   |
   /    |
  Ŷ----+-------- V_X
  ↑
  E[Y|X] = projection
```

The hypotenuse is $\tilde{Y}$ (total variation). The leg along $V_X$ is $\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X] - \mathbb{E}[Y]$ (explained variation). The leg perpendicular to $V_X$ is the residual (unexplained variation).

### 4.3 Tower Property: Projection of a Projection

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[Y \mid X, Z] \mid Z] = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid Z]$$

**Geometrically.** Let $V_{X,Z}$ be functions of $(X,Z)$ and $V_Z$ be functions of $Z$ alone. Clearly $V_Z \subseteq V_{X,Z}$.

Projecting $Y$ onto the larger subspace $V_{X,Z}$ gives $\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X,Z]$. Now project that onto the smaller subspace $V_Z$. The result is the same as projecting $Y$ directly onto $V_Z$:

$$\text{proj}_{V_Z}(\text{proj}_{V_{X,Z}}(Y)) = \text{proj}_{V_Z}(Y)$$

This is the **idempotency of nested projections** — a standard theorem in Hilbert spaces. The Tower Property is exactly this. $\blacksquare$

> _In $\mathbb{R}^3$: projecting a vector onto a plane, then projecting that result onto a line within the plane, gives the same answer as projecting the original vector directly onto the line. Draw this. It's obvious in 3D, and the same fact holds in $L^2$._

### 4.4 Linear Regression: Projection onto a Line

From [[Linear Regression]], the optimal linear predictor $\hat{Y} = a + bX$ minimises $\mathbb{E}[(Y - a - bX)^2]$.

Geometrically: restrict the projection to the subspace $V_\text{lin} = {a + bX : a, b \in \mathbb{R}} \subset V_X$, a two-dimensional subspace (spanned by $\mathbf{1}$ and $X$).

The slope formula $b = \text{Cov}(X,Y)/\text{Var}(X) = \langle \tilde{Y}, \tilde{X}\rangle / |\tilde{X}|^2$ is exactly the formula for the projection coefficient onto $\tilde{X}$ in $L^2$ — just like projecting a vector $\mathbf{v}$ onto a vector $\mathbf{u}$ gives coefficient $\langle \mathbf{v}, \mathbf{u}\rangle / |\mathbf{u}|^2$.

The minimum $\text{MSE}^* = \text{Var}(Y)(1 - \rho^2)$ is the squared length of the residual after projection — by the Pythagorean theorem in $V_\text{lin}$.

$$|\tilde{Y}|^2 = |\hat{\tilde{Y}}|^2 + |\tilde{Y} - \hat{\tilde{Y}}|^2$$ $$\text{Var}(Y) = \rho^2,\text{Var}(Y) + (1-\rho^2),\text{Var}(Y)$$

$R^2 = \rho^2 = \cos^2\theta$ where $\theta$ is the angle between $\tilde{Y}$ and $\tilde{X}$.

## 5. Cauchy-Schwarz is the Cosine Bound

In Euclidean space: $|\mathbf{u} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq |\mathbf{u}||\mathbf{v}|$ because $|\cos\theta| \leq 1$.

In $L^2$: exactly the same.

$$|\langle X, Y\rangle| \leq |X||Y| \iff |\mathbb{E}[XY]| \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X^2]}\sqrt{\mathbb{E}[Y^2]}$$

Applied to centred variables: $|\text{Cov}(X,Y)| \leq \text{SD}(X),\text{SD}(Y)$, i.e. $|\rho| \leq 1$.

**Proof in $L^2$.** For any $t \in \mathbb{R}$:

$$0 \leq |X - tY|^2 = |X|^2 - 2t\langle X,Y\rangle + t^2|Y|^2$$

This is a non-negative quadratic in $t$, so its discriminant is $\leq 0$:

$$4\langle X,Y\rangle^2 - 4|X|^2|Y|^2 \leq 0 \qquad \blacksquare$$

Equality holds iff $X = tY$ a.s. — i.e. one is a scalar multiple of the other, meaning $\rho = \pm 1$.

## 7. What the Geometry Cannot Tell You

The $L^2$ geometry captures **second-moment** information: means, variances, covariances, correlations, linear regression. It does not capture:

- Higher moments (skewness, kurtosis)
- Tail behaviour
- The actual conditional distribution $f_{Y\mid X}$ (only its mean $\mathbb{E}[Y\mid X]$)
- Independence (only uncorrelatedness / orthogonality)

For the bivariate normal, the second-moment picture is the whole picture — because the normal is entirely characterised by its first two moments. That's why the bivariate normal is the natural home for all of this geometry.
