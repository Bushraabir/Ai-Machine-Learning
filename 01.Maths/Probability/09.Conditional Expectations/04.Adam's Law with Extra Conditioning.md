
> _This is the version of Adam's Law that trips people up on exams and in proofs. The basic form $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]] = \mathbb{E}[X]$ is intuitive. But what happens when there's already some conditioning in the background — when everything is happening conditional on $Z$? The answer is clean, but you have to see it clearly at least once to trust it._

---

## 0. The Statement

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z]}$$

**Reading it:** inside, we condition on both $Y$ and $Z$. Outside, we average over $Y$ — but $Z$ is held fixed throughout. The result: the $Y$-conditioning disappears, and we're left with conditioning on $Z$ alone.

This is the **Tower Property** in full generality. The basic Adam's Law $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]] = \mathbb{E}[X]$ is the special case $Z = \emptyset$ (no background conditioning).

---

## 1. Proof

Work conditional on $Z = z$ throughout. For fixed $z$, apply ordinary Adam's Law in the conditional universe where $Z = z$:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z = z] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z=z] \mid Z=z]$$

Now $Z = z$ is fixed — so $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z=z]$ is a function of $Y$ alone (call it $g(Y)$ where $g(y) = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y=y, Z=z]$). Adam's Law in the $Z=z$ universe says:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(Y) \mid Z = z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z = z]$$

Since this holds for every $z$, we conclude:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z] \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _The proof is just: freeze $Z$, apply ordinary Adam's Law in the reduced universe, unfreeze. Every property of conditional expectation works this way — condition on $Z$ and you're back in familiar territory._

---

## 2. The Nesting Condition

The Tower Property requires that the **inner conditioning is finer than the outer**. Precisely:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{G}] \mid \mathcal{H}] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid \mathcal{H}] \quad \text{when } \mathcal{H} \subseteq \mathcal{G}$$

In variable terms: the outer conditioning must involve **less information** than the inner. Examples of valid nestings:

|Inner|Outer|Valid?|
|---|---|---|
|$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z]$|condition on $Z$|✓ — $Z$ alone is less than $(Y,Z)$|
|$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z]$|condition on $Y$|✓ — $Y$ alone is less than $(Y,Z)$|
|$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$|condition on $g(Y)$ for some function $g$|✓ — $g(Y)$ is coarser than $Y$|
|$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$|condition on $Z$ (unrelated)|✗ — no nesting, cannot simplify|

The last row is the common mistake. $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]\mid Z]$ does **not** generally simplify unless $Z$ and $Y$ are related (e.g. $Z$ is a function of $Y$, or $X \perp Z \mid Y$).

---

## 3. Two Directions of the Tower

There are two useful forms, both valid:

**Form 1 (average out the finer):** $$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z]$$ Conditioning on $Y$ additionally inside, then averaging $Y$ away outside, leaves $Z$.

**Form 2 (adding coarser conditioning outside does nothing):** $$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Z] \mid Y, Z] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z]$$ $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Z]$ is already a function of $Z$. Conditioning additionally on $Y$ outside cannot change a quantity that depends only on $Z$ — "taking out what is known."

> _Form 2 is the "taking out what is known" property in disguise. If you already have $Z$ in the outer conditioning, anything that depends only on $Z$ passes through unchanged._

## 4. Worked Examples

### 4.1 Three-Level Hierarchy

$\Theta \sim \text{prior}$, $\Lambda \mid \Theta \sim \text{something}$, $X \mid \Lambda, \Theta \sim \text{Poisson}(\Lambda)$.

Suppose $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Lambda, \Theta] = \Lambda$ (Poisson mean). What is $\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Theta]$?

$$\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Theta] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Lambda, \Theta] \mid \Theta] = \mathbb{E}[\Lambda \mid \Theta]$$

The tower collapses one level: $X$-given-$(\Lambda,\Theta)$ averaged over $\Lambda$ becomes $X$-given-$\Theta$. To go all the way:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Theta]] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[\Lambda \mid \Theta]] = \mathbb{E}[\Lambda]$$

Each application of Adam's Law strips one layer of conditioning.

### 4.2 Order Statistics Conditional Mean

Let $U_{(1)} \leq \cdots \leq U_{(n)}$ be order statistics of $n$ i.i.d. $\text{Uniform}(0,1)$. What is $\mathbb{E}[U_{(k)} \mid U_{(j)}]$ for $j < k$?

Given $U_{(j)} = u$, the variables $U_{(j+1)}, \ldots, U_{(n)}$ are the order statistics of $n - j$ i.i.d. Uniforms on $(u, 1)$ (by the memoryless-like property of order statistics given an intermediate value). So:

$$\mathbb{E}[U_{(k)} \mid U_{(j)} = u] = u + (1-u)\cdot \frac{k-j}{n-j+1}$$

The $k$-th order statistic among the upper $n-j$ uniforms on $(u,1)$ sits at position $(k-j)/(n-j+1)$ of the remaining interval $(u,1)$.

Now verify Adam's Law: $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[U_{(k)}\mid U_{(j)}]] = \mathbb{E}[U_{(k)}]$?

$$\mathbb{E}!\left[U_{(j)} + (1-U_{(j)})\frac{k-j}{n-j+1}\right] = \frac{j}{n+1} + \left(1 - \frac{j}{n+1}\right)\frac{k-j}{n-j+1}$$

$$= \frac{j}{n+1} + \frac{n-j+1}{n+1}\cdot\frac{k-j}{n-j+1} = \frac{j}{n+1} + \frac{k-j}{n+1} = \frac{k}{n+1} = \mathbb{E}[U_{(k)}] \qquad \checkmark$$

> _A nice sanity check: the tower collapses correctly, and the answer $k/(n+1)$ from [[Order Statistics]] comes out._

### 4.3 Conditional Variance via Eve's Law with Extra Conditioning

Eve's Law also has an extra-conditioning version:

$$\text{Var}(X \mid Z) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y, Z) \mid Z] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z)$$

**Proof.** Apply Eve's Law in the conditional universe given $Z = z$, then unfreeze $z$. $\blacksquare$

**Reading it:**

- $\text{Var}(X \mid Z)$ = total conditional variance given $Z$
- $\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y, Z) \mid Z]$ = unexplained variance (noise within $Y$-groups, averaged over $Y$)
- $\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z)$ = explained variance (spread of conditional means across $Y$-values)

Same decomposition as ordinary Eve's Law, but everything lives in the $Z$-conditional universe.

## 5. The Martingale in Disguise

_Plant this seed now — it will matter in Stat 212._

Define $M_n = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y_1, \ldots, Y_n]$ for a sequence $Y_1, Y_2, \ldots$ of observed variables. Then $(M_n)$ is a **martingale**:

$$\mathbb{E}[M_n \mid M_1, \ldots, M_{n-1}] = M_{n-1}$$

**Why?** $M_{n-1} = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y_1,\ldots,Y_{n-1}]$. By the tower property:

$$\mathbb{E}[M_n \mid Y_1,\ldots,Y_{n-1}] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y_1,\ldots,Y_n]\mid Y_1,\ldots,Y_{n-1}] = \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y_1,\ldots,Y_{n-1}] = M_{n-1}$$

The Tower Property **is** the martingale property. Every time you update a Bayesian posterior as new data arrives, you're running a martingale. The sequence of posterior means is a martingale with respect to the data filtration.

## 6. Common Mistakes

**Mistake 1: Collapsing without nesting.**

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y] \mid Z] \neq \mathbb{E}[X \mid Z] \quad \text{in general}$$

This would require $Z$ to be "coarser" than $Y$, i.e. $Z = g(Y)$ for some $g$. If $Z$ is independent of $Y$, the left side is $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]]= \mathbb{E}[X]$, not $\mathbb{E}[X\mid Z]$ (unless $X \perp Z$).

**Mistake 2: Forgetting the background $Z$ in the inner expectation.**

Writing $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]\mid Z]$ when you mean $\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y,Z]\mid Z]$ is a different object. In hierarchical models where $Z$ affects the distribution of $X$ directly, you must include $Z$ in the inner conditioning.

**Mistake 3: Applying tower to variance directly.**

$$\text{Var}(X) \neq \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$$

This ignores the explained variance term. The correct statement is Eve's Law. Variance does not telescope the way expectation does.

---

## 7. Summary

|Form|Statement|When to use|
|---|---|---|
|Basic Adam|$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]] = \mathbb{E}[X]$|No background conditioning|
|Tower (strip outer)|$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y,Z]\mid Z] = \mathbb{E}[X\mid Z]$|Average away finer conditioning $Y$|
|Tower (add coarser)|$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Z]\mid Y,Z] = \mathbb{E}[X\mid Z]$|Known function of $Z$ is unchanged|
|Eve's with $Z$|$\text{Var}(X\mid Z) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y,Z)\mid Z] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y,Z]\mid Z)$|Variance decomposition in $Z$-universe|

**The one-line rule:** The tower collapses the inner conditioning to match the outer, provided the outer is coarser. Coarser wins.
