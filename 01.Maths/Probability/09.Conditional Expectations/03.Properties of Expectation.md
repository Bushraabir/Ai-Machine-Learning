

> _These are the load-bearing axioms of probability calculations. None of them are surprising in isolation, but together they are the entire toolkit. Every complex expectation computation I've ever done reduces to some combination of linearity, non-negativity, and a clever conditioning argument. Prove each one carefully at least once — after that, use them without guilt._

## 0. Setup

$X, Y, X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are random variables on a common probability space. All expectations are assumed to exist (i.e. $\mathbb{E}|X| < \infty$) unless stated otherwise.

---

## 1. Linearity

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[aX + bY] = a,\mathbb{E}[X] + b,\mathbb{E}[Y]}$$

for all constants $a, b \in \mathbb{R}$.

**This holds regardless of whether $X$ and $Y$ are independent.**

**Proof (discrete case).**

$$\mathbb{E}[aX + bY] = \sum_{x}\sum_{y}(ax + by),P(X=x,, Y=y)$$

$$= a\sum_x x \underbrace{\sum_y P(X=x,Y=y)}_{P(X=x)} + b\sum_y y\underbrace{\sum_x P(X=x,Y=y)}_{P(Y=y)}$$

$$= a,\mathbb{E}[X] + b,\mathbb{E}[Y] \qquad \blacksquare$$

The proof is just marginalisation. Independence played no role.

**Extension to $n$ variables:**

$$\mathbb{E}!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n a_i X_i\right] = \sum_{i=1}^n a_i, \mathbb{E}[X_i]$$

by induction. And for countably infinite sums, linearity holds provided $\sum_i \mathbb{E}|X_i| < \infty$ (dominated convergence).

> _Linearity is the single most-used property in the entire course. Whenever you see a sum of random variables, your first instinct should be to push $\mathbb{E}$ inside. The fact that independence is not required is what makes this powerful — you can always decompose._

### 1.1 Indicator Trick (Fundamental Consequence)

For any event $A$, define $\mathbf{1}_A = 1$ if $A$ occurs, $0$ otherwise. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_A] = P(A)$$

Combined with linearity, this gives a powerful method for computing expectations of counts.

**Example.** $X = $ number of heads in $n$ fair coin flips. Write $X = \mathbf{1}_{A_1} + \cdots + \mathbf{1}_{A_n}$ where $A_i = {i\text{-th flip is heads}}$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{A_i}] = \sum_{i=1}^n P(A_i) = \frac{n}{2}$$

No need to use the Binomial PMF at all.

**Example.** Number of fixed points of a random permutation of ${1,\ldots,n}$. Let $X_i = \mathbf{1}_{{\sigma(i) = i}}$. Then $\mathbb{E}[X_i] = 1/n$ (by symmetry: each element is equally likely to be fixed). So:

$$\mathbb{E}!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n X_i\right] = n \cdot \frac{1}{n} = 1$$

The expected number of fixed points is always 1, regardless of $n$.

## 2. Non-Negativity and Monotonicity

$$\boxed{X \geq 0 \text{ a.s.} \implies \mathbb{E}[X] \geq 0}$$

$$\boxed{X \geq Y \text{ a.s.} \implies \mathbb{E}[X] \geq \mathbb{E}[Y]}$$

The second follows from the first applied to $X - Y \geq 0$.

**Proof (discrete).** If $X \geq 0$ a.s., then every term $x \cdot P(X=x)$ in $\sum_x x,P(X=x)$ is non-negative. $\blacksquare$

### 2.1 Consequence: Bounding Expectations

If $a \leq X \leq b$ a.s., then $a \leq \mathbb{E}[X] \leq b$.

If $|X| \leq Y$ a.s. and $\mathbb{E}[Y] < \infty$, then $\mathbb{E}|X| \leq \mathbb{E}[Y] < \infty$ — so $\mathbb{E}[X]$ exists.

## 3. Expectation of a Function: LOTUS

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \sum_x g(x),P(X=x) \quad \text{(discrete)}}$$

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty g(x),f_X(x), dx \quad \text{(continuous)}}$$

**This is the Law of the Unconscious Statistician (LOTUS).** You do not need to find the distribution of $g(X)$ first — just apply $g$ inside the sum/integral over the distribution of $X$.

**Proof (discrete).** Let $Y = g(X)$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[Y] = \sum_y y, P(Y = y) = \sum_y y \sum_{x:, g(x)=y} P(X=x) = \sum_x g(x),P(X=x) \qquad \blacksquare$$

The last step collects all $x$ with the same $g$-value.

> _LOTUS is why we can compute $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$, $\mathbb{E}[1/X]$, $\mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$ (the MGF) — all without deriving a new distribution. It's implicit in every moment calculation I've written in these notes._

**Example.** $X \sim \text{Uniform}(0,1)$. Compute $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \int_0^1 x^2 \cdot 1, dx = \frac{1}{3}$$

No need to find the distribution of $X^2$.

**Example.** $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ on ${1,2,\ldots}$. Compute $\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)]$ (the second factorial moment).

$$\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] = \sum_{k=1}^\infty k(k-1)(1-p)^{k-1}p$$

Differentiate the generating function $\sum_{k=0}^\infty r^k = 1/(1-r)$ twice with respect to $r$ to evaluate. Result: $\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] = 2(1-p)/p^2$, from which $\text{Var}(X) = (1-p)/p^2$.

## 4. Independence and Expectation of Products

$$\boxed{X \perp Y \implies \mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X],\mathbb{E}[Y]}$$

**Proof (discrete).**

$$\mathbb{E}[XY] = \sum_x\sum_y xy, P(X=x,, Y=y) \overset{\perp}{=} \sum_x\sum_y xy, P(X=x)P(Y=y)$$

$$= \left(\sum_x x,P(X=x)\right)\left(\sum_y y,P(Y=y)\right) = \mathbb{E}[X],\mathbb{E}[Y] \qquad \blacksquare$$

**The converse is false.** $\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$ (i.e. $\text{Cov}(X,Y)=0$) does not imply independence. Uncorrelated $\not\Rightarrow$ independent. Standard counterexample: $X \sim \text{Uniform}(-1,1)$, $Y = X^2$. Then $\text{Cov}(X,Y) = \mathbb{E}[X^3] - \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[X^2] = 0 - 0 = 0$, but $Y$ is a deterministic function of $X$.

**Extension.** For $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ mutually independent:

$$\mathbb{E}!\left[\prod_{i=1}^n g_i(X_i)\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[g_i(X_i)]$$

by the same factorisation argument applied to the joint PMF/PDF.

## 5. Jensen's Inequality

$$\boxed{g \text{ convex} \implies \mathbb{E}[g(X)] \geq g(\mathbb{E}[X])}$$

$$\boxed{g \text{ concave} \implies \mathbb{E}[g(X)] \leq g(\mathbb{E}[X])}$$

**Proof.** Since $g$ is convex, for any $\mu = \mathbb{E}[X]$ there exists a supporting hyperplane: a linear function $\ell(x) = g(\mu) + c(x - \mu)$ such that $g(x) \geq \ell(x)$ for all $x$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \geq \mathbb{E}[\ell(X)] = g(\mu) + c(\mathbb{E}[X] - \mu) = g(\mu) \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _The supporting hyperplane is the tangent line at $\mu$ (when $g$ is differentiable). Jensen says: convexity curves upward, so the function evaluated at the average is below the average of the function. Draw this — it's obvious geometrically, and the proof just formalises the picture._

**Common applications:**

|Function $g$|Convex/Concave|Jensen gives|
|---|---|---|
|$e^x$|Convex|$\mathbb{E}[e^X] \geq e^{\mathbb{E}[X]}$|
|$x^2$|Convex|$\mathbb{E}[X^2] \geq (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ — i.e. $\text{Var}(X) \geq 0$|
|$\log x$|Concave|$\mathbb{E}[\log X] \leq \log\mathbb{E}[X]$|
|$\sqrt{x}$|Concave|$\mathbb{E}[\sqrt{X}] \leq \sqrt{\mathbb{E}[X]}$|
|$1/x$|Convex ($x>0$)|$\mathbb{E}[1/X] \geq 1/\mathbb{E}[X]$|

The second row is particularly important: Jensen applied to $g(x) = x^2$ immediately gives $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 \geq 0$. Variance non-negativity is just Jensen.

**When does equality hold?** $\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = g(\mathbb{E}[X])$ iff $X$ is a.s. constant, or $g$ is linear on the support of $X$.

## 6. Markov's Inequality

$$\boxed{X \geq 0 \text{ a.s.},; a > 0 \implies P(X \geq a) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[X]}{a}}$$

**Proof.**

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}] + \mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X < a}] \geq \mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}] \geq a, P(X \geq a) \qquad \blacksquare$$

The first inequality drops a non-negative term; the second uses $X \geq a$ on the event ${X \geq a}$.

> _Markov's inequality is weak but universal — it needs only non-negativity and a finite mean. Every other tail bound (Chebyshev, Chernoff) is Markov applied to a smarter function of $X$._

**Chebyshev from Markov.** Apply Markov to $Y = (X - \mu)^2 \geq 0$ with $a = \varepsilon^2$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq \varepsilon) = P((X-\mu)^2 \geq \varepsilon^2) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^2]}{\varepsilon^2} = \frac{\text{Var}(X)}{\varepsilon^2}$$

**Chernoff from Markov.** Apply Markov to $Y = e^{tX}$ with $a = e^{ta}$:

$$P(X \geq a) = P(e^{tX} \geq e^{ta}) \leq \frac{\mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]}{e^{ta}} = e^{-ta} M_X(t)$$

Optimise over $t > 0$ to get the sharpest bound. This is the MGF method.

## 7. Tail Sum Formula

For a non-negative integer-valued RV $X$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P(X > k) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty P(X \geq k)}$$

For a non-negative continuous RV $X$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^\infty P(X > t), dt}$$

**Proof (continuous).** Switch the order of integration:

$$\int_0^\infty P(X > t), dt = \int_0^\infty \int_t^\infty f_X(x), dx, dt = \int_0^\infty \int_0^x dt, f_X(x), dx = \int_0^\infty x, f_X(x), dx = \mathbb{E}[X]$$

The region ${(t,x) : 0 \leq t \leq x}$ is integrated two ways: first $x$ then $t$, or first $t$ then $x$. $\blacksquare$

**Proof (discrete).** Write $X = \sum_{k=1}^\infty \mathbf{1}_{X \geq k}$, take expectations, apply linearity. $\mathbb{E}[\mathbf{1}_{X \geq k}] = P(X \geq k)$. $\blacksquare$

> _This formula converts a sum/integral over the distribution of $X$ into a sum/integral over the survival function $P(X > t)$. It's often easier to compute $P(X > t)$ than the full distribution, so this is a genuine computational tool — not just a curiosity._

**Example.** $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ on ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$ (number of failures before first success). Then $P(X > k) = (1-p)^{k+1}$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty (1-p)^{k+1} = \frac{1-p}{1-(1-p)} = \frac{1-p}{p}$$

No need for the standard $\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum k(1-p)^k p$ computation.

## 8. Symmetry

If $X$ is symmetric about 0 (i.e. $X \overset{d}{=} -X$) and $\mathbb{E}|X| < \infty$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = 0$$

**Proof.** $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[-X] = -\mathbb{E}[X]$, so $\mathbb{E}[X] = 0$. $\blacksquare$

More generally, if $X$ is symmetric about $\mu$ (i.e. $X - \mu \overset{d}{=} \mu - X$), then $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mu$.

## 9. Summary

|Property|Statement|Key condition|
|---|---|---|
|Linearity|$\mathbb{E}[aX+bY] = a\mathbb{E}[X]+b\mathbb{E}[Y]$|None — always holds|
|Non-negativity|$X \geq 0 \Rightarrow \mathbb{E}[X] \geq 0$|$X \geq 0$ a.s.|
|LOTUS|$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] = \int g(x)f(x)dx$|$g$ measurable|
|Product rule|$\mathbb{E}[XY] = \mathbb{E}[X]\mathbb{E}[Y]$|$X \perp Y$|
|Jensen|$\mathbb{E}[g(X)] \geq g(\mathbb{E}[X])$|$g$ convex|
|Markov|$P(X\geq a) \leq \mathbb{E}[X]/a$|$X \geq 0$, $a > 0$|
|Tail sum|$\mathbb{E}[X] = \int_0^\infty P(X>t)dt$|$X \geq 0$|
|Symmetry|$X \overset{d}{=} -X \Rightarrow \mathbb{E}[X] = 0$|Symmetric about 0|
