

> _Eve's Law is Adam's Law for variance. But while Adam's Law feels natural — of course you recover the expectation by averaging the conditional expectation — Eve's Law has a surprise in it. Variance doesn't just telescope. There's an extra term, and that extra term is the whole story: it separates what $Y$ explains from what it doesn't. Once you see the two-term decomposition clearly, you see it everywhere: ANOVA, $R^2$, the bias-variance tradeoff, Wald's identity, multi-level models._

---

## 0. Statement

Let $X$ and $Y$ be random variables with $\mathbb{E}[X^2] < \infty$. Then:

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)]}_{\text{unexplained variance}} + \underbrace{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y])}_{\text{explained variance}}}$$

Both terms are non-negative numbers. They sum to $\text{Var}(X)$.

---

## 1. Proof

Start from the identity $\text{Var}(Z) = \mathbb{E}[Z^2] - (\mathbb{E}[Z])^2$ applied to $Z = X$.

**Step 1.** Apply Adam's Law to $\mathbb{E}[X^2]$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid Y]]$$

Write $\mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid Y] = \text{Var}(X \mid Y) + (\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2$ (the conditional version of $\mathbb{E}[Z^2] = \text{Var}(Z) + (\mathbb{E}[Z])^2$):

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \mathbb{E}[(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2]$$

**Step 2.** Apply Adam's Law to $\mathbb{E}[X]$:

$$(\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = (\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]])^2$$

**Step 3.** Subtract:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$$

$$= \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \underbrace{\mathbb{E}[(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2] - (\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]])^2}_{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])}$$

$$= \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]) \qquad \blacksquare$$

> _The proof is two applications of Adam's Law plus the identity $\mathbb{E}[Z^2] - (\mathbb{E}[Z])^2 = \text{Var}(Z)$ recognised in the right place. The "explained" term $\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$ appears precisely because variance is nonlinear — if we were summing linear things, there'd be no extra term. This is why variance doesn't telescope the way expectation does._

---

## 2. Naming the Terms

Let $\mu(y) = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y=y]$ and $v(y) = \text{Var}(X\mid Y=y)$. Then:

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] = \int v(y), f_Y(y), dy$$

$$\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]) = \int (\mu(y) - \mathbb{E}[X])^2, f_Y(y), dy$$

The first is the **average within-group variance** — how spread out $X$ is within each $Y$-slice, averaged over $Y$.

The second is the **variance of group means** — how much the group means $\mu(y)$ themselves vary around the grand mean $\mathbb{E}[X]$.

**Verbal summary:** Total variance = average spread within groups + spread of group means.

This is exactly the ANOVA decomposition:

$$\text{SS}_\text{total} = \text{SS}_\text{within} + \text{SS}_\text{between}$$

---

## 3. Boundary Cases

These four cases build intuition for what each term measures.

**Case 1: $Y$ explains everything ($X$ is a function of $Y$).**

$X = g(Y)$ a.s. Then $\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] = g(Y) = X$ a.s., so $\text{Var}(X\mid Y) = 0$ a.s. Eve's Law:

$$\text{Var}(X) = 0 + \text{Var}(g(Y)) = \text{Var}(X) \qquad \checkmark$$

All variance is explained. The unexplained term vanishes.

**Case 2: $Y$ explains nothing ($X \perp Y$).**

$\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] = \mathbb{E}[X]$ (constant), so $\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]) = 0$. Eve's Law:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + 0 = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X)] = \text{Var}(X) \qquad \checkmark$$

All variance is unexplained. The explained term vanishes.

**Case 3: $\text{Var}(X\mid Y)$ is constant in $Y$.**

This is **homoskedasticity**. Then $\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] = \text{Var}(X\mid Y=y)$ for any $y$ — the average equals the constant. Eve's Law becomes:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \text{Var}(X\mid Y=y) + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$$

This holds exactly for the bivariate normal, where $\text{Var}(Y\mid X) = \sigma_Y^2(1-\rho^2)$ for all $X$.

**Case 4: $\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]$ is constant in $Y$ but $\text{Var}(X\mid Y)$ is not.**

Then $\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]) = 0$ (no explained variance) even though $X$ and $Y$ are not independent. The conditional means agree but the conditional variances differ — $Y$ tells you about the spread of $X$, not its centre. Eve's Law correctly assigns zero explained variance here.

---

## 4. The $R^2$ Connection

From [[Linear Regression]], when regressing $X$ on $Y$:

$$R^2 = \rho^2 = \frac{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])}{\text{Var}(X)}$$

This is the fraction of total variance that is explained. Eve's Law gives:

$$1 - R^2 = \frac{\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]}{\text{Var}(X)}$$

the fraction that is unexplained.

When $\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]$ is linear in $Y$ (as in the bivariate normal), $R^2 = \rho^2$ exactly. When the conditional expectation is nonlinear, the true $R^2$ (defined via Eve's Law) exceeds the squared correlation $\rho^2$ — the linear regression understates the explanatory power of $Y$.

---

## 5. Eve's Law with Extra Conditioning

The version from [[Adams Law with Extra Conditioning]]:

$$\text{Var}(X \mid Z) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y, Z) \mid Z] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Z)$$

**Proof.** Freeze $Z = z$. Apply Eve's Law in the conditional universe where $Z = z$. Unfreeze. $\blacksquare$

Same decomposition, but everything lives inside the $Z$-conditional world. Used in multi-level models where $Z$ is already controlled for.

## 6. Worked Examples

### 6.1 Beta-Binomial

$W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, $X\mid W \sim \text{Binomial}(n,W)$, $s = a+b$.

|Term|Value|
|---|---|
|$\mathbb{E}[X\mid W]$|$nW$|
|$\text{Var}(X\mid W)$|$nW(1-W)$|
|$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid W)]$|$n(\mathbb{E}[W] - \mathbb{E}[W^2]) = \dfrac{nab}{s(s+1)}$|
|$\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid W])$|$\text{Var}(nW) = \dfrac{n^2 ab}{s^2(s+1)}$|
|$\text{Var}(X)$|$\dfrac{nab(s+n)}{s^2(s+1)}$|

As $s \to \infty$ with $a/s \to p$ fixed (strong prior concentrating at $p$): $\text{Var}(X) \to np(1-p)$, the ordinary Binomial variance. The randomness in $W$ vanishes and we recover the fixed-$p$ case.

### 6.2 Wald's Second Identity

$N$ a non-negative integer-valued RV, $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, independent of $N$. Let $S = X_1 + \cdots + X_N$.

$$\mathbb{E}[S \mid N] = N\mu, \qquad \text{Var}(S\mid N) = N\sigma^2$$

Eve's Law:

$$\text{Var}(S) = \mathbb{E}[N\sigma^2] + \text{Var}(N\mu) = \sigma^2,\mathbb{E}[N] + \mu^2,\text{Var}(N)$$

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(S) = \sigma^2,\mathbb{E}[N] + \mu^2,\text{Var}(N)}$$

**Interpretation:**

- $\sigma^2,\mathbb{E}[N]$: variance from the randomness within the $X_i$'s — average number of terms times per-term variance
- $\mu^2,\text{Var}(N)$: variance from not knowing how many terms there are — each uncertain term contributes mean $\mu$, so uncertainty in count propagates as $\mu^2,\text{Var}(N)$

When $\mu = 0$: $\text{Var}(S) = \sigma^2,\mathbb{E}[N]$. The random count doesn't add extra variance because each term contributes nothing on average.

When $N$ is deterministic: $\text{Var}(N) = 0$, so $\text{Var}(S) = n\sigma^2$. Standard result for a fixed sum.

### 6.3 Mixture Distributions

$Y \sim \text{Bernoulli}(p)$ (group indicator), $X\mid Y=0 \sim N(\mu_0, \sigma_0^2)$, $X\mid Y=1 \sim N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] = \mu_Y = \begin{cases} \mu_0 & Y=0 \ \mu_1 & Y=1 \end{cases}$$

$$\text{Var}(X\mid Y) = \sigma_Y^2 = \begin{cases} \sigma_0^2 & Y=0 \ \sigma_1^2 & Y=1 \end{cases}$$

**Unexplained variance:**

$$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] = (1-p)\sigma_0^2 + p\sigma_1^2$$

the weighted average of within-group variances.

**Explained variance:**

$$\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]) = \text{Var}(\mu_Y) = (1-p)(\mu_0 - \bar{\mu})^2 + p(\mu_1 - \bar{\mu})^2$$

where $\bar{\mu} = (1-p)\mu_0 + p\mu_1$. This simplifies to $p(1-p)(\mu_1 - \mu_0)^2$.

**Total:**

$$\text{Var}(X) = (1-p)\sigma_0^2 + p\sigma_1^2 + p(1-p)(\mu_1-\mu_0)^2$$

> _The last term is the contribution from the groups having different means. Even if $\sigma_0 = \sigma_1 = 0$ (groups are deterministic), the mixture still has variance $p(1-p)(\mu_1-\mu_0)^2$ from the uncertainty in which group you're in. This is the variance of a Bernoulli times the squared gap — exactly right._

## 7. Geometric View

From [[Geometric Interpretation of Conditional Expectation]]: Eve's Law is the **Pythagorean theorem** in $L^2(\Omega)$.

Let $\tilde{X} = X - \mathbb{E}[X]$ (centred). Its projection onto $V_Y = {g(Y)}$ is $\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] - \mathbb{E}[X]$.

$$\underbrace{|\tilde{X}|^2}_{\text{Var}(X)} = \underbrace{|\tilde{X} - \text{proj}|^2}_{\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]} + \underbrace{|\text{proj}|^2}_{\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])}$$

```
        X̃ (total variation)
       /|
      / |
     /  | residual ⊥ V_Y
    /   | (unexplained: E[Var(X|Y)])  
   /    |
  proj──┼──────── V_Y = {g(Y)}
  ↑
  E[X|Y] - E[X]
  (explained: Var(E[X|Y]))
```

The right angle is the orthogonality of the residual to the subspace — which is exactly the projection theorem for conditional expectation. Eve's Law is not a separate fact. It is the Pythagorean theorem, stated in the language of variance.

## 8. Common Mistakes

**Mistake: Telescoping variance like expectation.**

$$\text{Var}(X) \neq \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$$

This drops the explained variance term. Always include both terms.

**Mistake: Confusing $\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$ and $\text{Var}(X\mid Y)$.**

The first is a number (the average). The second is a random variable. You can't use them interchangeably.

**Mistake: Applying Eve's Law to conditional variance as though it's additive.**

$$\text{Var}(X + Z \mid Y) \neq \text{Var}(X\mid Y) + \text{Var}(Z\mid Y)$$

unless $X \perp Z \mid Y$. There's a conditional covariance cross term.

---

## 9. Summary

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$$

|Term|Name|Vanishes when|
|---|---|---|
|$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)]$|Unexplained / within-group|$X = g(Y)$ a.s.|
|$\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$|Explained / between-group|$X \perp Y$|

The proof uses Adam's Law twice. The geometry is the Pythagorean theorem in $L^2$. The applications are everywhere.
