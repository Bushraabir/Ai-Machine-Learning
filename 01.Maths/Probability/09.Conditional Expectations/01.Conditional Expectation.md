

> _This is the note I wish I had at the start of the course. Conditional expectation appears in two completely different-looking forms — conditioning on an event vs. conditioning on a random variable — and if you don't clearly separate them in your head, everything downstream (Adam's Law, martingales, Bayesian updating) becomes a blur. Write this down carefully once, and refer back to it._

---

## 0. Motivation

We already know $\mathbb{E}[X]$ — the average of $X$ over all randomness. But often we have **partial information**: we know the value of some other variable $Y$, or we know that some event $A$ occurred. Conditional expectation is the right tool to update our estimate of $X$ given that information.

The two cases:

1. **Conditioning on an event** $A$: gives a **number** $\mathbb{E}[X \mid A]$
2. **Conditioning on a random variable** $Y$: gives a **random variable** $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$

This distinction is the entire conceptual core of the note.

---

## 1. Conditioning on an Event

### 1.1 Definition

For an event $A$ with $P(A) > 0$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X \mid A] = \frac{\mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_A]}{P(A)}}$$

Equivalently, in the discrete case:

$$\mathbb{E}[X \mid A] = \sum_x x, P(X = x \mid A) = \sum_x x, \frac{P(X=x,, A)}{P(A)}$$

and in the continuous case:

$$\mathbb{E}[X \mid A] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x, f_{X\mid A}(x), dx$$

where $f_{X\mid A}(x) = f_X(x \cdot \mathbf{1}_A) / P(A)$ — i.e., the density of $X$ **restricted to** $A$ and renormalised.

> _Think of it as: throw away all outcomes where $A$ didn't happen, renormalise what's left, then compute the ordinary expectation. It's just a weighted average over a restricted universe._

### 1.2 The Law of Total Expectation (LTE) — Event Version

If $A_1, A_2, \ldots, A_n$ partition $\Omega$ (disjoint, exhaustive) with $P(A_i) > 0$:

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[X \mid A_i], P(A_i)}$$

**Proof.** $$\sum_i \mathbb{E}[X \mid A_i] P(A_i) = \sum_i \mathbb{E}[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{A_i}] = \mathbb{E}!\left[X \sum_i \mathbf{1}_{A_i}\right] = \mathbb{E}[X \cdot 1] = \mathbb{E}[X] \qquad \blacksquare$$

The last step uses $\sum_i \mathbf{1}_{A_i} = 1$ since the $A_i$ partition $\Omega$.

### 1.3 Example: Geometric from First Principles

Let $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ — number of trials until first success. Compute $\mathbb{E}[X]$ by conditioning on the first trial.

Let $A = {\text{first trial is success}}$, $A^c = {\text{first trial is failure}}$.

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid A], p + \mathbb{E}[X \mid A^c],(1-p)$$

- $\mathbb{E}[X \mid A] = 1$ (done in one trial)
- $\mathbb{E}[X \mid A^c] = 1 + \mathbb{E}[X]$ (wasted one trial, back to start by memorylessness)

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = p + (1-p)(1 + \mathbb{E}[X]) = 1 + (1-p)\mathbb{E}[X]$$

$$p,\mathbb{E}[X] = 1 \implies \mathbb{E}[X] = \frac{1}{p}$$

> _This is the first-step analysis / recursive expectation trick. Conditioning on an event, setting up the recurrence, solving. Memorise this pattern — it appears everywhere: random walks, branching processes, Markov chains._

## 2. Conditioning on a Random Variable

### 2.1 Definition

For a random variable $Y$, $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ is **not a number** — it is a **function of $Y$**, hence itself a random variable.

**Discrete $Y$:** For each possible value $y$ of $Y$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = y] = \sum_x x, P(X = x \mid Y = y)$$

Then $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ is the random variable obtained by plugging in: wherever $Y = y$, the value is $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y=y]$. Formally:

$$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y] = g(Y) \quad \text{where} \quad g(y) = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = y]$$

**Continuous $Y$:** Use the conditional density $f_{X \mid Y}(x \mid y) = f_{X,Y}(x,y)/f_Y(y)$:

$$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = y] = \int_{-\infty}^\infty x, f_{X\mid Y}(x \mid y), dx$$

Again, $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y] = g(Y)$ is a random variable.

> _This is the part that trips people up. $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = 3]$ is a number. $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ is a random variable — it's uncertain because $Y$ is uncertain. Once $Y$ is observed to be $3$, $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ becomes $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y=3]$, which is then a number. The randomness in $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ comes entirely from the randomness of $Y$._

### 2.2 The Law of Total Expectation (Adam's Law)

$$\boxed{\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]] = \mathbb{E}[X]}$$

**Proof (discrete case).**

$$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]] = \sum_y \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y=y], P(Y=y) = \sum_y \sum_x x, P(X=x \mid Y=y), P(Y=y)$$

$$= \sum_x x \sum_y P(X=x, Y=y) = \sum_x x, P(X=x) = \mathbb{E}[X] \qquad \blacksquare$$

**In words:** average the conditional expectation over the randomness in $Y$, and you recover the unconditional expectation. The inner expectation uses the randomness of $X$ given $Y$; the outer expectation averages over $Y$.

> _This is sometimes called the Tower Property or iterated expectation. It's one of the most-used tools in all of probability. Every time you see a complex expectation, ask: "what should I condition on to make $X$ easy?" Then Adam's Law collapses the conditioning._

### 2.3 Key Properties

Let $g$ be any (measurable) function. Then:

**(i) Linearity:** $$\mathbb{E}[aX + bZ \mid Y] = a,\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] + b,\mathbb{E}[Z \mid Y]$$

**(ii) Taking out what is known:** If $h(Y)$ is a function of $Y$ alone: $$\mathbb{E}[h(Y)\cdot X \mid Y] = h(Y)\cdot \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$$

_Proof._ Given $Y=y$, $h(Y) = h(y)$ is a constant, so it factors out of the expectation. $\blacksquare$

**(iii) Independence:** If $X \perp Y$: $$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y] = \mathbb{E}[X]$$

_Proof._ $P(X = x \mid Y = y) = P(X=x)$ by independence, so conditioning gives nothing. $\blacksquare$

**(iv) Tower property (iterated conditioning):** $$\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y, Z] \mid Y] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$$

The coarser conditioning wins: conditioning on less information on the outside collapses the finer conditioning inside.

## 3. The Law of Total Variance (Eve's Law)

$$\boxed{\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y])}$$

**Proof.**

Start from $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X^2] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2$ and apply Adam's Law to each piece.

By Adam's Law: $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]]$, so $(\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = (\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]])^2$.

Also: $\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid Y]]$.

Now write $\mathbb{E}[X^2 \mid Y] = \text{Var}(X\mid Y) + (\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2$. So:

$$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \mathbb{E}[(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2]$$

Therefore:

$$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \mathbb{E}[(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2] - (\mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]])^2$$

$$= \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]) \qquad \blacksquare$$

**Naming the two pieces:**

|Term|Name|What it captures|
|---|---|---|
|$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X \mid Y)]$|**Unexplained variance**|Average spread of $X$ within each value of $Y$ — the noise that $Y$ cannot explain|
|$\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y])$|**Explained variance**|Spread of the conditional mean as $Y$ varies — the variation that $Y$ explains|

> _Eve's Law is Adam's Law for variance. If you understand the decomposition "total = unexplained + explained," you understand ANOVA, linear regression $R^2$, and the bias-variance tradeoff at their core. That's not a coincidence — it's all the same identity._

### 3.1 Example: Beta-Binomial Variance

$W \sim \text{Beta}(a,b)$, $X \mid W \sim \text{Binomial}(n, W)$.

From [[Beta Expectations]] §6:

- $\text{Var}(X \mid W) = nW(1-W)$ — Binomial variance given $p = W$
- $\mathbb{E}[X \mid W] = nW$ — Binomial mean given $p = W$

**Unexplained variance:** $$\mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid W)] = n(\mathbb{E}[W] - \mathbb{E}[W^2]) = n\left(\frac{a}{s} - \frac{a(a+1)}{s(s+1)}\right) = \frac{nab}{s(s+1)}$$

where $s = a+b$.

**Explained variance:** $$\text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid W]) = \text{Var}(nW) = n^2,\text{Var}(W) = \frac{n^2 ab}{s^2(s+1)}$$

**Total:** $$\text{Var}(X) = \frac{nab}{s(s+1)} + \frac{n^2 ab}{s^2(s+1)} = \frac{nab}{s^2(s+1)}(s + n)$$

Consistent with [[Beta Expectations]] §6. ✓

## 4. Conditional Expectation as Projection

_This is the graduate-level view, but it's worth planting the seed now._

$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ is the **best predictor** of $X$ given $Y$, in the sense of minimising mean squared error:

$$\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y] = \underset{g(Y)}{\arg\min}; \mathbb{E}[(X - g(Y))^2]$$

**Proof.** For any function $g$:

$$\mathbb{E}[(X - g(Y))^2] = \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] + \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] - g(Y))^2]$$

The cross term vanishes (by the property $\mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])h(Y)] = 0$ for any $h$ — a consequence of "taking out what is known"). So:

$$= \mathbb{E}[(X - \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])^2] + \mathbb{E}[(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y] - g(Y))^2]$$

The first term doesn't depend on $g$; the second is $\geq 0$ and equals 0 iff $g(Y) = \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]$ a.s. $\blacksquare$

> _In Hilbert space language: $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y]$ is the orthogonal projection of $X$ onto the subspace of $Y$-measurable random variables, with inner product $\langle X, Z \rangle = \mathbb{E}[XZ]$. Eve's Law is the Pythagorean theorem in this space. This is where the theory is going — keep it in mind._

## 5. Common Patterns and Pitfalls

### Pattern 1: First-Step / Recursive Conditioning

Set up $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[X \mid A],P(A) + \mathbb{E}[X \mid A^c],P(A^c)$, express $\mathbb{E}[X \mid A^c]$ in terms of $\mathbb{E}[X]$ using structure of the problem, solve. Seen in: Geometric mean, random walk hitting times, Gambler's ruin.

### Pattern 2: Condition on the "Random Parameter"

Hierarchical model: $\Theta \sim \text{prior}$, $X \mid \Theta \sim \text{likelihood}$.

- $\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X \mid \Theta]]$ by Adam's Law
- $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid\Theta)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid\Theta])$ by Eve's Law

Seen in: Beta-Binomial, Poisson-Gamma (negative binomial), any mixture model.

### Pattern 3: Simplify $\mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)]$ by conditioning on $Y$

$$\mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[g(X,Y)\mid Y]] = \mathbb{E}[h(Y)]$$

where $h(y) = \mathbb{E}[g(X,y) \mid Y=y]$. The inner expectation treats $Y=y$ as fixed and integrates over $X$.

### Pitfall: $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y] \neq \mathbb{E}[X \mid Y = y]$

The left side is a random variable. The right side (for fixed $y$) is a number. Don't write one when you mean the other.

### Pitfall: $\mathbb{E}[X \mid Y] \neq \mathbb{E}[X]$ unless $X \perp Y$

Conditioning changes expectations unless independence holds. This sounds obvious but gets misapplied under pressure.

## 6. Summary

||Conditioning on event $A$|Conditioning on RV $Y$|
|---|---|---|
|Result type|**Number**|**Random variable**|
|Definition|$\mathbb{E}[X\cdot\mathbf{1}_A]/P(A)$|$g(Y)$ where $g(y) = \mathbb{E}[X\mid Y=y]$|
|Total expectation|$\mathbb{E}[X] = \sum_i \mathbb{E}[X\mid A_i]P(A_i)$|$\mathbb{E}[X] = \mathbb{E}[\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y]]$|
|Total variance|—|$\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[\text{Var}(X\mid Y)] + \text{Var}(\mathbb{E}[X\mid Y])$|
