

> _"A stationary distribution is a fixed point of the dynamics. Start there, and you never leave — not because you're frozen, but because the outflow from every state is exactly balanced by the inflow. It's equilibrium, not stasis. The chain is still moving; the distribution just doesn't notice."_

---

## The Definition

Let $(X_n)_{n \geq 0}$ be a Markov chain on state space $\mathcal{S}$ with transition matrix $Q$.

**Definition.** A probability distribution $\mathbf{s}$ (row vector, $s_i \geq 0$, $\sum_i s_i = 1$) is a **stationary distribution** (also called _steady-state_, _invariant_, or _equilibrium_ distribution) if:

$$\mathbf{s} Q = \mathbf{s}$$

Entrywise, for each $j \in \mathcal{S}$:

$$\sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} s_i , q_{ij} = s_j$$

**Equivalent characterization:** If $X_0 \sim \mathbf{s}$, then $X_n \sim \mathbf{s}$ for all $n \geq 1$.

**Proof of equivalence:** If $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$, then $\mathbf{t}^{(1)} = \mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$, and by induction $\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{s}Q^n = \mathbf{s}$ for all $n$. Conversely, if $\mathbf{t}^{(1)} = \mathbf{s}$ whenever $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} = \mathbf{s}$, then $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$. $\blacksquare$

> _The language of "stationary" can be misleading. The chain is not stationary in the sense of being stuck — it's still making random transitions at every step. What's stationary is the **distribution**: the probability mass on each state doesn't change over time. Think of it like a river at steady state: water flows, but the water level stays constant._

## The Balance Equation

Rewrite $\sum_i s_i q_{ij} = s_j$ as:

$$s_j = \sum_{i \neq j} s_i q_{ij} + s_j q_{jj}$$

$$s_j(1 - q_{jj}) = \sum_{i \neq j} s_i q_{ij}$$

$$s_j \sum_{k \neq j} q_{jk} = \sum_{i \neq j} s_i q_{ij}$$

**Left side:** Total rate of flow _out_ of state $j$ (weighted by $s_j$).  
**Right side:** Total rate of flow _into_ state $j$ from all other states.

This is called the **global balance equation**: at stationarity, the flow out of every state equals the flow into it. It is the probabilistic version of conservation of mass.

## Existence and Uniqueness

### Theorem (Finite Irreducible Chains)

If $(X_n)$ is an **irreducible** Markov chain on a **finite** state space, then:

1. A stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}$ **exists**.
2. It is **unique**.
3. It is given by $s_i = \frac{1}{E[T_i \mid X_0 = i]}$ — the reciprocal of the expected return time to state $i$.

**Proof of existence.** Since $\mathcal{S}$ is finite, every state in an irreducible chain is positive recurrent (no escape to infinity, pigeonhole forces infinitely many returns, and recurrence is a class property). For a positive recurrent state $i$, define $s_i = 1/m_i$ where $m_i = E[T_i \mid X_0 = i] < \infty$. One can verify (via the renewal theory argument) that this defines a probability distribution satisfying $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$.

**Proof of uniqueness.** Suppose $\mathbf{s}$ and $\mathbf{s}'$ are both stationary. Then $\mathbf{s}Q^n = \mathbf{s}$ and $\mathbf{s}'Q^n = \mathbf{s}'$ for all $n$. For an irreducible aperiodic chain, $Q^n \to \mathbf{1s}$ (convergence theorem). Taking $n \to \infty$: $\mathbf{s}' = \mathbf{s}' \cdot \mathbf{1s} = \mathbf{s}$. $\square$

For an irreducible periodic chain, $Q^n$ doesn't converge, but the uniqueness of $\mathbf{s}$ still holds — periodicity only breaks convergence, not uniqueness.

> _The formula $s_i = 1/m_i$ is one of the most useful things to know. It says: the fraction of time the chain spends in state $i$ in the long run is the reciprocal of the average time between visits. This is exactly what you'd expect from the law of large numbers — if returns happen every $m_i$ steps on average, the chain is in state $i$ a fraction $1/m_i$ of the time. The stationary distribution is the long-run time average._

### What Can Go Wrong Without Irreducibility

**Multiple stationary distributions.** If the chain has two closed communicating classes $C_1$ and $C_2$, each has its own stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}_1$ and $\mathbf{s}_2$, and every convex combination $\alpha \mathbf{s}_1 + (1-\alpha)\mathbf{s}_2$ is also stationary. Infinitely many stationary distributions.

**No stationary distribution.** If all states are transient (e.g., a random walk on $\mathbb{Z}$ with drift), there is no stationary distribution.

**Null recurrence.** On an infinite state space, an irreducible chain can be recurrent but null recurrent — every state is visited infinitely often, but $m_i = \infty$ for all $i$. Then $s_i = 1/m_i = 0$ for all $i$, which doesn't define a probability distribution. No stationary distribution exists.

## How to Find $\mathbf{s}$: The Method

### The System to Solve

Given transition matrix $Q$, find $\mathbf{s}$ satisfying:

$$\mathbf{s} Q = \mathbf{s} \qquad \text{(balance equations)}$$ $$\sum_{i} s_i = 1 \qquad \text{(normalization)}$$

The balance equations $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$ are equivalent to $\mathbf{s}(Q - I) = \mathbf{0}$, i.e., $\mathbf{s}$ is a left null vector of $Q - I$.

**Important:** The system $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$ alone has rank $M - 1$ (for an irreducible chain on $M$ states) — one equation is redundant, since they all sum to the same thing. Always **drop one equation and replace it with the normalization** $\sum_i s_i = 1$.

### General Procedure

1. Write out $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$ as $M$ scalar equations.
2. Drop any one equation (it's redundant).
3. Add $\sum_i s_i = 1$.
4. Solve the resulting $M \times M$ linear system.

### Two-State Example

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} 1-\alpha & \alpha \ \beta & 1-\beta \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \mathbf{s} = (s_1, s_2)$$

Balance equations: $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$:

$$s_1(1-\alpha) + s_2 \beta = s_1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad -\alpha s_1 + \beta s_2 = 0 \quad \Rightarrow \quad s_2 = \frac{\alpha}{\beta} s_1$$

Drop the second equation (redundant). Add normalization: $s_1 + s_2 = 1$:

$$s_1 + \frac{\alpha}{\beta} s_1 = 1 \implies s_1 = \frac{\beta}{\alpha + \beta}, \quad s_2 = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}$$

$$\boxed{\mathbf{s} = \left(\frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta},\ \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}\right)}$$

Sanity check via $s_i = 1/m_i$: the expected return time to state 1 should be $(\alpha+\beta)/\beta$. From state 1, the chain goes to state 2 with probability $\alpha$, then returns to state 1 in a geometric number of steps. This is consistent. ✓

## Detailed Balance and Reversibility

### Definition

The chain satisfies **detailed balance** with respect to distribution $\mathbf{s}$ if for all $i, j$:

$$s_i , q_{ij} = s_j , q_{ji}$$

Detailed balance says: the probability flux from $i$ to $j$ equals the flux from $j$ to $i$, at every pair of states individually.

This is **stronger** than global balance. Global balance requires total inflow = total outflow at each state. Detailed balance requires this pairwise, for every directed edge.

### Theorem

If $\mathbf{s}$ satisfies detailed balance with respect to $Q$, then $\mathbf{s}$ is stationary.

**Proof.** Sum the detailed balance condition over $i$:

$$\sum_i s_i q_{ij} = \sum_i s_j q_{ji} = s_j \sum_i q_{ji} = s_j \cdot 1 = s_j$$

This is exactly $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$. $\blacksquare$

The converse is false: a chain can have a stationary distribution without satisfying detailed balance.

### Reversibility

A chain that satisfies detailed balance with respect to its stationary distribution is called **reversible**. The terminology comes from the following:

**Proposition.** If $(X_n)$ is a stationary reversible chain, then the time-reversed process $(X_{T-n})_{0 \leq n \leq T}$ has the same distribution as $(X_n)_{0 \leq n \leq T}$.

In other words, you cannot tell from the distribution whether the chain is running forward or backward in time.

> _Reversibility is a strong structural property. Physical systems in thermal equilibrium satisfy it (it's the content of the principle of detailed balance in statistical mechanics). In MCMC, the Metropolis-Hastings algorithm is specifically designed to satisfy detailed balance — that's how it guarantees the target distribution is stationary. So detailed balance is not just a mathematical curiosity; it's an algorithmic tool._

### Using Detailed Balance to Find $\mathbf{s}$

For reversible chains, detailed balance is often easier to solve than the global balance equations. The method:

1. Write $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$ for all pairs $(i,j)$ with $q_{ij} > 0$.
2. Express all $s_i$ in terms of $s_1$ (or any reference state) by multiplying ratios.
3. Normalize.

**Example — Birth-Death Chain:**

States ${0, 1, \ldots, M}$, $q_{k,k+1} = p_k$, $q_{k,k-1} = r_k$, $q_{kk} = 1 - p_k - r_k$. Detailed balance:

$$s_k p_k = s_{k+1} r_{k+1}$$

So $s_{k+1} = s_k \cdot \frac{p_k}{r_{k+1}}$. Telescoping:

$$s_k = s_0 \cdot \frac{p_0 p_1 \cdots p_{k-1}}{r_1 r_2 \cdots r_k}$$

Normalize to find $s_0$. Birth-death chains always satisfy detailed balance — they're always reversible.

> _Birth-death chains are the Coupon Collector and Gambler's Ruin in disguise. The Coupon Collector is a birth-death chain with $p_k = (n-k)/n$ and $r_k = 0$ (only births, no deaths — an absorbing chain). The two-state chain is the simplest birth-death chain. Detailed balance applies to all of them._

## The Stationary Distribution and Long-Run Behavior

### Theorem (Ergodic Theorem for Markov Chains)

For an **irreducible positive recurrent** chain, for any bounded function $f : \mathcal{S} \to \mathbb{R}$, almost surely:

$$\frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} f(X_k) \longrightarrow \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} s_i f(i) = E_{\mathbf{s}}[f(X)]$$

In particular, taking $f = \mathbf{1}_j$ (indicator of state $j$):

$$\frac{1}{n} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \mathbf{1}[X_k = j] \longrightarrow s_j$$

**The long-run fraction of time spent in state $j$ is $s_j$, regardless of the initial state.**

This is the law of large numbers for Markov chains. It holds without any aperiodicity assumption — unlike the distributional convergence theorem ($\mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \mathbf{s}$), which requires aperiodicity. The time-average always converges; the distribution at a fixed time might oscillate (if periodic).

> _This is why $\mathbf{s}$ is called the stationary distribution and not the "limiting distribution." Limiting distribution requires aperiodicity. Stationary distribution just requires irreducibility and positive recurrence. The ergodic theorem is the more fundamental statement — it's about time averages, which always converge._

## Stationary Distribution Does Not Require Convergence

A periodic chain still has a unique stationary distribution — it just doesn't converge to it in the distributional sense.

**Example:** Two-state chain with $\alpha = \beta = 1$ (deterministic alternation):

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

The stationary distribution is $\mathbf{s} = (1/2, 1/2)$ (solve $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$). But starting from state 1: $X_n = 1$ if $n$ even, $X_n = 2$ if $n$ odd. The distribution of $X_n$ oscillates between $\delta_1$ and $\delta_2$ — it never converges to $(1/2, 1/2)$.

However, the **time average** $\frac{1}{n}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \mathbf{1}[X_k = 1] \to 1/2$ almost surely. The ergodic theorem holds; distributional convergence doesn't.

## Summary: Three Levels of "Convergence"

|Statement|Requires|What it says|
|---|---|---|
|$\mathbf{s}$ exists and is unique|Irreducible + positive recurrent (automatic for finite irreducible)|There is a well-defined long-run distribution|
|Time average $\to s_j$ a.s.|Irreducible + positive recurrent|Fraction of visits to $j$ converges to $s_j$|
|$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \mathbf{s}$|Irreducible + aperiodic (+ positive recurrent)|Distribution at time $n$ converges to $\mathbf{s}$|

Each level is strictly stronger than the one above.

## Key Facts at a Glance

|Fact|Statement|
|---|---|
|Definition|$\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$, $s_i \geq 0$, $\sum_i s_i = 1$|
|Existence + uniqueness|Guaranteed for finite irreducible chains|
|Formula|$s_i = 1/E[T_i \mid X_0 = i]$|
|How to compute|Solve $\mathbf{s}(Q-I) = 0$ with normalization; drop one balance equation|
|Detailed balance|$s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$ $\Rightarrow$ $\mathbf{s}$ stationary (sufficient, not necessary)|
|Reversibility|Detailed balance $\Leftrightarrow$ chain is time-reversible|
|Ergodic theorem|Time average of $f(X_k)$ $\to$ $E_\mathbf{s}[f]$ a.s. (no aperiodicity needed)|
|Distributional convergence|$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \mathbf{s}$ requires aperiodicity in addition|

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> A stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}$ satisfies $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$; for finite irreducible chains it exists, is unique, and equals $s_i = 1/m_i$ (reciprocal of mean return time). Find it by solving the balance equations with normalization. Detailed balance $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$ is sufficient for stationarity and characterizes reversibility. The ergodic theorem says time averages converge to $s_j$ always; distributional convergence $\mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \mathbf{s}$ additionally requires aperiodicity.
