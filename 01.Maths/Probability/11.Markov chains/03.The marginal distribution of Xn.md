

> _" is the simplest question you can ask about a Markov chain after time zero. It's just LOTP, applied once. But the answer — $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$ — is the thread that connects initial conditions to long-run behavior."_

---

## The Question

I have a Markov chain $(X_n)_{n \geq 0}$ with state space $\mathcal{S} = {1, \ldots, M}$, transition matrix $Q$, and some initial distribution $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$. I want to know:

$$\text{What is } P(X_n = j) \text{ for each state } j?$$

This is the **marginal distribution of $X_n$** — the unconditional distribution of where the chain is at time $n$, averaging over all possible starting points.

Note the contrast with what I computed in [[n-Step Transition Probability]]: that was the **conditional** distribution $P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i)$. Now I'm asking for the **unconditional** $P(X_n = j)$, which means I need to average over the randomness in $X_0$ as well.

---

## Setup and Notation

**Initial distribution:** A row vector $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$ where:

$$t^{(0)}_i = P(X_0 = i), \qquad \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} t^{(0)}_i = 1, \qquad t^{(0)}_i \geq 0$$

**Marginal distribution at time $n$:** A row vector $\mathbf{t}^{(n)}$ where:

$$t^{(n)}_j = P(X_n = j)$$

The goal is to find $\mathbf{t}^{(n)}$ in terms of $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$ and $Q$.

---

## The Main Result

### Theorem

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$$

Entrywise:

$$P(X_n = j) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} P(X_0 = i) \cdot (Q^n)_{ij} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} t^{(0)}_i \cdot q_{ij}^{(n)}$$

### Proof

Apply the Law of Total Probability, conditioning on $X_0$:

$$P(X_n = j) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i) \cdot P(X_0 = i)$$

From [[n-Step Transition Probability]], we know $P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i) = q_{ij}^{(n)} = (Q^n)_{ij}$. Substituting:

$$P(X_n = j) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} t^{(0)}_i \cdot (Q^n)_{ij}$$

This is the $j$-th entry of the row vector $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$. $\blacksquare$

> _The proof is one line of real content: LOTP on $X_0$, then plug in the $n$-step result. The conceptual content is that the marginal distribution is a **mixture** of the $n$-step conditional distributions, weighted by the initial distribution. The initial randomness and the transition randomness combine via matrix multiplication._

---

## The One-Step Recursion

A special case worth isolating:

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n+1)} = \mathbf{t}^{(n)} Q$$

**Proof:** $\mathbf{t}^{(n+1)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^{n+1} = (\mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n) Q = \mathbf{t}^{(n)} Q$. $\blacksquare$

Entrywise:

$$P(X_{n+1} = j) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} P(X_n = i) \cdot q_{ij}$$

This is the most natural update rule: the distribution tomorrow is the distribution today, left-multiplied by $Q$. Each step applies one copy of $Q$ from the right.

> _I think of this as the "forward equation" for distributions. It's completely analogous to how transition matrices act on states — except instead of a state, I'm evolving an entire distribution. The chain on distributions is itself a linear dynamical system $\mathbf{t} \mapsto \mathbf{t}Q$._

## Special Initial Conditions

### Case 1: Deterministic Start at State $i$

If $X_0 = i$ with certainty, then $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} = \mathbf{e}_i$ (the $i$-th standard basis row vector). Then:

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{e}_i Q^n = \text{row } i \text{ of } Q^n$$

So the $n$-step conditional distribution $P(X_n = \cdot \mid X_0 = i)$ is just row $i$ of $Q^n$. The marginal distribution in this case is the same as the $n$-step transition distribution. No surprise — if the start is deterministic, there's nothing to average over.

### Case 2: Uniform Start

If $X_0 \sim \text{Uniform}(\mathcal{S})$, then $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} = \frac{1}{M} \mathbf{1}^\top$ (all entries $\frac{1}{M}$). Then:

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \frac{1}{M} \mathbf{1}^\top Q^n = \frac{1}{M} \mathbf{1}^\top$$

Wait — is that right? Only if $\mathbf{1}^\top Q = \mathbf{1}^\top$, i.e., if the uniform distribution is stationary. That's not true in general. In general:

$$t^{(n)}_j = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{i} (Q^n)_{ij} = \frac{1}{M} (\text{column sum of column } j \text{ of } Q^n)$$

Column sums of $Q^n$ are not 1 in general (only row sums are). The uniform distribution is stationary if and only if $Q$ is **doubly stochastic** (both rows and columns sum to 1).

### Case 3: Stationary Start

If $X_0 \sim \mathbf{s}$ where $\mathbf{s} Q = \mathbf{s}$, then:

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{s} Q^n = \mathbf{s}$$

The chain started in stationarity stays in stationarity for all time. This is the defining property of $\mathbf{s}$, and $\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$ makes it transparent.

---

## Interpretations

### As a Mixture

$$P(X_n = j) = \sum_i P(X_0 = i) \cdot P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i)$$

The marginal distribution of $X_n$ is a **mixture** of the conditional distributions $P(X_n = \cdot \mid X_0 = i)$, with mixing weights given by the initial distribution $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$. This is LOTP in its purest form — there's no Markov property needed here, just conditioning.

### As a Linear Map

The map $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} \mapsto \mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$ is a **linear map** on the space of probability vectors (the $(M-1)$-dimensional simplex). Different initial distributions evolve differently, but the evolution is linear — a key feature that makes Markov chains tractable.

### As Convergence

For an irreducible aperiodic chain with stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}$, we know $Q^n \to \mathbf{1s}$. Therefore:

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n \to \mathbf{t}^{(0)} \cdot \mathbf{1s} = \left(\sum_i t^{(0)}_i\right) \mathbf{s} = \mathbf{s}$$

**Every initial distribution converges to $\mathbf{s}$.** The marginal distribution of $X_n$ approaches stationarity as $n \to \infty$, regardless of $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$. The $n$-step marginal is the most direct way to see this: the initial distribution gets washed out.

> _This is the probabilistic content of the convergence theorem: I can start anywhere. If I wait long enough, I cannot distinguish the distribution of $X_n$ from $\mathbf{s}$. The initial condition becomes irrelevant. The chain "forgets" $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$ at rate $|\lambda_2|^n$._

## Worked Example

### Two-State Chain

$\mathcal{S} = {1, 2}$, $Q = \begin{pmatrix} 1-\alpha & \alpha \ \beta & 1-\beta \end{pmatrix}$, initial distribution $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} = (p, 1-p)$.

From [[n-Step Transition Probability]], the $n$-step matrix is:

$$Q^n = \frac{1}{\alpha+\beta}\begin{pmatrix} \beta & \alpha \ \beta & \alpha \end{pmatrix} + \frac{(1-\alpha-\beta)^n}{\alpha+\beta} \begin{pmatrix} \alpha & -\alpha \ -\beta & \beta \end{pmatrix}$$

So:

$$P(X_n = 1) = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n \text{ entry 1} = \frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta} + \frac{(1-\alpha-\beta)^n}{\alpha+\beta}\bigl(p\alpha - (1-p)\beta\bigr)$$

$$P(X_n = 2) = \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta} - \frac{(1-\alpha-\beta)^n}{\alpha+\beta}\bigl(p\alpha - (1-p)\beta\bigr)$$

**Check:** $P(X_n = 1) + P(X_n = 2) = 1$. ✓

**As $n \to \infty$:** Both converge to $\frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta}$ and $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}$ respectively — the stationary distribution — regardless of $p$. ✓

**Special case $p = \frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta}$:** The correction term vanishes for all $n$, confirming that starting in stationarity keeps you there.

## What $\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$ Does NOT Say

A point worth being careful about:

$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$ gives the **marginal** distribution of $X_n$ alone. It says nothing about the **joint** distribution of $(X_0, X_1, \ldots, X_n)$, or even the **joint** distribution of $(X_0, X_n)$.

For the joint distribution of a path, I need:

$$P(X_0 = i_0, \ldots, X_n = i_n) = t^{(0)}_{i_0} \cdot q_{i_0 i_1} \cdot q_{i_1 i_2} \cdots q_{i_{n-1} i_n}$$

The marginal at time $n$ is obtained by summing this over all intermediate states $i_1, \ldots, i_{n-1}$ — which is exactly what matrix multiplication computes.

> _This is the distinction I keep needing to remember: the marginal $P(X_n = j)$ is a one-number summary of where the chain is at time $n$. The full path distribution is a much richer object. The Markov property makes both tractable, but they're different things._

## Summary

|Object|Formula|How to compute|
|---|---|---|
|Marginal at time $n$|$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$|Raise $Q$ to the $n$-th power|
|One-step update|$\mathbf{t}^{(n+1)} = \mathbf{t}^{(n)} Q$|Left-multiply current distribution by $Q$|
|Deterministic start at $i$|$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \text{row } i \text{ of } Q^n$|Same as $n$-step conditional|
|Stationary start|$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{s}$ for all $n$|By definition of stationarity|
|Long-run limit|$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \mathbf{s}$|Convergence theorem (irred. aperiodic)|

---

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> $P(X_n = j) = (\mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n)_j$: condition on $X_0$ via LOTP, plug in the $n$-step transition probabilities $(Q^n)_{ij}$, and what comes out is a row-vector left-multiplication. Every initial distribution converges to the stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}$ as $n \to \infty$ for irreducible aperiodic chains.
