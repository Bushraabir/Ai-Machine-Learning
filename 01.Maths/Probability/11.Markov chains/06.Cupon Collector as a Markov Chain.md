

> _"The Coupon Collector is the Gambler's Ruin turned inside out. Instead of asking when the chain gets absorbed at a boundary, I'm asking when it climbs through every rung of a ladder. The first-step equation still does all the work — but the state space is cleverer, and the answer involves a harmonic sum that I should know by heart."_

---

## The Problem

There are $n$ distinct coupon types. Each box of cereal contains one coupon, chosen uniformly at random (with replacement) from the $n$ types. I buy boxes one at a time.

**Question:** How many boxes do I expect to buy until I have collected at least one of every coupon type?

This is a classical problem, and the answer — $n H_n = n\left(1 + \frac{1}{2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n}\right)$ — is one of the results I want to know cold. But the goal here is to derive it rigorously as a Markov chain, with the full machinery of first-step equations.

---

## Setting Up the Markov Chain

### The Key Modeling Insight

The raw state "which coupons do I have?" is $2^n$ states — exponential, intractable. But I don't actually care _which_ coupons I have; I only care **how many distinct coupons** I have so far.

**Why is this Markov?** If I currently have $k$ distinct coupons, the probability of getting a new one on the next draw is $\frac{n-k}{n}$, and this depends only on $k$ — not on _which_ $k$ coupons I have, nor on the order in which I collected them. The count $k$ screens off all the history.

This is the Markov property in action as a **modeling choice**: I collapsed a $2^n$-state chain into an $(n+1)$-state chain by identifying the right summary statistic.

> _This collapsing move — finding the right low-dimensional summary that is Markov — is one of the core skills in applied probability. The wrong state space makes a problem intractable. The right one makes it a two-line calculation. For the Coupon Collector, the count of distinct coupons is the right state._

### State Space and Transitions

**State space:** $\mathcal{S} = {0, 1, 2, \ldots, n}$, where state $k$ = "I currently have $k$ distinct coupon types."

**Transition probabilities:** From state $k \in {0, 1, \ldots, n-1}$:

$$q_{k, k+1} = \frac{n-k}{n} \qquad \text{(new coupon — probability of drawing one of the } n-k \text{ types I don't have)}$$

$$q_{k, k} = \frac{k}{n} \qquad \text{(duplicate — probability of drawing one of the } k \text{ types I already have)}$$

$$q_{kj} = 0 \quad \text{for all other } j$$

**Boundary:** State $n$ is absorbing: $q_{nn} = 1$. Once I have all $n$ coupons, I'm done.

**The transition matrix** is upper bidiagonal (only diagonal and superdiagonal entries are nonzero for interior states):

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} k/n & (n-k)/n & & & \ & \ddots & \ddots & & \ & & k/n & (n-k)/n & \ & & & 1 & \end{pmatrix}$$

with $k$ running from $0$ to $n$ along the diagonal.

---

## Classification of States

**State $n$:** Absorbing (recurrent). Once all coupons are collected, the chain stays.

**States ${0, 1, \ldots, n-1}$:** From any state $k < n$, there is always positive probability of eventually reaching $n$ (the chain can only move right or stay, so it must eventually hit $n$). But no state $k < n$ can be reached from $n$. So ${0, \ldots, n-1}$ is an **open communicating class** — actually, states ${0, \ldots, n-1}$ don't even communicate with each other in the usual sense, since the chain is monotone (it never goes left). Each state $k < n$ can reach $n$ but not vice versa.

More precisely: each state $k \in {0, \ldots, n-1}$ is **transient**. The chain visits each one at most once before moving right — in fact, each state $k$ is visited exactly once (the chain starts at 0, passes through $1, 2, \ldots$ in order, and is absorbed at $n$).

> _The Gambler's Ruin chain could go left or right — it was a genuine random walk. The Coupon Collector chain only goes right or stays. It's monotone. This makes the classification trivial (all non-absorbing states are transient) and makes the first-step equations much simpler — there's no need to solve a coupled system, because from state $k$ the chain never returns to states $< k$._

## Expected Collection Time: First-Step Equations

Let $d_k = E[\text{steps to reach state } n \mid X_0 = k]$ — the expected number of additional boxes given I currently have $k$ distinct coupons.

**Boundary condition:**

$$d_n = 0$$

**First-step equation** for $k \in {0, 1, \ldots, n-1}$: condition on the next draw:

$$d_k = 1 + \frac{k}{n} \cdot d_k + \frac{n-k}{n} \cdot d_{k+1}$$

The $1$ is for the box I just opened. With probability $k/n$ it's a duplicate and I'm still in state $k$; with probability $(n-k)/n$ it's new and I move to state $k+1$.

### Solving for $d_k$

Rearrange: subtract $\frac{k}{n} d_k$ from both sides:

$$d_k\left(1 - \frac{k}{n}\right) = 1 + \frac{n-k}{n} \cdot d_{k+1}$$

$$d_k \cdot \frac{n-k}{n} = 1 + \frac{n-k}{n} \cdot d_{k+1}$$

Divide both sides by $\frac{n-k}{n}$:

$$d_k = \frac{n}{n-k} + d_{k+1}$$

This is a **telescoping recurrence**. No coupled system to solve — it unravels immediately.

### Telescoping to the Answer

Unroll from $k$ to $n$:

$$d_k = \frac{n}{n-k} + d_{k+1}$$ $$= \frac{n}{n-k} + \frac{n}{n-k-1} + d_{k+2}$$ $$= \frac{n}{n-k} + \frac{n}{n-k-1} + \cdots + \frac{n}{1} + d_n$$

Since $d_n = 0$:

$$\boxed{d_k = n \sum_{j=1}^{n-k} \frac{1}{j} = n\left(\frac{1}{1} + \frac{1}{2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n-k}\right)}$$

### The Answer: Starting from $k = 0$

$$d_0 = n \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{1}{j} = n H_n$$

where $H_n = 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \cdots + \frac{1}{n}$ is the $n$-th **harmonic number**.

$$\boxed{E[\text{coupons to complete the set}] = n H_n}$$

---

## Decomposition: The Waiting Times Interpretation

The telescoping structure has a beautiful probabilistic interpretation that I want to make explicit.

Define the **phases** of the collection process:

- **Phase 1:** Start with 0 coupons; wait for the 1st new coupon. This always happens on draw 1 — in state $k=0$, any draw is new, so $d_0 - d_1 = \frac{n}{n}= 1$.
- **Phase 2:** Have 1 coupon; wait for the 2nd new one. $d_1 - d_2 = \frac{n}{n-1}$.
- **Phase $k+1$:** Have $k$ coupons; wait for the $(k+1)$-th new one. $d_k - d_{k+1} = \frac{n}{n-k}$.
- **Phase $n$:** Have $n-1$ coupons; wait for the last new one. $d_{n-1} = \frac{n}{1} = n$.

In state $k$ (have $k$ coupons), the next box is new with probability $\frac{n-k}{n}$. So the number of boxes to get a new coupon is $\text{Geom}!\left(\frac{n-k}{n}\right)$, with expectation $\frac{n}{n-k}$.

The **total collection time** is:

$$T = T_1 + T_2 + \cdots + T_n$$

where $T_{k+1} \sim \text{Geom}!\left(\frac{n-k}{n}\right)$ is the waiting time for the $(k+1)$-th new coupon, and $T_1, \ldots, T_n$ are **independent** (the phases are independent by the Markov property — once I enter state $k$, the future depends only on $k$).

By linearity of expectation:

$$E[T] = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} E[T_{k+1}] = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{n}{n-k} = n \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{1}{j} = nH_n$$

This decomposition is the cleanest proof — no recurrence to set up, just linearity of expectation applied to the sum of independent geometric random variables.

> _The Markov chain machinery gives the same answer as the direct decomposition, but through a different path. The first-step equations are the "correct" Markov chain approach. The phase decomposition is the "correct" probabilistic approach. They agree, as they must. I like having both — the first-step approach generalizes to questions beyond just the expectation (e.g., hitting time distributions), while the decomposition gives clearer intuition._

## Asymptotics: How Fast Does $nH_n$ Grow?

Since $H_n = \ln n + \gamma + O(1/n)$ where $\gamma \approx 0.5772$ is the Euler-Mascheroni constant:

$$E[T] = n H_n \approx n \ln n + \gamma n + O(1)$$

The expected number of draws is $\Theta(n \log n)$ — superlinear but subquadratic.

**Numerical feel:**

|$n$|$nH_n$|Approx|
|---|---|---|
|10|29.3|$\approx 10 \ln 10 \approx 23$ + correction|
|50|224.5||
|100|518.7||
|365|2364|(days to collect all birthdays)|

For $n = 50$ coupons, expect to buy about 225 boxes. The last few coupons dominate — phase $n$ (waiting for the last coupon) alone takes $n$ draws in expectation.

> _The last coupon is the killer. If I need 1 specific coupon out of $n$, each draw hits it with probability $1/n$, so I wait $n$ draws on average. If $n = 100$, I expect to spend 100 draws just for the last coupon — and the first 99 cost only $100 H_{99} - 100 \approx 360$ draws total. The harmonic sum front-loads the easy coupons and back-loads the hard ones._

## Variance of the Collection Time

The first-step approach gives the expected value. For the variance, the phase decomposition is cleaner.

$$\text{Var}(T) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \text{Var}(T_{k+1})$$

by independence of the phases. For $T_{k+1} \sim \text{Geom}(p_k)$ with $p_k = (n-k)/n$:

$$\text{Var}(T_{k+1}) = \frac{1 - p_k}{p_k^2} = \frac{k/n}{(n-k)^2/n^2} = \frac{kn}{(n-k)^2}$$

So:

$$\text{Var}(T) = \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \frac{kn}{(n-k)^2} = n \sum_{j=1}^{n} \frac{n-j}{j^2} = n\left(n\sum_{j=1}^n \frac{1}{j^2} - H_n\right)$$

Asymptotically, using $\sum_{j=1}^n 1/j^2 \to \pi^2/6$:

$$\text{Var}(T) \approx \frac{\pi^2}{6} n^2 \quad \text{as } n \to \infty$$

The standard deviation is $\sim \frac{\pi}{\sqrt{6}} n \approx 1.28n$ — so the collection time concentrates around $nH_n$ with fluctuations of order $n$. Since $nH_n \sim n\ln n \gg n$, the relative fluctuation $\text{SD}(T)/E[T] \to 0$ — the collection time concentrates around its mean.

## The Distribution of $T$ via Inclusion-Exclusion

The Markov chain gives expectations and (via phase decomposition) variance. The full distribution of $T$ can be obtained by inclusion-exclusion — this is the approach in B&H.

Let $A_i$ = event "coupon type $i$ never appears in the first $t$ draws." Then $P(T > t) = P(A_1 \cup \cdots \cup A_n)$.

By inclusion-exclusion:

$$P(T > t) = \sum_{k=1}^{n} (-1)^{k-1} \binom{n}{k} \left(\frac{n-k}{n}\right)^t$$

This is an exact formula for the CDF of $T$, but it's not what Markov chains give directly — it comes from a different method. I record it here for completeness, but the Markov chain approach is the right one for expectations and first-step analysis.

## Generalizations Worth Knowing

### Non-Uniform Coupons

If coupon $i$ appears with probability $p_i$ (not necessarily $1/n$), the problem is no longer Markov with the "count" state, because the probability of getting a new coupon depends on _which_ coupons I have, not just how many. The state space explodes back to $2^n$.

This generalization is much harder. The expected collection time becomes:

$$E[T] = \sum_{\emptyset \neq S \subseteq [n]} \frac{(-1)^{|S|+1}}{1 - \sum_{i \in S} p_i}$$

by inclusion-exclusion. No clean closed form in general.

> _The uniform assumption is what collapses $2^n$ states to $n+1$. Non-uniform coupons break this symmetry entirely. This is a good reminder that the Markov property is a gift — it should never be taken for granted._

### Double Dixie Cup Problem

How many draws to collect at least $m$ copies of every coupon? The state becomes a vector in ${0, 1, \ldots, m}^n$ — large but still Markov. The expected collection time involves higher-order harmonic numbers. A much harder problem.

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> Model the Coupon Collector as a Markov chain on ${0, \ldots, n}$ where state $= $ number of distinct coupons; from state $k$, move to $k+1$ w.p. $(n-k)/n$. The first-step equation telescopes to $d_k = n/(n-k) + d_{k+1}$, giving $E[T] = nH_n$ — equivalently, the total time is the sum of $n$ independent geometric waiting times with success probabilities $n/n, (n-1)/n, \ldots, 1/n$.
