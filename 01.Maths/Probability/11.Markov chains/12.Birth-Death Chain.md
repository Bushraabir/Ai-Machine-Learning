> _"Birth-death chains are the one-dimensional Markov chains. The state space is a subset of $\mathbb{Z}$, transitions only go left or right by one step, and that constraint — no jumps — forces the chain to be reversible. Always. The entire theory collapses into a single telescoping recurrence, and the stationary distribution is just a product formula. Everything is explicit. There is no other family of chains where I can write down every quantity in closed form this cleanly."_

---

## Definition

A **birth-death chain** is a Markov chain on state space $\mathcal{S} \subseteq \mathbb{Z}$ — either ${0, 1, \ldots, M}$ (finite) or ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$ (semi-infinite) — with the constraint that transitions only go to adjacent states:

$$q_{k, k+1} = p_k \quad \text{(birth probability)}$$ $$q_{k, k-1} = r_k \quad \text{(death probability)}$$ $$q_{k,k} = 1 - p_k - r_k \quad \text{(stay probability)}$$ $$q_{k,j} = 0 \quad \text{for } |j - k| \geq 2$$

with boundary conditions: $$r_0 = 0 \quad \text{(can't go below 0)}$$ $$p_M = 0 \quad \text{(can't go above } M \text{, for finite chains)}$$

For the chain to be valid: $p_k, r_k \geq 0$ and $p_k + r_k \leq 1$ for all $k$.

The name comes from population biology: state $k$ is population size, $p_k$ is the probability of a birth (population increases by 1), and $r_k$ is the probability of a death (population decreases by 1). But the model appears everywhere — queues, genetics, chemical reactions, random walks.

> _I think of the birth-death chain as the Markov chain equivalent of a tridiagonal matrix: the transition matrix $Q$ has nonzero entries only on the main diagonal and the two adjacent diagonals. All the structure is in those three diagonals, and the no-jump constraint is what makes everything solvable._

---

## Irreducibility

The chain is **irreducible** if and only if $p_k > 0$ for all $k < M$ and $r_k > 0$ for all $k > 0$ (in the finite case). In words: births are always possible (unless at the top) and deaths are always possible (unless at the bottom). Every state can reach every other via a sequence of births and deaths.

If $p_k = 0$ for some interior $k$, then state $k$ is a barrier the chain can never cross upward — the state space splits. Similarly for $r_k = 0$.

I'll assume irreducibility throughout: $p_k > 0$ for $k < M$ and $r_k > 0$ for $k \geq 1$.

---

## Reversibility: Always

**Theorem.** Every irreducible birth-death chain is reversible with respect to its stationary distribution.

**Proof.** I need to verify detailed balance $s_k q_{kj} = s_j q_{jk}$ for all pairs $(k,j)$.

For $|k - j| \geq 2$: $q_{kj} = q_{jk} = 0$, so both sides are zero. ✓

For adjacent pairs $(k, k+1)$: the detailed balance condition is: $$s_k q_{k,k+1} = s_{k+1} q_{k+1,k}$$ $$s_k p_k = s_{k+1} r_{k+1}$$

This is one equation per adjacent pair. I haven't yet shown this holds — I'll use it to _find_ $\mathbf{s}$ in the next section. The point is that the only non-trivial detailed balance conditions are for adjacent pairs, and there are only $M$ of them (for a chain on ${0, \ldots, M}$). A birth-death chain satisfies detailed balance if and only if these $M$ conditions hold — and I'll construct $\mathbf{s}$ to make them hold. $\blacksquare$

The structural reason for reversibility: the birth-death chain lives on a path graph (or half-line). A path graph has no cycles. By Kolmogorov's criterion from [[Detailed Balance and Reversibility]], reversibility requires that the probability of traversing any cycle in one direction equals the probability in the other — but there are no cycles on a path graph, so the condition is vacuously satisfied. Reversibility is a topological fact about the state space, not a special property of the rates.

> _This is the deepest reason to care about birth-death chains: they are always reversible, which means I always have a clean product formula for the stationary distribution. The absence of cycles is what does it. The moment the state space has a loop — like the three-state cycle — reversibility can fail. On a line or half-line, there's nowhere for irreversibility to hide._

## The Stationary Distribution: The Product Formula

### Derivation

Write the detailed balance condition for each adjacent pair:

$$s_k p_k = s_{k+1} r_{k+1} \quad \text{for } k = 0, 1, \ldots, M-1$$

This gives a telescoping recurrence:

$$s_{k+1} = s_k \cdot \frac{p_k}{r_{k+1}}$$

Unrolling from $k = 0$:

$$s_1 = s_0 \cdot \frac{p_0}{r_1}$$ $$s_2 = s_1 \cdot \frac{p_1}{r_2} = s_0 \cdot \frac{p_0 p_1}{r_1 r_2}$$ $$s_k = s_0 \cdot \frac{p_0 p_1 \cdots p_{k-1}}{r_1 r_2 \cdots r_k}$$

Define the **traffic intensity ratios**:

$$\rho_0 = 1, \qquad \rho_k = \frac{p_0 p_1 \cdots p_{k-1}}{r_1 r_2 \cdots r_k} = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{p_j}{r_{j+1}} \quad \text{for } k \geq 1$$

Then $s_k = s_0 \rho_k$. Normalization $\sum_{k=0}^M s_k = 1$ gives:

$$s_0 \sum_{k=0}^M \rho_k = 1 \implies s_0 = \frac{1}{\sum_{k=0}^M \rho_k}$$

$$\boxed{s_k = \frac{\rho_k}{\displaystyle\sum_{\ell=0}^{M} \rho_\ell}, \qquad \rho_k = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{p_j}{r_{j+1}}}$$

This is the **product formula** for birth-death chains. It is completely explicit — no linear system to solve, just multiply ratios of birth and death rates and normalize.

> _I find it remarkable that a problem which in principle requires solving an $M \times M$ linear system reduces to a product of $M$ scalars. The detailed balance condition is what makes this possible: instead of the global constraint $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$ (coupling all states simultaneously), I get local constraints $s_k p_k = s_{k+1} r_{k+1}$ (coupling only adjacent pairs), and those decouple into a one-directional recurrence._

### For the Semi-Infinite Chain ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$

The same formula holds, but I need the series $\sum_{k=0}^\infty \rho_k$ to converge for a stationary distribution to exist.

**Stationary distribution exists** $\iff$ $\sum_{k=0}^\infty \rho_k < \infty$.

If $\sum_{k=0}^\infty \rho_k = \infty$, the chain has no stationary distribution. Whether this means the chain is transient or null recurrent depends on further analysis — but either way, there is no limiting distribution.

---

## Classification: When Is the Chain Positive Recurrent?

For the **finite** irreducible birth-death chain on ${0, \ldots, M}$: always positive recurrent (finite + irreducible, from [[Existence and Uniqueness of Stationary Distributions]]). The product formula always gives a valid stationary distribution.

For the **semi-infinite** chain on ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$:

|Condition|Classification|
|---|---|
|$\sum_{k=0}^\infty \rho_k < \infty$|Positive recurrent; stationary distribution $s_k = \rho_k / \sum \rho_\ell$|
|$\sum_{k=0}^\infty \rho_k = \infty$, $\sum_{k=0}^\infty 1/(p_k \rho_k) = \infty$|Null recurrent|
|$\sum_{k=0}^\infty \rho_k = \infty$, $\sum_{k=0}^\infty 1/(p_k \rho_k) < \infty$|Transient|

The second and third conditions come from the theory of one-dimensional random walks — the quantities $\sum 1/(p_k \rho_k)$ appear when computing expected hitting times and absorption probabilities via first-step equations.

> _The transience/null-recurrence distinction for semi-infinite chains is the one piece of this theory I find genuinely unintuitive. You'd think: if $\sum \rho_k = \infty$, the chain must drift to $+\infty$ and be transient. But null recurrence is the sneaky middle case — the chain wanders to $+\infty$ but always comes back, just with infinite expected return time. The simple symmetric random walk on $\mathbb{Z}_{\geq 0}$ (constant $p_k = r_k = 1/2$) is the canonical null recurrent birth-death chain._

## Examples

### Example 1: The Simple Random Walk (Gambler's Ruin)

$p_k = p$, $r_k = q = 1-p$ for all $k \in {1, \ldots, N-1}$, with absorbing boundaries $p_N = r_0 = 0$.

$$\rho_k = \left(\frac{p}{q}\right)^k$$

For $p \neq q$:

$$s_k = \frac{(p/q)^k}{\sum_{\ell=0}^N (p/q)^\ell} = \frac{(p/q)^k \cdot (1 - p/q)}{1 - (p/q)^{N+1}}$$

But wait — the Gambler's Ruin has absorbing states at 0 and $N$. Those are absorbing, not reflecting. The stationary distributions are $\delta_0$ and $\delta_N$ (all mass at the absorbing boundaries). The product formula above applies when I think of the chain as having reflecting boundaries — i.e., states 0 and $N$ push back into the interior.

For the reflecting version ($r_0 = 0$, $p_N = 0$, $p_k = p$, $r_k = q$ for interior states):

$$\rho_k = (p/q)^k, \qquad s_k = \frac{(p/q)^k}{\sum_{\ell=0}^N (p/q)^\ell}$$

For the fair walk $p = q = 1/2$: $\rho_k = 1$ for all $k$, so $s_k = 1/(N+1)$ — the uniform distribution. This confirms: the simple symmetric random walk on ${0, \ldots, N}$ with reflecting boundaries has the uniform stationary distribution. Makes sense: all states have the same degree in the path graph (interior states have degree 2, except the endpoints which have degree 1 — actually the endpoints have a self-loop in the reflecting version, restoring degree 2).

> _Pedantic but worth noting: for the reflecting boundary at 0 with $p_0 = 1$ (deterministically go to 1), then $r_1 = q$ gives $\rho_1 = p_0/r_1 = 1/q$, which breaks the symmetry slightly. The clean uniform result requires $p_0 = p$ (same birth probability at the boundary), which is the lazy/reflecting boundary convention. The details matter for the normalization._

### Example 2: The Ehrenfest Urn

$M$ balls, $d$ total, state = number in urn 1. From [[Detailed Balance and Reversibility]]:

$$p_k = \frac{d-k}{d}, \quad r_k = \frac{k}{d}$$

$$\rho_k = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{(d-j)/d}{(j+1)/d} = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} \frac{d-j}{j+1} = \binom{d}{k}$$

$$s_k = \frac{\binom{d}{k}}{2^d} = \text{Binomial}(d, 1/2) \text{ PMF at } k$$

The stationary distribution is $\text{Binomial}(d, 1/2)$. This is the equilibrium distribution of balls between two chambers — the maximum entropy distribution consistent with $d$ balls and equal probability of being in either urn.

### Example 3: $M/M/1$ Queue (Semi-Infinite)

A queueing model: customers arrive at rate $\lambda$ (Poisson), each served at rate $\mu$. State = number of customers in system. In discrete time approximation:

$$p_k = \lambda, \quad r_k = \mu \quad \text{for all } k \geq 1, \qquad p_0 = \lambda, \quad r_0 = 0$$

Let $\rho = \lambda/\mu$ (traffic intensity).

$$\rho_k = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\mu}\right)^k = \rho^k$$

The series $\sum_{k=0}^\infty \rho^k = \frac{1}{1-\rho}$ converges if and only if $\rho < 1$.

For $\rho < 1$: positive recurrent, stationary distribution $s_k = (1-\rho)\rho^k$ — a **Geometric** distribution on ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$ with parameter $1-\rho$.

For $\rho \geq 1$: no stationary distribution. The queue grows without bound — arrivals outpace service, the system never reaches equilibrium.

$$\boxed{s_k = (1-\rho)\rho^k, \quad k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots \qquad \text{(for } \rho < 1\text{)}}$$

The mean queue length at stationarity:

$$E_\mathbf{s}[X] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty k (1-\rho)\rho^k = \frac{\rho}{1-\rho}$$

As $\rho \to 1^-$ (arrival rate approaches service rate), the mean queue length $\to \infty$. The queue is technically stable but barely — small perturbations cause blowup. This is a classical result in queueing theory, and the birth-death product formula gives it in two lines.

> _The $M/M/1$ queue is the reason queueing theorists love birth-death chains. Every $M/M/s$ queue, $M/M/\infty$ queue, and Erlang model is a birth-death chain. The product formula gives the stationary distribution immediately, and from there every queueing metric — mean queue length, mean waiting time, throughput — follows by direct computation. Little's Law ($L = \lambda W$) is usually the next step._

### Example 4: Moran Model (Population Genetics)

$N$ individuals, each of type A or B. State $k$ = number of type-A individuals. At each step:

- One individual is chosen to reproduce (uniformly at random)
- One individual is chosen to die (uniformly at random)

$$p_k = \frac{k}{N} \cdot \frac{N-k}{N} = \frac{k(N-k)}{N^2}, \quad r_k = \frac{N-k}{N} \cdot \frac{k}{N} = \frac{k(N-k)}{N^2}$$

So $p_k = r_k$ for all $k$. Then $\rho_k = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} p_j/r_{j+1} = \prod_{j=0}^{k-1} 1 = 1$.

All $\rho_k = 1$, so the stationary distribution on ${0, 1, \ldots, N}$ would be uniform: $s_k = 1/(N+1)$.

But wait — states 0 and $N$ are absorbing (once all individuals are the same type, the population stays there forever). The chain is not irreducible. The absorbing states have their own stationary distributions $\delta_0$ and $\delta_N$. The interior states ${1, \ldots, N-1}$ are transient — the chain eventually fixes at 0 or $N$.

The birth-death product formula applies only when the chain is irreducible (or when we restrict to a closed class). For the Moran model, the interesting question is the absorption probability — which type fixes — and the expected fixation time, both computed by first-step equations as in [[Gamblers Ruin as a Markov Chain]].

> _The Moran model is a case where the birth-death structure is visible ($p_k = r_k$, symmetric), but the chain is reducible due to absorbing boundaries. The product formula gives what would be the stationary distribution on the interior — uniform, reflecting the symmetry between types A and B — but the chain never actually reaches this distribution because it gets absorbed first. Always check irreducibility before applying the product formula._

## Hitting Times via First-Step Equations

For birth-death chains, first-step equations reduce to second-order linear recurrences — the same structure as Gambler's Ruin.

Let $h_k = E[T_0 \mid X_0 = k]$, the expected hitting time to state 0. Boundary: $h_0 = 0$.

First-step equation for $k \geq 1$:

$$h_k = 1 + p_k h_{k+1} + r_k h_{k-1} + (1 - p_k - r_k) h_k$$

Simplify:

$$(p_k + r_k) h_k = 1 + p_k h_{k+1} + r_k h_{k-1}$$

$$p_k(h_k - h_{k+1}) = 1 - r_k(h_k - h_{k-1}) \cdot \frac{r_k}{p_k}$$

This doesn't simplify as cleanly as the absorption probability equation because it's non-homogeneous. The standard approach: let $u_k = h_k - h_{k-1}$ (first differences). Substituting:

$$p_k u_{k+1} = r_k u_k - 1 \quad \Rightarrow \quad u_{k+1} = \frac{r_k}{p_k} u_k - \frac{1}{p_k}$$

This is a first-order linear recurrence in $u_k$, solvable by the integrating factor method. The solution involves the $\rho_k$ ratios and sums thereof — explicit but messy. For the $M/M/1$ queue ($p_k = \lambda$, $r_k = \mu$, $\rho = \lambda/\mu$):

$$u_{k+1} = \rho \cdot u_k - \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

which gives a geometric series. The hitting time $h_k = \sum_{j=1}^k u_j$ then telescopes.

The **key insight** is that for birth-death chains, first-step equations always reduce to a one-dimensional recurrence — no matrix inversion needed, just algebra — because the chain can only move by $\pm 1$.

## The Global Balance Interpretation

The detailed balance condition $s_k p_k = s_{k+1} r_{k+1}$ has a physical interpretation that's worth dwelling on.

Think of probability as "fluid" flowing along the state space. At stationarity:

- Fluid flows rightward from $k$ to $k+1$ at rate $s_k p_k$ (probability of being at $k$ times birth rate).
- Fluid flows leftward from $k+1$ to $k$ at rate $s_{k+1} r_{k+1}$ (probability of being at $k+1$ times death rate).

Detailed balance says these two flows are equal — the **net flow across each edge is zero**. This is stronger than global balance (which only requires zero net flow at each node). For a one-dimensional chain, these are equivalent: if the net flow is zero across every edge, then summing over a contiguous set of edges gives zero net flow into any interval ${0, \ldots, k}$ — which is exactly the global balance condition for all states simultaneously.

> _This is the "flux" picture. For a birth-death chain, global balance and detailed balance are actually equivalent — not just detailed balance implying global balance. The reason: on a path graph, every "cut" separating the state space into ${0, \ldots, k}$ and ${k+1, \ldots, M}$ has exactly one edge crossing it (the edge $(k, k+1)$). Zero flow across every single edge is the same as zero flow across every cut. On graphs with cycles, this equivalence breaks — there can be "circulating currents" that satisfy global balance but not detailed balance. The birth-death chain's path topology forbids such currents._

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> Birth-death chains: transitions only $\pm 1$, so the path-graph topology forces reversibility (no cycles $\Rightarrow$ Kolmogorov criterion vacuous). Detailed balance gives $s_k p_k = s_{k+1} r_{k+1}$, which telescopes into the product formula $s_k \propto \rho_k = \prod_{j < k} p_j/r_{j+1}$. For the semi-infinite chain, a stationary distribution exists iff $\sum \rho_k < \infty$. Every birth-death quantity — stationary dist, hitting times, absorption probs — is explicit because the tridiagonal structure always reduces to a one-dimensional recurrence.
