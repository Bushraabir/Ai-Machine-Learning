> _"The Markov property is the art of forgetting strategically. The entire past is irrelevant — all that matters is where you are right now. That's not an approximation. For a Markov chain, it's exact."_

## The Big Picture

Most stochastic processes are intractable because the future depends on the entire history. To predict where you'll be tomorrow, you need to know everything that happened before today. The **Markov property** is the assumption that cuts this Gordian knot: the future depends on the past _only through the present state_.

This is both a modeling assumption and a structural property. When it holds, an enormous amount of theory becomes available — stationary distributions, convergence, hitting times, all of it. When it's a modeling assumption, it's often a simplification, but frequently a good one: if the state space is rich enough, the present state really does encode all the relevant history.

The two objects that define a (discrete, time-homogeneous) Markov chain are:

1. The **initial distribution** — where you start.
2. The **transition matrix** $Q$ — where you go from each state.

Everything else — $n$-step distributions, stationary distributions, long-run behavior — is derived from these two.

## Setup

Let $X_0, X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be a sequence of random variables taking values in a **state space** $\mathcal{S}$.

For now: $\mathcal{S}$ is finite or countably infinite, and time is discrete ($n = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$). This is the setting of Stat 110 and B&H Chapter 11.

---

## The Markov Property

### Definition

The sequence $(X_n)_{n \geq 0}$ is a **Markov chain** if for all $n \geq 0$ and all states $i_0, i_1, \ldots, i_{n-1}, i, j \in \mathcal{S}$:

$$P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i,, X_{n-1} = i_{n-1},, \ldots,, X_0 = i_0) = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i)$$

whenever the conditioning event has positive probability.

In words: **given the present, the future is independent of the past.**

### Time Homogeneity

The chain is **time-homogeneous** if $P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i)$ does not depend on $n$. We then write:

$$q_{ij} = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i)$$

and call $q_{ij}$ the **transition probability** from state $i$ to state $j$.

Almost everything in Stat 110 and B&H assumes time homogeneity. I'll assume it throughout.

### An Equivalent Formulation

The Markov property can be stated symmetrically: past and future are **conditionally independent given the present**:

$$X_0, \ldots, X_{n-1} \perp X_{n+1}, X_{n+2}, \ldots \mid X_n$$

This is the version that reveals the deep structure. The chain "screens off" its past from its future. Knowing $X_n$ makes the past irrelevant for predicting the future, and makes the future irrelevant for retrodicting the past.

> _The symmetry of this formulation surprised me when I first saw it. You might expect that "future is independent of past given present" is an asymmetric statement — but it's actually symmetric in past and future. Running a time-reversible Markov chain backwards gives another Markov chain._

## The Transition Matrix

### Definition

For a finite state space $\mathcal{S} = {1, 2, \ldots, M}$, the **transition matrix** is the $M \times M$ matrix:

$$Q = (q_{ij})_{i,j \in \mathcal{S}}, \qquad q_{ij} = P(X_{n+1} = j \mid X_n = i)$$

**Row $i$** of $Q$ is the conditional distribution of $X_{n+1}$ given $X_n = i$.

### Two Properties That Must Hold

**1. Non-negativity:** $$q_{ij} \geq 0 \quad \text{for all } i, j$$

**2. Row sums equal 1:** $$\sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} q_{ij} = 1 \quad \text{for all } i$$

because the conditional distribution of $X_{n+1}$ given $X_n = i$ must be a valid probability distribution. A matrix satisfying both properties is called a **stochastic matrix** (or row-stochastic matrix).

> _Columns do not need to sum to 1 in general. If both rows and columns sum to 1, the matrix is called doubly stochastic — that's a special property with important consequences (it implies the uniform distribution is stationary)._

### Example: Two-State Chain

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} 1-\alpha & \alpha \ \beta & 1-\beta \end{pmatrix}, \qquad \alpha, \beta \in (0,1)$$

From state 1: stay with probability $1-\alpha$, jump to state 2 with probability $\alpha$. From state 2: jump to state 1 with probability $\beta$, stay with probability $1-\beta$.

Check: each row sums to 1. ✓

### Example: Random Walk on ${0, 1, \ldots, M}$

$$q_{i,i+1} = p, \quad q_{i,i-1} = 1-p = q, \quad q_{i,j} = 0 \text{ otherwise}$$

with reflecting or absorbing boundaries at $0$ and $M$. The transition matrix is tridiagonal.

## The Joint Distribution of a Markov Chain

This is where the Markov property earns its keep. For any finite path $i_0, i_1, \ldots, i_n$:

$$P(X_0 = i_0, X_1 = i_1, \ldots, X_n = i_n) = P(X_0 = i_0)\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} q_{i_k, i_{k+1}}$$

**Proof:** Apply the chain rule of probability and then the Markov property repeatedly:

$$P(X_0 = i_0, \ldots, X_n = i_n)$$ $$= P(X_0 = i_0),P(X_1=i_1|X_0=i_0),P(X_2=i_2|X_1=i_1,X_0=i_0)\cdots$$ $$= P(X_0=i_0),q_{i_0 i_1},q_{i_1 i_2}\cdots q_{i_{n-1}i_n}$$

where each conditional simplifies to a single transition probability by the Markov property. $\blacksquare$

This factorization is the fundamental computational tool. The entire joint distribution of the chain is determined by the initial distribution and the transition matrix.

## The $n$-Step Transition Matrix

### Definition

The **$n$-step transition probability** is:

$$q_{ij}^{(n)} = P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i)$$

and the $n$-step transition matrix is $Q^{(n)} = (q_{ij}^{(n)})$.

### The Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations

$$q_{ij}^{(m+n)} = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}} q_{ik}^{(m)}, q_{kj}^{(n)}$$

In matrix form: $Q^{(m+n)} = Q^{(m)} Q^{(n)}$.

**Proof:** Condition on the intermediate state at time $m$:

$$P(X_{m+n} = j \mid X_0 = i) = \sum_k P(X_{m+n}=j \mid X_m=k),P(X_m=k \mid X_0=i) = \sum_k q_{kj}^{(n)},q_{ik}^{(m)}$$

This is the $(i,j)$ entry of $Q^{(m)} Q^{(n)}$. $\blacksquare$

### The Key Consequence: $Q^{(n)} = Q^n$

Setting $m = 1$ repeatedly: $Q^{(n)} = Q^n$.

**The $n$-step transition matrix is the $n$-th matrix power of $Q$.**

This is the central computational result of Markov chain theory. To find where the chain is after $n$ steps, raise $Q$ to the $n$-th power:

$$P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i) = (Q^n)_{ij}$$

> _This feels obvious once you know it, but it's genuinely powerful. It means all the spectral theory of matrices — eigenvalues, diagonalization, Jordan forms — applies to Markov chains. The long-run behavior of the chain is controlled by the eigenvalues of $Q$. That's a remarkable bridge between probability and linear algebra._

## The Distribution at Time $n$

Let $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$ be the **initial distribution**: a row vector where $t^{(0)}_i = P(X_0 = i)$.

Then the distribution at time $n$ is:

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$$

where $\mathbf{t}^{(n)}_j = P(X_n = j)$.

**Proof:** By the law of total probability:

$$P(X_n = j) = \sum_i P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i),P(X_0 = i) = \sum_i t^{(0)}_i,(Q^n)_{ij}$$

which is the $j$-th entry of the row vector $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$. $\blacksquare$

**One-step update:**

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n+1)} = \mathbf{t}^{(n)} Q$$

Row vectors left-multiply $Q$. This is a notational convention — some books use column vectors and right-multiply by $Q^\top$, but B&H and Stat 110 use row vectors.

## Stationary Distributions (Preview)

A distribution $\mathbf{s}$ (row vector) is **stationary** for $Q$ if:

$$\mathbf{s} Q = \mathbf{s}$$

i.e., $\mathbf{s}$ is a **left eigenvector** of $Q$ with eigenvalue 1.

Equivalently, if $X_0 \sim \mathbf{s}$, then $X_n \sim \mathbf{s}$ for all $n$ — the distribution doesn't change over time.

**Why eigenvalue 1 always exists:** Since $Q$ is row-stochastic, $Q\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$ (the all-ones vector is a right eigenvector with eigenvalue 1). By the left-right symmetry of eigenvalues, there must also be a left eigenvector with eigenvalue 1. Under mild conditions (irreducibility, aperiodicity), this stationary distribution is unique and the chain converges to it from any starting point.

The full theory of stationary distributions and convergence deserves its own note — but the transition matrix is the object that everything is defined in terms of.

## The Spectral Picture

For a finite state space, $Q$ is an $M \times M$ matrix with eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_M$.

**Facts about eigenvalues of a stochastic matrix:**

- $\lambda_1 = 1$ always (Perron-Frobenius; $Q\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$).
- All eigenvalues satisfy $|\lambda_k| \leq 1$ — they lie in the unit disk.
- For an irreducible aperiodic chain: $\lambda_1 = 1$ is the unique eigenvalue with $|\lambda| = 1$.

If $Q$ is diagonalizable with eigenvalues $1 = \lambda_1 > |\lambda_2| \geq \cdots \geq |\lambda_M|$:

$$Q^n = \sum_{k=1}^M \lambda_k^n \mathbf{u}_k \mathbf{v}_k^\top$$

where $\mathbf{u}_k, \mathbf{v}_k$ are right and left eigenvectors. As $n \to \infty$, all terms with $|\lambda_k| < 1$ vanish, leaving only the $k=1$ term:

$$Q^n \to \mathbf{1}\mathbf{s}$$

where $\mathbf{s}$ is the stationary distribution. The **spectral gap** $1 - |\lambda_2|$ controls the rate of convergence — a large gap means fast mixing.

> _This is the eigenvalue story of Markov chains. It's the rigorous content behind the intuition that "chains forget their initial state." The speed of forgetting is $|\lambda_2|^n$, and maximizing the spectral gap is a core problem in the theory of mixing times._

## Constructing the Transition Matrix: A Checklist

When modeling a system as a Markov chain, I ask:

1. **What is the state space $\mathcal{S}$?** Make it rich enough that the Markov property is plausible — if the future depends on history, encode the relevant history _into_ the state.
2. **What are the transition probabilities $q_{ij}$?** Specify how the system moves from each state.
3. **Verify row sums equal 1.** Every row must define a valid probability distribution.
4. **What is the initial distribution?** Where does the chain start?

The Markov property is a modeling choice. If I define my state space to include, say, both current position and the last $k$ positions, the resulting process is still Markov — it just has a larger state space. The tradeoff is always state-space size vs. Markovian validity.

## Examples Worth Knowing Cold

### Gambler's Ruin

States ${0, 1, \ldots, N}$. From state $i \in {1,\ldots,N-1}$: go to $i+1$ with probability $p$, go to $i-1$ with probability $1-p$. States $0$ and $N$ are absorbing: $q_{00} = q_{NN} = 1$.

The transition matrix is tridiagonal with absorbing boundaries. The gambler's ruin probabilities are derived from this chain.

### Ehrenfest Urn Model

$M$ balls distributed between two urns, total $d$ balls. State = number in urn 1. From state $i$: a ball chosen uniformly at random is moved to the other urn — so go to $i+1$ with probability $(d-i)/d$ and to $i-1$ with probability $i/d$.

This chain is reversible and has a Binomial stationary distribution. It models diffusion and approaches equilibrium — a classic example of a physically motivated Markov chain.

### PageRank (Google)

The web is a directed graph. State = webpage. At each step, follow a random outgoing link with probability $\alpha$, or jump to a uniformly random page with probability $1-\alpha$ (the "teleportation" term). The stationary distribution of this Markov chain is the PageRank vector.

> _I keep this example in mind because it shows the transition matrix doesn't have to come from a physical process — it can be engineered. PageRank is the stationary distribution of a deliberately constructed Markov chain on a 50-billion-state space._

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> A Markov chain is defined by its transition matrix $Q$ (row-stochastic, entries $q_{ij} = P(\text{go to }j \mid \text{at }i)$) and initial distribution $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$. The Markov property says future $\perp$ past $\mid$ present. The $n$-step distribution is $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}Q^n$ — raise $Q$ to the $n$-th power. The eigenvalues of $Q$ live in the unit disk; the spectral gap controls how fast the chain forgets where it started.
