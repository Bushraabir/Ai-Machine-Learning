> _"Detailed balance is the condition that says the chain looks the same run forwards and backwards. Global balance says inflow equals outflow at every state. Detailed balance says inflow equals outflow on every directed edge. The second implies the first, strictly. The gap between them is where irreversible chains live — and irreversibility turns out to be physically meaningful, not just a mathematical curiosity."_

## Motivation: Two Notions of Equilibrium

From [[Stationary Distributions]], a distribution $\mathbf{s}$ is stationary if $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$, which entrywise says:

$$\sum_i s_i q_{ij} = s_j \quad \text{for all } j$$

Rewrite this as:

$$\sum_{i \neq j} s_i q_{ij} = s_j \sum_{k \neq j} q_{jk}$$

**Left side:** total probability flux flowing _into_ state $j$ from all other states.  
**Right side:** total probability flux flowing _out_ of state $j$ to all other states.

This is **global balance**: at stationarity, inflow = outflow at every state. It's a conservation law — the probability mass sitting at $j$ doesn't change over time because what comes in equals what goes out.

Now ask a finer question: can I require balance _edge by edge_, not just _globally per state_? That is:

$$s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji} \quad \text{for all pairs } i, j$$

This is **detailed balance** — and it is strictly stronger. A chain satisfying detailed balance is called **reversible**.

This note is about the relationship between these two conditions, why reversibility is the right framework for thinking about time-reversal, and how to use detailed balance as a computational tool.

## Detailed Balance

### Definition

A probability distribution $\mathbf{s}$ satisfies **detailed balance** with respect to transition matrix $Q$ if:

$$s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji} \quad \text{for all } i, j \in \mathcal{S}$$

The quantity $s_i q_{ij}$ is the probability flux from $i$ to $j$ at stationarity: the probability of being in state $i$ (which is $s_i$) times the probability of jumping to $j$ given you're in $i$ (which is $q_{ij}$). Detailed balance says this flux is symmetric — whatever flows from $i$ to $j$ flows equally from $j$ to $i$.

### Theorem: Detailed Balance Implies Stationarity

If $\mathbf{s}$ satisfies detailed balance with respect to $Q$, then $\mathbf{s}$ is stationary: $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$.

**Proof.** Fix any state $j$. Sum the detailed balance condition over all $i$:

$$\sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} s_i q_{ij} = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} s_j q_{ji} = s_j \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} q_{ji} = s_j \cdot 1 = s_j$$

The left side is $(\mathbf{s}Q)_j$. So $(\mathbf{s}Q)_j = s_j$ for all $j$, i.e., $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$. $\blacksquare$

> _The proof is one line. Detailed balance is the local condition; summing it over all $i$ gives the global condition for free. This is why detailed balance is so useful as a computational tool — if I can verify the local pairwise condition, I get stationarity for free, without solving the full balance equations $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$ as a linear system._

### The Converse Is False

A chain can be stationary without satisfying detailed balance. The canonical counterexample:

**Three-state cycle:** $\mathcal{S} = {1, 2, 3}$ with $q_{12} = q_{23} = q_{31} = 1$ and all other $q_{ij} = 0$.

The stationary distribution is $\mathbf{s} = (1/3, 1/3, 1/3)$ (by symmetry, or solve $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$). Check detailed balance for the pair $(1,2)$:

$$s_1 q_{12} = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 1 = \frac{1}{3}, \qquad s_2 q_{21} = \frac{1}{3} \cdot 0 = 0$$

These are not equal. So detailed balance fails — this is an irreversible chain. Probability flows around the cycle in one direction only; the time-reversed chain would flow in the opposite direction, and the two are distinguishable.

## Reversibility

### The Time-Reversal of a Chain

Let $(X_n)_{0 \leq n \leq T}$ be a Markov chain run for $T$ steps in stationarity (i.e., $X_0 \sim \mathbf{s}$ where $\mathbf{s}$ is stationary). Define the **time-reversed process**:

$$Y_n = X_{T-n}, \quad 0 \leq n \leq T$$

**Claim:** $(Y_n)$ is also a Markov chain, with transition matrix:

$$\tilde{q}_{ij} = \frac{s_j q_{ji}}{s_i}$$

**Proof.** First, verify the Markov property for $(Y_n)$. Given $Y_n = X_{T-n} = i$ and the past $Y_0, \ldots, Y_{n-1}$ (which is the future $X_{T}, \ldots, X_{T-n+1}$), the future of $Y$ is $Y_{n+1} = X_{T-n-1}$, which depends only on $X_{T-n} = i$ by the Markov property of $(X_n)$.

Now compute the transition probability:

$$P(Y_{n+1} = j \mid Y_n = i) = P(X_{T-n-1} = j \mid X_{T-n} = i)$$

By Bayes' theorem:

$$= \frac{P(X_{T-n} = i \mid X_{T-n-1} = j) \cdot P(X_{T-n-1} = j)}{P(X_{T-n} = i)}$$

Since the chain is in stationarity, $P(X_k = \ell) = s_\ell$ for all $k, \ell$:

$$= \frac{q_{ji} \cdot s_j}{s_i} = \tilde{q}_{ij}$$

$\blacksquare$

The reversed chain has transition matrix $\tilde{Q}$ with entries $\tilde{q}_{ij} = s_j q_{ji} / s_i$.

### Definition: Reversibility

A Markov chain $(X_n)$ in stationarity is **reversible** if the time-reversed chain has the same distribution as the forward chain. That is, for all $n$ and all paths $i_0, i_1, \ldots, i_n$:

$$P(X_0 = i_0, X_1 = i_1, \ldots, X_n = i_n) = P(X_0 = i_n, X_1 = i_{n-1}, \ldots, X_n = i_0)$$

In other words: you cannot tell whether the chain is running forwards or backwards by observing its distribution.

### Theorem: Reversibility $\Leftrightarrow$ Detailed Balance

A chain with stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}$ is reversible if and only if it satisfies detailed balance: $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$ for all $i, j$.

**Proof.**

($\Rightarrow$) Suppose the chain is reversible. Then $\tilde{Q} = Q$ (the reversed chain has the same transitions). So $\tilde{q}_{ij} = q_{ij}$, i.e., $s_j q_{ji} / s_i = q_{ij}$, i.e., $s_j q_{ji} = s_i q_{ij}$. This is detailed balance.

($\Leftarrow$) Suppose detailed balance holds. Then $\tilde{q}_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}/s_i = q_{ij}$ — the reversed chain has the same transition matrix as the forward chain. The initial distribution of $(Y_n)$ at time 0 is $Y_0 = X_T \sim \mathbf{s}$ (stationarity). So $(Y_n)$ and $(X_n)$ are both Markov chains with transition matrix $Q$ started from $\mathbf{s}$ — they have the same distribution. $\blacksquare$

> _This theorem is the reason "detailed balance" and "reversibility" are used interchangeably. They're the same condition, just stated in two different registers: detailed balance is the algebraic condition $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$, and reversibility is the probabilistic condition that the chain is time-symmetric. Proving they're equivalent is the content of the theorem._

## Using Detailed Balance to Find Stationary Distributions

This is the main practical payoff. Instead of solving the linear system $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$, $\sum_i s_i = 1$ — which for large state spaces is a painful system — I can:

1. Guess a candidate $\mathbf{s}$.
2. Check $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$ for all pairs $(i,j)$ with $q_{ij} > 0$.
3. Normalize.

If detailed balance holds, $\mathbf{s}$ is stationary. This avoids solving the full system.

### Why This Is Easier

The global balance equation $\sum_i s_i q_{ij} = s_j$ couples all states simultaneously — it's a system of $|\mathcal{S}|$ equations. Detailed balance gives $|\mathcal{S}|^2/2$ pairwise equations, each involving only two states. For chains with special structure (birth-death, graphs with nice symmetry), these pairwise equations decouple and can be solved by telescoping.

## The Birth-Death Chain: Canonical Example

A birth-death chain on ${0, 1, \ldots, M}$ has transitions only to adjacent states:

$$q_{k, k+1} = p_k, \quad q_{k, k-1} = r_k, \quad q_{kk} = 1 - p_k - r_k$$

with $p_M = r_0 = 0$ (reflecting boundaries).

**Claim:** Every birth-death chain satisfies detailed balance with respect to some distribution.

**Proof.** Detailed balance for adjacent pair $(k, k+1)$:

$$s_k q_{k,k+1} = s_{k+1} q_{k+1,k}$$

$$s_k p_k = s_{k+1} r_{k+1}$$

$$s_{k+1} = s_k \cdot \frac{p_k}{r_{k+1}}$$

For non-adjacent pairs $(i, j)$ with $|i - j| \geq 2$: $q_{ij} = q_{ji} = 0$, so $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji} = 0$ trivially. Detailed balance holds.

**Telescoping to find $\mathbf{s}$:**

$$s_k = s_0 \cdot \frac{p_0 p_1 \cdots p_{k-1}}{r_1 r_2 \cdots r_k}$$

Define $\rho_k = \frac{p_0 p_1 \cdots p_{k-1}}{r_1 r_2 \cdots r_k}$ (with $\rho_0 = 1$). Then $s_k = s_0 \rho_k$, and normalization gives:

$$s_0 = \frac{1}{\sum_{k=0}^M \rho_k}, \qquad s_k = \frac{\rho_k}{\sum_{\ell=0}^M \rho_\ell}$$

> _Birth-death chains are always reversible. The intuition: transitions only go left or right, one step at a time. The only way to get from $j$ to $i$ is to pass through every state in between — and the same is true in reverse. There's no "directionality" to exploit; every path from $i$ to $j$ has a mirror path from $j$ to $i$. The topology forces reversibility._

### Concrete Example: Ehrenfest Urn

$M$ balls distributed between two urns, total $d$ balls. State = number in urn 1. Transitions: pick a ball uniformly at random, move it to the other urn.

$$p_k = \frac{d-k}{d}, \quad r_k = \frac{k}{d}$$

The ratios:

$$\frac{p_{k-1}}{r_k} = \frac{(d-k+1)/d}{k/d} = \frac{d-k+1}{k}$$

So:

$$\rho_k = \prod_{j=1}^k \frac{d-j+1}{j} = \binom{d}{k}$$

The stationary distribution is:

$$s_k = \frac{\binom{d}{k}}{\sum_{\ell=0}^d \binom{d}{\ell}} = \frac{\binom{d}{k}}{2^d}$$

which is $\text{Binomial}(d, 1/2)$. At equilibrium, balls are distributed binomially — exactly the maximum-entropy distribution over ${0, \ldots, d}$ with mean $d/2$.

This makes physical sense: the Ehrenfest urn models diffusion. At equilibrium, the distribution of particles between two sides of a container is Binomial. Reversibility is the probabilistic statement of **microscopic reversibility** in physics.

## A Non-Example: The Three-State Cycle

Return to the three-state cycle $q_{12} = q_{23} = q_{31} = 1$. I showed detailed balance fails. What is the reversed chain?

$$\tilde{q}_{ij} = \frac{s_j q_{ji}}{s_i} = \frac{(1/3) q_{ji}}{1/3} = q_{ji}$$

So $\tilde{Q} = Q^\top$, i.e., the reversed chain is the cycle in the opposite direction: $q_{21} = q_{32} = q_{13} = 1$. The forward chain cycles $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 1 \to \cdots$ and the reversed chain cycles $1 \to 3 \to 2 \to 1 \to \cdots$. These are distinguishable — the chain is not reversible.

This is the archetypal **irreversible** chain. In physics, irreversibility corresponds to entropy production — systems that run their dynamics in a direction determined by the arrow of time. The three-state cycle is the simplest example.

## Kolmogorov's Criterion

For a general (not necessarily birth-death) chain, there is a clean necessary and sufficient condition for reversibility that doesn't require knowing $\mathbf{s}$ in advance.

### Theorem (Kolmogorov's Cycle Criterion)

An irreducible Markov chain is reversible (with respect to its unique stationary distribution) if and only if for every cycle $i_0 \to i_1 \to \cdots \to i_n \to i_0$ in the state space:

$$q_{i_0 i_1} q_{i_1 i_2} \cdots q_{i_{n-1} i_n} q_{i_n i_0} = q_{i_0 i_n} q_{i_n i_{n-1}} \cdots q_{i_1 i_0}$$

The probability of traversing the cycle in one direction equals the probability of traversing it in the opposite direction.

**Proof sketch ($\Rightarrow$):** If detailed balance holds, multiply the detailed balance equations around the cycle:

$$\prod_{k=0}^{n-1} s_{i_k} q_{i_k i_{k+1}} = \prod_{k=0}^{n-1} s_{i_{k+1}} q_{i_{k+1} i_k}$$

The left product is $\prod_k s_{i_k} \cdot \prod_k q_{i_k i_{k+1}}$, and similarly for the right. The $s$ factors are the same on both sides (since the $s_{i_k}$ appear once each on each side around the cycle). Cancel them to get the cycle condition. $\blacksquare$

**Why this matters:** Kolmogorov's criterion is checkable from $Q$ alone, without knowing $\mathbf{s}$. For complicated state spaces, checking all cycles is hard — but for small graphs it's immediate.

For the three-state cycle: the only non-trivial cycle is $1 \to 2 \to 3 \to 1$ (and its reverse). Forward product: $q_{12} q_{23} q_{31} = 1 \cdot 1 \cdot 1 = 1$. Reverse product: $q_{13} q_{32} q_{21} = 0 \cdot 0 \cdot 0 = 0$. Not equal. Not reversible. ✓

## The Reversed Chain Has the Same Stationary Distribution

**Claim:** The reversed chain $\tilde{Q}$ has the same stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}$ as the forward chain $Q$.

**Proof.** Check $\mathbf{s}\tilde{Q} = \mathbf{s}$:

$$(\mathbf{s}\tilde{Q})_j = \sum_i s_i \tilde{q}_{ij} = \sum_i s_i \frac{s_j q_{ji}}{s_i} = s_j \sum_i q_{ji} = s_j \cdot 1 = s_j$$

$\blacksquare$

So reversibility is a property of a chain relative to its own stationary distribution. The forward and reversed chains share the same $\mathbf{s}$, even when $Q \neq \tilde{Q}$ (i.e., even when the chain is irreversible).

## Reversibility in MCMC: Why It Matters Computationally

The most important application of reversibility in modern probability is **Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC)**. The goal: sample from a target distribution $\pi$ that I can evaluate (up to a normalizing constant) but cannot sample from directly.

**The strategy:** Construct a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is $\pi$, run it until it mixes, and treat the samples as approximate draws from $\pi$.

**The problem:** How do I construct such a chain? The detailed balance condition gives a sufficient condition that's easy to engineer:

$$\pi_i q_{ij} = \pi_j q_{ji} \quad \text{for all } i, j$$

If I design $Q$ to satisfy detailed balance with respect to $\pi$, then $\pi$ is automatically stationary. This is how the **Metropolis-Hastings algorithm** works.

### Metropolis-Hastings at a Glance

Given target $\pi$ and a proposal kernel $r(i, j)$ (symmetric for simplicity: $r(i,j) = r(j,i)$):

1. At state $i$, propose moving to $j$ with probability $r(i,j)$.
2. Accept the proposal with probability $\min\left(1, \frac{\pi_j}{\pi_i}\right)$.
3. If rejected, stay at $i$.

The resulting transition probabilities:

$$q_{ij} = r(i,j) \cdot \min!\left(1, \frac{\pi_j}{\pi_i}\right) \quad (i \neq j)$$

**Verify detailed balance:**

$$\pi_i q_{ij} = \pi_i \cdot r(i,j) \cdot \min!\left(1, \frac{\pi_j}{\pi_i}\right)$$

Case $\pi_j \leq \pi_i$: $\min(1, \pi_j/\pi_i) = \pi_j/\pi_i$, so $\pi_i q_{ij} = r(i,j)\pi_j$.  
And: $\pi_j q_{ji} = \pi_j \cdot r(j,i) \cdot \min(1, \pi_i/\pi_j) = \pi_j \cdot r(i,j) \cdot 1 = \pi_j r(i,j)$. Equal. ✓

Case $\pi_j > \pi_i$: symmetric argument. ✓

So Metropolis-Hastings is designed to satisfy detailed balance with respect to $\pi$ — by construction. I never need to know the normalizing constant of $\pi$ (it cancels in the ratio $\pi_j/\pi_i$). The algorithm samples from distributions I can only evaluate up to a constant. This is the miracle of MCMC, and detailed balance is the mathematical mechanism behind it.

> _This is the reason physicists care about detailed balance. In statistical mechanics, the Boltzmann distribution $\pi_i \propto e^{-E_i/kT}$ (where $E_i$ is the energy of state $i$) is the equilibrium distribution of a physical system at temperature $T$. To sample from it computationally, you run Metropolis-Hastings, which satisfies detailed balance with respect to $\pi$ — the same condition that holds for physical systems in thermal equilibrium. The algorithm is simulating thermodynamic equilibrium._

## Summary

|Concept|Definition|Key property|
|---|---|---|
|Global balance|$\sum_i s_i q_{ij} = s_j$ for all $j$|Necessary and sufficient for stationarity|
|Detailed balance|$s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$ for all $i,j$|Sufficient for stationarity; strictly stronger|
|Reversibility|$(X_n) \overset{d}{=} (X_{T-n})$ when started in $\mathbf{s}$|Equivalent to detailed balance|
|Reversed chain|$\tilde{q}_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}/s_i$|Always has stationary dist $\mathbf{s}$; equals $Q$ iff reversible|
|Birth-death chains|Transitions only to adjacent states|Always reversible; $s_k \propto \prod p_j/r_j$|
|Kolmogorov criterion|Product of $q$'s around any cycle = product in reverse|Checkable from $Q$ alone, no need to know $\mathbf{s}$|
|Metropolis-Hastings|MCMC algorithm built on detailed balance|Samples from $\pi$ using only ratios $\pi_j/\pi_i$|

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> Detailed balance $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$ is the pairwise version of stationarity — it implies $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$ immediately by summing over $i$. A chain satisfying it is reversible: the time-reversed chain $\tilde{q}_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}/s_i$ equals the forward chain. Birth-death chains are always reversible (telescope the pairwise equations). Kolmogorov's criterion detects reversibility from $Q$ alone by checking whether cycle products are direction-symmetric. MCMC (Metropolis-Hastings) is engineered to satisfy detailed balance with respect to the target distribution $\pi$ — that's the entire design principle.
