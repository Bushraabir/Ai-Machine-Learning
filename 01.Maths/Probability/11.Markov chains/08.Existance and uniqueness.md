

> _"Existence says there is somewhere for the chain to settle. Uniqueness says it can only settle in one place. Both feel obvious for well-behaved chains — and both fail in precisely the ways you'd expect when the chain misbehaves. The proofs make the intuition rigorous, and the counterexamples sharpen it."_

---

## The Question

From [[Stationary Distributions]], I know what a stationary distribution is: a row vector $\mathbf{s}$ with $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$, $s_i \geq 0$, $\sum_i s_i = 1$. The natural questions are:

1. **Does one exist?** Is the system $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$, $\sum_i s_i = 1$ always solvable?
2. **Is it unique?** Could there be multiple solutions?

The answer to both, under the right conditions, is yes — and those conditions are exactly **irreducibility** and **positive recurrence**. This note is the full proof.

---

## The Main Theorem

**Theorem (Existence and Uniqueness).** Let $(X_n)$ be a Markov chain on a countable state space $\mathcal{S}$ with transition matrix $Q$.

1. **(Existence)** A stationary distribution exists if and only if the chain has at least one positive recurrent state.
    
2. **(Uniqueness)** If the chain is **irreducible** and **positive recurrent**, then the stationary distribution is **unique**, and is given by:
    

$$s_i = \frac{1}{m_i}, \qquad m_i = E[T_i \mid X_0 = i]$$

where $T_i = \min{n \geq 1 : X_n = i}$ is the first return time to $i$.

3. **(Finite chains)** If $\mathcal{S}$ is **finite** and the chain is **irreducible**, then all states are automatically positive recurrent, so a unique stationary distribution exists.

---

## Part I: Existence

### The Construction

Fix any positive recurrent state $i \in \mathcal{S}$. Since $i$ is positive recurrent, $m_i = E[T_i \mid X_0 = i] < \infty$.

Define, for each $j \in \mathcal{S}$:

$$\pi_j = E!\left[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i - 1} \mathbf{1}[X_n = j] ,\Bigg|, X_0 = i\right]$$

This is the **expected number of visits to state $j$ in one excursion from $i$** — counting time steps $0, 1, \ldots, T_i - 1$, i.e., starting at $i$ and running until just before the first return to $i$.

**Step 1: $\boldsymbol{\pi}$ sums to $m_i$.**

$$\sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} \pi_j = E!\left[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i - 1} \sum_{j} \mathbf{1}[X_n = j],\Bigg|, X_0 = i\right] = E!\left[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i - 1} 1 ,\Bigg|, X_0 = i\right] = E[T_i \mid X_0 = i] = m_i$$

So define $\mathbf{s} := \boldsymbol{\pi} / m_i$. This is a probability vector: $s_j \geq 0$ and $\sum_j s_j = 1$.

**Step 2: $\mathbf{s}$ satisfies $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$.**

I need to show $\sum_j \pi_j q_{jk} = \pi_k$ for all $k$. Compute:

$$\sum_{j} \pi_j q_{jk} = \sum_j q_{jk} , E!\left[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i-1} \mathbf{1}[X_n = j],\Bigg|,X_0 = i\right]$$

Swap expectation and sum (Fubini, since everything is non-negative):

$$= E!\left[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i - 1} \sum_j q_{jk}, \mathbf{1}[X_n = j],\Bigg|,X_0=i\right] = E!\left[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i-1} P(X_{n+1}=k\mid X_n),\Bigg|,X_0=i\right]$$

$$= E!\left[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i-1} \mathbf{1}[X_{n+1}=k],\Bigg|,X_0=i\right] = E!\left[\sum_{n=1}^{T_i} \mathbf{1}[X_n=k],\Bigg|,X_0=i\right]$$

Now compare with $\pi_k = E\bigl[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i-1} \mathbf{1}[X_n=k] \mid X_0=i\bigr]$. The difference is:

$$\sum_j \pi_j q_{jk} - \pi_k = E!\left[\mathbf{1}[X_{T_i}=k] - \mathbf{1}[X_0=k],\Bigg|,X_0=i\right]$$

Since $X_0 = i$ and $X_{T_i} = i$, both indicator functions equal $\mathbf{1}[k=i]$. They cancel:

$$\sum_j \pi_j q_{jk} - \pi_k = \mathbf{1}[k=i] - \mathbf{1}[k=i] = 0$$

So $\boldsymbol{\pi} Q = \boldsymbol{\pi}$, and dividing by $m_i$: $\mathbf{s}Q = \mathbf{s}$. $\blacksquare$

> _The index-shift $\sum_{n=0}^{T_i-1} \to \sum_{n=1}^{T_i}$ is the entire proof. It introduces a mismatch of exactly one step at each boundary: the $n=0$ term (which contributes $\mathbf{1}[X_0 = k]$) and the $n = T_i$ term (which contributes $\mathbf{1}[X_{T_i} = k]$). Since $X_0 = X_{T_i} = i$, these cancel perfectly. The Markov chain literally proves its own stationarity by the fact that it returns to where it started._

### When Existence Fails

**Transient chains.** Every state is visited only finitely often almost surely. The chain drifts to infinity — no stable distribution to settle into. No stationary distribution exists.

**Null recurrent chains.** The chain returns to every state almost surely ($f_{ii} = 1$), but $m_i = \infty$ for all $i$, forcing $s_i = 1/m_i = 0$ for all $i$. That's not a probability distribution. No stationary distribution exists.

**Canonical example:** Simple symmetric random walk on $\mathbb{Z}$. Irreducible and null recurrent. If a stationary distribution existed, by symmetry it would assign equal weight to all integers — impossible for a probability measure. No stationary distribution.

## Part II: Uniqueness

### Theorem

If the chain is **irreducible** and **positive recurrent**, the stationary distribution constructed above is the **only** one.

### Proof

Let $\mathbf{s}$ be the stationary distribution from Part I, and let $\mathbf{s}'$ be any stationary distribution. I will show $s'_j = s_j$ for all $j$.

**Step 1.** Since $\mathbf{s}'Q = \mathbf{s}'$, we have $\mathbf{s}'Q^n = \mathbf{s}'$ for all $n \geq 0$:

$$s'_j = (\mathbf{s}'Q^n)_j = \sum_k s'_k (Q^n)_{kj} \qquad \text{for all } n$$

**Step 2.** Average this over $n = 0, 1, \ldots, N-1$:

$$s'_j = \frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \sum_k s'_k (Q^n)_{kj} = \sum_k s'_k \left(\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} (Q^n)_{kj}\right)$$

**Step 3.** By the **ergodic theorem for Markov chains** (which follows from positive recurrence and irreducibility):

$$\frac{1}{N} \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} (Q^n)_{kj} \longrightarrow s_j \qquad \text{as } N \to \infty, \text{ for all } k$$

This is the Cesàro convergence of time averages to the stationary distribution — it holds for all irreducible positive recurrent chains, including periodic ones.

**Step 4.** Since $\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}(Q^n)_{kj} \leq 1$ uniformly, the dominated convergence theorem (or just bounded convergence for finite sums) allows passing the limit inside:

$$s'_j = \sum_k s'_k \cdot \lim_{N\to\infty} \left(\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1}(Q^n)_{kj}\right) = \sum_k s'_k \cdot s_j = s_j \underbrace{\sum_k s'_k}_{=1} = s_j$$

So $\mathbf{s}' = \mathbf{s}$. $\blacksquare$

> _The Cesàro average is the key technique, and it's worth understanding why it's needed. For a periodic chain, $(Q^n)_{kj}$ oscillates — it might be 0 for all odd $n$ and positive for all even $n$. Pointwise limits don't exist. But Cesàro averages smooth out the oscillation: if a sequence oscillates between 0 and $2s_j$, its averages converge to $s_j$. Uniqueness holds even for periodic chains; only distributional convergence ($Q^n \to \mathbf{1s}$, not just in Cesàro sense) requires aperiodicity._

### Why Irreducibility Is Needed for Uniqueness

Without irreducibility, there can be multiple closed communicating classes $C_1, C_2, \ldots$ Each closed class $C_\ell$ is itself a finite irreducible chain, so it has a unique stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}^{(\ell)}$ supported on $C_\ell$. Every convex combination:

$$\alpha_1 \mathbf{s}^{(1)} + \alpha_2 \mathbf{s}^{(2)} + \cdots, \qquad \alpha_\ell \geq 0, \quad \sum_\ell \alpha_\ell = 1$$

is also stationary. As soon as there are two or more closed classes, there are uncountably many stationary distributions. Irreducibility — one class — is exactly what collapses this to uniqueness.

## Part III: Finite Irreducible $\Rightarrow$ Positive Recurrent

This is what makes finite chains so tractable: I never have to check positive recurrence separately.

### Theorem

If $\mathcal{S}$ is **finite** and the chain is **irreducible**, then every state is **positive recurrent**.

### Proof in Two Steps

**Step 1: All states are recurrent (not transient).**

Suppose for contradiction that all states are transient. Then for each $j$:

$$E[N_j \mid X_0 = i] = \sum_{n=1}^\infty (Q^n)_{ij} < \infty$$

Sum over all states $j \in \mathcal{S}$:

$$\sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} E[N_j \mid X_0 = i] = E!\left[\sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} N_j,\Bigg|,X_0 = i\right] = E[\text{total steps taken} \mid X_0 = i] = \infty$$

But with $|\mathcal{S}| = M < \infty$:

$$\sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} E[N_j \mid X_0 = i] = \sum_{j} \sum_{n=1}^\infty (Q^n)_{ij} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \underbrace{\sum_j (Q^n)_{ij}}_{= 1} = \infty$$

Wait — this gives $\infty = \infty$, not a contradiction. Let me be more careful. The contradiction is:

If all states are transient, each $\sum_n (Q^n)_{ij} < \infty$, so:

$$\sum_j \sum_n (Q^n)_{ij} = \sum_n \underbrace{\sum_j (Q^n)_{ij}}_{=1} = \infty$$

but also $= \sum_j E[N_j] < \infty \cdot M < \infty$. **Contradiction.**

The finite sum of finite terms $\sum_j E[N_j]$ is finite (since $M < \infty$ and each term is finite), but we just showed it equals $\infty$. So not all states can be transient. By the class property of recurrence (irreducible chain: all same class), all states are recurrent.

**Step 2: All states are positive recurrent (not null recurrent).**

Suppose state $i$ is null recurrent: $m_i = E[T_i \mid X_0 = i] = \infty$.

From the excursion construction: $m_i = \sum_{j \in \mathcal{S}} \pi_j$.

With $|\mathcal{S}| = M$, this is a sum of $M$ terms. So some $\pi_j = \infty$.

But $\pi_j = E[#\text{visits to }j\text{ before returning to }i]$. In a finite irreducible chain, from any state, every other state is reachable in at most $M-1$ steps. Between two visits to $i$, the chain visits at most... well, it could visit $j$ many times. But:

By the strong Markov property, each time the chain visits $j$ (during an excursion from $i$), it independently has some probability $P(T_i < T_j^+ \mid X_0 = j) > 0$ of returning to $i$ before revisiting $j$ (positive by irreducibility, finiteness). So the number of visits to $j$ per excursion is geometric with finite mean. So $\pi_j < \infty$ for all $j$.

Therefore $m_i = \sum_j \pi_j < M \cdot \max_j \pi_j < \infty$. Contradiction with null recurrence. So all states are positive recurrent. $\blacksquare$

> _Step 1 is the cleaner argument. The key is that a finite sum of finite numbers is finite, but the row sums of $Q^n$ are all 1, so summing over $n$ gives infinity — forcing at least one $E[N_j]$ to be infinite, hence at least one state recurrent. Step 2 is subtler: I use the geometric structure of visits (strong Markov property at each return to $j$) to show expected visits per excursion are finite. Together they nail down positive recurrence._

---

## The Formula $s_i = 1/m_i$: Proof

From the construction: $s_i = \pi_i / m_i$.

I claim $\pi_i = 1$. By definition, $\pi_i = E[\sum_{n=0}^{T_i-1} \mathbf{1}[X_n = i] \mid X_0 = i]$. The sum counts visits to $i$ at times $n = 0, 1, \ldots, T_i - 1$. At time 0, $X_0 = i$ — that's one visit. For $1 \leq n \leq T_i - 1$, by definition $T_i$ is the **first** return time, so $X_n \neq i$ for $n = 1, \ldots, T_i - 1$. Hence the sum equals 1 almost surely.

So $\pi_i = 1$, and:

$$\boxed{s_i = \frac{1}{m_i} = \frac{1}{E[T_i \mid X_0 = i]}}$$

$\blacksquare$

## Summary of the Three Parts

|Part|Theorem|Key technique|
|---|---|---|
|Existence|$\mathbf{s} = \boldsymbol{\pi}/m_i$ is stationary|Index shift: $\sum_{n=0}^{T_i-1} \to \sum_{n=1}^{T_i}$; boundary terms cancel|
|Uniqueness|Any other $\mathbf{s}'$ equals $\mathbf{s}$|Cesàro averaging of $\mathbf{s}'Q^n = \mathbf{s}'$; ergodic convergence|
|Finite $\Rightarrow$ pos. recurrent|Irreducible finite chain is always pos. recurrent|Pigeonhole: transience contradicts row sums of $Q^n$ being 1|

And the corollary tying everything together:

$$\text{Finite} + \text{Irreducible} \implies \text{Positive recurrent} \implies \text{Unique stationary distribution} \mathbf{s}, \text{ with } s_i = 1/m_i$$

---

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> Existence: construct $s_j = \pi_j/m_i$ from excursion counts and show $\boldsymbol{\pi}Q = \boldsymbol{\pi}$ by an index shift that cancels boundary terms at $X_0 = X_{T_i} = i$. Uniqueness: Cesàro-average $\mathbf{s}'Q^n = \mathbf{s}'$ and use ergodic convergence to force $\mathbf{s}' = \mathbf{s}$. Finite irreducible chains are always positive recurrent: transience would make $\sum_j E[N_j]$ simultaneously finite (M finite terms) and infinite (row sums of $Q^n$ are 1), a contradiction.
