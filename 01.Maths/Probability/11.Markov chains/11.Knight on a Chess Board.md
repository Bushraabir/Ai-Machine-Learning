> _"The knight is the strangest piece on the board — it's the only one that can jump over other pieces, the only one whose reachable squares aren't in a straight line, the only one that alternates color every move. That last fact is the mathematical heart of this problem. The chessboard is a bipartite graph, and bipartiteness is exactly periodicity. The knight has period 2."_

---

## The Setup

A knight starts on a square of an $8 \times 8$ chessboard and moves according to the standard chess rules: two squares in one direction, one square perpendicular. At each step, it picks a legal move **uniformly at random** from all available moves.

This is a Markov chain. The state space is $\mathcal{S} = {(r,c) : 1 \leq r, c \leq 8}$, with $|\mathcal{S}| = 64$ states.

The transition matrix $Q$ has:

$$q_{(r,c),(r',c')} = \frac{1}{\deg(r,c)} \cdot \mathbf{1}[(r',c') \text{ is a legal knight move from } (r,c)]$$

where $\deg(r,c)$ is the number of legal knight moves from square $(r,c)$.

This note asks: what is the stationary distribution? Is the chain reversible? Does it converge, and to what?

---

## The Degree of a Square

The key object is $\deg(r,c)$ — how many knight moves are available from $(r,c)$.

A knight at $(r,c)$ can move to $(r \pm 1, c \pm 2)$ and $(r \pm 2, c \pm 1)$ — eight potential targets. Squares near the boundary have fewer legal moves because some targets fall off the board.

By direct enumeration (or symmetry):

|Location|Degree|
|---|---|
|Corner squares (e.g., a1)|2|
|Edge-adjacent-to-corner (e.g., b1, a2)|3|
|Edge squares, general|4|
|One step from corner (e.g., b2)|4|
|Near-edge interior|6|
|Central squares (e.g., d4, d5, e4, e5)|8|

The four corner squares have degree 2 (the most restricted). The 16 squares around the center of the board have degree 8. Degree varies from 2 to 8.

> _This variation in degree is why the stationary distribution is not uniform. The random knight spends more time on high-degree squares — it gets "pulled toward" the center because there are more ways to arrive there. Corners are sparsely visited: hard to reach, and easy to leave (only 2 exits each)._

## The Stationary Distribution

### Finding $\mathbf{s}$ via Detailed Balance

The graph structure of knight moves is **undirected**: if $(r',c')$ is a legal move from $(r,c)$, then $(r,c)$ is a legal move from $(r',c')$. The adjacency is symmetric.

For an undirected graph with adjacency matrix $A$, define the **random walk** on the graph: from node $i$, jump to a uniformly random neighbor. The transition matrix is:

$$q_{ij} = \frac{A_{ij}}{\deg(i)}$$

**Claim:** The stationary distribution of this random walk is proportional to degree:

$$s_i = \frac{\deg(i)}{\sum_{j} \deg(j)} = \frac{\deg(i)}{2|E|}$$

where $|E|$ is the number of edges.

**Proof via detailed balance.** Check $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$ for all $i,j$:

$$s_i q_{ij} = \frac{\deg(i)}{2|E|} \cdot \frac{A_{ij}}{\deg(i)} = \frac{A_{ij}}{2|E|}$$

$$s_j q_{ji} = \frac{\deg(j)}{2|E|} \cdot \frac{A_{ji}}{\deg(j)} = \frac{A_{ji}}{2|E|}$$

Since the graph is undirected, $A_{ij} = A_{ji}$. So $s_i q_{ij} = s_j q_{ji}$. Detailed balance holds. $\blacksquare$

The random knight's walk is **reversible**, and its stationary distribution is:

$$\boxed{s_{(r,c)} = \frac{\deg(r,c)}{\sum_{(r',c')} \deg(r',c')}}$$

### Computing the Normalization

The total degree $\sum_{(r,c)} \deg(r,c) = 2|E|$, where $|E|$ is the number of knight-move pairs on the $8 \times 8$ board.

By enumeration (or symmetry arguments): $|E| = 168$ on the standard $8\times 8$ board. So $\sum \deg = 336$.

**Degree distribution on the $8 \times 8$ board:**

|Degree|Number of squares|Total degree contribution|
|---|---|---|
|2|4|8|
|3|8|24|
|4|20|80|
|6|16|96|
|8|16|128|

Total: $4 + 8 + 20 + 16 + 16 = 64$ squares ✓, and $8 + 24 + 80 + 96 + 128 = 336$ ✓.

So the stationary probabilities:

|Degree|$s_{(r,c)}$|
|---|---|
|2|$2/336 \approx 0.006$|
|3|$3/336 \approx 0.009$|
|4|$4/336 \approx 0.012$|
|6|$6/336 \approx 0.018$|
|8|$8/336 \approx 0.024$|

**The knight spends 4× as much time on a degree-8 central square as on a degree-2 corner square.** The ergodic theorem says this is literally the long-run fraction of moves spent on each square.

> _The fact that $s_i \propto \deg(i)$ is a general theorem about random walks on undirected graphs — not special to the knight. The proof above works for any undirected graph. The knight is just a particularly nice example because the degree function has a clean geometric interpretation: degree measures how "central" a square is, and the stationary distribution reflects that centrality. This is the same logic as PageRank — high-degree nodes are more visited._

## The Bipartite Structure and Periodicity

Here is the deepest structural fact about the knight's walk.

**Fact:** Every knight move changes the color of the square. From a white square, the knight always lands on a black square, and vice versa.

**Proof:** A knight move changes $(r,c)$ by $(\pm 1, \pm 2)$ or $(\pm 2, \pm 1)$. In either case, the total displacement is $1 + 2 = 3$ (in taxicab distance), which is odd. The color of a square is determined by the parity of $r + c$: white if $r+c$ is even, black if $r+c$ is odd. An odd displacement changes the parity of $r+c$, hence changes the color. $\blacksquare$

This means the knight-move graph on the $8 \times 8$ board is **bipartite**: every edge connects a white square to a black square. There are no edges within the same color class.

**Consequence for the Markov chain:** Starting from a white square, at even times the knight is on a white square, at odd times on a black square. The set of times the knight can return to its starting square is ${2, 4, 6, \ldots}$ — all even. So:

$$\gcd{n \geq 1 : q_{ii}^{(n)} > 0} = 2$$

**Every state has period 2.** The chain is **periodic** with period $d = 2$.

This is a direct application of the theory from [[Classification of States]]: bipartiteness of the underlying graph is equivalent to period 2 of the random walk.

> _This is the single most important structural fact about this chain. It means the convergence theorem as stated — $\mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \mathbf{s}$ for irreducible aperiodic chains — does NOT apply. The distribution of $X_n$ oscillates and never converges. If I start on a white square, at every even time I'm on a white square with high probability, and at every odd time I'm on a black square with high probability. The chain never "forgets" the color of the starting square in the distributional sense._

## Irreducibility

**Claim:** The knight's walk on the $8 \times 8$ board is irreducible — from any square, every other square is reachable.

**Proof:** It suffices to show that from any square $(r,c)$, any other square $(r',c')$ is reachable in finitely many knight moves. This is a fact about the knight-move graph being connected, which is well-known (and can be verified by exhaustive BFS). Every square of the $8 \times 8$ board is reachable from every other square. $\blacksquare$

Note the interaction with periodicity: the knight can reach any white square from a given white square in an even number of steps, and any black square in an odd number of steps. It cannot reach a square of the same color in an odd number of steps — that's what period 2 means.

So the chain is **irreducible with period 2**. The state space does not split into further communicating classes — there's only one — but within that class, the chain alternates between two "phases" (white and black squares).

## What Convergence Looks Like for a Periodic Chain

Since the chain is periodic with period 2, the distribution $\mathbf{t}^{(n)}$ does not converge as $n \to \infty$. But something does converge: the **Cesàro average**.

**Theorem (from [[Existence and Uniqueness of Stationary Distributions]]):** For any irreducible positive recurrent chain (periodic or not):

$$\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \mathbf{s} \quad \text{as } N \to \infty$$

And the ergodic theorem still holds:

$$\frac{1}{N}\sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \mathbf{1}[X_n = (r,c)] \xrightarrow{a.s.} s_{(r,c)} = \frac{\deg(r,c)}{336}$$

The long-run fraction of time on each square converges, even though the distribution at any fixed time oscillates.

### The Two-Step Chain Converges

For a period-2 chain, the standard fix for distributional convergence is to look at the **2-step chain** $(X_0, X_2, X_4, \ldots)$ — i.e., the chain with transition matrix $Q^2$.

**Claim:** $Q^2$ decomposes into two irreducible aperiodic chains: one on white squares, one on black squares.

**Proof:** From a white square, $Q^2$ takes two knight moves, landing back on a white square. Since the chain is irreducible and period 2, the white squares form an irreducible class under $Q^2$ — any white square is reachable from any other white square in an even number of moves. Similarly for black squares. Aperiodicity of $Q^2$: the period of $Q^2$ is $\gcd{n : (Q^2)_{ii}^{(n)} > 0} = \gcd{2n : q_{ii}^{(2n)} > 0}/2 = 1$ (since from any white square there exist both 2-step and 4-step returns; $\gcd(2,4)/2 = 1$). $\blacksquare$

So starting from a white square:

$$\mathbf{t}^{(2n)} \to \mathbf{s}^{\text{white}}, \quad \mathbf{t}^{(2n+1)} \to \mathbf{s}^{\text{black}}$$

where $\mathbf{s}^{\text{white}}$ is the stationary distribution of $Q^2$ restricted to white squares, and similarly for black. These are:

$$s^{\text{white}}_{(r,c)} = \frac{\deg(r,c)}{\sum_{(r',c') \text{ white}} \deg(r',c')}, \quad s^{\text{black}}_{(r,c)} = \frac{\deg(r,c)}{\sum_{(r',c') \text{ black}} \deg(r',c')}$$

> _This is a clean illustration of the three-level convergence table from [[Stationary Distributions]]:_
> 
> - _$\mathbf{s}$ exists and is unique: YES (irreducible + positive recurrent)_
> - _Time averages converge to $s_{(r,c)}$: YES (ergodic theorem)_
> - _$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} \to \mathbf{s}$: NO (periodic with period 2)_
> - _$\mathbf{t}^{(2n)} \to \mathbf{s}^{\text{white}}$: YES (2-step chain is aperiodic)_

## The General $n \times n$ Board

It's worth understanding how the analysis changes with board size. The key facts:

**Irreducibility:** The knight's graph on an $n \times n$ board is connected (irreducible) for $n \geq 5$. For $n \leq 4$, some squares are isolated or the graph disconnects — the knight cannot reach all squares from all starting positions. For example, on a $3 \times 3$ board, the center square has no legal knight moves (all targets are off the board).

**Periodicity:** The chessboard is bipartite for all $n \geq 1$ (color alternation is a property of the knight move, not the board size). So the period is always 2 — on any board where the chain is irreducible.

**Stationary distribution:** Always proportional to degree: $s_{(r,c)} \propto \deg(r,c)$, by the same detailed balance argument.

**For the $5 \times 5$ board:** Minimum degree is 2 (corners), maximum is 8 (some central squares), and the chain is irreducible. Everything works exactly as for the $8 \times 8$ case.

> _The $n < 5$ failure of irreducibility is a boundary phenomenon — the board is too small for the knight to maneuver freely. This is a good reminder that irreducibility is not automatic; it requires checking the graph structure. The period-2 property, however, is universal — it's a consequence of the knight move always changing color, which has nothing to do with boundary effects._

## Hitting Times: How Long to Reach a Corner?

First-step equations apply. Let $h_{(r,c)} = E[\text{steps to reach a corner} \mid X_0 = (r,c)]$.

The four corners are the target states — label them $C$. Boundary conditions:

$$h_{(r,c)} = 0 \quad \text{for } (r,c) \in C$$

First-step equation for interior states:

$$h_{(r,c)} = 1 + \frac{1}{\deg(r,c)} \sum_{(r',c') \text{ knight-reachable from } (r,c)} h_{(r',c')}$$

This is a linear system of $60$ equations in $60$ unknowns — no closed form, but numerically solvable.

By symmetry, all four corners give the same hitting time from a given square. The hardest square to reach a corner from is intuitively a central square (far from all corners, few direct paths). The easiest is a square already adjacent to a corner.

**Example: from d4 (a central square, high degree).**

The knight at d4 has 8 legal moves, none of which are corners (corners are at a1, a8, h1, h8). To reach a corner, the knight must navigate from the center outward — fighting against the stationary distribution, which pulls it toward the center. Corners have stationary measure $2/336 \approx 0.006$ — very small. By the formula $s_{(r,c)} = 1/m_{(r,c)}$ from [[Existence and Uniqueness of Stationary Distributions]], the expected return time to a corner from itself is:

$$m_{\text{corner}} = \frac{1}{s_{\text{corner}}} = \frac{336}{2} = 168 \text{ steps}$$

The expected return time to a corner is 168 steps. The expected hitting time to a corner from elsewhere will be on the same order — large, because corners are rarely visited.

> _$m_i = 1/s_i$ is one of the most useful formulas I know. It converts a stationary probability into an expected return time without solving any first-step equations. For the corner: $s = 2/336$, so $m = 168$ steps. For a central degree-8 square: $s = 8/336 = 1/42$, so $m = 42$ steps. The knight returns to the center every 42 moves on average, and to a corner only every 168 moves. The center is 4× more revisited than the corners — exactly the ratio of their degrees._

## Doubly Stochastic? No.

A matrix is **doubly stochastic** if both rows and columns sum to 1. For the random walk on an undirected graph, rows sum to 1 (by construction), but columns sum to $\sum_i q_{ij} = \sum_i A_{ij}/\deg(i)$, which equals 1 for all $j$ only if all degrees are equal.

The knight's transition matrix is **not** doubly stochastic because degrees are not all equal (they range from 2 to 8). Consequently, the stationary distribution is not uniform — central squares have higher probability than corner squares.

This is in contrast to, say, the random walk on a **regular graph** (all degrees equal $d$), where $q_{ij} = A_{ij}/d$ and every column sums to $\sum_i A_{ij}/d = \deg(j)/d = 1$. The uniform distribution is stationary for any regular graph.

**Is there a related regular graph?** Yes: the $8 \times 8$ toroidal chessboard — wrap the board into a torus so that the board has no boundary. Every square then has exactly 8 knight-move neighbors (the 8 potential targets are all on the board, modulo wrapping). The transition matrix is doubly stochastic, and the stationary distribution is uniform: $s_{(r,c)} = 1/64$ for all squares.

> _The toroidal knight is a beautiful object: a regular random walk on a Cayley graph of $\mathbb{Z}_8 \times \mathbb{Z}_8$ with the generating set ${(\pm 1, \pm 2), (\pm 2, \pm 1)}$. It's still bipartite (and hence period-2), still irreducible (on the torus, knight connectivity is easy to verify), but now the uniform stationary distribution makes the Fourier analysis tractable. Mixing times on Cayley graphs connect to representation theory. A rabbit hole I should explore in [[Random Walks on Groups]]._

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> Random knight on an $8\times 8$ board: irreducible (connected graph), period 2 (bipartite — knight always changes color), reversible (undirected graph $\Rightarrow$ detailed balance), stationary distribution $s_{(r,c)} \propto \deg(r,c)$ (corners least visited at $2/336$, center most at $8/336$). Distribution $\mathbf{t}^{(n)}$ oscillates forever; time averages converge to $\mathbf{s}$; the 2-step chain converges distributionally. Expected return time to a square is $336/\deg$.
