

> _"The Gambler's Ruin is the first Markov chain where something genuinely hard happens — absorption. The chain doesn't converge to a stationary distribution. It terminates. The question is no longer 'where does it end up in the long run' but 'which absorbing state does it get trapped in, and how long does it take.' These are harder questions, and the answers are beautiful."_

---

## Setup

A gambler starts with $k$ dollars. At each round, she wins 1 dollar with probability $p$ and loses 1 dollar with probability $q = 1 - p$. She stops when she is ruined (hits 0) or reaches her target fortune $N$.

**State space:** $\mathcal{S} = {0, 1, 2, \ldots, N}$

**Transition probabilities:**

$$q_{i, i+1} = p, \quad q_{i, i-1} = q = 1-p \quad \text{for } i \in {1, \ldots, N-1}$$

$$q_{00} = 1, \quad q_{NN} = 1 \quad \text{(absorbing boundaries)}$$

All other $q_{ij} = 0$.

The transition matrix $Q$ is tridiagonal with absorbing corners:

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \ q & 0 & p & \cdots & 0 & 0 \ 0 & q & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \ \vdots & & \ddots & \ddots & & \vdots \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & q & 0 & p \ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}$$

---

## Classification of States

**States ${0}$ and ${N}$:** Absorbing states. Each is its own closed communicating class. Both are recurrent (trivially — once you're there, you stay).

**States ${1, 2, \ldots, N-1}$:** These form a single communicating class (any interior state can reach any other in finitely many steps). But this class is **not closed** — from any interior state, the chain can reach 0 or $N$. By the theorem from [[Classification of States]], an open communicating class in a finite chain must be **transient**.

So the full classification:

|States|Class|Type|
|---|---|---|
|${0}$|Singleton|Absorbing / Recurrent|
|${1, \ldots, N-1}$|One open class|Transient|
|${N}$|Singleton|Absorbing / Recurrent|

> _This is the structural fact that makes the problem tractable. The interior states are transient — the chain visits each one only finitely many times and eventually gets absorbed. The only question is: absorbed where, and after how long?_

---

## The Two Questions

1. **Absorption probabilities:** Starting from state $k$, what is the probability of being absorbed at $N$ (winning) vs. at $0$ (ruin)?
    
2. **Expected absorption time:** Starting from state $k$, how many steps until absorption?
    

Both are answered by setting up **first-step equations** — condition on the first transition, use the Markov property, solve the resulting system.

---

## Question 1: Absorption Probabilities

### Setup

Let $r_k = P(\text{absorbed at } N \mid X_0 = k)$ be the probability of winning starting from $k$.

**Boundary conditions** (immediate from the absorbing state definitions):

$$r_0 = 0, \qquad r_N = 1$$

**First-step equation** for $k \in {1, \ldots, N-1}$: condition on the first step:

$$r_k = p \cdot r_{k+1} + q \cdot r_{k-1}$$

This is a **second-order linear recurrence relation** with constant coefficients.

### Solving the Recurrence

Rearranged: $p \cdot r_{k+1} - r_k + q \cdot r_{k-1} = 0$, or:

$$p \cdot r_{k+1} - (p+q) r_k + q \cdot r_{k-1} = 0$$

since $p + q = 1$. The characteristic equation is:

$$p \lambda^2 - \lambda + q = 0 \implies \lambda = \frac{1 \pm \sqrt{1 - 4pq}}{2p}$$

Note that $1 - 4pq = (p-q)^2$, so:

$$\lambda = \frac{1 \pm |p - q|}{2p}$$

The two roots are $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = q/p$.

### Case 1: $p \neq q$ (biased walk)

Two distinct roots $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = q/p$. General solution:

$$r_k = A \cdot 1^k + B \cdot \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^k = A + B\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^k$$

Apply boundary conditions:

$$r_0 = 0: \quad A + B = 0 \implies B = -A$$

$$r_N = 1: \quad A + B\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^N = 1 \implies A\left(1 - \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^N\right) = 1 \implies A = \frac{1}{1-(q/p)^N}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{r_k = \frac{1 - (q/p)^k}{1 - (q/p)^N}}$$

### Case 2: $p = q = 1/2$ (fair walk)

Repeated root $\lambda = 1$. General solution:

$$r_k = A + Bk$$

Apply boundary conditions: $r_0 = 0 \Rightarrow A = 0$; $r_N = 1 \Rightarrow B = 1/N$. Therefore:

$$\boxed{r_k = \frac{k}{N}}$$

For a fair game, the probability of winning is simply the fraction of the way to the target you start at. The probability of ruin is $1 - k/N = (N-k)/N$.

> _The fair game result $r_k = k/N$ is elegant and somewhat intuitive — your winning probability is proportional to your current fortune. But the biased case reveals something darker. If $p < q$ (unfavorable game), $(q/p)^k$ grows, and $r_k \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$ (infinite table). Even with a large starting fortune, playing against the house with a slight edge in their favor leads to certain ruin eventually. This is the mathematical foundation of why casinos win._

### The Ruin Probability

$P(\text{ruined} \mid X_0 = k) = 1 - r_k$. For $p \neq q$:

$$P(\text{ruined} \mid X_0 = k) = \frac{(q/p)^k - (q/p)^N}{1 - (q/p)^N}$$

### Limiting Case: $N \to \infty$ (playing against an infinitely rich opponent)

For $p < q$ (unfavorable): $(q/p) > 1$, so $(q/p)^N \to \infty$, and:

$$r_k \to \frac{1 - (q/p)^k}{\infty} = 0$$

**Ruin is certain** — you will be ruined with probability 1 regardless of your starting fortune.

For $p > q$ (favorable): $(q/p) < 1$, so $(q/p)^N \to 0$, and:

$$r_k \to 1 - \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^k$$

There's a positive probability of never being ruined.

For $p = q = 1/2$ (fair): $r_k = k/N \to 0$ as $N \to \infty$.

**Ruin is also certain for the fair game against an infinitely rich opponent.** This is the recurrence of the simple random walk on $\mathbb{Z}$ — starting anywhere, the walk hits 0 almost surely (and also $+\infty$... from both directions). A famous result.

## Question 2: Expected Duration

### Setup

Let $d_k = E[\text{absorption time} \mid X_0 = k]$.

**Boundary conditions:**

$$d_0 = 0, \qquad d_N = 0$$

**First-step equation** for $k \in {1, \ldots, N-1}$:

$$d_k = 1 + p \cdot d_{k+1} + q \cdot d_{k-1}$$

The $1$ accounts for the one step just taken. This is a **non-homogeneous** second-order linear recurrence.

### Solving the Recurrence

Rewrite: $p \cdot d_{k+1} - d_k + q \cdot d_{k-1} = -1$.

The homogeneous solution is the same as before: $d_k^{(h)} = A + B(q/p)^k$ (for $p \neq q$).

For a particular solution, try $d_k^{(p)} = Ck$:

$$pC(k+1) - Ck + qC(k-1) = -1$$ $$Ck(p - 1 + q) + C(p - q) = -1$$ $$C(p - q) = -1 \implies C = \frac{1}{q - p}$$

So the general solution for $p \neq q$:

$$d_k = A + B\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^k + \frac{k}{q-p}$$

Apply boundary conditions $d_0 = d_N = 0$:

From $d_0 = 0$: $A + B = 0 \Rightarrow B = -A$.

From $d_N = 0$:

$$A\left(1 - \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^N\right) + \frac{N}{q-p} = 0 \implies A = \frac{N}{(p-q)(1-(q/p)^N)}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{d_k = \frac{1}{q-p}\left(k - N \cdot \frac{1-(q/p)^k}{1-(q/p)^N}\right)} \quad (p \neq q)$$

### Case $p = q = 1/2$

Particular solution: try $d_k^{(p)} = Ck^2$:

$$\frac{1}{2}C(k+1)^2 - Ck^2 + \frac{1}{2}C(k-1)^2 = -1 \implies C = -1$$

General solution: $d_k = A + Bk - k^2$. Boundary conditions give $A = 0$, $B = N$:

$$\boxed{d_k = k(N - k)} \quad (p = q = 1/2)$$

Starting from $k$, the expected number of steps is $k(N-k)$. Maximized at $k = N/2$: starting in the middle, expected duration is $N^2/4$.

> _The formula $d_k = k(N-k)$ for the fair game is one of my favorite results in elementary probability. It says the expected game length is maximized when you start in the middle, and scales as $N^2$. If $N = 100$ and you start at 50, the expected game lasts 2500 rounds. If $N = 1000$ and you start at 500, it lasts 250,000 rounds. The quadratic scaling makes long fair games very long indeed._

## The First-Step Equation as a Method

The technique used above — conditioning on the first step — is completely general and deserves to be called out explicitly.

**Template:** For any quantity $h_k$ defined as an expectation or probability starting from state $k$:

1. Write the boundary conditions at absorbing states.
2. For interior states, write $h_k = (\text{immediate cost/contribution}) + \sum_j q_{kj} h_j$.
3. Solve the resulting linear system (recurrence relation in 1D chains, linear system in general).

The Markov property is what licenses step 2 — the future expected cost from state $j$ doesn't depend on how we got to $j$.

For the Gambler's Ruin, the 1D structure turns the linear system into a second-order recurrence, which is exactly solvable. In a general Markov chain, step 2 gives a system of $|\mathcal{S}|$ linear equations.

> _I think of first-step equations as the "dynamic programming" of Markov chain theory. The Markov property is the principle of optimality: given the current state, the future is independent of history. So I can recursively decompose any expectation or probability into a one-step look-ahead plus future. Every Markov chain problem I've seen in Stat 110 reduces to setting up and solving a first-step equation._

## Martingale Verification (Optional but Illuminating)

There is a slicker way to derive the absorption probabilities that bypasses the recurrence entirely, using **martingales**. I'll sketch it because it reveals why the answer has the form it does.

### Fair Game ($p = 1/2$)

$X_n$ itself is a martingale: $E[X_{n+1} \mid X_n] = \frac{1}{2}(X_n + 1) + \frac{1}{2}(X_n - 1) = X_n$.

By the Optional Stopping Theorem (under integrability conditions), $E[X_T] = E[X_0] = k$, where $T$ is the absorption time. But $X_T \in {0, N}$, so:

$$E[X_T] = 0 \cdot P(\text{ruin}) + N \cdot r_k = N r_k = k \implies r_k = \frac{k}{N}$$

### Biased Game ($p \neq q$)

$(q/p)^{X_n}$ is a martingale:

$$E\left[\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_{n+1}} \Bigg| X_n\right] = p \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n+1} + q\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n - 1} = \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n}\left(q + q\right)$$

Wait, more carefully: $= \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n} \cdot \left(p \cdot \frac{q}{p} + q \cdot \frac{p}{q}\right)$... let me redo:

$$= p \cdot \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n} \cdot \frac{q}{p} + q \cdot \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n} \cdot \frac{p}{q} = \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n}(q + p) = \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_n}$$

Yes, it is a martingale. By Optional Stopping:

$$\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^k = E\left[\left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^{X_T}\right] = \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^0 \cdot (1 - r_k) + \left(\frac{q}{p}\right)^N \cdot r_k$$

Solving for $r_k$ gives exactly the formula above.

> _The martingale approach feels like magic the first time. The recurrence approach is more elementary but less illuminating — you solve a system of equations and the answer pops out. The martingale approach reveals why the answer involves $(q/p)^k$: because $(q/p)^{X_n}$ is the "right" function of the state to track, the one that is preserved on average. The answer is essentially forced by the structure of the martingale._

## Summary

||Fair ($p = 1/2$)|Biased ($p \neq q$)|
|---|---|---|
|$P(\text{win} \mid X_0 = k)$|$k/N$|$\dfrac{1-(q/p)^k}{1-(q/p)^N}$|
|$P(\text{ruin} \mid X_0 = k)$|$(N-k)/N$|$\dfrac{(q/p)^k - (q/p)^N}{1-(q/p)^N}$|
|$E[\text{duration} \mid X_0 = k]$|$k(N-k)$|$\dfrac{1}{q-p}!\left(k - N\cdot\dfrac{1-(q/p)^k}{1-(q/p)^N}\right)$|
|$P(\text{ruin} \mid N \to \infty)$|$1$|$1$ if $p < q$; $(q/p)^k$ if $p > q$|

**State classification:** ${0}$ and ${N}$ are absorbing (recurrent). ${1, \ldots, N-1}$ are transient (open communicating class, finite chain).

**Method:** First-step equations — condition on the first transition, use the Markov property, solve the resulting recurrence with boundary conditions.

---

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> Gambler's Ruin is an absorbing Markov chain: interior states ${1,\ldots,N-1}$ are transient, boundaries ${0, N}$ are absorbing. Absorption probabilities and expected duration both satisfy first-step equations — second-order linear recurrences whose solutions are $r_k = \frac{1-(q/p)^k}{1-(q/p)^N}$ (biased) or $k/N$ (fair), and $d_k = k(N-k)$ (fair) — derivable either by recurrence or by martingale optional stopping.
