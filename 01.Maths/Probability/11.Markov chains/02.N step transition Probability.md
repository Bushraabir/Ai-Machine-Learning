## Why This Note Exists

The previous note ([[Markov Chains — Definitions and Transition Matrix]]) established the one-step transition matrix $Q$. The entry $q_{ij}$ tells me where the chain goes in a single step. But I immediately want to ask: **what about $n$ steps?** If I'm at state $i$ right now, where am I likely to be after $n$ transitions?

The answer — that the $n$-step transition matrix is literally $Q^n$, the matrix power — is one of those results that feels too clean to be true. This note is my attempt to be completely rigorous about why it's true, what it implies, and how I actually use it.

---

## Setup and Notation

$(X_n)_{n \geq 0}$ is a time-homogeneous Markov chain on a finite state space $\mathcal{S} = {1, \ldots, M}$ with transition matrix $Q$.

**Definition.** The **$n$-step transition probability** from state $i$ to state $j$ is:

$$q_{ij}^{(n)} := P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i)$$

More generally, by time-homogeneity:

$$q_{ij}^{(n)} = P(X_{m+n} = j \mid X_m = i) \quad \text{for any } m \geq 0$$

The **$n$-step transition matrix** is $Q^{(n)} = \bigl(q_{ij}^{(n)}\bigr)_{i,j \in \mathcal{S}}$.

**Boundary case:** $Q^{(0)} = I$, the identity matrix — in zero steps, I stay where I am with probability 1.

**Boundary case:** $Q^{(1)} = Q$ — this just recovers the original definition.

---

## The Chapman-Kolmogorov Equations

This is the engine. Everything else follows from it.

### Theorem (Chapman-Kolmogorov)

For all $m, n \geq 0$ and all states $i, j \in \mathcal{S}$:

$$q_{ij}^{(m+n)} = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}} q_{ik}^{(m)} , q_{kj}^{(n)}$$

In matrix form:

$$Q^{(m+n)} = Q^{(m)} , Q^{(n)}$$

### Proof

Condition on the intermediate state $X_m$:

$$P(X_{m+n} = j \mid X_0 = i) = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}} P(X_{m+n} = j \mid X_m = k, X_0 = i) \cdot P(X_m = k \mid X_0 = i)$$

Now apply the Markov property: given $X_m = k$, the future $X_{m+n}$ is independent of the past $X_0 = i$. So:

$$P(X_{m+n} = j \mid X_m = k, X_0 = i) = P(X_{m+n} = j \mid X_m = k) = q_{kj}^{(n)}$$

Substituting:

$$q_{ij}^{(m+n)} = \sum_{k \in \mathcal{S}} q_{ik}^{(m)} \cdot q_{kj}^{(n)}$$

The right-hand side is precisely the $(i,j)$ entry of the matrix product $Q^{(m)} Q^{(n)}$. $\blacksquare$

> _The Markov property does exactly one thing in this proof: it collapses the three-event conditional into a two-event conditional. Without it, I'd be stuck — the past $X_0 = i$ would pollute the future computation. With it, the sum over intermediate states $k$ decomposes cleanly._

---

## The Key Theorem: $Q^{(n)} = Q^n$

### Theorem

The $n$-step transition matrix equals the $n$-th matrix power of $Q$:

$$Q^{(n)} = Q^n$$

### Proof

By induction on $n$.

**Base cases:** $Q^{(0)} = I = Q^0$ and $Q^{(1)} = Q = Q^1$. ✓

**Inductive step:** Suppose $Q^{(n)} = Q^n$. Apply Chapman-Kolmogorov with $m = n$, $n = 1$:

$$Q^{(n+1)} = Q^{(n)} \cdot Q^{(1)} = Q^n \cdot Q = Q^{n+1}$$

$\blacksquare$

This is it. This is the theorem. **To find the probability of going from state $i$ to state $j$ in exactly $n$ steps, look at the $(i,j)$ entry of $Q^n$.**

> _I want to pause and appreciate this. The Chapman-Kolmogorov equations say that multi-step transitions compose like matrix multiplication. That's not a coincidence — it IS matrix multiplication. The entire spectral theory of matrices (eigendecompositions, diagonalization, Jordan forms) now applies to Markov chains. Linear algebra is not just a computational tool here; it's the right language._

## The Distribution at Time $n$

Let $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$ be the initial distribution — a row vector with $t^{(0)}_i = P(X_0 = i)$.

### Theorem

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$$

where $t^{(n)}_j = P(X_n = j)$.

### Proof

By the Law of Total Probability (conditioning on $X_0$):

$$P(X_n = j) = \sum_{i \in \mathcal{S}} P(X_n = j \mid X_0 = i) \cdot P(X_0 = i) = \sum_i t^{(0)}_i \cdot (Q^n)_{ij}$$

This is exactly the $j$-th entry of the row vector $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n$. $\blacksquare$

**One-step recursion:** $\mathbf{t}^{(n+1)} = \mathbf{t}^{(n)} Q$.

This recursion is useful for computation: if I know where the chain is at time $n$, left-multiply by $Q$ to get the distribution at time $n+1$.

> _The row-vector convention: B&H and Stat 110 use row vectors left-multiplied by $Q$. Some other texts use column vectors right-multiplied by $Q^\top$. The math is identical; just be consistent. I'll always use the row convention._

## Computing $Q^n$ in Practice

For small matrices and small $n$, just multiply. For large $n$ or symbolic computation, diagonalization is the tool.

### Diagonalization (When Available)

Suppose $Q$ is diagonalizable:

$$Q = P D P^{-1}$$

where $D = \text{diag}(\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_M)$ holds the eigenvalues and $P$ holds the eigenvectors as columns. Then:

$$Q^n = P D^n P^{-1}, \qquad D^n = \text{diag}(\lambda_1^n, \ldots, \lambda_M^n)$$

Raising a diagonal matrix to a power is trivial — just raise each diagonal entry to the $n$-th power.

**Explicit formula for two-state chain:**

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} 1-\alpha & \alpha \ \beta & 1-\beta \end{pmatrix}$$

The eigenvalues are $\lambda_1 = 1$ and $\lambda_2 = 1 - \alpha - \beta$ (I'll derive this below). The $n$-step matrix is:

$$Q^n = \frac{1}{\alpha+\beta}\begin{pmatrix} \beta & \alpha \ \beta & \alpha \end{pmatrix} + \frac{(1-\alpha-\beta)^n}{\alpha+\beta} \begin{pmatrix} \alpha & -\alpha \ -\beta & \beta \end{pmatrix}$$

As $n \to \infty$, $(1-\alpha-\beta)^n \to 0$ (since $|\lambda_2| < 1$ when $\alpha, \beta \in (0,1)$), and both rows converge to the stationary distribution $\bigl(\frac{\beta}{\alpha+\beta}, \frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}\bigr)$.

> _This is the convergence to stationarity in miniature. The second eigenvalue $\lambda_2$ decays exponentially, and its rate of decay determines how quickly the rows of $Q^n$ equalize. The spectral gap $1 - |\lambda_2| = \alpha + \beta$ controls everything._

### Derivation of Eigenvalues for the Two-State Chain

$$\det(Q - \lambda I) = (1-\alpha-\lambda)(1-\beta-\lambda) - \alpha\beta = 0$$

Expand:

$$(1-\alpha-\lambda)(1-\beta-\lambda) - \alpha\beta = \lambda^2 - (2-\alpha-\beta)\lambda + (1-\alpha-\beta) = 0$$

Factor: $(\lambda - 1)(\lambda - (1-\alpha-\beta)) = 0$, giving $\lambda_1 = 1$, $\lambda_2 = 1 - \alpha - \beta$.

## The Spectral Picture of $Q^n$

For a general finite-state chain, if $Q$ has eigenvalues $1 = \lambda_1, \lambda_2, \ldots, \lambda_M$ and is diagonalizable with left eigenvectors ${\mathbf{v}_k}$ and right eigenvectors ${\mathbf{u}_k}$:

$$Q^n = \sum_{k=1}^{M} \lambda_k^n , \mathbf{u}_k \mathbf{v}_k^\top$$

**Key facts about eigenvalues of a row-stochastic matrix:**

- $\lambda_1 = 1$ always. (Since $Q\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$, the all-ones vector is a right eigenvector with eigenvalue 1. By Perron-Frobenius, this is the largest eigenvalue.)
- All eigenvalues satisfy $|\lambda_k| \leq 1$ — they lie in the closed unit disk.
- For an **irreducible aperiodic** chain: $\lambda_1 = 1$ is the unique eigenvalue with modulus 1. All others satisfy $|\lambda_k| < 1$.

**Long-run behavior:** As $n \to \infty$, all terms with $|\lambda_k| < 1$ vanish:

$$Q^n \longrightarrow \mathbf{1} \mathbf{s}$$

where $\mathbf{s}$ is the stationary distribution (the left eigenvector corresponding to $\lambda_1 = 1$, normalized to sum to 1). The $(i,j)$ entry of $Q^n$ converges to $s_j$ — independent of the starting state $i$.

**Rate of convergence:**

$$\bigl|Q^n - \mathbf{1}\mathbf{s}\bigr| \sim C \cdot |\lambda_2|^n$$

The **spectral gap** is $\gamma = 1 - |\lambda_2|$. A large spectral gap $\Rightarrow$ fast convergence. Maximizing the spectral gap is the core problem in the theory of **mixing times**, and it connects Markov chain theory to expander graphs, random walks on groups, and the analysis of MCMC algorithms.

> _I find the spectral picture genuinely beautiful. The eigenvalue $\lambda_1 = 1$ is the "memory" that the chain retains forever — it's the stationary behavior. Every other eigenvalue is a "mode of forgetting" with timescale $-1/\log|\lambda_k|$. The chain forgets its initial condition one mode at a time, fastest last._

## Worked Example: Three-State Chain

Let $\mathcal{S} = {1, 2, 3}$ with transition matrix:

$$Q = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1/2 & 1/2 \ 1/3 & 1/3 & 1/3 \ 1/2 & 1/2 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$$

**What is $P(X_2 = 3 \mid X_0 = 1)$?**

I need the $(1,3)$ entry of $Q^2$. Compute $Q^2 = Q \cdot Q$:

The $(1,3)$ entry of $Q^2$:

$$q_{13}^{(2)} = q_{11} q_{13} + q_{12} q_{23} + q_{13} q_{33} = 0 \cdot \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot 0 = \frac{1}{6}$$

So $P(X_2 = 3 \mid X_0 = 1) = \frac{1}{6}$.

Alternatively, by Chapman-Kolmogorov directly: sum over intermediate states at time 1:

$$P(X_2 = 3 \mid X_0 = 1) = \sum_{k=1}^{3} P(X_1 = k \mid X_0 = 1) \cdot P(X_2 = 3 \mid X_1 = k)$$ $$= \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{3} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + ... $$

Wait — let me redo this carefully:

$$= P(X_1=1|X_0=1) \cdot q_{13} + P(X_1=2|X_0=1) \cdot q_{23} + P(X_1=3|X_0=1) \cdot q_{33}$$ $$= 0 \cdot \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{1}{3} + \frac{1}{2} \cdot 0 = \frac{1}{6}$$

Both methods agree. ✓ The Chapman-Kolmogorov approach is just the matrix product written out explicitly.

## The $n$-Step Distribution is Still Row-Stochastic

**Claim:** For each $n \geq 0$, the matrix $Q^n$ is row-stochastic.

**Proof:** By induction. $Q^0 = I$ is row-stochastic (trivially). If $Q^n$ is row-stochastic, then:

$$\sum_j (Q^{n+1})_{ij} = \sum_j \sum_k (Q^n)_{ik} q_{kj} = \sum_k (Q^n)_{ik} \underbrace{\sum_j q_{kj}}_{=1} = \sum_k (Q^n)_{ik} = 1$$

where the last equality uses that $Q^n$ is row-stochastic. $\blacksquare$

> _This is good to verify: every row of $Q^n$ is a valid probability distribution, as it should be — it's the conditional distribution of $X_n$ given $X_0 = i$._

## Connection to the Stationary Distribution

A row vector $\mathbf{s}$ is stationary if $\mathbf{s} Q = \mathbf{s}$, equivalently $\mathbf{s} Q^n = \mathbf{s}$ for all $n$.

This follows immediately: if $\mathbf{s} Q = \mathbf{s}$, then $\mathbf{s} Q^2 = (\mathbf{s} Q) Q = \mathbf{s} Q = \mathbf{s}$, and by induction $\mathbf{s} Q^n = \mathbf{s}$.

Conversely, the convergence $Q^n \to \mathbf{1s}$ says: starting from any initial distribution $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}$,

$$\mathbf{t}^{(n)} = \mathbf{t}^{(0)} Q^n \to \mathbf{t}^{(0)} \mathbf{1} \mathbf{s} = \mathbf{s}$$

since $\mathbf{t}^{(0)} \mathbf{1} = \sum_i t^{(0)}_i = 1$. The chain converges in distribution to $\mathbf{s}$ regardless of where it started. The $n$-step transition matrix is the bridge between the initial distribution and stationarity.

## One-Line Summary for Future Me

> The $n$-step transition matrix is $Q^n$: Chapman-Kolmogorov shows that conditioning on the intermediate state at time $m$ turns $Q^{(m+n)} = Q^{(m)}Q^{(n)}$ into ordinary matrix multiplication, and induction gives $Q^{(n)} = Q^n$. The distribution at time $n$ is $\mathbf{t}^{(0)}Q^n$, and for irreducible aperiodic chains this converges to the stationary distribution $\mathbf{s}$ at rate $|\lambda_2|^n$.
