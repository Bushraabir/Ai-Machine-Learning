
> _The MGF is one of those objects that seems like a clever trick the first time you see it — "oh, derivatives give moments, cute." But the deeper reason to care is the uniqueness theorem and what it implies for convergence. That's where the MGF earns its name as one of the most powerful tools in probability. Write this the right way._



We want a single object that encodes _all_ the moments of a distribution — mean, variance, skewness, kurtosis, everything — in a way that is algebraically tractable. The natural candidate is a generating function.

The idea: if we can write $E[e^{tX}]$ as a power series in $t$, then the coefficient of $t^n/n!$ will be $E[X^n]$, because:

$$e^{tX} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(tX)^n}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{t^n}{n!} X^n$$

$$E[e^{tX}] = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{t^n}{n!} E[X^n]$$

Differentiating $n$ times with respect to $t$ and setting $t = 0$ recovers $E[X^n]$. This is the MGF.

---

## Definition

Let $X$ be a random variable. The **moment generating function (MGF)** of $X$ is:

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]}, \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

whenever this expectation is finite. Explicitly:

$$M_X(t) = \begin{cases} \displaystyle\sum_x e^{tx} P(X = x) & X \text{ discrete} \[10pt] \displaystyle\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tx} f_X(x), dx & X \text{ continuous} \end{cases}$$

The MGF **exists** at $t$ if $E[e^{tX}] < \infty$. We say the MGF exists (in the useful sense) if it is finite on an open interval $(-\delta, \delta)$ containing $0$ for some $\delta > 0$.

> _Existence on an open interval around $0$ is crucial — it's what makes the power series argument work and what gives us the uniqueness theorem. A MGF that only exists at $t = 0$ (where $M(0) = 1$ trivially for any distribution) is useless._

---

## The Moment Extraction Property

**Theorem.** If $M_X(t)$ exists on $(-\delta, \delta)$ for some $\delta > 0$, then for all $n \geq 1$:

$$E[X^n] = M_X^{(n)}(0) = \left.\frac{d^n}{dt^n} M_X(t)\right|_{t=0}$$

_Proof._  
Since $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ and the MGF exists on an open interval around $0$, we can interchange differentiation and expectation (justified by dominated convergence — the existence assumption is precisely what provides the dominating integrable function):

$$\frac{d}{dt} M_X(t) = \frac{d}{dt} E[e^{tX}] = E!\left[\frac{d}{dt} e^{tX}\right] = E[X e^{tX}]$$

At $t = 0$: $M_X'(0) = E[X]$. Iterating:

$$M_X^{(n)}(t) = E[X^n e^{tX}]$$

At $t = 0$: $M_X^{(n)}(0) = E[X^n \cdot 1] = E[X^n]$. $\square$

**Alternatively**, via the power series: $$M_X(t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{E[X^n]}{n!} t^n = 1 + E[X]t + \frac{E[X^2]}{2!}t^2 + \frac{E[X^3]}{3!}t^3 + \cdots$$

Reading off coefficients: $E[X^n] = n! \cdot [t^n] M_X(t)$.

---

## The Uniqueness Theorem

This is the theorem that makes the MGF worth caring about deeply.

**Theorem (Uniqueness).** If $M_X(t) = M_Y(t)$ for all $t$ in some open interval $(-\delta, \delta)$, then $X$ and $Y$ have the same distribution.

That is: **the MGF, when it exists, uniquely determines the distribution.**

_Proof idea._ The MGF is essentially the bilateral Laplace transform of the distribution. Two distinct probability measures cannot have the same Laplace transform on an open set — this follows from the injectivity of the Laplace transform, which in turn follows from the uniqueness of analytic continuation (the MGF, when it exists on an open interval, extends to an analytic function on a strip of the complex plane). The formal proof requires complex analysis beyond this course, but the statement is exact. $\square$

> _This theorem is the real reason we care about MGFs. It means: to prove $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$, it suffices to show $M_X(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$. To prove a sum of independent Normals is Normal, compute its MGF and identify it. This is often dramatically easier than working with densities or CDFs directly._

**Warning:** The converse direction — all moments equal does not imply same distribution — is true in general only when the MGF exists. There exist distinct distributions with identical moments of all orders (the lognormal family provides counterexamples). Existence of the MGF on an open interval rules this out.

## Properties

**Proposition 1 (Normalization).** $M_X(0) = 1$ always.

_Proof._ $M_X(0) = E[e^0] = E[1] = 1$. $\square$

**Proposition 2 (Affine transformations).**  
$$M_{aX+b}(t) = e^{bt} M_X(at)$$

_Proof._ $$M_{aX+b}(t) = E[e^{t(aX+b)}] = e^{bt} E[e^{(at)X}] = e^{bt} M_X(at) \quad \square$$

_Corollary._ For the standardized variable $Z = (X - \mu)/\sigma$: $$M_Z(t) = e^{-\mu t/\sigma} M_X(t/\sigma)$$

**Proposition 3 (Independence and products).**  
If $X \perp Y$, then: $$M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t)$$

_Proof._ $$M_{X+Y}(t) = E[e^{t(X+Y)}] = E[e^{tX} e^{tY}] = E[e^{tX}] \cdot E[e^{tY}] = M_X(t) M_Y(t)$$

where the third equality uses independence. $\square$

> _This is the MGF's killer feature for sums of independent random variables. Computing the distribution of $X + Y$ via convolution of densities is painful. Multiplying MGFs is instant._

**Proposition 4 (Extension to $n$ variables).**  
If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent, then: $$M_{X_1 + \cdots + X_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{X_i}(t)$$

If additionally $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d.: $$M_{S_n}(t) = \left(M_X(t)\right)^n$$

## MGFs of Named Distributions

### Bernoulli $X \sim \text{Bern}(p)$

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = e^{t \cdot 0}(1-p) + e^{t \cdot 1} p = (1-p) + pe^t = q + pe^t$$

where $q = 1-p$. Exists for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

Moments: $M'(t) = pe^t$, so $E[X] = M'(0) = p$. $M''(t) = pe^t$, so $E[X^2] = p$. Then $\text{Var}(X) = p - p^2 = p(1-p)$. ✓

---

### Binomial $X \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$

By independence ($X = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$, i.i.d. Bern$(p)$): $$M_X(t) = (q + pe^t)^n$$

Exists for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

_Moment extraction:_ $$M'(t) = n(q + pe^t)^{n-1} \cdot pe^t$$ $$E[X] = M'(0) = n(q+p)^{n-1} \cdot p = np \quad \checkmark$$

---

### Poisson $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty e^{tk} \frac{e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k}{k!} = e^{-\lambda} \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\lambda e^t)^k}{k!} = e^{-\lambda} e^{\lambda e^t}$$

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}}$$

Exists for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

_Verification via Binomial convergence:_  
Since Pois$(\lambda)$ is the limit of Bin$(n, \lambda/n)$ as $n \to \infty$: $$\left(1 - \frac{\lambda}{n} + \frac{\lambda}{n}e^t\right)^n = \left(1 + \frac{\lambda(e^t-1)}{n}\right)^n \to e^{\lambda(e^t-1)} \quad \checkmark$$

_Additivity:_  
If $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$ and $Y \sim \text{Pois}(\mu)$ independent, then: $$M_{X+Y}(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t-1)} \cdot e^{\mu(e^t-1)} = e^{(\lambda+\mu)(e^t-1)}$$ So $X + Y \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda + \mu)$. The uniqueness theorem does the work. $\square$

---

### Geometric $X \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ (number of trials until first success)

$$M_X(t) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty e^{tk}(1-p)^{k-1}p = \frac{pe^t}{1-(1-p)e^t}, \quad t < -\log(1-p)$$

Exists only for $t < -\log(1-p)$ — the MGF has a finite radius of existence.

---

### Exponential $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$

$$M_X(t) = \int_0^\infty e^{tx} \lambda e^{-\lambda x}, dx = \lambda \int_0^\infty e^{-({\lambda - t})x}, dx = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}, \quad t < \lambda$$

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t} = \left(1 - \frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-1}}$$

_Moment extraction:_ $$M'(t) = \frac{\lambda}{(\lambda-t)^2} \implies E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda} \quad \checkmark$$ $$M''(t) = \frac{2\lambda}{(\lambda-t)^3} \implies E[X^2] = \frac{2}{\lambda^2} \implies \text{Var}(X) = \frac{1}{\lambda^2} \quad \checkmark$$

---

### Gamma $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$

By independence ($X = \sum_{i=1}^a X_i$, i.i.d. Expo$(\lambda)$ when $a$ is a positive integer): $$M_X(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda-t}\right)^a = \left(1 - \frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-a}, \quad t < \lambda$$

This holds for all $a > 0$ by analytic continuation.

---

### Normal $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$

The fundamental computation. Take $Z \sim N(0,1)$ first:

$$M_Z(t) = \int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{tz} \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}} e^{-z^2/2}, dz = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-\frac{1}{2}(z^2 - 2tz)}, dz$$

Complete the square: $z^2 - 2tz = (z-t)^2 - t^2$. So:

$$M_Z(t) = \frac{e^{t^2/2}}{\sqrt{2\pi}}\int_{-\infty}^\infty e^{-\frac{(z-t)^2}{2}}, dz = e^{t^2/2}$$

since the integral is $\sqrt{2\pi}$ (it's a Normal density integrating to 1).

$$\boxed{M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2}}$$

For $X = \mu + \sigma Z \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$, by the affine transformation property: $$M_X(t) = e^{\mu t} M_Z(\sigma t) = e^{\mu t} e^{\sigma^2 t^2/2}$$

$$\boxed{M_X(t) = \exp!\left(\mu t + \frac{\sigma^2 t^2}{2}\right)}$$

Exists for all $t \in \mathbb{R}$.

_Moment extraction from $M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2}$:_

$$M_Z(t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(t^2/2)^n}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{t^{2n}}{2^n n!}$$

The coefficient of $t^{2n}/(2n)!$ is $E[Z^{2n}]$, so: $$E[Z^{2n}] = \frac{(2n)!}{2^n n!} = (2n-1)!! = 1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2n-1)$$

All odd moments are $0$ (by symmetry). So $E[Z^4] = 3$, giving $\text{Kurt}(Z) = 3$. ✓

_Additivity of independent Normals:_  
If $X \sim N(\mu_1, \sigma_1^2)$ and $Y \sim N(\mu_2, \sigma_2^2)$ independent: $$M_{X+Y}(t) = e^{\mu_1 t + \sigma_1^2 t^2/2} \cdot e^{\mu_2 t + \sigma_2^2 t^2/2} = e^{(\mu_1+\mu_2)t + (\sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)t^2/2}$$

So $X + Y \sim N(\mu_1+\mu_2,, \sigma_1^2+\sigma_2^2)$. $\square$

## When the MGF Does Not Exist

Not every distribution has an MGF on an open interval around $0$.

**Example: Log-Normal.**  
If $X = e^Z$ with $Z \sim N(0,1)$, then: $$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = E[e^{te^Z}]$$ For any $t > 0$, $e^{te^Z}$ grows faster than any exponential as $Z \to \infty$, so $E[e^{te^Z}] = \infty$. The MGF of the lognormal does not exist for any $t > 0$.

**Example: Cauchy.**  
The Cauchy distribution has no finite moments at all. $E[e^{tX}] = \infty$ for all $t \neq 0$.

**Example: Pareto, $t$-distribution with small $\nu$.**  
Heavy-tailed distributions whose moments fail to exist beyond some finite order will have MGFs that fail to exist on any open interval around $0$.

> _When the MGF does not exist, we use the **characteristic function** $\varphi_X(t) = E[e^{itX}]$ instead — it always exists since $|e^{itX}| = 1$. This requires complex analysis but works universally. The MGF is the real-line version; the characteristic function is the full story._

## The MGF and the CLT

The MGF gives one of the cleanest proofs of the CLT (under the assumption that the MGF exists).

**Setup.** Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots$ be i.i.d. with mean $0$, variance $\sigma^2$, and MGF $M(t)$ existing on $(-\delta, \delta)$. Let $S_n = (X_1 + \cdots + X_n)/(\sigma\sqrt{n})$.

**Claim.** $M_{S_n}(t) \to e^{t^2/2}$ for each fixed $t$, which is the MGF of $N(0,1)$.

_Proof._

$$M_{S_n}(t) = E!\left[e^{t S_n}\right] = \left(M!\left(\frac{t}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right)\right)^n$$

Let $u = t/(\sigma\sqrt{n})$. As $n \to \infty$, $u \to 0$. Taylor expand $M(u)$ around $0$:

$$M(u) = 1 + E[X]\cdot u + \frac{E[X^2]}{2}u^2 + O(u^3) = 1 + 0 + \frac{\sigma^2}{2}u^2 + O(u^3)$$

since $E[X] = 0$ and $E[X^2] = \sigma^2$. Substituting $u = t/(\sigma\sqrt{n})$:

$$M!\left(\frac{t}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right) = 1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})$$

Therefore:

$$M_{S_n}(t) = \left(1 + \frac{t^2}{2n} + O(n^{-3/2})\right)^n \to e^{t^2/2}$$

by the standard limit $(1 + x/n)^n \to e^x$. By the uniqueness theorem (extended to convergence of distributions), $S_n \xrightarrow{d} N(0,1)$. $\square$

> _This proof shows exactly why the CLT is true: the first two cumulants of $S_n$ are fixed at $0$ and $1$, and all higher-order terms in the Taylor expansion vanish as $n \to \infty$. The $1/n$ and $1/\sqrt{n}$ decay of excess kurtosis and skewness from [[02.Skewness]] and [[03.Kurtosis]] is the same phenomenon, seen through cumulants._

## Things I Keep Getting Confused

> _Honest accounting._

1. **Thinking the MGF always exists.** It doesn't. Heavy-tailed distributions (lognormal, Cauchy, Pareto) may have no MGF. Always check existence before using.
    
2. **Differentiating inside the expectation without justification.** The interchange $\frac{d}{dt}E[\cdot] = E!\left[\frac{d}{dt}\cdot\right]$ requires dominated convergence, which is guaranteed by existence of the MGF on an open interval. This is why the open interval condition matters.
    
3. **Confusing MGF with probability generating function (PGF).** The PGF is $G_X(z) = E[z^X]$ for non-negative integer $X$. The MGF is $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$. They are related by $M_X(t) = G_X(e^t)$, but they are different objects used in different contexts.
    
4. **Using the uniqueness theorem sloppily.** The uniqueness theorem requires the MGF to exist on an _open interval_ around $0$, not just at $0$. Two distributions with the same MGF at a single point tell us nothing.
    
5. **Forgetting that $M_{X+Y} = M_X M_Y$ requires independence.** For dependent $X, Y$: $E[e^{t(X+Y)}] = E[e^{tX}e^{tY}] \neq E[e^{tX}]E[e^{tY}]$ in general.
