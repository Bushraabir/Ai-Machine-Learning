
> _This is one of those topics where the formula is deceptively simple and the justification is genuinely subtle. The $n-1$ denominator is not a correction pulled from thin air â€” it comes from a real calculation, and understanding why is worth every minute. I've seen people use this formula for years without knowing where it comes from. Don't be that person._


## Setup and Motivation

We have a population with some unknown mean $\mu$ and unknown variance $\sigma^2$. We observe a random sample $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ â€” i.i.d. draws from this population. We want to _estimate_ $\sigma^2$ from the data.

The natural first instinct: just compute the average squared deviation from the mean. But we don't _know_ $\mu$. So we substitute the **sample mean**:

$$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$

This substitution has a cost, and the $n-1$ is the price we pay for it.

---

## Definitions

**Sample mean:** $$\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^n X_i$$

**Sample variance:** $$S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$$

**Sample standard deviation:** $$S_n = \sqrt{S_n^2} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n-1} \sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2}$$

> _The $n-1$ in the denominator is called **Bessel's correction**. The quantity with $n$ in the denominator â€” $\frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$ â€” is sometimes called the **biased sample variance** or the **MLE of $\sigma^2$ under Normality**. We will see exactly why $n$ is wrong and $n-1$ is right._

---

## The Central Theorem: Unbiasedness of $S_n^2$

**Theorem.** $E[S_n^2] = \sigma^2$.

That is, $S_n^2$ is an **unbiased estimator** of the population variance $\sigma^2$.

_Proof._

We compute $E!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2\right]$ by a standard algebraic trick â€” adding and subtracting $\mu$:

$$X_i - \bar{X}_n = (X_i - \mu) - (\bar{X}_n - \mu)$$

Let $Z_i = X_i - \mu$ (centered observations) and $\bar{Z} = \bar{X}_n - \mu$. Then:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n (Z_i - \bar{Z})^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n Z_i^2 - n\bar{Z}^2$$

_Justification of the last step:_ $$\sum_{i=1}^n (Z_i - \bar{Z})^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n Z_i^2 - 2\bar{Z}\sum_{i=1}^n Z_i + n\bar{Z}^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n Z_i^2 - 2n\bar{Z}^2 + n\bar{Z}^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n Z_i^2 - n\bar{Z}^2$$

Now take expectations:

$$E!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n Z_i^2\right] = n \cdot E[Z_i^2] = n \cdot E[(X_i - \mu)^2] = n\sigma^2$$

$$E!\left[n\bar{Z}^2\right] = n \cdot E!\left[(\bar{X}_n - \mu)^2\right] = n \cdot \text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = n \cdot \frac{\sigma^2}{n} = \sigma^2$$

Therefore:

$$E!\left[\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2\right] = n\sigma^2 - \sigma^2 = (n-1)\sigma^2$$

Dividing by $n-1$:

$$E[S_n^2] = \frac{(n-1)\sigma^2}{n-1} = \sigma^2 \quad \square$$

---

## Why the Biased Version Is Biased

Define the **biased sample variance**: $$\widetilde{S}_n^2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2$$

From the proof above: $$E[\widetilde{S}_n^2] = \frac{(n-1)\sigma^2}{n} = \sigma^2 - \frac{\sigma^2}{n} < \sigma^2$$

So $\widetilde{S}_n^2$ systematically **underestimates** $\sigma^2$. The bias is $-\sigma^2/n$, which vanishes as $n \to \infty$ but is real for finite samples.

**Intuition for why it underestimates:**  
We are computing deviations from the _sample mean_ $\bar{X}_n$, not from the true mean $\mu$. The sample mean is the point that _minimizes_ the sum of squared deviations from the data â€” it is by definition closer to the data than $\mu$ is (unless $\bar{X}_n = \mu$, which happens with probability zero for continuous distributions). So deviations from $\bar{X}_n$ are systematically smaller than deviations from $\mu$, and we are underestimating $\sigma^2$.

Dividing by $n-1$ instead of $n$ inflates the estimate just enough to correct for this.

---

## Degrees of Freedom: The Right Way to Think About $n-1$

The most conceptually satisfying explanation of $n-1$ is through **degrees of freedom**.

The $n$ residuals $X_1 - \bar{X}_n, X_2 - \bar{X}_n, \ldots, X_n - \bar{X}_n$ satisfy the constraint: $$\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n) = 0$$

This is an identity â€” always true, for any data. So the $n$ residuals lie in an $(n-1)$-dimensional subspace of $\mathbb{R}^n$. They have only $n-1$ **degrees of freedom** â€” only $n-1$ of them can vary freely; the last is determined by the constraint.

We estimated one parameter ($\mu$, replaced by $\bar{X}_n$) from $n$ observations, consuming one degree of freedom. We have $n-1$ left. The sample variance divides by the remaining degrees of freedom.

> _This generalizes. In linear regression with $p$ predictors (including intercept), the residual variance is $\frac{1}{n-p}\sum \hat{\varepsilon}_i^2$, dividing by $n-p$ degrees of freedom â€” one lost per estimated parameter. The $n-1$ here is the special case $p=1$ (just the mean)._

## Properties of $\bar{X}_n$ and $S_n^2$

**Proposition 1.** $E[\bar{X}_n] = \mu$ and $\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \sigma^2/n$.

_Proof._ By linearity of expectation and independence: $$E[\bar{X}_n] = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n E[X_i] = \mu$$ $$\text{Var}(\bar{X}_n) = \frac{1}{n^2}\sum_{i=1}^n \text{Var}(X_i) = \frac{n\sigma^2}{n^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{n} \quad \square$$

**Proposition 2.** $\bar{X}_n$ and $S_n^2$ are **uncorrelated** for any population. They are **independent** when the population is Normal.

_Proof of independence under Normality (sketch)._  
Write $\mathbf{X} = (X_1, \ldots, X_n)^T \sim N(\mu\mathbf{1}, \sigma^2 I)$. The sample mean is $\bar{X}_n = \mathbf{a}^T\mathbf{X}$ for $\mathbf{a} = \frac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}$. The residual vector $(X_i - \bar{X}_n)$ lives in the orthogonal complement of $\mathbf{1}$ in $\mathbb{R}^n$. Since jointly Normal, orthogonality implies independence. And $S_n^2$ is a function of the residuals alone, so $S_n^2 \perp \bar{X}_n$. $\square$

> _This independence is far from obvious and is special to the Normal. It underlies the validity of the $t$-test: the test statistic $\frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{S_n/\sqrt{n}}$ has a $t$-distribution precisely because numerator and denominator are independent._

**Proposition 3.** Under Normality: $\frac{(n-1)S_n^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$.

_Proof (sketch)._  
The residual vector $(X_i - \bar{X}_n)$ is a Normal vector of dimension $n$ constrained to a $(n-1)$-dimensional subspace. Its squared norm, scaled by $1/\sigma^2$, is a sum of $n-1$ squared independent standard Normals â€” i.e., $\chi^2_{n-1}$.

More precisely: $$\frac{(n-1)S_n^2}{\sigma^2} = \frac{1}{\sigma^2}\sum_{i=1}^n(X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n\left(\frac{X_i - \bar{X}_n}{\sigma}\right)^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-1} \quad \square$$

This immediately gives: $$E!\left[\frac{(n-1)S_n^2}{\sigma^2}\right] = n-1 \implies E[S_n^2] = \sigma^2 \quad \checkmark$$

and: $$\text{Var}!\left(\frac{(n-1)S_n^2}{\sigma^2}\right) = 2(n-1) \implies \text{Var}(S_n^2) = \frac{2\sigma^4}{n-1}$$

## Sample Standard Deviation Is Biased

Here is a subtle and frequently overlooked point:

> **$S_n$ is _not_ an unbiased estimator of $\sigma$, even though $S_n^2$ is unbiased for $\sigma^2$.**

By Jensen's inequality, since $x \mapsto \sqrt{x}$ is **concave** for $x > 0$: $$E[S_n] = E[\sqrt{S_n^2}] < \sqrt{E[S_n^2]} = \sqrt{\sigma^2} = \sigma$$

So $S_n$ systematically _underestimates_ $\sigma$. The bias is of order $1/n$ and vanishes asymptotically, but it is real.

Under Normality, the exact correction factor is known. Since $(n-1)S_n^2/\sigma^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$, we can compute $E[S_n]$ exactly using the moments of the $\chi$ distribution:

$$E[S_n] = \sigma \cdot c_n, \quad \text{where } c_n = \sqrt{\frac{2}{n-1}} \cdot \frac{\Gamma(n/2)}{\Gamma((n-1)/2)}$$

An unbiased estimator of $\sigma$ under Normality is therefore $S_n / c_n$. In practice nobody uses this â€” $S_n$ is consistent and the bias is negligible for moderate $n$. But it's good to know that unbiasedness of $S_n^2$ does not pass through the square root.

---

## Consistency

Both estimators are **consistent**: they converge in probability to the true parameter as $n \to \infty$.

**Proposition.** $S_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} \sigma^2$ as $n \to \infty$.

_Proof._  
By the Law of Large Numbers: $$\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 \xrightarrow{p} E[X^2] = \sigma^2 + \mu^2$$ $$\bar{X}_n \xrightarrow{p} \mu \implies \bar{X}_n^2 \xrightarrow{p} \mu^2$$

Now use the computational formula: $$S_n^2 = \frac{n}{n-1}\left(\frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - \bar{X}_n^2\right) \xrightarrow{p} 1 \cdot \left((\sigma^2 + \mu^2) - \mu^2\right) = \sigma^2 \quad \square$$

Similarly $S_n \xrightarrow{p} \sigma$.

---

## The Computational Formula

For hand calculation (less numerically stable but instructive):

$$\sum_{i=1}^n (X_i - \bar{X}_n)^2 = \sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - n\bar{X}_n^2$$

_Proof._ $$\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2 = \sum X_i^2 - 2\bar{X}\sum X_i + n\bar{X}^2 = \sum X_i^2 - 2n\bar{X}^2 + n\bar{X}^2 = \sum X_i^2 - n\bar{X}^2 \quad \square$$

So: $$S_n^2 = \frac{1}{n-1}\left(\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^2 - n\bar{X}_n^2\right)$$

> âš ď¸Ź **Numerical warning.** This formula is algebraically correct but numerically dangerous for floating-point arithmetic when $\sum X_i^2$ and $n\bar{X}^2$ are both large numbers that nearly cancel. Always use the definition $\frac{1}{n-1}\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$ in code, or Welford's online algorithm.

## The $t$-Statistic

The canonical application of sample variance: constructing a pivot for inference on $\mu$ when $\sigma$ is unknown.

Under Normality, define the **one-sample $t$-statistic**: $$T = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{S_n / \sqrt{n}}$$

**Theorem.** $T \sim t_{n-1}$.

_Proof._  
Write: $$T = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \cdot \frac{\sigma/\sqrt{n}}{S_n/\sqrt{n}} = Z \cdot \frac{\sigma}{S_n} = \frac{Z}{\sqrt{V/(n-1)}}$$

where:

- $Z = \frac{\bar{X}_n - \mu}{\sigma/\sqrt{n}} \sim N(0,1)$
- $V = \frac{(n-1)S_n^2}{\sigma^2} \sim \chi^2_{n-1}$
- $Z \perp V$ (by Proposition 2 â€” independence of $\bar{X}_n$ and $S_n^2$ under Normality)

The ratio $Z / \sqrt{V/(n-1)}$ is the definition of a $t_{n-1}$ random variable. $\square$

> _This is the reason the $t$-distribution exists. Without the $n-1$ in Bessel's correction â€” without $S_n^2$ being built the right way â€” this pivot would not have a clean distribution and $t$-tests would not work._

## Things I Keep Getting Confused

> _Honest accounting._

1. **Dividing by $n$ instead of $n-1$ "just because it's the sample size."** No. The $n-1$ is the whole point. The biased version is fine asymptotically but conceptually wrong for finite samples.
    
2. **Thinking $S_n$ is unbiased for $\sigma$ because $S_n^2$ is unbiased for $\sigma^2$.** Jensen kills this. Concavity of $\sqrt{\cdot}$ means $E[S_n] < \sigma$ always.
    
3. **Forgetting that $\bar{X}_n \perp S_n^2$ is special to the Normal.** For a general population they are uncorrelated but not independent. The $t$-test technically requires Normality for exact validity.
    
4. **The degrees of freedom story sounding like a hand-wave.** It's not â€” it's a precise statement about the dimension of the subspace in which the residual vector lives. The constraint $\sum(X_i - \bar{X}_n) = 0$ is exact, not approximate.
    
5. **Using the computational formula in floating-point without thinking.** Catastrophic cancellation is real. Use the two-pass formula or Welford's algorithm when implementing.
