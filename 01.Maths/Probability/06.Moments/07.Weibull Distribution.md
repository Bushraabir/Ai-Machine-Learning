
> _The Weibull is one of those distributions that doesn't show up in the first half of a probability course and then suddenly appears everywhere in applications — reliability engineering, survival analysis, extreme value theory. The reason it's so useful is that its hazard rate is not constant: it can increase, decrease, or stay flat depending on a single shape parameter. That flexibility is the whole story. Understand the hazard rate and you understand the Weibull._

## Motivation

The Exponential distribution models waiting times under the **memoryless** assumption — the hazard rate (instantaneous failure rate) is constant. In reality:

- A new lightbulb is no more likely to fail in the next hour than it was an hour ago. ← Exponential is fine.
- A machine that has been running for 10 years is _more_ likely to fail than a brand new one. ← Increasing hazard. Exponential fails.
- Infant mortality: a product is _less_ likely to fail as it survives longer (early defects weed themselves out). ← Decreasing hazard. Exponential fails.

The Weibull is the minimal generalization that handles all three cases through a single **shape parameter** $k$.

---

## Definition

A random variable $X$ has the **Weibull distribution** with shape $k > 0$ and scale $\lambda > 0$, written $X \sim \text{Weibull}(k, \lambda)$, if its PDF is:

$$\boxed{f_X(x) = \frac{k}{\lambda}\left(\frac{x}{\lambda}\right)^{k-1} e^{-(x/\lambda)^k}, \quad x > 0}$$

and $f_X(x) = 0$ for $x \leq 0$.

The corresponding CDF is:

$$\boxed{F_X(x) = 1 - e^{-(x/\lambda)^k}, \quad x > 0}$$

_Proof that the CDF is correct:_ $$F_X(x) = \int_0^x \frac{k}{\lambda}\left(\frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{k-1} e^{-(t/\lambda)^k}, dt$$

Substitute $u = (t/\lambda)^k$, so $du = \frac{k}{\lambda}(t/\lambda)^{k-1}, dt$:

$$F_X(x) = \int_0^{(x/\lambda)^k} e^{-u}, du = 1 - e^{-(x/\lambda)^k} \quad \square$$

> _The substitution $u = (t/\lambda)^k$ is the key move that makes Weibull integrals tractable. Memorize this substitution — it appears in every Weibull calculation._

---

## The Fundamental Connection to Exponential

This is the most important structural fact about the Weibull.

**Theorem.** $X \sim \text{Weibull}(k, \lambda)$ if and only if $X^k \sim \text{Expo}(1/\lambda^k)$.

Equivalently: if $Y \sim \text{Expo}(1)$, then $X = \lambda Y^{1/k} \sim \text{Weibull}(k, \lambda)$.

_Proof ($\Rightarrow$)._ Let $X \sim \text{Weibull}(k, \lambda)$. Then: $$P(X^k \leq y) = P(X \leq y^{1/k}) = 1 - e^{-(y^{1/k}/\lambda)^k} = 1 - e^{-y/\lambda^k}$$

This is the CDF of $\text{Expo}(1/\lambda^k)$. $\square$

_Proof ($\Leftarrow$)._ Let $Y \sim \text{Expo}(1)$, $X = \lambda Y^{1/k}$. Then: $$P(X \leq x) = P!\left(Y \leq \left(\frac{x}{\lambda}\right)^k\right) = 1 - e^{-(x/\lambda)^k} \quad \square$$

> _This is the right way to think about the Weibull: it is a power transformation of an Exponential. Every property of the Weibull can be derived by applying this transformation to the corresponding Exponential property. This is how I remember the moments — transform, compute for Expo, transform back._

**Simulation corollary.** To simulate $X \sim \text{Weibull}(k, \lambda)$:

1. Generate $U \sim \text{Unif}(0,1)$.
2. Set $X = \lambda(-\log U)^{1/k}$.

This follows from the inverse CDF method: $F_X^{-1}(u) = \lambda(-\log(1-u))^{1/k}$, and $1 - U \overset{d}{=} U$.

## Special Cases

|Parameters|Distribution|
|---|---|
|$k = 1$|$\text{Expo}(1/\lambda)$ — constant hazard|
|$k = 2$|Rayleigh distribution — arises in signal processing|
|$k \approx 3.6$|Approximates $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ in shape|

When $k = 1$: $$f_X(x) = \frac{1}{\lambda}e^{-x/\lambda} \quad \checkmark$$ This is exactly $\text{Expo}(1/\lambda)$ in the parameterization where $\lambda$ is the mean. Perfect.

## Hazard Rate

The **hazard rate** (or **failure rate**) of a continuous distribution is:

$$h(x) = \frac{f_X(x)}{1 - F_X(x)} = \frac{f_X(x)}{S(x)}$$

where $S(x) = P(X > x)$ is the **survival function**. Intuitively, $h(x),dx \approx P(X \in (x, x+dx) \mid X > x)$ — the instantaneous probability of failure given survival to time $x$.

For the Weibull: $$h(x) = \frac{\frac{k}{\lambda}\left(\frac{x}{\lambda}\right)^{k-1} e^{-(x/\lambda)^k}}{e^{-(x/\lambda)^k}} = \frac{k}{\lambda}\left(\frac{x}{\lambda}\right)^{k-1}$$

$$\boxed{h(x) = \frac{k}{\lambda^k} x^{k-1}}$$

This is a **power law** in $x$. The behavior depends entirely on $k$:

- $k < 1$: $h(x)$ is **decreasing** in $x$ — infant mortality, early failures. The longer it has survived, the less likely it is to fail now.
- $k = 1$: $h(x) = 1/\lambda$ is **constant** — memoryless Exponential.
- $k > 1$: $h(x)$ is **increasing** in $x$ — wear and tear, aging. The longer it has survived, the more likely it is to fail now.

> _This is the Weibull's entire raison d'être. It is the parametric family that interpolates between decreasing, constant, and increasing hazard with a single parameter. In reliability engineering, fitting a Weibull to data and estimating $k$ immediately tells you which failure regime you're in._

The **cumulative hazard function** is: $$H(x) = \int_0^x h(t), dt = \left(\frac{x}{\lambda}\right)^k$$

And note $S(x) = e^{-H(x)} = e^{-(x/\lambda)^k}$ — the survival function is the exponential of the negative cumulative hazard. This is a general identity, not special to the Weibull.

## Moments

All moments of the Weibull are expressed using the Gamma function $\Gamma(z) = \int_0^\infty t^{z-1}e^{-t}, dt$.

**General $n$-th moment:**

$$\boxed{E[X^n] = \lambda^n ,\Gamma!\left(1 + \frac{n}{k}\right)}$$

_Proof._ Use the Exponential connection: $X \overset{d}{=} \lambda Y^{1/k}$ where $Y \sim \text{Expo}(1)$.

$$E[X^n] = E[\lambda^n Y^{n/k}] = \lambda^n E[Y^{n/k}]$$

For $Y \sim \text{Expo}(1)$, using LOTUS: $$E[Y^{n/k}] = \int_0^\infty y^{n/k} e^{-y}, dy = \Gamma!\left(\frac{n}{k} + 1\right) \quad \square$$

where the last step is the definition of the Gamma function.

**Mean:** $$E[X] = \lambda,\Gamma!\left(1 + \frac{1}{k}\right)$$

**Second moment:** $$E[X^2] = \lambda^2,\Gamma!\left(1 + \frac{2}{k}\right)$$

**Variance:** $$\text{Var}(X) = \lambda^2\left[\Gamma!\left(1 + \frac{2}{k}\right) - \left(\Gamma!\left(1 + \frac{1}{k}\right)\right)^2\right]$$

_Verification for $k = 1$ (Exponential):_

$$E[X] = \lambda,\Gamma(2) = \lambda \cdot 1! = \lambda \quad \checkmark$$ $$E[X^2] = \lambda^2,\Gamma(3) = \lambda^2 \cdot 2! = 2\lambda^2 \quad \checkmark$$ $$\text{Var}(X) = \lambda^2(2 - 1) = \lambda^2 \quad \checkmark$$

> _In practice, Gamma function values at $1 + n/k$ for non-integer $n/k$ require a table or software. The key special values: $\Gamma(1) = 1$, $\Gamma(1/2) = \sqrt{\pi}$, $\Gamma(n) = (n-1)!$ for positive integers $n$, and the recursion $\Gamma(z+1) = z\Gamma(z)$._

## MGF: Why It Doesn't Have a Closed Form

Unlike the Exponential and Normal, the Weibull does **not** have a closed-form MGF in general.

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}] = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{t^n}{n!} E[X^n] = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(t\lambda)^n}{n!},\Gamma!\left(1 + \frac{n}{k}\right)$$

For $k = 1$ (Exponential), $\Gamma(1 + n) = n!$ and the series collapses to $\sum (t\lambda)^n = 1/(1-t\lambda)$ — the Exponential MGF. For general $k$, no closed form exists.

**Consequence:** We cannot use the MGF to compute Weibull moments. Instead, we use LOTUS directly (as in the proof above) with the Gamma function. This is the correct tool when the MGF fails.

## Median and Mode

**Median.** Solve $F_X(m) = 1/2$: $$1 - e^{-(m/\lambda)^k} = \frac{1}{2} \implies (m/\lambda)^k = \log 2 \implies \boxed{m = \lambda(\log 2)^{1/k}}$$

**Mode.** For $k > 1$, the PDF has an interior maximum. Set $f_X'(x) = 0$:

$$f_X'(x) = 0 \implies \frac{k-1}{x} = \frac{k}{\lambda^k}x^{k-1} \implies x^k = \frac{(k-1)\lambda^k}{k}$$

$$\boxed{x_{\text{mode}} = \lambda\left(\frac{k-1}{k}\right)^{1/k}, \quad k > 1}$$

For $k \leq 1$: the PDF is decreasing on $(0, \infty)$, so the mode is at $x = 0$ (or $+\infty$ if you count the boundary).

## Skewness and Kurtosis

Using the general moment formula $E[X^n] = \lambda^n \Gamma(1 + n/k)$, define for brevity: $$\gamma_n = \Gamma!\left(1 + \frac{n}{k}\right)$$

Then: $$\mu = \lambda\gamma_1, \quad \sigma^2 = \lambda^2(\gamma_2 - \gamma_1^2)$$

**Skewness:** $$\text{Skew}(X) = \frac{\lambda^3(\gamma_3 - 3\gamma_1\gamma_2 + 2\gamma_1^3)}{(\lambda^2(\gamma_2 - \gamma_1^2))^{3/2}} = \frac{\gamma_3 - 3\gamma_1\gamma_2 + 2\gamma_1^3}{(\gamma_2 - \gamma_1^2)^{3/2}}$$

For $k = 1$: $\gamma_n = n!$, giving numerator $6 - 6 + 2 = 2$ and denominator $1$, so $\text{Skew} = 2$ ✓ (Exponential skewness).

The Weibull skewness is always positive for $k < 3.6$ approximately, and approaches zero as $k \to \infty$ (the distribution concentrates and symmetrizes).

**Key qualitative fact:** the Weibull is right-skewed for most practical values of $k$, becoming less skewed as $k$ increases.

---

## Fitting the Weibull: The Log-Transform

A useful practical fact. Taking logs of $X \sim \text{Weibull}(k, \lambda)$:

$$P(\log X \leq y) = P(X \leq e^y) = 1 - e^{-(e^y/\lambda)^k} = 1 - e^{-e^{k(y - \log\lambda)}}$$

This is the CDF of a **Gumbel distribution** (minimum extreme value distribution) with location $\log\lambda$ and scale $1/k$. So:

$$\log X \sim \text{Gumbel}!\left(\log\lambda,, \frac{1}{k}\right)$$

**Practical consequence:** to fit a Weibull to data, take logs of the observations and fit a Gumbel (or equivalently, use a Weibull probability plot — plot $\log(-\log(1 - \hat{F}(x)))$ against $\log x$, which should be linear with slope $k$ and intercept $-k\log\lambda$).

---

## The Weibull in Extreme Value Theory

The Weibull arises naturally in the theory of extremes. The **Weibull distribution** (in the extreme value parameterization) describes the limiting distribution of the **minimum** of many i.i.d. random variables under appropriate conditions — just as the Gumbel describes the maximum.

Specifically: if $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are i.i.d. with a distribution whose left tail behaves like a power law, then $\min(X_1, \ldots, X_n)$, properly normalized, converges in distribution to a Weibull. This is the **Fisher-Tippett-Gnedenko theorem** for minima.

> _This is why the Weibull appears in reliability: we are often interested in the time until the first failure in a system of many components — i.e., the minimum of many component lifetimes._

## Things I Keep Getting Confused

> _Honest accounting._

1. **Parameterization inconsistency.** There are two common parameterizations of the Weibull. Some sources write $f(x) = \frac{k}{\lambda}(x/\lambda)^{k-1}e^{-(x/\lambda)^k}$ (as above, where $\lambda$ is the scale). Others write $f(x) = kx^{k-1}e^{-x^k}$ (absorbing $\lambda$ into a unit-scale version). And some engineering texts use $\lambda$ as a _rate_, not a scale. Always check which convention is in use before computing moments.
    
2. **Forgetting the Gamma function for moments.** There is no MGF shortcut here. The moments require $\Gamma(1 + n/k)$, which for non-integer $k$ and $n$ is a transcendental number. Numerical computation is the norm.
    
3. **Confusing the Weibull CDF with the Exponential CDF.** The Exponential CDF is $1 - e^{-\lambda x}$. The Weibull CDF is $1 - e^{-(x/\lambda)^k}$. The $k$-th power inside the exponent is the entire difference.
    
4. **The hazard rate interpretation.** $h(x)$ is _not_ a probability. It's a rate — it can exceed $1$. The probability of failure in $(x, x+dx)$ given survival to $x$ is approximately $h(x),dx$, not $h(x)$.
    
5. **Assuming the Weibull MGF exists in closed form.** It doesn't, for general $k$. Don't try to differentiate a closed-form MGF that isn't there.
