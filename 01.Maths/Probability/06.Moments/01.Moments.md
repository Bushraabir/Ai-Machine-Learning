## 1. What Even Is a Moment?

Let $X$ be a random variable. The **$n$-th moment** of $X$ is

$$\mu_n' = E[X^n]$$

provided this expectation exists (i.e., $E[|X|^n] < \infty$).

The **$n$-th central moment** is

$$\mu_n = E[(X - \mu)^n], \quad \mu = E[X]$$

So the first central moment is always 0. The second is the variance. The third (normalized) is skewness. The fourth (normalized) gives kurtosis.

**Why central moments?** They're translation-invariant — shifting $X$ by a constant doesn't change them (except trivially for $\mu_1$). They tell us about _shape_, not location.

## 2. Existence of Moments

This is the part most courses gloss over. Worth pinning down carefully.

**Proposition.** If $E[|X|^n] < \infty$, then $E[|X|^k] < \infty$ for all $k \leq n$.

_Proof._ By Jensen's inequality (since $t \mapsto t^{k/n}$ is concave for $k < n$) applied to $|X|^n$... or more cleanly: use the fact that for any $a > 0$,

$$|x|^k = |x|^k \cdot \mathbf{1}_{|x| \leq 1} + |x|^k \cdot \mathbf{1}_{|x| > 1} \leq 1 + |x|^n$$

So $E[|X|^k] \leq 1 + E[|X|^n] < \infty$. $\square$

**Moral:** higher moments are "harder to have." A distribution with finite 4th moment automatically has finite variance. Not the other way around.

**Counterexample to keep in mind:** Cauchy distribution. No moments at all — not even the first. Fat tails destroy everything.

## 3. Moment Generating Function (MGF)

The **MGF** of $X$ is defined as

$$M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}], \quad t \in \mathbb{R}$$

whenever this expectation is finite in an open interval around $t = 0$.

### Why this works

Expand $e^{tX}$ as a power series:

$$M_X(t) = E\left[\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(tX)^n}{n!}\right] = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{t^n}{n!} E[X^n]$$

(interchange of $E$ and $\sum$ justified by dominated convergence when the MGF is finite near 0.)

**Key formula:**

$$\boxed{E[X^n] = M_X^{(n)}(0)}$$

i.e., differentiate $n$ times and evaluate at $t = 0$. This is what makes the MGF powerful — it _encodes all moments_ in a single analytic function.

### MGF Uniqueness Theorem

> If $M_X(t) = M_Y(t)$ for all $t$ in some open interval around $0$, then $X$ and $Y$ have the same distribution.

This is non-trivial — it says the MGF (when it exists) _uniquely determines_ the distribution. The proof runs through the theory of moment problems (Hamburger's theorem). I keep meaning to look at this more carefully.

### MGF of a sum

If $X \perp Y$, then $M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t) \cdot M_Y(t)$.

_Proof._ $E[e^{t(X+Y)}] = E[e^{tX} e^{tY}] = E[e^{tX}] \cdot E[e^{tY}]$ by independence. $\square$

This is how you prove the sum of independent normals is normal, sum of independent Poissons is Poisson, etc. Clean and uniform.

### When the MGF doesn't exist

Log-normal distribution: all moments are finite, but the MGF is infinite for every $t > 0$. So finiteness of all moments does **not** guarantee existence of the MGF. Good thing to remember.

## 4. Cumulant Generating Function (CGF)

Define

$$K_X(t) = \log M_X(t) = \log E[e^{tX}]$$

The coefficients of the Taylor expansion of $K_X$ at $0$ are the **cumulants** $\kappa_n$:

$$K_X(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \kappa_n \frac{t^n}{n!}$$

The first few:

- $\kappa_1 = E[X]$ (mean)
- $\kappa_2 = \text{Var}(X)$
- $\kappa_3 = E[(X - \mu)^3]$ (3rd central moment = skewness numerator)

**Why cumulants?** For independent $X, Y$:

$$K_{X+Y}(t) = K_X(t) + K_Y(t)$$

So cumulants _add_ under independence. Moments don't — they mix via the binomial theorem. For the normal distribution specifically, $\kappa_n = 0$ for all $n \geq 3$. This is actually a characterization of normality.

I find the CGF more elegant for theoretical work. In practice, MGF is what you compute with.

## 5. Method of Moments (MoM)

A classical estimation technique. The idea: match theoretical moments to empirical (sample) moments.

**Setup.** Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{iid}{\sim} F_\theta$ where $\theta = (\theta_1, \ldots, \theta_k)$ is unknown.

The $j$-th **population moment**: $\mu_j(\theta) = E[X^j]$

The $j$-th **sample moment**: $\hat{\mu}_j = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^n X_i^j$

**Method of Moments estimator $\hat{\theta}_{MoM}$**: solve the system

$$\mu_j(\theta) = \hat{\mu}_j, \quad j = 1, \ldots, k$$

for $\theta$.

### Example: Normal$(\mu, \sigma^2)$

Two parameters, so use two moments: $$\mu_1(\theta) = \mu = \hat{\mu}_1 = \bar{X}$$ $$\mu_2(\theta) = \mu^2 + \sigma^2 = \hat{\mu}_2 = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i^2$$

Solving: $\hat{\mu}_{MoM} = \bar{X}$, $\hat{\sigma}^2_{MoM} = \frac{1}{n}\sum(X_i - \bar{X})^2$.

Note: this is _biased_ for $\sigma^2$ (divides by $n$, not $n-1$). MoM doesn't guarantee unbiasedness — but by LLN, $\hat{\mu}_j \to \mu_j$ a.s., so MoM estimators are typically consistent.

**MoM vs MLE.** MoM is computationally simpler but generally less efficient (higher asymptotic variance) than MLE. There are distributions where MLE is hard to compute and MoM is the practical choice.

## 6. Moment Inequalities

The two fundamental inequalities — these are everywhere.

### Markov's Inequality

> Let $X \geq 0$ and $a > 0$. Then: $$P(X \geq a) \leq \frac{E[X]}{a}$$

_Proof._ $$E[X] = E[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}] + E[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X < a}] \geq E[X \cdot \mathbf{1}_{X \geq a}] \geq a \cdot P(X \geq a)$$

Divide both sides by $a$. $\square$

This proof is three lines but I still find it beautiful. You're just throwing away mass and using monotonicity.

**Note:** Requires $X \geq 0$. The bound is sharp — there exist distributions achieving equality.

### Chebyshev's Inequality

> For any $X$ with finite mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, and any $k > 0$: $$P(|X - \mu| \geq k) \leq \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}$$

_Proof._ Apply Markov to $Y = (X - \mu)^2 \geq 0$ with $a = k^2$:

$$P((X-\mu)^2 \geq k^2) \leq \frac{E[(X-\mu)^2]}{k^2} = \frac{\sigma^2}{k^2}$$

Note $(X-\mu)^2 \geq k^2 \iff |X - \mu| \geq k$. $\square$

**Common form:** With $k = c\sigma$:

$$P(|X - \mu| \geq c\sigma) \leq \frac{1}{c^2}$$

So at least $1 - 1/c^2$ of the probability mass lies within $c$ standard deviations of the mean — for _any_ distribution with finite variance. No normality assumption needed.

**Tightness:** Chebyshev is also sharp. Achieve equality with a two-point distribution: $P(X = \mu \pm k) = \sigma^2/(2k^2)$, $P(X = \mu) = 1 - \sigma^2/k^2$.

### The General Pattern

Both follow from the same trick: apply Markov to a non-negative function of $X$. This generalizes: Markov applied to $e^{tX}$ gives **Chernoff bounds**, which are exponentially tighter in the tails. That's a whole other note.

## 7. Connections I Keep Thinking About

- **CLT via MGFs:** Under conditions guaranteeing the MGF of $(X_i - \mu)/\sigma$ exists near 0, one can prove CLT by showing the MGF of the normalized sum converges to $e^{t^2/2}$ (the standard normal MGF). Elegant.
    
- **Moment problem:** When do the moments uniquely determine a distribution? The **Carleman condition** $\sum_{n=1}^\infty \mu_{2n}^{-1/(2n)} = \infty$ is sufficient. Log-normal fails this — a cautionary tale.
    
- **Variance of sample mean:** $\text{Var}(\bar{X}) = \sigma^2/n$ follows immediately from the second moment. This is why large samples concentrate around the mean — Chebyshev quantifies exactly how fast.
