> _Skewness was about which side the tail pulls toward. Kurtosis is about how heavy the tails are — how often the distribution produces extreme values. I find this one harder to visualize, and I think most people secretly do too. The "peakedness" intuition is wrong. Write this carefully._


## The Core Idea

Variance measures spread. Skewness measures asymmetry. But two distributions can share a mean, variance, and skewness, yet still differ in how often they produce extreme outliers. **Kurtosis captures tail heaviness** — the propensity of the distribution to generate values far from the mean.

The standard reference point is the **Normal distribution**, which has kurtosis $3$. Almost everything is defined relative to this benchmark, via **excess kurtosis** $= \text{Kurt}(X) - 3$.

> _The Normal as benchmark is not arbitrary — it's the distribution that the CLT pushes everything toward, so it's the natural "neutral" shape. Distributions with heavier tails than the Normal have excess kurtosis $> 0$; lighter tails, $< 0$._

---

## Definition

Let $X$ be a random variable with $\mu = E[X]$, $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}(X) > 0$, and $E[X^4] < \infty$.

$$\boxed{\text{Kurt}(X) = E!\left[\left(\frac{X-\mu}{\sigma}\right)^{!4}\right] = \frac{E[(X-\mu)^4]}{\sigma^4}}$$

The **excess kurtosis** (what most software and most people actually mean when they say "kurtosis") is:

$$\boxed{\widetilde{\kappa}(X) = \text{Kurt}(X) - 3 = \frac{\kappa_4}{\kappa_2^2}}$$

where $\kappa_4$ and $\kappa_2$ are the fourth and second cumulants respectively.

### Why the Fourth Moment?

- **Even power**: $(X - \mu)^4 \geq 0$ always, so kurtosis is always non-negative. Unlike skewness (which uses an odd power to detect direction), kurtosis uses an even power because it measures magnitude of deviation, not direction.
- **Fourth power**: Points far from the mean contribute _quartically_. A deviation of $2\sigma$ contributes $16\times$ more than a deviation of $\sigma$. Kurtosis is exquisitely sensitive to rare extreme events — far more than variance.
- **Why not just use $E[(X-\mu)^4]$?** Same reason as skewness — without standardization by $\sigma^4$, the quantity has units $[X]^4$ and changes under rescaling. We want a pure shape descriptor.

### Why subtract 3?

Because $\text{Kurt}(N(\mu, \sigma^2)) = 3$ for any $\mu, \sigma^2$. Subtracting $3$ recenters so the Normal has excess kurtosis $0$, making deviations from Normal-tail-behavior immediately readable. Distributions with $\widetilde{\kappa} > 0$ are called **leptokurtic** (heavy-tailed); $\widetilde{\kappa} < 0$ are **platykurtic** (light-tailed); $\widetilde{\kappa} = 0$ is **mesokurtic** (Normal-like tails).

## Properties

**Proposition 1 (Affine invariance).**  
For constants $a \neq 0$ and $b \in \mathbb{R}$: $$\text{Kurt}(aX + b) = \text{Kurt}(X)$$

_Proof._  
Let $Y = aX + b$, so $E[Y] = a\mu + b$ and $\text{SD}(Y) = |a|\sigma$. Then: $$\text{Kurt}(Y) = \frac{E[(aX - a\mu)^4]}{(|a|\sigma)^4} = \frac{a^4 E[(X-\mu)^4]}{a^4 \sigma^4} = \text{Kurt}(X). \quad \square$$

**Proposition 2 (Non-negativity and lower bound).**  
$$\text{Kurt}(X) \geq 1$$

_Proof._  
By the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality applied to $E[(X-\mu)^2 \cdot 1]$: $$\left(E[(X-\mu)^2]\right)^2 \leq E[(X-\mu)^4] \cdot E[1] = E[(X-\mu)^4]$$ So $\sigma^4 \leq E[(X-\mu)^4]$, i.e. $\text{Kurt}(X) \geq 1$.

Equivalently, by Jensen's inequality: since $x \mapsto x^2$ is convex, $$E[(X-\mu)^4] = E!\left[((X-\mu)^2)^2\right] \geq \left(E[(X-\mu)^2]\right)^2 = \sigma^4. \quad \square$$

> _So excess kurtosis $\geq -2$ always. The lower bound $\widetilde{\kappa} = -2$ is achieved only by a Bernoulli$(1/2)$ — the most "light-tailed" possible distribution on two points._

**Proposition 3 (Symmetry is not implied by zero excess kurtosis).**  
Just as with skewness, $\widetilde{\kappa}(X) = 0$ does not imply $X$ is Normal or even symmetric. It only means the fourth cumulant vanishes. The Normal is characterized by _all_ cumulants of order $\geq 3$ vanishing simultaneously.

## The Peakedness Misconception

> _This tripped me up for longer than I'd like to admit._

Kurtosis is often described as measuring "peakedness" — how sharp the central peak of the density is. **This is misleading at best, wrong at worst.**

The correct interpretation is **tail heaviness**. Here is why the peak story fails:

You can construct two distributions with identical variances where the "more peaked" one (sharper center) also has _lighter_ tails — contradicting the supposed peak-kurtosis link. The fourth moment is dominated by the tails, not the center. A point at $5\sigma$ from the mean contributes $5^4 = 625$ times more than a point at $\sigma$. The bulk of the distribution near the mean contributes almost nothing to $E[(X-\mu)^4]$.

**Correct mental model:**  
High kurtosis $=$ the distribution occasionally produces extreme outliers. The tails have more probability mass than the Normal. The "peak" may or may not be sharp — that's a separate feature. Think of the $t$-distribution with small degrees of freedom: heavy tails, occasional wild values, kurtosis $\to \infty$ as $\nu \to 4^+$.

## Relation to the CGF

As with skewness, the CGF is the cleanest framework. Let $K(t) = \log E[e^{tX}]$ with cumulants $\kappa_n = K^{(n)}(0)$.

The fourth cumulant is: $$\kappa_4 = \mu_4' - 4\kappa_3\kappa_1 - 3\kappa_2^2 - \kappa_1^4 = E[(X-\mu)^4] - 3\sigma^4$$

where $\mu_4'$ denotes the fourth raw moment. Rearranging:

$$E[(X-\mu)^4] = \kappa_4 + 3\kappa_2^2$$

Therefore: $$\text{Kurt}(X) = \frac{\kappa_4 + 3\kappa_2^2}{\kappa_2^2} = \frac{\kappa_4}{\kappa_2^2} + 3$$

And so: $$\boxed{\widetilde{\kappa}(X) = \frac{\kappa_4}{\kappa_2^2} = \frac{K^{(4)}(0)}{(K''(0))^2}}$$

**For independent $X, Y$:** $$\kappa_4(X + Y) = \kappa_4(X) + \kappa_4(Y)$$

For i.i.d. sum $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$: $$\widetilde{\kappa}(S_n) = \frac{n\kappa_4}{(n\kappa_2)^2} = \frac{\kappa_4}{\kappa_2^2} \cdot \frac{1}{n}$$

**The excess kurtosis decays like $1/n$.** Even faster than skewness ($1/\sqrt{n}$). The CLT is once again visible: $S_n$ approaches Normality ($\widetilde{\kappa} = 0$) as $n \to \infty$, and the fourth cumulant is the next correction term.

## Computing Kurtosis: The Expansion Formula

$$E[(X-\mu)^4] = E[X^4] - 4\mu E[X^3] + 6\mu^2 E[X^2] - 3\mu^4$$

_Proof._ Expand $(X - \mu)^4$ via the binomial theorem: $$(X-\mu)^4 = X^4 - 4\mu X^3 + 6\mu^2 X^2 - 4\mu^3 X + \mu^4$$ Take expectations and use $E[X] = \mu$: $$E[(X-\mu)^4] = E[X^4] - 4\mu E[X^3] + 6\mu^2 E[X^2] - 4\mu^3\cdot\mu + \mu^4$$ $$= E[X^4] - 4\mu E[X^3] + 6\mu^2 E[X^2] - 3\mu^4. \quad \square$$

In practice, for standardized $X$ (i.e., $\mu = 0$, $\sigma = 1$), this collapses to just $E[X^4]$, which is why the standardization step is always worth doing first.

## Kurtosis of Named Distributions

### Normal Distribution $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$

$$\text{Kurt}(X) = 3, \qquad \widetilde{\kappa}(X) = 0$$

_Proof via MGF._  
WLOG take $X \sim N(0,1)$. The MGF is $M(t) = e^{t^2/2}$, so the CGF is: $$K(t) = \frac{t^2}{2}$$ All derivatives of order $\geq 3$ vanish identically: $K^{(n)}(t) = 0$ for $n \geq 3$. So $\kappa_n = 0$ for all $n \geq 3$. In particular $\kappa_4 = 0$, giving $\widetilde{\kappa} = 0$. $\square$

> _This is the cumulant characterization of the Normal: it is the unique distribution (up to location and scale) whose CGF is a polynomial — specifically, a degree-2 polynomial. All higher cumulants vanish._

---

### Exponential Distribution $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$

The CGF of Expo$(\lambda)$ is: $$K(t) = -\log!\left(1 - \frac{t}{\lambda}\right), \quad t < \lambda$$

Differentiating: $$K'(t) = \frac{1}{\lambda - t}, \quad K''(t) = \frac{1}{(\lambda-t)^2}, \quad K'''(t) = \frac{2}{(\lambda-t)^3}, \quad K^{(4)}(t) = \frac{6}{(\lambda-t)^4}$$

At $t = 0$: $\kappa_2 = 1/\lambda^2$, $\kappa_4 = 6/\lambda^4$.

$$\widetilde{\kappa}(X) = \frac{6/\lambda^4}{(1/\lambda^2)^2} = \frac{6/\lambda^4}{1/\lambda^4} = \boxed{6}$$

The Exponential has excess kurtosis $6$, independent of $\lambda$. Heavy-tailed relative to the Normal — those rare large values really do contribute.

---

### Gamma Distribution $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$

By cumulant additivity (Gamma$(a,\lambda)$ is the sum of $a$ i.i.d. Expo$(\lambda)$ variables): $$\kappa_4 = a \cdot \frac{6}{\lambda^4}, \qquad \kappa_2 = \frac{a}{\lambda^2}$$

$$\widetilde{\kappa}(X) = \frac{6a/\lambda^4}{(a/\lambda^2)^2} = \frac{6a/\lambda^4}{a^2/\lambda^4} = \frac{6}{a}$$

$$\boxed{\widetilde{\kappa}(\text{Gamma}(a,\lambda)) = \frac{6}{a}}$$

Decays like $1/n$ — exactly the cumulant additivity prediction. As $a \to \infty$, Gamma $\to$ Normal and $\widetilde{\kappa} \to 0$.

---

### Poisson Distribution $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$

The CGF is $K(t) = \lambda(e^t - 1)$. All derivatives at $0$ equal $\lambda$: $\kappa_n = \lambda$ for all $n \geq 1$.

$$\widetilde{\kappa}(X) = \frac{\kappa_4}{\kappa_2^2} = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda^2} = \frac{1}{\lambda}$$

$$\boxed{\widetilde{\kappa}(\text{Pois}(\lambda)) = \frac{1}{\lambda}}$$

Again decays like $1/n$ as $\lambda \to \infty$ (Pois$(\lambda)$ is the sum of $\lambda$ i.i.d. Pois$(1)$ variables).

---

### Uniform Distribution $X \sim \text{Unif}(a, b)$

$$E[(X-\mu)^4] = \frac{(b-a)^4}{80}, \qquad \sigma^4 = \frac{(b-a)^4}{144}$$

$$\text{Kurt}(X) = \frac{(b-a)^4/80}{(b-a)^4/144} = \frac{144}{80} = \frac{9}{5}$$

$$\boxed{\widetilde{\kappa}(\text{Unif}(a,b)) = \frac{9}{5} - 3 = -\frac{6}{5}}$$

Negative excess kurtosis — the Uniform is **platykurtic**. Its tails are as light as possible (the distribution has bounded support), so it produces fewer extreme values than the Normal would with the same variance. This makes sense: there are no tails at all beyond $[a,b]$.

_Proof of the fourth central moment:_  
WLOG $X \sim \text{Unif}(-1/2, 1/2)$ (by affine invariance, kurtosis is the same). $$E[X^4] = \int_{-1/2}^{1/2} x^4, dx = 2\int_0^{1/2} x^4, dx = 2 \cdot \frac{x^5}{5}\Big|_0^{1/2} = \frac{2}{5 \cdot 32} = \frac{1}{80}$$ And $\sigma^2 = 1/12$, so $\sigma^4 = 1/144$. Then $\text{Kurt} = (1/80)/(1/144) = 144/80 = 9/5$. $\square$

---

### Bernoulli Distribution $X \sim \text{Bern}(p)$

$$E[(X-\mu)^4] = p(1-p)^4 + (1-p)p^4 = p(1-p)\left[(1-p)^3 + p^3\right]$$

Use the identity $(1-p)^3 + p^3 = 1 - 3p(1-p)$: $$E[(X-\mu)^4] = p(1-p)(1 - 3p(1-p))$$

And $\sigma^4 = (p(1-p))^2$. So: $$\text{Kurt}(X) = \frac{p(1-p)(1-3p(1-p))}{p^2(1-p)^2} = \frac{1 - 3p(1-p)}{p(1-p)}$$

$$\widetilde{\kappa}(X) = \frac{1-3p(1-p)}{p(1-p)} - 3 = \frac{1 - 3p(1-p) - 3p(1-p)}{p(1-p)} = \frac{1-6p(1-p)}{p(1-p)}$$

$$\boxed{\widetilde{\kappa}(\text{Bern}(p)) = \frac{1}{p(1-p)} - 6}$$

At $p = 1/2$: $\widetilde{\kappa} = \frac{1}{1/4} - 6 = 4 - 6 = -2$. This is the **global minimum** of excess kurtosis — consistent with Proposition 2 ($\text{Kurt} \geq 1$, i.e. $\widetilde{\kappa} \geq -2$).

As $p \to 0$ or $p \to 1$: $\widetilde{\kappa} \to \infty$. The Bernoulli becomes highly kurtotic when one outcome is very rare — the occasional occurrence of the rare event is the "extreme outlier."

## The $t$-Distribution: Kurtosis in the Wild

The $t$-distribution with $\nu$ degrees of freedom has:

$$\widetilde{\kappa}(t_\nu) = \frac{6}{\nu - 4}, \quad \nu > 4$$

For $\nu \leq 4$, the fourth moment does not exist and kurtosis is undefined. For $\nu > 4$:

- As $\nu \to \infty$: $\widetilde{\kappa} \to 0$ (the $t$ converges to Normal).
- As $\nu \to 4^+$: $\widetilde{\kappa} \to \infty$ (tails become so heavy the fourth moment barely exists).

This is why the $t$-distribution is the canonical example of a heavy-tailed distribution — and why it matters in statistics, where we use it precisely when we're worried about outliers.

## The CLT Through the Lens of Cumulants

For i.i.d. $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ with mean $\mu$, variance $\sigma^2$, let $\bar{X}_n = \frac{1}{n}\sum X_i$ (standardized appropriately). The cumulants of the standardized sum satisfy:

$$\kappa_r!\left(\frac{S_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}\right) = \frac{\kappa_r(X)}{\sigma^r \cdot n^{r/2 - 1}}$$

So:

- $\kappa_1 = 0$ (centered)
- $\kappa_2 = 1$ (unit variance)
- $\kappa_3 \sim 1/\sqrt{n} \to 0$ (skewness vanishes)
- $\kappa_4 \sim 1/n \to 0$ (excess kurtosis vanishes)
- Higher cumulants vanish even faster

The Normal has $\kappa_r = 0$ for all $r \geq 3$. The CLT says that as $n \to \infty$, every cumulant of order $\geq 3$ vanishes, and we're left with a distribution characterized only by $\kappa_1$ and $\kappa_2$ — i.e., a Normal. **Kurtosis going to zero is part of what it means to converge to normality.**

## Things I Keep Getting Confused

> _Honest accounting._

1. **"High kurtosis means a sharp peak."** No. High kurtosis means heavy tails. The peak is irrelevant — the fourth moment is dominated by distant values.
    
2. **Forgetting that kurtosis of Normal is $3$, not $0$.** When computing, I need $\text{Kurt}(X) - 3$ for excess kurtosis. Several times I've checked a computation against $0$ when I should check against $3$.
    
3. **Kurtosis undefined when $E[X^4] = \infty$.** The Cauchy distribution has no finite moments at all — kurtosis undefined. The $t_3$ distribution has finite variance but no fourth moment — kurtosis undefined. Always check existence first.
    
4. **Confusing "leptokurtic" and "platykurtic."** Mnemonic: _lepto_ = thin (the tails are thin in the _center_, fat at the _extremes_) — so leptokurtic = heavy tails. _Platy_ = flat (the distribution is flatter, more spread, lighter tails). Actually I just remember: $\widetilde{\kappa} > 0$ is leptokurtic. That's enough.
