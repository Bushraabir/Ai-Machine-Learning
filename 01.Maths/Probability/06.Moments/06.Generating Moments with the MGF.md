

> _This is the computational heart of the MGF — the part where you actually sit down and grind out $E[X^n]$ without doing a single integral by brute force. Once you have the MGF of a distribution, you own all its moments. The skill is knowing which method — differentiation or series expansion — is cleaner for a given distribution. I keep a mental ranking. Write that down here._


## The Core Principle

If $M_X(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ exists on an open interval around $0$, then the $n$-th moment of $X$ is recovered by:

$$\boxed{E[X^n] = M_X^{(n)}(0)}$$

Two methods to extract $E[X^n]$ from $M_X(t)$:

1. **Differentiate directly** — take $n$ derivatives, evaluate at $t = 0$.
2. **Expand as a power series** — read off the coefficient of $t^n/n!$.

Both are always valid. The question is which is less painful for the distribution at hand. Method 1 is better when $M(t)$ has a clean closed form that differentiates nicely. Method 2 is better when $M(t)$ is already a known series or has a form that expands cleanly.

---

## Method 1: Direct Differentiation

### Setup

$$M_X'(t) = E[Xe^{tX}] \implies M_X'(0) = E[X]$$ $$M_X''(t) = E[X^2 e^{tX}] \implies M_X''(0) = E[X^2]$$ $$M_X^{(n)}(t) = E[X^n e^{tX}] \implies M_X^{(n)}(0) = E[X^n]$$

The variance then follows from: $$\text{Var}(X) = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = M''(0) - (M'(0))^2$$

---

### Example 1: Exponential $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$

$$M_X(t) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t} = \lambda(\lambda - t)^{-1}, \quad t < \lambda$$

Differentiate: $$M'(t) = \lambda(\lambda-t)^{-2}$$ $$M''(t) = 2\lambda(\lambda-t)^{-3}$$ $$M^{(n)}(t) = n!,\lambda(\lambda-t)^{-(n+1)}$$

At $t = 0$:

$$\boxed{E[X^n] = \frac{n!}{\lambda^n}}$$

_Verification:_ $E[X] = 1/\lambda$ ✓, $E[X^2] = 2/\lambda^2$ ✓, $\text{Var}(X) = 2/\lambda^2 - 1/\lambda^2 = 1/\lambda^2$ ✓.

The pattern $E[X^n] = n!/\lambda^n$ is worth knowing. It follows from the MGF with almost no effort. Doing this integral directly — $$E[X^n] = \int_0^\infty x^n \lambda e^{-\lambda x},dx$$ — requires integration by parts $n$ times or the Gamma function. The MGF does it in one line.

---

### Example 2: Normal $Z \sim N(0,1)$

$$M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2}$$

Differentiating this repeatedly is annoying because of the chain rule compounding. Better to use Method 2 here — but let's do the first few to see the pattern:

$$M'(t) = te^{t^2/2}$$ $$M''(t) = e^{t^2/2} + t^2 e^{t^2/2} = (1 + t^2)e^{t^2/2}$$ $$M'''(t) = (3t + t^3)e^{t^2/2}$$ $$M^{(4)}(t) = (3 + 6t^2 + t^4)e^{t^2/2}$$

At $t = 0$: $$E[Z] = 0, \quad E[Z^2] = 1, \quad E[Z^3] = 0, \quad E[Z^4] = 3$$

This quickly becomes messy. The series method is far cleaner for the Normal.

---

### Example 3: Binomial $X \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$

$$M_X(t) = (q + pe^t)^n, \quad q = 1-p$$

$$M'(t) = n(q+pe^t)^{n-1} \cdot pe^t$$

At $t = 0$, using $q + p = 1$: $$E[X] = n \cdot 1^{n-1} \cdot p = np \quad \checkmark$$

For $E[X^2]$, differentiate $M'(t)$: $$M''(t) = n(n-1)(q+pe^t)^{n-2}(pe^t)^2 + n(q+pe^t)^{n-1}pe^t$$

At $t = 0$: $$E[X^2] = n(n-1)p^2 + np$$

Therefore: $$\text{Var}(X) = n(n-1)p^2 + np - n^2p^2 = np - np^2 = np(1-p) = npq \quad \checkmark$$

> _The trick here: always simplify $q + pe^t\big|_{t=0} = q + p = 1$ first, then collect terms. This avoids carrying unnecessary algebra._

## Method 2: Power Series Expansion

The MGF has the power series: $$M_X(t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{E[X^n]}{n!}, t^n$$

So if we can expand $M_X(t)$ as a power series around $t = 0$, we read off $E[X^n] = n! \cdot [t^n]M_X(t)$ directly. No differentiation needed.

---

### Example 4: Normal $Z \sim N(0,1)$

$$M_Z(t) = e^{t^2/2} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(t^2/2)^k}{k!} = \sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{t^{2k}}{2^k k!}$$

This is a power series in $t^2$ only — all odd powers of $t$ are absent, confirming $E[Z^{2k+1}] = 0$ for all $k \geq 0$.

For even moments, the coefficient of $t^{2k}$ is $\frac{1}{2^k k!}$, so:

$$E[Z^{2k}] = (2k)! \cdot \frac{1}{2^k k!} = \frac{(2k)!}{2^k k!}$$

$$\boxed{E[Z^{2k}] = (2k-1)!! = 1 \cdot 3 \cdot 5 \cdots (2k-1)}$$

where $(2k-1)!! $ is the double factorial. Explicitly:

|$k$|$E[Z^{2k}]$|Value|
|---|---|---|
|$0$|$1$|$1$|
|$1$|$1$|$1$|
|$2$|$3!! = 1\cdot 3$|$3$|
|$3$|$5!! = 1\cdot 3\cdot 5$|$15$|
|$4$|$7!! = 1\cdot 3\cdot 5\cdot 7$|$105$|

_This is the cleanest derivation of the Normal moments. No integrals, no Hermite polynomials, no recursion. Just expand $e^{t^2/2}$ and read off the answer._

The kurtosis check: $E[Z^4] = 3$ ✓, so $\text{Kurt}(Z) = 3$ ✓.

---

### Example 5: Poisson $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$

$$M_X(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$$

To expand, first expand $e^t - 1$: $$e^t - 1 = t + \frac{t^2}{2!} + \frac{t^3}{3!} + \cdots$$

So: $$M_X(t) = e^{\lambda\left(t + \frac{t^2}{2} + \frac{t^3}{6} + \cdots\right)}$$

For the first two moments, we only need terms up to $t^2$: $$M_X(t) = \exp!\left(\lambda t + \frac{\lambda t^2}{2} + O(t^3)\right)$$

Use $e^{a+b} \approx 1 + (a+b) + \frac{(a+b)^2}{2} + \cdots$ with $a = \lambda t$ and $b = \lambda t^2/2$:

$$M_X(t) = 1 + \lambda t + \frac{\lambda t^2}{2} + \frac{\lambda^2 t^2}{2} + O(t^3) = 1 + \lambda t + \frac{\lambda + \lambda^2}{2}t^2 + O(t^3)$$

Reading off: $$E[X] = \lambda \quad \checkmark$$ $$E[X^2] = \lambda + \lambda^2 \implies \text{Var}(X) = \lambda + \lambda^2 - \lambda^2 = \lambda \quad \checkmark$$

> _For Poisson, the cleaner path to $E[X]$ and $\text{Var}(X)$ is differentiation. But for higher moments like $E[X^3]$, the series expansion keeps all the bookkeeping in one place._

---

### Example 6: Gamma $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$

$$M_X(t) = \left(1 - \frac{t}{\lambda}\right)^{-a}, \quad t < \lambda$$

Use the generalized binomial series $(1 - u)^{-a} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \binom{a+n-1}{n} u^n$ with $u = t/\lambda$:

$$M_X(t) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \binom{a+n-1}{n} \frac{t^n}{\lambda^n}$$

Reading off the coefficient of $t^n/n!$:

$$E[X^n] = \frac{n!}{\lambda^n}\binom{a+n-1}{n} = \frac{n!}{\lambda^n} \cdot \frac{(a+n-1)!}{n!(a-1)!} = \frac{(a+n-1)!}{\lambda^n (a-1)!}$$

For integer $a$, using the rising factorial (Pochhammer symbol) $a^{(n)} = a(a+1)\cdots(a+n-1)$:

$$\boxed{E[X^n] = \frac{a(a+1)\cdots(a+n-1)}{\lambda^n} = \frac{a^{(n)}}{\lambda^n}}$$

_Check for $n=1$:_ $E[X] = a/\lambda$ ✓  
_Check for $n=2$:_ $E[X^2] = a(a+1)/\lambda^2$, so $\text{Var}(X) = a(a+1)/\lambda^2 - a^2/\lambda^2 = a/\lambda^2$ ✓

## Combining Both Methods: A Worked Example

**Problem.** Let $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$. Find $E[X^3]$ and $E[X^4]$.

**Step 1.** Write $X = \mu + \sigma Z$ where $Z \sim N(0,1)$. By the binomial theorem:

$$E[X^3] = E[(\mu + \sigma Z)^3] = \mu^3 + 3\mu^2\sigma E[Z] + 3\mu\sigma^2 E[Z^2] + \sigma^3 E[Z^3]$$ $$= \mu^3 + 0 + 3\mu\sigma^2 + 0 = \mu^3 + 3\mu\sigma^2$$

$$E[X^4] = E[(\mu + \sigma Z)^4] = \mu^4 + 4\mu^3\sigma E[Z] + 6\mu^2\sigma^2 E[Z^2] + 4\mu\sigma^3 E[Z^3] + \sigma^4 E[Z^4]$$ $$= \mu^4 + 0 + 6\mu^2\sigma^2 + 0 + 3\sigma^4 = \mu^4 + 6\mu^2\sigma^2 + 3\sigma^4$$

**Step 2.** The Normal moments $E[Z^k]$ come from the series expansion of $e^{t^2/2}$ computed above. No integrals needed at any stage.

> _The strategy: reduce general $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ moments to standard $N(0,1)$ moments via the affine transformation, then use the MGF series to read off $E[Z^k]$. This is almost always the cleanest path._

## The Log-Trick for Variance

For any distribution, there is a slick way to extract the variance directly from the MGF without computing $E[X^2]$ and $E[X]$ separately. Define:

$$K(t) = \log M_X(t) \quad \text{(the CGF)}$$

Then: $$K'(t) = \frac{M'(t)}{M(t)} \implies K'(0) = \frac{M'(0)}{M(0)} = \frac{E[X]}{1} = E[X]$$

$$K''(t) = \frac{M''(t)M(t) - (M'(t))^2}{M(t)^2} \implies K''(0) = M''(0) - (M'(0))^2 = E[X^2] - (E[X])^2 = \text{Var}(X)$$

$$\boxed{K'(0) = E[X], \qquad K''(0) = \text{Var}(X)}$$

This is the CGF doing what it does best — the second derivative gives the variance directly, without the subtraction step. For distributions where $\log M(t)$ is simpler than $M(t)$ (e.g., Normal, where $K(t) = \mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2$ is a polynomial), this is far more efficient.

---

## Practical Decision Tree

$$\text{Need } E[X^n] \text{ from } M_X(t)?$$

```
Is M(t) a rational function?        → Differentiate directly (Expo, Gamma with integer a)
  e.g. λ/(λ-t), (λ/(λ-t))^a

Is M(t) an exponential of a         → Series expand the exponent (Pois, Normal)  
  simple function?
  e.g. e^{t²/2}, e^{λ(eᵗ-1)}

Is M(t) a polynomial/power?         → Differentiate (Bern, Bin)
  e.g. (q+peᵗ)ⁿ

Do you only need E[X] and Var(X)?   → Use K(t) = log M(t), take 1st and 2nd derivatives
```

## Things I Keep Getting Confused

> _Honest accounting._

1. **Evaluating derivatives at $t = 0$ before simplifying.** Always simplify the derivative expression first, _then_ set $t = 0$. Setting $t = 0$ mid-differentiation loses terms.
    
2. **Series expansion bookkeeping for the Poisson.** The composition $e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$ requires expanding $e^t - 1$ first, then re-exponentiating. Don't try to expand $e^{\lambda e^t}$ directly without the $-\lambda$ adjustment — you'll get the raw moments of the wrong distribution.
    
3. **Forgetting that the coefficient of $t^n$ in $M(t)$ is $E[X^n]/n!$, not $E[X^n]$.** The factorial is there because of the Taylor series structure. Off by $n!$ is a common and painful error.
    
4. **Trying to compute $E[X^3]$ and $E[X^4]$ for $N(\mu, \sigma^2)$ by differentiating $e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2}$ directly.** Painful. Always reduce to $Z \sim N(0,1)$ first using the affine transformation, then use the series expansion of $e^{t^2/2}$.
    
5. **Using the MGF to compute moments when the MGF doesn't exist.** If $X$ is lognormal or Cauchy, you can't use this method. Check existence first.
