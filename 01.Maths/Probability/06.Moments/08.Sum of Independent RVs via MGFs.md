
> _The MGF is one of those tools that seems like overkill until the day you need it — and then it becomes indispensable. The key insight here is almost embarrassingly clean: independence turns convolution into multiplication._

---

## Why MGFs at all?

Computing the distribution of $X + Y$ directly means **convolving** the densities (or PMFs). That's painful. The MGF converts this convolution problem into an algebraic one: independence means the joint factors, and the expectation of a product becomes a product of expectations.

This is the same philosophical move as using Fourier transforms for convolution in signal processing. The MGF is (essentially) a two-sided Laplace transform of the distribution.

---

## The Main Theorem

**Theorem.** Let $X_1, X_2, \ldots, X_n$ be **independent** random variables with MGFs $M_{X_1}(t), \ldots, M_{X_n}(t)$, each finite in some open interval $(-h, h)$ around $0$. Define $S_n = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_n$. Then:

$$\boxed{M_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{X_i}(t)}$$

### Proof

This is one of those proofs where, once you see it, you wonder why anyone writes it out. But let's be careful.

$$M_{S_n}(t) = \mathbb{E}\left[e^{t S_n}\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[e^{t(X_1 + \cdots + X_n)}\right] = \mathbb{E}\left[\prod_{i=1}^n e^{t X_i}\right]$$

Now I use the key step: **for independent random variables, the expectation of a product equals the product of expectations**. This is not obvious — it requires independence, not just uncorrelatedness.

> **Lemma.** If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are independent, then for any Borel functions $g_1, \ldots, g_n$ (with finite expectations): $$\mathbb{E}\left[\prod_{i=1}^n g_i(X_i)\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}[g_i(X_i)]$$
> 
> _Proof sketch._ For $n=2$: independence means the joint density (or PMF) factors as $f_{X_1, X_2}(x_1, x_2) = f_{X_1}(x_1) f_{X_2}(x_2)$. Then: $$\mathbb{E}[g_1(X_1)g_2(X_2)] = \int \int g_1(x_1)g_2(x_2) f_{X_1}(x_1)f_{X_2}(x_2), dx_1, dx_2$$ $$= \left(\int g_1(x_1) f_{X_1}(x_1), dx_1\right)\left(\int g_2(x_2) f_{X_2}(x_2), dx_2\right) = \mathbb{E}[g_1(X_1)] \cdot \mathbb{E}[g_2(X_2)]$$ Induction handles $n > 2$. $\square$

Applying the lemma with $g_i(x) = e^{tx}$:

$$M_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n \mathbb{E}\left[e^{t X_i}\right] = \prod_{i=1}^n M_{X_i}(t) \qquad \square$$

---

## The Uniqueness Theorem (Critical!)

The above theorem would be useless without this:

**Theorem (Uniqueness of MGFs).** If two distributions have MGFs that are equal and finite on some open interval $(-h, h)$, then the two distributions are identical.

_This is the theorem that lets you "read off" the distribution from the MGF._ Without uniqueness, knowing $M_{S_n}$ tells you nothing about the law of $S_n$.

> I always need to remind myself: the uniqueness theorem is **non-trivial** and its proof goes through the theory of moment problems / analytic functions. Blitzstein & Hwang sketch this; the full proof lives in Billingsley or Durrett. Accept it as a black box for now unless you want to go down that rabbit hole.

## Working Examples

### 1. Sum of Independent Poissons

Let $X_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_i)$, independent. Recall: $$M_X(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$$

Then: $$M_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n e^{\lambda_i(e^t - 1)} = e^{(\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i)(e^t - 1)}$$

This is the MGF of a $\text{Poisson}(\lambda_1 + \cdots + \lambda_n)$. By uniqueness:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \sim \text{Poisson}!\left(\sum_{i=1}^n \lambda_i\right)$$

_This is the **reproductive property** of the Poisson._ Makes sense intuitively: merging independent Poisson processes (with rates $\lambda_i$) gives a Poisson process with rate $\sum \lambda_i$.

---

### 2. Sum of Independent Normals

Let $X_i \sim N(\mu_i, \sigma_i^2)$, independent. Recall: $$M_X(t) = e^{\mu t + \frac{1}{2}\sigma^2 t^2}$$

Then: $$M_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n e^{\mu_i t + \frac{1}{2}\sigma_i^2 t^2} = e^{(\sum \mu_i)t + \frac{1}{2}(\sum \sigma_i^2)t^2}$$

By uniqueness:

$$\sum_{i=1}^n X_i \sim N!\left(\sum \mu_i,, \sum \sigma_i^2\right)$$

_This is the most important special case. The MGF proof is far cleaner than the density convolution approach._

---

### 3. Sum of $n$ i.i.d. Exponentials → Gamma

Let $X_i \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$ (rate $\lambda$), i.i.d. Recall: $$M_X(t) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}, \quad t < \lambda$$

Then: $$M_{S_n}(t) = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right)^n$$

This is exactly the MGF of a $\text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$ distribution. So:

$$X_1 + \cdots + X_n \sim \text{Gamma}(n, \lambda)$$

_This is how you prove that a Gamma is the sum of i.i.d. Exponentials — the "waiting time for the $n$-th arrival in a Poisson process" interpretation made rigorous._

---

### 4. Sum of Independent Gammas (same rate)

More generally: $X_i \sim \text{Gamma}(a_i, \lambda)$, independent, **same rate $\lambda$**. Then:

$$M_{S_n}(t) = \prod_{i=1}^n \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right)^{a_i} = \left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}\right)^{\sum a_i}$$

So $S_n \sim \text{Gamma}(\sum a_i, \lambda)$. The Gamma is also reproductive — in its shape parameter, holding rate fixed.

> ⚠️ **Watch out:** This breaks down if the rates differ. $\text{Gamma}(a_1, \lambda_1) + \text{Gamma}(a_2, \lambda_2)$ with $\lambda_1 \neq \lambda_2$ does **not** have a closed-form Gamma distribution. The MGF is $\left(\frac{\lambda_1}{\lambda_1-t}\right)^{a_1}\left(\frac{\lambda_2}{\lambda_2-t}\right)^{a_2}$, which doesn't simplify to a standard family.

## Cumulant Generating Function (a cleaner object)

Define the **CGF**: $K_X(t) = \log M_X(t)$.

Then independence gives:

$$K_{S_n}(t) = \sum_{i=1}^n K_{X_i}(t)$$

The CGF turns **products into sums**, and its derivatives at $0$ give the **cumulants**: $\kappa_1 = \mathbb{E}[X]$, $\kappa_2 = \text{Var}(X)$, $\kappa_3 = \mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)^3]$, etc.

So the fact that $\text{Var}(X+Y) = \text{Var}(X) + \text{Var}(Y)$ (for independent $X, Y$) is just $K''_{X+Y}(0) = K''_X(0) + K''_Y(0)$, which is obvious from the sum formula.

## Common MGF Reference

|Distribution|MGF $M_X(t)$|Domain|
|---|---|---|
|$\text{Bernoulli}(p)$|$1 - p + pe^t$|all $t$|
|$\text{Binomial}(n,p)$|$(1-p+pe^t)^n$|all $t$|
|$\text{Poisson}(\lambda)$|$e^{\lambda(e^t-1)}$|all $t$|
|$\text{Expo}(\lambda)$|$\frac{\lambda}{\lambda - t}$|$t < \lambda$|
|$\text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$|$\left(\frac{\lambda}{\lambda-t}\right)^a$|$t < \lambda$|
|$N(\mu, \sigma^2)$|$e^{\mu t + \sigma^2 t^2/2}$|all $t$|
|$\chi^2_n$|$(1-2t)^{-n/2}$|$t < 1/2$|

> The $\chi^2_n$ row: since $\chi^2_n = Z_1^2 + \cdots + Z_n^2$ with $Z_i \sim N(0,1)$ i.i.d., and $Z_i^2 \sim \text{Gamma}(1/2, 1/2)$, you get $M_{\chi^2_n}(t) = (1-2t)^{-n/2}$ by the Gamma reproductive property. Clean.

## Pitfalls and Subtleties

**1. MGF may not exist.**  
The MGF $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$ can be infinite. For example, the log-normal distribution has no finite MGF at any $t > 0$. In those cases, the **characteristic function** $\varphi_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{itX}]$ always exists (since $|e^{itX}| = 1$), and the same product formula holds for sums of independent RVs. This is the basis of the proof of the CLT.

**2. Independence is essential.**  
The product formula $M_{X+Y}(t) = M_X(t) M_Y(t)$ requires independence. For general $X, Y$: $M_{X+Y}(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{t(X+Y)}]$, and this does **not** factor unless $X \perp Y$.

**3. Domain must overlap.**  
Each $M_{X_i}(t)$ must be finite in a common open interval containing $0$. If the domains don't overlap, the product isn't valid.

## Connection to the CLT

The MGF/CF approach is the clean path to the Central Limit Theorem. If $X_i$ are i.i.d. with mean $\mu$ and variance $\sigma^2$, standardize:

$$Z_n = \frac{S_n - n\mu}{\sigma\sqrt{n}}$$

Then $M_{Z_n}(t) \to e^{t^2/2}$ as $n \to \infty$ — the MGF of $N(0,1)$. The machinery behind this is a Taylor expansion of $\log M_X(t/\sigma\sqrt{n})$ around $0$. See the CLT note.
