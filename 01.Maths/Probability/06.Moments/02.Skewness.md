
> _I keep coming back to this — skewness is one of those things that feels obvious once you see it geometrically, but the moment you try to prove anything about it the third moment bites back. Write this carefully._
## The Core Idea

Variance tells me how _spread out_ a distribution is. But two distributions can have identical means and variances yet look completely different — one might have a long right tail dragging probability mass out, another a long left tail. **Skewness captures this asymmetry.**

The intuition before any formula: if the right tail is heavier, large positive deviations from the mean are more common (or more extreme) than large negative ones. The distribution is _pulled_ rightward. This is **positive skew**. The mirror image is **negative skew**.

---

## Definition

Let $X$ be a random variable with $\mu = E[X]$, $\sigma^2 = \text{Var}(X) < \infty$, and $E[|X|^3] < \infty$.

$$\boxed{\text{Skew}(X) = E!\left[\left(\frac{X - \mu}{\sigma}\right)^{!3}\right] = \frac{E[(X-\mu)^3]}{\sigma^3}}$$

The standardization by $\sigma^3$ is not cosmetic — it makes skewness **dimensionless** and **scale-invariant**. Without it, multiplying $X$ by a constant would change the skewness, which is absurd for a measure of _shape_.

### Why the Third Moment?

- The **first central moment** $E[X - \mu] = 0$ always. Useless.
- The **second central moment** $E[(X-\mu)^2] = \sigma^2$. Symmetric around zero, so it can't detect asymmetry.
- The **third central moment** $E[(X-\mu)^3]$: cubing _preserves sign_. Positive deviations contribute positively, negative ones negatively. If the right tail dominates, the positive contributions win. Perfect.

---

## Properties

**Proposition 1 (Affine invariance).**  
For constants $a > 0$ and $b \in \mathbb{R}$: $$\text{Skew}(aX + b) = \text{Skew}(X)$$

_Proof._  
Let $Y = aX + b$. Then $E[Y] = a\mu + b$ and $\text{SD}(Y) = a\sigma$. So: $$\text{Skew}(Y) = \frac{E[(Y - (a\mu+b))^3]}{(a\sigma)^3} = \frac{E[(aX - a\mu)^3]}{a^3\sigma^3} = \frac{a^3 E[(X-\mu)^3]}{a^3\sigma^3} = \text{Skew}(X). \quad \square$$

> _This is the whole point of standardizing. Location and scale are irrelevant to shape._

**Proposition 2 (Sign flip).**  
$$\text{Skew}(-X) = -\text{Skew}(X)$$

_Proof._ $E[(-X - (-\mu))^3] = E[-(X-\mu)^3] = -E[(X-\mu)^3]$, and $\text{SD}(-X) = \sigma$. Divide and the sign flips. $\square$

**Proposition 3 (Symmetry implies zero skewness).**  
If $X - \mu$ and $-(X - \mu)$ have the same distribution (i.e., $X$ is symmetric about $\mu$), then $\text{Skew}(X) = 0$.

_Proof._  
Let $Z = X - \mu$. By symmetry, $Z \overset{d}{=} -Z$. Then: $$E[Z^3] = E[(-Z)^3] = -E[Z^3]$$ So $2E[Z^3] = 0$, i.e. $E[Z^3] = 0$. $\square$

> ⚠️ **The converse is false.** Zero skewness does _not_ imply symmetry. A distribution can have $E[(X-\mu)^3] = 0$ without being symmetric — the positive and negative cubic contributions simply cancel. Don't get sloppy here.

## Geometric Intuition: Tail Behavior

Here's the picture I keep in my head:

```
Negative skew          Symmetric           Positive skew
(left tail heavy)                          (right tail heavy)

   ████                  ████                    ████
  ██████               ████████                ██████
 ████████             ██████████              ████████
████████████        ████████████            ████████████
←——————————→        ←——————————→            ←——————————→
  long left           balanced               long right
```

But be careful about the **mean vs. mode** relationship. A common heuristic says:

> _Right-skewed $\Rightarrow$ mean $>$ median $>$ mode_

This is often true but **not a theorem**. It's a rule of thumb that fails for sufficiently pathological distributions. What _is_ always true: in a right-skewed distribution, the mean is pulled in the direction of the long tail (because the mean is sensitive to extreme values, while the median is not).

The deeper reason: $E[(X-\mu)^3]$ is dominated by the tails. Points far from the mean contribute _cubically_ — a deviation of $2\sigma$ contributes $8$ times more than a deviation of $\sigma$. So the sign of the skewness is really telling you which tail has the more extreme (or more probable extreme) values.

## Computing Skewness: The Expansion Formula

Computing $E[(X-\mu)^3]$ directly is painful. The expansion is your friend:

$$E[(X-\mu)^3] = E[X^3] - 3\mu E[X^2] + 3\mu^2 E[X] - \mu^3 = E[X^3] - 3\mu\sigma^2 - \mu^3$$

_Proof._ Expand $(X - \mu)^3 = X^3 - 3\mu X^2 + 3\mu^2 X - \mu^3$ and take expectations: $$E[(X-\mu)^3] = E[X^3] - 3\mu E[X^2] + 3\mu^2\mu - \mu^3$$ Use $E[X^2] = \sigma^2 + \mu^2$: $$= E[X^3] - 3\mu(\sigma^2 + \mu^2) + 3\mu^3 - \mu^3 = E[X^3] - 3\mu\sigma^2 - \mu^3. \quad \square$$

So in practice: $$\text{Skew}(X) = \frac{E[X^3] - 3\mu\sigma^2 - \mu^3}{\sigma^3}$$

---

## Relation to the MGF and CGF

This is where it gets elegant. Let $M(t) = E[e^{tX}]$ be the MGF (assuming it exists in a neighborhood of 0), and let $K(t) = \log M(t)$ be the **cumulant generating function (CGF)**.

Recall that the **cumulants** $\kappa_n$ are defined by: $$K(t) = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \kappa_n \frac{t^n}{n!}$$

The first three cumulants are: $$\kappa_1 = \mu, \qquad \kappa_2 = \sigma^2, \qquad \kappa_3 = E[(X-\mu)^3]$$

So:

$$\boxed{\text{Skew}(X) = \frac{\kappa_3}{\kappa_2^{3/2}} = \frac{K'''(0)}{(K''(0))^{3/2}}}$$

_Why does this work?_

$K'''(0) = \kappa_3$ by definition of cumulants. The third cumulant equals the third central moment (this is a theorem — cumulants and central moments agree starting from the third). And $K''(0) = \kappa_2 = \sigma^2$, so $\kappa_2^{3/2} = \sigma^3$.

**Why cumulants are nicer than moments for skewness:**  
For _independent_ $X, Y$: $$\kappa_n(X + Y) = \kappa_n(X) + \kappa_n(Y)$$

Cumulants add. Moments don't. So if $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ with i.i.d. terms: $$\text{Skew}(S_n) = \frac{n\kappa_3}{(n\kappa_2)^{3/2}} = \frac{\kappa_3}{\kappa_2^{3/2}} \cdot \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$$

This is beautiful: **the skewness of a sum of i.i.d. random variables decays like $1/\sqrt{n}$.** This is exactly what the CLT is telling us — $S_n$ is becoming more symmetric as $n \to \infty$, and the rate is $1/\sqrt{n}$.

## Skewness of Named Distributions

### Normal Distribution $X \sim N(\mu, \sigma^2)$

$$\text{Skew}(X) = 0$$

_Proof._ The normal distribution is symmetric about its mean by definition. Apply Proposition 3. $\square$

---

### Exponential Distribution $X \sim \text{Expo}(\lambda)$

$$E[X] = \frac{1}{\lambda}, \quad E[X^2] = \frac{2}{\lambda^2}, \quad E[X^3] = \frac{6}{\lambda^3}$$

So $\mu = 1/\lambda$, $\sigma^2 = 1/\lambda^2$, and: $$E[(X-\mu)^3] = \frac{6}{\lambda^3} - 3\cdot\frac{1}{\lambda}\cdot\frac{1}{\lambda^2} - \frac{1}{\lambda^3} = \frac{6 - 3 - 1}{\lambda^3} = \frac{2}{\lambda^3}$$

$$\text{Skew}(X) = \frac{2/\lambda^3}{(1/\lambda^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{2/\lambda^3}{1/\lambda^3} = \boxed{2}$$

_Remark._ The skewness of Expo is always $2$, regardless of $\lambda$. By the affine invariance proposition, this had to be the case — $\lambda$ is a scale parameter.

The value $2$ makes sense geometrically: the exponential has a hard left boundary at $0$ and an infinite right tail. It's definitively right-skewed.

---

### Gamma Distribution $X \sim \text{Gamma}(a, \lambda)$

A Gamma$(a, \lambda)$ random variable is the sum of $a$ i.i.d. Expo$(\lambda)$ variables (when $a$ is a positive integer). Using the cumulant additivity property:

$$\kappa_3(X) = a \cdot \kappa_3(\text{Expo}(\lambda)) = a \cdot \frac{2}{\lambda^3}$$ $$\kappa_2(X) = a \cdot \frac{1}{\lambda^2}$$

$$\text{Skew}(X) = \frac{2a/\lambda^3}{(a/\lambda^2)^{3/2}} = \frac{2a/\lambda^3}{a^{3/2}/\lambda^3} = \frac{2}{\sqrt{a}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Skew}(\text{Gamma}(a,\lambda)) = \frac{2}{\sqrt{a}}}$$

_This is exactly the $1/\sqrt{n}$ decay._ As $a \to \infty$, the Gamma becomes more symmetric — consistent with the fact that Gamma$(a, \lambda)$, normalized, converges to a Normal by the CLT.

---

### Poisson Distribution $X \sim \text{Pois}(\lambda)$

The MGF of $\text{Pois}(\lambda)$ is $M(t) = e^{\lambda(e^t - 1)}$, so the CGF is: $$K(t) = \lambda(e^t - 1)$$

Then: $$K'(t) = \lambda e^t, \quad K''(t) = \lambda e^t, \quad K'''(t) = \lambda e^t$$

At $t = 0$: $\kappa_1 = \kappa_2 = \kappa_3 = \lambda$. So:

$$\text{Skew}(X) = \frac{\lambda}{\lambda^{3/2}} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda}}$$

$$\boxed{\text{Skew}(\text{Pois}(\lambda)) = \frac{1}{\sqrt{\lambda}}}$$

_Remark._ Poisson is right-skewed (the support is ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$, bounded below). As $\lambda \to \infty$, it becomes more symmetric — again the CLT, since Pois$(\lambda)$ is the sum of $\lambda$ i.i.d. Pois$(1)$ variables.

---

### Beta Distribution $X \sim \text{Beta}(a, b)$

$$\text{Skew}(X) = \frac{2(b-a)\sqrt{a+b+1}}{(a+b+2)\sqrt{ab}}$$

I won't prove this here in full — the computation via raw moments is algebraically brutal. The key observations:

- When $a = b$: skewness $= 0$. (Beta$(a,a)$ is symmetric about $1/2$.)
- When $a < b$: skewness $> 0$. The distribution is right-skewed — mass concentrated near $0$.
- When $a > b$: skewness $< 0$. The distribution is left-skewed — mass concentrated near $1$.

_Geometric check:_ Beta$(1,1)$ is Uniform$[0,1]$, which is symmetric. Beta$(0.5, 5)$ has most mass near $0$ with a long right tail — positive skew. Consistent with the formula.

## A Warning About Zero Skewness

As noted in Proposition 3's remark: **zero skewness $\not\Rightarrow$ symmetry**.

It's worth constructing a counterexample mentally. Take a discrete distribution on ${-2, -1, 0, 1, 2}$ with carefully chosen probabilities such that $E[X] = 0$ and $E[X^3] = 0$ but $P(X = -2) \neq P(X = 2)$ and $P(X = -1) \neq P(X = 1)$. The cubic contributions of $\pm 2$ (which are $\pm 8$) and $\pm 1$ (which are $\pm 1$) can cancel without the distribution being symmetric.

This matters statistically: skewness is a _moment-based_ summary. It cannot capture all features of distributional shape.

## Things I Keep Getting Confused

> _Self-notes — honest mistakes I've made._

1. **"Positive skew means the bulk of the data is on the right."** No. Positive skew means the _right tail is heavier/longer_. The bulk of the data is typically on the left, with a long tail dragging right. Think Expo — most values are small, but there's a long tail of large values.
    
2. **Forgetting the $\sigma^3$ in the denominator.** The numerator $E[(X-\mu)^3]$ alone is not skewness. It has units of $[X]^3$. Divide by $\sigma^3$ to get a dimensionless quantity.
    
3. **Assuming zero skewness means the distribution is symmetric.** It doesn't. The converse of Proposition 3 is false.
    
4. **Thinking skewness is additive for independent variables.** It's not — _cumulants_ are additive. Skewness, being a _ratio_ of cumulants, is not simply additive.
