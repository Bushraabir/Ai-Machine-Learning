

> _The PGF is the MGF's more elegant sibling, tailor-made for non-negative integer-valued RVs. Where the MGF encodes moments, the PGF encodes the entire probability sequence — and you can literally read off $P(X = k)$ by differentiating $k$ times. That's not a trick; it's the point._

---

## Motivation

Suppose $X$ takes values in ${0, 1, 2, \ldots}$. We could use the MGF $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$. But for integer-valued RVs there's a more natural object: substitute $z = e^t$, and instead of expanding in powers of $e^t$, expand in powers of $z$. The PGF is a **power series whose coefficients are exactly the probabilities**.

This makes it a generating function in the combinatorial sense — the same object that appears in enumerative combinatorics, which is no coincidence.

---

## Definition

**Definition.** Let $X$ be a non-negative integer-valued RV. The **probability generating function (PGF)** of $X$ is:

$$G_X(z) = \mathbb{E}[z^X] = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P(X = k), z^k$$

defined for all $z \in \mathbb{C}$ for which the series converges. It always converges for $|z| \leq 1$ (since $\sum P(X=k) = 1$), and often in a larger disk.

**Relation to MGF:** $G_X(z) = M_X(\log z)$, or equivalently $M_X(t) = G_X(e^t)$. They carry the same information; the PGF is just a reparametrisation optimised for discrete distributions.

---

## Recovery of Probabilities

This is the key property that justifies the name. Since $G_X(z) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P(X=k), z^k$ is a power series, we can recover each probability by differentiating:

$$P(X = k) = \frac{G_X^{(k)}(0)}{k!}$$

where $G_X^{(k)}$ denotes the $k$-th derivative. This is just the standard formula for Taylor coefficients.

**Proof.** Differentiate $G_X(z) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty P(X=k), z^k$ term by term (justified by uniform convergence on $|z| < R$ where $R$ is the radius of convergence):

$$G_X^{(k)}(z) = \sum_{n=k}^\infty P(X=n), \frac{n!}{(n-k)!}, z^{n-k}$$

Evaluating at $z = 0$ kills every term except $n = k$:

$$G_X^{(k)}(0) = k!, P(X = k) \implies P(X = k) = \frac{G_X^{(k)}(0)}{k!} \qquad \square$$

## Moments from the PGF

Define the **factorial moments**: $\mathbb{E}[X(X-1)\cdots(X-k+1)] = \mathbb{E}[(X)_k]$, the $k$-th **falling factorial moment**.

**Theorem.** $G_X^{(k)}(1) = \mathbb{E}[(X)_k]$.

**Proof.** Differentiate $G_X(z) = \mathbb{E}[z^X]$ under the expectation $k$ times:

$$G_X^{(k)}(z) = \mathbb{E}\left[\frac{d^k}{dz^k} z^X\right] = \mathbb{E}[X(X-1)\cdots(X-k+1), z^{X-k}]$$

Set $z = 1$: $G_X^{(k)}(1) = \mathbb{E}[X(X-1)\cdots(X-k+1)]$. $\square$

**Extracting ordinary moments:**

$$\mathbb{E}[X] = G_X'(1)$$ $$\mathbb{E}[X^2] = G_X''(1) + G_X'(1)$$ $$\text{Var}(X) = G_X''(1) + G_X'(1) - [G_X'(1)]^2$$

> The variance formula is worth memorising. $\text{Var}(X) = \mathbb{E}[X(X-1)] + \mathbb{E}[X] - (\mathbb{E}[X])^2 = G''(1) + G'(1) - (G'(1))^2$.

## Sum of Independent RVs

The same product formula from [[08.Sum of Independent RVs via MGFs]] carries over:

**Theorem.** If $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ are **independent** non-negative integer-valued RVs, then:

$$G_{X_1 + \cdots + X_n}(z) = \prod_{i=1}^n G_{X_i}(z)$$

**Proof.** Identical to the MGF proof: $\mathbb{E}[z^{X_1 + \cdots + X_n}] = \mathbb{E}[\prod z^{X_i}] = \prod \mathbb{E}[z^{X_i}]$ by independence. $\square$

## Standard PGFs

### Bernoulli$(p)$

$$G_X(z) = (1-p) + pz = q + pz$$

### Binomial$(n, p)$

$X = \sum_{i=1}^n B_i$, $B_i \sim \text{Bern}(p)$ i.i.d. By the product formula:

$$G_X(z) = (q + pz)^n$$

_This is the reproductive property of the Binomial: $\text{Bin}(m,p) + \text{Bin}(n,p) \sim \text{Bin}(m+n, p)$._

### Geometric$(p)$ (number of trials until first success, supported on ${1, 2, \ldots}$)

$P(X = k) = q^{k-1}p$ for $k \geq 1$:

$$G_X(z) = \sum_{k=1}^\infty q^{k-1}p, z^k = pz \sum_{k=0}^\infty (qz)^k = \frac{pz}{1-qz}, \quad |z| < 1/q$$

### Negative Binomial$,(r, p)$ (number of trials until $r$-th success)

$X = \sum_{i=1}^r G_i$, $G_i \sim \text{Geom}(p)$ i.i.d.:

$$G_X(z) = \left(\frac{pz}{1-qz}\right)^r$$

### Poisson$(\lambda)$

$P(X = k) = e^{-\lambda}\lambda^k/k!$:

$$G_X(z) = \sum_{k=0}^\infty e^{-\lambda}\frac{\lambda^k}{k!}z^k = e^{-\lambda}\sum_{k=0}^\infty \frac{(\lambda z)^k}{k!} = e^{\lambda(z-1)}$$

Check: $G_X'(1) = \lambda e^{\lambda(z-1)}\big|_{z=1} = \lambda = \mathbb{E}[X]$. ✓

**Reproductive property:** $\text{Poisson}(\lambda_1) + \text{Poisson}(\lambda_2) \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda_1 + \lambda_2)$, since: $$e^{\lambda_1(z-1)} \cdot e^{\lambda_2(z-1)} = e^{(\lambda_1+\lambda_2)(z-1)}$$

## Compound Distributions (The Real Payoff)

This is where PGFs earn their keep. A **compound distribution** is $S = X_1 + X_2 + \cdots + X_N$, where $N$ itself is a random variable, independent of the i.i.d. $X_i$.

Think: $N$ is the number of customers today, $X_i$ is what customer $i$ spends. What's $G_S(z)$?

**Theorem (Compound PGF).** If $N \perp (X_1, X_2, \ldots)$ with $X_i$ i.i.d. copies of $X$, then:

$$\boxed{G_S(z) = G_N(G_X(z))}$$

That's the PGF of $N$, evaluated at $G_X(z)$. **Composition**, not multiplication.

### Proof

Condition on $N$:

$$G_S(z) = \mathbb{E}[z^S] = \mathbb{E}\left[\mathbb{E}[z^S \mid N]\right]$$

Given $N = n$: $S = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$, so $\mathbb{E}[z^S \mid N = n] = G_X(z)^n$ (by independence and the product formula). Therefore:

$$G_S(z) = \mathbb{E}\left[G_X(z)^N\right] = G_N(G_X(z))$$

where the last step uses the definition of $G_N$: $G_N(w) = \mathbb{E}[w^N]$, with $w = G_X(z)$. $\square$

### Example: Compound Poisson

Let $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$, $X_i \sim \text{Poisson}(\mu)$. Then:

$$G_S(z) = G_N(G_X(z)) = e^{\lambda(G_X(z) - 1)} = e^{\lambda(e^{\mu(z-1)} - 1)}$$

This is the PGF of a **compound Poisson distribution** (relevant in insurance risk theory, queueing, etc.).

> The compound PGF formula $G_S = G_N \circ G_X$ is one of my favourite results in all of probability. It's the kind of thing where the notation is doing serious mathematical work — iteration of functions as a model of randomness built on randomness.

## Branching Processes

The most beautiful application of compound PGFs. A **Galton–Watson branching process** models population growth:

- Generation 0: one individual (the ancestor).
- Each individual independently produces $k$ offspring with probability $p_k$, $k = 0, 1, 2, \ldots$
- $Z_n$ = population in generation $n$.

Let $G(z) = G_{Z_1}(z) = \sum_k p_k z^k$ be the offspring PGF.

**Claim:** The PGF of $Z_n$ is the $n$-fold iterate of $G$:

$$G_{Z_n}(z) = \underbrace{G \circ G \circ \cdots \circ G}_{n \text{ times}}(z) =: G^{(n)}(z)$$

**Proof by induction.** Base: $G_{Z_1} = G$. ✓

Inductive step: $Z_{n+1} = \sum_{i=1}^{Z_n} X_i$, where $X_i$ are i.i.d. offspring counts. By the compound PGF theorem:

$$G_{Z_{n+1}}(z) = G_{Z_n}(G(z)) = G^{(n)}(G(z)) = G^{(n+1)}(z) \qquad \square$$

**Extinction probability.** Let $q = P(\text{extinction}) = P(Z_n = 0 \text{ for some } n) = \lim_{n\to\infty} P(Z_n = 0)$.

Since $P(Z_n = 0) = G^{(n)}(0)$ and extinction is an increasing event:

$$q = \lim_{n \to \infty} G^{(n)}(0)$$

One can show $q$ satisfies the **fixed-point equation**:

$$q = G(q)$$

and is the **smallest non-negative fixed point** of $G$.

**Theorem.** Let $\mu = G'(1) = \mathbb{E}[Z_1]$ be the mean offspring number. Then:

- If $\mu \leq 1$: $q = 1$ (certain extinction).
- If $\mu > 1$: $q < 1$ (positive probability of survival), and $q$ is the unique fixed point of $G$ in $[0, 1)$.

> I find the fixed-point characterisation of the extinction probability beautiful. The equation $q = G(q)$ is saying: to go extinct, either the first individual has no children ($p_0$), or it has some children, each of whose lineages independently go extinct. That's just conditioning on the first generation. The PGF encodes this recursion automatically.

## PGF vs MGF: When to Use Which

||PGF $G_X(z) = \mathbb{E}[z^X]$|MGF $M_X(t) = \mathbb{E}[e^{tX}]$|
|---|---|---|
|**Best for**|Non-negative integer-valued RVs|Any RV (continuous, discrete, mixed)|
|**Probabilities**|Directly: $P(X=k) = G^{(k)}(0)/k!$|Not directly readable|
|**Moments**|Factorial moments from $G^{(k)}(1)$|Ordinary moments from $M^{(k)}(0)$|
|**Compound distributions**|Clean: $G_S = G_N \circ G_X$|Messier|
|**Branching processes**|Natural (iterated composition)|Unnatural|
|**CLT / continuity theorem**|Not the right tool|Yes (or use characteristic functions)|

---

## Uniqueness

Just as with MGFs: **the PGF uniquely determines the distribution.** Since $G_X(z) = \sum_{k \geq 0} P(X=k), z^k$ is a power series, and power series have unique coefficients, knowing $G_X$ is equivalent to knowing all of $P(X=0), P(X=1), \ldots$ — i.e., the entire distribution.

## Worked Problem (Stat 110 Style)

**Problem.** $N \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda)$ light bulbs, each independently survives one day with probability $p$. Let $S$ = number of bulbs surviving. Find the distribution of $S$.

**Solution via PGF.** Each bulb survives $\sim \text{Bern}(p)$, so $G_{\text{Bern}}(z) = q + pz$. By the compound formula:

$$G_S(z) = G_N(G_{\text{Bern}}(z)) = e^{\lambda(q+pz - 1)} = e^{\lambda p(z-1)}$$

This is the PGF of $\text{Poisson}(\lambda p)$. So $S \sim \text{Poisson}(\lambda p)$.

_This is the **Poisson thinning** result: thin a Poisson by independent $p$-coin flips, get a Poisson with rate $\lambda p$. Proved in one line with PGFs._
