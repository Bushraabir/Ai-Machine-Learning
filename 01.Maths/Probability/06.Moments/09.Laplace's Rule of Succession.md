> _Laplace asked: the sun has risen every day for 5000 years — what's the probability it rises tomorrow? The answer he derived is one of the most elegant pieces of Bayesian reasoning ever written down. The mathematics is simple. The philosophy it encodes is deep enough that people still argue about it._

---

## The Problem

You observe a sequence of trials. The first $n$ are all successes. What is the probability the $(n+1)$-th trial is also a success?

The frequentist answer is $\hat{p} = 1$, which is absurd — we've never seen a failure, so we just declare certainty? Laplace's answer avoids this by placing a prior on the unknown success probability.

**Laplace's Rule of Succession:** After observing $n$ successes in $n$ trials:

$$P(\text{next trial is a success} \mid \text{$n$ successes in $n$ trials}) = \frac{n+1}{n+2}$$

More generally, after $s$ successes in $n$ trials:

$$P(\text{next is success} \mid s \text{ successes in } n \text{ trials}) = \frac{s+1}{n+2}$$

---

## Setup and Proof

**Model.** Suppose the trials are i.i.d. $\text{Bernoulli}(p)$, but $p$ is unknown. We model our uncertainty about $p$ with a prior distribution.

**Laplace's prior:** $p \sim \text{Uniform}(0, 1)$, i.e. $f(p) = 1$ on $[0,1]$.

This is the **principle of indifference** — with no prior knowledge, treat all values of $p$ as equally likely. (We'll discuss whether this is reasonable.)

Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n \overset{\text{iid}}{\sim} \text{Bern}(p)$, and let $S_n = X_1 + \cdots + X_n$ be the number of successes.

We want $P(X_{n+1} = 1 \mid S_n = s)$.

**Step 1: Likelihood.**

$$P(S_n = s \mid p) = \binom{n}{s} p^s (1-p)^{n-s}$$

**Step 2: Posterior via Bayes.**

$$f(p \mid S_n = s) = \frac{P(S_n = s \mid p), f(p)}{P(S_n = s)} = \frac{\binom{n}{s} p^s (1-p)^{n-s} \cdot 1}{\int_0^1 \binom{n}{s} p^s (1-p)^{n-s}, dp}$$

The normalising constant is:

$$\int_0^1 p^s (1-p)^{n-s}, dp = B(s+1,, n-s+1) = \frac{s!,(n-s)!}{(n+1)!}$$

using the Beta function identity $B(a,b) = \frac{(a-1)!(b-1)!}{(a+b-1)!}$ (for integer arguments). So:

$$f(p \mid S_n = s) = \frac{p^s(1-p)^{n-s}}{B(s+1, n-s+1)} = \frac{(n+1)!}{s!,(n-s)!}, p^s(1-p)^{n-s}$$

This is exactly a $\text{Beta}(s+1,, n-s+1)$ density. So:

$$p \mid S_n = s \sim \text{Beta}(s+1,, n-s+1)$$

**Step 3: Posterior predictive.**

$$P(X_{n+1} = 1 \mid S_n = s) = \mathbb{E}[p \mid S_n = s]$$

The last equality holds because, given $p$, $X_{n+1} \sim \text{Bern}(p)$, so $P(X_{n+1}=1 \mid p) = p$, and:

$$P(X_{n+1} = 1 \mid S_n = s) = \int_0^1 P(X_{n+1}=1 \mid p), f(p \mid S_n = s), dp = \mathbb{E}[p \mid S_n = s]$$

The mean of $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ is $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha + \beta}$, so:

$$\mathbb{E}[p \mid S_n = s] = \frac{s+1}{(s+1)+(n-s+1)} = \frac{s+1}{n+2}$$

Therefore:

$$\boxed{P(X_{n+1} = 1 \mid S_n = s) = \frac{s+1}{n+2}}$$

$\square$

For the all-successes case $s = n$: $P = \frac{n+1}{n+2}$. For $n = 5000$ years of sunrises, this gives $\frac{5001}{5002} \approx 0.9998$. Not certain, but very close — and the answer is finite, unlike the frequentist estimate of 1. $\square$

## The Beta-Binomial Conjugate Pair

The proof above reveals something more important than just the formula: the **Beta-Binomial conjugacy**.

**Definition.** A prior family $\mathcal{F}$ is **conjugate** to a likelihood if the posterior is also in $\mathcal{F}$.

**Theorem.** The $\text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ prior is conjugate to the Binomial likelihood. Specifically:

$$p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta), \quad S_n \mid p \sim \text{Bin}(n, p)$$

$$\implies p \mid S_n = s \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha + s,, \beta + n - s)$$

**Proof.**

$$f(p \mid S_n = s) \propto f(p) \cdot P(S_n = s \mid p) \propto p^{\alpha-1}(1-p)^{\beta-1} \cdot p^s(1-p)^{n-s}$$ $$= p^{(\alpha+s)-1}(1-p)^{(\beta+n-s)-1}$$

This is proportional to a $\text{Beta}(\alpha+s, \beta+n-s)$ density. Since it must normalise to 1, it _is_ that density. $\square$

**Interpretation.** $\alpha$ and $\beta$ act like "prior pseudo-counts": $\alpha$ = prior successes, $\beta$ = prior failures. Each observed success increments $\alpha$ by 1; each failure increments $\beta$ by 1.

Laplace's uniform prior is $\text{Beta}(1, 1)$ — one phantom success and one phantom failure, encoding total ignorance.

The posterior predictive mean is:

$$\frac{\alpha + s}{\alpha + \beta + n}$$

which interpolates between the prior mean $\frac{\alpha}{\alpha+\beta}$ and the MLE $\frac{s}{n}$, with the balance determined by how much data you have relative to the strength of the prior ($\alpha + \beta$).

---

## What the Formula Is Really Saying

Rewrite:

$$\frac{s+1}{n+2} = \frac{n}{n+2} \cdot \frac{s}{n} + \frac{2}{n+2} \cdot \frac{1}{2}$$

The prediction is a **weighted average of the MLE** $s/n$ **and the prior mean** $1/2$, with weights proportional to $n$ and $2$ (the "strength" of the uniform prior = $\alpha + \beta = 2$).

As $n \to \infty$, the data overwhelms the prior and the formula converges to $s/n$ — exactly what you'd expect from any reasonable Bayesian procedure.

---

## A More General Version

**Theorem.** Suppose $p \sim \text{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ and we observe $s$ successes in $n$ trials. Then the probability of $m$ successes in the next $k$ trials is:

$$P(S_k^{\text{new}} = m \mid S_n = s) = \binom{k}{m} \frac{B(\alpha + s + m,, \beta + n - s + k - m)}{B(\alpha + s,, \beta + n - s)}$$

This is the **Beta-Binomial distribution**. Laplace's rule is the special case $k = 1$, $m = 1$, $\alpha = \beta = 1$.

---

## Philosophical Caveats

> _I feel obligated to record these, because a result this clean always conceals some philosophical debt._

**1. The uniform prior is not "assumption-free."**  
The principle of indifference — assigning equal probability to each value of $p$ — is not a neutral act. Under a change of variables $\theta = p^2$, the uniform prior on $p$ becomes a non-uniform prior on $\theta$. There is no such thing as an objective ignorance prior in general. This is **Bertrand's paradox** applied to priors.

Jeffreys proposed a prior that is invariant under reparametrisation: for the Binomial, the **Jeffreys prior** is $\text{Beta}(1/2, 1/2)$, giving the prediction:

$$P(X_{n+1} = 1 \mid S_n = s) = \frac{s + 1/2}{n+1}$$

which is slightly different from Laplace's formula.

**2. The exchangeability assumption.**  
The model assumes the trials are i.i.d. given $p$, and that the data is exchangeable. This is reasonable for coin flips; less so for sunrises (the sun doesn't flip a coin each morning).

de Finetti's theorem makes this precise: a sequence is exchangeable if and only if it can be represented as i.i.d. given some latent parameter with some prior. So the Beta-Binomial model is the most general exchangeable model for binary sequences.

**3. The rule handles zero observations correctly.**  
Classic problem: you've never seen a nuclear war. Does that mean $P(\text{nuclear war tomorrow}) = 0$? The frequentist says yes. Laplace says $\frac{0+1}{n+2} = \frac{1}{n+2}$, which is small but positive — a much saner answer.
